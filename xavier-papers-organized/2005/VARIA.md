2) Marie-Dominique Chenu

For the last few years, in an effort to understand the historical roots of our contemporary conceptions of philosophy and of theology, I have immersed myself in the literature concerning the shattering changes in the European cultural landscape that took place in the twelfth and the thirteenth centuries. One of the most salient and significant  of those changes was the transformation of the theological practice during that period from a mostly devotional reading and meditation of the biblical text, carried out in the monasteries and cathedral schools, into a highly formalized course of studies carried out exclusively in the universities and under the direction of certified specialists. Nothing can give a better idea of the distance traversed in the theological practice in a little more than a century than the contrast between the biblical text itself, which accompanied by the commentaries of the Fathers of the Church on it was originally the textbook for the course of studies which today we would call theological, and any of the numerous and highly technical Summae Theologicae [and commentaries on Peter the Lombard's Liber Sententiarum] which by the middle of the thirteenth century had come to dislodge the Bible from its central place in the curriculum.

In La théologie comme science au XIIIe siècle , a work that has remained a classic for the understanding of the development of theology into a scientific discipline, Jean-Marie Chenu explored the transformation undergone by the theological practice in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries. It is one important aspect of Chenu's thesis that the last decisive step in the evolution of theology was taken under the strong influence of the just rediscovered Aristotelian idea of scientific knowledge, which resulted in what later was to become known as "Scholastic theology." Chenu's thesis is amply supported by the available texts. Indeed, since ... Summa Aurea (about 1230) on, all the Summae Theologicae open with the question " an theologia sit scientia ," that is, whether theology could and should function as a science in the sense given by Aristotle to this term.

A second and no less important aspect of Chenu's thesis is that, unlike all his immediate predecessors interested in the question, Aquinas gave it an affirmative answer and was the first ever to do so, thereby conferring to the theological practice its definitive identity and the fully mature shape towards which it had been developping.

Whether or not Chenu was right in his interpretation of Aquinas' views on the scientific character of theology and, consequently, in his appraisal of Aquinas' contribution to the practice of theology has been the subject of much dispute, and I definitely lack the expertise to participate in the debate. Fortunately for my present purposes, the whole question concerning Aquinas himself may be bracketted. What is certain--and Chenu is quite right on this point--is that none of Aquinas' immediate predecessors or contemporaries who raised the question whether theology is a scientific discipline according to the Aristotelian model answered the question affirmatively, though it is true that some of them contributed elements that were later used to construe an affirmative answer to the question. It is no less certain, however, that many of them, if perhaps not all, were avidly eager to answer the question affirmatively, for they had come to see in Aristotle's views on scientific knowledge, as they understood them, the very standard to which every truth, the truths of their Catholic faith included, had to live up to qualify as certainly true. If, therefore, theology dealt with revealed truths which were by definition infallibly true, theology could and should be a scientific discipline according to the Artistotelian model. Yet, for all their eagerness, they found themeselves constrained by what they understood to be the nature of the Christian faith to deny that theology actually was, or even could conceivably be, a science.

If we are to listen to Chenu, what prevented them from acknowledging the scientific character of theology was not the nature of their faith but their lack of ingenuity to solve the difficulties with which they found themselves confronted. A genius was required for that, and Aquinas was that genius. Thanks to him theology was to acquire definitively the genuinely scientific character towards which it had been obscurely evolving for more than a century. But are we to listen to Chenu? Whatever the role that is to be attributed to Aquinas in this evolution and whichever the side on which Aquinas is to be rightfully placed in the controversy concerning the scientific character of theology, may it not conceivably be the case that those who answered the question negatively were right, and that the shape that the theological practice ended up taking to accommodate itself to the Aristotelian model was an aberration? This is the question I want to deal with .

Even as it stands, this question is too large for one paper.

I will therefore limit myself here to investigate just that aspect of the debate on the possibility of theology as a scientific discipline that concerns the Bible in particular. One of the main reasons that forced all of Aquinas' predecessors to concede that theology could not constitute a science after the Aristotelian model was the character of the biblical text itself. As they saw the matter, theology was the study of what had been divinely revealed, and what had been divinely revealed was identical with the scriptural text. It happened, however, to be the annoying case--annoying, that is, for those who aspired to practice theology as a scientific discipline--that the Bible was not, nor could without a distortion that amounted to a falsification be understood as, a scientific text. It, therefore, followed that theology itself, whose subject matter was the Bible's content, God's own word, could not become a scientific discipline without betraying itself.

One way of understanding Frei's thesis concerning the eclipse of Biblical narrative is the following: if the Bible was to be read as a narrative, as it had previously been read in the Christian tradition, then it could not be read realistically,  that is, not as history but as fiction, for narratives are fiction whereas history is realistic; if, on the other hand, the Bible was to be read as history, as it had previously been read in the Christian tradition, then it could not be read as a narrative, that is, as a dramatic unit developping across time, but only as a series of statements.

(3) Speech acts

DON'T SMOKE - DRINK COKE - We say that one means (because it is meant) as an invitation, the other as an order. The grammatical form is the same, but not the speech act. Understanding a language as a competent hearer is understanding the communicative intention incarnated in speech acts. My friend was a competent hearer, but for his own purposes was feigning not to be such. This game is more easily played concerning written texts, where the speaker is not there to determine how he-she meant what was said. Still, though the case I am referring to was a matter of a written text and the speaker (or writer) was not present, with a little social practice or inculturation in a twentieth century society in which there is public transportation as well as publicity, nobody misunderstands DRINK COKE for an order or DON'T SMOKE for an invitation. Social practice plus concrete context or setting generally suffice to make us competent readers of the sort of written texts I am referring to. The case, however, becomes thornier when we are confronted with a written text out of context. If in the street I find a piece of paper with a hand-written text that says TAKE THE BUS AND GO HOME, I would not know at all whether it was meant as a suggestion or as an order. Orson Wells's broadcast of The War of the Worlds is a striking case in point. Still in these two cases we hearers share with the speaker the same social-institutional context, which tends to guide our understanding, that is, our conduct as hearers. Not so in the Bible's case, whose entire text was written not only in an epoch very remote from us in time, but in a cultural setting entirely different from ours.

[ This, indeed, is the point of those who insist that language consists of speech acts, and that it is not words that mean but speakers who mean linguistically.

[ Certainly, this is a far cry from the naive and perhaps not uncommon view that the words themselves mean and all people do is choose which ones to use in order to communicate what they want to communicate, much as one could choose among many rocks the ones that have a certain shape in order to build a wall. Yet, this latter view is not totally farfetched, as acknowledged by the very people who insist on speech acts."Ich komme von dem Land wo die Citronen blumen" cannot properly be used to say "I am a German speaker," because people express linguistically what they mean by saying it, and the words quoted do not say "I am a German speaker" but "I come from the land where the lemon-trees flower."

[ Sentences express a complete intention. But what is a complete intention? Not just, say, an order, or a suggestion, but that which is ordered or suggested. I can order you to go home or to stay here, but I cannot give you an order without ordering you something in particular. And conversely, I cannot express anything in particular without intending it in a certain way, be it as an order, as a suggestion, as an insult, as a salute, as a compliment, as a consolation, as a piece of information, etc.

[ Sentences express complete intentions in order to communicate them--at least most of the time. One says something to someone . But as one cannot order without ordering something, so one cannot intend to communicate without intending to communicate something. However, the word "communicate" is here to be understood in a particular way--not simply in the sense of saying something to be heard and understood, but rather in the sense of intending to perform something by communicating it . Thus, in saluting I do not intend to inform you that I am saluting you, I intend to salute you and for you to become effectively saluted. In consoling you, the same. In threatening you, the same. In demanding you, in ordering you..., the same. Now, since speech acts expressed in complete sentences are performatives , their full success as lingustic acts does not consist in communicating but in actually achieving their intended performance. If I invite you for dinner, I fully succeed if you accept my invitation and actually come to dine with me, not if you only feel invited and decline my invitation, much less if you are just informed that I invited you and respond to me with an insult.

There are purely expressive speech acts and transitive speech acts. In both cases--IN MY OPINION--it is a matter of performance, of doing something to someone lingustically; hence--PACE SEARLE--no merely illocutionary effect , but always perlocutionary effect intended . But in the case of expressive speech acts the effect obtains by mere practical acceptance of the person spoken to and terminates there, whereas in the case of transitive speech acts the agency of the person spoken to is required to perform the ultimately intended effect. Thus, in saluting, which is expressive, the effect is obtained by the addressee's 'acceptance' of his or her being saluted, whereas in blackmailing someone the act is successful only if the addressee, say, pays the ransom.

(4) The second order discourse on language I am here conducting

It is a linguistic act, no doubt, at least parasitically, in the sense that I am thinking linguistically, for there is no other way of thinking. But it is not communicative but exclusively cognitive. Its point is to come to know, whether speculatively or practically matters little right now. It is like observing in order to understand what one is exposed to in a still ambiguous situation like "Is there somebody downstairs?."   Except that here it is second order discourse , not about dispelling the ambiguous situation, but about the dispelling itself of ambiguous situations, where the point is not to face a particular situtation successfully as an exposed agent, but to grasp the secret of dispelling ambiguous situations, as if situations were not particular and unrepeatable.

Aside from being primarily communicative, speech acts are acts, performances, practical and first order. The same is the case in observing to dispel a particular ambiguous situation . One does not observe oneself but a trait of the situation. And if one succeeds in the observation's purpose, the situation comes to be practically defined so that a particular response becomes appropriate rather than another or than no response (say, to call the police because someone is downstairs). But in the second order discourse I am considering the point is not to perform but to 'understand' . What is one trying to understand? And what does one achieve by understanding in this way? Is oneself or anybody better off by understanding in this second order way?

Let us say that I understand that language is performative rather than representative or spectacular . (It is like understanding-realizing that hammers are tools, not substances on which to hang theoretical predicates. The person who uses a hammer to pound has the right practical understanding . The person who understands that hammers are tools to be used, not substances to be understood, has the right practico-theoretical understanding . Not only has this person the general practical disposition to use all hammers as hammers rather than trying to understand them as substances, but can attempt to persuade of their error those who would try to understand hammers as substances rather than as tools, insofar as they are capable and in need of using hammers. So there is usefulness to practico-theoretical understanding .) [ Assuming that this is correct, I have been performing all my life as a speaker without knowing it. And my ignorance did not prevent me from performing efficiently as a competent speaker, nor will my new knowledge enhance my performance as a speaker. Yet, my new knowledge is not useless, for it prevents me from trying to understand or discuss language as representative--that is, it guides my second order pratice which is purely linguistic .

Practico-theoretical understanding--as all practical understanding--is self-referential to the subject-agent in a practical way toward the future . It involves not only perspective but situational perspective. It implies not reference to object, but reference to an inhabitable world of exposure contrasted with world actually inhabited by the agent, the world in which the agent actually lives by being geared or braced to it. This world, though open-ended and contingent.

(5) What about theoretical understanding?

"The picture which holds traditional philosophy captive is that of the mind as a great mirror, containing various representations--some accurate, some not--and capable of being studied by pure, nonempirical methods. Without the notion of the mind as mirror, the notion of knowledge as accuracy of representation would not have suggested itself. Without this latter notion, the strategy common to Descartes and Kant--getting more and more accurate representations by inspecting, repairing, and polishing the mirror, so to speak--would not have made sense" (Richard Rorty, Philosophy and the Mirror of Nature, Princeton, Princeton University Press, 1979, p. 12).

"... Nietzsche claims that it is essentially the same sun of Platonism and Christianity that illuminates Kant's philosophy, but 'shining through mist and skepticism'" (TI IV). (Kurt Mosser, "Nietzsche, Kant,, and the Thing in Itself ," International Studies in Philosophy, XXV/2, p. 76).

Unerstanding a map is being capable of using it as a map in the appropriate circumstances. A map is not a picture, not even a stylized, abstract one. [ Using a map is not seeing the map focally as something to be known, the way Descartes saw mental entities; it is. through the use of the map, fore-seeing the land mapped as a particular hodological space, as a space to be traversed ]. If one conceived that maps are pictures of a land and had never heard of their use and treated them as pictures, one would always misunderstand maps, never use them as maps, and would criticize them for not being good pictures, resemblant pictures, pictures that correspond to reality. Such criticism would not be bad criticism, criticism of a bad judge, of a poorly gifted judge; it would rather be totally wrongheaded criticism, judgment of a judgeless judge (a completely nonsensical expression!), for maps are not meant to resemble reality.  

Spectacles are tools to have something seen, not objects to be seen. Understanding them as objects to be seen is misunderstanding them. Knowing-treating them as objects to be seen is wrongheaded. Yet they are 'real', no less real than maps and hammers or any other tools. But their reality as tools consists in their efficiency as tools. Spectacles that cannot be used to see are not spectacles, do not exist as spectacles.

It seems that philosophers, starting with the Greeks, took theoretical knowledge as the princeps analogatum of knowledge. Furthermore, because they did so, they misunderstood the nature of knowledge. For them, wanting to know was wanting to see-object, an operation that might incidentally be useful but that as such terminated in seeing-understanding-object, that is, reality. Knowing was seeing, bringing to sight, making evident; acting was doing. Two very different things, though knowing was foundational of doing and doing was ultimately subordinated to knowing, to contemplation as the truly distinctive human stuff.

Knowing was the way of access to reality; real is what can be known (hence, for instance, Plato's world of Ideas, the world of numbers, etc., since the Ideas are known and the numbers are known). Now, knowledge is of reality, but is in the mind: adaequatio mentis to reality. On the other hand, knowing is not nothing, hence it is real. But knowing is seeing, hence knowledge of what is seen by the mind and is in the mind has to be similar to reality.

But what is the alternative? The above amounts to privileging and making foundational and paradigmatic of linguistic activity a parasitic linguistic act, the act of thinking. In what way is it parasitic?
