2003-April-2-La Rupture Epistemologique April 18, 2003

    LA RUPTURE EPISTEMOLOGIQUE

Rereading C. Dumont’s “La reflexion sur la methode theologique” ( Nouvelle Revue Theologique , 93e annee, December 1961, and 94e annee, janvier 1962), I think that I finally came to understand fully the epistemological rupture that took place progressively within the Church in the 12th and 13 th centuries. I had become aware of that rupture reading Chenu’s article “La theologie comme science au 13e siecle,” but without being able  to diagnose it precisely for what it was and what it has actually meant for the intellectual life of the Church . This is what I will attempt to do here.

Generally, the appropriation of Aristotle by the medieval Christian intellectuals has been seen as an enormous progress for the Church, a sort of passage from obscurantism to enlightenment, consecrated very particularly by what has come to be known as the Aquinas’s synthesis of faith and reason. Such synthesis supposedly made possible for intellectuals to become Christians without ceasing to be genuine intellectuals and for Christians to be genuine intellectuals without having to give up their faith. For my part, I have come to think that the adoption of Aristotelianism as the framework for our understanding of the Christian faith and of ourselves as Christians has resulted in a big mess in which we are still entangled.

As is well known, the introduction of Aristotelianism in the medieval universities, both in the philosophical and the theological curricula, did not go without problems. A number of people, particularly among the teachers of theology, adamantly resisted the innovation it brought about in the early years. For its part, Rome saw the adoption of Aristotelianism by its teachers with much suspicion too, at least initially. There were repeated Roman interventions against what seemed an increasing naturalism and rationalism incompatible with the Christian faith which depended on divine revelation. Still as late as 1277 took place one of the most famous of those interventions (in which Aquinas himself was condemned by name). To no avail. The Geistzeit clearly was in favor of the ‘enlightened’ Aristotelianists against the ‘obscurantism’ of the anti-artistotelianists. By the 14 th  century the Aristotelians were practically in possession of the field in all the universities across Europe. This situation prevailed in the European universities until the 19 th century when, as a result of the secularism brought about by the Enlightenment and by the progress of the natural sciences and their method, the universities ceased to be confessional. In Spain, in the USA and in the rest of the Western world, where confessional universities subsisted for a longer time –in some cases till today– the allegiance to Aristotelianism as such and to the Scholastic method, faded rather suddenly toward the middle of the 20 th century.

Today, the adoption of Aristotelianism in the 12 th and 13 th centuries is generally seen by the Catholic intellectuals as a significant progress in the life of the Church, as a passage from obscurantism or at least intellectual primitivism toward rational maturity and openness to scientific truth. As an example,  both Chenu and Dumont, the already mentioned Catholic theologians who have studied in most detail the controversy on the adoption of the Aristotelian notion of science in theology, are of the same mind in considering it as a great progress.

Why? Without expressing themselves in exactly these terms, both of them, in agreement with Aquinas at least on this point, seem to see a profound compatibility and even a sort of  connaturality between the Biblical Christian faith and the intellectual structure of Aristotelianism. Thus they see in the adoption of Aristotelianism as the epistemological framework of the doctrines of the Church a powerful instrument of intellectual self-awareness and development toward full maturity on the Church’s part. One feels that they, as Aquinas himself and so many others, almost  feel tempted to say that the structure and philosophical validity of the essential lines of Aristotle’s system, rediscovered and adopted by the Catholic intellectuals between the 12 th and the 13 th  centuries, constituted a providential event for the intellectual growth of the Church.   

Of course, the assumption on which this view rests is that the Christian faith and the adopted Aristotelianism are indeed compatible and almost connatural, as all truth no matter what its origin is supposed to be. What  for my part I believe – and such will be my main thesis here–  is that the Aristotelianism adopted by the medieval Catholic thinkers, far from being compatible,  much less connatural with the Christian faith, was radically impermeable to it. In my opinion, its adoption involved a radical epistemological rupture with the Biblical faith of the Church. Moreover, insofar as the rupture went undetected, it brought about, not a change in the Christian faith but a non-diagnosed state of permanent schizophrenia, a hidden conflict between the actual  Biblical Christian life of the Church and the means for self-interpretation forced by the adopted Aristotelianism.  

Probably the most significant manifestation of the epistemological rupture was the shift in the theological practice from narrative theology, constituted by biblical commentary, to conceptual or doctrinal theology, constituted by theoretical treatises. As nobody has studied more in depth this shift than Hans Frei, I will take his main work as my central point of reference in an attempt to complete and, in certain points, refine his analysis.
