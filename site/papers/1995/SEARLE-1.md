Searle

Searle's theory of speech acts distinguishes illocutionary from perlocutionary effects, of which only the former are genuinely linguistic or 'essential' in the sense of obtaining whenever language is properly used and in the sense of being distinctively characteristic of language. *

* An instructive example cited by Searle: "Kenst du das Land  wo die Zitronen blühen?" The American speaker's intention is to be treated by the German addressees as a German by suggesting that he is a German soldier. ( This shows that we speak and hear applicatively, for the American soldier does not want his addressees to concentrate on what he says--the meaning that the words have in German--but through them on what he means, namely, I am a German soldier ). According to Searle, being treated as a German is a perlocutionary effect. Even if it obtains, though, it is not a linguistic effect, according to Searle, for the speaker does not say that he is a German soldier, and what characterizes linguistic effects is that they obtain thanks to their being said. (And, remember, whatever can be meant can be said). But, furthermore, it seems to me that, according to Searle, the perlocutionary effects are not essentially linguistic effects. Indeed, in the first place, there are speech acts, like "Hello," which according to him involve no perlocutionary effects, for "Hello" only says the knowledge that the speaker intends them to convey, namely, the speaker's intention to greet the addressee, and nothing else. Now, if perlocutionary effects were essential for language to obtain, then greeting, which for Searle is a fully successful speech act when the speaker's intentiona of greeting is known by the addressee by understanding "Hello," would not be a full speech act. In the second place, a speech act does not need its said perlocutionary effect to obtain in order to be a full-fledged, successful speech act; all that needs to obtain is the killocuntionary effect, that is, the knowledge of the speaker's intention. In other words, the achievement of the perlocutionary effect is only accidentally linguistic].

According to Searle, then, the illocutionary effect obtains when the addressee (or hearer?) recognizes (understands, comes to know, becomes informed of) the intention that the speaker intends to communicate by saying what is said: my commanding something, my affirming something, my asking for something, my insulting somebody. In this view, then, language is essentially informative (of intentions); we speak to communicate--that is, to make known--our intentions, not to have our intentions realized (or, in the case of statements, to actually obtain the addressee's assent, which on pp. 47 Searle implicitly identifies as the perlocutionary effect of stating). When the illocutionary effect obtains, the addressee gains knowledge of the speaker's intention, which is what the speaker intended as a speaker and all he/she can intend as a speaker , (though as an agent who speaks he can parasitically intend a perlocutionary effect). The actual communication of this knowledge is what makes successful a spech act as a speech (that is, as a communicative, as a linguistic) act, different from non speech acts.

The perlocutionary effect , by contrast, does not consist in knowledge of the intentions of the speaker but in something else in the addressee than being informed of the speaker's intention (such as assenting, believing, being edified, etc.) or in a behavior on the addressee's part corresponding to the behavioral aspect of the intention made known by the speaker.

Searle amd myself

Searle's view, unlike my own, is that language is theoretically rather than practically intelligible.

I reject Searle's distinction between illocutionary and perlocutionary effects for two reasons. (1) It reduces all speech acts to theoretical communications of intentions to be understood, which they are not, hence perpetuating the myths of language as pictorial and of the primacy of statements as true pictures of the world to be contemplated. (2) It separates practice (conduct) from language, thus preserving the distinction between knowledge and action (leaving no room for what Aquinas calls actus humanus ), as well as the subordination of action to knowledge.

The distinction between illocutionary and perlocutionary effects not only ignores that the failure to produce its intended effects, which are not merely communicative, represents a misfunction of language, not the paradigmatic case, but it also consecrates the misfunction as the rule--language is primarily to be understood--, so that that failure to produce its actually intended (perlocutionary) effects appears linguistically irrelevant.

I am talking of the language of and among exposed, vulnerable agents, which is the basic form of language, on which all other forms (written, reflective, etc.) are dependent. In my view, there is no genuine linguistic communication unless the intended linguistic effect is produced, and the linguistic effect is not primarily mere understanindg. I deny that what Searle's understands by language, namely, language overheard by a theorist of language, is living language. In fact it is totally dead, for it is not made of speech acts. It is like WRITTEN Gospels in which nobody believes, which cannot be reactivated in their original function, and which therefore are READ as simply to be understood, which automatically converts them into fairy tales as far as agents are concerned . Much as we read the stories of the Greek gods, which were alive and meaningful at a certain point but are nowadays sedimented geological layers.

Once this objectified, dead language, ossified out there, is taken as the norm--and this is the view that deconstructionists who deal with "texts" share with the Greeks (at least with the Platonic, theoretical Greeks, perhaps also with the Aris who assimilates practical to theoretical knowledge or at least is equivocal in this regard)--the notion of statements as true or false pictures (rather than as referring practically to an object for response), of statements as unique repositories of truth, of truth as correspondence, of the separation between knowledge and action, of the primacy of the former over the latter follow. And since truth of this kind is impossible as Kant rightly argued, deconstructionism triumphs and leaves us in the lurch for lack of a viable alternative.]  

What I propose as an alternative is a practical view of language , for our language is the language of vulnerable agents-patients, not of contemplators. I do not want to deny that language is to be understood, but it is to be understood practically, applicatively , where the action that obtains is itself linguistic, in response to the speaker, not merely behavioral, and where, if the practical understanding that is identical with the action meant does not obtain, the speech act cannot be deemed successful as such, for both the speaker and the addressee are agents and communicate as agents.

Of course, this does not imply that the linguistic effect is to be reduced to action-behavior. Saluting is a linguistic action, and being saluted is a linguistic effect, too, for words of saluting, loving, etc. are gestures, incarnated intentions. As kissing is not a mere behavior but an actus of love, a gesture of love, so being kissed is an actus of receiving the kiss, not merely of knowing that a kissing behavior took place or even that an actus of kissing took place. Similarly, Being saluted is not understanding that one is being saluted + pleasure; rather, the pleasure is the unique pleasure of being saluted by this person and is as constitutive of being saluted as the realization that one is saluted . In other words, the recognition involves pleasure and the pleasure involves recogntion, in such a way that the recognition without the pleasure is not what being saluted is, and the pleasure without the recognition is not what being saluted is. As one cannot have successful salutation, that is, actual being saluted without realizing that one is saluted, so one cannot have actual being saluted without the at least customary pleasure of being saluted. Both would be missed if one were not saluted or were positively denied salutation. And this is the effect that the speaker intends to produce, which is neither a piece of knowledge, nor a pleasure, nor a combination of both, but the unique practical relationship between saluter and saluted by which the saluter gives the saluted the pleasure of being recognized as a fellow human being. This is what we call 'salutation' .

By contrast, Searle's understanding is purely contemplative, literal understanding of what is said, because what is said is the speaker's intention .  In his view of language, as the function of all speech acts is reduced to that of making intentions known, so addressee and witness are reduced to contemplative over-hearers who have only to understand the speaker's intention as a overhearers do, and who are expected to do nothing as linguistic agents.

Let me now clarify and present more in depth my "practical view of language" by means of examples . If I tell you "Your house is burning," I am referring you to object for response (because we both are agents-patients and automatically understand as such under natural conditions), not telegraphing you linguistically a picture to be reproduced in your mind . That such is the case is manifested by my implicit expectations which, if frustrated, will become explicit. (Nor are you totally unaware of my expectations, for you would be the first to be surprised if I were satsified if you told me that you got the picture). Now, this exactly is te case according to Searle: what I am doing in making the statement to you is to telegraph you linguistically the picture of your house burning, and if in your mind you reproduce correctly the picture I telegraphed you, the stating speech act as such has succeeded, which amounts to saying that what you do as a result is only accidentally linguistic , extrinsically linguistic .

Now, that this is not the way in which competent users of language use language seems clear. As a speaker (and even as a witness of the linguistic transacftion) I would be baffled if, in response to my statement that your house is burning, you told me placidly that you got the picture (of your house burning), that is, that you understood what I said , and then you continued to do what you were doing, or proceeded to invite me to the movies. (In fact, such a behavior would not be, nor would it be seen as, a response to what I said , at all, for it is (intentionally, gesturally) unrelated to it. I am sure that, as a competent user of language, Searle would be baffled too. Yet this is what his illocutionary meaning and illocutionary effect amount to in the case of statements! *

* At most, in the case of statements, he might say that the illocutionary effect obtains if the addressee "recognizes" that the speaker wants him to believe (after all, there is a truth-claim in statements) that his/her house is burning, and that the perlocutionary effect obtains if the addressee actually believes that his house is burning, though the addressee does not have to believe (or assent to) the statement for the speech act to be succesful. (Notice, however, that Searle explicitly says that there are cases in which the stater does not particularly care to be believed).  

Remember that we are talking of spoken, not written, language; of language among actually exposed agents talking to each other, and talking to each other not about what WAS said or what WAS the case in the past. Now, in my view of language, the meaning of a speaker can be cashed, as such, only in actu exercitu , and only by the addressee(s), and is cashed through practical understanding, not through contemplative understanding. Practical understanding has two aspects: (1) The reference in every case, even in the case of statements, is to object for response, not to picture; (2) Because the understanding is practical, because the reference is practical, the understanding is applicative, incarnated in practical disposition to respond and, in the ideal case, in actual response to object for response. Thus practical understanding is understanding that by its very nature terminates in actus humanus , unless overriden by other factors. And the paradigmatic linguistic case is the successful case, not the case in which overriding factors prevent success. Normally, then, that is, as the normative case, a speech act succeeds when the practical effect intended by the speaker and actually communicated through practical understanding actually obtains. Thus, a command succeeds when what is commanded to the addressee is done by the addressee, not--as Searle claims--when the addresse understands the command, much less if--as is the case in Searle's view--the addressee understands as a contemplator, without 'feeling' any more commanded--that is, without being any more disposed to obey--than an overhearer or a hearer in a lesson of language . In short, in the ideal, fully successful, paradigmatic case, being commanded = obeying, which is not a mere behavior but a linguistically informed conduct in response to a command . It is a conduct which establishes (begins or reaffirms) a relationship between commander and commanded, which may be master-slave, Creator-creature, parent-child, etc. This conduct incarnates and consecrates a practically (not merely theoretically assented to) recognized superiority-inferiority between addresser and addressee in at least one respect which may simply bre power.

This, I repeat, is the normal case, the normative one for grasping the function of language. All other cases are parasitic on this one . For there are, as I have said, other cases, when other overriding factors intervene that prevent the normal termination, the successful achievement, of the speech act. Thus, a commandment is not always obeyed. Yet the addressee understood the commandment, that is, the commanding intention of the speaker, that is, the speaker's intention to be obeyed, not (as Searle imagines) the speaker's intention to inform of his command. For instance, when a father commands his child to plunge from the tremplin, the scared child may refuse out of fear . The child understands the command, and understands that he is commanded to plunge, not that he is informed of his/her father's commanding intention, but fear prevents the child from obeying, that is, from understanding practically, for practical understanding amounts to practical recognition, to conduct in response informed by understanding. Or, to give another example different for the previous one of akrasia , the child, commanded by another child to plunge, refuses to obey because the other child has no authority to command him or her. In both of these cases there is understanding, and understanding of a kind that is possible only to competent speakers of language, but not the understanding that is normative for language. Thus, I do not deny that these forms of 'understanding' exist. What I affirm is that in order to get the correct idea of language we have to consider as normative the way speaker and addresse actually use language and the successful case of practical understanding that terminates in the addressee's action or passion. Hence the understanding involved in the just mentioned cases, not to say anything of the understanding that consists in mere recognition of the speaker's intention, is parasitic, aberrant, not normal, not the correct basis for grasping the way in which language functions. Much less should we take as the basis for our view of language written language, that is, ossified, objectified language without concrete circumstances and without concrete speakers and addressees. * Even less should we take as our basis for our view of language the apparently theoretical language of 'objective,' explanatory science, as suggested by Plato.

* This is what the deconstructionists call "text." Written language has to be reactivated, and in many cases it cannot be fully reactivated because the relationship to be established between speaker and addressee is not possible between author and reader, given that the reader does not stand to the author as an addressee nor was meant by the speaker to be the addressee. Since, in this case, there is no speaker and no addressee, there really is no witness but just a Serlean overhearer.

What Searle does, unwittingly following the Greek tradition, is to take this parasitical understanding of language as the rule . From this it follows the foundational character of statements, the understanding of statements as pictures, the understanding of truth as correspondence between picture and reality, and from the impossibility of knowing so conceived the emergence of skepticism or at least theoretical relativism. In addition, emotions become blind, unintelligible feelings, knowing and acting are divorced, intentional action loses its intelligibility, and the passage from is to ought becomes an unsolvable problem once the separation betwen knowing and acting and the subordination of the latter to the former becomes accepted. This comedy of errors is the field in which deconstructionism thrives, while carrying within itself the seeds of its own deconstruction . This is why I reject deconstructionism.

However, if I admit that there is an understanding of language that consists in understanding the speaker's intention, though I consider this understanding parasitic and secondary, am I not already conceding what will become the basis of the comedy of errors? I do not think so. In the first place, because, unlike Searle, I do not believe that linguistic meaning amounts to a mere piece of information about the speaker's intentions nor, consequently, that such information about his/her intentions is what the speaker means to convey at all and whose conveyance makes the speech act successful. Indeed, this is why I reject Searle's notion of illocutionary meaning and effect, as well as the identification he makes of the illocutionary with the essential effect by which language is defined, thereby making this meaning-effect into the norm . But if this understanding is not "knowledge," or "information," as Searle claims, what is it then?

Let us for a moment forget about the addressee to consider the speaker's case who says "Your house is burning." Before telling me this, he knows that my house is burning. But he as vulnerable agent knows that as object for response, which is why he rushes to tell me that and why his tone is not objectively-informative but emotive. So the agent's knowledge is itself applicative, practical, not a matter of pictures . *

( * This, I think, is what Aristotle has in mind when he speaks of practical knowledege, though he does not quite realize that he is equivocating by calling it knowledge, as if it were a species of what he calls knowledge, which is speculative knowledge corresponding to the disinterested desire to know ).   Language is primarily language among people, each of whom is an agent-patient who is so situated in relation to the surrounding world and who therefore has his/her, personal, situated, practical, tacit understanding of it at any given moment. (This tacit element of linguistic communication, namely the nature of speaker, addressee and witness, becomes explicit when the agent's expectations are frustrated). By personal I mean practico-emotional-dispositional.

To be concrete, how did the speaker get the knowledge that my house is burning, in the first place? He got it linguistically, as an agent-patient (needful, desirous, preferring) member of an English speaking culture who was introduced practically--immdediately and through practical statements and stories and commands and interdictions, etc.--to objects for response, such as houses, fire, etc. This is the speaker-agent who addresses other hearer-agents in the presence of other witnesses-agents, all of whom have themselves their own past and present linguistic experience of the world. This accumulated, sedimented past experience of active exchanges with the surrounding world as a vulnerable agent-patient shaped by a culture, combined with the present experience of the world at any moment, constitute the concrete, tacit dimension of the agent-interpeter who speaks, who is addressed, who functions as a witness, and who therefore, depending on his/her past and present experience, is referred practically, or who fails to be referred, by my statement that "your house is burning."

The question of truth

If not practically referred by my statement, one of my addresseee's possible reactions is to contest the truth of my statement. Let us pause here, for this precisely is the case from which the view of language and of knowledge which I am critizing and dismissing seems to originate . Indeed, this opens the stage for reflective language, for language about language, for language about what WAS SAID. This is the language that most philosophers (Searle included) apparently consider primary and foundational. Here originates the view of knowledge as resemblant picture , and of language as communicating pictures, all of them to be understood, but only some of them being true or false and meant as true.

What happens here is the objectification of language. Language has ceased to function as language and, therefore, to function as such, and has become an object, an object to be known, rather than a practico-referential instrument . A comparison might help. When I wear my glasses they function as glasses, but I do not see my glasses, I see through them. When I take my glasses off, I may see them. But if my understandig of glasses is based on the glasses I see rather than on the glasses I wear, my understanding denatures glasses completely, for as glasses they are not to be seen but to see through. (Notice, however, that the analogy is not perfect insofar as it suggests that the world is to be seen rather than responded to or, when one has the initiative, changed by the agent). Now, assume I am so close to blindness that I cannot see at all without my glasses, so that if I take my glasses off I cannot see my glasses. Such is communicative language, which in use should be totally transparent, purely referential. Now, as long as we do not objectify language to theorize about it, to know it, that is, as long as we do not isolate a piece of language to examine it, we use language and it functions as language. For language, like glasses, is language only when it functions as such, and only functions as such in actu exercitu . Converting it into object seen denatures it completely.

The Greeks taught us to objectify language and built a theory of knowledge and of language based on that objectification . (This is the grudge expressed by Hkeidegger in What Is Metaphysics ). They consequently bequeathed us all the puzzles I have mentioned, which for reasons that do not belong here have particularly affected religion, that is, the cultural, linguistic practice called religion .

Statements among agents do not refer contemplatively to what is said but applicatively to object of exposure, much as spectacles do not present photos to be contemplated and, insofar as affirmed, to be assented to, but objects of exposure to be responded to by the hearer-agent. However, statements refer to objects for response by stating something about the objects.

As in the case of begging the point is not to understand but to be efficiently begged and, therefore, to grant what is begged, so in the case of stating the point is not to understand what is said, but to be referred to the object as object for response.

Yet in the case of statements, unlike in other cases, the question of their truth arises, and one cannot be referred to object for response unless one agrees with the stated. [Rather, one is referred not if one agrees with the statement but if one does not disagree with it, because what is said is not heard as such, objectively, as something to be assented to, but one is referred through it and referred practically. Agreement and disagreement do not emerge in direct (by contrast to reflective) language, that is, in the language among vulnerable, desirous, exposed agents. Rather, agreement and disagreement result from the failure of being referred and inaugurate reflective discourse or language]. This is commonly thought. But is it correct? For it seems that there are two kinds of assent: practical and theoretical. [Rather, practical assent is not assent but non-dissent. Assent and dissent belong in the sphere of reflective language, of statements objectified as pictures of reality. The agent who is not referred practically by "Your house is burning" does not dissent as agent with the statement; he/she is simply not referred to the object for response, for he/she is not referred practically. Only when the agent ceases to act and considers the piece of language as a picture does the agent dissent or disagree in the sense of these words that is pertinent here]. Practical is identical with being referred practically, with bracing oneself for encounter with object as stated, with actual response to object for response. Whoever does this agrees applicatively, practically, not by seeing that what is said is the case, for one does not hear objectively what is said, but by bracing himself or herself toward the object for response and actually responding to it, which is what being referred--that is, being sucessfully referred, for otherwise there is no reference--amounts to in the case of statements.

This was the case with those who feared the Martians and began conceiving of projects to escape or committed suicide . Theoretical dissent and the search for theoretical assent, on the other hand, stop the practical flow--the practical reference--to the object as object for response, focus on what the statement says rather than on what it applicatively means. By the same token the statement ceases to function as a statement, to refer to object for response, and appears to  refer to what the object is not, namely, object for contemplation .

We may now return to the original point: Truth is dialectically subordinated to practice .

Like faith, the agent's knowledge-situated-perception is prospective, applicative, for action's sake , because the agent inserted in the midst of the world is desirous and vulnerable, actively and passively desirous, transformative of the world and adaptive to the world. Action is not true or false, it is appropriate or inappropriate. And the apropriateness or inappropriateness is revealed only post factum , in terms of the iintended success. Initially, without past experience to guide us, the action is totally blind and pure risk. Furthermore, insofar as past experience never places us in full control of the future, action remains risky. However, the agent is also reflective, that is, retrospective for the sake of being successfully prospective. Not only does the agent tend automatically to repeat the actions that succeeded, but, in the human agent's case, objectifies the objects responded to and the actions in response, and explains--that is, makes intelligible, the past success, the proportion between the action and the object responded to.

Now, this is a different sense of "reflection" from the one alluded to above . The latter was reflection on language, objectification of "what WAS said (or judged) ," in order to find out whether it corresponds to the reality referred to, that is, pictured. The reflection we are talking about now is objectification of object to consider it not in relation to the interests of the agent but in itself, in order to reach an explanatory understanding of it. From what it is descriptively (abstraction of its constant, descriptive features) to what it is intelligibly, that is, of what explains its constant descriptive features.

This is the knowledge Aristotle called speculative, the knowledge that, according to him at the beginning of the Metaphysics , people by nature desire to acquire independently of all practical interest. Practical knowledge they share, at least to a degree, with beasts; but speculative, contemplative knowledge which they seek for its own sake is unique to them. * ( * Notice, however, that Aris has a different notion of knowledge and of practical knowledge in De Motu Animalium , where there seems to be no room for speculative knowledge, for pure objective contemplation of the true).

This speculative knowledge per causas , particularly per causam formalem , in its turn works as the evidence (the proof) that makes certain, necessary, the judgment that originally was only a factual, accidentally true, perception, and an accidentally true memory of constancy. In this case, however, perception has ceased to be the practical, applicative perception of a living agent-patient (what it always remains in animals, what is reflected in the De Motu Animalium ) and has become theoretical vision leading to intellectual contemplative fruition of the truth. In fact, however, this vision of the truth of the judgment refers to the object as being so and, because we are agents patients, refers to the object applicatively, instrumentally for our own purposes, when we have the initiative, expectationally when we don't. Thus even the truths of geometry are to be applied to problems if the understanding is genuine understanding of object and not of mere idea.

The knowledge per causam formalem , that is, per internam intelligibilitatem obviously is inspired by geometry. Aside, however, from the fact that this intelligibility is practical and that it is not a priori , it is intelligibility in reference to action, that is, of effects to be expected under specifiable conditions. Now, these effects as well as the conditions under which they are to be expected can only be figured through observation and hypothesis to be verified through experiment, not through mere thinking. But this way of understanding Aris did not take into account.
