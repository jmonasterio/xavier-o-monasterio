December 8, 1991

Dear Father Jim:

I just finished reading very thoroughly the book by Nicholas Lash, Easter in the Ordinary . Thanks for recommending it to me. I found it fascinating and enriching in many ways, though very insatisfactory in others. Sharing with you  my reactions will help me to formulate them clearly. In addition, this might tempt you to share yours with me sometime in a conversation.

Let me reconstruct Lash's position as I see it.

To start with, according to Lash, William James had the great merit of reacting against intellectualism. What is that? Here is one of his best characterizations:

"And because the intellectualism that James deplored has done at least as much damage in theology as in philosophy, we can wholeheartedly welcome his insistence that reality is richer than reflection; that it is not by pure reason alone that we can take our bearings and find our way (quite apart from the fact that reason is never as pure, as devoid of passion and particular interests, as its advocates suppose it to be); that quality of feeling is no less important to our well-being than quality of argument; that imperialitst pretensions on the part of particulaqr ideals of explanation are always to be resisted; and that the life of religion (as, indeed, of much else) is ever under threat from that passion for law and order, for tidiness and clarity, which is a feature of the internal dynamics both of institutional power and of reflective or theoretical curiosity" (pp. 86-87).

Another great merit of James's, in Lash's view, is his attempt to overcome Cartesian dualism:

"... Richard Bernstein remarked that 'James sees how much of modern philosophy has been trapped within the limiting and distorting confines of Cartesian dualism... This entire frameworkd needs to be challenged and overthrown.' Indeed it does... " (p. 35).

Unfortunately, James took the wrong path, for his alternative to intellectualism and Cartesian dualism is grounded on a "contracted notion of experience" which is unacceptable.

What are the shortcomings of James's contracted notion of experience? In this regard Lash is highly unsystematic: his criticisms of James pop up from  different points of view and in different directions. What one senses clearly, however, is that Lash condemns James, among other things , for taking religious experience as a totally private experience of a special kind and having a special object (the transcendent), an experience reserved to religious geniuses (of whom the ordinary practitioners of institutionalized religion are blind, merely habitual followers without personal religious experience), and resulting in a reduction of all religions to the same common core.

Lash uses James as his foil to make his position and his own project understandable. Like James, he wants to avoid the pitfalls of intellectualism and Cartesian dualism, but by means of a notion of experience that makes more sense than James's. Toward this he moves by combining insights gained from Schleiermacher, Newman, von Hügel, Buber, and Rahner (and to a lesser extent from Kant and Hegel). To oversimplify, the main points of Lash's alternative to James (which are often very obscure and presented in a very unsystematic way) are the following.

(1) A very important point for Lash is that there is no experience of God, properly speaking. All experience of God would reduce God to an "it" (rather than a Thou), to an object. Only that can be experienced which is part of the world; God is not, and if He were He would be no God.

(2) Obviously, however, Lash, who is a Christian, does not want to deny all contact between us and God. Though "contact" is not a word he uses, I may use it to distinguish it from the I-it relationship which he calls "experience" when he denies the possibility of experiencing God. We enter in contact with God through God's initiative. The paradigm for this contact is inter-personal relationship, the encounter between Thou and Thou.

(3) The name that the encounter or contact between people and God takes is "Prayer." "Prayer," however, as Lash understands this word, is not a private dialogue between an isolated individual and God, it is the deed of a community and is possible only within a historical community and only in the measure in that the community is a genuine community whose members treat each other and all other people as Thous.

(4) Since there is no "experience" of God, it would seem that theology--that is, talk about God, talk which is not itself contact with God and cannot avoid treating God as an "object", an "it"--would be impossible, a form of idolatry. Lash realizes his predicament, yet refuses to give up talking about God, for he considers such talk necessary. Here I find him very obscure and unpersuasive. At any rate, once he has decided (rightly or wrongly that talking about God is necessary, his problem is the following: "That God is other than the world, that he is distinct from whatever else there is, and that the world adds nothing to his holiness or perfection, is a common place of Jewish, Islamic, and Christian belief. But since it is the world, the universe, the sum total of things, 'which forms the context for all we know and do,' it follows that God 'must be deemed unknowable' or, as we said earlier, incomprehensible. As a result, 'the quintessential theological task' becomes that of so formulating the difference between God and the world 'as to assure the required transcendence, while allowing us to have some notion of what it is we are referring to in addressing 'the Holy One,' 'our Father,' or 'Allah Akbar'" [the quotations are from David Burrell, Knowing the Unknowable God ] (p. 226).

The execution of the 'quintessential theological task' "will vary from time to time, and from culture to culture... Aquinas's handling of the distinction between esse and 'essence'... enabled him to indicate how we might speak of God while yet insisting that, concerning God, we can only say what He is not. And if this claim is clearly consistent with Rahner's contention that Aquinas's doctrine of God's incomprehensibility 'opens up all the heights and depths of Thomist theology and philosophy,' it may surprise those who remember that Aquinas did, after all, continue to speak of God as cause" (p. 227). In any case, says Lash, "Aquinas's distinction... cannot be of direct assistance to us... because the context in which we seek to display the difference between God and the world is not that of a culture whose imagination is shaped by Neoplatonic images of emanation. What form, then, might executing 'the quintessential theological task' take in our place and time? We could, I suggest, do worse than to return to Buber's image of homelessness" (p. 228).

Whether the quintessential theological task is to be executed in terms of the image of emanation and return or of homelessness, however, in the final analysis the theological language about God boils down to protocolary, intra-communitary rules for speaking of God in such a way that it respects the difference between God and the world. In other words, if I understand Lash correctly, the theological talk about God is not really about God (who is unknowable or incomprehensible), but it regulates negatively our contact with God bypreventing all forms of 'idolatry'.

Not only is the above summary of Lash's book and views very incomplete and highly oversimplificatory; it may be even wrong. But the reason for this is not, I believe, my incompetence but, rather, the shakiness and unclearness of Lash's own position and exposition. He is good and reasonably clear in his rejection of James; he is far less good and clear in defining his own alternative. And, insofar as his alternative can be understood, or I understand it, I find it seriously unsatisfactory, sympathetic as I am to Lash's rejection of intellectualism, of Cartesian dualism, and of James's empiricism.

I will comment now in particular on a few points where I find Lash deficient and his position either unclear or unacceptable.

Concerning his criticism of James

For all his good points about James, particularly of James's contracted notion of experience, I think Lash fails to put his finger on the really critical spots. In other words, what he attacks is the symptoms, but he does not quite diagnose the illness.

The illness is that James remains a Cartesian . Descartes himself was an empiricist of sorts. The evidence on the basis of which he will affirm of deny is a clear and distinct idea supposedly furnished by experience. Descartes takes stock of all his experiences and, initially, finds only one that is irrecusable: the experience of his own existence. On this firm basis he can build.

In Bernstein's words, "He [James] advocated a doctrine of 'pure experience' which is the basis of his radical empiricism. According to this doctrine, there is no epistemological duality between the reality known and the knowing consciousness" (p. 35).

I am in fundamental agreement with Lash's rejection of "intellectualism," which is the point on which he is sympathethic to William James. As you may well suspect, however, I think that Lash is wrong in placing the birth of intellectualism in the eighteenth century (an affirmation for which, by the way, he offers no evidence). The contracted notion of "experience" which he rightly criticizes in James and which he rightly sees as a prevalent cultural assumption among contemporary intellectuals has its origin in Descartes.
