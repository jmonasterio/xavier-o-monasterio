2005-September 12- Polanyi and practical knowledge

POLANYI AND PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE

We attribute knowledge to those animals whose activity, because it is adapted to new circumstances, shows that they ‘voluntarily’ or ‘intentionally,’ rather than mechanically, pursue their ends through their actions. So, judging by this case, we implicitly acknowledge that desire or need is an intrinsic constitutive of what we call knowledge. But there is also  such a thing as mechanical teleological action to satisfy a need. Such action is inherited – an automatic, biologically programmed reaction to a sensed or perceived feature of the environment that functions as a sign for the reaction. And that is the beginning of what in both animals and in  us, humans, will become knowledge.

The feature of the environment that provokes the reaction is ”Sensed” tacitly rather than “perceived” objectively because, conjoined with need or desire on the subject’s part, it functions as a sign FOR the reaction, not as an object. Since the sign is tacit, as all signs are, because  they are signs FOR action, not signs OF anything, the subject’s awareness cannot be ex pressed or enunciated in words, since it literally has no object or “content”. In short, the subject’s awareness is not meant to terminate in linguistic enunciation but in action.

This is what the Platonic-Aristotelian epistemic tradition couldn’t grasp. Inspired by a misconceived model of  vision, its adherents approached epistemology with the preconception of  the knowing mind (the subject’s awareness) as a mirror of objects, and of knowledge as a correct or faithful representation or reproduction of the object mirrored. Consequently,  they were bound to subordinate the appropriate action in response to the object to the correct representation or ’knowledge’ of the object. This subordination of action to knowledge is reflected in Searle’s conviction that I cannot lend you my pen if I do not know that you ask me to lend it to you –hence action depends on knowledge. The same subordination is manifested in Aquinas’s matter-of-factly assertion that in order to pursue an end one first needs to know it.

The main problem with the subordination of action to knowledge was pointed out by Hume regarding the impossibility of the passage from an objectively known state of affairs to an action, for no objectively known fact can motivate an action. As to the validity of the conception of knowledge as the correct representation of reality, Kant raised the never solved objection that, if such is indeed the case, then it is impossible to know whether or not we know, for there is no independent means of knowing whether or not the correspondence between the representation and reality  obtains in any particular case.

Of course these two unsolved problems have not prevented the scientific undertaking to go on. What is particularly curious is that this has occurred with the blessing of the two philosophers who raised the just mentioned unanswered objections against the notion of knowledge as correspondence. Indeed, however illogically, Hume explicitly excluded from his skeptical conclusion the “abstract sciences”  that deal with mathematical relationships concerning number and quantity as well as the sciences that affirm facts on the basis of experiment, though these experimental sciences involve  a blind act of faith in the resemblance of the future to the past. ^(F1) No less illogically, Kant continued to affirm the validity of the synthetic a posteriori judgments of the experimental sciences when supported by “intuition,” in spite of the impossibility of knowing whether they actually corresponded to the  thing in  itself.  Typical, in our days, of the same illogical exclusion by philosophers of the conclusions of the experimental sciences from their very objections against the epistemology they presuppose is Rorty’s sleight of hand through which he continues to give his allegiance to the prevailing view in the physical sciences, in spite of the fact that its  truth presupposes the very correspondence he systematically attacks.

By contrast to other philosophers, Hume has the distinction of having suggested, at least indirectly, a way out of the epistemological quandary in which the Platonic-Aristotelian notion of knowledge as correspondence places us. Indeed, he distinguishes between  theoretical skepticism and human or practical skepticism. As a thinker, one can be a skeptic, but not as an agent. As Hume sees it, no philosopher, no matter how skeptic he may be, ever distrusts his perceptions to

the point of  attempting to get out of a second-floor room through the window. Be a philosopher, concludes Hume, but above all be a human being! In other words, reason in conformity with  agency instead of trying to conform or subordinate agency to a preconceived idea of knowledge.

Hume’s alternative, though far from fully developed, maintains the primacy of action over knowing. Knowing is subordinated to action, not the other way around –as it has consistently been assumed by the Platonic-Aristotelian tradition. This is the alternative I want to explore.

Kuhn’s alternative notion of science

Kuhn’s proposes his interpretation of science by contrast to the image of science propagated by the scientific textbooks. The textbook image of science rejected by Kuhn is underpinned by two assumptions.

The first assumption concerns the nature of knowledge , to wit: (1) That knowledge consists in the correspondence between what is in the mind of the scientist and what is the case in reality.

The second assumption concerns the relationship between language and knowledge , to wit: (2) That the knowledge possessed by the knowing subject

can be faithfully translated into statements that express the knowledge.

Therefore, a double correspondence is involved: the correspondence of the mind to the object, in which knowledge consists, and the correspondence or equivalence between knowledge as it is in the mind and as it is linguistically expressed in statements. As to the kind of correspondence involved, it is assumed to be by similarity. First, the object as is it known in the mind is a faithful image of the object as it is in reality, out of the mind. Second, the statement of what is known  is a faithful image of the object known as it is in the mind.

As for the connection of knowledge with action, it is just tacitly assumed that action is subordinated to knowledge. The knowing subject deals with the object according to the knowledge he possesses of the object. Action follows knowledge.

What has all this to do with Kuhn? He rejects what he calls “the textbook image of science.” Now the textbook image of science rejected by Kuhn is underpinned by the just delineated notions of knowledge, of the relationship between knowledge and statements, and of the relationship between stated knowledge and action. Without explicitly referring to the mentioned assumptions, Kuhn rejects the textbook image because at every step he finds that the actual practice of science does not correspond to these assumptions, by which the textbook  interpretation of science is underpinned. . FOR INSTANCE...

The statements about science found in the textbooks regard , in the first place, the observed and measured phenomena. These preliminary observations and measurements lead, when the undertaking succeeds, first to the statement by the scientist, as a working hypothesis, of the law that binds the observed phenomena , and then, on the basis of experiments that confirm the validity of the hypothesis, to the statement of the law as a fact of nature. That law will constitute a first piece in the edifice of science, to be completed by the successive contributions by other scientists according to the same pattern. In this way, in the long run, scientific knowledge grows piece by piece, cumulatively, and terminates in an ever more complete and more accurate view of the world.

Why does Kuhn reject the textbook view of science? Because he finds it at odds with the actual practice of science. By contrast to that, he proposes his own interpretation. But his own interpretation of science, though also expressed in statements, implies that statements do not mean as descriptions that correspond  to the reality described, but as tools for action under the appropriate circumstances.   However, Kuhn is referring exclusively to the statements of the scientists, to scientific statements . Now Kuhn’s interpretation of science is itself, like the view of science presented in the textbooks, a reflection on science, not itself science. His statements, therefore, are not scientific statements; they are  reflective, “second-order” statements, statements on what is stated and done by scientists. Though Kuhn’s statements are meant to correct the idea that scientific statements  –first order statements– mean descriptively rather than applicatively or practically, science cannot be learned through them, anymore that it can be learned by reading and understanding the scientific textbooks, *

* Hume distinguishes between what he (and other people, philosophers in particular) believes as a thinker , and what he believes as an agent .  Aristotle and Searle, as we shall see, are Humean “thinkers.” Polanyi and Kuhn are Humean “agents.”

Thinkers deal with other people’s statements, with already made statements–with the “content” of statements, with what is said– and hence they tend to assume that statements mean descriptively, that what is said corresponds , or at least ought to correspond,  to what is the case, in the sense of saying or enunciating what is the case, or at least what the author of the statement believes to be the case. What is said is either true or false, and the appropriate response to that is assent or dissent.

Agents , by contrast, interact with the surrounding world in the attempt to achieve their ends, that is,  to satisfy their needs and desires. This applies to both animals and people –to all the creatures that have needs to be satisfied by their environment and whose action, be it purely instinctive and inherited, or adapted by the creature itself to the circumstances, functions as means to  achieve their end. Of all the needful creatures only human beings are linguistic and therefore capable of judging and making statements. In other words, only their action, or at least certain of their actions, can be linguistic, that is, be informed or mediated by language. What difference does language make?

The answer to this question depends on the way in which the language of judgments and statements by which action is guided is assumed to function. If it is assumed that statements function descriptively , as Aristotle and Searle do, the answer is one. If, on the contrary, it is assumed that statements function instrumentally , as Polanyi and Kuhn do, the answer is totally different.

Before moving ahead it is necessary to distinguish between first order statements, which are judgments, and second order statements, for only the former can function instrumentally.
