Dan Fouke              Argument in Pascal's pensées

(1) - P insists on the insufficiency of reason (after all, he was  at  least
attracted to Jansenism): (a) enough remnant idea of truth and  happiness  to
look for them, not to find them; (b) insistence on  the  opposition  between
"dogmatism" and "scepticism". Given  this,  are  not  his  own  "evidential"
(rather than deductive) 'proofs' affected? Is P consistent? (N.B.  on  p.64,
Dan writes: "the evidential proiofs  are  precisely  intended  to  show  the
superior likelihood of Xtianity and to make it the 'live' option  the  wager
requires." NOTICE THAT IT IS A MATTER OF DECISION: INVOLVED PARTICIPANT  WHO
HAS TO OPT.

(2) - Deductive proofs would have to show that it is absurd  to  deny  God's
existence. But that is not the case. But his main objection  is  that  these
proofs "misrepresent the epistemological  claims  of  Christianity.  In  P's
view Xtianity makes God's personal agency essential to knowing him, so  that
reconciliation with and knowledge of God coalesce" (P. 62).  Does  not  this
argument apply to P's own "evidential" or "probabilistic" proofs?  Elaborate
on this point and give us your personal evaluation of its validity.

(3) - The interest of the wager is that it is a matter of  decision  (though
perhaps not a matter of ordinary deliberation (which involves what is to  be
chosen as an end, not only the means, in a concrete decision).  SEE  END  OF
(1) ABOVE.

-  (4)  "Proofs  by  the  machine":  "They  do  not  prove  the   truth   of
anythging..., they are humanly devised instruments  which  generate  beliefs
in accordance with law-like rules": "followingreligious practices  in  order
to encourage a tendency towards religious belief." "P. regards  this  method
as important because proof of the likelihood of Xtianity  and  the  benefits
of belief is often insufficient to generate belief  because  of  interfering
passions" (p. 64).

- (5) The value and  order  of  the  proofs:  "Proofs  by  the  machine  are
important only after the evidential proofs have led to the  conclusion  that
Xtianity is probably true, after the wager has proved the  superior  utility
of theistic belief, and after the realization that one is unable to  believe
by a simple act of will. (IN THAT CASE,  IS  THE  WAGER  'RATIONAL'?).  Only
after "the mind has seen where the truth lies" (fragment 821) is the  reader
urged to subdue those passions which  prevent  God  from  revealing  himself
(Fragment 119)" (p. 65).

- (6) An additional element for a fideistic interpretation of  the  Pensées:
P's position that "inspiration" or religious experience, and not  reasoning,
is the sole source  of  faith  or  saving  religious  knowledge  (Fr.  110).
"Inspiration is an encounter with God  himself  who  acts  on  the  seeker's
heart by 'une force toute puissante et surnaturelle" (Preface sur le  Traité
du Vide, L. 230), enabling him to kperceive the reality of God  so  that  he
may have certainty (Fr. 913) (p. 65). "P clearly believes that reasoning  is
neither a necessary nor a sufficient condition for religious knowledge  (Fr.
588)" (p. 65). "This  is  'knowledge  par  sentiment  du  coeur  (Fr.  110),
without knowing prophecy, without reading scripture, and  without  examining
the proofs. "Intellectual persuasion of Xtianity's truth  is  not  faith  in
the full sense (Fr. 808). Faith may  or may not  be  preceded  by  argument"
(P. 65). Answer: The proofs  are  preparatory:  they  show  that  there  are
probabilities  that  we  should  care  about  religious   truth,   and   our
helplessness to reach  certain  conclusions  makes  us  desire  to  ask  for
certainty in God.

- (7) P's criticisms of Natural Theology are a  protest  against  a  certain
kind of religiosus proofs--namely those which assign to man an  intellectual
power he lacks--(p. 67). AND, I  ADD-CONERNING  P'S  ORIGINALITY--AGAINST  A
CERTAIN INTELLECTUALISTIC CONCEPTION OF FAITH AND OF THE OBJECT OF FAITH.
