Essay.3 (Chant)

Essay 3. The difficulties encountered expressed in objections

Status quaestionis

A protestant author (cf my paper on the CIT) has remarked

that though the positions concerning faith and reason changed from Aquinas to Kant, the underlying understanding of faith, of reason, and of their relationship has remained constant--the same Aquinas had or has traditionally been understood to have had. Yet this understanding has an historical origin, however 'natural' it may have appeared to be. Furthermore, it cannot be a priori excluded that this understanding be a misunderstanding.

This paper deals with the progressive formation of this understanding.

Chenu made a detailed study of the formation of this understanding to which Aquinas gave final shape. However, Chenu  assumed the correctness of the underlying understanding to which I have been referring. Consequently, thern, as a result of his perspective, Chenu implicitly treated as obscurantists those who opposed the progression toward the final shape that such understanding took, and he assumed that the solution given to the difficulties encountered in that progression was valid and adequate. My perspective intends to reopen the issue. A long meditation on the difficulties encountered by the participants in that progression, on the reasons opposed by some to the direction taken, on the validity and adequacy of the solutions offered to those difficulties, and on the historical effects of the synthesis of faith and reason achieved by Aquinas has given me pause. Rather, then, that taking for granted that every step in the gradual formation of that understanding was a progress, from which it follows that the difficulties encountered by the participants in the constitution of that understanding turned out to be pseudo-problems and that the solutions given were adequate, I will attend very particularly to the difficulties encountered  and submit to critical examination the solutions given.

The difficulties encountered were multiple. However, in the preface to the third edition "revue et augmentee" de son ouvrage, Chenu seems to me to indicate the deep and common source of the resistance:

" La repugnance tenace de la sagesse augustinienne a s'ouvrir a la raison aristotelicienne au coeur de la foi , nous rend sensible, avec la difficulte de l'entreprise, la qualite synthetique de la solution: c'est le titre scientifique lui-meme de la theologie qui requerra la presence mystique de la foi. La theorie de la subalternation des sciences resout l'antinomie, a laquelle se butaient les maitres anterieurs. Menes en quelque sorte par leur propre travail a constater les multiples activites rationnelles de l'intellectus fidei, ils ne consentaient cependant a laisser passer le mot de 'science' qu'en l'enervant de sa puissance et de ses exigences . Kilwardby en denonce l'equivocite, et Rigaud, qui pourtant utilise l'epistemologie aristotelicienne, refuse de maintenir son sens propre. C'est que ni les uns ni les autres--hormis une amorce chez Bonaventure--n'ont mis en oeuvre la theorie de la subalternation des sciences, grace a laquelle seule on peut rendre raison d'un usage de l'appareil scientifique en doctrine sacree, grace a laquelle aussi on peut a cet effet en modifier interieurement les lois, sans la denaturer" (p. 12. My emphasis).

Congar does as much:

"Le P. G[agnebet] montre..., par une etude de S. Augustin et des grands predecesseurs ou contemporains de S. Thomas qui ont suivi en ceci S. Augustin--S. Anselme, Alexandre de Hales, Robert Kilwardby, S. Bonaventure, S. Albert le Grand--, qu'il regnait alors une conception de la theologie comme science affective, c'est-a-dire subordnonnant la connaissance a la 'piete', a la croissance de la charite et a lunion de charite avec Dieu. S. Thomas, au contraire, concoit la theologie comme une activite principalement speculative, et tel est le sens qu'il donne a l'application qu'il lui fait de la notion aristotelicienne du connaitre scientifique. Il nes'agit pas, pour S. Thomas, en science theologique, de faire servir l'intelligence, directement et immediatement, aux fins de l'union de charite avec Dieu, mais simplement, de connaitre; de reconstruire en soi, intentionnellement , (c'est-a-dire dans l'ordre du connaitre), pour le contempler , l'ordre de la science de Dieu" (Bulletin Thomiste, XV (1938), p. 504).

It may well be that all those who absolve Aquinas of having contributed to the rationalism that began to manifest itself during Aquinas' life are right. In any case, my point is not to settle such a complex issue. The fact remains, nonetheless, that

whether or not Aquinas' own Aristotelianism was compatible with the faith he lived, most of his most distinguished predecessors and contemporaries understood Aristotelianism in such a way that it appeared to them as incompatible with the faith they lived and, consequently, refused to range theology--that is, the faith they lived and the Bible they read--among the Aristotelian sciences.
