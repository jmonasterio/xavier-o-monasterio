Narrative and Statement

"Literal or historical sense: First signification, by which words signify things. All the other senses are founded on the literal, from which alone can an argument be drawn, and which cannot be false." "It is called history when something is simply related." Thus Aquinas in the Summa.

Does the above presuppose that the literal sense can only be possessed by statements? And, if so, what notion of statement does Aquinas have in mind? Much of my thesis hinges on this.

Scripture is to be assented to by the act of faith. It seems that one can assent only to judgments-statements by means of another affirmative judgment. And revelation is a teaching, a doctrine, the inchoation of our future beatifying vision of the good ultimate truth; on earth we assent to what we do not see. And only statements can be true or false; but the Scripture is true...

But are not stories true? Yes, the biblical stories are, in all their details, like that X had two sons. But they are true because they are statements by God, the Author of the Scriptures. So, for Aquinas, the biblical narratives are either a unitary statement or a collection of discrete statements.

Furthermore, Aquinas has no serious interest in historical facts per se, in "individual facts," but in doctrines. The Trinity and the Incarnation are seen by him as doctrines, not as operative realities but as facts stated by God. Aquinas does not seem to see the difference between the two, and on this I will comment in a moment under his Aristotelianism; from him, we do not assent to statements but to the realities stated. But he confuses realities stated with operative realities (which, when assented to, are abstracted from a history, not from a vision). The Incarnation is a historical fact primarily, not a doctrine or a teaching.

Why does Aquinas confuse stated facts with operative realities? Because of his Aristotelianism. He sees reality not as that to which we are exposed but as that which we see, as a seen. He thinks of judgment as the affirmation of what is seen because it has been understood. He sees truth as the assentive understanding of what has been understood because of the grasp of sufficient reason for the first assent. We are in the order of vision, not of exposure--exactly as Aristotle.
