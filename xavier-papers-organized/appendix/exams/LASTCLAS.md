                 IMPORTANT POINTS ON MARX AND MASLOW

- The lower needs are animal needs, the higher needs are  human  needs.  No:
all needs are human.

- Maslow's view of normality.  Maslow  thinks  that  what  is  ordinary,  or
average, or more common in a given society is normal. No: normal is what  is
in accord with human nature.

- Normality is what is in accord with human  nature.  But  human  nature  is
conflictive.  (And  there  are  environmental  conditions  which   make   it
extremely difficult or even impossible  to  act  in  accordance  with  human
nature). When it comes to the American society Maslow  ignores  this.  As  a
result he speaks of self-realization as if it were always an easy thing.
- If one agrees with Maslow, is one logically bound to  disagree  with  Marx
concerning the evils of capitalism? Only to an extent, for Marx  exaggerates
the defects of  capitalism,  but  is  not  totally  wrong.  Some  degree  of
competition is necessary  for  efficiency  in  the  transformation   of  the
environment and for people's motivation, for we are  not  angels.  Morevoer,
since people are not necessarily determined by the environment, as shown  by
the exceptions in the concentration  camps,  we  are  less  alienated  under
capitalism than Marx thinks (of which he as a revolutionary  was  the  first
proof). Yet Marx and Maslow would have to agree in criticizing capitalism:
      - The plight of the poor.
      - The horrors of maximized competition  and  of  the  survival  values
(survival of the fitest for competition and reduction of everything  to  the
buck) with no attention to growth values.
      - There is a fundamental contradiction in a mode of  production  which
is  necessarily  collective  (cooperative)--otherwise  it   would   not   be
efficient in the transformation of the environment), and  which  nonetheless
treats its members--rewards them--and  leads  them  to  see  themselves  and
others as if they produced individually and were self-sufficient.

- Marx thinks that the capitalists are mean people (because they  treat  the
proletarians badly), and that the proletarians are good people. No, it  does
not depend on them, under capitalism.

- Because the pigmies  treat  each  other  well,  Marx  would  prefer  their
society to a capitalistic society. No! They treat each  other  well  because
they are lucky enough to live in a plentiful environment. And they are on  a
par  with  animals,  not  having  yet  reversed  their  dependence  on   the
environment.
