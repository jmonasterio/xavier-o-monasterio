The Distinction between Philo and Theo

"Voila ton affaire: arriver a trouver ou git le malentendu entre la speculation et le Christianisme" (K. Bouillard, p. 69).

"Le Chretien fonde son salut eternel sur le rapport a quelque chose d'historique, a savoir que 'Dieu', l"Eternel, est apparu a un certain moment du temps comme un homme individuel" (K. Bouillard, p. 75).

The distinction between philo and theo appears to us natural and commonsensical. Yet it is historical, it began at a certain historical moment. When, and why, and how it started I want to investigate.

I want to investigate that because the distinction has enormous consequences. As an example: there are in American universities separate and independent Departments of philo and theo, which presupposes the legitimacy of the distinction. Within the philosophy Departments there is a specialty called philosophy of religion, which again presupposes the legitimacy of the distinction. But is the distinction legitimate? Does it make sense? Another example: Certain philosophers who are Christians would adamantly claim that, as philosophers interested in religious matters, they are obligated to neutralize their faith in order to proceed as philosophers are uspposed to. This, again, presupposes a definite conception of philosophy and religion and the validity of the distinction between the two. But is the distinction legitimate? A last example: the ambiguous and often debated status of "Christian philosophy." The fact that for many the expression itself is oxymoronic, sharp-and-dull, depends on the validity of the distinction.

The distinction is closely connected--indeed, it is correlative to--a particular conception of faith and reason and their distinction, whose history and legitimacy is itself in need of investigation. I pointedly speak together of history and legitimacy because a condition for contesting the legitimacy of a distinction is the realization that, however natural it may appear to us, it is not natural but historical and, therefore, susceptible of being substituted for something else that makes more sense, if the distinction proves to be illegitimate.

The distinction between faith and reason is also closely connected--I believe, correlative to--a particular conception of natural and supernatural. Sometimes natural is opposed to revealed. Thus, we speak of natural knowledge of God, by contrast to the knowledge we owe to God's revelation. This is still complicated by the distinction between human nature before and after the fall, with its different possibilities.

A few hints

Bouillard comments that St Anselm "distingue fort bien la ratio de l' auctoritas , mais sa ratio nous parait souvent meler raison philosophique et raison theologique" (p. 29). This suggests that while Anselm distinguishes what he accepts on the basis of evidence from what he accepts on the basis of revelation, he does not have the conception of philosophy and theology that came later to prevail. His idea of philo is the Augustinian idea explained by van Steenberghen in his explanation of Bonaventure's philosophy--a Weltanschaung, the Weltanschaung of faith. This confirms the view that the distinction between philo and theo is historical rather than natural and obviously posterior to the time of Anselm.

Yet, St Anselm is clearly a precursor in the move away from revelation as history to revelation as divine statement, as doctrine imparted by a teacher. Anselm practices speculative theology. He wants to prove, to bring to apodictic evidence, God's existence which he knows through faith. And he tries to deduce the Trinity from the essence of the Supreme Spirit, as Hegel will do. What I am suggesting is that Anselm reduces the intellectus fidei (understanding of the sense of faith) to proof and logical demonstration, and that this is a decisive step toward the situation within which the distinction between philo and theo necessarily emerges. Curiously enough, Bouillard rejects this reduction of the intellectus fidei, which arises from the move away from history to concepts and essence of concepts, yet he sides with the distinction between philo and theo. But can one, in all consistency maintain such a position? Yet that is what Bouillard does. For he maintains the distinction: "Or..., nous estimons qu'une preuve de l'existence de Dieu releve de la philo, des lors qu'elle tend 'a une structure logique, independante de toute autorite, ayant valeur universelle.' n'est-ce pas le cas de la preuve du Proslogion , dnas la mesure ou elle tente de surmonter par la seule raison l'incroyance possible?" (P. 112-113). He senses, though, that it would be profitable to escape the distinction between philo and theology which is unknown to Anselm, and yet claims that we have to work within the constraints of that distinction: "Depuis lors, avec St Thomas, puis Suarez, puis Descartes, s'est progressivement introduite une distinction nette, et ensuite une separation, entre philo autonome et theo xtienne. On a meme souvent abouti, en fin de compte, d'une part a une philo sans Dieu, d'autre part a une theo sans pensee. Philo de la religion et theo speculative se trouvent alors pareillement supprimees. Ce resultat nous invite a estimer qu'il y a sans doute entre elles une solidarite que, pour les restaurer l'une et l'autre, il y aurait peut-etre profit a regarder du cote d'Anselme. Mais il nous faut partir aujourd'hui de la distinction qui s'est imposee et que nous ne pouvons pas meconnaitre " (p. 29). Why cannot we disavow the distinction? May it not be that the distinction itself is part of the problem with which we have to struggle and that is to be overcome?

Auctoritas et ratio

Let us begin with Anselm's distinction: auctoritas et ratio.

He makes the judgement-statement that God exists on God's authority, meaning that the reason grounding his judgment is God's authority, not the self-evidence of the statement or any other evidence which would render the statement evident. Ratio, then, judges on the basis of evidence; faith on the basis of authority. Anselm wants to find out whether what he holds on the basis of authority can also be held, adhered to, on the basis of ratio, of evidence.

What, exactly, is Anselm's project? He is a believer. He does not need evidence in order to believe. He will go on believing in God's existence even if he finds no evidence supporting it. Moreover, his faith is the regulatory principle of his search. God exists whether or not there is evidence supporting this judgment; and Anselm would dismiss as erroneous any evidence to the contrary. Thus he works within faith as someone who wants to understand. Yet, as someone who wants to understand--that is, to know, not only to believe--he is similar to the unbeliever, to the one who does not know whether there is a Gop, or who thinks he knows there is no God. This is what Bouillard calls Anselm's modernity: "Ce qui nous interesse, c'est le projet d'un croyant qui, introduisant dans sa recherche le point de vue de l'incroyant, veut decouvrir dans le contenu de sa foi une ratio, un sens, une structure intelligible valant par elle meme, et qui entend lui conferer une valeur universelle en manifestant son rapport a l'experience humaine. C'est ce que j'appellerai la modernite d'Anselme" (p. 25). Anselm manifests

the relationship of his faith to human experience because he does not rely on his faith-judgment but on something created; in other words, he does not move from one idea of faith to another idea of faith but relates an idea of faith--that God exists--to a rational interpretation of human experience--our capacity to conceive of God as that greater than which nothing can be (p. 25). Anselm is modern because far more than he was we are confronted with massive atheism and different religions, and Anselm takes a universal point of view in his attempt to understand.

But what is what Anselm tries to understand? Avowedly, his faith. But does what he actually does correspond to his avowed desire? There are reasons to doubt it. If his faith is that God exists, does he understand any better God's existence after his proof than before? I do not see how. He understands exactly the same as before, to wit: that God exists. Actually, he himself is disappointed on this score. "Pourquoi, Seigneur mon Dieu, mon ame ne te sent-elle pas, si elle t'a trouve?" (Prosl., ch. 14). As Bouillard comments, "Si sa raison est satisfaite, le desir qui l'a mise en branle ne l'est pas. Il cherchait la face de Dieu, sa presence: il ne l'a pas trouvee" (pp. 26-27). In fact, I feel tempted to say, he understands less, or something else, than what he understands through faith. For his faith is such that it allows him to talk as present to present with his existing God, to call Him in the vocative as one talks in actual interchange with a living person--"Poruquoi, Seigneur mon Dieu", "Quare, Domin e Deus meus"--as Anselm does throughout the whole Proslogion (which, incidentally, means conversation with someone), while the God whose existence he has rationally discovered appears to him as an impersonal concept--"that than which nothing greater can be conceived" with whom one can hardly have a conversation.

This leads us to a partricularly important point. Has the rational process actually brought Anselm to the existence of the God of his faith , to the God he adores, or rather to something else? The pointers, in the Proslogion are ambiguous, so ambiguous as to suggest that Anselm himself sensed that his project had failed, that he had not reached the kind of understanding of the God of his faith he was supposed to reach. Indeed, it is clear that Anselm purposely intends to prove the existence of the very God in whom he believes. As indicated by him at the beginning of the Proslogion , the monks for whom he writes asked for rigorous proofs capable of convicing those who did not believe out of ignorance. As Bouillard comments in this connection, "Quand le croyant se place ainsi, par hypothese, au point de vue de l'incroyant, il n'y a aucune difference logique entre l'argument qui peut preserver le premier du doute et celui qui devrait convaincre le second. Il convient donc de penser qu'au moment ou il prouve l'existence de Dieu, St Anselme entend, comme Descartes, et malgre ce qui les distingue, formuler une demonstration valable aux yeux de l'incroyant" (p. ll7). And again: "Par leur attitude, il est vrai, croyant et incroyant ne se trouvent pas sur le meme terrain. Neanmoins, ils ont en commun le langage rationnel, qui permet au premier de s'adresser au second avec l'espoir d'etablir une communion dans le meme acte d'intelligence. Assurement, la preuve tournee vers l'exterieur n'est pas autre que la preuve tournee vers l'interieur. Mais la reciproque est egalement vraie pour Anselme . Et ce qui permet cette reciprocite, c'est une structure rationnelle identique chez le croyant qui parle et l'incroyant qui l'ecoute... telle est bien le sens de la preuve de l'existence de Dieu dans le Proslogion " (p. 114). By virtue, then, of the same rational structure in the believer and the unbeliever, Anselm's proof, if valid, will be equally convincing for both. This is the first pointer. The second pointer, however, contradicts the first. Anselm affirms that only those who have faith can understand the proof, that is, that only they can grasp the proof as a proof and be convinced by it. Bouillard puts very well the paradox involved here: "On nous propose une regle dialectique censee intelligible pour l'incroyant meme et conduisant par une stricte logique a affirmer l'existence de Dieu; on ajoute presque aussitot que cette regle n'est bien comprise que de celui qui veut croire" (pp. 124-126). Bouillard thinks that the solution of the paradox is to be found in a passage of the Epistola De Incarnatione Verbi (ch 2): "Ne vous etonnez pas que je dise: celui qui ne croit pas ne comprendra pas. Car celui qui ne croit pas, ne fera pas l'experience, ne connaitra pas. En effet, autant l'experience depasse la simple audition, autant la science de celui qui fait l'experience l'emporte sur la connaissance de celui qui ne fait qu'entendre" (pp. 124-126). Follows Bouillard's comment: "La nouveaute de ce text, c'est l'explication qu'il nous apporte: la connaissance de la verite est conditionnee par une experience qui n'a lieu qu'au sein de la foi. C'est cette experience qui manque a l'incroyant pour acceder a la veritable intelligence des affirmations xtiennes. Entre la connaissance qu'il en a et celle du croyant, il y a toute la distance qui separe l'oüi dire de l'experience" (pp. 124-126). "La preuve de Dieu n'est pas une pure dialectique ascendante qui rencontrerait son objet au terme de sa course logique; elle est la structure rationnelle du mouvement par lequel l'homme se convertit a l'adoration. Universelle et necessaire, s'imposant en principe a tout esprit qui reflechit, elle ne se developpe effectivement qu'au sein de cette conversion" (p. 137).

I value highly Bouillard's view concerning the importance attributed by Anselm to experience and intend to exploit it later. For the moment, however, my concern is with the paradox, or should I say the contradiction, involved in the two pointers of the Proslogion . On the one hand, everything leads us to believe that Anselm intends to offer and believes he is offering a rational proof for God's existence, valid for any rational person. On the other hand, he ends up confessing that, though the proof is rational, it cannot be accepted as valid but by those who either already believe or are at least willing to believe. Is this a paradox, as Bouillard thinks, or simply a contradiction?

Would a similar paradox be acceptable in the demonstration, say, of a geometrical theorem? Obviously not. By virtue of the same rational structure shared by the teacher who already knows that the three angles of a triangle amount to two square angles and of the student who does not know it yet, the demonstration, if valid, is equally compelling for both. In other words, to say that the proof is rationally valid and that for its validity to be grasped something more or else than rationality is required is a contradiction in terms, not a paradox.   

Sometime later Aquinas will propose his five proofs for God's existence. Like Anselm, he considers them rationally compelling. Unlike Anselm, however, he does not think that they can compel only those who already believe in God's existence through faith. In fact, he grants that philosophers have actually come through proofs of that kind to know that God exists. Yet, like Anselm, he senses that what philosophers have reached in that way is not what the believer possesses through faith. He struggles with the point in different ways. Sometimes he says that God's existence is not a matter of faith but merely a praeambulum to faith. Other times he contends that what philosophers know through the proofs is different from what the believer grasps through faith. After all, it is impossible to know something and accept it on the basis of authority at the same time. And if faith were a matter of intelligence only the more intelligent would have faith. And they would have it on their own strength, not as God's gift. And faith would have no merit, for the intellect is not free not to yield to compelling evidence.

Both, therefore, Anselm and Aquinas, each in his own way, after proposing rational proofs for God's existence, finally refuse to identify the God of their faith with the God of reason.

Either reason by itself fails to reach God because it lacks the experience of God, or it reaches God but not the God of faith.

Neither, however, draws the conclusion that seems to follow, to wit: that reason, understood as they understood it , cannot lead to the God of Xtianity. This proved to be tragic. For by proposing 'purely rational' arguments for God's existence they consecrated the idea of "reason" as a purely intellective faculty or ability by which people are distinguished from animals and which allows them to know with certainty what is the case. At the same time, they created the illusion (though partly disavowed by both of them) that the God they adored could be known that way, for the very purpose of the proofs they offered was to come to know the God in whom they believed. Other people to follow would buy the idea of reason consecrated by Anselm and Aquinas. If they remained as attached to their faith as Anselm and Aquinas were, they were bound to continue to attempt to reach God through proofs, for the same reason that prompted Anselm and Aquinas to go that way--the necessary harmony between faith and reason--and to take refuge in their faith if the proofs failed, as both Aquinas and Anselm were avowedly willing to do, but not without sacrificing their own rationality. Those, however, who bought the idea of reason bequeathed by Aquinas and Anselm, but who either did not share their faith in God or their unconditional attachment to their faith, were bound to find what both Anselm and Aquinas had at least suspected, namely, that "reason" could not reach the God in whom Anselm and Aquinas believed or, indeed, any God at all worthy of being adored. And then, these people, precisely in the name of the notion of their own rationality bequeathed to them by Anselm and Aquinas, would rightly refuse to continue to accept blindly a God who did not harmonize with the rational character of the creatures He had supposedly created.

Aquinas's and Anselm's notion of reason

What notion of reason did Anselm and Aquinas have?

To answer this question we have to return to the issue of the distinction between philo and theo. Supposedly Anselm still mixes the two, though he already distinguishes authority and evidence, whereas Aquinas already clearly distinguishes philo and theo and even accords to the latter a certain autonomy within its own realm. In what way does Anselm mix the two?

Reason, for us, has come to signify something related to our distinctive human ability to know insofar as we are concerned with truth and certainty. Animals perceive and, in this sense, know. But they are not concerned with whether they actually know, that is, with truth. In this way, reason or rationality is characteristically human, it implies and demands reasons that explain why what we think we know is valid knowledge, true knowledge.

What Bouillard means by saying that Anselm does not distinguish between philo and theo is the following. Not only does not Anselm do not know whether he is practicing philo or theo, which presupposes an already established division between the two, but that he is actually mixing the two. He is actually reflecting on his faith as someone who is a believer. Supposedly, he even takes from his faith the notion of God. Furthermore, his avowed purpose is to reach and understanding of the faith to which he adheres. Finally, he explicitly avows that his adherence to his faith will remain the same whether his undertaking succeeds or not. In all these ways Anselm is acting as what today we understand as a theologian. At the same time, however, his undertaking can be considered as a purely rational one. Though derived from his faith, Anselm's notion of God is a purely intelligible one, as intelligible to a non-believer as it is to Anselm. In other words, the notion itself is intelligible independently of whether or not it is derived from the Christian faith professed by Anselm. And the process through which he moves from the intelligibility of that notion is a purely rational one, as accessible to the believer as to the non-believer. In this way, Anselm is acting as a philosopher could and would. Thus, Anselm is acting both as a theologian and as a philosopher. As a philosopher because the data on which he relies are accessible to a rational being without need of any divine or supernatural revelation, and because the process followed is the rational one, namely, the process of bringing to evidence what was previously inevident to the person who follows the process. The fact that, also and at the same time, Anselm is acting as a theologian does not detract from this. So far so good. But is he mixing philo with theo? There are indicators that such is the case. Is it actually possible to reach an understanding of faith by means of a purely philosophical move like Answelm's? In that case, why is Anselm disappointed? Why cannot he quite recognize the God of his faith in the God of his proof? And is it possible to reach the God of faith through a purely rational process? In that case, why, according to Anselm, who does not recognize in the God of his reason the God of his faith, neither can the non-believer recognize the God of the Christian faith in the God he has reached through the Anselmian rational process, or--in another possible interpretation--why cannot the non-believer who lacks the experience of God in faith grasp the validity of the proof?

"St Anselm, then, was unable to express clearly and distinctly what he wanted to say about the relations between faith and reason. However, what his intentions were in this matter are now sufficiently clear; and for our purposes here what is important is that one of the things St Anselm intended to say was that reason was to some extent capable of understanding God and the things of God prior to faith and independently of faith... St Anmselm implicitly allows the possibility of a rational approach to God, logically prior to and independent of a properly theological approach " (Charlesworth, p. 40).

"Gerem then, we have the two sides to St Anselm's thought. On the one hand, reason is allowed an independent function prior to faith and, in some sense, reason can bring us to assent to the truths of faith.

On the other hand, St. Anselm clearly acknowledges that faith is possible without any kind of prior rational preparation orjustification, and that for the believer the only function of reason is to understand what is already believed. Clearly, as they stand, these two sides to St Anselm's thought are not consistent, and some kind of distinction between the function of reason which brings us to faith and the function of reason which operates within faith would have to be made to make them consistent. However, like St Augustine, St Anselm does not have the categories with which to make this distinction clearly and unequivocally, and the two strains in his thought exist in an unresolved, uncrystallized state" (Ibid., p. 34).

The key is in this "function of reason which brings us to faith."

St Anselm was perfectly familiar with Aris's logic. This logic deals with concepts. Concepts are constituted by essences or essential properties expressed in definitions. Reason is the faculty that enables us to move from something known to something previously unknown by means of necessary connections between two concepts or between a concept and a particular instantiation of a concept. Assuming that reason brings us to faith amounts to assuming that we can move from something known, that is, evident and compelling assent, to something inevident but revealed, by means of necessary connections. Furthermore, it amounts to assuming implicitly that what is revealed is a knowable of the kind that can enter into a syllogism, that is, either a concept (a purely intelligible definition) or a particular instantiation of an intelligible concept.

On the other hand, particulars as such are unintelligible, that is, indefinable in their particularity. Therefore, assuming that what is revealed is a knowable, that is, either a concept or an instantiation of a concept, amounts to assuming that its particularity is irrelevant, that its singularity--its thisness, its hereness, its nowness--does not count. This is what led Aristotle to place history, what actually happens or happened out of the scientific realm. At least drama deals with the possible, with the conceivable!

Another point. Bringing an unknown to evidence is making it object of intelligent assent, where intelligent assent means its being immediately or mediately (through necessary connections) evident. Therefore assuming that the revealed is knowable is assuming that the revealed as such is object of unintelligent adherence to it as being the case by authority and susceptible of being object of intelligent assent through evidence. Therefore, faith is implicitly conceived as assent to a conceptual truth, to something. Another way of putting the point I am driving at. A definition expresses a concept. A judgment expresses a predication of a concept; it involves assent to the predicated as predicated. All that one can do about a predicate is assent to it or dissent from it. If faith is a knowable of an intelligible concept, all one can do about it is, in the best case, assent to it.

Aris is an intellectualist. Strictly speaking, for him, knowing is knowing why, that is, understanding through formal cause, through essential definition. But only that is definiable which is intelligible, which has an essence, which is necessarily so
