Speculative and Practical knowledge

"Let no one enter here who does not pursue geometry."

"Quia parvgus error in principio

magnus est in fine" (De Ente et

Essentia, 1). "To en arche mikron en te teleute ginetai pammegezes" (Aris, De Caelo, lib. I, cf. 5, A 271 b 8-l3).

(1) One of Aris's distinctions between S and P knowledge has to do with the purpose of the knower: to satisfy his natural desire to know (or understand), or to act appropriately.

(2) Another distinction has to do with particulars as such and the reason for the particulars (formal cause).

(3) Yet another distinction has to do with knowledge that terminates in mere assent and knowledge that terminates in action.

(4) A last distinction has to do with things as they are in themselves and things as they are in relation to us, as they move us.

The last two distinctions come first.

What characterizes the agent's knowledge is that it terminates in action. And, insofar as it is conveyed, it is related to action. This is the knowledge Aris has in mind in his refutation of scepticism: nobody who responds to objects and aims at responding appropriately is a sceptic as to things in relation to him, though he may be a sceptic as to how things are in themselves.

However, when Aristotle gives indications of speaking of things in themselves he has not in mind what Plato or Kant do. He has in mind things understood in their essence. That is, things as they are not for agents but for understanders, what any and every understander judges with unrestricted assent. The agreement that is not possible on the level of perception, because of its contextuality and particularity, is possible on the level of understanding.

Where does "action" fall within this Aristotelian map?

Science is of the fix; but there is no fixity in action. Therefore, no scientific knowledge, no epistéme of action.

The agent is phronetic. But this does not prevent Aristotle from looking for the good agent and the good life in a transcultural, purely human sense. Assuming that such knowledge is possible, which is highly doubtful, my present question is: Is this knowledge speculative or practical? I will return to this.

Why is the agent phronetic? Because the agent deals with the concrete and particular, and there are no strict universals concerning action;  only, at best, rules of thumb. Does this imply that the knowledge pursued by Aris in the Ethics is not scientific? It does not, if Nussbaum is right. The good life and the good agent can be defined, and be object of assent, as much as the movement of animates. Does this knowledge possess strict universality? Is it explanatory knowledge? Well, it grasps the essence of the good life to be pursued and of the good agent who is to pursue it. However, according to Aris, this life cannot be desired, not even defined as object for assent, but by an agent who is prepared to be good. Orexis and phronesis are inseparable in practical knowledge, whereas in theoretical knowledge it is just a matter of assent. Thus the knowledge pursued by Aris in the Ethics is practical, for the sake of action and capable of resulting in action. Yet it is not less scientific than the knowledge of the Metaphysics or the De Anima. The difference is that episteme terminates in assent-vision, whereas phronesis terminates in action; the latter is horetic-knowledge, whereas the former is just knowledge.

One point that disturbs me in Aristotle is the practical theorem from which the practical syllogism follows. For Aristotle, it is a matter of the practical logic that subtends or underpins action, which is as much grounded on vision of the good life and the good agent as episteme is grounded on theoretical vision of what is the case. Theorems are general and are drawn from the data whose intelligible pattern is the quarry. I think that the right substitute for the theorem is a concrete history in the making . We look for a theorem, which we never get, because we do not have a shared history. And even those of us who have a shared history but are misled by the mirage of a universal theorem, since we have to live with others who having no history aim for theorems and propose theorems, and with others who share a different history from ours, aim for a universal theorem to be shared by all and submit ourselves to the theorem that is most popular at the time--the American theorem. A history in the making has a relatively open-ended temporal pattern, sufficiently defined to be a pattern, sufficiently undefinded and open to a contingent future as to allow for practical invention. And the pattern is phronetic and prescriptive, not epistemic and purely intelligible like a theorem.

Mardi

The primary communication in everyday language is of objects for response among those who have to respond to them. So is perceptual experience. We do not perceive or communicate "facts," pieces of information to be merely understood and assented to. We communicate fire as vitandum for an agent exposed to it.

Fire is what has the properties of fire for sensitive, needful agents exposed to it.

Experience accumulates and gets sedimented in practical memory. Not only the experience of the object for response but of successful and unsuccessful coping with it. We develop techniques for coping successfully.

We do not only perceive and communicate as immediately exposed, but also as generally vulnerable and needful, in view of the future. We communicate objects for response even when not exposed to them (Don't touch fire; fire burns), and techniques for coping succesfully, for avoiding exposure, etc.

Till now we are in objects for response, in good and bad, in objects quoad nos-vulnerable, needful agents. This is the type of reference involved in statements of this nature. The statements define the object as object for response.

But the neutralization of exposure temporarily frees the agent from the coping intention. It is the time of meditation for philosophy, of self-understanding in relation to the inhabited world, and of ultimate explanation of it all, either through storical myths, or through "reason," or through revelation. It is also the time for free chat, for amorous expression and communication, for playfyul communication and activity. But it is the time, too, for objectification .

Objectification is the fixation of the object as such, in se et per se, as object of inquiry (not--or at least not immediately--as object for response), with all its observed predicates, abstracting from concrete circumstances of encounter. It looks for the what it is in itself , given what it looks like and does and is done by other objects; it looks for the reason why of all that is observed. It extrapolates to the best available explanation of observed effects. This is understanding, explanatory knowledge, epistéme . It terminates in pure assent to the object as understood. It may be pursued for practical purposes of foreseeance, of control, of applications. But it may be pursued for its own sake. In either case, however, it has a sort of self-sufficiency, the self-sufficiency of the understood in itself, independently of whehter it has practical applications. In order to apply, one has first to understand what is to be understood--the object in itself. This is the essence incarnated in the particulars of which they are concrete exemplifications. But notice that it is a class of particulars represented by the object of inquiry, which is itself an abstraction from this or that observed individual, where the class is constituted by the relevant regularities of observed conduct (effects produced and received).

N. B. The passage from the object for response to the object in itself presupposes that they are the same, that one knows the same (and in the same way) when one knows object for response and when one knows the object in itself.

This step toward the object in itself is of great importance for several reasons. It became the ideal model of knowledge, though in fact it is based through abstraction on the classes of objects carved out by natural languages, which themselves have as their primary object objects of encounter, that is, good and bad. A consequence of this is that whatever cannot be known epistemically is to be considered as not real (for real becomes identified with object of epistemic knowledge). And, since by the very nature of the abstraction involved in this knowledge the vulnerability of the subject to the object as well as the capacity of the object to satisfy needs are systematically ignored, these 'properties' of objects become non-real, purely subjective. This, I think, happened to a large extent through the adoption of the Aristotleian view of science, of knowledge, and of the intellect as the only genuinely knowing faculty.

The case is even more complicated, for there are other confusions involved.

The names in natural languages function primarily as identifiers of objects for response in circumstances of encounter. (For the moment I ignore the case of proper names and their use in the vocative). They convey or stand for the general properties of objects as good and bad--fire burns, but also helps to keep warm and to cook food--from which the vulnerable needful agent, given the circumstances, is to construct the salient relevant features (the formal object as good or bad, as vitandum or amplexendum). In other words, in conjunction with the circumstances and the interpretive activity of the subject, they are prescriptive identifiers that result not in assent but in action. When, however, the situtation is such that the coping activity of the subject is not urgently demanded, there is room for other more playful, more self-expressive activities. But there is room, too, for the knowing-referential activity that gives rise to the thing in itself and the epistemic knower. The latter, however, is a specialization and a refinement of a knowing-referential activity that is not scientific. I want to focus on this one.

As in the scientific activity, here, too, there is objectification. (This, I think, is the objectification that results in Searle's idea of illocutionary meaning, but this remains to be explored). Common sense is realistic. What it experiences of the object it projects into the object as real. A lion is as fearful as it has two eyes and four legs and a tail, though obviously its being fearful is a function of circumstances, whereas its having four legs and a tail is not.

Some reflection, therefore, leads the knowing commonsensical person to understand by the name "lion" only those descriptive characteristics which are approximately common to all lions in all circumstances, by which they are different from other objects, and which therefore function as identifiers of lions for agents, independently of circumstances. (In this way, I can identify a radio to a child who has never seen one as "the box that talks and sings," where my references are to traits of sensorial experience. This leads to natural classifications--natural classes of objects. These classes, comprehended by a common descriptive definition, are also abstractions, like the explanatory definitions of epistéme, for they are meant to be general rather than individual. Furthermore, like the latter, they abstract from good and bad, partly because good and bad are not generalizable, since they are functions of circumstances, partly because good and bad are not describable, since they are not object of sensory experience. They differ, however, from scientific classes of observeds, in that they have no explanatory purpose of the conduct of the object.

Sometimes, however, on a higher and less immediatly practical level, they become quasi-scientific, that is, quasi explanatory, when they become concerned with conduct or functioning rather than with looks and mere practical identification. For instance, the soul is an explanatory principle for the conduct of the human being both in its biological functioning and in its intentional functioning. And, no less than in the case of scientific explanations, these explanations are realistic, project the explained or understood of the thing as a reality into the perceived. I think this is a dead end from the point of view of knowledge, but this needs to be explored. In any case, it is also an attempt to reach the thing in itself, rather than quoad nos, and the attempt is characterized by objectification of the object and the pure contemplator's position in its regard. And it leads to speculative rather than practical knowledge.

The natural classes are classifications of objects for response, or at least for imaginary response, for thrills. Thus, before the new natural history, there is objectification for a contemplator. But the object objectified is still the object of natural experience the lion seen in the zoo, which is the same as the lion encountered in the forest, only under different conditions), or at least not clearly distinguished from it. It is not yet the lion object of scientific inquiry, or at least not clearly distinguished from it. into the totally different epistemic paradigm of object of inquiry ande assent.

Another source of confusion (to continue what I said on p. 6) is one with which Aristotle struggled, between speculative and practical knowledge, between epistéme and phrónesis , between object of epistéme and action.

The primary function of the agent is coping, not inquiring, still less inquiring for the sake of mere understanding. Aristotle acknowledges that action is not object of episteme because of its lack of fixity which prevents universalization.

But "action" is a highly ambiguous name. For instance, the "motion" that he studies in the De Anima and the De motu can equally be named "action," and nonetheless it is object of episteme and it yields knowledge pursued for its own sake.

Why then does he say that there is no science of action? Evidently, in the Ethics he understands action in another way or ways. And the reasons why he declares action not to be a possible object of scientific knowledge may be no less ambiguous than his understanding of action. There are at least four different candidates to qualify as the reason of which Aristotle is talking about: lack of fixity, particularity, contingency, and object of desire.

To start with the reason of particularity. For Aristotle, there is no science of the here and now or of the then and there, that is, of individuals, of actual existents as such. These, as such, are surds, without intelligibility.

My point has to be more radical . Aristotle is not interested in the particular but in the universal, not in the singular but in the general . Singulars are a matter of "perception." But Aris does not see, or at least not quite clearly, the radical difference between perception in encounter and perception in pure contemplation. He senses the radical differences between the domain of action (encounter) and the domain of contemplation (vision). But he has only one theory of truth: the wise agent's generalizations based on the wise agent's vision are the true ones. Thus, fundamentally, he reduces knowledge to vision: that is good in general which the wise agent sees as good by desiring it, by responding to it as desirable. If I desire something else and therefore see it as good, I should come to see as good what the wise man desires so as to be able to desire it. The ethics is a persuasive argument, based on my general desire to be happy, to give a specific content to my desire, to make me see what the wise Aris desires and I don't so that I come to desire it. What is not clear is whether Aris considers the object of inquiry and the object of desire to be one and the same. To clarify the confusion: Aris grants and even argues that the physician does not cure man, but this man. The desire to cure is presupposed in the physician. The physician sees this man as sanandus. But the singular man the physician as such sees is an exemplification of man as object of medical inquiry; not this man as a character in the drama of life, with a particular situation within it, as a source of projects, of desired outcomes. This man prefers to be left alone and die, because the best he can expect is to go on living diminished in a way that is unacceptable to him. What Aris looks for is a hierarchical, universal system for desires , so that there be no conflict between the physician's desire as physician and the ill person's desire; or, if there is conflict, the conflict can be rationally solved by reference to Aris's ethics.

There are interesting transitions involved in this process.

Medicine is practical. What does this mean? That the symptoms are symptoms of an illness, of something undesirable to which the appropriate desire is to be get rid of and the appropriate response is to cure. Illness is not a neutral name like gravity . (I contend that God is a name like illness, not like gravity; like Santa, not like illness, in that it is a proper name).

However, it can be objectified and transformed into object of inquiry. What is this illness? This "what" is explanatory: it is such and such a mixture of humours. That is what is to be corrected. But, thanks to the modern divison of labor, to the pure biologist the mixture of humours appears as neutral a term as the gravity of the physicist; it appears as pure object of inquiry. Of course, there would never have been such an object of inquiry if people had not noticed illnesses. And they did not notice them as gravity but as undesirable, as actual or potential victims of it . ( Searle's illocutionary meaning is of this kind . "Slavery" is the condition of a human being forcibly belonging to another called "master" as a mere possession, ind ependently of whether it is desirable or undesirable, and desirable for whom--the master or the slave). But that is not the way we use words, not the way we speak or hear them. That is the way a transcendent contemplator would speak about slavery. But such a contemplator would not understand slavery at all, as lived, for he has no idea of what being the oppressor or the oppressed means).

There is an important difference. Slavery is a word of a natural language, not of a technical language. Natural language is the language of vulnerable, needful agent-patients. Searle's definition of slavery takes it as if it were a word of pure contemplators. But don't we all agree on the definition of slavery, whether we consider it as desirable or undesirable? And don't we have to agree on it to discuss whether it is desirable or undesirable? This presupposes that being a slave and not being a slave are facts separable from any attitude toward that. Slavery is the condition of being a slave, as illness is the condition of being ill--an abstraction which would have no meaning if there were not and had not ever been slaves. We possess things, but nobody speaks of things as slaves, but as being owned, as being possessed, because things are indifferent to being possessed, while people mind--and ought to mind--being slaves.

I want to backtrack to the difference I sensed between natural language (that is, the natural usage of phonemes) and technical language. What do I mean? Something connected with the God of philosophers and the God of Abraham.

Let us start with being a slave. Being a slave is not the same as talking about being a slave. A slave is not someone who necessarily talks about being a slave, nor is the one who talks about being a slave necessarily a slave. Even talking about slavery presupposes someone being in the condition of slavery, as talking about illness presupposes the condition of being ill realized in at least one ill person. A slave is someone who acts, talks, and conceives himself and his future as being a slave

as something not chosen by him but imposed on him, suffered and hated by him. It is a defining characteristic of a character, that is, a trait that is identical with the identity of a character as patient. It is is a for-himself (he has to obey), a for-his-master, and for-other-agents.

Let us now come to talking to a concrete slave , to someone who is a slave. What one says and can appropriately say to someone who is a slave, as a slave, depends on whether or not one is a slave or a free person. For being free, as being a master, are no less constitutive of the identity of a character, and no less for-himself, for the slave, and for other agents as witnesses as being a slave.

The word "slave" may not even have appeared. But, since it makes a significant difference, for it confers being to the characters involved, it will appear as a common name, as a name that identifies a class of characters. Where differences make no difference they are irrelevant, go unnoticed and untalked for the most part, and there are no names for them. In a society in which the color of the skin would make no difference, the names "black" and "white" as identifiers of people would not exist. We, therefore, now have "slave" and "slaves," "master" and "masters."

This makes possible to talk about a slave, the slaves, and their condition (slavery) .

This transition interests me, for it  involves a certain objectification, at least in the sense of carving out an object in what otheriwse would be mere background for other named-objects. This objectification, however, is initially practical, for agents-patients. It is a shortcut for a particular object for response in relation to the speaker and the hearer, depending on the relationship in which they stand to the object talked about in the presence of the object : as master of, as slave of, as one of the free, as one of the slaves, as the slave talked about, etc.

This objectification, therefore, is practical in that it implicitly carves out practical possibilities. It has to be contrasted with another kind of objectification-identification which is still practical, but not immediately practical, for it is not in the îmmediate presence of the object for response . By not immediate presence I mean no need of immediate response, which opens room for deliberation. The only things this adds are possibility of choosing and planing one's course of action.

What is characteristic of practical objectification is that it is appetitive, evaluative, emotive, prescriptive of action, motivating. It has to be contrasted with theoretical objectification which terminates not in action but in categorical judgment, in assent. The object is considered as mere object of inquiry; it ceases to be object for response. It is not object for you or for me or for us as exposed to it, as needful of it, as vulnerable to it. It is object for understanding. Not only is the world put in abeyance, but the relationship of the subject to the object is radically transformed. The problem is that because the name remains the same the transformation and its consequences may remain unnoticed or be confused.

How does this transformation occur? Take "slave." Practical recognition of a slave amounts to acting appropriately as a slave in response to other slaves and not slaves, or as a free person in response to other free people and slaves. Naming someone as a slave amounts to identifying him as a slave for practical purposes. Unless slaves are identifiable by some visible sign, such as chains or color of the skin, a very young child cannot meaningfully use the word "slave." And, until the child "knows his place," he cannot respond appropriately to slaves or as a slave. Here we are in the domain of exposure and of primary usage of natural languages. Here the name is a function, a relationship between namer and named (and acknowledgement by the named as named), for practical purposes. In theorecitcal objectification the case is not such. The name is a shortcut for a set of abstracted observed or observable properties, not for object for response. This is the way in which the name "triangle" functions in geometry. It  does not 'refer' to object encountered. Any triangle of whatever size and color will do as an example, precisely because what the name names is not object for response, even for potential response, but an abstract set of observed or observable properties or attributes. This is what the name is an identifier of. These properties are neither here nor there; they are not like the box that sings and speaks which is in the living-room. In fact, as Lonergan explains, the name in this case is the name for a concept. And the same is true of "slave": a person owned by another person.

However, insofar as a particular triangle is a triangle the relationships established in Euclidean geometry are true of the triangle, when predicated of it. And our Serlean definition of the notion of slave, which is what is meant by the name in the case of theoretical objectification, is true of any slave as a slave, when predicated of it. And this is what everybody understands by "slave." NO. THIS IS WHAT EVERYBODY WHO REDUCES A SLAVE TO A NOTION UNDERSTANDS. AND THIS IS WHAT WE ALL DO WHEN WE TALK THORETICALLY ABOUT SLAVES AND SLAVERY, WHEN WE TALK ABOUT IN THE THEORETICAL MODE . BUT THIS IS NOT WHAT WE DO IN THE ORDER OF EXPOSURE AND INTERACTION.

What I am trying to understand is the transition of "God" as object for response to object of inquiry, what happens when the God of the Bible is transformed into object of knowledge.

Abraham, Searle and Aquinas would say, had a notion of God, which could be explicitated in a concept, which is the same in all those who speak about God and who, as far as believers are concerned, is verified in God. Do I contend that Abraham had no such notion of God? What exactly is my point?

Aris acknowledges that in matters of action there is no epistéme. I agrree, though why there is no epistéme in these matters remains to be clarified. I contend that God belongs in the order of action, not of speculation, and that Aquinas treats God as if He belong in the order of speculation. That much is clear to me. But showing that such is the case, and that it is a mistake with enormous consequences remains to be done .

The slave to be responded to is a matter of phrónesis. Insofar as the slave is object, he is object of desire or aversion, not neutral object. If Abraham had a concept of God, it was as adorandus. In other words, whatever predicates Abraham's supposed concept of God involved, if any, they were adorabiles.

The concept "slave" (person owned by another person) is practical: person who acts as owned because he practically acknowledges himself as owned, and to be treated as owned according to respective roles. It has a certain generality, for it does not say who is a slave, by whom the slave is owned, and how concretely he is to be treated. "Slave" is a common name, God is a proper name. One important difference. Is there such a thing as the concept corresponding to a proper name? If Abrahama had a concept of God, it was a concept of this kind--the concept of a character that emerges from a past history and is projected toward the future as the subject of a history, as a historical subject. ( Notice that history, for Aris, is even below poetry, for it deals with the purely contingent, with what happened to be so) . Without knowing the particular history, one cannot know the character. Furthermore, the knowledge is at least imaginarily practical, knowledge of object for response.

Natural classes, classes of natural languages, such as persons, animals, and things, are practical classes, classes of objects for response. But they are classes and, therefore, abstractions. As such, they do not define object for response, for an abstraction, that is, a concept, cannot be responded to, nor can it be desirable or undesirable; only concrete singulars can be such. The concept "slave" is of this kind. Abraham's concept "God," which is a shortcut for this and no other historical character, is not of this kind.

Yet, though natural classes are practical, in that they are abstracted from praxis and are general identifiers of objects for response, they can be taken, or mistaken, for speculative. Aris, for instance, in his De Anima, took the class persons for speculative classes. What did he do, exactly? Indeed, this is the kind of objectification that is genuinely important, for it is the wrong one that Aris and Aquinas took for the right one, for the model of knowing.

He neutralized his exposure to persons, animals, and things, thereby converting them into objects of observation, of inquiry.

What results from that in De Anima is the epistemic knowledge of persons and animals. Since Aris himself admits that there is no epistéme of action, obviously he was not taking people as agents. He was 'observing' them think, desire, act. 'Observing,' under inverted comas, because obviously he did not see them think, desire, and act intentionally. He projected his own experience into the observed objects that looked similar to him, the distinguishing mark between animals and people being thinking. He, therefore, made into his object of inquiry the descriptive class of those who looked like him--bipeds, two-handed, erect, talkers, writers, etc. As Sartre would say, he attached to the Other's body as observable the properties of his own observed

experience of desiring, thinking, speaking, writing, etc.

This was his explanandum, the "appearances to be saved," the common properties and the different properties. His explanation was the three different kinds of animae, the different faculties, etc., as well as the class-identity of those animae in particular and of those faculties. Taken seriously, this concept of man supposedly verified in each singular human was bound to result in the intractable problems of philosophy : solipsism, epistemic scepticism, irrationality of emotions, separation between knowing, feeling, and acting, etc. Of course, actual life continued its course unabated, but when it came to discussing knowledge it all could be pure illusion, for our understanding of knowing is informed by this view of the relationship between "man" and the surrounding world. Aristotle himself had no problems, partly because he did not see them, partly because his commonsensical realism prevented from seeing them--a certain pre-established homomorphism between the mind and reality. This realism is evident from the way in which he speaks of "perception" of singulars in the Ethics as something beyond dispute, and from his refutation of scepticism.

I contend that this objectification results in a spurious object . For Aris, "man" as object of inquiry and epistéme is exemplified in the agent as man. The agent I am and the agents I perceive are, and are what Aristotle says they are. In the Ethics Aris turns to man as agent without noticing the radical heterogeneity between the order of contemplation and the order of action . The heterogeneity shows in the fact that the agent as man has "fixity" and is object of universal, explanatory judgments, whereas the man as agent lacks fixity and is not object of epistéme. Clearly, since they are subjects of opposite predicates, agent as man and man as agent cannot be the same subject.

What goes on in the objectification of the agent as man?

The agent's relationship to the world is practical, orexic, to concrete objects for desire or aversion in an open-ended temporal framework of exposure, in which he himself is object for desire or aversion and even mere object of non-intentional action.

The agent neither knows nor presupposes that he has faculties or a soul, anymore that he knows or presuposes that he has lungs and that his blood is circulating. This is similar to the agent interacting with God without knowing or presupposing that he has a thing called "faith." Faith is the name for an explanatory concept that "saves the appearances" of the agent's interaction with God. But which are the "appearances"? The orexic God to whom the agent is orexically exposed, to whom corresponds the agent's orexically intending that God and being orexically exposed to Him. When that particular mode of relationship ceases to take oplace, "faith" ceases to exist, it becomes a pure possible concept. This is similar to "slavery." It is a name for a relationship. When that relationship does not take place or ceases to take place (perhaps for lack of social, sancioned recognition), "slavery" becomes a name for a pure concept. But there are concepts and concepts, depending at least in part on whether they are of exposure or of mere contemplation. Slavery is a name for a relationship of reciprocal exposure. The concept is therefore intrinsically evaluative. It involves the owning of a person by another and the being owned of a person by another. Aris's concept of the person in the De Anima is not evaluative, it is purely factual. Searle's illocutionary meaning of "slavery" is purely factual, not evaluative. It is what everybody is supposed to understand by the term "slavery" whether the relationship actually takes place or not; it really is a name for a pure concept. But with one important assumption: that if the term refers, that is, if the concept has a concrete exemplification, it refers to a neutral fact--a relationship, independently of whether the relationship is good or bad, desired or hated, desirable or undesirable. That is the really real, what objectively exists, and the rest is either purely subjective, or at least parasitic on what is objective, as if what is objective could have exist and entered into the language without being desired by the master(s) and hated by the slave(s). It seems to me that the case is the other way around: the objective is parasitic on the lived (which is neither 'objective' nor 'subjective', given the way in which these terms are used).

Even if what I just said were granted, the illocutionary meaning of "slavery" may be useful. Useful for what? For talking about slavery, for making slavery into object of inquiry, for making sure we are talking and inquiring about the same. Take, for instance, "Aids." Of course, aids is undesirable--everybody would grant it; it is, by definition, an "illness," and everybody prefers to be healthy. Now, this does not prevent the usefulness of objectifying aids, not only for the purpose of making sure that we are talking about the same when we talk about aids, but in  order to explain and cure aids Is not "slavery" comparable to "aids"? Let us see.

Aids, when objectified, remains an illness to be cured. In that sense, it is object of practical knowledge. Explaining aids is explaining what produces it, how it functions, so that it can be cured. Once this is known, since everybody agrees that aids is to be cured, that is, since all the participants are actual or potential victims of aids, abstracting from the undesirability of aids is harmless, for it is really presupposed. The participants in this case do not need to be persuaded as to the desirability of finding the cure. But that is not the case with "slavery."

Aids is the name for an undesirable in the abstract, that is, all other things being equal, because obviously gettting rid of aids is not the only desirable in the world. But "slavery," supposedly, is a neutral name for a reality exemplified in the case of any slave owned by any master, much as gravity is exemplified in the case of any falling body. As, descriptively, that is what anybody understands by gravity, that is what anybody understands by slavery. Slavery in itself, then, is neither desirable nor undesirable. This is very different from "aids," which is, by definition, an illness--something that is integral to and constitutive of the meaning of the language of agents. Aids in itself does not exist; it exists for agents as undesirable, or it does not exist at all even in the language.

Furthermore, on the level of biology and physiology, "aids" does not function as a name or identifier for a concept; it is a heuristic name for the etiological explanation to be found--perhaps a virus of such biological characteristics which, consequently, reacts to such and such treatment. From the complex of symptoms of the illness, which constitute the appearances to be saved by the explanation, we have moved to a heuristic name. The descriptive, that is, the appearances, are already taken for granted. (AT A CERTAIN POINT, I WILL HAVE TO MOVE TO ARIS'S MAN AS MAN AND MAN AS AGENT, AND TO RETURN TO GOD AND FAITH AS NEUTRAL TERMS). They are exemplified in any observable case of aids, which as far as agents are concerned is the name for an undesirable. How does this compare with "slavery"? If "slavery" names a neutral relationship the comparison is faulty, for "aids" is not a neutral term. But even if we assume, contrary to Searle , that "slavery" is not a neutral term, the comparison is faulty. Indeed, aids is the name of an undesirable for agents.

Obviously, however, that is not the case of slavery. If slavery were as undesirable to the masters as it is to the slaves (all other things being equal), slavery would have never existed, for nobody whould have desired to own a person. Here not all the participants are "victims," some of them are "beneficiaries" and may find slavery very comfortable and profitable. In other words, if slavery is not a neutral term, it cannot be simply taken as the name for a moral illness to be get rid of, for some of the participants constitute it as a good to be preserved. It is at this point, I think, that the attempt at creating the illocutionary meaning, the neutral meaning of "slavery," occurs.

How and why?

If there is going to be communication among the parties, they have to speak of the same. They cannot speak of it as an illness or as a desirable, for they disagree. Therefore they speak of the concept of slavery, much as  physicists speak of

gravity, independently of whether it is desired or averted, desirable or undesirable. "Slavery" has become object of inquiry, either to add to the description or to explain the appearances described.

Take the term "paralysis." For the physician it means a syndrom observed. For the paralytic it is a detestable mode of his being exposed to the world whose correlate is the world exposed to him. Are the doctor, the investigator, and the paralytic speaking of the same when they speak of it? The name is the same. According to Sartre, though, they are not speaking of the same. For the biologist it is object of inquiry, directed by the fact that it is undesirable. For the doctor it is object of medical cure to be treated, in general or in the particular case. For the paralytic it is is suffered, hated practical exposure to the world. Do they have different concepts, or can their concepts fall under the same definition of paralysis? The medical biologist's descriptive concept is composed of the observed syndrom, but as object of inquiry and explanation. The doctor's concept is the same syndrom but as object to be get rid of. For the paralytic as agent it is not a syndrom, rather the syndrom is a limiting, hated part of his being, of his from-where of exposure.

For the biologist, the understood object is object for assent. For the doctor, it is transcended object for cure. For the paralytic, reflectively , it is hated object to be done away with. The biologist objectifies into object of inquiry and explanatory understanding. The doctor objectifies the understood object into object for potential response and the observed syndrome is responded to as curandum. Is there any objectification on the paralytics' part?

If the paralytic reflects, that is, observes himself as the doctor observes him, there is. But that is a specialized function; the paralytic does not spend his life observing himself. He lives responding to the world from where he is. The surrounding world is not for the paralytic object of inquiry; it is for response in a context of reciprocal exposure .

There is no inquiry without objectification; inquiry requires an objectified object . Inquiry is questioning, and questioning is questioning about objectified object. Response is not inquiry. Not that response has no object, but it has no objectified object . Response is a different relationship of the person to the surrounding world.

What do I mean by saying that response has no objectified object? An objectified object is object of observation or consideration by itself, as such, not in terms of the observer's or anybody else's exposure to it, in an observation or consideration informed and directed by the "pure" desire to know and understand it. There may be an ulterior practical motivation to the inquiry, or the inquiry may lead to a knowledge that can be applied in action, but that is not the immediate purpose of the inquiry. The inquirer as such is related to the object as mere object of inquiry, not as object of exposure. Exposure is neutralized. It is not merely that the inquirer's exposure is neutralized, but that the order of exposure is neutralized. By

contrast, the order of exposure is the very order in which the agent acts. Moreover, the inquiring intention here is only one specialized function which obtains sometimes but which is netither the only function nor the most fundamental one.

The exposed agent puts the world in abeyance and opens the space of practical inquiry only when he does not know what the object for response is or, what amounts to the same, how he should respond. This was the case of the captain of the burning ship. But most of the time the agent 'knows'. His energies are liberated for what he does. A novel, for instance, is a narration of what an agent does, which is not, most of the time, put the world in abeyance , much less put the whole world or the dimension of exposure in abeyance . Nor does the reader, who enters into the spacve of the novel, put his world in abeyance.

An example. Abraham , when ordered by God to leave his country, did not put the world in abeyance to inquire about the One who gave him the order; he obeyed the order. His faith consisted in intentional obeying, in his submissive response to the One who ordered. No inquiring objectification was involved here . Of course, after the event, Abraham could have made the statement: "God appeared to me and ordered me to leave my country. " But he did not mean it as a conclusion he reached based on 'evidence' after a process of inquiry without which the conclusion would be impossible, for Abraham never went through that process. Moreover, if he had gone through such process, he would have never obeyed. For that process would involve an objectification of the appearances--the phenomenon--with which Abraham was confronted. This is the "quaestio" of the medievals, which is about the phaenomenon, the 'appearances to be saved.' Supposedly this would lead to the existence of the One who appeared both for Abraham who was exposed and for anybody who is not exposed. What Abraham did, which was to obey, is not part of the equation, for the object so objectified is object for assent, not for response.

Here we come to the problem of speculative and practical reason . The model for speculative reason was geometry, where it is a matter of pure understanding and assent, for the geometrical object is not object of observation for response. This is the Aristotelian model for judgment. For Aris, the particular observable as such is not intelligible; only the particular as instantiation of the universal. This presupposes "fixity," which is found in the natural world (but not in the human world?). Now, to the transition to the world of exposure, for so far we have been out of it. Insofar as the obseved objects instantiate the universal and the universal is understood, what is explained is their behavior: what the explanation amounts to is the intelligible 'law' of their behavior which, if understood, gives the agent expectations about the object for response. Insofar as the agent has foreseeance, that is, insofar as the object has "fixity," the agent has control, in the sense of a definite notion of the object for response when confronted with it.

Here, however, a problem arises concerning the De Anima .

This book has to do with the characterization of the human being as having a 'nature.' But 'nature' is not understood, as it is in physics, as the law of behavior. For the 'law' of the behavior of the intentional agent is the intended object for response, the object orexically intended as, in the particular situation of the subject. This, in each case, is "particular" and, therefore, unintelligible, no object of epistéme. What, then, is Aris doing in the De Anima? From acts to potencies, from potencies to principles of explanation or archés. But explanations for whom? Not for the agent, for the explanation of his behavior is the object orexically intended-as in his situation. Not for other agents, for the same reason. So Aris is not treating the human being as object for response in any way, but as object of observation. But not as object of observation in its behavior, as Galileo did in physics. As what, then? As psychological object of 'observation'. Is this a pseudo-object? If so, why?

This seems to be connected with 'natural classes,' identifications of objects for response. "Homo" is object for response, like "lion" or "snake." The "snake" of ordinary language names in practical relation to us: a reptant that is poisonous and to be avoided, and good to be caught and eaten.

It is not considered in itself; its life, its 'ethos', interests the zoologist. In this sense, "snake" is object of manipulative or controlling interest, of not interest by itself. How does the Aristotelian "Homo" compare to this? Where does it fall? Not with "snake," for De Anima is neutral to desire; it deals with the thing in itself. Not with the snake of the zoologist, for Aris does not do what anthropologists do. Aris's "homo" is considered in itself, not in its relationship to the world he inhabits and to which he is orexically (dramatically?) related and exposed.

Comparing Aris's "Homo" to Sartre's we might come to something.

Sartre's homo is as much an abstraction as Aris's homo. But it is an abstraction within the order of exposure; Aris's is not.

S's homo, like Marx's, conceives projects to be realized, projects whose realization takes time and is always contingent, for it can be frustrated. [S's homo inhabits a world in general; Marx's inhabits a capitalistic world. Both inhabit their worlds as reflective adults, as partial initiators of traditions]. A's homo, by contrast, is an object exposed to nothing. From a man who actually desired something with an open future, A has made an object which has things called desires but who desires nothing and is a-temporal and, therefore, not contingent, who has nothing to do, who is not in the world and has no world in face of which to build his character by expressing himself as to be realized in his actions. This is the object of speculative reason--a spurious, bastard object, an observed behavior (phaenomenon) to which purely immanent experiences supposedly like mine are attached. Among the things this homo has, according to Aquinas, is "faith."

To spell this out in more detail. Sartre is after the body-subject. But first he criticizes the bastard object composed of my body as seen--not even seen, but ' objectified '--by others (which is not the body I live) and of the other's experiences or immanent acts. To this thing "body" I attribute "experiences." Or, as Skinner would say, behind this thing "behavior" I put the "inner man" who puppeteers the behavior. What is involved here is attaching to a thing a consciousness. But don't we do this in everyday life? No. I perceive your body--or rather you--as agent for whom I am agendus, and as agendus for myself agent; in short, as human for response, as transcendence capable of being transcended. A "body" is an abstraction and an objectification. But is not the body of which Sartre speaks--my body for me, my body for the other, my body for me as a result of my body for the other--an abstraction and an objectification? Yes, but of a different kind, for it has an implicit reference to the order of exposure, which can only be instantiated in a story written and heard as a story . For Sartre, as for Aris in the Ethics, a desire is not a feeling, it is an existential, orexic relationship to its object without which the desire is not only unintelligible but inconceivable, so much so that if the object were specified in a different way it would cease to be object-of-desire.

More detail is needed. Objects are not subjects because they do not encounter the world; they produce and undergo a succession of non-intended, non-desired changes. Animals intend and produce changes according to the standards of their species and, as 'individuals,' according to heredity, imitation, success and failure, and situation in which they find themselves. People, within limits, choose their character by choosing their world. The contingency of their achievements does not depend only on the world but also, partly, on them, on what they have made of themselves; and what they have made of themselves depends, even to a larger extent, on their culture and on themselves.

I return to abstraction and objectification. What is wrong with Aris's objectification of homo, and how does it differ from Sartre's? There is objectification in "illness" and there is objectification in "slavery." I approve of the former and condemn the latter. Why? Because slavery is a relationship of a human being to another human being; illness is not. Illness is tollenda, but it is not a human being; the slave is, and the slave is respectandus. Illoness happens to us; we make slavery happen. Understanding an illness is understandingits internal mechanics, the mechanics of its propagation and the mechanics of its cure, which are independent on wheter the illness is good or bad for us. But the cure of slavery is not independent from its being good or bad. As long as the powerful master grasps slavery as good it is incurable, for it is partially a willful illness of the master (favored or hindered by social institutions). But is not the definition of slavery the same for master or slave? Is it not objective? (This is the kind of definition Socrates (Plato) was looking for, even for the case of the good. Plato's Socrates was looking for definitions because, supposedly, the right definition would yield the essence of things, the essence of "aids", the essence of "slavery," that is, what corresponds in the observed thing to what is understood when the concept is understood. He did not distinguish between neutral and evaulative terms; he just assumed that the logic of evaluative terms could be reduced to the logic of neutral terms, that the logic of the good was the same as the logic of the true, and that the logic of evaluative terms was the same in the case of things like illness and of subjects like master.

The obvious rejoinder is that the logic of the good--whether reducible or not to the logic of the true, is subordinated to the latter. And that the logic of the true is value-neutral. Slavery is such and such a relationship, whether or not such relationship is good and whether it exists or not. This is the illusion. One thing is to stand in a certain relationship, quite another to observe and define the relationship. He who stands in the relationship of slavery stands as a master or as a slave and lives it from where he stands. He who observes does not. If he lives in a society in which there is slavery, he is either free or slave, and it is from there that he lives slavery, that he 'gathers the appearances.' But the appearances are not separable from the way in which slavery is lived, as servanda or delenda. This is what the definition does. It takes the relationship as lived by nobody, as a not lived relationship. The relationship is habitable only in two ways: as a master or as a slave. The definition is uninhabitable. It objectifies the relationship, that is, de-subjectifies it, transforms it into object of inquiry.

But is not the case of illness the same? And yet, the objectification works in that case. No, the case is not the same. Illness emerges as object as delenda. Descriptively it names the syndrome, composed of observable and experienced symptoms. The syndrome is delendum by finding its cause, which is delenda too.

Take away the undesirable character of the syndrome and its cause and the object illness does not emerge. Even if it emerged, without the undesirable character there would be no motivation to look for its cause. This objectification is not de-subjectifying in that the undesirability conditions the emergence of the object, motivates the inquiry, and the inquiry has a practical purpose: delenda. Illness is object of practical reason because it is undesirable through and through. It belongs in the order of exposure. If it enters the order of contemplation or inquiry, it enters because undesirable, and inquiry is conditioned by and subordinated to the order of exposure. "Slavery" would be like "illness" if it involved " undesirable relationship of ownership of one human being by another."

Yet the 'factual' content of "illness," that is, illness independent from desirability, is object of curiosity, of neutral inquiry, of speculative reason. In other words, the etiology of illness is independent from the undesirability of illness and from the possibility of curing it or not. Yes. But this is due to the fact that "illness" is not a subject but a thing. In oder words, its mode of acting does not involve intentionality. Illness does not intend to attack the patient or to profit from affecting the patient. The master desires theslave as a slave. The only cure for slavery, as far as the master is concerned, is his coming to respond to slavery as undesirable, because the etiology of slavery involves its desirability for someone.

Suppose that master and slave discuss slavery. They are talking about their reciprocal relationship. Are they talking about the same? It would seem so, since they would probably agree on the Searlean definition of slavery. This definition, however, abstracts from, or rather does not mention explicitly, desirability and undesirability; it is the name for a concept-a result of pure, neutral understanding-- the concept that an angel who is and can be neither slave nor master would have. This would be object of speculative inquiry, like the "piety" of Socrates. But master and slave are not discussing this object. They are discussing practical object, object to be responded to. Slavery names an encountered relationship between two human beings.Their definitions are intrinsically evaluative and opposite. Agreement, there, would consist in responses to slavery considered by both as appropriate responses, as desirable responses given the undesirability of slavery. And, if in that case, they talked with each other about slavery, their definition would sound very much Searlean without actually being Searlean, for it would be implicitly evaluative. This would become obvious if they interacted and discussed with someone who disagreed with them, that is who responded and considered appropriate responding to slavery in the opposite way. This is what happens in contemporary discussions of abortion. People try to reduce it to object of speculative inquiry by abstracting from their evaluative attitude toward abortion, but in vain, for the evaluative character of their definition pervades the defined, so that changing the defined wouyld amount to changing the evaluative character of the definition, much as the definitions of abortion differ according to the response the definer considers appropriate. In this sense, "God" is an evaluative term like "abortion" and "slavery," for before being object of reflective definition God is object of encounter, of exposure, object for response.

For the believer, for the Christian, God is adorandus, and this is either implicitly or explicitly reflected in the definition, much as Santa's definition is definition of expectandus.

This brings us back to Aris's "homo." As Aquinas argues in De Ente et Essentia , definitions express the essence or form by which a being falls under a genus and a differentia. But genus and differentia are logical notions, not ontological. Thus, Santa falls under the genus animale and the differentia rationale. Theindividuality of Sant is completely left out. In material creatures the individuality supposedly comes from matter--"materia quantitate signata." So Santa is an individual homo, different from other homines by materia. The definition of Santa, therefore, amounts to the definition of homo, plus a reference to his actual existence.

But is there such a thing as a definition of Santa? Santa is, to start with, a proper name. Are proper names, as such, susceptible of definition? If definition is of the purely intelligible, it does not seem so. ("Haec autem materia [quantitate signata] non ponitur in definitio] in definitione hominis, in quantum est homo, non ponitur, sed poneretur in definitione Socratis, si Socrates definitionem haberet" - De Ente et Essentia, 7).Yet common names are susceptible of definition. Why are common names susceptible and proper names (at least names of persons) not?

Let us start with common names. Common names are susceptible of definition. But of which definition: descriptive? explanatory? explanatory of what? As objects for response? As objects of inquiry?

Common names are names of classes of objects. But there are natural classes and artificial classes. Natural classes are of objects for response, not of objects of inquiry. In a society of blind people the words "light" and "color" would not exist. And, by the same token, there would be no inquiry about colors and light. The basic natural classes are persons, animals and things. There are as many subclasses as relevant distinctions for response.

Nihil in intellectu est nisi prius fuerit in sensu. But also, nihil in intellectu speculativo est nisi prius fuerit in sensu practico . There is no object for inquiry that is not related to object for response. But how does the transition occur? And what consequences does it entail?

In object for response in situation of encounter the question is how it will act in respect of me or of what I care for, given my project. But my project itself may fully depend on me or fully depend on the object, depending on respective power--who or which is more in control. All this is in relation to the surrounding world of exposure. Objectification for inquiry amounts to transformation of object for response into object in itself. Not like the Brittish captain, who remains exposed and in whose case it is just a matter of delayed response depending on further definition of object for response. But like Aris's "homo." Let us examine the lattter.

When I enconter this object in a certain situation of interaction I identify him as homo because of my practical expectations from him, which include practical beliefs 'about' him as homo, much as the captain encounters fire in his ship.

(A child who has no previous experience of exposure to fire has no practical expectations; if attracted by fire, the child will be burnt). Aris takes this practical expectations as part of the phaenomenon; this is what he calls "perception" --practical expectations of an object of exposure that define descriptively the object. This is why he argues against scepticism from the practice of people, not from their statements . But he neutralizes exposure and converts the object into object for inquiry. This is "objectification." He collects the observed in others and the observed self-experiences and hangs them on the name "homo" as attributes of any of the objects that have human shape, as the phaenomenon, the "appearances." But is this the same homo as the homo I meet in exposure?
