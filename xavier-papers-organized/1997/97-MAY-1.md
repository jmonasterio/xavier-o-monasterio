On the Notion of Truth and that Truth is One

As Aquinas realizes after Aristotle, the notion of truth is fundamentally linguistic. It applies exclusively to those intellectual acts which affirm or deny something, namely judgments, and to the speech acts in which they are expressed, namely, statements.

Yet, there is an anomaly. For both Aquinas and Aristotle (unlike Plato) grant that there is material truth in perception, and even in animal perception, which of course is not linguistic.

There is material truth because there is conformity of the perceiving mind or consciousness with the real, as shown by the appropriation of the action to the concretely encountered object. However, there is no formal truth in perception in that the perceiver grasps correctly the object but doesn't grasp the correctness, that is, the truth of its perception (much as, according to Kant, one can act in accordance with the law without necessarily knowing the law and acting out of respect for it).

This anomaly, however, is revealing in that it shows two different notions of truth, or at least a significant difference between truth and knowledge. Truth consists in the conformity of the mind to its object, but knowledge requires legitimate or justified realization of that conformity. This involves reflection. Knowledge, therefore, can obtain only in judgment, which is a reflective act in which sufficient reason for affirming or denying something is grasped (for that is what intellectual evidence amounts to)--and derivatively in the statements in which a judger expresses his or her judgments, that is, his or her commitment to the truth of what he or she states (which can always be supported by him or her with argument, that is, with the intellectual evidence or sufficient reason grasped by the judger).

The anomaly to which I alluded above is located here. It consists in the attribution of knowledge to animals, which have no intellect. The fact is that, for Aquinas and Aris, knowledge is identified both with material truth and with formal truth. However, since animals are non-linguistic creatures and cannot reflect, they know without knowing that they know.

What is of interest to me is that the conformity in which truth consists is of the order of vision by contrast to the order of action, and so is knowledge. Truth is the truth of a concrete, particular view of obhect, and knowledge is the realization of the justified, guaranteed of the truth of a concrete, particular view. Truth resides in the mind that conforms by seeing its object conformly, not by doing anything, and knowledge resides in the mind that sees or grasps the conformity of the concrete vision to its object, not in anything else or more than seeing. (As in the case of akrasia , the (practical) judgment is real knowledge in spite of the fact that the appropriate action that should follow in the case of a practical judgment doesn't follow. It is a correct view of the object but without motivating force).

We may see the point more clearly if we move to expressed judgment, that is, to statement as a speech act. The statement objectifies what is assentingly viewed by the judger. Nothing more or other than stating, whose material effect is what is stated, is required for the statement to be true or false, as nothing more than judging, whose intentional effect is what is judged, is required to have knowledge or lack thereof. Perceiving is seeing- as, a sort of inchoate judgment, and judging is committing oneself explicitly and rationally to what one sees as.

Given these notions of knowledge and truth, the relationship between knowledge and action is extrinsic, at least as far as knowledge is concerned, since knowledge is complete and selbständig without action. (Whether or not action is selbständig in  respect of knowledge, and how the passage can be effected from the order of seeing to the order of action--from "is" to "ought"--is another matter with which we have not to concern ourselves for the moment).

Because true statements conform to the reality stated and refer to it, it follows that the complete collection of true statements about the whole of reality would conform to the whole of reality and describe or state it as it is. It is from this conception that the idea that "the truth is one" originates. By that is meant that no true statement can contradict (or diverge from) another true statement, since each true statement expresses what is, and what is cannot at the same time not be. Thus if a true statement, that is, a statement that expresses what is, is contradicted by another statement, the latter cannot be true, for what is expressed in the former statement cannot fail to be. All true statements, therefore, have to be compatible with each other, for the truth is one, much as a photograph or picture of an entiure person is one, though each of the portions of the photograph or picture is different. This means, concretely, that all the true statements of physics have to be compatible with all the true perceptual statements, if indeed perceptual statements and the statements of physics are to be true. Precisely because of this unitary character of the truth we people are baffled when physicists tell us that the color we perceive doesn't exist as we perceive and state it. Similarly, given the unitary character of the truth, all the true statements of religion have to be compatible with all the true statements of, say, biology. Precisely because of this, we Christians are baffled and try to find a persuasive explanation when we are confronted with what we grasp as a contradiction between the statements of biologists and those of the first book of the Bible.

Here the unitary coherence is attributed to an already acquired and expressed corpus of knowledge--physics, biology, perception... It is something in the mind of knowers that is conform to reality. It is what commonsensical or specialized people think and say about reality.This knowledge is scientific or not depending on the way it is reached. The way in which the pieces of knowledge are reached and the principles by which they are organized into a coherent whole reflects on the character of the corpus, which is acordingly scientific or unscientific.

So far I have considered the notion of knowledge as vision. The correlate of the understanding of the knower as contemplator is a view of the real as mere object of contemplation, independently of its coefficient of adversity or favorableness to the agent exposed to it. However, there is an alternative possibility in which knowledge is intrinsically connected with action as a moment thereof. What are knowledge and truth in this other context?

We attribute knowledge to animals because they vary their actions appropriately to obtain their natural, given, desired goals.
