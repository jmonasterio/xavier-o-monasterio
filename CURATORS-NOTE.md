# Curator's Note

**Jorge Monasterio**
January 2026

---

My father, Xavier O. Monasterio, passed away in 2011, leaving behind decades of philosophical writing stored on aging computers and in formats that were becoming increasingly difficult to access. WordPerfect files from the 1990s, Microsoft Word documents scattered across backup folders, duplicate copies everywhere—the digital equivalent of boxes in an attic.

Over the years, colleagues who knew my father's work asked if his unpublished writings might be made available. He had continued thinking and writing long after his formal publications, and there was interest in what those later reflections contained. But the task of organizing 1,700+ files across obsolete formats felt overwhelming, and so it waited.

## Why Now

This project finally came together for two reasons.

First, the requests continued. People who had read my father's published work, or who remembered him from conferences and conversations, wanted access to the rest. His unpublished book manuscript on Aquinas and Aristotelianism, his continued engagement with Sartre and Camus, his notes and drafts—these deserved to be accessible rather than locked away in deteriorating file formats.

Second, I was curious whether AI tools had matured enough to assist with a project like this. What you see here is partly an experiment: could a large language model help catalog, deduplicate, convert, and organize a substantial archive of academic writing? Could it read WordPerfect files from 1991 and produce clean Markdown? Could it understand enough about the content to create useful indices and abstracts?

The answer, it turns out, is yes—with supervision. The AI handled the technical heavy lifting: converting formats, identifying duplicates, extracting dates from file metadata, categorizing documents by topic. But every significant decision required human judgment. What counts as a duplicate? Which version of a document should be kept? How should the collection be organized? These remained human choices.

## What This Collection Contains

The organized archive spans roughly 1989 to 2009 and includes:

- **An unpublished book manuscript** (8 chapters) arguing that Aquinas's Aristotelian synthesis fundamentally altered Christianity's understanding of faith
- **Major essays** on violence, bad faith, and related themes
- **Course materials** from decades of teaching philosophy at the University of Dayton
- **Working notes and drafts** showing a mind still actively engaged with the questions that drove his published work
- **Bibliography and vita** documenting his scholarly career

The documents have been converted to Markdown format for long-term accessibility and organized both chronologically and by topic.

## What Might My Father Have Thought?

I've wondered what my father might have made of an AI reading his work and "thinking" about it.

His unpublished book manuscript argues that Aquinas's error was reducing faith to intellectual assent—treating what should be a total personal relationship with God as primarily a matter of propositional knowledge. The eighth chapter, his most philosophical, critiques John Searle's theory that understanding is separable from response. My father insisted that understanding and responding are inseparable: "being a competent linguistic agent is being capable of grasping appropriately the performative force of pieces of language... not... getting a piece of information about your intentions."

An AI can extract information from his texts, identify patterns, generate summaries. It can recognize that COLLOQUI.md discusses Aquinas and categorize it accordingly. But it cannot be moved by his arguments, cannot take a stance for or against them, cannot enter into the dramatic relationship with ideas that he saw as essential to genuine understanding.

He might have found this philosophically interesting. The AI is, in a sense, Searle's ideal reader—grasping the "illocutionary force" (the propositional content) while remaining neutral to the "perlocutionary effects" (being moved, responding). My father argued such a reader doesn't truly understand at all. Yet here is an AI that clearly does *something* with language. What is that something? Is it understanding? A kind of understanding? Or something else entirely?

He might have been intrigued by the question, even as he remained skeptical of calling it understanding in the full sense. And he might have appreciated the irony: using a tool he'd consider philosophically limited to make his arguments against that very limitation more accessible to human readers who *can* engage them fully.

I suspect he might have approved of this project, not despite the AI's involvement but because of the clarity it brings to what understanding requires. The AI can organize his papers. Only you, the reader, can understand them.
