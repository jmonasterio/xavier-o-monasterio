"After Vritue" and Narrative Structure

(Chapter 15 of After Virtue, pp. 191-209)

"...Mac Intyre quietly drops After Virtue 's interesting account of the importance of story-telling in giving moral terms their meaning." (M. Nussbaum, The NYR, p.38).

"It is by all means a dubious thing to depend and rest on what an author himself has brought to the forefront. The important thing is rather to give attention to those things he left shrouded in silence.' Such was the methodological advice given in 1924 by Heidegger.

Narrative and selfhood

An action is always an episode in a history:

The modern conception of selfhood, atomistic and episodic, deprives the self from the arena of social relationships in which the Aristotelian virtues function. The self thus conceived cannot be the bearer of the Aristotelian virtues. Interest in the virtues demands a concomitant concept of selfhood--a concept of self whose unity resides in the unity of a narrative which links birth to life to death as a narrative beginning to middle to end . Such concept played a key part in cultures which are the predecessors of our own and is still implict in many of our ways of thinking and acting . For we think of the self in a narrative mode. We cannot characterize behavior independently of intentions, and we cannot characterize intentions independently of the (social) settings which make those intentions intelligible both to the agents themselves and to others. Without the setting and its changes through time the history of the individual agent and his changes through time will be unintelligible.

Where intentions are concerned , we need to know what certain of his beliefs are (that gardening is healthy exercise and pleases his wife), and which of them are causally effective (would he go on gardening if it did not please his wife?). And, in a set of compatible answers to the question "What is he doing?", we need to know how the shorter term intentions are related to the longer. Once again we are involved in writing a narrative history. Intentions thus need to be ordered both causally and temporally, and both orderings will make reference to settings. There is no such thing as 'behavior' (as distinct from physical movement), to be identified prior to and independently of intentions, beliefs and settings.

Interrelationships of the intentional, the social and the historical . We identify a particular action only by invoking two kinds of context, implicitly if not explicitly. We place the agent's intentions in causal and temporal order with reference to their role in his or her history; and we also place them with reference to their role in the history of the setting or settings to which they belong . In determining what causal efficacy the agent's intentions had in one or more directions, and how his short-term intentions succeeded or failed to be constitutive of long term intentions, we ourselves write a further part of these histories. Narrative history of a certain kind turns out to be the basic and essential genre for the charactrisation of human actions.

Analytic philosophers see a course of human events as a complex sequence of individual actions, where an individuated action is composed of elements in a sequence like a recipe. But performing according to a recipe requires an appropriate context to be intelligible. Intelligibility is intrinsic to action. Unintelligible actions are failed candidates for the status of (intelligible) actions. The concept of intelligibility is closely related to the fact that the most basic distinction of all embedded in our discourse and our practice in this area is that between human beings and other beings. Human beings can be held to account for that of which they are the authors; other beings cannot. To identify an occurrence as an action is in the paradigmatic instances to identify it under a type of description which enables us to see that occurrence as flowing intelligibly from a human agent's intentions, motives, passions and purposes. It is therefore to understand an action as something for which someone is accountable, about which it is always appropriate to ask the agent for an intelligible account. When an occurrence is apparently the intended action of a human agent, but nonetheles we cannot so identify it, we are both intellectually and practically baffled. We do not know how to respond; we do not know how to explain. In each case an occurrence or an utterance becomes intelligible by finding its place in a narrative .

To this it may be replied that the supplying of a narrative is not necessary, for speech-acts and purposes suffice. But speech acts and purposes too can be intelligible or unintelligible. Both require contexts . [INTELLIGIBLE SETTINGS BY REFERENCE TO WHICH HUMAN ACTIONS AND HUMAN TRANSACTIONS ACQUIRE GENERIC IDENTITIES, SUCH AS CONVERSATIONS, BATTLES, CHESS GAMES, COURTHSIPS, PHILOSOPHY SEMINARS, ETC.].

Human actions and transactions are enacted narratives; they possess a narrative form, a basically historical character . It is because we all live out narratives in our lives and because we understand our own lives in terms of the narratives that we live out that the form of narrative is appropriate for understanding the actions of others. Stories are lived before they are told, except in the case of fiction.

To the contrary Mink : "Stories are not lived but told . Life has no beginnings, middles, or ends. There are meetings, but the start of an affair belongs to the story we tell ourselves later... There are hopes, plans, battles and ideas, but only in retrospective stories are hopes unfulfilled, plans miscarried, battles decisive, and ideas seminal.

Answer : Only retrospectively can hopes be characterized as unfulfilled, but we so characterize them in life as much as in art. Final partings do not take place only in stories. Have you never heard of death? What is true is that in taking an event as a beginning or an ending we bestow a significance upon it which may be debatable. The same with genres and with the embedding of one history or narrative in another . But it makes sense to ask who is right. In the case of Thomas Becket, "the answer appears to be clearly that the true genre of his life is neither hagiography nor saga, but tragedy. So of such modern narrative subjects as the life of Trotsky or Lenin, of the history of the Soviet Communist Party or the American Presidency, we may also ask: To what genre does their history belong? And this is the same question as: What type of account of their history will be both true and intelligible?

[CONFUSES DRAMATIC WITH A-DRAMATIC NARRATIVES AND INTELLIGIBLE ACCOUNTS WITH JUSTIFIED ORACCEPTABLE ACCOUNTS].

What an agent is able to do and say intelligibly as an actor is deeply affected by the fact that we are never more (and sometimes less) than the co-authors of our own narratives. Only in phantasy do we live what story we please . We are always under certain constraints. We enter upon a stage which we did not design and we find ourselves part of an action that was not of our own making. Each of us being a main character in his own drama plays subordinate parts in the dramas of others, and each drama constrains the others. Each of our dramas exerts constraints on each other's making the whole different from the parts, but still dramatic. An action, then, is a moment in a possible or actual history or in a number of such histories. The notion of a history is as fundamental as the notion of an action. Each requires the other . Sartre denies this.

Sartre makes Roquentin argue not only that narrative is very different from life, but that to present human life in the form of a narrative is always to falsify it. There are not and cannot be true stories, for human life is composed of discrete actions which lead nowhere, which have no order.

Answer : (1) Human actions deprived of any falsifying narrative would be like Dr Johnson's diary: the disjointed parts of some possible narrative. (2) What I have called a history is an enacted dramatic narrative in which the characters are also the authors. The difference between imaginary characters and real ones is not in the narrative form of what they do; it is in the degree of their authorship of that form and of their own deeds, which is constrained by the actions of others and by the social settings presupposed in their actions. Marx saw this clearly, but forgot that in an enacted dramatic narrative we do not know what will happen next, though the empirical generalisations which social scientists discover provide a kind of understanding of human life that is compatible with that structure .

This unpredictability coexists with a crucial characteristic of all lived narratives: a certain teleological character . We live our lives in the light of certain conceptions of a possible and shared future, a future in which certain possibilities beckon us forward and others repel us. There is no present which is not informed by some image of the future which always presents itself in the form of a telos--of a variety of ends or goals towards wich we are either moving or failing to move in the present . Thus the narratives which we live out have both an unpredictable and a partially teleological character . It is always the case that both there are constraints on how the story can continue and that within these constraints there are indefinitely many ways that it can continue.

A central thesis begins to emerge: man is in his actions and practice, as well as in his fictions, essentially a story-telling animal . He becomes through his history a teller of stories that aspire to truth. I can only answer the question "What am I to do?" if I can answer the prior question "Of what story or stories do I find myself a part?" We enter human society with imputed characters, roles into which we have been drafted. We learn what those roles are through the exemplary stories of our culture, through the stock of stories which constitute its dramatic resources . That moral tradition is right which takes the telling of stories as a key part in educating us into virtues.

The narrative concept of selfhood

I suggested earlier that 'an' action is always an episode in a possible history; I would now like to make a related suggestion about the concept of personal identity . What is crucial to human beings as characters in enacted narratives is that, possessing only the resources of psychological continuity, we have to be able to respond to the imputation of strict identity. I am forever whatever I have been at any time for others--and I may at any time be called upon to answer for it, no matter how changed I may be now. There is no way of founding my identity--or lack of it--on the psychological continuity or discontinuity of the self. The self inhabits a character whose unity is given as the unity of a character--pace the empiricists or analytical philosophers. The latter lack something which makes the problem insoluble: the background provided by the concept of a story and by the continuity of character that a story requires. Just as a history is not a sequence of actions but a moment in an actual or possible history abstracted for some purpose from that history, so the characters in a history are not a collection of persons, but the concept of a person is that of a character abstracted from a history .

The narrative concept of selfhood requires two tings:

(1) I am what I may justifiably be taken by others to be in the course of living out a story that runs from my birth to my death, namely, the subject of a history that is my own and no one else's and that has its own peculiar meaning . People whose life does not move toward a climax or telos, who see no point to do one thing rather than another, lack meaning. To be the subject of a narrative that runs from one's birth to death is to be accountable for the actions and experiences which compose a narratable life, to be able to give an intelligible narrative account enabling others to understand how one could at different times and places be one and the same persons and yet be differently characterized. Personal identity is the identity presupposed by the unity of the character which the unity of a narrative requires. Without such unity there would not be subjects of whom stories could be told.

(2) Secondly and correlatively I am one who can always ask others for an account . I am part of their stories as they are part of mine. Asking you what you did and why, saying what I did and why, pondering the differences between your account of what I did and my account of what I did, and vice versa, these are essential constituents of narratives.

N. B. There is mutual presupposition between the concepts of narrative, intelligibility and accountability and the concept of personal identity. The attempt to elucidate the notion of personal identity independently of the notions of narrative, intelligibility and accountability is bound to fail .

In what does the unity of an individual life consist ? It is the unity of a narrative embodied in a single life. To ask what is the good for me is to ask how best I might live out that unity and bring it to completion. To ask what is the good for man is to ask what all answers to the former question must have in common.

The unity of a human life is the unity of a narrative quest. A quest for what? Two features of the medieval conception of a quest need to be recalled: (1) Without some at least partly determined conception of the final telos there cannot be any beginning to a quest. Some conception of the good for man is required. Whence is such conception to be drawn? From those questions which led us to attempt to transcend that limited conception of the virtues which is available in and trhough practices. It is in looking for a conception of the good which will enable us to extend our understanding of the purpose and content of the virtues that we initially define the kind of life which is a quest for the good. (2) The medieval conception of a quest is not that of a search for something already adequately characteirzed, as miners search for gold. It is in the course of the quest, in actual coping with the world, that the goal of the quest is finally understood. A quest is an education both as to the character of that which is sought and in self-knowledge. The virtues are those disspositions which will not only sustain practices and enable us to achieve the goods internal to practices, but which will also sustain us in the relevant kind of quest for the good and which will furnish us with increasing self knowledge and increasing knowledge of the good.

But I am never able to seek for the good or exercise of the virtues only qua individual, because we all approach our own circumstances as bearers of a particular social identity . I am a member of a family, of a profession, of a city, of a country. Hence what is good for me is what is good for the one who inhabits these roles. These constitute the given of my life, my moral starting point.

Modern individualism rejects this . From Sartre 's or Goffman's perspective a self has no history. The contrast with the narrative view of the self is clear, for my story is always embedded in the story of those communities from which I derive my identity. The possession of an historical identity and the possession of a social identity coincide. NOTICE THAT REBELLION AGAINST MY IDENTITY IS ALWAYS ONE POSSIBLE MODE OF EXPRESSING IT.

THE FACT THAT THE SELF HAS TO FIND ITS MORAL IDENTITY IN AND THROUGH ITS MEMBERSHIP IN COMMUNITIES DOES NOT ENTAIL THAT THE SELF HAS TO ACCEPT THE MORAL LIMITATIONS OF THE PARTICULARITY OF THOSE COMMUNITIES. Whithout such particularities there would be nowhere to begin; but it is in moving forward from such particularities that the search for the good consists. Yet particularity can never be simply left behind or obliterated. The notion of escaping from it into a realm of entirely universal maxims which belong to man as such (in its Kantian form or in the form of some modern analytical moral philosophies) is an illusion .

What a practice is depends on a mode of understanding it which has been transmitted often through many generations. Insofar as the virtues sustain the relationships required for practices they have to sustain relationships to the past and to the future as well as in the present. But the traditions through which particular practices are transmitted and reshaped never exist in isolation form larger traditions. What constitutes such traditions?

Against Burke: (1) Tradition is not opposite to reason, for all reasoning takes place within the context of some traditional mode of thought, transcending through criticism and invention the limitations of what had hitherto been reasoned in that tradition.

(2) Tradition is not opposite to conflict, for all tradition in good order is always partially constituted by an argument about the goods the purusit of which gives to that tradition its particular point and purpose. Traditions, when vital, embody continuities of conflict. When a tradition becomes Burkean, it is always dying or dead.

Within a tradition the pursuit of goods extends through generations. Hence the individual's search for his-her good is characteristically conducted within a context defined by those traditions of which the individual's life is a part. Once again the narrative phenomenon of embedding is crucial.

Traditions decay and disappear. What sustains and strenthens traditions? What weakens and destroys them? The exercise or lack of exercise of the relevant virtues. Lack of justice, of truthfulness, of courage, of the relevant intellectual virtues corrupt traditions as well as those institutions and practices wich derive their life from the traditions of which they are the contemporary embodiements. Hence an additional virtue is that of having an adequate sense of the traditions to which one belongs or by which one is confronted. An adequate sense of tradition manifests itself in a grasp of those future possibilities which the past has made available to the present. Living traditions, because they continue a not-yet completed narrative, confront a future whose determinate and determinable character derives from the past.

Tragic choices :

Virtues enable their possessors to pursue both their own good and the good of the tradition of which they are the bearers even in situations defined by the necessity of tragic, dilemmatic choice. Such tragic choices, understood in the context of the tradition of the virtues, are very different from those which face the modern adherents of rival and incommensurable moral premises. Wherein does the difference lie?

J. L. Austin suggests that EITHER we can admit the existence of rival and contingently incompatible goods which make incompatible claims to our practical allegiance, OR we can believe in some determinate conception of the good for man. WHAT THIS CONTENTION IS BLIND TO IS THAT THERE MAY BE BETTER OR WORSE WAYS FOR INDIVIDUALS TO LIVE THROUGH THE TRAGIC CONFRONTATION OF GOOD WITH GOOD. AND THAT TO KNOW WHAT THE GOOD LIFE FOR MAN IS MAY REQUIRE KNOWEING WHAT ARE THE BETTER AND WHAT ARE THE WORSE WAYS OF LIVING IN AND THROUGH SUCH SITUATIONS. NOTHING A PRIORI RULES OUT THIS POSSIBILITY. WITHIN A VIEW SUCH AS AUSTIN'S THERE IS A CONCEALED AND UNACKNOWLEDGED EMPIRICAL PREMISE ABOUT THE CHARACTER OF TRAGIC SITUATIONS .

One way in which the choice between rival goods in a tragic situation differs from modern choice between incommensurable moral premises is that BOTH of the alternative courses of action which confront the individual have to be recognized as leading to some authentic and substantial good. Whatever I do, I shall have left undone what I ought to have done. THE TRAGIC PROTAGONIST, UNLIKE THE MORAL AGENT AS DEPICTED BY SARTRE OR HARE, IS NOT CHOOSING BETWEEN ALLLEGIANCE TO ONE MORAL PRINCIPLE RATHER THAN ANOTHER, NOR IS HE DECIDING UPON SOME PRINCIPLE OF PRIORITY BETWEEN MORAL PRINCIPLES . HENCE THE 'OUGHT' INVOLVED HAS A DIFFERENT MEANING AND FORCE FROM THAT OF THE 'OGHT' IN MORAL PRINCIPLES UNDERSTOOD IN A MODERN WAY . AND ANY ATTEMPT TO MAP THE LOGIC OF SUCH 'OUGHT' ASSERTIONS ON TO SOME MODAL CALCULUS SO AS TO PRODUCE A VERSION OF DEONTIC LOGIC HAS TO FAIL . ( See Bas C. Van Fraassen1973 ). Yet it is clear that the moral task of the tragic protagonist may be performed better or worse, independently of the choice between alternatives that he makes. He may behave heroically or unheroically, generously or ungenerously, prudently or imprudently.

What, then, about the objectivity of moral judgments? The existence of tragic dilemmas does not detract from the thesis that assertions of the form 'To do this in this way would be better for X and/or his family, city or profession' are susceptible of objective truth and falsity, any more than the existence of alternative and contingently incompatible forms of medical treatment casts doubt on the theis that assertions of the form 'To undergo this medical treatment in this way would be better for X and/or his familty' are susceptible of objective truth and falsity. ( See the illuminating discussion in Samuel Guttenplan 1979-80, pp. 61-80 ). The presupposition of this objectivity is of course that we can understand the notion of 'good for X' in terms of some conception of the unity of X's life. What is better or worse for X depends on the caracter of that intelligible narrative which provides X's life with its unity. It is the lack of any such unifying conception of a human life which underlies modern denials of the factual character of moral judgements and more especially of those judgments which ascribe virtues or vices to individuals .

Conclusion

Every moral philo has some particular sociology as its counterpart (see pp. 22, 58, 70, 116, 118, 119, 215). I have spelled out in this chapter the kind of understanding of social life which the tradition of the virtues requires--a kind of understanding very different from those dominant in the culture of bureaucratic individualism. Within that culture conceptions of the virtues become marginal and the tradition of the virtues remains central only in the lives of social groups whose existence is on the margins of the central culture. Within the central culture of liberal or bureaucratic individualism new conceptions of the virtues emerge and the concept of a virtue is itself transformed. To the history of that transformation I therefore now turn.
