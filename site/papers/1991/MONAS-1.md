                                  MONAS 1

      It is my  conviction  that  the  medieval  controversy  and  its  far-
reaching  consequences  cannot  be  fully  understood  and  appreciated  but
against the background of a particular, and  particularly  complex,  problem
inherent in Aristotle's views. In this chapter I will try to determine  that
problem.
Speculative and practical knowledge in Aristotle
      I take my starting point in Devereux's  interpretation  of  Aristotle,
which I find peruasive. As it is universally accepted, Aristotle  cannot  be
fully understood but by reference to Plato. Now, in  the  middle  dialogues,
especially in the Republic,
      "The form of the good serves  a  double  function:  it  is  the  first
      principle of ethics, and also the foundation of metaphysics insofar as
      it is the basis of our understanding  of  reality  as  such.  Platonic
      dialectic is a confusing blend of practical and theoretical. Questions
      about value and how we ought to act are not regarded as  different  in
      kind from questions about the ultimate nature  of  reality.  The  same
      method of treatment  is  apparently  appropriate  for  both  types  of
      question" (Rev. of Metaphysics 39 (March l986): 483.
      By contrast,
      "Aristotle  thought  his  predecessors  in  general,  and   Plato   in
      particular, made a serious mistake in failing to mark  the  boundaries
      separating  the  different  sciences  and  branches  of  philosophical
      inquiry. All of them  failed  to  grasp  the  fundamental  distinction
      between practical and theoretical knowledge. Ethics and politics,  the
      prime examples of practical knowledge, differ  from  such  theoretical
      sciences as metaphysics and physics not only  in  their  aims  but  in
      their methods and subject matter as  well.  Indeed,  Aristotle  thinks
      that the differences are such that  we  cannot  regard  practical  and
      theoretical knowledge as two species of a single genus, for  there  is
      no common definition of knowledge which applies to both (ibid.).
      For my purposes, it is important to  keep  in  mind  that  Aristotle's
position  on  this  issue  was  reached  only  gradually,  as  suggested  by
important differences between EE and NE
(see ibid., pp. 502-504).  Moreover,  there  was  a  moment  in  Aristotle's
intellectual evolution when he himself--like Plato, though not to  the  same
extent that Plato in the middle  dialogues--considered  practical  knowledge
as a variety of theoretical knowledge. In Devereux words,
      "He eventually came to believe that action is both the  goal  and  the
      subject mater  of  phrónesis;  and  since  action  is  concerned  with
      particulars, phronesis must be primarily about particulars rather than
      universals... The notion of practical knowledge did not spring up  all
      at once, fully formed; there was a  time  when  he  thought  practical
      knowledge was much like theoretical, the only important contrast being
      the different aims of each" (ibid., pp. 483-4).
       Devereux  concludes  his  article  with  the  following  enlightening
comment:
      "Faithful adherents of Plato's account of phrónesis  in  the  Republic
      must have regarded Aristotle's innovations as somewhat perverse.  Both
      philosophers use the term to refer to that knowledge or  wisdom  which
      they regard as the most important, the most valuable in guiding  human
      affairs. But for Plato the  objects  of  this  knowledge  are  sharply
      distinguished from the objects of opinion--the particulars we find  in
      everyday experience, things which are infinitely complex and  variable
      and thus not graspable by the intellect. Plato speaks  with  scorn  of
      those  who  concern  themselves  with  such  objects;  one  who  seeks
      phrónesis turns away from the world of  particulars  and  directs  his
      thought towards that which is eternal and unchanging. Aristotle  turns
      this Platonic conception upside down. For  him,  phrónesis  is  not  a
      science or epistéme; it is primarily concerned  with  the  objects  of
      opinion--the complex and  ever-changing  particulars  which  we  grasp
      through experience. Knowledge of that which is eternal and  unchanging
      is set apart and given a different name (sophía); such  knowledge  has
      no bearing in practical affairs--it would be of no use to a  statesman
      or ruler" (ibid., p. 504).
      Much as I have learned from Devereux's treatment of the complex  issue
of practical knowledge in Aristotle, I cannot help but  suspecting  that  he
exaggerates the rift between Aristotle and  Plato.  In  my  view,  Aristotle
remained till the end a half-hearted Platonist, with  consequences  for  the
future of philosophy from which we are still suffering.
      I will begin to make my position  more  clear  by  zeroing-in  on  two
sentences of Devereux's with which I find myself  in  serious  disagreement.
"Aristotle," he tells us, turns this Platonic conception  upside  down."  It
seems to me, however, that, most unfortunately, Aristotle did  not  go  that
far. Rather, with the important modifications that  everybody  admits,  such
as bringing the intelligible forms to  earth  from  the  heaven  of  eternal
Ideas, Aristotle kept Platonism alive  in  his  reverence  for  and  intense
cultivation of epistéme. I will be  my  point  that  the  phrónesis  of  the
Nicomachean  Ethics  and  the  Politics  was  modelled  after  epistéme  and
constituted a degraded--and, in  my  view,  spurious--version  of  it.  This
brings me to the second of Devereux's sentences  by  which  I  am  bothered:
"Indeed,  Aristotle  thinks  that  the  differences  [between   ethics   and
politics, the prime examples of practical knowledge,  and  such  theoretical
sciences as  metaphysics  and  physics]  are  such  that  we  cannot  regard
practical and theoretical knowledge as two species of a  single  genus,  for
there is no common definition of  knowledge  which  applies  to  both."  The
explanation of my disagreement with Devereux  on  this  precise  point  will
take us to the heart of the problem with which I am really concerned.
      One point seems to me beyond dispute and is certainly not disputed by
Devereux: Aristotle never gave up on the possibility of epistéme or
scientific knowledge within its proper domain, that is, in such theoretical
sciences as metaphysics and physics, not to mention mathematics.(Certain
people, like Nussbaum, seem to find disputable this point. Later on I will
come to their views). This is clearly the situation presupposed by EN, for
practical knowledge is characterized by contrast to theoretical knowledge.
Practical knowledge, observes Aristotle, does not deal, as the theoretical
sciences do, with objects "whose principles cannot be other than they are,"
but with what is variable and contingent (note 4). Furthermore, the
theoretical sciences concern themselves only with universals, whereas the
practical sciences concern themselves with particulars (note 5). The fact,
therefore, that there are practical sciences where epistéme is impossible
does not detract, in Aristotle's eyes, from the fact that epistéme is
attainable in other domains. And what epistéme means is knowledge of the
purely intelligible, of the universal, of what is necessarily what it is.
Actions, by contrast, which are particular and contingent cannot be objects
of epistéme; only of phrónesis. For, as underscored by Devereux, in the
Posterior Analytics, where Aristotle defines epistéme, he repeatedly
remarks that the particular cannot be object of epistéme (note 13).
      Thus, admittedly, epistéme and phrónesis constitute two very
different kinds of knowledge. The question is whether for Aristotle, as
Devereux thinks, they are so different as not to fall under the same genus.
Nowhere, to my knowledge, do we find such a statement in Aristotle's works.
Now, this is a point that could hardly have escaped Aristotle, given his
habitual precision and its obvious relevance when, as it is the case in EN,
he is intent in making clear the difference between theoretical and
practical knowledge. The omission, therefore, is itself significant, for it
seems to suggest that Aristotle did not think what Devereux attributes to
him, or at least to his views as a clear implication.
      A second reason for my disagreement with Devereux is that I find it
difficult to place within the Aristotelian paradigm a knowledge that would
be human knowledge without falling under the same genus under which
epistéme falls. Knowledge is, for Aristotle, adaequatio mentis rei. The
fact that Aristotle deals with what for Plato would have been objects of
opinion--"the particulars we encounter in everyday experience, things which
are infinitely complex and variable and thus not graspable by the
intellect," as correctly characterized by Devereux--does not imply that
Aristotle considers them unknowable, as Plato does. Were that the case,
Aristotle would have said, not only that there is no epistéme, no
scientific knowledge, of actions, as he does, but that there is no
phrónesis of actions either, which would have rendered absurd his whole
project in the Ethics and the Politics. Now, if as Devereux and I agree,
Aristotle considers Phrónesis as knowledge, it is because he thinks that it
will lead him to an adaequatio mentis rei ethicae sive politicae. Granted,
the nature of the res ethica is such that it does not lend itself to the
purely intellectual grasp the res physica does, but only to a grasp through
an aiszésis  developed thanks to experience. This, indeed, makes makes
phrónesis different from  epistéme.. But this does not imply that there is
no adaequatio of the knower's mind to the thing known. Now, if because the
avowedly significant differences between the two forms of knowledge
Devereux prefers not to classify phrónesis under the same genus as
epistéme, but grants that for Aristotle in the case of phrónesis there is a
special adaequatio mentis rei by which the res ethica becomes known to the
knower, our quabble is merely about words. For my point is that, in
considering phrónesis as knowledge while underscoring its differences with
epistéme, Aristotle has not changed his idea of what knowledge as such is,
though he has distanced himself from Plato by not equating the knowable
with the purely intelligible.
      Had Aristotle changed his mind about what knowledge is he would have
felt forced to revise, or at least to enlarge, his notion of truth, which
is for him an exclusive predicate of judgments and consists in the
realization by the knower that
the object judged is so as judged.
      Finally--and this is particularly important--the difference between
epistéme and phrónesis does not prevent Aristotle from comparing the latter
to the former and finding the latter inferior to the former. Inferior in
what? Not in the fact that in in the case of the former there is adaequatio
mentis rei but not in the latter, for in that case there would be no reason
to consider phrónesis as a form of knowledge, but in the fact that
phrónesis, by the nature of its object, cannot possibly reach the necessity
and universality of scientific knowledge. What makes possible the
comparison as well as the gradation is the idea of knowledge as adaequatio
mentis rei, which is not at stake, for Aristotle does not think that ethics
and politics are the domain of opinions. Such comparisons are not
infrequent in Aristotle's works, even outside the Ethics and give rise to
different taxonomies of the sciences, all of them Aristotelian, depending
on whether Aristotle is considering the nobility of the object known, its
universality or all-pervasiveness (being), etc. An interesting example of
this in the Rhethoric is the comparison between poetry and history, in
which the latter comes as the loser in terms of the particularity and
contingency of its subject-matter.
      In find an indirect confirmation of my view on Aristotle in
Nussbaum's confident affirmation that Aristotle is not an ethical
relativist, that he is aiming in the Ethics at the knowledge of the one
ordering of human desires that "saves the appearances," much as he looks in
the De Motu and the De Anima for the one ordering of motion in animates
that "saves the appearances." This, unless I am badly mistaken, is why
Nussbaum speaks of Aristotle's "scientific ethics of the non-scientific."
In other words, it is not the scientific project that changes between the
theoretical and the practical works, but the accommodation of the project,
necessitated by the nature of the objects to be known.
The desire for understanding which opens the Metaphysics and which subtends
Aristotle's entire project remains the same.
      And yet... Yet, I think that Devereux hints at an extremely important
point in his denial that epistéme and phrónesis can fall under the same
genus, as I will explain below. But that is something Aristole did not
himself realize, precisely because he did not turn Plato upside down,
because he remained a half-Platonist, because all he did was to build a
half-house between Platonism and a genuine alternative to Platonism.
Nussbaum's Aristotle
      Nussbaum's study of Aristotle in The Fragility of Goodness is far
larger in scope than Devereux's article. The latter is only attempting to
clarify Aristotle's mature position concerning practical knowledge. The
former is much more encompassing and, consequently, much more complex. I
will not even try to summarize her main moves. Rather, I will confine
myself to those points which are important for my purposes.
      The central point, already alluded to, is that the project of
Aristotle in his scientific works, such as the Metaphysics, the De Anima,
the De Motu, etc., remains the same in his practical works, such as the two
Ethics and the Politics, namely, the "saving of appearances," that is, an
intelligible order of the experienced object at stake that preserves the
experienced and reveals its intelligibility. In this way, according to
Nussbaum, not only has Aristotle resolutely placed the Forms within the
universe of human experience, thereby rejecting the Platonic move away from
experience toward a transcendent beyond, but in the realm of practical
reason he has definitely opted for a species-related notion of the good,
unlike Plato, for whom the good was conceivable from a transcendent, divine
point of view. In this sense, the project of the practical works is
"anthropocentric in a stronger sense" than the project in the scientific
works. This explains the all-important difference between the two kinds of
works.
      I think that Nussbaum is fundamentally right about Aristotle as well
as in her personal rejection of Platonism. I think, too, however, that she
is wrong in her adoption of the Aristotelian project in ethical matters,
precisely because, contrary to what she and Deveruex think, Aristotle did
not turn Plato upside down. In other words, Aristotle's project in the
ethics, which is the one so perspicuously identified by Nussbaum, is still
a half-Platonic project. How so?
      A fundamental point to be kept in mind throughout this discussion is
that my argument rests on the basis, common to Nussbaum and myself, that
the Aristotelian project in the ethical works remains the same as the
project in the scientific works, so that the clear and significant
differences between the scientific and the ethical works depend on the
significant differences between the res ethica and what we might call the
res scientifica.
      What is the project of the scientific works? The knowledge of reality
as intelligible, that is, of what is possible, of what is impossible, given
the actual, and of what is intelligible in the actual. This is the field of
speculative understanding, characterized by the necessity and the
universality of the intelligible. This is the project of epistéme, defined
in the Posterior Analytics and carried out in the scientific works. What
characterizes speculative understanding is that knowledge is pursued for
its own sake.
      The project of the ethical works is explicitly defined by Aristotle
as practical, as destined to terminate in action, in the best concretely
possible action (see quotation in Devfereux p. 494, note 31). It seems,
therefore, that  Nussbaum is dead wrong in reducing the project of the
ethical works to the project of the scientific works. But is she?
      One thing is what one believes one is doing, another what one is
actually doing. This is particularly true in intellectual matters. What
Aristotle believes he is doing in his ethical works is pursuing knowledge
in view of action, not in view of contemplation, and knowledge capable of
resulting in action. I do not think, however, that this is what he actually
does. What he actually does is, as Nussbaum herself believes, to pursue the
same sort of knowledge he pursues in his scientific works, insofar as such
knowledge can be reached in matters related to action. And because that is
indeed what Aristotle does, I maintain that Aristotle remained a Platonist.
      Now, assuming that I am right, which of course remains to be seen,
how could Aristotle fall pray to such confusion? And how can Nussbaum
continue to be confused after him? Because both Aristotle and Nussbaum
share the only view of knowledge and of truth Aristotle has, the view
presented in the Posterior Analytics, a view of knowledge and of truth
which is modelled after scientific knowledge, after epistéme, not after
phrónesis.
And it is in this connection that I find Devereux's view extremely
enlightening. He sees what neither Aristotle nor Nussbaum realize: that if
epistéme is knowledge and phrónesis is knowledge, then they are of such a
different kind that they do not fall under the same genus, for they are not
susceptible of being defined in the same way. In other words, if Devereux
is right on this point, as I think he is, using the same word, "knowledge,"
to name things that are different amounts to equivocating. And the
equivocation is not without consequences. For, if "knowledge" is defined as
epistéme, then phrónesis is not knowledge. And, if "knowledge" is defined
as phrónesis, then epistéme is not knowledge. The latter,k however, is
something that Aristolte never did or attempted to do. But he attempted to
do the former. For epistéme remained always his model of knowledge, by
reference to which he characterized phrónesis as knowledge in a totally
equivocal way.
      Now, since Aristotle did not see this, what he actually pursued in
his ethical works was the only kind of knowledge of which he had a
conception, namely scientific knowledge, assuming that the same and only
kind of knowledge could be pursued for its own sake, and then it was
scientific, or for the sake of action, and then it was practical. As for
Nussbaum, the same confusion is reflected in her "mixed style," as I will
explain later. First I have to make my case persuasive concerning
Aristotle.
Aristotle on speculative and practical knowledge
      There are two things going on in EN, which are not clearly
distinguished: what the agent on whom Aristotle reflects does, and what
Aristotle does in his reflection on the agent. Hecuba's deliberate action
at the death of her grand-child, to borrow an example from Nussbaum, is
very different from what Nussbaum does in reflecting on Hecuba's deliberate
action. Nussbaum is making her point Aristotelian point about deliberate
action understandable and, hopefully, understood; Hecuba is most certainly
not doing that. In the same way, what Aristotle does is very different from
what the agent does. For what the agent does is to act--ideally, the the
appropriate way--when faced with a given situation, but what Aristotle does
is to make understandable and, ideally, understood, what agents who act
appropriately do, which is very different from acting appropriately. The
latter is, in the best case, speculative knowledge about the agent, whereas
what the phronetic agent has is something very different, namely the
practical knowledge that arises from fruitful experience and that enables
the agent to act appropriately in concrete situations.
      I will take my main points from Devereux's article, which I think is
quite accurate in its presentation of Aristotle's views on phronesis or
practical reason, as distinguished from epistéme or speculative reason. (*
For the textual support for this view see Devereux).
      Aristotle himself repeatedly uses a comparison with the medical
science to explain the practical knowledge in which he is interested in his
ethical works, particularly in EN. The comparison is enlightening. There
are theoretical sciences and practical sciences. What charactrizes
"sciences" is that they share in common the fact that that the knowledge
they achieve and convey is "universal," exclusively, because it is strictly
explanatory. Not all generic knowledge is "universal." That fire is hot is
generic knowledge,  but it would not be included in a "science," for it is
not "universal." Any person of experience knows through experience that
fire is hot. This empirical or experiential knowledge is contrasted with
knowledge of the reason why fire is hot. The latter is "universal" in the
relevant sense. "Sciences" are exclusively concerned with, and
characterized by, this sort of knowledge. As expressed by Devereux, "The
important difference seems to be that the universal knowledge is
explanatory in a way that the particular is not" (491). This is the
knowledge that Aristotle calls "epistéme", which is the only kind of
knowledge that enters into genuine "sciences."
      There are, however, two kinds of sciences: speculative, and
practical. Both are exclusively concerned with "universal," that is,
explanatory knowledge. What makes the difference between them is the
purpose for which epistéme (scientific knowledge, explanatory knowledge) is
sought: if it is sought for its own sake, to satisfy our natural desire to
understand and regardless of whether or not it has paractical applications,
the science and the knowledge included in the science are "speculative."
If, by contrast, the same knowledge is pursued for practical purposes, the
science is "practical." An example of a "practical" science is, according
to Aristotle, the medical science. The medical science, however, should not
be confused with the practice of medicine. The medical science is a science
and, therefore, exclusively composed of universal, explanatory knowledge,
whereas, as we shall see in a moment, the practice of medicine is not of
this kind. The medical science is, therefore, at least in Aristotle's eyes,
a genuine science, an epistéme, as much as, say, the science of being,
metaphysics. But metaphysics is a speculative science, whereas the medical
science is a practical one, because the former seeks the knowledge of being
for its own sake, whereas most people who study medicine seek medical
knowledge for the sake of curing illnesses.
      Let us now move to the practice of medicine. Conceivably, in
Aristotle's view, one could be a pure medical scientist, that is, study
medical science for the sake of knowing, and acquire a science constituted
by pure universal knowledge. Obviously, however,  practically all those who
study medical science study it in order to cure illnesses, that is, as a
practical science. Now, a person who studied only medical science, the
speculative discipline, would not thereby become a good medic, that is, a
good practitioner of medicine. Why not? Because curing illnesses does not
only require universal knowledge (epistéme), but also practical knowledge
that comes out of medical experience. For only through experience can the
person who possesses universal knowledge come to know which pieces of
universal knowledge apply to which particular cases. In fact, as Aristotle
suggests, a purely empirical practitioner of medicine who never studied the
medical science but learned the medical trade by pure practice
may be better at curing, thanks to his practical knowledge, than someone
who studied the medical science but has had little experience in the
practice of medicine.
      Before making a further step, it will be important for us to
distinguish the person who knows only the theory of medicine, and whom I
will call the medical scientist, from the person who knows the theory of
medicine and possesses much medical practice, whom I will call the medical
practitioner, and from the person who never studied the medical science but
who through much experience in curing has become very skillful at curing
illnesses, whom I will cal the empiric. The distinction between these three
types will help us enormously to understand the way in which Aristotle
conceives ethics.
      There are, therefore, according to Aristotle, speculative sciences
and practical sciences, depending on whether their purpose is to reach
knowledge or understanding for its own sake or for the sake of the
practical benefits of the knowledge. All the sciences, however, whether
speculative or practical, insofar as they are sciences, are characterized
by the fact that the kinowledge they are after is strictly "universal,"
explanatory knowledge. The practical disciplines, however, by their very
nature, have to deal with particulars. As Aristotle says, the medical
practitioner does not cure man, he/she cures Socrates and Callias and
Corsicus; and he does not cure them from illness, he/she cures them from
particular illnesses. Therefore, though some practical sciences, like the
medical science, have a scientific component, they cannot be scientific all
the way, for they demand not only universal knowledge, but also knowledge
of particulars--and knowledge of particulars is not scientific knowledge,
is not epistéme. Why not? Because the particular, being contingent or
variable or "without fixity" by its very nature, does not lend itself to
the invariability of the universal known by the sciences. Thus, the medical
scientist is a scientist, but the medical practitioner is a scientist only
insofar as he has universal knowledge, which in and ofitself involves no
knowledge of particular illnesses and particular patients; therefore, the
medical practitioner, as a practitioner, is not a scientist, for the
knowledge on the basis of which he cures is knowledge of particular
illnesses and particular patients, which can arise only from long
experience of contingent, concrete cases.
      We may now understand the particular place occupied by ethics in
Aristotle's map of knowledge. Ethics is a practical discipline in that it
pursues knowledge not for its own sake but for the practical purpose of
appropriate action. But action, by its very nature, is particular and
contingent, for it depends on circumstances that are variable. Furthermore,
given the purely contingent subject-matter of ethics, in ethics, unlike in
medicine, there is no place at all for universal, explanatory knowledge.
Thus, ethics is in no way a science. Unlike in medicine, where there is
medical science and medical practice, and where the medical practitioner is
partly a scientist, for he/she gets from medical science his/her knowledge
of universals, in ethics there is no ethical science comparable to medical
science, for the knowledge involved is always knowledge of particulars,
which can only be gained through long practical experience. Thus, there is
no room for epistéme in ethics; there is room only for phrónesis, that is,
practical wisdom concerning action, gained through long ethical experience
in making decisions. (Devereux summarizes this neatly on pp 493-5; pp. 14-
16 of my notes on this chapter; see infra).
      For my purposes, it is important to determine as precisely as
possible the kind of knowledge which, according to Aristotle, is involved
in ethical matters. Devereux's analysis of this point is simply superb (see
infra "Devereux continues"):
      I think that Devereux's analysis reflects perfectly Aristotle's views
on the kind of knowledge involved in ethical matters, that is, on the kind
of knowledge that "practical wisodom" is. However, I think, too, that
Aristotle is wrong on this all-important point.
      As Devereux says, in Aristotle conception "practical wisdom... is
analogous not to medicine but to medical skill." But, then, he adds: "But
there is a part of practical wisdom which is analogous to medicine and,
like medicine, it lacks precision and self-sufficiency. This is the
component of practical wisdom that is concerned with universals" (493).
      I am in full agreement with the way in which Devereux understands
Aristotle. I question, however, whether Aristotle can have it both ways:
that practical wisdom is analogous to medicine, and that it lacks
precision. For medicine--the medical science, not the practice of medicine,
the medical skill--is, for Aristotle, an epistéme, a genuine science
concerned with strict universals, whereas practical wisdom involves no
strict universals, only general and imprecise "rules of thumb" which are in
no way explanatory knowledge and which cannot play at all the role played
in the medical skill by the universals that the medical practitioner gets
from the science of medicine. For Aristotle (Whether or not he is right on
this point matters little), that the symptoms Socrates shows are symptoms
of phlebitis is something that only the skillful medical practitioner can
come to know, but that phlebitis is to be treated with, say, anti-
coagulants is scientific knowledge, epistéme, a strict "universal" of the
practical medical science.
To say, therefore, that practical wisdom is like medical science but that
its universals are not universals amounts to saying that practical wisdom
is not like medical science. In fact, I think, this is what Devereux
himself senses when he says that practical wisdom and epistéme cannot fall
under the same definition of knowledge. Clearly, however, Aristotle did not
realize it, for Devereux is right in saying that, according to Aristotle,
"there is a part of practical wisdom which is analogous to medicine."
      Let us assume, at any rate, that I am right and Aristotle is wrong on
this point. What would, in that case, be the right analogy concerning
practical wisdom? To whom should we compare the ethical practitioner? In
the case of the practitioner of medicine, of the "medically skillful," the
practitioner of medicine is analogous to the medical scientist in that
he/she possesses the epistéme of the medical scientist; in addition, the
practitioner of medicine is also analogous to the "pure empiric" who has
learned to cure illnesses through long experience, in that the medical
practitioner has learned tthrough long experience to apply his universals
to the particulars of illnesses and ill people. But, in the case of
practical wisdom, where there are no strict universals, the pure empiric of
ethics and the ethical practitioner are one and the same, a person who
through long experience makes appropriate decisions on the basis of
"inevitably... inaccurate and unreliable... rules of thumb," imprecise
generalizations which are of use only to the experienced person who knows
how to use or not to use them in each case. No wonder that Devereux sensed
that, if ethical wisdom--what Aristotle calls phrónesis--which is composed
of long experience and vague generalizations of experience which by
themselves do not lead to ethically wise decisions is to be called
"knowledge," and if epistéme is also to be called "knowledge," then the
term "knowledge" as used by Aristotle is an equivocal term, for it covers
two things so different that they cannot possibly fall under a common
definition.
      From this two important consequences follow. The first concerns
Aristotle's relation to Plato. The second, the actual status of the ethical
discipline as conceived and carried out by Aristotle in EN in particular.
      To start with the first consequence, Devereux thinks that in his
treatment of practical reason Aristotle escaped Platonism by turning Plato
upside down, as a result of a gradual process. My contention is that
Aristotle in his ethical works failed to complete the process and remained
till the end under the bad influence of Plato. As far as ethical matters
are concerned, Plato's Platonism consisted, as Nussbaum has succintly put
it, in ..., which amounts to reducing ethics to a strict science, to an
epistéme. Escaping Platonism in ethics would consist in giving up totally,

for Aristotle medicine is an epistéme and the medic is a scientist, much  as
we  conceive  of  medicine  and  the  medic  today).  The  empiric  acts  as
appropriately as the medically skilled. Therefore, the sempiric "knows"  how
to cure as well as the medically-skilled, orders  the  salient  features  as
well as the latter. What the  latter  supposedly  has  is  quasi-principles,
which neither are explanatory nor can be applied as principles but  only  as
rules of thumb. But are these quasi-principles anything  more  than  general
identifiers of the object for response, gained through experience,  much  as
hot objects are not to be touched? To say  that  the  medically  skilled  is
analogous to the medic is to say that he has  the  universal  principles  of
the medic (epistéme) plus the fruitful experience of  the  empiric.  To  say
that the wise person is analogous  to  the  medically-skilled,  not  to  the
medic, is to say that, in the domain of action, he has universal  principles
plus fruitful experience. To add that the principles of the wise person  are
not universal, epistemic, is to  deny  the  analogy  between  the  medically
skilled and the wise person, for it is to say  that  the  medically  skilled
has nothing more  than  the  empiric,  namely,  experience  that  makes  him
skilful at curing. Medically-skilled and  empiric  are  one  and  the  same.
Practical scientist and wise person are one and the same.
      Another way of making the same point. Medicine is,  for  Aristotle,  a
speculative science. Medical skill is a practical science.  Since  Aristotle
thinks--whether rightly or wrongly matters  little  for  our  purposes--that
medicine is an epistéme, he thinks that  medical  skill  is  an  episteme  +
fruitful experience. Thus, he thinks that the  medically-skilled  learn  the
same as medics, except that they learn it not for the sake of knowledge  but
for the sake of curing. But  the  knowledge  is  the  same  in  both  cases.
Suppose, however, that Aristotle had conceived medicine not as  an  epistéme
but as  a  form  of  phrónesis,  as  the  knowledge  that  enables  to  cure
illnesses. In that case, there would  be  no  "medics"  (as  different  from
medically-skilled), for there would  only  be  the  practical  knowledge  or
phrónesis that enables the person who possesses it to cure  illnesses,  that
is, certain principles that work merely as rules of thumb + the  ability  to
cure that comes from experience. Now, this, precisely, is the  case  of  the
wise man. For the wise man has no episteme, no universal principles at  all;
just rules of thumb + the ability to act appropriately  that  comes  through
fruitful experience. Thus, Aristotle is wrong in making  the  analogy,  even
partly, between
