(3) On the language of statements

      The confusion of practical science with the practitioner’s knowledge
      has its origin in a reflective, objectifying view of statements as
      expressing what is the case in reality, which results in  exclusive
      attention to their content and to the truth of what is stated. This
      view is based on reflection on already uttered statements. Hence it
      does not consider statements presently being made in particular
      situations as well as the actions that accompany or follow them either
      by the author of the statement or by the addressees. Rather, it
      focuses on  WRITTEN OR ALREADY SPOKEN STATEMENTS CONSIDERED IN
      THEMSELVES, independently of the particular communicative intention of
      the speaker manifested by the speaker’s own conduct or the
      addressees’s conduct. THE SPEAKER’S ACTUAL COMMUNICATIVE INTENTION IS
      IGNORED BECAUSE, AS ARISTOTLE DID, IT IS ASSUMED THAT, BY THEIR VERY
      NATURE, STATEMENTS ARE INFORMATIVE, THAT IS, COMMUNICATIVE OF TRUTHS
      KNOWN OR ASSENTED TO BY THE SPEAKER AND TO BE KNOWN OR ASSENTED TO BY
      THE ADDRESSEE(S).* THIS IS WHY ARISTOTLE SAYS THAT STATEMENTS ARE THE
      ONLY FORM OF SPEECH THAT CAN BE TRUE OR FALSE. INTERESTED AS HE WAS AS
      A PHILOSOPHER IN GENUINE KNOWLEDGE, HE FOCUSED ON STATEMENTS THAT
      EXPRESS SCIENTIFIC JUDGMENTS. HENCE THE NEED TO GIVE EVIDENCE OR
      SUFFICIENT REASON FOR AFFIRMING, TO DEMONSTRATE LOGICALLY, AND, IN
      MODERN TIMES, TO PROVE EXPERIMENTALLY THE TRUTH OF WHAT IS STATED, OR
      TO BRING IT TO INTUITION –all of which operations aim at terminating
      in the addressee’s assent. Steeped in the Aristotelian tradition,  WE,
      TOO, WHEN WE REFLECT (BY CONTRAST TO WHAT WE DO WHEN WE ACT) FOCUS ON
      WHAT IS STATED, THAT IS, ON ALREADY COMPLETED STATEMENTS, ON WHAT IS
      STATED, ON THE SO CALLED ‘CONTENT’ OF THE STATEMENTS –AND, IN
      “SCIENTIFIC’ STATEMENTS AS A MODEL, WHICH HAVE TO BE UNIVERSAL–
      ISOLATED FROM THE SITUATION IN WHICH THEY ARE MADE BY THE SPEAKER TO
      WHOM THE STATEMENTS IMPLICITLY REFER THE ADDRESSEES (indexicality), AS
      WELL AS OF THE SPEAKER’S COMMUNICATIVE INTENTION REGARDING THE
      ADDRESSEE OR ADDRESSEES, who themselves are not philosophical
      contemplators exclusively curious about theoretical truth, but
      interested, concerned agents whose possible intentions are not
      necessarily to inform of an objective truth and are in part determined
      by the situation . After all, when we make a statement, we usually
      address it to somebody with the intention of communicating something
      to him or her in particular because it is relevant to him or her in
      particular as an agent who has goals in a particular situation. HENCE,
      PACE ARISTOTLE, THE PRIMACY OF ORAL OVER WRITTEN STATEMENTS, AND THE
      RADICAL DIFFERENCE BETWEEN THE TWO. Instead, however, of taking all
      this into account, the Aristotelian scientific statement’s meaning is
      reduced to its content, that is, to what is said objectively or purely
      observationally about the object to which it  refers (ex his quae sunt
      propria rei), independently of the way the addressees may be affected
      by it in their given situation, which is what as Aristotle did in his
      Peri hermeneias.[1]
* In spite of his allegiance to the view of language as composed of speech-
acts, SEARLE is not far from Aristotle in this regard. At least in the case
of statements, action is mediated by and subordinated to theoretical
understanding of what is said. What distinguishes language from other games
is that it says what it means, and what is said is something to be
understood by the addressee in the sense of being informed of it, whether
or not it is believed by the addressee.

      To detect the difference between the textbook view of science and
Kuhn’s own we may refer ourselves to ordinary language, in particular to
the case of statements.[2] After all, laws and theorems in textbooks are
written stated laws and theories. Thus it might be relevant to understand
the way in which statements –both oral and written–  function.

      According to one view –and this is the one analogous to the textbook
view of science– by making a statement the speaker intends to communicate
to the addressee a  reproduction or picture of the real state of the world
to which he or she refers through the statement. Thus, for instance, if in
the office we share I tell you, “Your sister is on the phone,” I am
transmitting you verbally a picture of a real state of the world at a
certain time and place, and my statement  is meant to be understood or
decoded by you, the addressee, as a piece of information about that state
of the world. By decoding it correctly you become informed of that state of
the world, or at least of my intention of informing you about it. Thus, to
continue with my example, if through your linguistic decoding you ‘get’ the
picture that your sister is on the phone on the other side of the line, you
‘got’  what I meant, for that is the representation of reality, that is,
the piece of information,  that I meant to transmit to you. In general, in
the case of any statement in English, if you ‘get’ the picture by
understanding what is said, you are a competent speaker of English.*
* Notice that Aristotle and Searle differ significantly in certain
respects. For Aristotle, in the case of statements. the  assent of the
addressee is intended by the speaker, and the communicative act is
successful if, and only if, the addressee assents to what the speaker
states. For Searle, by contrast, only the understanding of the message as
intended by the speaker is required for success. Whether or not the
addressee agrees with the speaker’s judgment of the st ate of the world is
an independent matter. What is required is that the addressee understand
what the speaker intends to communicate.

       According, however, to a different view of linguistic statements
–and this is the one analogous to Kuhn’s view of science–  statements are
not verbal pictures or representations of states of the world.
Consequently, statements –scientific statements in particular– are not
meant to function as pictures of states of the world to be mentally seen by
the addressee. And if they are understood as pictures they are simply
misunderstood. As to how , precisely, are they to be grasped, if not as
pictures, we will come to that in moment.

      Let us backtrack a little and present the two views in more detail.
According to the Aristotelian interpretation, to which the first view is
related, statements express judgments. A judgment, in Aristotle’s view, is
an epistemic or cognitional act. It is part and parcel of the silent
epistemic dialogue which a subject in search of genuine knowledge, that is,
of actual truth entertains with himself or herself. Indeed, a judgment is
the act by which a subject who reflects on conception to determine its
truth or falsity  judges or passes sentence on its conformity or lack of
conformity with reality on the basis of his or her having found sufficient
reason for affirming or denying it. In this view, then, a judgment is
primarily an epistemic act by which a subject gains knowledge,
independently of whether or not the piece of knowledge is communicated to
others. Secondarily, however, when and if the knower chooses to communicate
that piece of knowledge to others, then he or she utters the external
(vocal or written)  words that express the judgment, and thereby the
judgment becomes an affirmative or negative statement that carries a
communicative intention. A statement, therefore, is meant to communicate to
the addressee or addressees a piece of knowledge possessed by the utterer
and to be appropriated by the addressee by assenting to it as true.[3] This
is why Aristotle, in the Peri hermeneias –where he is speaking of
communicative intentions, not of epistemic or merely cognitive intentions–
asserts that statements are the only speech-acts that can be true or false,
for they are the only speech acts that claim to be true, since they express
a speaker’s judgment. As a consequence, statements are the only speech-acts
of which a science that looks for certain truth can be composed. [4]
      Philosophers are in search of genuine knowledge which they will share
with others either authoritatively or evidentially. Knowledge consists in
adaequatio mentis ad rem. Mens can be adequate only when it conceives
something definite about the world, for otherwise there is nothing that can
be adequate. Conception needs to be linguistic, for we cannot thin k
without words. But one can conceive without affirming or denying. Only when
one commits oneself to the truth of the conceived through affirmative or
negative judgment is there the possibility of  truth.
And only when one commits oneself to the truth of a conceived on the basis
of rationally compelling reason can there be certain truth, that is,
genuine knowledge. Since statements are the natural expression of
judgments, their intrinsic communicative intention can only be to
communicate the truth known by the speaker not in terms of the speaker’s
interests or affections but ex his quae sunt propria rei. Hence the
philosophical-scientific literary genre or, ideally, scientia
demonstrativa.

      On this basis, let us consider the example mentioned above. Suppose I
tell you in the office we share, “Your sister is on the phone.” According
to Aristotle, thereby I mean to share with you the piece of knowledge I
have reached by judging that what I state conforms to reality.* As far as I
am concerned, as a cognitive act,  my judgment is successful; indeed, that
is, why I believe that it conforms to reality. However, for my
communicative act to be successful as communicative, you, too, have to
assent to what I believe, for by stating it I am claiming that it is true.
Hence the only condition for my communicative act to be successful as such
is that you assent to the fact that  your sister is on the phone, for
thereby you will gain the piece of knowledge that I possess and that my
statement means to communicate to you.
* Except that this is a particular and perceptual judgment whose truth
Aristotle would accept if reached under certain conditions. However, in
Aristotle’s view, this  judgment-statement cannot be scientific and,
therefore, cannot be part of a scientific treatise.

      Such, then, is the way in which statements are meant to function,
according to this first, Aristotelian view: they express a piece of
knowledge to which the speaker, having found sufficient reason for
affirming or denying –and, in the case of scientific judgments, having
found necessary reason for affirming or denying-- assents as true. As
expressive of judgments, statements aim at transmitting that piece of
knowledge conform to reality by  eliciting the assent of the addressee to
that piece of knowledge.

      In the Peri hermeneias Aristotle deals exclusively with enunciative
communicative speech, which signifies the concept of the mind,  which he
distinguishes from non-enunciative speech. The former aims at communicating
truth as it is grasped by the speaker’s intellect, and therefore attempts
to elicit the addressee’s assent ex his quae sunt propria rei,  whereas non-
enunciative speech aims at eliciting assent –ASSENT?, OR RATHER ACTION
GROUNDED IN ASSENT?–  per dispositionem audientis, that is, appealing to
the desires and interests of the addressee, through “moving,” as in
rhetoric and poetry.  The former form of speech alone interests Aristotle
in this treatise, as observed by Aquinas,

       “for this book is ordered directly to demonstrative science, in which
      the mind of man is led by an act of reasoning to assent to truth from
      those things which are proper to the thing; to this end the
      demonstrator uses only enunciative speech, which signifies things
      according as truth about them is in the mind. The rhetorician and the
      poet, on the other hand, induce assent to what they intend not only to
      what is proper to the thing but also through dispositions of the
      hearer. Hence, rhetoricians and poets for the most part strive to move
      their auditors by arousing certain passions in them, as the
      Philosopher says in his Rhetorica” I, 2, 1356a 2, 1356a 14; III, I,
      1430b 12.[5]

      This applies to the scientific literary genre, of which the
scientific textbooks are an example. It is composed of statements that
express the judgments of people who know –the  discovers a scientific
truths who by stating them, together with the sufficient reason for
assenting to them (namely, the experimental evidence), aim at sharing that
knowledge with others who don’t know. Two things, it is to be remarked,,
distinguish the statements of which the scientific  literary genre is
composed. First, the transmission of knowledge is not authoritative. Hence
the statements are to be supported by evidence, so that they elicit assent
not on the basis of the speaker’s authority or of an appeal to the
addressee’s interests (wishful thinking), but on the basis of the evidence
that prompted the judgment of the discoverer in the first place. Thus they
are purely objective for they exclusively refer “to those things which are
proper to the thing,” independently of the addressee’s desires or fears.
Second –and this relates to what is usually called ‘the scientific method,’
the evidence admissible is of a different and higher quality than any
other, so as to elicit genuinely scientific assent. In modern science, the
entire procedure by which evidence is reached and calibrated is minutely
regulated: systematic observation, detailed measurements, measurements and
experiments carried out with the appropriate technological instruments and
according to very exacting standards of accuracy, replicability of
experiments, etc....

      Obviously, then,  modern science differs from Aristotelian science in
significant ways. For one, the evidence involved is experimental rather
than purely logical; that is, it is the result of experiment. Moreover,
concerning the entire ‘scientific method’ – quality of observation,
measurements, technological instruments of measurement and observation, and
above all experimental evidence– modern  science is different and highly
superior to its Aristotelian counterpart. Moreover, precisely because it is
experimental, modern science relies much more on the senses than on the
intellect than its Aristotelian counterpart, and consequently aims at
discovering causal relations rather than essential natures.[6]
Nevertheless, at least as it is understood in the textbook view, modern
science remains Aristotelian in that it is composed of statements that
express the judgments of the scientists who through esperiment discovered
the stated laws, and who communicate their judgments through those
statements in order to elicit the addressees’s assent to their truth.

      The above reflects what Kuhn calls the textbook image of science,
which he rejects. Before moving to Kuhn’s own view, however, let us subject
the textbook image to criticism. This view rests on the assumption that
statements, as expressive of judgments, both correspond to what is the case
in reality and aim at eliciting assent. Moreover, because those statements
are ‘scientific’ they are worthy of assent. That this view is not exclusive
of the scientific textbooks can easily be gathered from the fact that THE
COMMONLY ACCEPTED SCIENTIFIC VIEWS OF THE UNIVERSE ARE SUPPOSED TO
REPRESENT REALITY FOR EVERYBODY (ex his quae sunt propria rei), so much so
that any other view –be it religious, or commonsensical– that is at odds
with the accepted scientific view is supposed to be false. (Hence, among
many other problems, the discredit of religious views and, in general, THE
CONFLICT BETWEEN FAITH AND REASON TO THE SYSTEMATIC DISPARAGEMENT OF
REASON).*
* This is the insight of those apologists for the Christian faith that
argue that the Bible is not a scientific treatise and doesn’t make
scientific statements –that is, doesn’t transmit  scientific views of
reality. However, the underlying wrongheaded assumption is that both
scientific treatises and the Bible convey pictures of what is the case.
Hence, the non-scientific pictures of reality transmitted by religion have
to compatible, in one way or another, with the true scientific pictures!

      Supposedly, then, it is of the nature of statements to aim at
obtaining assent. Such, at least, is the case of the scientific statements
contained in textbooks, and for that very reason they
are supported by evidence, rather than being merely authoritative. For his
part, Kuhn disputes that conception. First of all, if experiments yielded
truths to be assented to, every time that an experiment fails to confirm a
stated law, the law ought to be discarded. And as long as a law is not
confirmed by experimental evidence, it ought not to be taken seriously. But
this is not what actually happens, at all. Secondly, Kuhn argues, the
students of science accept what their teachers teach them not on the basis
of evidence but of the authority of their teachers who show by example how
the laws apply in the solution of problems.

      We turn now to the second view of the way in which statements
function in ordinary language, whose main purpose is not, like in the case
of science,  to establish and enunciate the truth. To go back to our
example, suppose that in response to my statement, “Your sister is on the
phone,”  you tell me, “All right,” That is, I agree, I assent to what you
say, but nonetheless continue doing whatever you are doing, instead of
taking the phone or at least explaining to me why you do not take the phone
or at least telling me what I am  supposed to tell your sister. If that is,
indeed, your reaction, my suspicion is that you misheard or misunderstood
my statement. So I repeat it. If  your impatient response is, “Yes, yes, I
get it, my sister is on the phone,” but you still continue doing whatever
you are doing, I realize that you do not understand how statements are
meant to function and, consequently, that you are not a competent speaker-
hearer of English.*
* In other words, statements in the mood of exposure are to be heard in the
same  mood in which a dog hears its master’s name, to wit: as an
announcement of his presence to be responded to through action. (Cf. Helen
Keller’s interpretation by Langer, which reflects the latter’s unconscious
Aristotelian bias typical of philosophers, by attributing paradigmatic
priority to theoretical  enunciation, that is, to epistemic judgment over
communicative statement or speech-ACT.

      Has the person I just described really understood my statement as it
is meant to be decoded? Well, if statements are meant as purely objective
pictures of real states of the world, and if my interlocutor ‘got’ the
picture that her sister is actually at the other end of the line, then my
interlocutor really understood my statement and  is a competent English
speaker. (This is Searle’s position). Yet I and, surely, every other
competent English speaker, will say that if this person doesn’t do anything
in response to what I stated, but simply continues doing what she was
doing, (and finds surprising or inexplicable the astonishment of other
people at his or her conduct),  this person doesn’t understand statements,
even if he gets in her mind a very clear picture of her sister being at the
other end of the line. The reason is that statements of the kind I am
referring to(namely, oral, perceptual statements addressed to someone in
particular in a given situation), whether in English or in any other
language, are not meant to function as pictures of reality to be understood
and contemplated, or as judgments to be assented to, as Aristotle assumes.
Rather, they are meant to function –to employ Kuhn’s expression in
reference to the statements of scientific laws– as tools that function as a
guide for action in response to what is stated.

       Indeed, the person who in the situation just described guides her
action by what I stated and responds to my statement by taking the phone to
talk to her sister is the person who understands my statement and who knows
practically how statements are meant to function –that is, applicatively,
as tools for immediate of future action, depending on the circumstances.
And this person shows that she understands my statement and how statements
are meant to function, not by saying that she understands, and by getting
in her mind the picture of her sister at the other end of the line, or even
by deeming that picture true, not even by actually showing me the actual
picture of my sister on the phone, but by actually acting from my
statement, to use Polanyi’s expression, and taking the phone and talking to
her sister. In short, understanding statements does not consist in
‘getting’ a picture, but in  acting appropriately on the basis of what is
stated.*
* Even when statements appear to be ‘ex his quae sunt propria rei,’ they
are addressed to people who have interests. For instance, physicians hear
applicatively, though it doesn’t appear to be the case, for they implicitly
have the professional interest in curing, that is, in applying those
statements to curing, which is their commonly shared goal.

       This, I believe, is what Kuhn means by saying that the statements of
scientific laws are meant to function as tools, not as pictures. His point
may be related to what Polanyi  (whom Kuhn mentions with approval) has
called “indwelling” as a result of “interiorizing” or appropriating into
our own body. And this is what I mean by hearing applicatively, that is,
prospectively, toward the future, and practically.

      Think of the case of spectacles. Spectacles are instruments or tools
we use for seeing. But we use them in a very special way. By wearing them
and making them part of our body, we are not aware of them,  we see the
surrounding world from them. As we do not see our eyes but from our eyes,
so we do not see the spectacles we wear but from the spectacles. In other
words, the spectacles are not on the side of the world we see, but on the
side of our eyes, of our own body. We use the spectacles as we ‘use’ our
eyes, if that is the right word, to see the world. We “indwell” in the
spectacles for the purpose of seeing as we indwell in our eyes, not by
seeing our eyes but by seeing from them.

      Now, this is the way in which statements –whether scientific or
other– are meant to function: not as having a content to be seen, but as
indwelling in them as we indwell in our eyes to see from them. However, it
is important for our purposes to be more precise as to the kind of seeing
involved in the seeing from our eyes to which I am referring here, for we
tend to think that what we see from our eyes is a spectacle, that is,
something to be contemplated, whereas in fact what we see is to not be
contemplated but to be interacted with. What happens in the experiment with
inverting spectacles is enlightening in this respect, for it indicates not
only a strong connection between knowing and acting but shows that knowing
is a ‘moment’ of the process of acting.

      The following quotations from Polanyi’s book  are enlightening as to
what is meant by application and statements functioning as tools:

      “Whenever we use certain things for attending from them to other
      things, in the way in which we always use our own body*, these things
      change their appearance. They appear to us now in terms of the
      entities to which we are attending from them,  just as we feel our own
      body in terms of the thing outside to which we are attending from our
      own body. In this sense we can say that when we make a thing function
      as the proximal term of tacit knowledge, we incorporate it in our own
      body or extend our body to include it– so that we come to dwell in it”
      (p. 16)
      * Notice: Our own body, not out bodyless mind or nous or intellect (as
      Plato and Aristotle assumed). which only ‘sees’ and cannot function as
      a tool.

      “To interiorize is to identify ourselves with the teachings in
      question, by making them function as the proximal term of a tacit
      moral knowledge as applied in practice.  This establishes the tacit
      framework for our moral acts and judgments. And we can trace this kind
      of indwelling to logically similar acts in the practice of science. To
      rely on a theory for understanding nature is to interiorize it. For we
      are attending from the theory to the thing seen in its light, and are
      aware of the theory, while thus using it, in terms of the spectacle
      that it serves to explain. This is why mathematical theory can be
      learned only by practicing its application: its true knowledge lies in
      our ability to use it. - The identification of tacit knowledge with
      indwelling involves a shift of emphasis in our conception of tacit
      knowing. We had envisaged tacit knowing in the first place as a way to
      know more than we can tell... Since we are not attending to the
      particulars in themselves, we could not identify them; but if we now
      regard the integration of particulars as interiorization, it takes on
      a more positive character. It now becomes a means of making certain
      things function as the proximal term of tacit knowing, so that instead
      of observing them in themselves, we may be aware of them in their
      bearing on the comprehensive entity which they constitute. It brings
      home to us that it is not by looking at things but by indwelling in
      them, that we understand their joint meaning”  (pp. 17-18).

      “And we have seen also that a true knowledge of a theory can be
      established only after it has been interiorized and extensively used
      to interpret experience. Therefore: a mathematical theory can be
      constructed only by relying on a prior tacit knowing, which consists
      in our attending from it to the previously established experience on
      which it bears” (Michael Polanyi, The Tacit Dimension, p. 21).

       Kuhn maintains that the textbook view of science errs in locating
scientific knowledge in contemplative understanding. As in the case of
statements in ordinary, everyday language, understanding the formulations
of scientific laws consists in the habitual disposition, gained through
practice of application, to apply  appropriately the law. In other words,
it consists in guiding one’s action by what is stated by the law or the
theorem, not in any contemplative grasp of what the law states. This is
why, contrary to the textbook view of science, actual application of the
laws in problem-solving is not, according to Kuhn, something in addition to
scientific knowledge; it is the way of acquiring what the scientific
activity is supposed to yield, to wit: the habitual disposition to ‘see
applicatively’ or “in the appropriate gestalt.”

 Pointers
- All traffic signs are to be under stood or appropriated or interiorized
in this instrumental way. They are signs for action, not signs of a state
of the world that can be object of objective predication. A map is not a
picture; seeing it as a picture is misunderstanding it; it is an indexical
sign for action.
- The case of inverting lenses is instructive. It shows not merely that
there is a connection between vision and action, but that the vision
itself, that is, what is seen, is a result of action, not the other way
around. And it shows, too, that the vision is not a picture but a guide for
future action, which presupposes desire or aversion, a goal, that
determines the coefficient of adversity or favorableness of the viewed.
Hodological space. Practical indexicality of perception. The space-time of
science, the space of the Cartesian coordinates is the space of an
abstract, un-situated, pure and therefore purely objective contemplator
without distances of exposure. It is a non-dramatic, uninhabitable space-
time.

(4) The representational conception of knowledge

      In rejecting the notion of knowledge as correspondence with the real
Kuhn is fighting against a long and deeply ingrained epistemic tradition in
which he himself and we all have been steeped. Indeed, the commonsensical
notion that knowledge consists in the match or  correspondence between what
the subject has in mind and what is the case in reality, based on a
superficial reflection on visual perception as a mirror image of the object
seen, correlated with a particular interpretation of perceptual statements
as claiming structural  ontological correspondence with real states of
affairs, received its philosophical consecration from Plato and Aristotle.*
* Notice that Plato gave up on sensible knowledge and felt constrained to
postulate the actual eternal existence of the Ideas precisely on the
assumption that knowledge consists in ontological correspondence or even
intentional identity between what the nous understands and the real .

      Commonsensical as this notion of knowledge as correspondence appears
to be, Plato realized that it  is far from being unproblematic even on the
perceptual level. How, indeed, can one be sure that what one sees actually
corresponds to what is the case? After all, everyone has had the experience
of having been wrong some times in perceptual matters, after being
convinced of being right. In other words, the notion of knowledge as
correspondence to what is the case, coupled with the obvious fact that
putative knowledge or opinions proves some times to be false, created for
Plato the problem of discerning between merely apparent and genuine
knowledge. To distinguish the two, at least in name, Plato called the
former “opinion” or “belief” (doxa) and the latter “knowledge” (episteme),
and he tackled the problem of discerning the two in the following way. Only
those realities can be object of “knowledge” which are absolutely
unchangeable or permanent because they exist necessarily and are
necessarily what they are. For if the knower grasps such necessity, then
the knower grasps that what he or she knows necessarily corresponds to what
is the case. Now, no sensible object is of that kind, for all of them are
contingent rather than necessary, that is, they are subject to change in
the flux of time. Hence,  even assuming that our perceptions of sensible
objects actually correspond at a certain instant to what is the case, they
cease to be true in the next instant as the sensible objects change. Thus
Plato felt constrained to relegate all the particular objects of
perception to the realm of opinion or mere belief. Furthermore,  he
determined that only the intellect –nous–  (as opposed to the senses),  was
capable of grasping intelligible objects, which being purely immaterial and
purely intelligible are incorruptible and therefore not subject to temporal
change but, rather necessary and universal, could attain genuine knowledge
–episteme by contrast to doxa.

      We owe Plato the notion of episteme or “scientific knowledge,” that
is, of a knowledge whose truth or conformity to reality is guaranteed by
necessity (hence the need for proofs and demonstrations) and,
correlatively, whose  resulting rational certainty in the knower has little
to do with the merely subjective feeling that accompanies what ordinary
people mistake for certainty. Socrates deflated the epistemic hubris of the
people he cross-examined by showing them that they did not know what they
were talking about since, concerning the very objects they claimed to know,
they were unable to provide essential definitions –that is, definitions
that were necessarily true because they transcended the sensible
appearances and grasped their intelligible essences, not only the quia or
mere fact but the propter quid, the explanatory necessary reason for the
defined being what it is and is affirmed to be.[7] Of course, such
definitions were possible only of purely intelligible objects. This led
Plato to his postulation –not demonstration–  of  the eternal existence of
the Ideas to guarantee the truth of intellectual intuitions and essential
definitions.*
* Both Plato and Aristotle confused conceptual necessity to assent to a

       Regarding Plato’s obsession with scientific or necessarily true
knowledge, suffice it to remember that at the door of the Platonic academy
there was an inscription to the effect that only those were to be admitted
who were proficient in geometry, a science whose objects were purely
intelligible and whose truths, consequently, were either self-evident in
their necessity, or resulted from strict demonstration, that is, a chain of
logically connected statements derived from the axioms..

      Aristotle, for his part, demolished the Platonic theory of the Ideas
but preserved the dream  of scientific knowledge. Thus, Aristotle invented
the science of logic, which guaranteed the truth of statements by virtue of
their form, though not of their content. As for the truth of contents,  in
the Analytica Posteriora Aristotle formulated an ideal of science after the
deductive model of geometry, but to be applied  in other fields than the
purely mathematical ones. As it turned out, however, Aristotle himself was
unable to realize his ideal in any of his varied scientific endeavors.
Actually, there are good reasons to think that Aristotle did not even try
to realize his ideal of science in any of his particular scientific
endeavors, because he soon came to the realization that the Platonic demand
of necessary, guaranteed truth as an essential condition for knowledge by
contrast to opinion was an arbitrary demand, in that it did not take into
account the difference in the objects in the different realms. In the realm
of the sensible and the particular, Aristotle acknowledged, experience
plays a foundational role analogous to that played by intellectual
intuition and demonstration in the realm of the abstract and universal
concepts, as indicated by the universal agreement of the wise*. Thus to ask
for the same certainty of knowledge in the two domains, as Plato had done
–with the disastrous epistemic consequence of excluding the entire field of
perception from the domain of knowledge (disastrous because all concepts
originate from experience through abstraction)–  came to be seen by
Aristotle as hopelessly aprioristic.  Not for nothing is Aristotle often
celebrated as ‘pragmatic’ by comparison to Plato.

      Pragmatic as Aristotle may have been in his relaxation of the
requirement of necessary correspondence between the known and reality –and
therefore of the requirement of demonstration– , he nonetheless REMAINED AT
ONE WITH PLATO IN THE HIGHLY PROBLEMATIC CONVICTION THAT KNOWLEDGE CONSISTS
IN CORRESPONDENCE WITH REALITY. But while, unlike Plato, he did not demand
from the different sciences the same degree of rigor in the ostension of
correspondence with the real, he nevertheless maintained that it was
incumbent on each science to bring to evidence that correspondence in the
measure allowed by the particular nature of its object. Thus, for instance,
no strict  demonstration of truth should be expected in matters of ethics,
for the ethical science deals with the particular and concrete; but that
does not excuse the practitioner of the science from rendering as probable
and persuasive, by means of argument and reference to experience, the truth
of his or her position.

      Needless to say, when strict proof is available, the correspondence
should be made evident by the presentation of the rational proof
(scientific syllogism is syllogismus faciens scire) or the manipulation of
images that bring the truth of the theorem to intuition, or of the
experiment that proves the law . That this requirement remained alive in
the tradition many centuries after Aristotle’s death shows in the medieval
frenzy for logic and for demonstrative proofs in general, such as the
‘proofs’ for the existence of God and the discussion of their validity (as
for example the validity of the Anselmian so-called ontological proof was
discussed by the Scholastics and later by Kant). A more interesting case of
the vitality of the Aristotelian requirement of making evident that is, of
showing the necessity of, the correspondence between knowledge and reality
is the protracted medieval discussion whether theology could be a science
in the Aristotelian sense, finally ‘solved’  by Aquinas in a quasi-
affirmative sense. [8]  This explains  de Lubac’s justified
characterization of the XII-XIII Scholastic period as the beginning of “the
age of proofs.”  Closely related to this is the textbook view of science,
whose interpretation of the experimental method in the natural sciences
results in the certain truth of its pronouncements, for they are supposedly
guaranteed by systematic observation, measurement, and experimental proof.
In the area of those sciences, systematic observation and experimental
proof are the equivalent of intellectual intuition and  logical
demonstration by deduction in the  conceptual sciences of old.

The origin of the representational view: statements

      From where do we get the idea that knowledge is, in one way or
another, representational of what is the case? Or, to put the same question
in a different way, that knowledge consists in conformity between the
mental object and reality? From the fact that we make to other people
statements –in particular perceptual-visual statements–  through which we
refer to what is the case, and that through them we refer to what is the
case precisely as being conform to what we have in mind, to the ’mental
object,’ to what we perceive or judge, which is what we state in words.
Indeed, to make a statement is not simply to refer to something,  but to
claim that what we say about that to which we refer is true as we state it,
that is, corresponds to what we have in mind. This is why, as remarked by
Aristotle in Peri hermeneias, statements are the only form of speech
susceptible of being true or false. Indeed, the truth of the statement is
claimed by the speaker  by the very fact of making the statement. When I
tell you, “the glass is on the table,” I am referring to a reality as I
perceive it, and I am simultaneously claiming that  what I perceive in my
mind conforms to reality.*
* Statements are primarily communicative acts among agents exposed to the
world, not cognitive acts, not pieces of a silent epistemic dialogue of the
philosopher with himself. They are made by a particular exposed speaker to
a particular exposed addressee, in particular circumstances. I just don’t
go around stating that the glass is on the table; I tell that only to whom
it concerns because of his or her surmised or explicit project. - Even in
the private dialogue with myself, I don’t go around telling myself that the
milk is in the refrigerator; I register this fact, that is, tell  myself
that fact, only when it is relevant to the satisfaction of a need, and to
be understood  applicatively at the appropriate time. Actually, the ‘fact’
that the milk is in the refrigerator is just an abstraction from my
successful action in the past to satisfy my need fore milk, much as the
‘fact’ that this kind of plant cures diarrhea is an abstraction from the
successful action of eating it.

       N.B. EPISTEMIC dialogue means: reflective dialogue with oneself (or
      with other thinkers) for critical epistemic purposes of assessing its
      truth, which may or may not be useful for the satisfaction of the
      subject’s needs, and independently of whether it is useful or not.
      (Hence primacy of theoretical over practical, and subordination of the
      latter to the former). Aristotelian “judgment” is of this kind.
      Actually, the entire philosophical project as inaugurated by Socrates
      and Plato, continued by Aristotle, and reflected in the textbook view
      of science, is of this kind. Notice the separation of knowledge and
      action as well as the suppression of passion.

      N.B. ON THE NOTION OF CORRESPONDENCE
      It should be remarked that the notion of knowledge as correspondence
      COMBINES OR RATHER COLLAPSES TWO DIFFERENT THINGS: the notion of truth
      as a property of statements that express affirmative or negative
      JUDGMENTS (with sufficient reason for affirming or denying) OF WHAT IS
      THE CASE, THAT IS OF A CERTAIN STRUCTURE OR STATE OF AFFAIRS, and the
      notion of knowledge as pictorial correspondence or resemblance of a
      MENTAL IMAGE OF THE REAL OBJECT it supposedly represents. AN OBJECT
      MAY BE REAL OR NOT REAL, BUT IT CANNOT BE TRUE OR FALSE. The same is
      the case with ‘mental objects’ or images. ONLY STATEMENTS OF WHAT IS
      THE CASE, OF A SUPPOSEDLY REAL SATE OF AFFAIRS, CAN BE TRUE OR
      FALSE..* The combination (or collapse) just alluded to is indicated by
      the fact that the spontaneous, paradigmatic exemplification of the
      idea of knowledge as correspondence is not just any statement
      whatsoever but a perceptual statement in particular, and not any kind
      of perceptual statement but a visual statement in particular
      susceptible of ostension, of being pointed at. A concrete illustration
      of this point is the example of statement I just used, to wit, “the
      glass is on the table.” The reason for this is that visual statements
      seem to possess a privileged epistemic status in that they allow  for
      ostensive and, so to say, public or intersubjective verification of
      their conformity to reality or their lack thereof.  For instance, if I
      tell you that the water is hot but you feel it only warm, I cannot
      show you that it is actually hot, for we cannot compare our respective
      sensations to the actual temperature of the water. In matters of
      visual perception, by contrast, there is the possibility of direct
      ostension. If I state that this glass is on the table and you don’t
      believe it, I can bring you to the room and show you the glass on the
      table. Thus, in the case of visual statements we seem to have the
      confirmation that statements not only refer to reality, but also that,
      when true,  they do conform to reality.
      * Yet both Aristotle and the scholastics speak of the material truth
      of perception, because they too confuse mirroring correspondence with
      structural correspondence.

      Three different things are involved in the notion of the
      correspondence of mind to reality: (a) correspondence by pictorial
      resemblance (the mental object resembles or re-presents the real
      OBJECT); (b) correspondence by structural resemblance of t he
      statement or  predication with the real STATE OF AFFAIRS  (what is
      affirmed or denied is to be assented to, regardless of its existence),
      and ontological correspondence (correspondence of an extra-mental
      object to the mental object). Remember Locke’s distinction between
      primary and secondary qualities:

      As for the further avatars of the notion of knowledge as
correspondence with what is the case in reality, and the intense scrutiny
and criticism to which it has been subjected for centuries, the story has
been told a hundred times. For my purpose, it may suffice to say that Kuhn
is not alone, by far, in finding such notion defective, however difficult
it may have proven to find a better alternative. It is fair to say that
Kant, who gave up on the notion of knowledge as correspondence with the
real in subordination to the real on the basis that, since we have no
independent means of access to the real other than knowledge itself, we

corresponds to “the thing itself,” is, in this particular regard, Kuhn’s
direct predecessor. Whether, however, Kant proposed a viable alternative to
the notion of knowledge as correspondence, or simply left us in the abyss
of skepticism, is not my question. Whatever the case may be with Kant,  my
question concerns  Kuhn: Does he or doesn’t  he furnish us with a viable
alternative to the traditional notion of knowledge as correspondence which
he so boldly discards, at least in the exemplary case of science?

      We will find less outlandish Kuhn’s usage of the word ‘knowledge’ if
we reflect on the fact that it comes to us very naturally to attribute
knowledge to animals exclusively on the basis of their acquired problem-
solving ability reflected in their behavior (and based in the inherited
needs of each species).  When an animal who previously was incapable of
behaving in the appropriate way to satisfy its needs or the needs of its
species acquires the ability to do that on a habitual basis, as shown by
its behavior, we reckon that as knowledge. But why call this ‘knowledge’ if
it involves no truth as visual correspondence to what is the case?

(5) Practical knowledge is not representational: Ortho-doxy and ortho-

      Completing the analysis I began in  my <2002-April-3-Aris-Devereux:

      subordinating it to, to theoretical knowledge. Indeed, as indicated in

      an element of universality characteristic of Plato’s intellectualistic
      conception of knowledge, while at the same time, as we shall presently
      see, he made of its connection with action, which is particular
      through and through, the distinctive characteristic of practical
      knowledge. Let us explore these points in some detail.

      The very fact that both theoretical and practical knowledge bear the
name of knowledge in Aristotle’s writings might suggest that Aristotle sees
them as two species of the genus ‘knowledge’ rather than as two totally
different things. Is this really the case? Of one thing, at least,  we can
be sure. As Plato’s disciple, Aristotle initially identified knowledge with
theoretical knowledge exclusively. It was only later, as he started sensing
a possible difference between knowledge motivated by the pure desire to
know and knowledge motivated by other desires or needs, that he developed
the notion of practical knowledge. Now the very fact that he  gave the
latter the name of knowledge indicates that he saw practical knowledge as
being only specifically different from theoretical knowledge. In other
words, Aristotle saw both kinds of knowledge as conforming to the real
–which justified their common name of ‘knowledge’–, and differing only in
their respective connection with action, which is typical of practical
knowledge and not found in theoretical knowledge. Actually, this is one of
the ways in which Aristotle distinguishes practical from theoretical
knowledge.

      In addition, this explains why, as it appeared in the previous paper,
 Aristotle insisted on including in practical knowledge a universal
component, which is typical of theoretical knowledge. Since both kinds of
knowledge shared the same genus,  in addition to action , which
distinguished it from theoretical knowledge, practical knowledge had to
involve vision, which is what it had in common with theoretical knowledge.
This is to say that Aristotle conceived of practical knowledge as
subordinated to theoretical knowledge, for he saw the latter as a necessary
condition for the former. To put it more concretely, in Aristotle’s view,
appropriate action is based in, and presupposes, correct vision. In order
to respond correctly to an object, which is what practical knowledge
consist in, one needs first to have a view of that object that conforms to
it, which is what theoretical knowledge consists in.

       Such a view is strongly reminiscent of Plato’s own. Indeed, having
no definite sense of the difference between theoretical and practical
knowledge, Plato had implicitly assimilated theoretical to practical
knowledge when he identified knowledge with virtue. According to Plato, for
instance, the person who knew what piety essentially is would automatically
act piously. That was the reason why Socrates in the Euthyphro so intensely
wanted the essential definition of piety. In a parallel way, because he
thought that practical and theoretical knowledge only differed in their
respective relations to action, Aristotle had to assume that in order to
act virtuously the agent needed to possess knowledge of the theoretical
doctrine he presented in his NE, and that in order to cure the medical
practitioner needed to possess knowledge of the theoretical doctrine found
in medical books.

      Whether or not this Platonizing conception of practical knowledge
that subordinates it to theoretical knowledge makes sense remains, of
course,  to be seen. All we can say is that we have already found in this
view an inconsistency indicated by Aristotle himself. Contrary to t he
demands of his own notion of practical knowledge, the medical practitioner
who doesn’t have any  knowledge of the universal medical doctrine but
relies exclusively in his or her experience is capable of curing, whereas
the person who knows the doctrine but for lack of experience doesn’t know
how to apply it cannot cure.

      [Why did Aristotle so spontaneously assume that practical knowledge

experience, so to say. But what, precisely, is the nature of this
knowledge? Does the animal possess a kind of mental representation of the
plant’s purgative virtue? Or is it rather a disposition to respond in a
certain way when the need for the beneficial effect is felt?

      It seems that Aristotle remained ambiguous on this important matter,
as suggested by the unanswered question whether he attributes to animals
knowledge quia (“in animals non nisi parum”). Given his notion of knowledge
as conformity of the mind with the real, in attributing knowledge to
animals he seemed to attribute to them mental  images of the perceived
objects, which could conform pictorially to the real. Hence the Scholastics
thought that Aristotle attributed to animals material knowledge, as
explained above. Thus, though he affirmed the connection of knowledge to
action in the case of practical knowledge,  he maintained the subordination
of praxis to theory.

      Whatever Aristotle’s position may have been, let us raise the
question on our own: Does the commonsensical notion of knowledge as mental
correspondence apply to animals? It would seem that this, precisely, is
what we are implicitly asking when we raise the question whether animals
know. However, if that is indeed what we are asking, then the answer has to
be that animals are incapable of knowing, or at least that it is impossible
to find out whether they actually know or not. Why? Because there is no
reason to think that animals have mental objects capable of being conform
to reality, since they do not make statements. That you, a person, have
mental objects, I can accept because, like me, you make statements, that
is, you implicitly claim that what you say and have in mind conforms to
what is the case. SUCH AT LEAST SEEMS TO BE THE CASE WHEN YOU AND I
DISAGREE AND BEGIN TO DISCUSS REFLECTIVELY THE TRUTH OF THE STATEMENT
BEFORE ACTING (“It is true that” or “It is not true that.”) But animals do
not make statements at all. Hence, even in the unlikely case that animals
have mental objects that conform by resemblance to what is the case
(unlikely, because animals lack language), the least we have to say is that
we shall never know whether animals know.

      Now this negative answer is predicated on the assumption that
knowledge does indeed  consist in the conformity between the mind and
reality. Yet most of us are convinced that though animals do not make any
statements, at least the higher species of mammals, such as apes, dogs,
cats, horses, etc., are capable of knowing and do actually know in certain
cases. Is our conviction groundless? If that were the case, then our no
less strong conviction that one year old infants actually know before they
acquire language and make any statements –at which stage they are
comparable to animals– would be equally groundless. But of course our
conviction concerning the epistemic capacity of infants and of higher
mammals is not groundless: it is grounded in their behavior. As we can know
that a tiger is blind, that is, that it lacks visual knowledge, just by
observing its behavior in attempting to hunt other animals, so we know that
a year old infant who still lacks linguistic ability  knows just by
observing his non-verbal behavior, as so minutely described by Piaget in
The Origin of Intelligence in Children.

      Let us now pause for a moment and reflect. Although apes and infants
do not speak and do not make any statements that show that they have mental
images of objects, we nevertheless are certain that they know, not because
they have mental images of any kind, to which we have no access, but
because of the way they behave in relation to the objects they encounter in
their environment. What kind of behavior? Think of a blind person who is an
adult. In most cases, as in the case of the tiger just alluded to, we do
not need to be told that she is blind. Just by seeing the clumsiness and
hesitancy of her actions to achieve her goals and realize her plans,
compared to the security and smoothness of the actions of sighted people,
we realize that she is deprived of the perceptual knowledge that comes with
sight. Undoubtedly, when we say that the person is blind  we spontaneously
think of deprivation of the visual experiences we sighted people have when
we see an object, which supposedly correspond to the object. Hence we
conclude that the blind person lacks knowledge, for we think of knowledge
as the correspondence of the mental experience of object with what is the
case. And indeed a blind person is deprived of such internal experiences,
as we realize it when through conversation with blind people we compare our
visual experiences with the lack thereof in the blind people.

      But is the absence of visual experiences like the ones we sighted
people have to be equated with the absence of knowledge? Perhaps not. After
all there are certain animals that have no eyes, and nonetheless they move
in relation to the objects in their environment as securely and efficiently
as if they had the experiences of sighted people, because those animals
–bats, for example–  possess a different sense, a sort of natural radar
that informs them of the presence of any object in their path and allows
them to avoid collisions. Of these animals, then, we can say that they do
not have visual experiences, but not that they do not know the objects that
are in their path, as we sighted people know the objects that our in our
path. This example indicates that, contrary to our commonsensical idea,
what characterizes knowledge is not the visual resemblance Locke had in
mind when he spoke of knowledge, between the object as it is in the mind
and as it is in reality, but the knower’s ability to deal appropriately
with the surrounding world in order to achieve or realize his or her aims.
And in addition,  in the case of people, to invent instruments (and
instrumental complexes: theories, machinery) by means of which to realize
their aims . That we actually believe that knowledge consists in the
ability to deal aptly with the realities of our surrounding world rather
than in the resemblance between  what is in the subject’s mind and what is
the case shows in the confidence with which we attribute knowledge to
animals, based not on their internal mental experiences to which we are not
privy, but on their purposive acquired behavior in relation to their
environment. We even speak of inherited knowledge in the case of animals,
when we see them interact successfully with their environment   right after
birth, with no previous experience of the world.

knowledge that Kuhn is struggling to liberate both himself and us in the
area of the experimental sciences.

      I said that nothing corresponds to the universal which the
theoretician has in mind. In other words, the judgment, expressed in the
statement, that all light meats are wholesome, neither possesses nor is
meant to possess any truth-value, that is any resemblance, that is, any
either pictorial or structural homophormism to what is the case. Such
judgment is not objective, in that it grasps no object; no reality-fact, no
intelligible nature that corresponds to it.[9] Nor can anything of that
sort correspond to it, since all reality is particular. To repeat, ‘light
meats’ are those meats which the ‘experts’ or experienced people recognize
as light meats in terms of their expectation and desire of wholesome, that
is, healthy, beneficial effects. Accordingly, they will eat them, or direct
others to eat them, under the appropriate circumstances which cannot be
specified and whose practical recognition is the tacit result of experience
or of imitation of those who have experience* This recognition cannot be
formalized in a theorem, much less in an algorithm, for it is not
‘objective’; it needs to be internalized (Aristotle might have said
‘inferred’ –but practically inferred under the form of expectation and
disposition to respond –)   through practice. This is why the student
becomes a scientist only through the practice of application, guided by an
experienced teacher.

      Thus, it is not only that the Platonic eternal Ideas required to save
the objectivity of universal necessary judgments do not exist, as Aristotle
rightly argued, but neither do exist the necessary unchangeable “natures”
that he inferred objectively from the perceptual observation of certain
constant natural phenomena., With those ‘natures’ he replaced the Platonic
Ideas and attempted to preserve the Platonic notion of “scientific
knowledge,” of episteme, of necessarily true knowledge, that has survived
in the modern notion of the radically  superior certainty of the sciences
whose results are guaranteed by experiment. In short, I AM SAYING THAT  the
Humean criticism of the validity of scientific induction is legitimate, AS

constancy of nature in certain areas is merely contingent, and our

that the ‘same’ ‘effects’ will be produced in the future by the ’same’
‘causes.’

      What do I mean by saying that a paradigm is not a particular view
(which therefore cannot be verbally specified) but a habitual ability?
Think of a person who is quite familiar with a section of a city. We say
that this person knows that section of the city. But what do we mean
thereby? That at any time that person ‘knows’ how to go from any point to
any other point within that city. It happens that if this person is an
educated adult ( at least of a Western society), he or she usually can draw
a map, or at least give verbal directions to somebody else. Thus tend to
imagine that such a person sees  a map of that section in his or her head.
But that is not the case. A primitive person may not be able to draw a map
of a region with which he or she is quite familiar, but is capable of
reaching unerringly any place. He or she may not be able to give verbal
directions, but is capable of guiding others unerringly from any place to
the place they want to reach. And to reach that place he or she doesn’t
have to give himself or herself directions, as he or she would give to
other people. This person ’just knows,’ that is, knows tacitly, in an
unspecifiable way, not objectively and predicatively, as a result of
practice. He or she possesses a practical spatial motor scheme of the
region, constantly modified according to his or her shifting positions .
This motor scheme, though, does not consist in any particular view of the
region (as a map is not a view of a region but an instrument for action to
be used by an agent who can situate himself or herself at a particular
point in the region thanks to the map, and who wants to go to another
particular point, and who can situate that desired particular point thanks
to the map). This tacit knowledge that generates the conducts of moving
unerringly and facilely in the region, which the expert possesses
(comparable to the ‘knowledge’ of the grammar of his or her language that a
native has), is acquired thanks to the experience of moving successfully in
the region. We can say that his or her body ’knows.’ This motor scheme is
what I described above as a habitual ability to move around in a certain
region to achieve our goals.*

area of the experimental sciences, and of physics in particular, which is
the area of which Kuhn is talking. A great discoverer is the creator of a
paradigm, or the one who extends it to the solution of other problems
previously not dealt with within the paradigm. A paradigm is comparable to
the habitual motor scheme just mentioned. Kuhn speaks of it as a shared way
of seeing, but he realizes that the ‘seeing’ is metaphorical. It is a
practical construction or organization of the scientific field
–unspecifiable in words because it is not objective knowledge–  that
renders possible the scientific doing, the actual problem-solving.[11] As
all habits, it is acquired or internalized through practice until its
appropriate exercise becomes automatic. The sketch or statement of the law
is an instrument comparable to a map. The problem-solving is comparable to
the use of the map by actually going to desired places until the map become
superfluous because the appropriate practice is internalized and becomes
habitual.. Very appropriately, Kuhn compares the acquisition of a paradigm
to the acquisition of a second language in which one ends up becoming
‘native’ thanks to much practice.
      Now there is a great difference between the geniuses who inaugurate
or significantly extend a paradigm and those who appropriate the paradigm
by being taught by those who already know. The difference is comparable to
that between a person who knows a region through practical familiarity with
it, so that he or she can reach any particular point from any particular
point without the help of a map (which is comparable to solving problems in
science), and a person who reaches one particular point thanks to the
directions received from someone who knows the region. The latter,
following the directions, can reach only one particular place and from one
particular place, whereas the former can reach any place, even places where
he or she has never actually been. The latter, by contrast, has not learned
‘how to solve problems,’ yet, because he or she has not appropriated the
paradigm, yet.

      The process through which we come to know a region so as to be able
to go from any place to any other place and to give directions to others is
to an extent mysterious, at least in
that the actual procedure cannot be verbally specified. All we can say is
that it is a matter of practice, or of experience through the repeated
practice of moving around in the region, as one acquires one’s situation in
the world through the repeated practice of acting in the world . In a
comparable way, Kuhn says that the student learns to do science through the
practice of solving the problems typical of the particular scientific
field; in that way he or she becomes ‘native’ in the field, as one becomes
‘native’ in a second language.

(6) The primacy of theoretical statements in Plato and Aristotle

      In his very attempt to distinguish doxa from episteme, merely
hypothetical from certain, genuine knowledge, opinion or belief from
science,  Plato had implicitly made an epistemic determination in favor of
the purely intelligible –that is, the abstract and universal rather than
the concrete and particular, and the  necessarily existent rather than the
contingent objects ,to the exclusion of sensible objects, and,
consequently, of the intellect to the exclusion of the senses as the
epistemic organ. More pragmatic than Plato regarding the degree of
certainty that is possible concerning different areas of reality, Aristotle
would reintegrate the sensible world to philosophical exploration, but
would keep the judging intellect to the exclusion of the senses as the
epistemic organ. Now, judging is a reflective operation of the intellect.
It is reflective in that the intellect examines an already formed opinion
or belief, that is, a hypothesis; weighs whether there is sufficient reason
for assenting or rejecting it, and passes judgment on that basis. Though
the judgment that results from the judging operation is not identical with
the verbal statement, the judgment reached finds its natural expression in
an affirmative or negative verbal statement, which is the only linguistic
form that expresses a claim to truth, that is, of certain correspondence
with the real –and truth is after all the business of philosophy. Hence the
exclusive concentration on statements and arguments in support of
statements, typical of the philosophical literary genre. Not for nothing
did Aristotle develop a science of logic that aimed at determining the
truth of stated-statements from the point of view of their form,
independently of their content, and in the Analytica Posteriora sketched an
ideal science composed of statements all of which either possess the self-
evidence of undeniable principles, or rest on strictly rational
demonstration.

      It is abundantly evident, though, that Aristotle did not think that
the absolute certainty of correspondence with the real at which Plato had
at least initially aimed when he conceived his theory of the Ideas could be
attained in all fields. Perhaps he even ended up thinking that such
certainty could not be attained in any field, that pursuing such certainty
was unreasonable, and that the thinker should be satisfied with the degree
of certainty that was possible within his or her given field. Thus, for
instance, he explicitly granted that in matters concerning virtuous action
the certainty attainable in mathematical matters was not possible. However,
for all his diminished optimism as to the certainty of conformity between
the mind and the real,  in at least one respect  Aristotle remained
consistent with THE CONVICTION THAT UNDERPINNED his logic and the ideal of
science expounded in the Analytica Posteriora and that had led him to
concentrate on statements, TO WIT: THAT TRUTH  CONSISTED IN CONFORMITY WITH
THE REAL, AND THAT THE ONLY TRUTH-CLAIMING AND TRUTH CONVEYING FORM OF
SPEECH WAS THE  STATEMENT.

(7) APPENDIX: ON STATEMENTS, PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE AND PRACTICAL SCIENCE

      In my paper on Devereux’s interpretation of practical knowledge in
Aristotle (2002-june-1-Devereux) I detected a harmful confusion by
Aristotle between practical knowledge and practical science, as if they
were identical. I have a hunch that something of the same confusion is
involved in the understanding of science that underpins the textbook image
of science.

      As argued by Kuhn, the textbook image of science results, not from
the (study of the) actual scientific activity that led to those results but
from a reflective study of already completed scientific results as recorded
 in the classics, This is what is TAUGHT OR COMMUNICATED OR WRITTEN by the
authors of textbooks. These are people who themselves are convinced that
knowledge is a matter of correspondence with what is the case, who believe
in Greek ‘scientific knowledge’ and who therefore confuse the knowledge of
the practicing scientists with what is stated and supposedly proven
experimentally. The same seems to be the case with “practical science,”
which is the result of Aristotle’s theoretical reflection on action, not
the deliberation of the agent actually engaged in action. By contrast,
Practical knowledge, which corresponds to Kuhn’s image of science, is the
experiential knowledge-disposition-general PROJECT to act in a certain way,
developed through symbolic application, by which the practitioner of a
science  guides his decisions in concrete situations.* Practical science,
by contrast, which corresponds to the textbook image of science, is the
objectification of the practical   judgements of the practitioner, which
are mistaken for pictures of reality. Practical science, though, is neither
knowledge nor practical, since it is not efficacious.
* Polanyi astutely observes: “It turned out that all the severely sceptical
experts had involuntarily and unknowingly signalled to the horse to stop
tapping at the point where they –knowing the right answer– expected him to
stop. This is how they made the answers invariably right; and this is
exactly how philosophers make their descriptions of science, or their
formalized procedures of scientific inference, come out right. They never
use them to decide any open scientific problem, whether past or present,
but apply them to scientific generalizations which they regards as
indubitably established... It is only when we are confronted with the
anxious dilemma of a live scientific issue, that the ambiguity of the
formal processes and of the various attenuated criteria of scientific truth
becomes (SIC) apparent, and leaves (SIC) us without effective guidance”
(Polanyi, Personal Knowledge,, pp. 169-170).

      This mistake occurs because of a preconceived idea of the nature of
science as constituted by demonstrated or proven statements,  the very idea
that led Aristotle to sketch the structure of the ideal science in the
Analytica Posteriora. and which we inherited from him. The following are
the main characteristics of that notion of science.

      Let us begin with a contemporary cultural fact. In our common
vocabulary there is the expression “scientific knowledge.” We use it by
implicit contrast to non-scientific knowledge, to ungrounded opinion,  and
even to ignorance. Indeed, by “scientific knowledge” we mean the highest
kind of knowledge – a knowledge that is unique and superior to all other
kinds by the   certainty of its truth. Now our notions of knowledge, truth,
scientific knowledge, and certainty are, as we shall presently see, deeply
marked by three Greek philosophers, namely, Socrates, Plato and Aristotle.

      By knowledge the three of them understood, either implicitly or
explicitly, the conformity between the knower’s mind or intellect (nous) to
reality. Unfortunately, there is such a thing as pseudo-knowledge, putative
knowledge that lacks certainty or simply turns out to be false. Socrates,
Plato’s master, spent his life cross-examining people who either cynically
pretended to know or honestly believed they knew, and showing them that
actually they didn’t know what they were talking about. Socrates himself
was not particularly successful in his quest for genuine knowledge, but at
least he knew that he didn’t know. Plato took matters from there. Working
on Socrates’ shoulders, he distinguished between pseudo-knowledge or merely
putative knowledge  and genuine knowledge. The former he called opinion or
belief –doxa–, and the latter episteme, by which he meant something like
“scientific knowledge” or “scientifically proven knowledge.” For knowledge
to be genuine knowledge, Plato reasoned, it needed to be true. To be true,
what was in the knower’s mind had to conform to what was actually the case.
As long, however, as such correspondence was uncertain, knowledge was not
genuine knowledge but mere opinion or belief. Thus, for a piece of
knowledge to be the genuine article, its truth, that is, its conformity to
reality, had to be necessary and grasped as such by the knower. Not for
nothing on the door of Plato’s academy an inscription prohibited the
entrance to those who did not know geometry – a deductive WRITTEN science
THE CONTENT OF all of whose statements is deducted with rational necessity
from a few definitions and self-evident axioms or principles. Plato
attempted to transfer this mathematical model to other non-mathematical
matters, such as politics and ethics, that is, to human affairs, which were
his real interest. This he thought he had achieved by postulating the
eternal EXISTENCE of the Ideas that guaranteed the truth-correspondence of
universal judgments.

      Aristotle seems to have been the victim of the same confusion in the
Analytica Posteriora, for he identified the knowing subject’s knowledge
with the content of the statements in which it is supposedly expressed or
contained, and saw that content as having to conform to what is the case in
order to be true. Indeed, that  was the point of showing that the content
of each of the statements involved in the science necessarily conformed to
what is the case and, therefore, when communicated, compelled assent to its
truth. Thus Aristotle failed to grasp the practical origin of all knowledge
 –included what goes by the name of theoretical knowledge–  and, therefore,
its applicative character.

      Of the same confusion are victims those who are surprised that
Aristotle doesn’t practice in his own work, as Euclid actually did in his
geometry, the deductive, strictly demonstrative method he presented as the
model of science in the Posterior Analytics. Indeed, neither his ethical
treatises nor any other of his works are more geometrico. Neither, as I
argued in my paper on Devereux, are even his ethical treatises really
practical, for the agent’s practical knowledge is dispositional as a result
of practice, APPLICATIVE. Thus the agent doesn’t  need to assent to the
doctrine contained in the NE, much less to have it demonstrated or proven
–which to his great regret Aristotle avows is impossible– in order to make
correct ethical decisions. (As one doesn’t need to know objectively the
grammar of one’s own native language to generate grammatically correct
sentences).The idea that the agent  needs to know that doctrine is but a
variation of the Platonic prejudice of the primacy of theoretical over
practical knowledge –knowing what is right amounts to doing what is right,
or is at least  a necessary condition for doing what is right.*
* Again, of the same mistake are victims those who think that experimental
demonstration of laws in the natural sciences or bringing to Kantian
contemplative intuition geometrical theorems is equivalent to acquiring
knowledge.

(8) Why doesn’t Aristotle follow the method of demonstrative science in his
own works?
      The answer given to those who are surprised by the fact that
Aristotle didn’t follow his deductive demonstrative model of science in his
own works is that it is a model not for research but for teaching or
presenting to others the already acquired results of research.* (Remember
Aquinas’s scientia demonstrativa and syllogisumus fciens scire, which are
in order to teach the already acquired knowledge on the assumption that the
person who acquired it acquired it by demonstrating rather than by
applying). Owen suggested as much in the above quotation, and Barnes
confirms the point in the following one: “The recipe plays small part in
his scientific and philosophical inquiries just because it is not a model
for inquiry at all but for subsequent exposition of the results of
inquiry.[12] ( BUT THE QUESTION IS  WHETHER OR NOT THE  RESULTS OF INQUIRY
EXPOSED IN THIS MANNER ARE THEMSELVES TO BE IDENTIFIED WITH THE  KNOWLEDGE
POSSESSED BY THE PRACTICING SCIENTISTS).
 * But it is not presented (not even by Aristotle) as a model for
communicating the already acquired results, but, at least implicitly, as a
model for acquiring scientific results. Hence the insistence on
demonstration of the stated results to insure that they correspond to
reality, as if that were the point in science. If Aris were interested only
in presenting the results, he would just present the statements of the laws
or theorems without any proof or demonstration or persuasive argument. His
insistence on demonstrations, when possible,  manifests, precisely, his
confusion of the agent’s practical knowledge with the practical science.
Moreover, why should this be deemed a model for communicating already
acquired results, if not because it is confused with the way in which
scientific results are actually acquired?

      Barnes’s and Owen’s explanation is not convincing. Indeed, even if he
had tried, Aristotle would have been unable to teach the results of his own
ethical research in the deductive, demonstrative shape Euclid presented the
results of his geometrical research. Actually, Aristotle confesses as much
in the NE, at least  by implication, when he admits that  not all objects
of study are rigorously regular and intelligible in the way in which the
abstract objects of geometry are. When the objects of study are of this
other kind, they simply cannot be presented more geometrico; they cannot be
demonstrated and, therefore, they are not object of scientific knowledge.
So it is not a matter of research going one way and teaching another way.
What happens is that Aristotle was trapped by his Platonic notion of
knowledge as necessarily true-correspondent, which supposedly required
universal and necessary (or necessarily existing?) objects, as shown by
Plato’s postulation of the eternal EXISTENCE of the Ideas, and aimed at
that ideal as much as the particular subject matter allowed it..

      In short, the choice of  the demonstrative way of presenting the
results of research is not accidental. It is naturally preferred by
Aristotle because, in his view, it confers on its contents the universality
and necessity that supposedly constitutes the superiority of scientific
knowledge. Demonstrating the theorems in geometry is a correlate of the
idea that the propositions that enunciate the theorems conform to reality
(that is, a case of theoretical knowledge), so that when the geometrical
proposition is demonstrated the necessity and universality of its
corresponding object has to be assented to by the knowing mind. However,
the attempt itself to demonstrate* is prompted by the confusion of science
as doctrine as if it were identical with the ‘knowledge’ by which the
practitioner actually guides his or her conduct. Since, however, THE STATED
DOCTRINE IS NOT THE SAME AS THE PRACTITIONER’S KNOWLEDGE,’ neither does the
doctrine constitute the practical knowledge by which the practitioner
guides his or her conduct, nor does the doctrine have the ontological value
or import supposedly possessed by theoretical knowledge.
* Or later, under Kant’S influence, the attempt to bring to “intuition” or
experimental evidence.

      From mistaking what is judged and stated for what is “known” or to be
known –that is, for a representation of reality–  arises the need to
demonstrate intuitively the theorem in geometry or to prove experimentally
the law in the natural sciences, as if grasping, and  assenting to, its
necessity (that is, the necessary correspondence of what is judged and
stated with what is the case) were the point. An it is, indeed, the point,
in the conception of knowledge as conformity. But not in the conception of
knowledge as the ability to respond aptly to realize one’s projects, and,
in the case of ethics, to respond in the appropriate way.

      In another context, Kuhn has an interesting observation that may be
pertinent here. The student of science is taught not by demonstration or
proof but by authority. For the sake of  simplicity in the illustration,
let us transfer his point from physics to geometry. The student of geometry
doesn’t need to know the truth of a particular theorem by demonstration in
order to solve problems in which the theorem applies. Which means that the
demonstration is irrelevant to the practice.* The student may take the
stated theorem as true on Euclid’s or on his own teacher’s authority.
Taking it as true, however, doesn’t  mean thinking that the statement re-
presents something that is the case in reality; indeed, all realities are
particular and concrete, whereas the theorem (or the law) is abstract and
‘universal.’ Taking the theorem as true means, rather, using the law
sketch, that is, the statement of the law, as a tool or guide for solving
geometrical problems either on paper or in reality, much as taking as true
your statement that my sister is on the phone is responding to it
appropriately by taking the phone rather than understanding and assenting
to it as a doctrine that is true, that is, as a picture of reality to be
contemplated.
* The demonstration is irrelevant because it leads to, and terminates in,
intellectual assent to the stated, whereas the statement of the law aims at
being understood by being ‘acted upon’ or ‘dwelt in’ or ‘applied,’ as
Galileo did in order to formulate the relation among the relevant factors
expressed by the law.

      I am uncomfortable with the expression “taking as true,” which occurs
repeatedly in t he previous paragraph. There is only one appropriate
meaning in the tradition for that expression, to wit: believing or
assenting to the stated as representing and corresponding to what is the
case in reality. In practical matters, however, STATEMENTS do not mean or
function that way, and if they are so understood they are misunderstood in
a radical way as by someone who is not a competent speaker of the language
in which statements are made. An instance of this radical misunderstanding:
if I shout “Fire” in a theater, and you don’t move because you think that I
am just saying that there is the glowing-reddish stuff, as if it were a
mere visual phenomenon to which you are not exposed, and if you are
convinced that that is the way statements mean in English, then it is
evident that you don’t understand English because you are not a competent
speaker of English.

       But what if you don’t move because you don’t believe my statement
–that is, you don’t assent to my statement as being true? The assumption
here is that you understand perfectly my statement,  you just don’t believe
it. Isn’t this distinction legitimate?  Only if you think, with Searle,
that language says what it means in the sense of being informative of what
is said, and that getting the information by understanding it constitutes
successful linguistic communication (independently of –in the case of
statements–  whether or not the piece of information is believed).* But
Searle is working on the wrong linguistic model. His position seems to make
sense only because his model of language is written language, or the
defused oral language spoken in  language in a course of language, where by
convention statements are not to be believed , that is, not ‘acted upon,’
because they are not genuine statements, and orders are not to be obeyed
because they are not genuine orders. In real life, though, and in situation
of exposure in which language functions that is not the case at all. An
order can be obeyed or disobeyed, but only because primarily it is to be
obeyed, not what it ‘says’ to be understood. If no obedient or disobedient
action ever followed an order, and if no positive or negative sanction ever
followed the obeying or disobeying an order, a child would never learn the
linguistic game of commands, no matter how well the child understood the
words of commands in a classroom. Perceptual language, that is the language
by which agents communicate to each other what they perceive – which is the
paradigmatic case of the use of language– is not communication among pure
contemplators, but among exposed participants. That is why speech acts are
acts, not merely intelligible sounds that say something.
* If all I intended by shouting “Fire!” in the movie theater was for you to
get the picture, why am I surprised, indeed baffled by your doing nothing,
even if you assure me that you understand what I said?

      What, then, about statements in particular? If, in the office we
share, I tell you, “Your sister is on the phone,” the appropriate
linguistic response is for you to take the phone and talk to your sister.
The reason is that a statement is not meant to convey  a piece of objective
information to be assented to, or at least to be understood as
representational of what I believe to be the case. If you ‘get the picture”
but do nothing, and if you think that you have understood my statement, you
are not a competent speaker of English, for you don’t know how to play the
linguistic game of statements.  Certainly, you may not believe what I
state, in which case you will not respond to the stated as stated. But this
doesn’t imply that statements are meant to be assented to or dissented
with, in the sense of believed or disbelieved. Rather, they are meant to be
acted upon, to in-form the addressee’s action, to be read as maps for
action. Disbelieving, here, means not being disposed to act upon or apply
what is  stated; it doesn’t primarily mean refusing intellectual assent to
what is stated.* Refusing to act upon the stated, that is, to map one’s
action according to the statement, is a legitimate linguistic response,
much as disobeying is, because it coheres with the way statements function.
By contrast, refusing assent is not a legitimate linguistic response,
because it implies a misunderstanding, not of the particular statement, but
of the way statements mean. This is what I call a radical misunderstanding.
* Of course, if your statement does not dispose me to act upon it, we are
going to find out when we reflect and discuss its truth that I don’t assent
to it. And the inverse is the case if your statement disposes me to act
upon it. Hence intra-paradigmatic truth. But this doesn’t imply the primacy
of contemplation over action and the subordination of action to
contemplation.

      Now, in the Greek philosophical tradition, underpinned by the notion
of knowledge as correspondence, the meaning of “believing” is judging with
assent what is the case, or assenting to a statement that informs of what
is the case by representing it, which is very different from “acting upon”
or “dwelling in” what one as an exposed agent judges to be the case, or
“acting upon” what another exposed agent judges to be the case. Thus, what
I am saying is that, concerning statements, Aristotle committed a radical
misunderstanding by seeing them as expressive of what is the case in
reality and, therefore, as demanding assent as the appropriate response.
Moreover, the Greek tradition separates knowledge (that is, conformity with
what is the case) from action, and makes of the former a condition for
action. Which of course creates the unsolvable problem of the passage from
“is” to ‘ought,” from the objective knowledge of what is the case to acting
in response to what is stated.

      As a concrete example of the two different senses of “believing,”
take belief in God. According to Aquinas, faith is intellectual assent to
what God says, to what God reveals, on the basis of His authority. Yet he
deems mere intellectual assent to be mortua fides. Why dead, if the person
assents to what God  reveals, and nothing more is included in, and demanded
by, his definition of faith? The case would be different if Aquinas had
said that God doesn’t give us pieces of information to be assented to,
about what is the case with Himself or with anything else, but laws or maps
for appropriate action in response to Him or to something else related to
Him. In that case faith or belief, as a habit, is the disposition to
respond to God in the way appropriate to his revelation.*
* Plantinga with his distinction between believing-that and believing-in is
a perfect instance of what I have called the radical misunderstanding of
the stating linguistic game.

(9) Does the medical practitioner need to know the medical doctrine stated
in books?
      Let us return to our question concerning whether the medical
practitioner  needs to know the medical doctrine. The question arose from
the fact that, as established in my previous paper, the ethical agent or
practitioner has no need to know the ethical doctrine elaborated by
Aristotle in his ethical treatises in order to act correctly. Is the case
the same for the medical practitioner?
Does the practitioner need to know the theoretical doctrine in order to be
successful? Or is the knowledge of the doctrine superfluous for the
practice, as is the case with the knowledge of the doctrine contained in
Aristotle’s ethical treatises?

      As observed by Aristotle himself, the theoretical or doctrinal
knowledge that lean meats are in reality (something to be assented to)
wholesome or salutary is not efficacious  without the practical knowledge
of lean meats, that consists in the disposition to treat certain meats as
lean-healthy by prescribing them to cure certain ill people. Now this
practical knowledge is acquired through experience of the salutary effects
of the meats therefore identified as lean. As Kuhn indicates, the words are
learned on the occasion of the experience of the salutary effects of
certain meats and together with the practical ability to identify those
lean meats. In other words, the purely theoretical doctrine about lean
meats being salutary is, by itself, practically meaningless and
superfluous. Moreover, as observed by Hume, it is undemonstrable because it
is not ‘rational.’

      Of course, there is a significant difference between ethics and
medicine. The practice of ethics deals exclusively in concrete particulars.
The ethical generalizations of the written doctrine are mere rules of thumb
drawn from reflection on the past actions of the prudent agents, which
rules of thumb, as acknowledged by Aristotle himself, are to be superseded
by the agent’s judgment in the particular case when they are at odds with
that judgment. In this case the judgment of the prudent agent is the only
standard. In medicine, by contrast, there are genuine observational or
experiential regularities, such as light meats are wholesome for bilious
people (at a primitive stage of medicine), or such drugs are efficient in
curing cancer of the prostate because they are of the kind that inhibits
the multiplication of unhealthy cells (at a higher level of development of
medicine). This is the doctrine to be applied by the skillful practitioner,
who in his or her turn becomes the standard. In ethical matters, whether or
not the practitioner, that is,  the agent,  is virtuous is all-important
because it determines whether or not the agent will in her action pursue
the good as her goal. In medicine the goal of curing can be  assumed to be
spontaneously shared by the practitioners of medicine, for they joined the
profession for the sake of curing. Because of these differences the ethical
doctrine can at best be constituted by rules of thumb drawn from the past
actions of prudent agents, and can be ignored when the particular case
dictates a different judgment. But the case of medicine is different.
Ethics is not a science (or an Aristotelian ‘art,’) in any  way, for these
involve at least the “universality’ based on strict past uniformity,
whereas in matters of action each particular case is different. Medicine,
by contrast,  is an art, though far less exact or uniform in its
applications than physics. Indeed, the natural regularities in physics are
simpler, more accessible and  measurable than those in medicine, which
renders their applicative use simpler (because more regular, less subject
to deliberation) than in medicine, but this doesn’t exclude the less
regular but real regularities in medicine.*
* The regularity in medicine is in terms of the uniform goal of producing
health, which depends in part on the regularity of effects on the object’
side. But there is no uniform goal in ethical action, for the
circumstantial effects of an action may be good for one person and bad for
another, and good in terms of one criterion and bad in terms of a different
criterion. Hence tragic conflicts for the good agent.
                                                               --------

       Now, these real but less exact and more complex regularities are,
indeed,  to be known by the medical practitioner. Otherwise the
practitioner would have no regular expectations and would be like the
ethical agent whose only model, for lack of regularity, is the prudent
agent’ actions.  But ‘known,’ here, means appropriated or internalized
through (in the case of the disciple but not of the discoverer, initially
supervised) practice as guides for action under the appropriate
circumstances, rather than to be assented to and taken for necessarily true
or resemblant pictures of something in the world – a world populated by
such and such factual entities (as the Aristotelian ‘natures’ seem to be.
Hence the dictum, “Entia non sunt multiplicanda sine necessitate”).




      This may be the opportunity to tackle the issue of the ontological
value of laws, theorems, etc., which so far I have failed to confront. To
formulate the problem in an acute way, God is, for me, the central
character in the narrative story of the world in which I too participate as
a character. I know Him by responding to Him, and being disposed to respond
to Him,  appropriately. Obviously, however, God is not because I respond to
Him and only insofar as I respond to Him. Now, what I have been saying may
sound as if that were the case, for knowledge consists in practical
disposition to act under the appropriate circumstances. But knowledge only
makes the relationship of the knower to the known possible in the measure
in which the known is known; it doesn’t substitute for, or suppress, the
independence of the known from the knower.

      Laws and theorems are abstract generalizations of uniformities meant
as guides for actions. The more rigorous the uniformity, the more
“necessary” it appears to the knowing mind as a sufficient reason for
assenting. But the uniformities or regularities function as hypothetical
laws for action; the hypothesis becomes particularized in the particular
application by the interested expert agent. Through practice of
applications, the agent interiorizes and renders habitual the appropriate
way of ‘seeing,’ the appropriate gestalt that favors the appropriate
action. But the particular objects for response – and God is one of them,
though absolutely unique – are not abstract generalizations; they are
singular agents –the ones who make the generalizations in order to respond.
They exist for the agent in the sense of respondenda. to be responded to by
 singular agents in particular circumstances. Initially the appropriate way
to respond to them is indicated by heredity and culture, and is learned by
the human agent linguistically through guided application confirmed by
communicative success. But for linguistic agents there are not only common
names but also proper names as rules for action.
