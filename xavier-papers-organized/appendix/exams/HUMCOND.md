                       CLUSTER ON "THE HUMAN CONDITION"
Course: Existentialism (Phl. 360)
       The  very  expression  "The  Human  Condition"  was  coined   by   an
existentialist. This course, which focuses on Albert Camus'
philosophical and literary works, follows in detail his personal  evolution.
Starting with the 'existentialist' view that he contributed  to  popularize,
according to which  the  human  condition  is  characterized  by  its  utter
absurdity, Camus draws the conclusion that morals are a matter of  arbitrary
preference. Further reflection, however, prompted by his active  involvement
against  Nazism,  leads  Camus  to  the  realization  that  the  appropriate
response to the absurdity of the human condition is not  "to  add  absurdity
to absurdity" but "to create happiness in a world of unhappiness."   In  the
process,  Camus  contrasts  his  final  position  with  the  way  in   which
Christians view the human condition, which adds a further dimension  to  the
theme.  The  close  connection   which   Camus   establishes   between   the
interpretation of the human condition and the kind of ethics  by  which  one
lives renders its practical relevance to this theme, which  at  first  sight
might appear as purely theoretical.
