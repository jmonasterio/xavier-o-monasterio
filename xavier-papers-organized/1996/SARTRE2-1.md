Sartre on the existence of others

Katherine Morris's article in the latest issue of Sartre Studies International furnishes me a welcome opportunity to go back to Sartre's treatment of the Other's existence, which I have always found fascinating, but about whose actual import and philosophical value I have wavered through the years. Why not attempt to make up my mind once and for all?

Morris takes Gregory MaCulloch to task for having misunderstood "not just what Sartre is saying but what Sartre is doing " in Being and Nothingness concerning the Other's existence. ^(E1)  Though an analytic philosopher herself, Morris thinks that McCulloch "imposes our conceptions onto Sartre's dicussion and criticises him on that basis." ^(E2). In Morris's opinion,

Sartre does repay analytical treatment: that is to say, he himself is a philosopher, and a good one. But it does not follow from this that he is engaged in exactly the familiar sort of enterprises that we analytical philosophers are engaged in". ^(E3)  

Morris's view of what Sartre is saying and doing

Sartre is saying that the Other appears to me in two different ways: as an object and as a subject. As an object in my perceptual experience, my knowledge of the Other as another like me, that is, as an experiencing subject, is only probable, since in this mode I do not experience the Other as experiencing subject but as experienced object. Therefore I can never be absolutely certain that this object that looks and acts like me who am a subject is in fact a subject, however probable that may be. But let this Other-object I have been observing look at me, let this Other catch me peering through a keyhole, and in my very shame of myself before this Other I experience myself as object experienced by the experiencing Other.  Shame is, in other words, the experiential revelation of my objectivity for the Other as subject. Thus, I know the Other as subject in my very experience of being known as an object by the Other.

As for the certainty of my knowledge of the Other as subject, Sartre maintains that it is absolute. The reason for this, as Morris explains, is that, unlike in the case of my knolwledge of the Other as object, where knowledge involves an extrapolation from what is intuited--namely the appearance and the behavior of this human-looking body--to what is not intuited--namely, the Other's subjectivity--, in the case of my knowledge of the Other as subject there is no extrapolation to be done; rather, the Other's subjectivity is intuitively or directly experienced in the very experience of my being object of experience for the Other, of my being ashamed of being object for the Other.

The intuitive immediacy to me of the Other as subject, argues Morris, is, in Sartre's view, of the same type as the intuitive immediacy of my own existence as subject to myself, expressed in the Cartesian cogito . Consequently, for the same reason, the existence of the Other as subject is as infallibly certain as my own existence as subject. Unpacking the fact of the intuitive immediacy of this knowledge of the Other as subject and its paralelism to the Cartesian cogito , Morris comments:

... the fact that he [Sartre] feels shame in these circumstances does not give him a reason for believing that Others exist, it does not prove their existence, as if before he was uncertain of his own existence and only afterwards he was certain... Shame simply discloses a certainty that was already there. ^(E4)  

What, then, is Sartre actually doing , according to Morris?

In appealing to our experience of shame, he is furnishing us " watertight " argument of the Other's existence, thereby dissolving the traditional sceptical arguments concerning the Other's existence.   

Morris's criticism of McCulloch

The Sartrean distinction between knowledge of objects and knowledge of subjects is alien to the analytical tradition, McCulloch misses it completely.  As a consequence, he expects Sartre's 'proof' of the Other's existence, that is, of the existence of the Other as subject, to function as a proof of the existence of an object would, which would at best yield probability rather than certain knowledge.  Hence McCulloch accuses Sartre of wavering between simply assuming the Other's existence on the basis of a merely subjective feeling of certainty, and affirming the Other's existence on the basis of empirical knowledge which is never absolutely certain.

In Morris's view, the Sartrean distinctions between the Other as subject and the Other as object as well as between  empirical knowledge of the Other, which is merely probable and is at best knowledge of the Other as object, and intuitive knowledge of the Other as subject, which is certain, are alien to the analytic tradition. As a result, McCulloch completely misses them, which  leads him to a totally wrongheaded interpretation of what Sartre is saying and doing. When Sartre speaks of "knowledge," the analytic philosopher hears "justified true belief," so that McCulloch thinks that the experience of shame is offered by Sartre as reason for believing that the Other exists. When Sartre speaks of "certainty" the analytic philosopher simply hears

'very strong belief' or 'subjective conviction' (which can therefore be false. But the distinction Sartre draws between knowledge and certainty is not that between justified true belief and mere strong conviction, but between empirical knowledge and what Descartes called 'intuitive' 'knowledge.' The claim that I am certain of something (say my own existence is thus not a 'weaker' claim  than that I know something; certainty of something is definitely not compatible with the thing's being false. ^(E5)

Since McCulloch thinks that Sartre is furnishing a proof in the analytic sense, that is, se, "a reason  of " the existence of the Other. This amounts to charging Sartre with the intention of proving the existence of the Other as object, which of course would yield mere probability. Unsurprisingly, McCulloc finds such proof defective and far below the level of absolute certainty that Sartre claims for his own kind of proof. Hence McCulloch accuses Sartre of simply assuming that the Other exists, on the basis of his naive, natural but purely subjective certainty that the Other exists, and calls Sartre's proof "pretty weak."

From original page 1. When analytic philosophers speak of "knowledge" they mean "justified true belief."

On this conception, what counts as justification can be empirical, logical, conceptual, intuitive or anything you like. Sartre's conception of knowlege is much more specific and delimited... Only empirical justification yields knowledge in his sense. At the same time, McCulloch interprets Sartre's term 'certainty' as simply meaning....   Sartre cautions us against 'a confusion... between two distinct orders of knowledge and two types of being which canot be compared' (BN 276); McCulloc, I submit, has not heeded this caution." ^(E6)

Then, to compound his misunderstanding, McCulloch ends up saying that, after all, the failure of Sartre's proof is of no great consequence, for Sartre was not engaged in addressing the traditional sceptical arguments concerning the Other's existence but was doing something different. In McCuilloch's own words, "Sartre is developing a non-Cartesian framework, and so it is far from clear that sceptical worries are inevitable or urgent..." ^(E7)      

 A curious aspect of the dispute between Morris and McCulloch is that McCulloch, who thinks that Sartre's argument for the Other's existence fails and, therefore, that Sartre simply assumes the Other's existence, finds "Sartre's procedure... to be above reproach" ^(E8), whereas Morris thinks that if McCulloch's criticism of Sartre's were justified it would be serious to the point of being "damning:" "after all, if Sartre is simply assuming that we encounter Others, then he is simply assuming, that Others as subjects exist, and to call this an 'assumption' is surely incompatible with calling it certain in Sartre's sense of the term." ^(E9)  Obviously they see Sartre doing very different things in his treatment of the Other's existence.  Unlike Morris, McCulloch considers his own criticism of Sartre's argument justified; yet he doesn't think that it is fatal to Sartre's undertaking because McCulloch doesn't think that Sartre is trying to respond to the classical skeptical arguments against the knowledge of the existence of other minds but is, rather, "developing a non-Cartesian framework . ^(E10) For her part, Morris thinks that, if justified, McCulloch's argument would be fatal to Sartre because she obviously thinks that Sartre is actually responding to the classical skepticism about the existence of other minds.   

A very annoying feature

A very annoying feature of Morris's article is her avowed non-evaluative stand. Supposedly she is just explaining what Sartre is saying and doing, so that a fairer evaluation than McCulloch's own, which rests on a total misunderstanding, may be made on the basis of her explanation. However, Morris claims that she herself is not at all engaged in such an evaluative enterprise.

"Sartre's notion of being-for-others is intricate and, I think, of great philosophical importance; that I have said nothing about it reflects, not lack of concern, but the fact that the 'logic' of the argument concerning the existence of others doesn't require a full exploration of the 'content' of the concept of being-for-others." (Note 29, p. 62).

"I do not mean to suggest that there are no criticisms that could be made of Sartre's arguments or procedures, though I have not chosen to pursue them here. But any such criticisms must surely be based on a fair-minded understanding of what he is actually saying and doing" (Note 31, p. 62).

Why should this non-evaluative position be annoying? Because in fact Morris doesn't observe it. After all, doesn't she categorically state against McCulloch that Sartre's argument based on the experience of shame, when correctly understood, is "watertight," that it yields absolute certainty? And doesn't this amount to saying that Sartre's treatment of the problem of the Other's existence is essentially successful, that the traditional sceptical arguments against the Other's existence have been dissolved? However, since supposedly Morris herself is making no evaluation, she cannot be called questionned as to the validity of Sartre's argument. In this way, she can dismiss McCulloch's rejection of the certainty of the knowledge of the Other's existence on the technicality that McCulloch doesn't really understand what Sartre is talking about, without herself being open to objections as to the validity of Sartre's argument. Let me, however, follow Morris's own indirect tip as to the possibility of criticizing Sartre's arguments or procedures, which she chose not to pursue in her article.

Let us not beat around the bush and go directly to the critical point. Why precisely does McCulloch reject Sartre's contention that he, or anybody else for that matter, can know with certainty the Other's existence? Because, McCulloch argues, on the one hand, Sartre claims that the experience of shame infallibly reveals to the ashamed person the Other's existence as subject, that is, as someone who looks at the ashamed person and to whom he or she is KNOWN AS object, and, on the other hand, Sartre concedes that the ashamed person can discover that he or she was mistaken in believing that he or she was looked at. Indeed, as Sartre himself writes,

Nevertheless, someone will say, the fact remains that I can discover that I have been mistaken.  Here I am bent over the keyhole; suddenly I hear a footstep. I shudder as a wave of shame sweeps over me. Someone has seen me. I straighen up. My eyes run over the deserted corridor. It was a false alarm. I breathe a sigh of relief (BN 277). ^(E11)           

Now, McCulloch reads this passage as an admission on Sartre's part that the experience of shame is compatible with the non-existence of the Other, given that one can be ashamed when in fact there is no Other before whom to be ashamed. However, Morris argues that there is no such admission on Sartre's part, since Sartre presents the case of the mistaken person not as his own view but as the view of an objector,  and his own reply to the objection is the following:

What then is it which falsely appeared... when I discovered the false alarm? It is not the Other-as-subject... It is the Other's facticity , that is,

the contingent connection  between the Other and an-object-being in my world. Thus what is doubtful is not the Other himself. It is the Other's being there ; i.e., that concrete, historical event which we can express by the words "There is someone in the room (BN 277. ^(E12)

It is clear, then, that, contrary to what McCulloch believes, Sartre does not concede but rather positively denies that the ashamed person can be wrong concerning the Other as subject. Thus Morris's comment is quite right:

Although it is evidently tempting to suppose that the claim is that I can believe that I am being Looked-at when in fact I am not, this is not Sartre's claim. The claim he makes here is simply that I can ( in fact ) be Looked-at even where there is no concrete set of eyes to do the Looking. That he is being Looked-at in this example is never called into question. ^(E13)

Hence Morris's verdict on McCulloch's mistake, which confirms her view that MaCulloch is blind to those Sartrean distinctions which are not current in the analytic tradition:

McCulloch has, in short, confused that Other as subject with the actual concrete presence of the Other, the presence of the Other as a possible object in my world ." ^(E14)

Now, it is interesting to note that Morris herself acknowledges how "evidently tempting" it is to side with the objector, as McCulloch does, and conclude that Sartre's proof of the Other's existence fails miserably, rather than siding with Sartre who coldly maintains that I can be Looked-at even when there is no set of eyes to do the looking. Morris gives no explanation for this evident temptation. She just assumes that McCulloch, because of his analytic blindness, is incapable of understanding Sartre concerning the difference between the Other as subject and the Other as object, as well as the difference between the infallible certainty of the intuitive knowledge of the existenc of the Other as subject and the  mere probability of the empirical knowledge of the Other as object. Because the only alternative is the one proposed by Sartre. And this alternative is so nonsensical that one can excuse McCulloch for not having taken seriously the existence of the ghostly, eyeless Other-subject by whom one is supposedly Looked-at from nowhere. Yet, if Sartre is right, such is the Other-subject whose existence is revealed to us in our experience of shame and thanks to whose absent presence the rift of solipsism  is overcome.

Is Morris herself, who finds evidently tempting McCulloch's mistake, really willing to follow Sartre to the bitter end of his logic? In other words, does she really believe in the existence of the ghostly Other-subject yielded by Sartre's water-tight argument? One cannot answer these questions one way or another. For though she does "not mean to suggest that there are no criticisms that could be made of Sartre's arguments or procedures,' she has "chosen not to pursue them here." ^(E15) What she has chosen is, rather, to defend against McCulloch's interpretation what Sartre is actually saying and doing.

McCulloch thinks that Sartre is offering a proof of the Other's existence, a clinching reason for believing that Others exist. No wonder that McCulloch found Sartre's supposed proof "pretty hopeless." Indeed, Sartre himself would have found any proof of this kind not only pretty hopeless but totally wrongheaded, for it could lead at best to probable knowledge of the existence of the Other as object in myu world rather than to the intuitive, infallible certainty of the Existence of the Other as subject . Now it is precisely the Other missing in the unavoidable solipsism of both the realists and the idealists criticized by Sartre. Thus, Morris concludes, contrary to McChulloch's wrongheaded opinion, "Sartre's 'argument'... far from being 'pretty hopeless', is watertight." ^(E16)
