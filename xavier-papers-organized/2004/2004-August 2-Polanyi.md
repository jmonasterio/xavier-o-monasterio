            Michael Polanyi: Linguistic action and Tacit Knowledge[1]

Tacit Knowledge

      Initially, Michael Polanyi introduced the notion of “tacit
knowledge,” by which he became known, to refer to those cases in which a
speaker “knows more than he can verbalize.” He exemplified this situation
with the experiment conducted by psychologists Lazarus and McCleary in
1949.

      “These authors presented a person with a large number of nonsense
      syllables, and after showing certain of the syllables, they
      administered an electric shock. Presently the person showed symptoms
      of anticipating the shock at the sight of ‘shock syllables’; yet, on
      questioning, he could not identify them. He had come to know when to
      expect a shock, but he could not tell what made him expect it. He had
      acquired a knowledge similar to that which we have when we know a
      person by signs we cannot tell.”[2]

      On reflection, the very notion of tacit knowledge (from now on TK),
as characterized by Polanyi, sounds nonsensical. Indeed, it amounts to an
unconscious knowledge, for the subject who supposedly knows is not aware of
what he knows, since he cannot say what he knows. Now, an unconscious
knowledge is an oxymoron. Why? Because the very notion of knowledge, or at
least the ordinary notion of knowledge that common sense seems to impose on
us, implies on the subject’s part the awareness of what he knows. He knows
what he knows thanks to the awareness he has of what he knows, so that he
would not know what he knows without that awareness.
      As an example of this ordinary notion of knowledge, let us consider
the knowledge of which Searle spoke in the preceding chapter, since he used
the word ‘knowledge’ in its ordinary meaning. According to Searle, in
linguistic communication the hearer who understands what the speaker says
thereby acquires the knowledge  –that is, becomes aware– of what the
speaker intends to communicate to him. And it would be inconceivable that
the hearer knew what the speaker intends to communicate if the hearer had
no awareness at all of what he knew. In other words, what a subject knows
becomes object known to him by the very fact of his becoming aware of it.
Hence no knowledge of object without awareness of what is known, because
knowledge implies and demands the awareness of the object known. Thus, when
Polanyi defines KT as a knowledge in which the subject cannot say what he
supposedly knows, we have to conclude that KT is a knowledge in which the
subject is not aware of what he knows, which is a contradiction in terms.


      For his part, Polanyi would reply to this objection by referring us
to the undeniable facts of the experiment he described: the subject of the
experiment cannot say which are the shock-producing syllables because he
does not know them, that is, is not aware of them, has no consciousness of
them. Yet the subject’s behavior in reaction to those syllables shows that
he knows them in a certain way –and this certain way of knowing is what
Polanyi calls CT.

      As these facts seem undeniable, we cannot ignore them. Let us then
try to see things more clearly. For this purpose, it might be helpful to
compare Polanyi’s TK to what Sartre called “non-thetic or non-positional
awareness” in an analysis that led him to his famous notion of “Bad
faith.”[3]  According to Sartre, the “censor” of which Freud speaks and to
which Freud attributes the task of preventing the psychoanalysand from
acknowledging his own shameful impulses by rendering them unconscious, is
nothing else than the psychoanalysand himself who prevents himself from
acknowledging them precisely because he is conscious of those. How, indeed,
Sartre reasons, could the Freudian censor censure only the shameful
impulses and not others, if they were totally unconscious? What happens
actually, answers Sartre, is that the psychoanalysand, who is conscious of
his shameful impulses, does not want to assume responsibility for them.
Now, what renders possible this hypocrisy on the psychoanalysand’s part,
who pretends to himself not to know at all the impulses he himself censures
with such discrimination, is that he is conscious or aware of them only
under the mode of not being explicitly aware of them, because he has not of
them the kind of thetic or positional consciousness of object we normally
have of what  we know. The psychoanalysand, in Sartre’s view, has only an
implicit or not verbalized awareness of his shameful impulses, and his
awareness is only implicit precisely because it does not reach the level of
verbal formulation. In short, the psychoanalysand knows his shameful
impulses because he is conscious of them, yet he can pretend not to know
them because he is only non-positionally aware of them, that is, cannot
verbalize what he is aware of.

      Let us now inquire whether Sartre’s non-positional consciousness is
equivalent to Polanyi’s TK. Thanks to his notion of non-positional
consciousness Sartre can show, against Freud, that the psychoanalysand is
conscious of his shameful impulses, that is, knows them, and is therefore
responsible for his refusal to assume responsibility for them – a
responsibility of which he is discharged by Freud under the pretext that
they are unconscious. Sartre’s reply is that in order  to be conscious of
something and, hence, to know it, it is not required to verbalize
explicitly that of which one is only non-positionally conscious.
Consequently,  the psychoanalysand does not formulate to himself that of
which he is implicitly aware simply because he does not want to. Indeed,
that is why Sartre considers him responsible for his shameful impulses. But
this very trait of Sartre’s non-positional consciousness, which he rightly
equates with our ordinary notion of knowledge because it involves the
consciousness of what is known (though not the explicit consciousness
brought about by verbalization), cannot be equated with Polanyi’s TK. The
reason is that the subject who has TK of the shock-producing syllables does
not know at all, either explicitly or even implicitly, which are those
syllables. Different in this from the Sartrean psychoanalysand, the subject
of the experiment does not identify verbally the shock-producing syllables,
not because he does not want to render explicit his knowledge of his
shameful impulses in order not to have to assume responsibility for them,
but because the subject of the experiment literally has no knowledge of
them.

      Let us be clear. Sartre does not reject the ordinary notion of
knowledge as consciousness of object. He simply claims that the
consciousness in which knowledge consists can take two forms: the form of
non-positional consciousness of object, thanks to verbalization, as well as
the less common form of merely implicit consciousness of object. In other
words, verbalization is not a necessary condition for knowledge, because
the consciousness in which knowledge consists is possible without
verbalization. Verbalization only renders explicit the implicit
consciousness of object that the subject possesses before verbalization.
Thus, Sartre’s subject is initially non-positionally aware of his shameful
impulses, so that, if he so decides, he can express in words the
characteristics of the object of which he is non-positionally aware. The
subject who possesses TK, by contrast, cannot say which are the shock-
producing syllables because he does not know them at all. In other words,
if TK is knowledge, it is not knowledge in the ordinary sense of the word,
for it does not consist in awareness of object.

      For my present purposes it is irrelevant whether or not one accepts
Sartre’s view of implicit consciousness. His analysis was only helpful in
that it shows that the commonly accepted notion of  knowledge, in whatever
variety,  implies consciousness of object, for it is thanks to that
consciousness that the object becomes known to the subject.

      What now about Polanyi’s TK? In what sense is it knowledge, if at
all?

      In his first analysis of the notion of TK, in The tacit Dimension,
referring to the experiment with the shock-producing syllables, Polanyi’s
emphasis was on the fact that the subject knows more than he can say. Later
on, however, the emphasis shifts to what Polanyi calls “interiorization.”

the fact that TK is intrinsically connected with action. Herein lies the
radical difference between the ordinary notion of knowledge and Polanyi’s
TK: the ordinary notion of knowledge involves the consciousness of object
as vision of its object, with no direct  relationship to action.

      Let us follow in detail Polanyi’s analysis of TK. According to the
traditional notion, knowledge consists in the revelation of the object to
the knowing subject thanks to the awareness he has of the object. This
awareness of object amounts to  is a purely theoretical vision of the
object known. (Let us remember that the Greek word 2,@D4" means vision as
opposed to action.) TK, by contrast, does not involve any objective
content, because it doe not function as awareness of object but as
instrument for action.

      The instrumental role of TK by contrast to the vision or revelation
of object involved in the ordinary notion of knowledge may be illustrated
by means of a comparison to the way in which eyeglasses function. When the
eyeglasses function as such, that is when they are worn by someone, not
when they lie on a table and are looked at, the person who wears the
eyeglasses does not see them, that is, has no awareness of them as object
of knowledge, as is the case when they lie on a table and are looked at.
When the eyeglasses are worn, they do not function as object known but as
an instrument. Now, we have to resist the temptation to complete the
preceding sentence by saying that when worn on the nose the eyeglasses
function as instrument of knowledge, that is as instrument to acquire
knowledge of other objects than the eyeglasses, the objects seen through
the eyeglasses. But that is not what Polanyi would say. He would say that
the eyeglasses function as instruments for action, that is, as instruments
that guides the agent’s action. Such is the role played by TK, according to
Polanyi, and therein lies the radical difference between our ordinary
notion of knowledge and TK. But in order to fully grasp this difference we
have to follow in some detail Polanyi’s analysis of TK in which the knowing
subject of the classical epistemological tradition is replaced by the
agent.

      Depuis le debut Polanyi avait placé l’agent au centre de son analyse
de la CT, puisqu’il commencait par nous referer au cas ou “the subject sait
ou connait plus que ce qu’il peut dire.”
Cependant, ce n’est qu’avec la notion d’ “interiorisation,” introduite plus
tard, ainsi qu’avec les exemples fournis par Polanyi pour illustrer cette
notion, qu’il nous sera possible de parvenir a comprendre le deplacement du
sujet connaissant de la tradition epistemologique classique par l’agent,
impliqué par la CT.

      Interiorizing something, Polanyi explains, is to identify ourselves
with it by making it function as the immediate term of TK, thereby
transcending it toward something else. To illustrate his point Polanyi
mentions the case of the blind person who, as the result of a long
practice, finds his way with the help of a stick. This blind person knows
how to avoid the obstacles by “inhabiting” –by opposition to “observing” or
“paying attention to”– the sensations he experiences in his hand every time
the stick impinges on something. “Inhabiting” is different from
“knowing.”The sensations experienced by the blind person are not known in
themselves by the blind person; rather, they function as “that from which”
the person finds his way. If the blind person is asked to what he pays
attention, he won’t mention the sensations he experiences in his hand, for
he has no objective awareness of them. Yet he uses them as instruments to
find his way. He ”inhabits” them, or “dwells in” them, as when we walk we
use the sensations we experience on the plant of our foot at each step we
make, we transcend them as “that from which” we make the next step. The
blind person doesn’t know the sensations he experiences in the palm of his
hand, he knows them only tacitly in that he uses them to make his next
step. He ‘inhabits” them as we inhabit our body when we use it as
instrument to realize our projects. Thus, when  we walk we don’t pay
attention to the sensations we experience on the plant of our feet every
time they touch the ground; we “dwell” in them to make the next step, we
pass through them as “that from which” we make the next step, much as right
now I pass through the sensations I experience at the tips of my fingers
every time I type on the keyboard toward the word I want to write. The
stick in the hand of the blind man has become an extension of his body, for
he dwells in it as he dwells in his body. Given that TK functions as an
instrument, not as object of knowledge, it is not, in Polanyi’s words, a
‘knowledge what” but a “knowledge how.”

      Descartes eut certainment une intuition passagere de cette forme de
conscience, mais sans realiser sa valeur instrumentale pour l’action,
lorsqu’il distinga la conscience que chacun de nous a de son propre corps
de la conscience d’objet, c’est-a-dire, de ce q ue lui-meme comme membre
d’une longue tradition epistemologique entendait par connaissance:

      “La nature m’apprend aussi par ces sensations de douleur, de faim, de
      soif, etc., que je ne suis pas seulement loge dans mon corps comme le
      pilote est loge dans son bateau, mais que je suis rattache a mon corps
      si tres etroitement et comme melange avec lui que je forme avec lui
      une seule chose, pour ainsi dire.”[4]

      We have seen that Polanyi had initially explained TK as knowing more
than one can say. The “interiorization” involved in TK helps him to become
more precise:

      “The identification of tacit knowing with indwelling involves a shift
      of  emphasis in our conception of tacit knowing. We had envisaged
      tacit knowing in the first place as a way to know more than we can
      tell. We identified the two terms of tacit knowing, the proximal and
      the distal, and recognized the way we attend from the first to the
      second, thus achieving an integration of  particulars to a coherent
      entity to which we are attending. Since we were not attending to the
      particulars in themselves, we could not identify them; but if we now
      regard the integration of particulars as an interiorization, it takes
      on a more positive character. It now becomes a means of making certain
      things function as the proximal terms of tacit     knowing, so that
      instead of observing them in themselves, we may be aware of them in
      their bearing on the comprehensive entity which they constitute. It
      brings home to us that it is not by looking at things, but by dwelling
      in them, that we understand their joint meaning... By concentrating
      attention on his fingers, a pianist can temporarily paralyze his
      movement.[5]

      When playing the piano, the pianist is using it as an instrument to
produce the desired music. In that case, the consciousness he has of the
keys he touches is instrumental. If the word knowledge is to be employed in
this context, it has to be described as “tacit,” for it is totally
different from the consciousness of object with which the ordinary notion
of knowledge is identified. So much so, as just explained by Polanyi, that
if, in the process of playing, that is, of having tacit knowledge of his
fingers and of the keys they touch, the pianist turns his attention to his
fingers and observes them, thereby turning them into objects of knowledge,
automatically  his activity of playing becomes impaired. Similarly, if,
while reading a text, we shift our attention from the meaning we are
getting through reading,, to the letters that compose the text, of which we
have merely tacit knowledge, in order to know them as such, our reading
becomes impossible. In reading, we have TK or tacit awareness of the
written signs; in turning our attention to the signs, we gain ordinary
knowledge of them. The two forms of consciousness are not only different
from each other but incompatible with each other.

      The awareness each of us has of his own body at the moment of acting,
by contrast to the objective awareness we have of things when we observe
them, and also by contrast to the objective awareness  we have of our own
body when we look at it in the mirror as something external to us, becomes
for Polanyi the model of TK:

      “But our awareness of our body for attending to things outside it
      suggests a wider generalization of the feeling we have of our body.
      Whenever we use certain things for attending from them to other
      things,  in the way in which we always use our own body, these things
      change their appearance. They appear to us now in terms of the
      entities to which we are attending from them, just as we feel our body
      in terms of the things outside to which we are attending from our
      body. In this sense we can say that when we make a thing function as
      the proximal term of tacit knowing, we incorporate it in our body or
      extend our body to include it –so that we come to dwell in it.”[6]

      The generalization of the awareness of our body at the time of action
as the model of the interiorization involved in TK allows Polanyi to
discover the mechanism of agency. He finds it exemplified in a number of
particular cases. In the first place, in the case of the acquired bodily
skills, such as playing the piano or typing, and in the case of those more
complex skills like driving a car. All these skills are fundamentally
similar to the way a blind person ‘drives’ his own body with the  help of a
stick. They are acquired as a result of along practice of interiorization
through which the agent transforms the stick, the typewriter, the piano,
the car into extensions of his own body by using them as he uses his own
body. At the end of that long practice of interiorization the use of those
objects becomes as natural to the agent as the use of his own body in, say,
the action of walking. When a child learns to walk, he is guided by the
sensations he experiences on the plants of his feet every time he makes a
step, but the does not focus his attention on them, he does not observe his
 sensations, they are only “that from which” he makes the subsequent steps.
      The typewriter, the piano, the car are examples of the material
instruments invented by human agents to multiply the efficiency of his
agency and extend its reach in order to satisfy first his elementary needs
and then the desires that emerge as the transformation of the environing
world allows the emergence of such desires. However, in addition to the
material instruments and no less important than them is the invention of
symbolic instruments, among which the most important are spoken and written
language, because they make possible the invention of all other symbolic
instruments. such as mathematical symbols, scientific theories, etc. The
creation of significant symbols makes possible the exchange of concept s
and the conjugated action of a plurality of agents. However, our purpose
here is not to develop a theory of communication but simply to explain the
displacement of the knowing subject of the classical epistemological
tradition by the agent, involved in TK. Let us then return to the examples
of TK furnished by Polanyi.

      After the case of acquired bodily skills as examples of TK, Polanyi
refers us to the use of
maps by an agent who, with their help, attempts to find his way in an
unfamiliar territory. Before analyzing that case, let us remark that the
reading and actual use of maps are far more complex operations than they
seem to be for those who have already mastered them. Actually, a child,
even
a child who has already acquired the skill of reading written language, is
not thereby capable of using a map to find his way in unfamiliar territory,
much less of drawing a map. The reason for this difficulty is that a map is
not a picture of a territory, any more than a name is a picture of the
person named. A map is a symbolic sign that refers to a territory, but not
as a territory to be contemplated but to be walked through. In other words,
not only is a map to be deciphered, as all symbols are, but to be
deciphered in relation to the action of moving through the territory to
which the map refers, in order to reach a particular place within that
territory. In other words, a map is a symbolic instrument to be deciphered
and used by an agent as an instrument to achieve his goa;. That is why, if
somebody claimed to understand a map but proved himself incapable of using
it in practice, he wouldn’t know what he is saying. Indeed, since the map
is an instrument for action, understanding it amounts to being able to
manifest that understanding in one’s action. His mistake would be
comparable to the one Searle makes when he claims     that a person who is
given an order understands it if only he grasps objectively the meaning of
the words, without realizing that an order is to be obeyed, not to be
understood objectively. In short, the genuine understanding of a map is to
be manifested in the conduct that the map is meant to render possible, that
is, in the conduct or reaching the desired place within the territory to
which the map refers, as the genuine understanding of a verbal joke is to
be manifested in the affection of being actually amused by it.

      Polanyi’s view of the reading of maps of the kind we are referring to
goes as follows. The agent who reads the map must “inhabit” the map as he
inhabits his body when he acts to realize a project. In other words, he
must make function the figure on the map not as something to be looked at
and observed in itself, but as “that from which” he must act in order to
arrive at the desired place. In short,  TK is always practical, an
instrument to be used by the agent to achieve an intended goal, not a
representation of object. Inso far, then, as TK is knowledge, it is
practical knowledge, instrumental knowledge, by contrast to the ordinary
notion of knowledge that conceives of as pure vision of object, for that is
what consciousness of object ultimately amounts to.

      A more striking example of a symbolic instrument mentioned by Polanyi
is the case of scientific theories. Used, as we are, to think of knowledge
as a visual representation of object thanks to which the object becomes
known, we will be surprised by the idea that scientific theories function
instrumentally rather than representationally. In other words, we tend to
consider the statement in which, for instance, a physical law is expressed
as a symbolic representation of the intelligible ‘nature’ of the particular
phenomenon to which the law refers. For his part, Polanyi argues that such
an interpretation is wrong. In his view, the statements of scientific laws
are meant to be used by the scientific agent as instruments to guide his
conduct in the solution of concrete problems related to the portion of the
surrounding material world the law refers to, much as a map is meant to
functions as an instrument for the person who uses it to find his way
within the mapped territory.

      In the following text, selected among many others, Polanyi explains
how theories function in the actual practice of science, that is, when the
theoretical instrument is actually used rather than talked about:

      “To rely on a theory for understanding nature is to interiorize it.
      For we are attending from the theory to things seen in its light, and
      we are aware  of the theory, while thus using it, in terms of the
      spectacle that it serves to explain. This is why mathematical theory
      can be learned only by practicing its application: its true knowledge
      lies in our ability to use it.”[7]

      This text, quoted  in isolation from its context, may be too dense
and too cryptic to make us understand how, in Polanyi’s view, scientific
theories function. What is clear is that Polanyi’s view contradicts the
prevailing conception of science as a an exact, or at least an increasingly
approximative, representational  view of reality. Thomas Kuhn, who on this
fundamental point  repeatedly indicates his agreement with Polanyi,
expressed it forcefully:

      “A scientific theory is usually felt to be better than its
      predecessors not only in the sense that it is a better instrument for
      discovering and solving  puzzles but also because it is somehow a
      better representation of what nature is really like. One often hears
      that successive theories grow ever closer to, or approximate more and
      more closely to, the truth. Apparently generalizations like that refer
      not to the puzzle solutions and concrete predictions derived from a
      theory but rather to its ontology, to the match, that is, between the
      entities with which the theory populates nature and what is ‘really
      there.’ Perhaps there is some other way of salvaging the notion of
      ‘truth’ for application to whole theories, but this one will not
      do.... the notion of a match between the ontology of a theory and its
      ‘real’ counter part in nature now seems to me illusive in principle”
      [8]

      What Kuhn is pointing out here is that the statements in which
scientific theories are expressed are not meant to function as
representations of reality but as symbolic instruments to solve  problems
that the surrounding world raises. In this regard, though, scientific
statements constitute no exception. Indeed, if Polanyi is right, no
statement –whether scientific or not– is  meant to function as a
representation of its referent; rather, all statement are meant to function
as  instruments for action, not as representations.

Searle and Polanyi on language and knowledge

       At the end of chapter one I expressed my dissatisfaction with
Searle’s philosophy of language. Let us now contrast it with  with
Polanyi’s own, to find out both how they differ and whether the latter’s
views on language throw any light on the points in which Searle’s position
proved to be unsatisfactory.

THE PRIMARY AND ESSENTIAL FUNCTION OF LANGUAGE IS COGNITIVE, FOR IT IS TO
REVEAL WHAT IS THE CASE BY ENUNCIATING IN WORDS WHAT IS THE CASE.
      Of course, it may be argued that at no point of my summary of his
views did Polanyi claim to possess, much less to be presenting, a
philosophy of language. That is quite correct. However, as we shall now
see, his work on tacit knowledge implies an alternative to Searle’s views
on the function of language. This may sound surprising, given that tacit
knowledge was introduced by Polanyi precisely by contrast to the situation
in which what is the case is explicitly expressed linguistically. Indeed,
concerning the experiment with the shock-producing syllables, Polanyi
underscores that the subject of the experiment cannot say which syllables
produce shocks , yet he knows them, since his conduct manifests that he
braces himself for a shock when one of the shock-producing syllables
appears on the screen.  in a wordless but behavioral way As we shall see,
however, this amounts to a rejection of the very philosophy of language
defended by Searle, whose central points are: (1) the essential function of
language concerning knowledge, to wit: that knowledge is necessarily
linguistic, in that knowledge is identical with the enunciative expression
of what is known. (2) that knowledge is different from, and independent of
action. (3) that knowledge is  is a necessary condition for (appropriate)
action, so that action is dependent on and subordinate to knowledge. For
his part, to the above conception of language opposes his own alternative
conception that dislodges language –or should I say, enunciative language–
from its essential dominant function. Indeed, it follows from Polanyi’s
position: (1) that knowledge does non consist in the enunciative expression
of what is known, but in appropriate action constituted by what Polanyi
calls “Tacit Knowledge,” which is not linguistic but behavioral. (2) That
the appropriate action in which TK consists, far from depending on
enunciative language, gives origin to enunciative language. (3) That
enunciative language, however,  is not representational of what is the
case, as assumed by the traditional conception of knowledge and of
language, of which Searle’s philosophy of language is a particular version.
Rather, the enunciation of what is the case,  conjoined to the desire of a
certain goal by an agent, functions as an instrument for appropriate action
under the appropriate conditions of application of the linguistic
instrument.

      Hence Polanyi’s genral epistemic thesis that constitutes his
alternative to Searle’s linguistic
can be formulatedd as follows: thtat tacit tacit knowleddge, which is
behavioral and instrumental rather tham representational, actually performs
the function traditionally attributed to the explicit awareness of object
expressed linguistically in representational enunciative language.


      In other words, what the hearer gains by understanding what the
speaker says constitutes, in the case of successful communication, a
faithful representation of what the speaker means to communicate to him. If
this effect, which Searle calls “illocutionary,” obtains, the speech act is
successful, for in every case of communication the speaker intends to
inform the hearer of what he means to communicate. However, there are cases
in which, apart from the knowledge of what he means to communicate, the
speaker also intends to produce in the hearer other effects, such as being
obeyed when he gives an order, amusing the hearer when he tells him a joke,
getting the hearer’s money when he begs for it, etc.  by returning to
Polanyi’s and Searle’s respective conceptions of knowledge and language
with which we have dealt. These effects other than knowledge Polanyi calls
“perlocutionary.” Notice, however, that, according to Searle, these effects
are accidental, in the sense that they may or may not be intended in
communicative acts, whereas the illocutionary effect is essential to
communication, for the speaker cannot possibly intend to communicate
without intending the hearer to know what he means. Notice, moreover that
the actual realization of the illocutionary effect is essential to the
success of the communicative act, whereas the achievement of the
perlocutionary effects, when intended, is not. Indeed, if the knowledge of
what the speaker means to communicate is not obtained by the hearer, no
other perlocutionary effect can obtain. For instance, if I do not know what
you command me, I cannot obey your order. By contrast, I can know your
order, that is, you may obtain the illocutionary effect you meant to
produce, without myself obeying your order.

      Before coming to details, it might be helpful to explore the radical
difference between Searle and Polanyi concerning what Searle call the
illocutionary force of speech acts. All speech acts, Searle claims, possess
illocutionary force, whereas only some of them possess perlocutionary
force. Why? Because in all saying, by the very nature of saying or
speaking, the speaker necessarily  intends to communicate the knowledge of
what he says, that is, of what he intends to communicate, and such
knowledge constitutes the illocutionary effect. In other words, saying or
speaking, amounts to the speaker’s expressing in words the knowledge of
what he intends to communicate to his interlocutor, so that he cannot say
or speak without charging what he says with illocutionary force, for that
precisely is what saying amounts to. By contrast, not all saying involves
the speaker’s intention of obtaining other effects than the knowledge of
what he says, in addition to such knowledge. Hence, not all saying is
necessarily charged with perlocutionary force. So much for the speaker’s
linguistic action.

      What now about the hearer’s linguistic action? Since what is said is
meant to convey the speaker’s communicative intention, to wit: the
knowledge of what he intends to communicate,  such communication obtains
through the hearer’s gaining such knowledge thanks to his understanding
what the speaker says. So, on the hearer’s part, the linguistic action
consists in understanding what the speaker means by saying what he says.
The speaker says something to be understood, and the hearer fulfills the
speaker’s communicative intention by gaining, through understanding what is
said, the knowledge of what the speaker means.

      The above indicates Searle’s conception of knowledge. By replicating
in his own mind, thanks to his understanding of what the speaker says, what
the speaker intends to communicate, the hearer gains the knowledge of what
the speaker means or wants to communicate. Thus, according to Searle,  at
least in the case of linguistic communication, knowledge consists in the
correspondence between the object to be known and what the knower comes to
have in mind thanks to its knowing activity. Such, needless to say, is the
ordinary notion  of knowledge, which the Scholastics, following Aristotle,
defined as adaequatio mentis ad rem: the conformation of the knower’s mind
to reality. What Searle does is to extend to the field of language the
ordinary  notion of knowledge, that refers primarily to the knowing
subject’s surrounding world, that is, to “reality.” Now it is precisely
regarding  this conception of knowledge that Polanyi parts ways with
Searle. For Polanyi knowing is not a matter of becoming aware of the object
to be known by acquiring a sort of reproduction of it that corresponds to
the object and that is true in the measure in which it corresponds to the
object. Rather, knowledge is tacit because it is essentially practical, not
theoretical. It does not consist in a reproduction of the object in the
mind, in which case the knowledge of the object would be explicit rather
than tacit. Knowledge consists, rather,  in a practical  disposition to
respond to the encountered object in the way appropriate to achieve the
knower’s purpose. Thus, in the case of the shock-producing syllables, the
subject has no awareness of the individual characteristics of the
syllables; for that very reason the subject cannot say or express in words
which are those characteristics of which he has no visual awareness. What
the subject acquires thanks to his past painful experience is not an image
of the syllables, but the practical disposition to brace himself  for the
shock when and if the shock-producing syllables appear again on the screen,
in order to protect himself as much as possible from the shock.

         If, on this basis, we analyze in detail the different ways in
which Searle and Polanyi construe the relationship between knowledge and
action, we shall be in a position to appreciate the very significant
differences in their respective notions of knowledge. To begin with Searle,
he sees knowledge as a necessary condition for action, but he deems
knowledge  independent from  action. Thus, the perlocutionary effects in a
communicative exchange, which consist in actions, cannot obtain without the
hearer’s knowledge of that the speaker means to communicate, in which
knowledge the illocutionary effect consists; however,  the knowledge in
which the illocutionary effect consists can obtain without any
perlocutionary effect, which consists in an action, obtaining. More
concretely, if I do not know what you mean when you ask me to lend you
pen, I cannot lend you my pen, but I can know that you ask me for my pen,
and not lend it to you. Moreover, you can produce in me an illocutionary
effect without intending any perlocutionary effect. Thus, when you salute
me, all you intend is to produce in me the knowledge that you mean to
salute me, and the communicative exchange succeeds if only I gain that
knowledge. So knowledge is independent from action, but action is dependent
on knowledge.

      Polanyi, for his part, construes the relationship between knowledge
and action in a totally different way. He does not ignore that knowledge is
related to action, for he identifies knowledge with disposition to act and
with the actual appropriate action at the right time; by the same token, he
denies that knowledge can be independent from action, as Searle claims.
Indeed,  as explained above, Polanyi cannot conceive of the illocutionary
effect as separated from action, as Searle does. For Polanyi, the knowledge
involved in the communicative exchange is practical, not a theoretical
awareness of a fact, as it is for Searle. For example, knowing an order
received does not consist, according to Polanyi, in becoming aware of that
fact, but in the practical disposition to obey the order received, and in
actually obeying it at the appropriate time. Similarly, according to
Polanyi, knowing a promise received does not consist in becoming
objectively aware of that fact, as one becomes aware of a color when one
sees it, but in actually bracing oneself for the fulfillment of the
received promise at the appropriate time. If you promise to repay me the
$100.00 you owe me when I come to pick them up at your office at 3.00 P.M.,
the appropriate understanding of your promise does not consist in
registering that fact and then doing nothing, as Searle contends,  but in
expecting the fulfillment of your promise and in the action of actually
coming to your office at 3.00 P.M. to collect my money.

      Whose conception of the relationship of knowledge to action is right:
Searle’s or Polanayi’s?

      Concerning those acts in which a perlocutionary effect is intended by
the speaker, I have already shown how unpersuasive I find Searle’s view
that in every act of communication the speaker intends to produce the
knowledge of his communicative intentions –that is, the illocutionary
effect–  and that this knowledge suffices to insure the success of the
communicative act. For this means, concretely, that when I give you an
order, what I intend primarily is that you understand and thereby be
informed or acquire factual knowledge that I give you an order, not that
you obey my order. Searle is so convinced that in the case of an order what
the speaker intends is that his order be understood rather than obeyed that
he considers the communicative exchange successful when the order is not
obeyed as long as the order is understood by the hearer.

      Why do I think that Searle is wrong on this point? Because this
aspect of Searle’s characterization of linguistic exchanges is  at odds
with the practice of all competent linguistic agents. It is true that I
cannot disobey an order without understanding it and thereby knowing it.
But what is in contention is what constitutes the appropriate understanding
of an order and, in general, of all those linguistic acts that possess
perlocutionary force, such as threats, warnings, promises, demands, etc.
According to Searle, the primary intention of the speaker in all of the
them is to inform the hearer of what he means, and only secondarily and
accidentally to achieve the perlocutionary effect typical of the particular
perlocutionary act, such as being obeyed in the case of an order, expecting
the fulfillment of the promise in the case of promising, etc. But the
linguistic facts contradict Searle’s opinion. Indeed, Searle’s opinion does
not do justice to the practice of linguistic agents conerning the use of
the indicative mood, on the one hand, and of the subjunctive and of the
imperative moods, on the other. If  Searle were right, there would be no
difference between the grammatical imperative, used in the case of orders,
and the grammatical indicative, used when one wants to inform of a fact.
Furthermore, the imperative mood  would be totally superfluous,  for
imperatives should be understood as  indicatives. Indeed,  if Searle were
right, giving an order (in the imperative) would be exactly equivalent to
saying or enunciating (in the indicative) that one gives an order. But such
is not the case. When I give an order, I do not say – in the sense of
enunciating– something to be merely understood, to wit: that I give an
order; rather, I say the order I give in the sense of giving an order to be
obeyed. When I say something in the sense of enunciating, I refer my hearer
to what I enunciate as something to be understood, that is, I intend to
produce in my interlocutor the knowledge in which, according to Searle, the
illocutionary effect consists. Therefore, if in giving an order I were
enunciating that I give an order and the order I give, I should be
satisfied if the addressee responded  “I understand your order.” But that
is not the case. When I give an order –and this is why I use the
imperative, not the indicative, I expect and intend my order to be obeyed,
which is the appropriate ‘understanding’ of an order by a competent
linguistic agent.

      Why does Searle identify saying with enunciating?  Because his
linguistic characterizations and, hence, his entire conception of language,
 is based not on his own experience as a linguistic agent who actually
participates in communicative interactions, but as a curious philosopher
who overhears the words said by those who participate in communicative
interactions, or who reflects after the fact on the words said by himself
and others when he participated in communicative interactions. I underline
“the words said,” to indicate that Searle’s characterization of language
focuses on the words said, independently of the conduct and the intentions
of those who say or hear them as actual participants in linguistic
exchanges. This amounts to identifying linguistic meaning with saying in
the sense of enunciating something, and, consequently, it amounts to
considering the conduct of the linguistic agents as accidental to
linguistic meaning, rather than integral to, or constitutive of, linguistic
meaning.

      Thus Searle excludes from linguistic meaning anything that in a
linguistic exchange is not  words in which something is said or enunciated
to be known, and such is the case with  the actions and affections intended
by the speaker in the communicative exchange. which are therefore excluded
by Searle from being as constitutive elements of linguistic meaning. Such,
indeed, was the reason why, in his example of the American soldier captured
by the Italians, Searle explicitly denied that the action by which the
American tricked the Italians into believing that he was a German soldier
by uttering the sounds “Ich komme von dem Land wosa di Zitronen bluhen” was
a linguistic acction, since it was not expressec in words that said or
enunciated the action intended.
      The exclusion from linguistic meaning of whatever is not explicitly
said in words is reflected in the separation Searle postulates between the
illocutionary and the perlocutionary effects, as well as in the secondary
and merely accidental role he attributes to the latter. Searle himself , in
explaining his principle of expressibility that  “whatever can be meant can
be said,” implicitly acknowledges that whatever is not said in words cannot
be meant, for he excludes from meaning the perlocutionary effects:

      “The principle of expressibility does not imply that it is always
      possible to find or invent a form of expression that will produce all
      the effects in a hearer that one means to produce; for example,
      literary or poetic effects, emotions, beliefs, and so on. This topic
      will be expanded in chapter 2."[9]

      Now his chapter 2 deals precisely with the perlocutionary effects,
which, according to his principle of  expressibility, cannot be meant
linguistically because they do not consist in words but in actions or in
affections of the hearer intended by the speaker.

      In short, because he identifies linguistic meaning with what is
expressed in words that say something to be understood, Searle
automatically excludes from linguistic meaning those no less linguistic
effects that do not consist in words to be understood, namely, the
perlocutionary effects. No wonder, then, that  the way in which language
functions in the artificial context of a course of a language for
foreigners, in which only understanding what is said counts as linguistic,
to the systematic exclusion of any of the affections or actions intended by
the speaker to be produced on the hearer’s part, ends up representing the
linguistic situation that corresponds to Searle’s defective philosophy of
language.


      Now, to understand the differences between them, we have to begin by
realizing that Searle’s linguistic philosophy amounts to an application to
the field of language of the Platonic-Aristotelian epistemology inherited
by the Western philosophical tradition. In that tradition,  knowledge
consists, on the subject’s part,  in the representational conformity of his
mind to reality, and, on the object’s part, in the ontological
correspondence or conformity to what is in the subject’s mind. By contrast,
 Polanyi’s conception of tacit knowledge constitutes an alternative to that
tradition, for tacit knowledge is instrumental knowledge, not
representational knowledge.

      The radical difference between the two conceptions can  best be
appreciated by attending to the different relationship in which knowledge
stands to action in each of them. In Searle’s view, who follows the
Platonico-Aristotelian tradition, knowledge is a sort visual awareness of
its object,  independent from action, whereas action is dependent on
knowledge. In Polanyi’s  view, by contrast, far from being independent from
action, knowledge results from action and is meant to terminate in action.

perlocutionary effects as Searle conceives them, that is, as separated from
the illocutionary effect, are a constitutive element of linguistic meaning,
for the Serlean conception of the illocutionary element from the
perlocutionary presupposes the legitimacy of Searle’s conceptual
distinction between them. Polanyi contends, rather, that this Serlean
distinction reflects the separation between the knowing subject and the
agent that is inherent to the epistemology inherited from Aristotle and
Plato that begins by reflecting on knowledge as different from and
separable form action. The Searlean distinction between illocutionary and
perlocutionary effects is possible only on the wrong assumption , common to
Aristotle and Plato, that knowledge and action stand to each other in the
relationship presupposed in the epistemology inherited from Plato and
Aristotle by the Western philosophical tradition.

sans rapport intrinseque avec l’action, du marteau dans la salle a manger,
ainsi que Searle le pense ou devrait le penser s’il croit vraiement a sa
theorie linguistique. De meme, pour terminer, dans le cas de n’importe
quelle assertion linguistique d’un etat concret particulier du monde
environnant.

      En fait, j’avais deja plus haut fait la critique de la position de
Searle au sujet de la connaissance objective dans la communication
linguistique, en disant que son modele du fonctionnement du langage est la
maniere bâtarde dont le langage fonctionne dans le cadre artificel d’un
cours de langue pour des etrangers. La seule difference est que dans le
chapitre precedent je me referait au chapitre de son essaai où Searle parle
de tous les actes linguistiques sous la rubrique de la communication, et
ici  je me refere au chapitre oul il etudie lecas particulier des
assertions dont le referent est ce qui est le cas en realite. En tout cas,
pour mon propos, cette difference n’est pas significative, puisque Searle
concoit les deux cas comme examples de connaissance objective ou
connaissance d’objet, tandis que je soutiens avec Polanyi que ce que nous
appelons ordinairement “connaissance” fonctionne comme instrument pour
l’agent, non comme representation d’objet pour le sujet connaissant de la
tradition epistemologique classique.

      Quant a la fertilite de la substitution du sujet connaissant par
l’agent dans la conception polanyienne de la CT, nous aurons l’occasion de
l’apprecier au prochain chapitre dans son application dans le terrain de la
science. Effectivement, ainsi que la conception polanyienne fournit une
alternative a la facon ordinaire de comprendre le fonctionnement du
langage, representée par la theorie de Searle, ainsi elle fournit egalement
une alternative a la facon ordinaire de concevoir le fonctionnement de la
science, representée par la conception classique de la science comme vision
correcte ou du moins de plus en plus approximative de la nature de la
realité.
