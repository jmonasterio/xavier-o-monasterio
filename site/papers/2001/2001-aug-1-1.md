2001 -aug-1                             August 13, 2001

TILLEY: INVENTING CATHOLIC TRADITION

“That there are, at least to my knowledge, no Catholic theologians or philosophers firmly committed to an anti-realist position is, in my judgment, very significant” (Tilley. Inventing Catholic Tradition , Orbis Books, New York 2000, p. 159, note 12). ^(F1)

“The conceptual difficulties involved in traditional propositional notions of authoritative teaching have contributed to discrediting the whole doctrinal enterprise. They have helped legitimate unnecessary and counterproductive rigidities in practice because, first, propositionalism makes it difficult to understand how new doctrines can develop in the course of time, and how old ones can be forgotten or become peripheral. Second, propositionalist accounts of how old doctrines can be reinterpreted to fit new circumstances are unconvincing: they have difficulty in distinguishing between what changes and what remains the same. Third, they do not deal adequately with the specifically ecumenical problematic: how is it possible for doctrines that once contradicted each other to be reconciled and yet retain their identity. Experiential-expresssivisms that reduce doctrines to non-discursive symbolisms have an exactly opposite set of difficulties... How can a religion claim to preserve ‘the faith which was once for all delivered to the saints’ (Jude 3), as all religions in some sense do, when it takes so many forms in both the past and the present? - One reaction to these considerations is relativistic. There is no faith once for all delivered to the saints. No self-identical core persists down through the centuries and subsists within the different and usually competing traditions that inevitably develop. Everything is in flux... - The contrary extreme is, on the Protestant side, biblicistic, and on the Catholic, traditionialist. The attempt  in both cases is to preserve identity by reproducing as literalistically as possible the words and actions of the past. The defect of this tactic is that it confuses the letter and the spirit... The meaning of rites and utterances depends on contexts. To replicate the old forms in new situations frequently betrays the original meaning, the original spirit. Just as the only way to love parents, spouses, children, and neighbors is to behave toward each in a distinctive fashion, so often the only way to convey the same message is to proclaim it differently.... - Doctrine and the theory of doctrine can make only a small contribution to the task of ascertaining which of the changing forms is faithful to the putatively abiding substance. Those who are best able to judge in these matters... are those who have effectively interiorized a religion. They know by connaturality, as Aquinas might say, whether specific usages are in conformity to the spirit, the interior rule of faith, and do not need the clumsy directives of official dogma. Yet there is also a place for the clumsy directives. Saints and prophets are rare, and doctrinal decisions and reflection on those decisions, while poor substitutes for inspiration, are preferable to uninspired and unreflective prejudice. Thus theoreticians may be of some modest help to religious communities if they can show how doctrines can be both firm and flexible, both abiding and adaptable. To the extent that they are unable to do so, their theories are theologically and pastorally unfruitful” (George A. Lindbeck, The Nature of Doctrine: Religion and Theology in a Postliberal Age , The Westminster Press, Philadelphia 1984, pp. 78-79).

I found a number of good points in Tilley’s understanding of tradition: (1) It cannot be identified with tradita, that is, with a particular theoretical content, which would reduce tradition to a doctrine or doctrines merely to be assented to as referring to objective facts independent from the knower’s attitudes or dispositions to respond. (2) Rather, it is constituted  by learned and received communal practices in which the tradition itself finds its realization and its expression and through which it is transmitted. A practice is a purposive conduct, a “human act,” which involves not only action but also attitudes, that is, affective-‘intellectual’ dispositions to act.

Insofar as Tilley’s view de-interiorizes and de-intellectualizes tradition, I agree. Insofar, however, as Tilley clings to the notion of “realistic’ truth in traditions, I think that he is inconsistent with his own view and remains incoherently subservient to the  Greek idea of a traditum that corresponds to reality.

Tilley’s assessment notion of truth implies the recognition that practices are assessed by its expert practitioners in terms of their satisfactory appropriateness, not of their correspondence to reality. Like Kant, Tilley grants that whether such correspondence obtains cannot be known, but he assumes that it either obtains or doesn’t. Furthermore, Tilley maintains that in the case of the faith-practices correspondence obtains. Now, Kant had no alternative to the Greek notion of knowledge as correspondence, so his position is consistent; he had to assume that in any given case correspondence either obtains or doesn’t. But Tilley’s notion of tradition as a practice or system of practices is precisely the alternative to the view of tradition as a true traditum corresponding to reality .

Aristotle and Aquinas (cf. Peri Hermeneias ) were right that only judgments (as they understood that term) could conceivably be true or false, that is, correspond to what is the case. If so, it doesn’t make sense to say of a practice that it is true or false, for a practice neither affirms nor denies anything and, therefore. can neither correspond nor fail to correspond to what is the case. That could be correct only if a tradition consisted in a traditum, but Tilley rightly denies this. Yet by declaring realism compatible with his notion of tradition Tilley incoherently regresses to the identification of tradition as a traditum!

Why does he do this? Because he still thinks that the convictions involved in a practice are judgment-like, as if going to communion involved intellectual assent to the doctrine of the real presence in the way one assents to a theorem in geometry. This reminds me of Plantinga’s view of belief-in as consisting in belief-that plus attitudes. ^(F2)  Certainly, as someone who receives communion, if asked whether I believe in the real presence of Christ I answer affirmatively. But my belief is no intellectual assent prompted by trust in God who knows and says that Christ is present; rather it is conviction-disposition to respond to Christ present as present. ^(F3)

In short, Tilley’s affirmation of the compatibility of his views on tradition with realism obscures his whole enterprise. What makes necessary the shift to Tilley’s identification of tradition with a practice rather than with a doctrine or traditum is precisely the insufficiency of the old paradigm, one of whose central pieces is the Greek view of knowledge as contemplative correspondence. Thus to regress to the old paradigm as if it were functional is grossly incoherent.

^(F1) I, for one, am a Catholic not committed to a realist position, for I don’t think that the Greek notion of knowledge as correspondence makes sense. Yet I am convinced that the practice of faith in the Catholic Church is the appropriate one. This is the Augustinian sense of “Extra Eccclesiam nulla salus”

^(F2) See my “Plantinga and the Two Problems of Evil.”

^(F3) I say “conviction-disposition,” not merely “disposition,” in order to indicate that it is not a blind reflex but a human act that involves a practical grasp of that to which the linguistic agent responds. Searle may be wrong in his understanding of the meaning of language as propositional rather than practical, but he is right in saying that language is unlike chess in that it means.
