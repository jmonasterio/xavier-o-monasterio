                 LONERGAN: ASSENTING AND CONSENTING

                                    "For   Augustine    our    hearts    are
                 restless until they rest in God;
                                   for  Aquinas,  not   our   hearts,    but
                           first     and     most     our     minds      are
            restless       until       they       rest       in       seeing
Him" (Lonergan, Verbum, p. 90)
Faith and rationality
      Assent, Lonergan says, is an act of the intellect; consent is  an  act
of the will. One  assents--indeed,  cannot  help  assenting--to  an  evident
judgment. To what does one consent? "The good is in things, but the true  is
in the mind; consent is a motion of the will with respect to  a  thing,  but
assent is a motion of the intellect with respect to a conception  (De  Ver.,
q.  10,  a.  1  c)...  We  assent  to  first  principles,  to   demonstrable
conclusions, to the affirmations of reliable authority. Assent  occurs  when
we judge a conception of the thing to be true" (Lonergan, Verbum, p. 61).
      All the above are statements. All  one  can  do  about  statements  is
assent or  not  assent.  Hence  statements  express  conceptions.  What  are
conceptions?  Understandings  of  something,   definitions   of   something.
Contrary, however, to the common opinion, according to  which  understanding
is passive to the thing understood, understanding is creative, inventive  of
the thing understood within the parameters of the  thing  perceived.  It  is
not  the  thing  evident  that  produces  the  understanding,  it   is   the
understanding the makes evident  the  thing  which  was  not  evident.  Only
later, in attempting to persuade  others,  that  is,  of  making  the  thing
evident to others, does one use evidence as an argument.
       In  fact,  even  before  understanding  there  is  what  Aris   calls
"perception," which in animals is  non-linguistic  but  determined  by  what
Marx calls "the standards of the species"  and  is  purely  active  or  non-
contemplative, for it is of object for response. And  perception  itself  is
evident if it occurs. This is to say that perception neither  needs  to  nor
can be proved; "all perceiving is perceiving  as"  and  can  be  substituted
only by another perceiving as. As long as there is no reason for doubting  a
perceived is evident, which does not  mean  that  perception  is  infallible
anymore than understanding is.
      Distinguish immediate from discursive understanding. That A=C  because
A=B  and B=C is discursive. That A is A is immediate, at least on the  level
of perception, though it might be discursive on  the  level  of  explanatory
understanding which is by reference to the appropriate "phantasms."  To  use
Aris's example, what an eclipse is would be evident to someone  standing  on
the moon, but to us it becomes evident only through  the  best  explanation,
that is, an explanation that explains all cases  of  eclipse  and  which  we
have no positive reason to doubt. As Lonergan remarks,  though,  by  knowing
the universal the scientist's knowledge "will not give him knowledge of  any
particular eclispse as particular 'sicut rusticus cognoscit'; for  inso  far
as the astronomer knows  future  eclipses  as  particular,  it  is  only  by
relating his calculations to a sensibly given  here  and  now"  (Verbum,  p.
40). (The phantasm  is  a  sensible,  perceived  or  imagined,  but  already
abstracted from its irrelevant particularities, a  sort  of  middle  between
the universal concept and the concrete particular).
      Now, Lonergan may be right about the reflective  aspect  of  judgment.
But he seems to consider it as a grasp of a positive reason  for  affirming,
whereas I think that it is, at best, a grasp of the absence  of  a  positive
reason for doubting. This is why, as Lonergan  himself  suggests  somewhere,
the rash are stupid in assenting-affirming too quickly, but  the  timid  are
stupid in not affirming when the intelligent do. This is why, too, there  is
no regress and infinitum in knowledge, because  positive  proof  is  neither
possible nor needed. And this applies as well  to  "the  first  principles,"
which are not positively  evident  in  a  more  special  way  than  what  is
perceived but in which any reason  to  doubt  is  absent.  Thus  Nussman  is
right: Aris's refutations of scepticism concerning the first principles  are
not proofs of the first principles. And she is right  about  Aris  beginning
with "what we say," with the best  available  perceptions.  In  this  sense,
Plato is a foundationalist who wants to go beyond opinions; Aris is not.  He
just discards the opinions for which he has positive doubts, confident  that
his perceiving-as, which he  cannot  presently  doubt,  will  be  shared  by
others and taken as their basis,  much  as  we  all  start  with  linguistic
perceiveds. (In fact, however, I think that  Aristotle  was  of  two  minds,
that he did not quite get  rid  of  evidentialism  (positive  evidence)  and
foundationalism, and that the medievals--or at least the late  scholastics--
were not totally wrong in reading him as an evidentialist foundationalist.
      But was Aquinas himself an evidentialist foundationalist?
His case is very complicated. It can best  be  approached  through  what  he
thought of philosophy in relation to faith. He certainly did not think  that
faith needed to be grounded by reason and  on  reason.  It  is  grounded  on
God's veracity. If it is not grounded on God's veracity, it  is  not  faith,
it is mere  knowledge--something  totally  different  and  without  "merit."
Furthermore, the most important truths of faith are inaccessible  to  reason
left to its own forces and, in this life  at  least,  even  inaccessible  to
faith in their evidence. Yet, Aquinas clearly  assumed  the  continuity  and
complementarity between reason and faith as far as their  respective  truths
go.  The  continuity,  because  he  accepted  the  possibility  of  apparent
contradictions between the truths of faith and  the  truths  of  reason.  He
positively  rejected  the  Averroistic   notion   of   "two   truths."   The
complementarity,  because  he  establishes  a  parallelism   between   God's
knowledge shared  with  us  through  revelation  and  human  knowledge,  and
between the revealed and the  known.  Furthermore,  closely  connected  with
this, there is Aquinas's doctrine of the praeambula fidei, so  equivocal  as
to the relationship between reason and faith. In fact, the very  project  of
a scientific theology would have been inconceivable without a certain  sense
of the rationality of faith in the Aristotelian sense.
      The notion of the praeambula fidei involves at  least  the  following:
(1) Not all the truths of faith are inaccessible to reason left to  its  own
strength and abilities. (2) Faith and reason have  a  partly  common  domain
concerning truths directly or indirectly related to God and religion.  (That
this creates a problem for him, since  what  is  known  cannot  be  accepted
through faith, is evident from his  discussion  of  the  loss  of  merit  in
faith, etc.). (3) Though faith  is not a product of reason,  it  is  not  a-
rational, much less irrational. The praeambula fidei are, in a certain  way,
rational grounds for faith, at least as conditions of possibility. If  faith
were a-rational, there could  be  no  even  apparent  contradiction  between
reason and faith. Moreover, if God's existence could not be  proven,  or  if
it could be proven that God does not care about us or wants  us  ill,  faith
would be impossible, given  the  identity  of  the  subject  who  knows  and
believes. This is a powerful indicator that certain  truths  of  reason  are
foundational for faith, in Aquinas's view, who  maintains  that  God  cannot
contradict Himself in demanding faith from a creature He  created  incapable
of refusing assent to evidence.
       What,  then,  is  the  conclusion?  Was  Aquinas   an   evidentialist
foundationalist? It seems to me that he separated and,  at  the  same  time,
mixed illegitimately the product of thinking and the  process  of  thinking.
The notion of truth (the Aristotelian  one)  is  not  independent  from  the
process through which it is reached, which involves at  least  understanding
and negative evidence and which results in  "knowledge."   In  other  words,
the conformity between intellectus and res is not  mere  conformity  between
the affirmed and the res--for in that case  there  would  be  truth  in  the
utterance of a parrot conditioned  to  say  something  true--but  conformity
between the affirmed with assent on the basis of grasped  sufficient  reason
and the res affirmed. I  understand  the  said  because  I  understand  with
assent why (I say) it is so. And if I do not understand with assent  why  it
is so, I do not understand the said, I "affirm" nothing. As far as  Aris  is
concerned, from his scientific point of view, I am speaking like  a  parrot,
or at least I have a mere opinion. In other words, for Aris, the  assent  of
faith is not assent, not a committed judgment. God  who  reveals  knows  and
his statement is true in the relevant sense. I who believe do not  know  and
my statement is not true because it is not known  as  true.  When  I  affirm
God's existence on the basis of one of the five  ways  (not  because  Thomas
affirmed it on the basis of his  understanding,  but  because  I  understand
it), then and only then do I know God's existence, then and only then  do  I
judge and, since only judgments are true or false and this is  a  scientific
judgment, then and only then is my judgment  true.  Aris,  therefore,  would
deny the complementarity and continuity between faith and reason.
      This is why I think that Aquinas separates illegitimately the  process
of knowing from the result. In  the  case  of  faith  he  takes  the  result
separated from the process. And he mixes it illegitimately with  results  of
the process of reasoning to build out of the two a picture of the world.  No
wonder that all scientifically-minded people like Aris  reject  whatever  in
the picture is not reached through the rational process, for  by  definition
it cannot register as part of the picture.
      The obvious objection to this is that most of our knowledge  is  based
on  authority,  even  within  a  science.  Hence  the  subordination   among
sciences. The answer, however, is not difficult. It is  known  and  knowable
because it is known by somebody like us who starts with sensible  data,  and
because if I replicated the rational process I would reach the same  result,
much as scientists rely on the possible replication of the  experiment.  But
if I replicated the process  or  the  experiment,  which  are  in  principle
replicable, and reached different  results,  I  could  not  possibly  go  on
relying on the unconfirmed result. However,  by  definition,  God's  results
cannot be reached through a rational process since God does not  start  with
the sensible.
      But what if Aquinas, on the basis of his five ways, tells me that  God
exists? Do I know if I affirm that? Can I use it as part of the  picture  of
the universe? Yes, but only because and  only  insofar  as  the  process  is
replicable and when replicated yields the same results  uniformly.  This  is
the subalternatio scientiarum Aris has in mind. But  there  is  no  possible
subalternatio of knowledge to faith, of philosophy to theology.
      Still a possible  rejoinder  is  that  I  trust  God  because  God  is
infinitely veracious and knowledgeable. The question is:  do  I  know  this,
that is, is this knowledge the result of the rational process?  It  is  here
that the praeambula fidei enter. I can know that this  infinitely  veracious
and knowledgeable One exists. In that case, we have  rationalism:  my  faith
rests on and depends on my knowledge as the reason  for  my  assent  to  the
truths revealed by God. This Aquinas neither can nor wants to  say.  Yet  he
speaks of the rationality of faith, which  implies  this,  and  which  makes
possible his spurious picture composed of results of faith  and  results  of
reason. This is the way  he  was  understood  by  certain  rationalist  late
scholastics, like Báñez, and not without reason, for otherwise  faith  would
not be rational, something to which Aquinas ferociously  clung  to.  And  if
Aquinas denies that faith rests on reason and affirms, rather,  that  reason
rests on faith, something  which  Aquinas  neither  can  nor  wants  to  say
either, for he maintains the autonomy  of  faith  and  reason  within  their
respective domains, then the idea of the  rationality  of  faith  disappears
and we are left with fideism.
The heart's reasons
      However, my main grudge against Aquinas is that he took as  his  model
Aristotelian Scientific reason. Faith may be rational,  at  least  not  less
rational  than  many  other  positions,  but  certainly  cannot   have   the
rationality of Aristotelian epistéme with which Aquinas connected it.  Faith
for Aquinas is a matter of assent, and our ultimate end is  a  rest  of  the
mind; for Augustine and the Fathers, faith is a matter of total  consent  to
God, and our ultimate end is a rest of the heart. The "heart,"  however,  is
not unsighted as Aquinas's will is; therefore, "consent" is not mere  giving
in, it is giving in to something loved.
