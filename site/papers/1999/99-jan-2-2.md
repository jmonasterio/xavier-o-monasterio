The issue

For the nth time I return to the thirteenth century shift from affective Augustinian to scientific theology.  I continue to think that the shift involved what St Bonaventure called a determinatio distrahens , a paradigmatic shift from the sphere of faith, which belongs in the dimension of exposure, to the sphere of specular knowledge, assent and truth (expressed in specularly understood statements by theologians and confused with the ‘truths’ of faith), which belongs in the dimension of contemplation. (Much as Plantinga reduces the pr0oblem of suffering and death to the truth of falsity of specularly understood statements).

I think, however, that now I am better prepared than in the past to deal with the issue, thanks to the realization that the problem is connected with the confusion inherited from Aristotle between speculative and practical knowledge . Not only cannot practical knowledge  be scientific, that is, its correspondence with reality cannot be guaranteed [EXACT CORRESPONDENCE, OR NECESSITY OF CORRESPONDENCE?] as Aristotle came to realize, but it involves no truth-correspondence and, therefore, is not knowledge at all–something of which Aris never became aware. (Indeed, the intellectual virtues, by contrast to the moral ones, involve the grasp of truth, that is, of the appropriateness of the conduct required).

Were Aristotelian practical knowledge the model for scientific theology, that would be bad enough, for without the realization that faith being applicative is not knowledge, scientific theology would still aim at reaching at least imprecise, predicative, spekcular truths, as Aris does in the NE. But the case is even worse, for the model for scientific theology is not practical knowledge but the deductive, strictly demonstrative science of the Analytica Posteriora. Thus the exposed agent’s applicative grasp that terminates in action is reduced to a specular truth and then is treated as if it were essential or at least causal, explanatory, knowledge.  

The strategy

The real question for me now is the strategy to be followed. In “\wp\truth” I tried to begin with Aris as interpreted by Devereux. According to him, Aris progressively came to distinguish

speculative from practical knowledge. If I follow this strategy again, the point will be to show that

(a) Devereux is wrong in saying that Aris came to see that practical and theoretical knowledge are not two species of the same genus [WHAT EVIDENCE IS THERE FOR THIS? AND, IF SO, WHY THEN DID ARIS CALL BOTH SUPPOSEDLY DIFFERENT KINDS BY THE SAME NAME, ‘KNOWLEDGE’? AND WHY DID HE COMPARE THEM TOI EACH OTHER AND FOUND ONE SUPERIOR TO THE OTHER IN PRECISION? ONE DOESN’T COMPARE APPLES AND PEARS].  In addition, the point will be to show that

(b) Devereux is wrong in his at least implicit claim that the way in which  Aris’ distinguishes speculative and practical knowledge is sufficient and correct, for in fact they remain two species of the same genus. In Devereux’s view, according to Aris they came to be distinguished by their respective goals in the first place, and , secondly practical knowledge (unlike speculative knowledge) doesn’t deal with intelligible, explanatory causes (formal or efficient) but with (merely factual) particulars which are unintelligible (surd, unexplainable, without necessity, purely contingent).

Here enters my substantive claim:

- Aris began with Plato’s conception of knowledge, namely, speculative-specular knowledge.

- He progressively realized that practical knowledge differed from speculative knowledge, but he never gave up the Platonic conception of knowledge as correspondence (whether by  intuition or by conformity matters little).  As a consequence, he couldn’t come to realize that practical knowledge involves no correspondence between the intellect and reality.

Devereux places the emphasis on Aris’ recognition that action is about particulars ( which are

for Plato not objects of knowledge, since they are unintelligible (that is, not necesarily so as they are) and therefore unknowable , objects of mere belief or opinion–whereas for Aris they are objects of imprecise knowledge) . What neither Aristotle nor Devereux realized is that  the agent’s deliberative thinking is concerned with particulars but is not about particulars as objects of knowledge-assent, but is in the mode of exposure and meant to bring about a desired state of affairs chosen by the agent under the pragmatic constraints of his or her situation.

What then?

- What is required is the new, non-Platonic beginning that Aris in his dependence from Plato’s wrongheaded beginning never fully envisioned. Yet, there are seeds for this new beginning in the first lectio of the   Metaphysics and in the 20 th lesson of the Analytica Posteriora , in the treatment of prudence or pro-spective, applicative ‘knowledge’ of animals and its similarity with the prudence-decision of people. No specular knowledge here, not even in the case of people, for in their case, too, the grasp is applicative. It is the grasp of a chosen goal to be pursued by a preferential (and therefore goal-pursuing) agent exposed to objects-for-response, an agent who finds himself or herself in an hodological time-space.

- Ultimately, when speculative and practical knowledge are coordinated–but this I will not attempt in this first step–, speculative (that is, specular,  non-applicative) knowledge is to be eliminated , for all ‘knowledge’ is implicitly applicative, practical.

Desirous knowledge

According to Nussbaum, Aris wondered why not all knowledge terminated in action. His conclusion, at least at a certain point in his development,  was that, unlike speculative knowledge, only practical knowledge was informed by desire–that is by interested desire. Now this trait of practical knowledge is not taken into consideration by Devereux, according to whom what came to distinguish practical from speculative knowledge for Aris at his most unPlatonic is that it is pursued for different interests and that it deals with particulars, namely, actions. [IT IS PURSUED FOR DIFFERENT INTERESTS, BUT IN BOTH CASES IT CONSISTS IN CORRESPONDENCE OR ‘IDENTITY. AND IT DEALS WITH PARTICULARS RATHER THAN WITH UNIVERSAL AND NECESSARY OBJECTS, BUT IN BOTH CASES THE OBJECTS ARE OBJECTS OF KNOWLEDGE, THOUGH NOT WITH THE SAME PRECISION]. ’Perhaps desire is included under practical interest, but this is not explicitly said and perhaps not meant or really even taken into account by Aris. In addition, since practical knowledge is still deemed to be knowledge, the realizations that it does not involve truth-correspondence is not reached. Let us then begin again with the Metaphysics.

Aris speaks of animal and human prudence : Ex praeteritorum memoria de futuris providentia, aestimatio de convenientibus prosequendis et fugiendis nocivis. In hominibus quidem est prudentia secundum quod ex ratione deliberant quid eos oporteat agere–recta ratio agibilium. Iudicium autem de rebus agendis non ex rationis deliberatione, sed ex quodam naturae instinctu, prudentia in aliis animalibus dicitur. Unde prudentia in aliis animalibus est naturalis aestimatio de convenientibus prosequendis et fugiendis nocivis, sicut agnus sequitur matrem et fugit lupum.

In both cases there is positive preference for what is ‘conveniens’; and negative preference against what is ‘nocivum.’ Only preferential beings can be motivated to act, either by actively looking for the desired object in the case of locomotive, prudent animals), or by actually being exposed to the desired or naturally harmful object by chance. Animal preference is partly inherited (sicut agnus sequitur matrem et fugit lupum) and partly acquired (as to the means to attain the natural ends). But animals inherit their ends to which all their actions are applicative means, some of which means are learned through experience and some genetically transmitted. As for people, as Maslow suggests, most of their preferences are culturally shaped on the basis of inherited needs, rather than merely inherited. But what is important is that, within limits, they can deliberate because their desires (whether inherited, or culturally shaped, or reflectively invented) are not determinant of conduct. People can choose their ends or goals and consequently their means, which is what they do through deliberative thinking. Furthermore, their means are not passively received but learned through trial and error or through scientific experiment and explanation, and in some ends and means become possible in the  process of transforming the environment to tailor it to their needs..

The point that people ex ratione deliberant quid oporteat eos agere–recta ratio agibilium– might indicate that there is a natural, objective order of ends or goals to be discovered rationally and, consequently, pursued as goals by all people. Aris’s own investigation of what people as people ought to do, the standards by which they ought to regulate  their preferences and their conduct, seems to suggest such a thing. And, I think, the tradition of ‘natural law’ is based on the understanding that there is such a natural, objective order. Now this issue is all-important for my investigation.

I think the just mentioned view is typical of the confusion between speculative and practical knowledge, where ‘human nature’ is misunderstood as paralleling the explanatory and supposedly specular nature of things. However, in the order of exposure–and goals belong in that order, as much as means–a goal is a goal only if and when it is chosen and actually pursued as a goal. To say, for instance, that God is the goal of human life, or the goal of all humans, is, in a certain way, meaningless, a pious desire by someone who believes in God and attempts to subordinate his or her conduct to Him. Indeed a goal that doesn’t actually function as a goal by shaping the conduct of a person in its pursuance is no goal at all. Furthermore, I think that Aristotle himself suggested the contrary when he argued that only the morally well trained would ‘see’ the goals that ought to be pursued.. That is, only those who are practically trained to believe in God practically, or who through grace come to believe in God, can ‘see’ in a practical way that He ought to be the ultimate goal in all their actions.    

Aris also distinguishes between knowledge for its own sake (propter ipsum congnoscere, propter ipsum scire, cuiusmodi sunt scientiae mathematicae [scientiae autem dignitatem ostendit per hoc quod naturaliter desideratur ab omnibus tamquam finis], and knowledge for its usefulness for something else (propter necessitatem actionis ad utilitatem vitae, propter voluptatem, ad introductionem in aliis scientiis sicut scientiae logicales). Symptomatically, Aris considers the first kind of knowledge superior to the second and the  speculative sciences superior to the practical ones: Illi artifices sunt sapientiores quorum scientiae non sunt ad utilitatem inventae, sed propter ipsum scire, cuiusmodi sunt scientiae speculativae [See “\wp\98\apr-2" and “Monas\bible-5"].
