ON THE SPECULATIVE-PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE DISTINCTION

I make judgments-statements such as “The hammer is in the other room,” not out of the blue but only when they are relevant for action : either I or the other addressee need a hammer and is looking for it. The perlocutionary force-effect of the judgment-statement is for the addressee to go to the other room in search of the hammer. Only when there is disagreement or disbelief on the addressee’s part (which of course cannot happen in the case of a judgment, only of a statement to somebody else) can the illocutionary force-effect of language appear as different from the perlocutionary and as a condition for it. Curiously, it is this invented illocutionary force-effect that becomes primary and renders possible and persuasive the representational view of knowledge. In both cases the invention is the deed of a philosopher-contemplator, for the agent is all involved in looking for the hammer, not in studying knowledge or language (Monasterio, Nov.3.99, added paragraph on p. 7).

The invention of the illocutionary

Exactly as speculative knowledge preceded its distinction from practical knowledge and was even given the primacy over the latter and made into a condition for the latter, so the illocutionary force of language was distinguished from its perlocutionary force-effects and even became a condition for the perlocutionary. Both speculative knowledge and illocutionary force-effect are inventions, and both inventions are the deed of a contemplator philosopher reflecting on objectified language, not of an agent-user of language. I want to explore this phenomenon in the field of language, in the hope of reaching a better understanding of the wrongheaded distinction between speculative and practical knowledge and of its disastrous effects.

Polanyi and Sartre

I sense that Polanyi’s “tacit knowledge,” which applies as much to scientific discoveries as to ordinary people’s knowledge and skills, will be of help here.

The representational view of language in which judgments-statements --as the only linguistic pieces susceptible of truth-correspondence-- occupy the central, paradigmatic place for the understanding of language , seems to be a result of assuming that using language or acting linguistically is saying something objective to be understood (and, then, accidentally , to be or not to be acted upon, for not all speech acts produce or intend to produce perlocutionary effects ). Such is Searle’s view in Speech Acts. Actually, however –such  is my opinion, anyway– the appropriate  understanding even of  a statement is perlocutionary, for it is practical understanding that consists in performing the expected-intended conduct in linguistic, free response to the speaker’s intention. In other words, in the actual use of language the addressee doesn’t understand objectively or speculatively what is said (the content), thereby getting merely informed –  as Searle and, in general, the Platonists imagine. Rather, to use Polanyi’s expression, the addressee ‘dwells’ linguistically in the stated by the speaker to produce the expected conduct in response. Thus, pace Searle,  if this linguistic conduct doesn’t obtain, linguistic communication fails, even if the addressee understands what the speaker says,  for what was expected-intended, that is, meant to obtain,  was a conduct-in-response, not a speculative understanding of something said that supposedly reproduces objectively what is the case.

How does the distinction illocutionary-perlocutionary originate? When the communicative act fails in the case of statements because of disagreement or disbelief on the addressee’s part about the referent of the statement, the participants’ attention turns to the content of the statement and objectifies it, instead of dwelling in it practically. [MY NOTE: This is the case with the papers presented at philosophical conventions. They express a thesis: “It is true that...” They do aim at speculative assent, unlike religious sermons or the religious reading of the Bible, which are direct communication, not reflective communication on something already said.]. Thus, when normal, interactive communication is interrupted because of diagreement ordisbelief, the statement’s “content,”which was to be ‘dwelled in’ or ‘understood applicatively’ rather than ‘objectively,’ is detached from the speaker’s practical intention and becomes speculative, something said about the object referred to, something to be considered speculatively and assented to or disagreed with. With this –not in the moment of using language but in the moment of studying it– the representational view of knowledge and the illocutionary notion of language (which lies at the origin of the illocutionary-perlocutionary distinction) are born.

To come now to Polanyi, he speaks of “tacit knowledge,” because the agent knows more than what he can say. For instance, in the case of electric shocks, the person doesn’t know objectively and predicatively which syllables to avoid in order not to get a shock, nor that he is avoiding them for that purpose, yet he avoids them; so, concludes Polanyi, he ‘knows’ them, that is, knows them tacitly or unknowingly ( The Tacit Dimension , pp. 7-9).  [MY NOTE: To translate this into my idiolect, what Polanyi seems to be saying is that the person   knows the syllables practically , since he acts appropriately in response to the syllables to avoid the shock, but doesn’t know them theoretically at all , since he cannot state which syllables are accompanied by shocks. But doesn’t this interpretation amount to the perpetuation of the Aristotelian mistake of calling two different things by the same  name of “knowledge” and assuming that speculative knowledge not only is the princeps analogatum but is a necessary condition for practical knowledge ? That the two things, namely, speculative and practical knowledge, are not the same thing is obvious in this example, since the person has no speculative knowledge and has practical knowledge. Hence they should not be called by the same name: ‘knowledge.’ ]

Like Aristotle, Polanyi insists in using the word knowledge --“tacit knowledge” is his expression-- to name the unconscious or unknowing knowledge of this person (or animal?). But is this not a contradiction in terms? After all, what else is this unconscious or tacit knowledge but a case of knowing unknowingly? And what can possibly be meant by knowing unknowingly other than responding appropriately on the basis of past experience? And is this not what Aristotle should have meant by the practical knowledge he attribute to animals and people, rather than what  he calls practical knowledge in the Metaphysics ?

Sartre ‘platonizes’ in a similar way. Indeed, in the case of the Freudian unconscious “censor” to which he refers, Sartre solves the problem by saying that the person knows very well what is censored, only he knows it non-tethically or non-positionally, that is, without stating it explicitly to himself. [MY NOTE:  Sartre, however, seems to assume that the person voluntarily avoids stating it explicitly, and therefore could state it if he wanted to. Thus, unlike Polanyi who is more ambiguous, Sartre would definitely reduce practical knowledge to theoretical knowledge, or at least make it into a condition for practical knowledge. That is, he would say that if the person states explicitly, which he or she can do since she or he  knows implicitly, then the person will automatically be liberated and will act authentically. Which is like saying that speculative knowledge has the primacy, for it is the necessary and even sufficient condition for acting appropriately].

Polanyi’s analysis, though ultimately Platonic, for it doesn’t explicitly do away with speculative knowledge, is more sophisticated. And I think that what Sartre calls knowing non-positionally (that is, non-verbally, non-judgingly or non-statingly,, non-predicatively) could be profitably translated into what Polanyi calls knowing by ‘indwelling’ or ‘attending from.’. [MY NOTE: And this I call  practical knowledge, but not as a variety of knowledge as representational of what is the case, for I don’t think that representational knowledge exists at all]. So, unlike myself,  both – Sartre certainly and Polanyi probably-- think with Aristotle that practical knowledge is a species or a variety of knowledge, though of a special variety. But are they right? Polanyi himself makes me hesitate to agree with him on this, for he says:

Since we were not attending to the particulars in themselves, we could not identify them. [MY NOTE: Even if we identified them objectively or thetically, that wouldn’t function as practical knowledge, for the two are different (as Polanyi himself sometimes acknowledges, but not in the case of the physiologist’s knowledge of the body). But, then, since speculative knowledge is not related to action, it is not really knowledge at all]. But if we now regard the integration of particulars as an interiorization, it takes on a more positive character. It now becomes a means of making certain things function as the proximal terms of tacit knowing , so that instead of observing them in themselves, we may be aware of them in their bearing on the comprehensive entity which they constitute. It brings home to us that it is not by looking at things, but by indwelling in them, that we understand their joint meaning . - We can see know how an unbridled lucidity can destroy our understanding of complex matters... By concentrating attention on his fingers, a pianist can temporarily paralyze his movement (p. 18).  

We possess a practical knowledge of our own body , but the physiologist’s theoretical knowledge of it is far more revealing (p. 20). [MY NOTE: Like Aristotle who compares speculative and practical knowledge as to their precision, thereby assuming that they have a common genus , Polanyi makes a similar mistake. Actually my (knowledge of)  my body for me is totally different from the physiologist’s knowledge of the body as Polanyi understands it. Actually, however, the physiologist’s knowledge of the body is practical, not speculative or representational, and thus it is not knowledge. The physiological regularities of the human body are the basis of the medical science, which is applicative.] The formal rules of prosody may deepen our understanding of so delicate a thing as a poem. - But my examples show clearly that, in general, an explicit integration cannot replace its tacit counterpart. The skill of a driver cannot be replaced by a thorough schooling in the theory of the motorcar, the knowledge I have of my own body differs altogether from the knowledge of physiology ,* and the rules of rhyming and prosody do not tell me what a poem told me, without any knowledge of its rules (p. 20) - And we have seen also that a true knowledge of a theory can be established only after it has been interiorized and extensively used to interpret experience (p. 21).

[* MY NOTE: As indicated in the previous note, the physiologist’s knowledge is itself applicative, like a map. The interest of the physiologist’s observation of regularities (as well as of Galileo’s formula of the fall of bodies on inclineds) is that it can be applied. If there were no applications, nobody would have ever made, or made much case of,  the observations of physiologists. Concerning Aristotle on this point, he explicitly says that in the moment of practice or application there is no difference between speculative and practical knowledge, because by calling both ‘knowledge’ he reduces  speculative to practical knowledge. Yet in granting explicitly  that the knowledge of the practitioner is superior to that of the purely speculative knower, he implicit ly indicates that, after all, speculative knowledge is not the same as practical knowledge. ]

My point against Polanyi (and Sartre) is that in “attending from” –be it in physiology or in the application of a formula to a particular problem to solve it in theory or to transform the  environment in practice– we do not know speculatively or objectively at all, for the formula or the verbal  formalization are themselves meant to function as maps; they do not say objectively or representatively “the nature” or “the essence” of the particulars . In fact, as Polanyi says, we do not know the particulars, we ‘know’ from them, and that which we ‘know’ from them is not an ‘ Objekt,’ as Kant calls the term of the act of  knowing. This is why the knowledge of the particulars from which we ‘know’ is not needed and may even disturb the conduct. In the case of typing, for instance, I begin by pushing a letter and see that that letter appears on the paper. The next time I do that on purpose. However, the full indwelling obtains only when I do that not on purpose but automatically, when I attend to the letter to be written from the letter pushed –pushed, not known--, when I apply the stated recipe for typing. Seeing the letter typed on the paper refers to me who have an interest in typing as an agent, not as a contemplator. I can know theoretically how to type, or the way in which typewriters function, but this knowledge doesn’t make me a typist, anymore than the speculative knowledge about driving cars makes me a driver or is even required for driving. The reason is not only that speculative knowledge is not necessary in order for the agent to perform correctly, but that there is no such a thing as speculative knowledge in the sense of something that reproduces what is the case . For the so-called speculative knowledge does not reproduce speculatively or objectively what is the case (namely, that a typed letter appears on the paper when the letter is pushed), but is to be dwelled in by the interested agent , not to be seen or understood by a spectator. The disinterested spectator who grasps that the letter appears on the paper every time the letter is pushed knows nothing, literally, for this is not to be grasped as a fact, but to be dwelled in by the interested agent as a map or a recipe mediated by desire . (Much as the person who understands what is said in a Serlean manner understands nothing, for that is not the way in which language means).

But is there such a thing as practical knowledge? If by knowledge we understand the factual or objective reproduction of what is the case, the answer is no. If by knowledge we understand an agent’s ability to interiorize a regularity and attend from it in order to achieve a purpose, the answer is yes. But then, rather than equivocating by speaking of “knowledge,” whose paradigmatic case involves reproduction, and “truth,” which amounts to certitude of correct reproduction, we should speak of different kinds of agents and agency: from purely mechanical effect of the environment on non-sensitive agents without needs and desires (causality), to deliberate transformation of the environment (work) and deliberate self-creation by the agent.

Now we can understand where Polanyi and Sartre went wrong: (a) they still believed that there was such a thing as speculative, objective knowledge; (b) although dwelling in and attending from and non-positional do not involve reproduction, they insisted, like Aris, in calling them “knowledge,” which is totally equivocal.

There remain two important issues. (1) Doesn’t Searle have a point when he says that language, unlike other instruments, involves understanding and, therefore, is cognitive? (2) If all knowledge is practical or functions as a map or recipe, what is the truth-value of these reflections?

(1) Searle on language as representational  

“My knowledge is based on knowing how to play baseball, which is inter alia having internalized a set of rules [MY NOTE: Internalized by an interested agent and by actually following the rules as an agent, not by understanding them speculatively. When I study a text of geometry I internalize speculatively or predicatively that the sum of the three angles of a triangle equals two straight angles. But this doesn’t guarantee that I’ll know how to apply the rule to solve a problem. It is by actually applying the rule that I internalize it practically . Searle wrongly  thinks that the user of language follows the speculative linguistic characterizations he himself makes when he contemplates language as an object of study .] I want to suggest that my knowledge of linguistic characterizations is of the same kind...   On this account there is nothing infallible about linguistic characterizations; speakers’ intuitions are notoriously fallible. It is not always easy to characterize one’s skills and the fact that in these cases the skill  is involved in giving the characterizations does not serve to simplify matters. There is also the general difficulty in CORRECTLY FORMULATING KNOWLEDGE THAT ONE HAS PRIOR TO AND INDEPENDENT OF ANY FORMULAT ION, of converting knowing how into knowing what . [MY NOTE: This is what he is attempting to do, wrongheadedly, for he wrongly thinks that what the agent does according to a rule is equivalent to and grounded in the speculative knowledge of the rule]. We all know in one important sense what ‘cause’ ‘intend,’ and ‘mean’ mean, but it is not easy to state exactly what they mean’” ( Speech Acts, p. 14). [MY NOTE: A word means when it is used appropriately in the appropriate communicative context. The (stated) definition of what a word means is not the same as what the word means, for the definition is not a representation of that the word means but is to be dwelled in in the use of the word. And, when used, the word means practically, perlocutionarily. By stating exactly what a word means Searle would think (if he followed his own recipe for meaning) that he will know what the word means, as if meaning were equivalent to speculative knowledge of what a word  means. This reminds me of  Plato’s Socrates in the case of piety (cf. the Euthyphro) , who seemed to believe that in order to be a pious agent one needed to possess the exact, scientific or essential definition of piety. And Socrates would have applied to “God” or “the gods” what he applies to ‘piety.”]

“... the speaker in uttering one of these [sounds-sentences] is characteristically saying something and not merely mouthing words. In uttering (1) (“Sam smokes habitually”) the speaker is making... an assertion ... Austin baptized these complete speech acts with the name ‘ illocutionary acts , ’ and I shall henceforth employ this terminology (ibid., p. 23)... I now wish to add Austin’s notion of the perlocutionary act . Correlated with the notion of illocutionary acts is the notion of the consequences or effects such acts have on the actions, thoughts, or beliefs, etc., of hearers [MY NOTE: hearers, or addressees?] . For example,... by warning him I may scare or alarm him, by making a request I make get him to do something (ibid. p. 25). - Here, incidentally, is another point at which our analogy between performing speech acts and playing games breaks down. The pieces in a game like chess are not characteristically said to have a meaning, and furthermore when one makes a move one is not characteristically said to mean anything by that move (p. 43). GRICE gives the following analysis of the notion of “non-natural meaning”. To say that a speaker S meant something by X is to say that S intended the utterance of X to produce some effect in a hearer H by means of the recognition of his intention (p. 43) GRICE in effect defines meaning in terms of intending to perform a perlocutionary act [MY NOTE: To perform? Or, rather, to have performed by the addressee by virtue of the practical understanding characteristic of linguistic communication which is “attending from”? This, I believe, is what Grice means or should have said. As for myself, I agree with Grce who reduces meaning to the perlocutionary and does away with the invention of the illocutionary that results from reflection] , BUT saying something and meaning it is a matter of intending to perform an illocutionary act, not necessarily a perlocutionary act [MY NOTE: exactly the opposite of what I just said in the previous note. For in these paragraphs Searle has separated the illocutionary from the perlocutionary by distinguishing them more Platonico-Aristotelico. And by making the understanding of what is said the esential linguistic effect, he has attributed primacy to the illocutionary over the perlocutionary ]. ... Suppose that I am an American soldier in the second World War and that I am captured by Italian troops ... But does it follow from this account that when I say, Kenst du das Land , what I mean is I am a German soldier ? (p. 44) [MY NOTE: What follows is that linguistic creatures can apply their linguistic skills in irregular ways and convert certain sounds into signs as they can convert certain movements into signs-gestures. Although by virtue of its conventional illocutionary meaning “ Kenst du. ..” is not equivalent to “ I am... ,” nonetheless, by virtue of their ordinary practical perlocutionary meaning, which is what counts in communicative interactions, the two expressions are equivalent, under the particular circumstances of the linguistic agents involved, because they fujnction equivalently] .  ... What we can mean is at least sometimes a function of what we are saying. Meaning is more than a matter of intention, it is also at least sometimes a matter of convention (p. 45) [Against Grice], “ I wish to argue that saying something and meaning it is a matter of intending to perform an illocutionary act [ because, he argues,  many acts like “Hello” have no perlocutionary effect ]. “When I say Hello and mean it, I do not necessarily intend to produce or elicit any state or action in my hearer other than the knowledge that he is being greeted. But that knowledge is simply his understanding what I said ” (p. 46)... “ The characteristic intended effect of meaning is understanding , but understanding is not the kind of effect that is included in Grice’s examples of effects ” (p. 47). “Understanding a sentence is knowing its meaning” (p. 48).

In the above text together with my notes are expressed my differences with Searle.

His view, against Grice’s , is that all speech acts say something to be understood speculatively, with no direct, but merely accidental connection with action ; in addition to understanding , some speech acts intend to produce a perlocutionary effect, that is, an effect that is different from understanding, but this is not essential for the success of the speech act as such .

I think that the problem with Searle is that he doesn’t get at all the practical understanding involved in speech acts among interested agents, but separates it from the action intended and reduces it to a speculative understanding of a pure meaning among uninterested angels. As if when I give an order I intended primarily the addressee to understand that I am ordering something rather than that my order be carried out by him. Actually, I intend my order to be understood practically, not as a pure meaning, as something meant, and that practical understanding amounts to, and is shown in, my order being freely carried out in response to me. Thus Searle is wrong in saying that understanding is not the kind of effect included in Grice’s examples of effects, for Grice explicitly says that the speaker intends to produce an effect in the addressee by the latter’s recognition of his intention, which is precisely what practical linguistic understanding amounts to . [MY NOTE: But Searle is right in thinking that understanding as he, Searle, understands it, as speculative understanding , is not included in Grice’s examples of effects. On the other hand, he is wrong in the essential point, for linguistic understanding is of the sort Grice has in mind, not of the sort Searle claims.] In short, the recognition of which Grice speaks is practical understanding which, unless prevented by the circumstances or by the speaker’s decision, consists in actually obeying the S’s order . The “understanding from” characteristic of language is practical, for it trans-forms the agent by disposing him – as a free subject, for in its paradigmatic form it is communication among human adults -- to act in a certain way in recognition of S’s intention. In the case of “Hello” there is no ‘object,’ that is, no reference to the environment. Here the communication is from subject to subject; therefore the appropriate response is to respond to the saluter by saluting him (which involves the acknowledgment of his salutation), as in the subject to subject communication “I love you” the appropriate response is, “I love you too.” Thus Searle is wrong: in every speech act there is an intended perlocutionary effect and no illocutionary effect.

In different ways, the same reduction of the practical to the speculative seems to have been the problem with many important figures of the Western philosophical tradition. Plato began as a contemplator of knowledge obsessed with necessity and universality. Therefore he reduced practical to speculative knowledge without even noticing the difference between them. Aristotle distinguished the two, but didn’t escape sufficiently from Plato’s influence. Thus, by calling “ knowledge ” both the practical and the speculative, and by borrowing his notion of knowledge from Plato, he ultimately confused practical with speculative knowledge. Moreover, by deeming f ‘ episteme ’ or scientific knowledge the highest kind, he attributed to speculative knowledge the primacy over imprecise ( non-akribos ) practical knowledge. Hume is the one who came closer to the acknowledgment of interiorized experience as knowledge from, as a practical map for action and decision. However, he didn’t quite escape the tradition, for “as a philosopher” he still attributed the primacy to speculative knowledge (hence his skepticism), while recognizing the impossibility of moving from presentation to action, from ‘is’ to ‘ought.’ Sartre and Polanyi recognized the essentially practical character of interiorized experience, yet they still thought of it as a sort of knowledge, that is, speculative reproductive knowledge. Finally, in the area of language, because he confused the language he overheard as a philosopher of language with the language he spoke and heard as a user , Searle returned to Plato before Aristotle and totally ignored knowledge from, that is, interiorized practical experience that works for the agent as a map or a recipe for his or her future actions in relation to the surrounding world.

(3) On Kuhn and on the value of these reflections

“The paradigm as shared example is the central element... Exemplars will therefore require more attention than the other sort of components of the disciplinary matrix. Philosophers of science have not ordinarily discussed the problems encountered by a student in laboratories or in science texts, for these are thought to supply only in practice the application of what the student already knows. He cannot, it is said, solve problems at all unless he has first learned the theory and some rules for applying it. Scientific knowledge is embedded in theory and rules; problems are supplied to gain facility in their application. I have tried to argue, however, that this localization of the cognitive content of science is wrong. After the student has done many problems, he may gain only added facility by solving more. But at the start and for some time after, doing problems is learning consequential things about nature. In the absence of such exemplars, the laws and theories he has previously learned would have little empirical content ” [MY NOTE: Notice that the view Kuhn is attacking here is equivalent to Searle’s.] (Kuhn, The Structure of SR , Postscript, pp. 187-8. See what follows as an example on p. 188). - That sort of learning is not acquired by exclusively verbal means. Rather it comes as one is given words together with concrete examples of how they function in use; nature and use are learned together . To borrow once more Michael Polanyi’s useful phrase, what results from this process is ‘tacit knowledge’ which is learned by doing science rather than by acquiring rules for doing it ” ( Ibid. , p. 191). - “It is just because so very few ways of seeing will do that the ones that have withstood the tests of group use are worth transmitting from generation to generation. Equally, it is because they have been selected for their success over historic time that we must speak of the experience and knowledge of nature embedded in the stimulus-to-sensation route.   Perhaps ‘knowledge ‘ is the wrong word, but there are reasons for employing it. .. Those are characteristics of knowledge, and they explain why I use the term. But it is strange usage, for one other characteristic is missing. We have no direct access to what it is we know, no rules or generalizations with which to express this knowledge. Rules which could supply that access would refer to stimuli not sensations, and stimuli we can know only through elaborate theory. In its absence, the knowledge embedded in the stimulus-to-sensation route remains tacit ... Seeing water droplets or a needle against a numerical scale is a primitive perceptual experience for a man unacquainted with cloud chambers and ammeters . [MY NOTE: a primitive perceptual experience to be dwelled in or applied at the appropriate time in the appropriate context or circumstances]. It thus requires contemplation, analysis, and interpretation (or else the intervention of external authority) before conclusions can be reached about electrons or currents. But the position of the man who has learned about these instruments and had much exemplary experience with them is very different, and there are corresponding differences in the way he processes the stimuli that reach him from them . Regarding the vapor in his breath on a cold winter afternoon, his sensation may be the same as that of the layman, but viewing a cloud chamber he SEES (HERE LITERALLY) not droplets but the tracks of electrons, alpha particles, and so on. Those tracks are, if you will, criteria that he interprets as indices of the presence of corresponding particles, but that route is both shorter and different  from that taken by the man who interprets droplets ... In the metaphorical no less than in the literal use of ‘seeing’ interpretation begins where perception ends . The two processes are not the same , and what perception leaves for interpretation to complete depends drastically on the nature and amount of prior experience and training” ( Ibid. , pp. 196-198). - “The proponents of different theories are like the members of different language-culture communities. Recognizing the parallelism suggests that in some sense both groups may be right. Applied to culture and its development that position is relativistic. But applied to science it may not be...” (Ibid., p. 205). - “Compared with the notion of progress most prevalent among both philosophers of science and laymen, however, this position lacks an essential element. A scientific theory is usually felt to be better than its predecessors not only in the sense that it is a better instrument for discovering and solving puzzles but also because it is somehow a better REPRESENTATION of what nature is really like. One often hears that successive theories grow ever closer to, or approximate more closely, to the truth. Apparently generalizations like that refer not to the puzzle-solutions and the concrete predictions derived from a theory but rather to its ontology, to the match, that is, between the entities with which the theory populates nature and what is ‘really there’... Perhaps there is one other way of salvaging the notion of ‘truth’ for application to whole theories, but this one will not do. There is, I think, no theory independent way to reconstruct phases like ‘really there’; the notion of a match between the ontology of a theory and its real counterpart in nature now seems to me illusive in principle . Besides, as a historian, I am impressed with the implausibility of the view. I do not doubt, for example, that Newton’s mechanics improves on Aristotle’s and that Einstein’s improves on Newtons’ AS INSTRUMENTS FOR PUZZLE-SOLVING. But I can see in their succession no coherent direction of ontological development” ( Ibid.  p. 206). - “A few readers have noticed that I repeatedly pass back and forth between the descriptive and the normative modes, a transition particularly marked in occasional passages that open with ‘But that is not what scientists do,’ and close by claiming that scientists ought not to do so. Some critics claim that I am confusing description with prescription, the time-honored philosophical theorem: ‘Is’ cannot imply ‘ought.’ [MY NOTE: cannot for a contemplator, but can and does for an agent]. That theorem has, in practice, become a tag, and is not longer everywhere honored. A number of contemporary philosophers have discovered important contexts in which the normative and the descriptive are inextricably mixed... (See Stanely Cavell, Must we Mean what We say?, N.Y. 1969, ch. 1). But no recourse to the subtleties of contemporary linguistic philosophy is needed to unravel what has seemed confused about this aspect of my position. The preceding pages present a viewpoint or theory about the nature of science, and like other philosophies of science, the theory has consequences for the way in which scientists should behave if their enterprise is to succeed. [MY NOTE: THIS APPLIES TO SEARLE’S LINGUISTIC CHARACTERIZATIONS AND TO MINE]. Though it need not be right, any more than any other theory, it provides a legitimate basis for reiterated ‘oughts’ and ‘shoulds.’ Conversely one set of reasons for taking the theory seriously is that scientists, whose methods have been developed and selected for their success, do in fact behave as the theory says they should. My descriptive generalizations are evidence for the theory precisely because they can also be derived from it, whereas on the other views of the nature of science they constitute anomalous behavior. The circularity of my argument is not, I think, vicious (pp. 207-208).

On p. 3 above I raised two questions. One concerned Searle, and I took care of that in the preceding section. Now I want to deal with the second, whose point is to determine the value of my reflections in this paper, given that all knowledge is practical and, when stated, is meant to function in the appropriate circumstances as a map or a recipe. After all, my present reflections may be said to be valid only if what is the case corresponds to what I say, which involves the very notion of knowledge and truth that I have attacked. But that is a misunderstanding. In short,  I deny that what Searle says about language is valid because language is not an object but an appropriated instrument.

Yet I seem to be doing the same as Searle, for I myself  am using speculative language that doesn’t function as an instrument. But if that is the case,  then what is the use of the theory?

[MY NOTE: We theorize because we make mistakes. Our theories should be a prescriptive formulation of what we actually do when we succeed. We do not need them in order to act successfully, since they are grounded in the successful practice rather than the other way around.

But not all theories or characterizations are correct formalizations. And when they are, they lead to malpractice, or at least wrong self-understanding by the agent. Hence they need to be opposed with the correct theory.]

A theory of the kind we are considering here is like  the linguistic characterizations of which Searle speaks. (And like the philosophy of science Kuhn champions) . A linguistic characterization supposedly is a formalization of what we do when we use language. Now  I have been saying  that Searle’s own characterization of language is wrong, that we do not use language the way he says we do, that Searle himself doesn’t use language according to his characterization but according to mine. In short, I have been saying that his characterization is wrong because it doesn’t correspond to what we do when we use language when we communicate, and that mine is right because it corresponds. But there is an alternative: it is not a matter of true or false, but of persuasive or not persuasive to the very linguistic agent whose linguistic action is characterized. In fact, the agent neither has a theory of speech acts nor does he use a theory.

A HINT: As Polanyi says above, the formalization of a theory follows a long practice: “And we have seen that a true knowledge of a theory can be established only after it has been interiorized and extensively used to interpret experience” ( Tacit Knowledge , p. 21). The important point, for my purposes, is that a theory is a formalization of a practice. Thus, at least initially, it doesn’t precede but follows the practice. Moreover, the theory doesn’t determine the practice; it only makes explicit what we actually do in practice without an explicit theory, only with the tacit knowledge or non-verbal, practical internalization that cannot be substituted by any verbalization . [MY NOTE: APPLY THIS TO NEWTON’S CASE CONCERNING FALLING BODIES ON INCLINEDS].   In short, the theory is an explicit formulation of the rules of the game we play, but we play it without explicitly knowing the rules; we know the rules only in and by applying them . And we do not need to know speculatively the rules because the rules are practical, part and parcel of the practice. What counts is the practical internalization involved in the practice and, in the case of acquired habits, made possible by the frequent practice.

An example: Typing. [MY NOTE: This example is poorly chosen, for it is complicated by the communicative meaning of what one types. A mere skill, like car-driving, in which no meaning is involved, would be better]. The apparently speculative judgment-statement “A letter pushed shows on the paper” is not speculative for it is a formalization of the practice by an agent or for an agent interested in typewriting and is therefore meant to other interested agents as applicative, or directly herd-understood by the interested agent applicatively, as map for action under the appropriate circumstances. However, even this applicative interiorization that has the form of a rule (gerundive) remains theoretical until it actually informs action. As a person to whom an order is addressed understands an order by and in obeying it, for obedience to it is the practical understanding meant by the speaker, so it is in the actual pushing of letters by an interested agent to fulfill the communicative desire that the apparently speculative formulation reaches fruition. So the supposedly speculative formulation is an illusion of a contemplator, and understanding it as speculative, disconnected from action and unmediated by desire ,  is simply a mistake. Moreover, identifying the understanding therein involved as speculative is highly misleading as to our self understanding as people. for it makes us see ourselves primarily as disinterested contemplators in search of whatever can be known, as Aristotle imagined. [MY NOTE: Searle’s characterization of language as meaning something to be understood or known theoretically is of this kind and in no way corresponds to what the linguistic agent does or to the way in which language actually functions. Fortunately, not even the theorists like Searle and those ‘philosophers of language’ who  assent theoretically to that formulation, assent to it practically, for if they used language in that way they wouldn’t be speaking and hearing language, anymore than the skeptics would be alive if they exited from rooms through the windows rather than through the doors, or anymore than the students of language making sentences in the classroom are speaking-hearing language. ]

Thus even the applicative theoretical interiorization is by itself useless, for it is meant applicatively, and thus, as just said,  it is useful only when applied by agents. In fact, however, this theoretical applicative interiorization doesn’t precede the practice; it follows the practice –and only for purposes of transmitting the practice to interested agents. Indeed, only in the case of those who learn from the experts does the applicative theoretical ‘interiorization’ obtain, in the sense that it is stated by the teacher . And not quite, really, for it doesn’t obtain as theoretical but as practical, in actual application, for it is not genuinely internalized unless it is actually practiced and through practice. The original expert himself or herself didn’t need the theoretical statement. Geometry started as a practice of interested users in Egypt. Euclid formulated and systematized it, but not as a purely speculative science. The teacher knows that the disciple has understood, not when the disciple recites the theorem or even when the disciple demonstrates the theorem, but when the disciple has interiorized the model theorem as a ‘rule’ so that it can solve easily different problems by applying the rule, much as the teacher of a foreign language gives ‘problems’ to the disciples that lead them to apply the rule to different cases, so that the application of the rule becomes a facile habit until, ultimately, the disciple goes native in what previously was a foreign language. The teacher cannot teach the disciple to see a particular situation as one in which a particular problem can be applied. The disciple has to find out by himself or herself the situation in which a  particular problem applies.

These ‘bodies’ are neither phenomenal nor noumenal, for they are not term of a
