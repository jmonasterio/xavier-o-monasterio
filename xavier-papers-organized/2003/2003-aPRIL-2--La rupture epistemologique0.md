2003-April-2-La Rupture Epistemologique April 18, 2003

    LA RUPTURE EPISTEMOLOGIQUE

Rereading C. Dumont’s “La reflexion sur la methode theologique” ( Nouvelle Revue Theologique , 93e annee, December 1961, and 94e annee, janvier 1962), I think that I finally came to understand fully the epistemological rupture that took place progressively in the Church in the 12th and 13 th centuries. I had become aware of that rupture thanks to Chenu’s article “La theologie comme science au 13e siecle,” but without being able to diagnose it exactly. From the dire effects of that rupture we are still suffering, in part at least because we have been unable to diagnose it precisely for what it was and what it actually meant for the intellectual life of the Church . This is what I will attempt to do here.

Generally, the appropriation of Aristotle by the Christian intellectuals has been seen as an enormous progress, a sort of passage from obscurantism to enlightenment, consecrated very particularly by what has come to be known as the Aquinas’s synthesis of faith and reason. Such synthesis supposedly made possible for intellectuals to be people of faith without ceasing to be genuine intellectuals and for Christians to be genuine intellectuals without having to give up their faith. For my part, I have come to realize that the adoption of Aristotelianism as the framework for our understanding of the Christian faith and of ourselves as Christians has resulted in a big mess in which we are still entangled.

As is well known, the adoption of Aristotelianism in the medieval universities, both in the philosophical and the theological curricula, did not go without problems. A number of people, particularly among the teachers of theology, adamantly resisted the innovation it in the early years. For its part, Rome saw the adoption of Aristotelianism by its teachers with much suspicion too, at least initially. There were repeated Roman interventions against what seemed an increasing naturalism and rationalism incompatible with the Christian faith which depended on divine revelation. Still as late as 1277 took place one of the most famous of those interventions (in which Aquinas himself was condemned by name). To no avail. The Geistzeit clearly was in favor of the enlightened aristotelianists against the obscurantism of the anti-artistotelianists, who by the fourteenth century were practically in possession the field in all the universities across Europe. This situation prevailed in Catholic universities until the 20 th century when, as a result of the secularism brought about by the Enlightenment and by the progress of the natural sciences and their method, the allegiance to Aristotelianism as such and to the Scholastic method, faded rather suddenly. Even now, however, the adoption of Aristotelianism continues to be generally seen as a significant progress in the intellectual  life of the Church from obscurantism or at least intellectual primitivism toward rational maturity and openness to scientific truth. In fact, both Chenu and Dumont, the already mentioned authors who have studied in most detail the controversy on the adoption of the Aristotelian notion of science in theology, agree on considering it as a great progress.

Why? Without expressing themselves in exactly these terms, both of them, in agreement with Aquinas at least on this point, seem to see a profound compatibility and even a sort of  connaturality between the Biblical Christian faith and the intellectual structure of Aristotelianism. Thus they see the adoption of Aristotelianism as the interpretative framework of the doctrines of the Church as a powerful instrument of intellectual self-awareness and development toward full maturity on the Church’s part. One feels that they, as Aquinas himself and so many others, almost would feel tempted to say that the structure and philosophical validity of the essential lines of Aristotle’s system, rediscovered and adopted by the Catholic intellectuals between the 12 th and the thirteenth centuries, constituted a providential event for the intellectual growth of the Church.   

Of course, the assumption on which this view rests is that the Christian faith and the adopted Aristotelianism are indeed compatible and almost connatural, as all truth no matter what its origin is supposed to be. What I have come to realize, for my part, is that far from being compatible and connatural, the adoption of the Aristotelian views involved a radical epistemological rupture between the Biblical faith of the Church and the self-interpretation forced onto the church by the adopted Aristotelianism.
