                             EXISTENTIAL ABSTRACTION
The two strands of Aristotle's thought
      Socrates spent his time debunking pseudo-knowledge,  the  illusion  of
knowledge entertained  by  naive  people.  As  is  well  known,  his  method
consisted in extracting from them a statement of what they  believed  to  be
the  case  concerning  a  particular  matter  and   then   deflating   their
pretention. Implicit in Socrates debunking of naive beliefs was  the  notion
that real, genuine knowledge obtains if and only if what is believed  to  be
the cease  is  necessarily  so  as  believed  and  stated,  though  Socrates
admitted that he himself possessed not even one  piece  of  such  knowledge.
Hence his "I only know that I know nothing," which for him  constituted  the
beginning of wisdom for it was the  necessary  condition  and  beginning  of
"the examined life."
      Plato preserved his master's notion of  genuine  knowledge.  For  that
reason, he relegated the sensible world to the realm  of  opinion,  for  the
sensible world is constituted by the changing and merely  contingent,  which
by its very  nature  is  not  susceptible  of  being  genuinely  known,  and
declared the eternal world of the intelligible ideas, of which the  sensible
world is a mere shadow, to be  the  object  of  genuine  knowledge.  To  the
contemplation of that world and to  reshaping  the  polis  of  the  sensible
world in which we live after the image of the eternal world of  ideas  Plato
devoted his life.
      Aristoteles, Plato's disciple, has been said with  some  justice  that
he  "turned  Plato's  views  upside  down."  This  is  correct  as  a  rough
characterization insofar as it  suggests  that  Aristoteles  placed  Plato's
intelligible ideas within the sensible world while preserving Socrates'  and
Plato's notion of genuine knowledge under  the  name  of  epistéme,  thereby
remaining a Platonist of sorts. In the process of  this  reversal,  however,
Aristoteles found himself constrained to modify very  significantly  Plato's
views. The modifications are  radically  connected  with  the  role  of  the
sensible.
      For Plato the sensible world belonged  within  the  realm  of  opinion
because of its changing and contingent character. Having, however,  replaced
the intelligible within the sensible world, Aris had,  therefore,  to  start
with the sensible as the basis for knowledge and could  not  dismiss  it  as
irrelevant as Plato had  done.  Actually  much  of  the  deep  ambiguity  of
Aristotle's views is to be traced to his ambiguous, not to say  irremediably
conflicting, attitude toward  the  sensible.  For,  on  the  one  hand,  his
Platonism predisposed him against the sensible, which he granted  not  being
susceptible as of genuine knowledge. On  the  other  hand,  his  instinctive
realism, his pragmatic temperament, and the fact that in order to reach  the
intelligible which he had placed within the sensible world he had  to  start
with the sensible, forced him to take the sensible very seriously.
      Determining whether or not Aristoteles succeeded in reconciling  these
two strands is not an integral part of my present task, though  I  think  he
did not and many of the problems we are still today struggling  with  depend
on this unresolvable conflict in our heavily Aristotelian  heritage.  All  I
want to do is to highlight certain features of Aristoteles' views.
      One  of  those  important  features  is  Aristotle's  intellectualism.
Episteme, genuine knowledge, is a  matter  of  the  intellect,  not  of  the
senses. What is to be known is  the  intelligible,  not  the  sensible,  and
knowing is a task of the intellect, not of the senses. True, what is  to  be
known is what is intelligible in the sensible and we would know  nothing  if
there were no sensible to be understood or if we had no  senses  from  which
the abstractive operation  that  leads  to  the  intelligible  could  start.
However, it is  in  the  intellectual  act  of  judging  that  knowledge  is
reached, not in the act of perceiving, and it is the  intelligible  that  is
known, not the sensible.
      A second important feature of  Aristotle's  views,  closely  connected
with the preceding, is the process of  abstraction  through  which  we  move
from the level of the sensible to  the  level  of  the  intelligible  to  be
known. Very roughly speaking, a sensible presentation gives  us  an  initial
conviction that something  is  and  what  it  is.  For  immediate  practical
purposes  this  may  suffice  and  pass  for  knowledge.  However,   genuine
knowledge,  according   to   the   Socratic-Platonic   tradition,   involves
necessity.  We  only  know  genuinely  when  we  understand  what  something
necessarily is and that what we understand is necessarily the case. This  is
beyond the capacity of the sensible faculty. For instance,  to  use  one  of
Aristotle's favorite examples, we experience an eclipse of  the  sun.  In  a
way, we know what the eclipse is, since we experience it;  we  even  have  a
name for it--an eclipse, an occultation of the sun. In  the  important  way,
however, (at least in the important way for Aristotle) we do  not  know.  We
do not know, because we know only this particular eclipse,  something  which
happens to be one moment and will cease to be the  next  moment.  Obviously,
if what is contingent is not susceptible of genuine knowledge because it  is
contingent, we do not know anything by 'knowing'  this  ocultation  oif  the
sun. Furthermore, we really do not know what an eclipse really is, since  we
have no explanation for the eclipse, we do not  grasp  any  reason  for  the
occultation of the sun. However, in that sensible  experience  we  have  the
basis for genuine knowledge,  for  without  it  we  would  have  nothing  to
inquire about, we could not wonder why eclipses  occur.   What  we  want  to
know is the reason why of eclipses, which amounts to knowing  what  eclipses
really are--the essence or "ousía" of  eclipses,  what  necessarily  is  the
case if and whenever and wherever a solar eclipse occurs because it is  what
makes eclipses be what they are. Thus knowledge has its  basis  on  sensible
experience, though sensible experience itself is  not  knowledge.Another  of
Aristotle's examples: one 'knows' thunder through sensible experience,  that
is, one has the experience of thunder; but one  really  knows  thunder  only
when one knows what thunder essentially is, what makes thunder thunder,  why
thunder is what it is.
       How  does  one  reach  such  knowledge  of  the  intelligibility   of
phaenomena? A fundamental condition for it is  the  abstractive  ability  of
the human mind. The very fact  that  we  are  not  satisfied  with  sensible
'knowledge' but want to know the reason why of  the  experienced  phaenomena
and ask questions on the basis of sensible  experience  but  which  sensible
experience does not and cannot answer is already  a  manifestation  of  that
ability. For, though we may ask what an eclipse really or essentially is  on
the occasion of one particular eclipse, what we want to know  does  no  only
refer to this particular eclipse but to all possible eclipses,  to  eclipses
as such. In other words, our very desire to  know  the  why  or  essence  of
eclipses as such reveals and initiates a shift  from  the  sensible  to  the
conceptual or intelligible level.  As  analyzed  by  Aristotle,  this  shift
involves a reduction of all our memories of  past  sensible  experiences  of
eclipses to one typical image,  and  a  dematerialization  of  this  typical
image through which it is reduced to its bare  intelligible  structure.  The
reduction of  our  successive  memories  of  eclipses  to  a  typical  image
involves already a sort of abstraction from all  other  data  of  experience
except those which are common to all the eclipses  we  remember,  namely,  a
progressive occultation of the sun. However, we are still on  the  level  of
the sensible. Indeed, though we  are  not  seeing  any  one  eclipse  but  a
typical image that, so to say, summarizes all the eclipses  we  remember  or
have heard of, we are still working  with  something  imaginable.  What  the
agent intellect proceeds to do as a further step is  to  dematerialize  this
image  by  reducing  it  to  its  bare   intelligible   structure,   namely,
progressive  occultation  of  the  sun.  It  is   on   this   dematerialized
intelligible structure elaborated by the agent intellect, a  material  which
abstracts from here or there, from now or then, and from  all  sensible  and
even imaginable aspects, that the question concerning what makes an  eclipse
be what it is can be answered.
      A more perspicuous example also alluded to by Aristotle might help  to
clarify the passage from the sensible to the intelligible level. Suppose  we
are going to do some Euclidean geometry  concerning  triangles.  How  do  we
proceed? We draw a picture of a triangle. Of course, the triangle  drawn  is
one particular triangle. However, we do not 'view'  it  as  this  particular
triangle of such a shape and such dimensions, but merely as a typical  image
of triangles. This 'viewing' represents the process  of  reduction  of  past
experiences of triangles to a typical image. Furthermore, we do  not  'view'
the typical image of triangles which we  have  drawn  as  composed  of  real
lines like the ones actually drawn which have a certain thickness  to  them,
but as formed by ideal or immaterial lines which occupy no space at all  and
which are unrelated to the space in which things occupy a  place.  In  other
works, we are working within a purely intelligible, 'geometrical' space  and
with a purely intelligible, dematerialized triangle. This is the  immaterial
idea, not the image, of  triangle  on  which  the  question  concerning  the
essential properties of triangles will be worked out and answered.  For  the
process  of  abstraction,  in  its  turn,  is  followed  by  a  process   of
understanding which, when successful, leads to an  intelligible  prospective
answer to the question. And, if and when this prospective answer is, in  its
turn, grasped by the  intellect  as  grounded  on  compelling  evidence,  it
results  in  the  true  unconditional  judgment  that  expresses   what   is
necessarily the case and constitutes the genuine knowledge looked for.
      The process just described--constituted by experiencing,  questioning,
abstracting, ideating, conceiving an  answer,  and  judging--is,  given  the
Aristotelian notion of epistéme, the  only  process  through  which  genuine
knowledge or epistéme can be reached, for  it  is  the  only  process  which
leads to what is necessarily the case, to what is so essentially or  by  its
nature  and,  therefore,  cannot  be  otherwise  in  any  particular   case.
In connection with this sort of knowledge  and  obviously  taking  from  the
field of mathematics his model of a whole body of genuine knowledge, in  the
Posterior Analytics Aristotle outlines his ideal of a  science:  an  already
constituted body of knowledge  all  of  whose  principles  are  evident  and
universally valid, all of whose basic statements are statements  of  essence
reached in the way just described, and all  of  whose  ultimate  conclusions
are  reached  through  a  process  of  strict  logical   deduction   through
combination of principles and basic statements.  This,  as  we  shall  later
see, was the ideal of science which the thirteenth century  theologians  had
in mind when they discussed the  problem  of  the  scientific  character  of
theology.
      All of the above shows  the  strong  Platonic  strand  in  Aristotle's
thought,  with  its  insistence  on  the  intelligible,   unchangeable   and
necessary. I have already mentioned, though,  that  there  is  also  in  his
thought a strong realistic and pragmatic strand,  some  of  whose  facets  I
want now to highlight.  Indeed, had Aristotle been entirely and  exclusively
sold  to  the  Socratic-Platonic  idea  of  knowledge  as  that   which   is
necessarily true and cannot be otherwise, he would have simply dismissed  as
spurious, as Plato did, whatever 'knowledge' concerns directly the  sensible
world and relegated it to the realm of "doxa," THAT IS,  of  the  ungrounded
pretention of knowledge, of the mere illusion of  knowledge  (See  Mansion).
Actually, however, Aristotle did not do that. Though he was  decidedly  more
interested in the intelligibility of  what  exists  than  in  what  actually
exists,  though he clearly realized that the realm of the  sensible  is  the
realm  of  the  constantly  changing  and  contingently  existing  and  that
perceptual knowledge is, if only by the very  nature  of  its  changing  and
contingent object, fallible, he nevertheless  implicitly  and  wholehartedly
took for granted the cognitive ability of the perceptual apparatus   and  as
known the existence of the  contingent  and  changing  sensible  world.  His
remarks about scepticism are very telling in this  regard.  Furthermore,  he
also realized that, were he to apply  strictly  his  stringent  standard  of
genuine knowledge, of epistéme, our knowledge not only of the existence  the
natural phaenomena which we get from perceptual observation but even of  the
intelligibility of those phaenomena would have to go down the  drain.  Hence
the  notion  of  "ousia"  or  essence  he  actually  used  in  practice  was
sufficiently  loose  as  to  to  accommodate  not  only  strictly  essential
properties but even such contingent events as the fact that  the  orbits  of
the earth and the moon are such that eclipses can occur; in other words,  in
his practice and even in his examples he came to equate  the  regularity  of
sensible phaenomena with mathematical, conceptual  regularity.  In  baffling
addition to this, as has been  remarked  by  his  commentators,  the  actual
procedure followed by Aristotle in many of his  concrete,  less  theoretical
investigations, such as the De Motu, barely corresponds to his own  precepts
to reach genuine knowledge. In fact, as perspicuously shown by Nussbaum,  in
those investigations Aristotle relies heavily on  "what  is  said"  about  a
phaenomenon by the learned rather than on  observation  of  the  phaenomenon
itself, and concludes to "saving the appearances," that  is,  to  preserving
that version of what is said which better  coheres  with  human  experience.
Finally,  realizing  that  practical  knowledge  whose  purpose  is  to  act
appropriately  is  of  a  different  kind  from  the  purely   contemplative
knowledge of intelligible essences represented by epistéme, and that in  the
realm of practical knowledge there is no room  for  intelligible,  necessary
essences but only for rough approximations, that is, for what is  true  only
"for the most part"--"epi tou pollú"--, Aristotle  explicitly  admitted  the
validity of such knowledge and pursued it vigorously in his  ethical  works.

      My purpose in  highligting  the  realistic  and  pragmatic  strand  of
Aristotle's thought which coexists in a  highly  problematic  way  with  his
Platonic, essentialist strand is not, as I already said, to  decide  whether
the  two  strands  are  logically  compatible  and  whether   Aristotle   is
ultimately incoherent or not. It is only to show that Aristotle himself,  in
some cases explicitly  and  in  many  others  implicitly,  acknowledged  the
practical impossibility of  reducing  the  human  ability  to  know  to  the
ability to know the purely intelligible, of following in  many  matters  the
abstractive  process  delineated  by  him  to  reach  the   level   of   the
intelligible (a process which supposedly should apply to all matters  to  be
known), and of restraining the sphere of the  known  to  what  is  filtrated
through that process and understood and judged in the way he  had  dictated.
Now,  this  practical  impossibility,  at  least  implicitly  reflected   in
Aristotle's own  works,  is  a  powerful  indicator  that  an  entirely  new
beginning is in order. After all, it is hardly  probable  that  out  of  the
pieces of a theory of knowledge that was conceived with the notion that  the
world we have corresponds in some measure to Plato's  world  of  ideas,  and
which in the hands of his own creator  proved  its  practical  inability  to
deal with the only world we have, we will be able to
make a working whole. At any rate, for the present I will confine myself  to
a more modest task than an entirely new beginning by  concentrating  firstly
on the issue of abstraction and, then, on the  issue  of  understanding  and
judging  because  of  their  direct  bearing  on  the  medieval  theological
controversy.
Eliminating the irrelevant
      Abstraction, a certain form of  abstraction,  pervades  and  underpins
Aris's  views  on  knowledge  and   reality.   The   real   is   potentially
intelligible. Knowledge, as opposed to opinion, is knowledge of  reality  as
intelligible, for only such knowledge can possess the  compelling  evidence,
universality and necessity that Aristotle's Platonic inheritance forced  him
to postulate  initially  as  constituting  genuine  knowledge.  How  is  the
intelligible reached? Throgh abstraction from the singular or collection  of
singulars to the typical image  and  through  the  futher  dematerialization
effected by the intellectus agens to reach the  intelligible  idea  (see  S.
Mansion). As someone said, Aris brings down to the world Plato's  forms.  In
this sense, Aris remains  a  Platonist.  And  through  him,  thanks  to  the
assimilation of Aris by the Christian world in the  XIIth-XIIIth  centuries,
which dictated the future of science, philo and theo, we all have remained.
       Following  Aris,  for  instance  Lonergan,  Aris's   abstraction   is
considered  in  view  of  determining  what   the   thing   at   stake   is,
explanatorily, and is described as "the elimination  of  the  intellectually
irrelevant":
      "What is variously termed materia in individualis, materia  designata,
      materia signata, the hic et nunc, cannot be an explanatory  factor  in
      science; it  is  irrelevant  to  all  scientific  explanation;  it  is
      irrelevant apriori; time and place as such explain  nothing,  for  the
      reason for anything, the cause of anything, is never this instance  at
      this place and time, but always a nature which, if found here, can  be
      found elsewhere, if found now,  can  be  found  later.  Hence  natural
      scientist,  mathematician  and   metaphysician   all   abstract   from
      individual matter. Intellect abstracts from the  hic  et  nunc...  The
      astronomer can predict all the eclipses of coming centuries;  but  his
      cience as such will not give him knowledge of any  particular  eclispe
      as particular 'sicut  rusticus  cognoscit';  for  in  so  far  as  the
      astronomer knows future eclipses as particular, it is only by relating
      his calculations to a sensible given here and now. Properly  intellect
      does not remember; to know the past as past, like knowing the present,
      is  the  work  of  sense.  Why  are  all  these  statements  made   so
      confidently? Because it is common to all science to consider  the  per
      se and disregard the per accidens. In other words, the 'here and now,'
      or the 'there and then,' as  such  are  irrelevant  to  understanding,
      explanation, the assigning of causes;  and  from  them  the  intellect
      abstracts,   inasmuch   as,   and   because,   it   understands   that
      irrelevance...
      "The second degree of abstraction is similar  to  the  first:  as  all
      science  prescinds  from  the  'here  and  now,'  so  all  mathematics
      prescinds from all sensible qualities--from  colors,  sounds,  tactile
      experiences, tastes, odors... the theorem  and  the  judgment  of  the
      geometer are  independent  of  sensible  fact  and  are  content  with
      imagination... Finally, the third degree of abstraction prescinds from
      all matter, individual and common, sensible and intelligible, to treat
      of 'ens, unum, potentia et actus, et  alia  huiusmodi.'  It  does  so,
      because metaphysical theorems are valid independently of any  sensible
      matter of fact and of any condition of imagination" (Verbum,  pp.  39-
      43).
      Three points  in  this  quotation  interest  me.  Th  first  is  thazt
abstraction consists in "the elimination of the  irrelevant  because  it  is
understood to be irrelevant" (p. 39). But irrelevant for what  or  to  what?
"To understanding, explanation, the assigning of causes".  In  other  words,
irrelevant to the  pursuance  of  the  only  kind  of  knowledge  considered
genuine by Socrates and Plato and defined by Aristotle as epistéme.  Indeed,
to say that something is relevant or irrelevant presupposes a  principle  of
selection which arises from that which is pursued. In  Aristotle's  case  as
well as in Lonergan the principle of selection arises  from  the  notion  of
genuine knowledge Aristotle  inherited  from  Plato.  Relevant  is  what  is
conducive to knowledge; irrelevant is what is not  conducive  to  knowledge.
But what is Aris's idea  of  "knowledge"?  Plato's  idea  of  knowledge.  Of
course, insofar as one assumes, as  Aristotle  clearly  did  intially,  that
knowledge, in order to genuinely be  knowledge,  has  to  be  universal  and
necessary, one will agree with this. But what if one does not  assume  that,
for the very same reasons that forced Aristotle to be inconsistent with  his
initial assumption--an assumption which he never came to reject?
      What is "relevant" and "irrelevant" is, then, in  this  passage,  tied
to epistéme, that is, to the kind  of  knowledge  that  concerned  Aristotle
after Plato.  Indeed, only to someone who  pursues  the  kind  of  knowledge
Aristotle defines as epistéme is irrelevant the hic et nunc  and  the  there
and then that sort of knowledge is that kind of abstraction  relevant.  For,
obviously, to someone interested in buying a piece of meat  are  the  color,
the smell, the juiciness, and the thickness of a piece of meat  as  relevant
as they may be irrelevant to someone who pursues scientific knowledge.  Even
Aristotle conceded that not all  matters  are  susceptible  of  episteme  or
scientific knowledge and settled for a debased version--to  epi  tou  polu--
for certain other matters, and even granted some sort  of  knowledge  or  at
least doxa even to animals which only know particulars.  Why,  then,  should
we assume that only what is relevant to scientific knowledge is relevant  to
anybody who wants to know?
      The second point that interests me  in  the  quotation  is  that  "The
astronomer can predict  all  the  eclipses  of  coming  centuries;  but  his
science as such will not give him knowledge of  any  particular  eclipse  as
particular 'sicut rusticus cognoscit', for  in  so  far  as  the  astronomer
knows  future  eclipses  as  particular,  it  is  only   by   relating   his
calculations to a sensible given here and now" (p. 40).  What  interests  me
is to explore how the rusticus knows the eclipse (not  whether  or  not  the
eclipse will occur) and whether or not this is part of reality,  though  the
astronomer as such does not know it.
      The fundamental difference between the knowledge of the  rusticus  and
that of the scientist lies in objectification and  abstraction  that  enters
in  the  scientist's  undertaking.  Objectification   amounts   to   viewing
something as subject of predication. What is this?
       Experience,  in  the  case  of  encounter,  is  structured  but   not
predicative. It is  practical.  It  is  experience  by  exposed  subject  of
exposed object in a common world. The object is viewed in  terms  of  action
and passion  and  according  to  the  present  project  or  outcome  of  the
transaction desired by the subject. The object  is  viewed  with  motivating
qualities in the framework of the present  project,  not  as  a  theoretical
subject  of  theoretical  predicates.  For  the   scientist   the   observed
occultation of the sun is an isolated, selb-standing given to be  observed--
neither here nor there in relation to the  observer--,  a  mere  describable
collection of selected attributes hung  up  on  the  name  "eclipse,"  which
itself works as a support for further  questions,  much  as  the  conceptual
definition of the name will be  a  mere  support  for  further  intelligible
attributes laboriously hung up upon it by means of predicative judgments.
      What do I mean  by  theoretical  subject  of  theoretical  predicates?
Hume's "expectations" might help here. These are of two  sorts:  observable,
and of exposure
       The  tird  point  that  interests  me  is  that  "natural  scientist,
mathematician, and  metaphysician all abstract from individual matter"  (pp.
39-40). Where is the theologian to be placed in this list: as  a  physicist,
as a mathematician, as a metaphysician? In other words, is abstraction  part
of the job of the theologian?
Or, rather, as Ií suspect,  should  the  theologian  be  placed  in  another
category as pursuing a different kind of knowledge than  the  one  Aristotle
called epistéme?
       My  interest  in  these  particular  points  is  connected   to   the
assimilation  of  Aristotelianism  by  the  thirteenth   century   Christian
theologians.

      Rain.
      Understanding - Believing that - Believing in.
      Experiential notion of rain:
                 water pouring from the sky that  wets  the  earth...  which
makes crops possible... without which there is nothing  to  eat...  so  that
people die...
                 water pouring from the sky  that  creates  rivers...  which
makes fluvial navigation possible...  without  which   goods  do  not  reach
distant towns... so that people...
                 water pouring from the  sky...  which  makes  public  games
impossible... which... so that people...
       Believing  in  rain   is   projective   (toward   the   future)   and
dispositional. It is desiring or fearing  or  enjoying  or  detesting  water
pouring from the sky under a particular set of  experienced  effects.  These
effects are constitutive of rain. They are not effects, actually,  but  rain
itself, since they are constitutive of  the  experiential  notion  of  rain.
Rain is, inseparably, desirable pouring of water from  the  sky-getting  the
earth wet-conditioning crops...
      Believing that water pouring from the sky wets the earth,  swells  the
rivers...--independently of benefits or malefices received from it.
            If  "believing  in  rain"  by  definition  involves  fearing  or
desiring rain as constituted by a particular set of traits, then  "believing
that rain..." is idiosyncratic. I believe that  rain  wets  the  earth,  you
believe that rain swells the rivers, he believes that rain  prevents  public
games. In other words, a "belief that" is common only to  those  who  desire
or fear rain  under  the  same  set  of  traits.  And  only  in  this  sense
"believing in" implies "believing that." For you "believe that  rain  swells
the rivers," whereas I "do not believe that rain  swells  the  rivers,"  but
"that rain wets the earth. This  commonality  among  believers-in  makes  it
possible for the members of a particular community to talk among  themselves
about the rain in the nominative without equivocating;  obviously,  however,
they would equivocate if they talked about the  rain  with  the  members  of
other communities, for their definition of rain involves different  sets  of
desirables or undesirables.
      In the case of rain, though, even though the sets of desirable  traits
constitutive of rain are different, the  set  of  observable  phenomena  are
common to the  members  of  different  communities  of  believers-in:  water
pouring from the sky-wetting the  eart-swelling  rivers...  Now,  that  rain
wets the earth is an essential trait  for  the  members  of  one  community,
since it is constitutive of the rain they desire, while  that  it  wets  the
earth is an inessential property  for  the  members  of  another  community.
However, from another point of view,  abstracting  from  their  desires  and
fears and placing themselves as pure observers, they can create a  community
of pure observers  of  'rain  in  itself'  and  talk  about  'rain'  in  the
nominative without equivocation. On this level they all believe "that  water
pouring from the sky wets the earth,  swells  the  rivers,  prevents  public
games..."
      How does this kind of "belief that" relate to "belief in"?
Let us call this new universal community the community of observers and  the
previous different communities the communities  of  practitioners.  All  the
people who have an experiential notion of rain are members of the  community
of observers, but only some of them are members of each of the community  of
practitioners. But can a member continue to be a believer-in, a member of  a
particular community of believers-in, while being  a  member  of  the  large
community of believers-that? After all, we previously said that the beliefs-
that of different communities are different, that I believe that  rain  wets
the earth and you do not believe that. It seems that what  happens  here  is
that we are equivocating in a very radical  way.  Indeed,  we  have  shifted
from  the  universe  of  desire-aversion  to   the   universe   of   neutral
observation. We might think that we are speaking about the  same  rain,  for
water pouring from the sky is an observable common to  all  the  communities
of practitioners. But each of those communities is a  community  of  desire-
aversion, not of mere observation,  and  in  the  order  of  desire-aversion
water pouring from the sky wetting the earth is constitutive of rain  as  an
indissoluble whole. Water pouring from the sky is not a mere observable  for
the community of practitioners that has the  experiential  notion  of  water
wetting the earth.
      More clarification of this important point is  needed.  One  important
difference between animals and people is that people  are  not  just  agents
but lingustic agents who, as such, do not only encounter but also think  and
talk about what they encounter.
For me, initially, rain is water pouring from the  sky  wetting  the  earth.
For you, itis water swelling the rivers. When we act, are we  responding  to
the same? If we were capable only of encountering rain and only  as  wetting
the earth and swelling the rivers, we would not be able  to  spear  to  each
other about rain without  equivocation.  The  fact  that  we  are  lingustic
agents
capable of naming what we encounter  and thus,  of  talking  about  what  we
encounter, not only of responding to it, allows us to enrich  our  practical
notions. Thus rain becomes for us an  open  'that'  as  object  of  possible
responses: that which looks the ways it looks  and  wets  the  eart,  swells
rivers, prevents games, makes taking baths possible, quenches thirst...  Are
we talking about the same when we talk about rain? Yes,  if  two  conditions
obtain. If both of us are talking about rain  as  object  for  response  for
people and if we understand, as  linguistic  agents,  each  other's  present
practical stand in relation to the rain we are talking  about.  If  you  are
hoping for rain because of the crops and I  understand  you  to  talk  about
impending rain as preventor of games assuming that your project is  to  play
or to attend the games, we are using the name rain  for  the  impending  one
equivocally. This equivocation, though, will be easily  discovered  and  can
easily be corrected. If, for instance, you tell me that you cannot wait  for
the rain when I believe youare talking about the rain as preventor  of  your
game, I realize that you are not talking about the rain under  that  set  of
practical expectations.
Other equivocations, however, are irremediable and  incorrigible,  for  they
reflect different beliefs-in. For instance, you believe in  abortion  and  I
dont. This means that we have totally different dispositions toward  it  and
in respect of it, so that you would practice, and approve  of,  the  use  of
abortion, and I would not. The  same  with  slavery.  In  all  these  cases,
however, the equivocation is less radical than in the following.
      The following is the case in which on of the interlocutors is  talking
on the practical level of object forresponse and the  other  on  the  purely
theoretical level. Some clarification is needed, however, to make  sense  of
what I am now talking about.
      Socrates was interested in definitions  because  he  thought  that  in
order to say something true one had to know  what  one  was  talking  about,
where knowing  what  one  is  talking  about  means  knowing  the  essential
definition of a subject of predication.
For instance, Socrates would think that I cannot speak of rain until I  know
what rain is in itself, what makes  rain  be  what  it  is  and,  therefore,
function as rain.
