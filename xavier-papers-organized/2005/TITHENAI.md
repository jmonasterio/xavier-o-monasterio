                             THITHENAI TA PHAINOMENA
Owen and Nussbaum
      Reread Owen on "tithenai" and Nussbaum on "Saving Aristotle's
appearances."
      What is "tithenai ta phainomena"? Setting down the phaenomena that
are to be explained. This setting down takes place when one is on the
scientific gear on which Aristotle is, that is, when one wants to know the
"why" of a phaenomenon, as Aristotle does. The material, so to say, from
which the phaenomena are to be set down by the inquirer is ordinary
experience, pre-scientific experience, without which there would be nothing
about which to ask questions. Both Owen and Nussbaum agree on this. The
issue between them is whether there are two or only one way of setting down
the phaenomena and how they are related to pre-scientific or ordinary
'experience.' Owen thinks that Aristotle sets down the phaenomena in two
different ways: (1) a purely empirical, "Baconian way," by means of
uninterpreted, theory-free observation; (2) a conceptual way that takes as
its data "ta legoumena" of the many or the wise. For her part, Nussbaum
claims that only the second way is practiced by Aristotle.
      Both Owen and Nussbaum seem to agree that there is an at least
seeming or apparent discrepancy between the way in which Aristotle claims
in his theoretical writings that the phaenomena are to be set down and the
way he actually follows in his practice. Furthermore, what both of them
primarily take into consideration is the way followed by Aristotle in his
practice. However, while Owen is less explicit concerning the question
whether the Baconian way of setting the phaenomena corresponds to the way
defined by Aristotle in his theoretical writings Nussbaum explicitly denies
that there is any discrepancy between Aristotle's theory and his practice.
According to her, there is only one way Aristotle sets down the phaenomena
in both his 'scientific' and his 'philosophical' writings (in the modern
sense of these words, where science and philosophy are taken to constitute
different undertakings). This only way practiced by Aristotle, again
according to Nussbaum, was to start with what the many or the wise say--"ta
legoúmena"-- and this is what Aristotle always did in both his 'scientific'
and his 'philosophical' works. The reason why there is no discrepancy
between Aristotle's theory and his practice is because, in Nussbaum's eyes,
Aristotle did not mean that the "principles" of the ideal science he
portrayed in the Posterior Analytics were self-evident, indemonstrable, a
priori, necessarily true, and known in a special way thanks to a particular
faculty as the mediaeval understood to be the case, but meant it in another
less strict way which there is no point in characterizing here. In fact,
though this is less clear in Nussbaum's article, the principles of a body
of knowledge are reached in a comparable way to that in which are reached
all the pieces of knowledge that will function as the basis for deductive
conclusions. The refinement of this point may wait for later.
            At first sight, Owen seems to have a scoop against Nussbaum in
the fact that, while claiming that the way in which Aristotle sets down the
phaenomena is never Baconian, Nussbaum is forced again and again to grant
that there are 'differences' between, say, the more empirical works of
Aristotle, such as                 , and his more philosophical works, such
as the Ethics and the Politics. In addition, she admits that Aristotle
himself allowed that matters like the ones he dealt with in the latter
works were not susceptible of epistéme, and that not all subjects lent
themselves to the same degree of precision. On the other hand, Nussbaum
seems to have a scoop against Owen in the fact that the latter grants that
in at least certain of his 'scientific' works, that is, works that deal
with rather empirical or observable matters such as movement, Aristotle
sets down the phaenomena not by means of empirical observation but by
relying on what is said by the many and the wise.
      These two observations furnish me my guiding questions.
If the pahenomena are always set down in the same way by Aristotle, as
Nussbaum contends, why are they significant differences, acknowledged by
Aristotle and Nussbaum, in his different works, depending on their
respective subject-matter?
Secondly, if there are two different ways of setting down the phaenomena,
as Owen maintains, why does Aristotle rely on "ta legoumena" in certain
treatises that deal with empirical matters? Finally, is there a way of
reconciling Owen's and Nussbaum views, irrespective of what Aristotle
himself thought?
Aristotle's notion of experience
      For Aristotle the issue of "setting down the appearances" arises in
the context of his project of reaching the kind of knowledge he is after,
namely, explanatory knowledge, knowledge of what he calls "causes,"
knowledge that explains the phaenomena set down. In other words, "setting
down the phaenomena" amounts to defining or delimiting or delineating or
characterizing clearly the particular phaenomena to be explained.
      Taking this for granted, I return to a comment with which I started.
Both Owen and Nussbaum agree that, in one way or another, the phaenomena to
be set down by Aristotle originate or rest on pre-scientific experience. In
other words, Aristotle has only to delimit the phaenomena to be explained;
he does not have to invent them. And he does not have to do that, among
other reasons, because he is interested in the knowledge of reality, not of
the imaginable or conceivable, and the knowledge of reality starts with the
experienced world. Because of this, Owen and Nussbaum find it natural that
Aristotle, in setting the phaenomena, get his material from what is said by
the many and the wise, for what is said by the many and the wise is
precisely the world as experienced by people. On this point, the difference
between Owen and Nussbaum is that Owen maintains that people experience the
world in two ways--one of which is by means of simple, uninterpreted,
sensory observation, and the other by means of prejudiced experience--,
whereas Nussbaum claims that Aristotle always sets the phaenomena by
reference to the prejudiced experience of people for the simple reason that
there is no uninterpreted observation, not unprejudiced experience.
"Empiricism," in other words, as we have come to known it since Bacon, is
not only a silly fiction, in Nussbaum's eyes, in that it does not represent
the way either common people or even hard-scientists experience, but it is
"anachronic" to attribute such idea of experience to Aristotle.
                                  .........
      The insight I want to explore and to test against the data of this
case is that "experience" assumes different meanings depending on whether
it refers to observation or to exposure. This carries consequences
concerning the theoretical and the practical sciences.
      In Galileo's case, setting the phaenomenon was a matter of
observing-measuring. This set the phaenomenon to be explained, that is, the
regularity of the relationship among certain observable and measurable
data. This setting of the phaenomenon presupposed and was guided by
Galileo's interest in understanding the fall of bodies.
      In the case of akrasía the phaenomenon is not observable in the same
way. Yet, it is 'observable': we have a name for it and most people of
experience understand what the name means: acting against one's conviction
of what is best. For Plato, however, akrasia was impossible; what people
called akrasia was just another case of ignorance of what is best. (For
Plato, furthermore, there was no distinction between knowing what is and
knowing what is good). Hence the aporia or conflict between what the many
say and what Plato says.
      What was setting down the phenomenon in this case?
Bringing the aporia to the fore and explaining it in such a way as to save
the common legoumenon. The phaenomenon here is, therefore, not an
empirically observable but a legoumenon because the name itself "akrasia"
involves a certain understanding of the experienced discomfort. Akrasia is
not just a feeling of discomfort, but the discomfort experienced in failing
to do what one knows is best.
      Galileo took for granted the fall of bodies on inclined planes
perceivable by everybody, transformed it into a set phaenomenon by
determining the relevant variables, and explained it by determining the law
of regularity in the observable (through memory) changes of observable
variables. What this process yielded was a scientific legoumenon. From now
on the learned could take as their basis for further inquiries that the
fall of bodies on inclined planes is such a relationship among such
variables. This would stand as their basis until and unless an aporia arose
and a better, more persuasive because more all-embracing explanatory law
arose.
      Now, Nussbaum grants, as Aris does, that there are differences
between the field of physics and the field of ethics (Aris makes the
differences a matter of akribousia--precision, exactness in the 'laws'--due
to the nature of the subject matter); she denies, however, that the
differences affect the way of setting down the phaenomena in physics and in
ethics: "as in all other cases"--she makes Aris say, setting down a
phaenomenon is gathering ta legoumena. And her scoop is that, as
acknowledged by Owen, Aris does exactly that in the Physics, not only in
the Ethics. That is, he starts, unlike Galileo, with legoumena rather than
with empirical observations. Owen, for his part, maintains that the
differences determine two different ways of setting the phaenomena, though
he absolves Aris of inconsistency between the Analytica Posteriora and the
Topics, because "If there is more than one use for the expression
'phainomena,' the uses have a great deal in common" (p. 117). [IS THERE
REALLY A DISCREPANCY BETWEEN OWEN AND NUSSBAUM ON THIS POINT?].
      It might be that we are here faced with an "aporia." In that case,
who is right? Or it may be that Owen and Nussbaum are in agreement. In that
case, how? Or it may be that Aristotle put both of them on the wrong track.
In that case, what is the right track?
      If Aristotle had to study "fall," as Galileo did, how would he have
set the phaenomenon: empirically, as Galileo did, or conceptually, as Aris
does in the Physics for place, motion, etc.? I think that the latter would
have been the case. Why?
Because "fall" is a term of ordinary language as much as place and motion,
and Aris took these terms as he supposed they were used in ordinary
language, as "legoumena." Galileo, however, did not do that. He was not
interested in defining what people conceive fall to be when they talk about
fall. Ordinary language concerning fall, insofar as it refers to fall,
directed him to the observable phaenomenon. But this was not the
phaenomenon he set and inquired about. The phaenomenon he set and inquired
about was a set of interrelated, relevant variables, which is not at all
what people observe and talk about in ordinary language. By contrast, what
Aris set as the phenomenon of place was what the learned or different
learned say about place, which reflects their (explanatory?) understanding
of place. Owen speaks of  the "misreading [of] the Physics as a confused
and cross-bred attempt at empirical science (p. 119). Perhaps what the
Physics really is exactly that. In other words, my contention is that Aris
is misapplying to the field of physics the method of legoumena that would
perhaps be appropriate in the field of ethics, if at all.
      Galileo deals with a phaenomenon of nature, not with a human
phaenomenon like akrasia. The former is like the phaenomenon thunder
studied by Aristotle. And in the case of thunder Aris does not bring any
legoumena, anymore than when he attributes the fall of bodies to their
natural desire to fall. Like Galileo, though in a very different and rather
arbitrary way, he makes his own legoumenon. Of course, as Nussbaum would
say, if there were no human experience of fall and of thunder there would
be no scientific explanation of either. This does not imply, however, that
there is no relevant difference between a phaenomenon of nature and a human
phaenomenon and that the two kinds are to be set for investigation by
relying on the legoumena of the many and the wise. In fact, ordinary
language as such involves no legoumena, no explanatory conceptions, of
phaenomena of nature like thunder and fall, anymore that it involves a
scientific conception of electricity. In other words, there are no
legoumena of the many concerning phaenomena of nature such as place,
motion, time, space, etc. However they are defined explanatorily it is
irrelevant to the agent who has to deal with them or take them into
account.[This may be wrong. Electricity is an explanatory notion actually
used by us as agents. But it is not explanatory of the word, as Aris did in
the case of place, but of the set phaenomenon, as he did in the case of
thunder].
      Ordinary language deals with the world we encounter as agent-patients
and with our responses to that world. It involves conceptions, but these
conceptions are primarily the conceptions of an agent-patient who
encounters in the world and wants to cope successfully with what he
encounters. The scientist, whose immediate purpose is to understand, to
explain, is guided by ordinary language, without which there would be
nothing to understand or explain. In this sense, our scientific theories
are human and constrained by our human experience, something which Owen
would obviously grant. But this would not force Owen to Grant, as Nussbaum
seems to imply, that the same approach with only minor differences is apt
for both, that Aristotle used only the approach by legoumena and, in any
case--as Nussbaum at least implicitly claims--that the approach by
legoumena is the only legitimate and possible one. And it does not imply
this because, in his search for the understanding which will yield an
explanation, the natural scientist effects an abstraction that neutralizes
his exposure to fall, to thunder, to eclipse. The first step in setting
down a natural phaenomenon is objectifying it, converting it into mere
object of curiosity. Galileo can be substituted by any curious observer of
fall who, if replicating Galileo's moves, will obtain the same data. The
falls observed by Galileo can be substituted by other falls that make a
different noise and involve different amounts of friction. Here we are
moving in an abstract order in which the object is constituted into object
of mere curiosity guided by a question, and the subject into a mere
scientist, that is, a mere curious subject in search of understanding of
this abstract object. But this abstract order has a very different
structure from that of the universe of exposure in which we live.
      [Temporality, spatiality, mutability are not beings but relational
properties of beings. Fall is not just movement but downard movement in
relation to something else and, ultimately, either to a contemplating
subject or to an exposed subject].
      The universe of exposure involves relationships of exposure.
Akrasia is a relationship of this order, a relationship to something
understood as appetendum or avertendum, and responded to as not-appetendum
in the first case or as not avertendum in the second case, because of lack
of strength of character and, therefore, for which the subject is
accountable. Here there are legoumena, practices: accusations of akrasia,
despisements because of akrasia, shames because of akrasia, promises or
deciwsions to correct one's akrasia in the future, etc. The very definition
 of akrasia just given involves a relationship of exposure to a good or a
bad and a consequent self-understanding and understanding of others and by
others as good or bad, that is, as appropriately being responded as good or
bad. From this it seems to follow that the phaenomena of the order of
exposure cannot appropriately be set in the same way as the phaenomena of
nature are, for this way involves a neutralization of what constitutes the
order of exposure. This may be why, or at least one of the reasons why
human matters, or even animal matters of the order of exposure, are not
susceptible of epistéme, another being the consequent lack of repetitive
regularity of these matters).
      Abstracting, in the case of akrasia, from whether or not it is good,
defining it in purely descriptive terms as one would describe the texture
and color of a person's hair, for purposes of understanding and explaining
it would not do, for it would amount to depriving it of what constitutes
it. But why, in the order of the phaenomena of nature, can we abstract
without loss from relations of exposure? Galileo was directed to fall on
inclined planes by ordinary language, and the relevant relationships he
observed-measured were among observable data, and his predictions were
about observable falls which occur in the universe of exposure. In
addition, those predictions allow us to control the world around us, the
world to which we are exposed. Why could we not do the same concerning
akrasia?
      Notice that Galileo had no definition of "fall" or "falling."  He had
no definition of the type attempted by Aris in the Physics about motion, or
even a descriptive definition. And obviously he did not need a definition
at all. His practice as a linguistic agent sufficed him to identify what he
wanted to observe. But he wanted to observe it for purposes of
understanding it. And here the "it" does not refer to this or that fall but
to the phaenomenon he set. But why this phaenomenon was 'universally
representative"? And, first of all, why was it 'the same' as the fall of
exposure?
      It was the same because it was guided by the experiential notion of
fall everybody encounters. It was the law of this fall Galileo was after.
It was not the same, however, in that it did not investigate fall as object
of encounter, as an element of a dramatic episode. Galileo explained, made
understandable, certain constant aspects of the downward movement of
bodies, which is an element of the dramatic world; but Galileo did not say
a word, nor could he have, about dramatic relations, which are always
singular and in a diven dramatic setting.
      By contrast, akrasia is always a feature of encounter, when it
obtains; it is a dramatic failure. Why is there no scientific explanation?
Not because it is not observable, but because it is a relationship of the
dramatic order. But why are there no scientific explanations that deal with
'entities' of the dramatic order?
      Aristotle would contend that there are. According to him the soul is
the explanatory principle of thinking and of acting rationally. But he is
considering man as an objectified object of curiosity, as a thing like
fall. But fall and man are not of the same order. The explanation of my
dramatic action, which is concrete, is my understanding-dramatic
relationship to the world. Aris would rejoin that he is explaining this
possibility of being so related to the world by introducing the soul. I
want to deny this, Why?
      I make a gesture of saluting to somebody by waving my hand to him. It
is a response to somebody and to be received by him as object for response
in a dramatic transaction. The explanation of my gesture is my dramatic
relationship to somebody; that is its principle of intelligibility. I value
you as valuable because you are worthy. Aris would rejoin that chicken
cannot do that, but people can because people have a soul. But this is the
ghost in the machine: a spirit that produces effects in a machine that
produces effects in another machine which transmits it to another spirit.
What is wrong here?
      Man is like fall, an object of encounter. It is as object of
encounter. There is no cause behind for either. Gravity is a relationship
among bodies. Bodies are what relate so. Take away gravity under the right
conditions and you have no bodies left. Deinig the law of gravity is not
defining any cause but a constant relationship among bodies. Bodies do not
have gravity inside. Gravity causes nothing; itis just a constant
relationship among bodies under certain conditions. Similarly, people do
not have souls inside. They are related to other bodies and to other
people. Some of their relationships are voluntary, others not.
The intelligibility of a particular fall is the same as that of any other,
but it is not inside the body; it is the body itself as related to such
other bodies in such particular circumstances. This is why the same stone
in the sun is far heavier than on earth and far lighter in the moon. The
sun does not cause the body to be heavier, as we would tend to say; the sun
and this stone are so related under these circumstances of proximity; that
is all. The same with man. But man is man, not stone, because it enters
into relationships that stones do not enter into. So we have a general
notion of man, as we have a general notion of stone. But precisely because
the relationships are different they have a different intelligibility. In
man some are independent of his will and some are dependent on his will
and, in concrete circusmtances, some are against his will and some
according to his will. Saying, then, that man has a soul is a short formula
for saying that man enters into willful relations, that is, relations
related to projects and to self-invented projects. But the soul does not
produce anything and, therefore, is the explanation for nothing. It is a
mere abstraction related to another abstraction--certain conducts in
themselves isolated from the one who behaves, who enters into those
relationships.
      Objectifying is fixing the object as enriched by its relationships
for purposes of further understanding. What counts is the past sedimented
through experience. Insofar as the relationships are constant, explaining
the relationships allows for foreseeance in future possible encounters. The
relationships, however, are of two different kinds: as object for response
and, therefore, motivating, and as object for curiosity. In ethics we are
concerned with the former, in physics with the latter. In ethics we are
considering how the object (subject) ought to be related and, therefore,
how it ought to make himself in order to enter into such relations. In the
human sciences, how it is actually related. In physics, how it is actually
related (the intelligibility of it) but as object, not as subject of
willful relations. In the case of ethics, the background setting obviously
is the dramatic world. In the case of physics, the background setting is
the adramatic paradigm. What about the human sciences?
      History is one of them. It is about the dramatic world as it was for
past agents and was made for us by them. It is about the singular and
concrete--the irregular and irrepetible. It is about the world in which and
to which they responded, on the basis of what Ricoeur calls "traces".
Irrational man
      Barrett's complain in Irrational Man, Appendix I, finds echoes in me.
It has for a long time. There are two points: the substantialism of the
Aristotelians, and blindness as an ens rationis, as a privatio. The former
is connected with objectification. The latter, more directly, with the
dramatic structure of the world of encounter. Of course, the two are inter-
connected.
      A sound in the night. Do we hear the sound studied in physics? Not
anymore than we see the triangle studied in geometry. Both are abstracted
notions studied in order to come to know their abstract properties. The
relevant data for Galileo were the ones that entered into fixed, constant
relations. He was guided by their repetiteveness to pay attention to them.
In this sense, the data are experiential, that is, come from the world of
experience. But it takes objectification with a particular curiosity to
observe them as the physicist observes them, to find them at least possibly
relevant. Those data are relevant to a physicist who wants to understand.
What we want in life is the realization of our wishes. Our space and time
are hodological, that is, subtended by appetitive or aversive structures of
distance to vulnerable subjects. Desires and aversions become projects of
action to realize the intended desired or averted, depending on the
circumstances, for both projects and their realization are subject to
material conditions. Here knowledge enters.  Projects presuppose a contact
with an encountered on the basis of which the desirable or aversible arises
and arises as possible or impossible, as realizable or unrealizable, as
avoidable or unavoidable, as depending on me or not. Practical knowledge by
trial and error guided perhaps by instinct, education, or past experience.
What follows what and, therefore, what to expect. Also theoretical
knowledge that allows deductive connections.
      That which is encountered is not, as such, object of knowledge but
object for response in view of the desirable or aversible. Responding is
not primarily knowing that something exists but responding to it as such an
existent. It is not true that in order to respond one needs to know;
rather, knowing is a datum abstracted from the response. This, in fact is
Aris's argument about practice belying expressed belief. Response is real
belief from which stated belief is abstracted, but response is not composed
of stated belief plus action.
      Aris's argument deserves attention. What exactly is the argument and
why is it, at least prima facie, persuasive? Let us start with the data as
provided by Nussbaum:
      - "Instead of the sharp Baconian distinction between perception-data
and communal belief, we find in Aristotle, as in his predecessors, a loose
and inclusive notion of 'experience', or the way(s) a human observer sees
or 'takes'the world, using his cognitive faculties (all of which Aris calls
'kritika', 'concerned with making distinctions')." (274)
      - "... Owen convincingly established that not only in the ethical
works, but also in Physics, de Caelo, and other scientific works, Aris's
phainomnena must be understood to be our beliefs and interpretations, often
as revealed in linguistic usage. To set down the phainomena is not to look
for belief-free fact, but to record our usage and the structure of thought
and belief which usage displays. For example, the Physics accounts of place
and time begin not with an attempt to gather 'hard' data, but with
observations about what we say on this subject, designed to give us a
perspicuous view of our current conceptions. By showing us the prevalence
of conceptual and linguistic considerations in the scientific works, Owen
went a long way towards correcting a previously prevalent view, according
to which Aris makes a sharp distinction between 'science' and 'metaphysics'
or Weltanschauun--a view in which the Physics had always figured as a
problematic, or even confused work." (273)
      - "It has often been noted with alarm that the HA, Aris's data-book,
mentions beliefs and stories side by side with records of field-work.
Properly understood, this should not alarm us." (274, note 12).
      - Cf. also Cael. 303a22-3, where Aris criticises a view on the
grounds that it 'does away with many common beliefs (endoxa) and many
perceptual appearances (phaenomena kata ten aisthesin): apparently two
subdivisions of the phaenomena, broadly construed." (274, note 12).
      - "Owen correctly emphasises that Aris is composing these
methodological remarks in the shadow of Parmenides, who repudiated
together, without distinction, both the evidence of sense-perception and
the data of shared language and belief; all this he derides as mere
'convention' or 'habit'. Aristotle, answering him, promises to work within
and to defend a method that is thoroughly committed to the data of human
experience and accepts these as its limits." (275).
      - "But we can elicit from his theoretical remarks and from his
practice a rich account of philosophical procedure and philosophical
limits." (275).
      - See 279-281 concerning the passage from natural classes or
definitions to scientific definitions.
      - See 281, note 20, on the problem of relating this norm [of
scientific definitions?] to Aris's practice in his scientific treatises,
where deductions of this sort are very rarely present. Either Aris was not
ready to claim full episteme, or, as argued in the second essay in the De
motu, the evidence unconvered in actual scientific work led Aris to make
some revisions in his methodological norms, especially as to the autonomy
of the sciences.
      - "In ethics, on the other hand, he takes pains to argue that our
beliefs about practice do not yield the demand for a deductive system" (281-
2). The significant differences between th4e sciences and ethics concern
the degree of consistency required and the nature of the consistency
sought, as well as the amount of vagueness or indeterminacy in the subject-
matter, which has implications for the usefulness and feasibility of
generalizations. These issues discussed in Essay 4 of De Motu.
      - Concerning the principle of contradiction, "all he says is that we
cannot assail the principle; but neither can we demonstrate it in the
demanded way. It is for us the starting point of all discourse, and to get
outside it would be to cease to think and to speak. So in a very important
way Aris does not answer the opponent's challenge. He does not offer him
the exterior, Platonic certainty he wants. And if the opponent does choose
to isolate himself from discourse, even the limited 'elenctic
demonstration' will not succeed." (286).
      - Concerning the rejection of the Eleatic One on the grounds that not
even a lunatic believes in it if we judge from his actions; "Here, too,
Aris stops short of calling Parmenides' conclusion wrong of the world as it
is apart from all conceptualisation. All he says is that no human being who
undertakes to act in the human world... can be seriously holding this view.
Action, even bizarre and abnormal action, commits itself to the existence
of movement and plurality. Aris makes this same point later, extending his
discourse argument for the Principle of Non-Contradiction to cover cases in
which the opponent, though possibly silent with respect to the argument's
verbal demands, reveals his commitment to the principle through his
practices" (See Aris's own words quoted below on p. 287). (286-287).
      This much concerning Nussbaum's interpretation. The following are
instances of Aris making his own argument.
      - Aris criticises philosophers who attend to internal clarity and
consistency, ignoring the return to the phainomena. F. E., the Platonist
theory that physical bodies are generated from triangular surfaces: "What
happens to these people is that in a discussion about the phainomena they
say what is not in conformity with the phainomena. The reason for this is
that they have the wrong notion of the first principles and want to bring
everything into line with some hard-and-fast theories."(277)
      - Of the Eleatic conclusion, the denial that plurality is a genuine
feature of the world, Aris says: "Although these opinions appear to follow
if one looks at the arguments, still to believe them seems next door to
lunacy when one considers practice. For in fact no lunatic stands so far
outside as to suppose that fire and ice are one." They should have regarded
the human strangeness of the conclusion as a sign that something was wrong
with the argument. (277).
      - Aris answers thinkers who create puzzles about perception by
pointing out that our practices reveal a set of standards for arbitrating
disagreements: "It is worthy of amazement if they create a puzzle about
whether magnitudes are of such a size, and colors of such a quality, as
they appear (phainetai) to those at a distance or to those who are near,
and whether they are such as they appear to the healthy or to the sick; and
whether those things are quite heavy which appear so to the weak or to the
strong; and whether those things are true which appear so to the sleeping
or the waking. It is obvious that they do not really think that these are
matters for doubt. At any rate nobody, if, while he is in Lybia, he has
imagined one night that he is in Athens, [wakes up and] heads for the
Odeion. Again, as for the future, as even Plato says, the opinions of the
doctor and the ignorant man are not equally authoritative as to whether
someone is or is not going to be healthy." (278-9).
      - How should we deal with an opponent who challenges the principle of
contradiction and demands a demonstration. He charges the objector with
apaideusia, the education by practice and precept, for not recognizing of
what you should look for a demonstration and of what not. He does not
charge him with stupidity, absurdity, logical error, even wrong-headedness.
How should we deal with the objector? If he will not say anything, stop
worrying about him: "It is comical to look for something to say to someone
who won't say anything. A person like that, insofar as he is like that, is
pretty well like a vegetable." If he says something, he is in fact
believing and making use of the very principle he attacks. For in order to
say something definite he has to be ruling out something else as
incompatible: at very least, the contradictory of what he has asserted."
(284-5). And see notes 26 (on saying just a word), and 27 (on Long and
Irwin).
      - Aris, extending his discourse argument for the principle of non-
contradiction to cover cases in which the opponent, though possibly silent
with respect to the argument's verbal demands, reveals his commitment to
the principle through his practices:  "It is most obvious that nobody
really is in this condition [of believing the denial of the principle of
Non-Contradiction], neither those who make the argument, nor anybody else.
For why does he go to Megara and not stay put, when he thinks he should go?
Why doesn't he go straight out early in the morning and throw himself into
a well or off a precipice, if there chance to be one, but instead obviously
avoids this, as though he does not actually hold that it is not good and
good to fall in? It is clear that he believes one thing better and the
other thing not better." (287) Comment by Nussbaum: "The opponent can
defeat us, then, only be ceasing to act humanly in our world, as well as by
ceasing to speak. He is accepting certain appearances, both perceptions and
common human beliefs--e.g. beliefs about the badnes of early death, about
the danger of being killed if one walks off a precipice, about the fact
that he is a mortal, bodily creature with bones that can be broken and
blood that can be spilled--as having a bearing on his life and actions. He
is not accepting their contradictories as having equal force. He is
allowing the humanity that he shares with us to govern his choice. But this
Aristotleian reply, once again, comes from within human practices. It makes
clear the cost of refusing the principle: immobility as well as silence,
the utter loss of community. It does not seek to ground the principle in
anything firmer than this. But this is firm enough; this is true,
necessary, as firm as anything could be. - Aris does not, however, assert
that there is nothing more to non-contradiction than paideia or our
practices. He would say, I think, that we are not in a position to judge
this; that this claim, like the sceptic's denial of the principle, asks us
to stand outside language and life, and is therefore doomed to
incoherence." (Ibid.)
      - Aris considers Parmenides claim that change and motion are merely
conventional. As in the Metaphysics, he rejects the Eleatic demand that he
demonstrate his basic appearance: "To try to show that nature exists is
comical; for it is obvious that there are many such [i.e. changing] things.
And to show the obvious through the obscure is what someone does who is
unable to distinguish what is self-evident from what is not. It is possible
to be in that state: a man blind from birth might try to give a proof from
premises concerning colours. But it is necessary that the talk of such
people will be mere words, and that they will have no nous about anything."
(288-9). Comment by Nussbaum: "The Eleatic is comical because he does not
succedd in singling out or indicating the unchanging, unidivided One...
Neither he nor anyone else in his community can have had experience of it.
Therefore, Aris would say, he cannot introduce it into discourse;
discourse, even when vague and imprecise, is bounded by the experience of
the group. Therefore, although the Eleatic believes that he is saying
something bold and strange, he is really saying nothing at all. This is why
we can say that his talk is 'mere words' without understanding. (289)

      - Same for the Platonist: he fails to "indicate" or refer to his
cherished entities, his self-subsistent forms of colours, which always
occur in our experience as the properties of some substance. "So goodbye to
the Platonic forms. They are teretismata, and have nothing to do with our
speech." Teretismata are meaningless sounds, dum-de-dum-dums, noninoes. The
platonist is a completely self absorbed individual saying to himself what
neither anyone else nor ultimately he can understand. When the Platonist
speaks of The Good or The White, he is not referring to anything, much less
communicating anything to us... Aris says that Plato's arguments tried to
create a non-relative class of relative terms, "of which we say there is no
all-by-themselves class" (290).
Analysis of Aris's arguments
      First of all, what is Nussbaum point in all this? To show that for
Aris experience is the beginning and the end. But she admits a subdivision
of experience: common beliefs (endoxa) and perceptual appearances. And she
seems to assume that this does not make a fundamental difference as to the
way the phaenomena are to be set, which is precisely the point of
contention. "Here as in all other cases (p. 267). Does Aris follow the same
way for all cases: for thunder, for akrasia, for the happiness pursued by
everybody? Whether or not he does, should he have? THIS IS THE ISSUE
BETWEEN HER AND OWEN WHICH I AM SUPOSED TO DEREEM.
      Secondly, concerning the analysis of Aris's argument(s).
      - Physical bodies generated from triangular surfaces. They believe
it. BELIEF THAT HAS NO PRACTICAL CONSEQUENCES. Yet it is not to be
believed, for it is not rational. Why? Because not grounded on experience
but on pure theory, which shows a misunderstanding of first principles.
      - The Eleatic One. Even a lunatic does not believe that fire and ice
are one (and the same). Would not state that? Would not respond to them as
one and the same?
      - Puzzles about perception. Even they do not doubt, for when they
experience Lybia they do not walk to the Odeion. PRACTICE WITHOUT THEORY.
Are these rational convictions?
      - Pple of contradiction. The practice of stating belies his theory,
for a statement implies the exclusion of the contradictory to be
meaningful. And he does not throw himself into a well: PRACTICE (DEFINITE
EXPECTATIONS WHICH EXCLUDE THE CONTRADICTORY AND BY WHICH ONE ABIDES).
      - Parmenides on changes and motion. NO POSSIBLE PRACTICE HERE? But
the obvious does not need demonstration. His statement is as meaningless as
the talk of a blind about colors. Cannot be introduced into discourse,
discussion because we can have no experience of a world without change and
motion.
      - Platonic forms of 'accidents': same as Parmenides. That good and
color are of something is obvious. Good in itself and color in itself are
meaningless. WOULDN'T GOD BE A TERISMATON?
      In the above we have three theories (Generation, Eleatic One, and
Parmenidian change and motion), then Puzzles about perception that lead to
intellectual doubt, and puzzle from the undemonstrability of the principle
of contradiction that leads to
intellectual doubt.
      There are two kinds of doubt: intellectual and practical.
      Intellectual doubt: arises out of positive reasons for not assenting
(perception), or of grasped lack of sufficient reasons for assenting (pple
of contradiction). The (theoretical) intellect is such that it does not
assent when it grasps that sufficient reason for assenting is lacking.
Reasons for doubting are reasons understood as lack of sufficient reason
for assenting. This is what HUME does. "My practice, you say... But you
mistake the purport of my argument." - Here the object is abstracted,
considered by itself, and only as object of understanding. - This judgment
is theoretical. It sees the object as object of contemplation and mere
understanding, not as object for response.
      Practical doubt: Encountering-perception is such that it motivates to
act. I am hungry and see a champignon as food; I eat it. I am hungry and
see a champignon as poisonous; I abstain from eating it. I am hungry and
see a champignon as doubious, as possibly food, possibly poisonous; I
hesitate. This is practical doubt. Depending on the circumstances, I will
eat it or abstain, acting both in fear and in desire as far as feelings go,
but acting in fear or in desire as far as practical attitudes go.
      Back to Aristotle. Those who raise puzzles about perception do not
really doubt. Certainly not in their practice. They are as little thorn
internally and as decisive as anybody. But what exactly is his argument
concerning this? (1) That he who does not doubt in practice cannot possibly
doubt in theory, that is, intellectually? In other words, even
theoretically they believe what everybody who perceives believes--whatever
that is. (2) That they do doubt in theory, but that their intellectual
doubt is not to be taken seriously, since they themselves do not take it
seriously as shown by their practice? (3) That they do doubt
intellectually, but that their intellectual doubt is itself irrational?
      (3) Perception is "obvious." Not distinguishing the obvious from the
non-obvious is apaideusia. And the obvious or self-evident is
undemonstrable, but does not need demonstration. Anything that clashes with
what most people believe is silly or irrational, however rational it may
appear on the purely intellectual level. Anything that clashes with the
beliefs implied in the linguistic and not purely linguistic practices of
the many is irrational, however rational it may appear on the purely
intellectual level. Because these practices are constitutive of the 'game'
they are fundamental and cannot be given up. Perhaps this is what he meant
by 'they have the wrong idea of the principles.' Could have added,
concerning the principle of contradiction, that to demand a demonstration
is incoherent, since the demonstration would imply, use, and presuppose the
validity of the principle. Could have added, concerning the 'they cannot
indicate the experience'that this is foundational of the linguistic
perceptual 'game', without which there would be nothing to ask questions
about. Perhaps in both cases this is what he meant to say by 'terismata'--
icoherent, meaningless babbling which seems to mean because it uses sounds
that coincide with words but which do not mean anything even to themselves,
much less to others, like "unrelative class of relative terms."
            Aristotle does not directly refute the puzzles about
perception, which would be the way to satisfy the intellect by showing that
its reasons for doubting are pseudo-reasons. But he offers arguments. These
arguments do not refute directly because they do not rest on the
intellectual, Platonic assumption that only that is worthy of assent or can
justifiably call for assent which is grasped as being necessarily true;
that is, that what makes a judgment rational is a reason of that kind, and
anything that does not live up to that standard is irrational and mere
ungrounded opinion. What he offers in response is is, at least in part,
arguments ad hominem from their practic, which show that they do not live
up to their proposed intellectual standard.
      What is the force of these arguments? Certainly not demonstrative.
Yet they have a persuasive force. This force cannot be purely intellectual,
since the arguments are not demonstrative. Yet they give "reasons". This
reasons address the intellect of an agent involved in the practices of an
agent and of a questioner or researcher involved in the practice of
researching. In other words, the intellect of someone who lives, not an
abstract intellect that would be as terismatic as the Platonic forms of the
Good and of color, and which is conceived after the intellect involved in
the mathematical and geometrical practice.
      Still, Aris's fundamental project--though the case of the Ethics,
supposedly concerned with action is more obscure--is primarily theoretical,
concerned with acquiring knowledge. In other words, Aris is an academician,
a contemplator, not an achiever. His point is to understand the world, not
to transform the world by bringing it from the less desirable state at
which it is to a mre desirable one, as Marx would say in the Theses on
Feuerbach. In other words, it is not a project to be realized by changing
the stuffs of nature but by accepting them as they are and ordering them in
an intelligible shape.
      For this reason, in Aris no more than in Plato and perhaps even less--
for Plato had at least the tension between the world of ideas and the world
of appearances--, there is in Aris no room for history or for the pro-
jective, historical perception-imagination of the agent.
      This shows in the arguments we are considering. They are meant to
render the activity of research possible by persuading the prospective
researcher, by creating in the researcher the necessary set of beliefs that
make possible the activity of researching, the very beliefs or 'hopes' that
are implicit in their practice as agents who have to cope with the world.
(Engaged agents ask practical questions; they do not do research). The
activity of coping with the world is different from the activity of
researching and has its own inbuilt set of conditions which are activated
by the encounter itself and which are constitutive of being an agent in the
world. There seems to be some confusion on Aristotle's part between these
two different activities. This is what I want to explore now.
      What is the confusion I suspect? In what is it manifested?
One thing  is  persuading  by  giving  reasons,  another  being  practically
disposed to cope with the world. There is no such a  thing  as  a  practical
syllogism for the one who copes with the world. Assuming that  there  is  is
the "intellectualistic fallacy."
It consists in confusing the activity of  coping,  an  aspect  of  which  is
practical knowledge, with theoretical or research or explanatory knowledge.
      Assuming that there is no innate  knowledge,  as  Aris  also  assumes,
knowledge arises out of experience, at least originally.
Hume is useful here. Coping has to do with what something does.
It is not by seeing water that we can come to know that it quenches  thirst.
Having thirst quenched for  the  first  time  we  'know'  that  it  quenches
thirst. But a child does not know it by way of a predicate  but  by  way  of
disposition to interact  with  water  in  the  mode  of  having  his  thirst
quenched. A syllogism presupposes predicative knowledge, of  which  children
have none. Predicative knowdlege not only involves language,  which  animals
do  not  possess,  but  also  reflective  understanding  on  the  basis   of
objectification. Not only is water objectified as subject of predicates  but
thirst too.  Obviously,  however,  objectified  thirst  is  not  experienced
thirst. Thist is already  an  abstract  name  for  the  condition  of  being
thirsty. Once someone has experienced thirst and has had his thirst  slacked
by what looks like water it will go for what looks like that to  quench  his
thirst (HUME AND SKINNER). Once he has objectified  water  quenching  thirst
and predicated it, he can pass verbal  information  about  it.  And  someone
whose thirst has been quenched by water  can  receive  information  that   a
yellow liquid called beer also quenches thirst. But this  information  is  a
latent disposition to go for beer, which will be activated or actualized  by
the appropriate circumstances of thirst and  presence  of  beer,  all  other
things being equal.
       In  a  linguistic  agent,  there  are   practical   beliefs   (Hume's
expectations) fixed by past success or failure in action. In the  linguistic
agent the beliefs normally take the form of predicative judgments  expressed
in statements. Yet, they are practical beliefs. Thus, someone who  does  not
have  the  practical  belief  that  water  quenches  thirst,  who   in   the
appropriate circumstances and all other things being equal does not  go  for
water when thirsty, does not believe that water quenches thirst.
      Can someone who does  not  have  the  practical  experience  of  water
quenching thirst acquire that  belief?  Yes,  through  information  received
from someone who has the practical  experience,  provided  that  he  can  be
meaningfully referred to "water" and "thirst". This obtains if he  has  some
perceptual experience of water  and  some  personal  experience  of  thirst.
Without that, he will be as a blind person hearing about colors  or  a  deaf
person hearing about sounds. The belief so acquired  is  practical,  though,
in that it creates in the person the same practical disposition  to  respond
to water as that of the person who has the practical experience. Of  course,
to acquire the belief in this manner implies trust in the  person  conveying
the information. But normally one trusts--because  that  is  the  nature  of
linguistic interaction in the case of statements, unless  one  has  positive
reasons to distrust.
        Whence   does   the   confusion   arise   which   constitutes    the
intellectualistic fallacy?
      It seems that there are several possible  sources:  (a)  When  we  ask
questions as researchers we  are  not  in  interactive  encounter.  (b)  The
physical scientist goes on purely perceptual  experience,  not  on  personal
experience.  NOTICE  THAT  THIS  MAY  BE  THE  FUNDAMENTAL  REASON  FOR  THE
DIFFERENCES IN SETTING DOWN THE PHAENOMENA  BETWEEN  THE  PHYSICAL  SCIENCES
AND THE NON-PHYSICAL SCIENCES. (c) Explanatory knowledge  is,  or  at  least
can seem to be, purely theoretical.
      (a) By researcher I mean someone whose main purpose  is  to  know,  to
understand, to explain; someone who has  questions,  who  is  curious  about
something and will by satisfied if his curiosity is  satisfied,  whether  or
not the knowledge acquired is otherwise useful. Naturally, this is  not  the
mood of the person involved in interaction, sollicited by the  urgencies  of
the moment. The agent is pro-jective, oriented toward the  future,  and  his
thinking is eminently practical. The researcher is oriented toward the  past
or  the  always  present  and  his  thinking  is  explanatory,   guided   by
theoretical questions.
      The questions I have in mind are mainly  about  perception  and  about
linguistic meaning, particularly of statements.
            About perception. Greek philosophers were interested  in  truth,
in valid knowledge. This is a reflective interest.  The  involved  agent  is
not primarily interested in truth, that is, in  the  correspondence  between
belief and object, but in success of  his  project,  in  achieving  what  he
wants to  achieve.  Any  reflective  interest  abstracts.  It  transforms  a
particular piece of the world  of  encounter--of  success  or  failure--into
object  of  curiosity,  and  it  defines  or  "sets  down"  the  phaenomenon
according to its line of questioning. Now, the  man  who  is  interested  in
success because he is at stake was "set down" by philosophers  according  to
their line of questioning, which was the validity  of  his  knowledge,  that
is, the correspondence between his beliefs and the object  of  his  beliefs.
In doing this  they  only  refined  what  people  spontaneously  do  in  the
reflective mood, when they themselves are not involved in interaction,  when
they are observers. Except that  people  usually  observe  and  question  as
agents (as linguistic agents, but as agents, not  as  contemplators).  Their
questions   are   practical.   Understanding    practically    amounts    to
reconstructing the relationship between  the  subject  and  his  object  for
response, something the agent does not need to do  because  he  is  in  that
intentional relationship. And, when it is a matter not of  the  subject  but
of the object, the implicit referent is the exposed  subject,  so  that  the
object is questioned as object  of  possible  response,  not  as  object  of
contemplation. In other words, the questions of people do  not  "objectify",
since they preserve the world of exposure. But the Greeks did  not  preserve
this world.
      It seems that the Greeks' mistake is connected with what  Sartre  says
about  the  body.  They  confused  the  object  they  saw  as  contemplative
researchers or inquirers with the agent's  object  for  response,  and  they
confused the inquiring subject with the  exposed  agent.  Why  did  they  do
that?
      Their center of interest was knowledge,  not  successful  action.  For
them, who were not involved in action, the  paradigmatic  case  of  putative
knowing was seeing. In the man they saw seeing they saw  themselves  seeing.
In the object they saw as contemplators they saw the object seen by the  man
they saw. Seeing the object they  believed  that  such  an  object  existed.
Therefore, the man they saw seeing the object believed that such  an  object
existed. Therefore, truth was the correspondence between  what  is  believed
(assented to) and what is the case; falsity the lack of correspondence

      Aris  is  supposedly  talking  about  rational  convictions,  rational
beliefs. For Plato a belief is rational if, and only if, either  the  reason
for the believed is immediately understood as necessary in itself, or  as  a
necessary implication of  something  necessary  in  itself.  In  both  cases
necessity is by virtue of essence, essential necessity. What about Aris?
      His notion  of  episteme  and  his  ideal  science  in  the  Posterior
Analytics are, at their face value, strictly  Platonic.  However,  Aris  has
good reasons to reject the  Platonic  world  of  ideas.  Plato's  ideas  are
terismata, strictly  inconceivable,  beyond  all  possible  experience  that

contingent, the changeable. From this  it  would  seem  to  follow  that  no
knowledge--that  is,  no  strictly   rational,   demonstrable   beliefs--are
