PRACTICAL UNDERSTANDING

I continue here the exploration begun in <\wp\98\98-apr-1>.

I will attempt to explain the practical understanding of which I speak there in contrast to Searle's theoretical understanding of language.   I will take my initial cue from Aristotle's treatment of speculative and practical knowledge in the first Lecture of the Metaphysics as commented by Aquinas. See my analysis of that section in <wp\monas\bible-5>.

Speculative and practical knowledge

Aris distinguishes knowledge (desired or pursued) for its own sake (propter ipsum cognoscere) from knowledge (desired or pursued) for its usefulness for something else, such as propter necessitatem actionis ad utilitatem vitae.

According to him, knowledge is desired by all people tamquam finis, that is, all people spontaneously desire to know for the sake of knowing, out of sheer curiosity or disinterested desire to know. Sometimes, however, people desire to know not for the sake of knowing, or not only or primarily for the sake of knowing, but because of the usefuleness of what is to be known. Animals, by contrast, know and desire to know, or look for knowledge, only because of the biological usefulness of what is known.

* I think that , in this regard, there is no diference between animals and people, for there is no such thing as speculative knowledge. Knowledge is always propsective (therefore not reproductive but instrumental) and intrinsically practical, that is, essentially related to action either immediately or potentially. Both animals and people naturally 'know' and want to know for the sake of acting. In other words, we 'know' or 'encounter practically' or 'encounter zu-handen' as exposed, vulnerable agents. The pure contemplator's knowledge whose correlate is an 'object" by itself, something to be contemplated, is a pure invention of the Greeks, who reflected on knowledge (thereby converting it into object) in order to guarantee its validity and achieve absolute certainty, ideally even in matters of conduct.

Wait a minute! Is it true that all knowledge is prospective and for the sake of action? Wouldn't it be more accurate to say that knowledge in the mode of exposure, implicitly or non-thetically related to the knower as vulnerable agent, is the original and foundational form of 'knowledge,' but that other deirvative forms of knowledge arise from there, such as knowledge for the sake of somebody else's interests or goals, and knowledge from another's point of view which is just comprehension of the Other (as in certain cases of historical biography), and farther down the line reflective, objective knowledge whose purpose is to understand a pehnomenon--be it in geometry, in physics, or even in philosophy?

But, in that case, am I regressing to the idea of knowledge as reproductive image? I don't think so. All knowledge was originally applicative, and tends to remain potentially so. It is in the applicative moment that knowledge functions as knowledge. However, not every agent is exposed to everything either in space or in time. I don't have to avoid all the cars you have to avoid when you are driving where you are driving, and you don't have to avoid now that you are at home all the cars you did have to avoid yesterday when you were driving. Language, with its connotative character and with its tensed-spaced connotation, which implicitly discerns the present of exposed perception from the past and the future, helps us to distinguish what was from what is. And the "traces" (both physical and traditional) that last help us to distinguish the imaginary from the real. But there is no reproductive knowledge, for that would involve us again in the Platonic view that demands

the eternity and a-historicity or unchangeability of all objects, as well as the unicity of the single knowing intellect.

Certainly the child has to be introduced to language orally, because the origin of language is by reference to the self-centered desires and fears or aversions of the child, without which relationships of need there would be no anchorage for language. However, animals have that anchorage and therefore respond to "signs," and yet they never achieve the passage to meaningful language--the foundational 'knowledge' in humans.

Hellen Keller originally knew water as object of desire, the way animals know water. Then she was originally referred by her teacher to water as object of desire in a pre-linguistic way, as a dog is referred to the imminent presence of its master by the master's sign-name. The telegraphed sign W-A-T-E-R signaled to Keller the imminent presence of water-to-be-encountered, and her desire or aversion supplied the 'for-response' to the object. But then, thanks to language, thanks to her being referred-signally-practically to this and to that and to that other particular object of desire or aversion she realized practically that she herself could refer her teacher to those objects. Otherwise said, she realized that those were word-names, by experiencing them used as names by others who referred her to their referents and by herself using them as names to refer others.  

There are two things going on in language: reference (connotation and denotation) and communication. Or, rather, communicative reference. There is the agent referring, the agent referred or spoken to, and the way in which the agent is referred. One is referred by others and refers others. And since the reference is originally practical, perlocutionary, subtended by desire and aversion, the reference is not exhausted by its object, for it is never purely objective. It is also subjective, practical reference of a subject-agent by another subject, exposed agent. Hence, to speak of names in the nominative as the paradigmatic case of linguistic reference, as does S. Langer, may be a mistake. But what else then? The perlocutionary character of linguistic communication, which involves other speech-acts than stating, and which involves stating as one perlocutionary speech-act among others.

Let us apply this to the case of H. Keller, as Langer does. Certainly the first step was signaling with arbitrary signs, telegraphed touching signs. That placed Keller on a par with dogs.

What happened next? Practical realization that diferent signs signaled different objects. Then reflective realization that different signs signaled different objects and signaled them in different perlocutionary ways, because she was being addressed by another. Then reciprocity in her addressing others.

Animals are related to objects only by inclination and aversion. Signs are just signs of imminent presence of desired or feared objects. For animals, then, objects are in no way objectified. People, by contrast, are related to objects not only by inclination but also by others who address them and who, on the basis of needful anchorage, refer them in ways that do not necessarily coincide with their inclination. This is what makes decision possible and necessary. At the same time, since language involves connotation and their needs are sometimes neutralized, people can treat objects as objects for consideration and observation, that is, objectively, as objects for curiosity rather than for concern. Here enters the use of names in the nominative, aks objects of objective and subjective predication.          

There is such a thing as practical knowledge because there is a connection between knowledge-encounter and action geared to satisfy need or desire or even choice. Through experience, that is through their own action guided by natural inclination, and through the imitative observation of other animals of their own species, superior animals--those gifted with locomotion, memoria and phantasia--learn what effects that affect them are obtained through what actions. This is " experimentum ," which involves a sort of generalization, at least through automatic association: from several cases to the general rule. In Hume's formulation, from objects that look similar similar effects are automatically expected.

Compared to people, however, animals "non nisi parum participant" in experimentum, says Aquinas commenting Aristotle. This is a somewhat clumsy way of expressing certain fundamental differences between animals and people that stem from the fact that people are lingiuistic creatures. Which differences?  

(1) The generalization of animals is purely practical. It takes the form of a practical disposition to act in determinate ways in response to determinate objects, thanks to past experience. Following Hume, we may call this disposition a habitual "expectation," which tends to result in efficient action on the animal's part to achieve the goals set to them by nature. Now, we people also develop expectations. This is the " parum " we share in experimentum with animals, clearly observable in young children. As Hume argues, a baby who has been burnt by the flame of a candle once will avoid touching a flame, much as Hellen Keller expected to go out as soon as her hat was put on her. In us, however, thanks to our linguistic ability, what is expected can be objectified and verbalized. In other words, we do not only act in accordance with the rule of thumb; we grasp the rule of thumb itself, what is expected and to-be-expected. Thanks to that the rule can be transmitted verbally to others who may profit from our experience and develop their expectations accordingly, if they trust us. Animals, by contrast, cannot teach their youngsters but by example, and only if instinct leads themir youngsters to to this sort of learning. Because animals lack language, they don't grasp the rule, the abstract quasi universal object ("fire burns and because it burns it is not to be touched"; they know the rule only applicatively, only under the form of expectation and concrete disposition to act in a certain way that is conducive to their natural biological, passively received ends. People, by contrast, know the rule formulatively or objectively, not only in their applying it but in itself, even when they are not applying it and when their knowledge of it leads to no immediate beneficial effects. It is in this formulated, auasi-objective, not immediately applied aspect of experimentum that animals do not share. This is why in experimentum animals "non nisi parum participant." They know general rules only as rules actually regulating their own action through expectation subtended by desire and aversion, only as these rules are inscribed in their bodies thanks to past experience, and only insofar as such rules lead to the achievement of their natural ends. People, by contrast, know the rules both as acquired practical dispositions inscribed in their bodies, and also objectively, formulatively.

Each species of superior animals comes to know-practically only the rules that enhance the achievement of the natural ends of that species. A human being, by contrast, who can know rules objectively, can come to know, out of sheer curiosity, rules--that is, regularities of nature--that do not enhance either his own or his species' biological ends. In the latter case, obviously, the knowledge is not only theoreticalor speculative in that it is not a disposition to act in a certain way, but also in the sense that it is pursued for its own sake, since it has no utility for the knower or the knower's species. Another way of putting it is that animals know only objects for response, that is, as they relate to themselves and their biological interests, and know them only applicatively, whereas people also know objects 'objectively' and independently of any relationship to their biological ends .

This is to say that, in the case of experimentum, as far as people are concerned, the knowledge can be theoretical and practical, for its own sake and for the sake of its utility. And this, precisely, is what Aristotle implies by expounding the difference between experimentum in superior animals and experimentum in people. In people, experimentum is not only practical knowledge (ex multis memoriis unius rei accipit homo experimentum de aliquo, quo experimento potens est ad facile et recte operandum); it also is "cognitio quia" of ratio particularis, formulated or at least formulable grasp of the factual rule o4r regularity.

There is, however, according to Aristotle, another species of knowledge that is purely theoretical: "cognitio propter quid." "Cognitiones singularium magis sunt propriae sensibus quamam alicui alteri cognitioni, cum omnis cognitio singularium a sensu oriatur. Sed talem nullum sensum dicimus sapientiam, scilicet propter hoc quod aliquis sensus cognoscat quia , tamen non propter quid cognoscit... unde experti qui habent cognitionem singularium causam ignorantes , sapientes dici non possunt." Modus autem quo ars fit ex experimento, est idem cum modo praedicto, quo experimentum fit ex memoria. Nam sicut ex multis memoriis fit una experimentalis scientia, ita ex multis experimentis apprehensis fit universalis acceptio de omnibus similibus. Unde plus hoc habet ars quam experimentum: quia experimentum tantum circa singularia versatur, ars autem circa universalia."

This knowledge propter quid or sapientia or ars can be pursued only for its own sake and cannot be known applicatively for, at least supposedly, it has no practical application, since, as we shall see in a moment, it doesn't enhance or improve the application. Such is the knowledge not of the general rule, which is a product of experience, but of the intelligibility of the rule, of the cause-reason for the rule, that is, for the uniformity or regularity of the phenomenon and, consequently, for the gerundive character, for the rule-governed character, of the behavior, if and when the knowledge is to be applied. In this case, for instance, I know not only that aspirin heals headaches, but the reason why. I know not only that an eclipse produces darkness but the cause why.

Asindicated by  these examples, the speculative "knowledge propter quid" of which Aristotle speaks may consist in the grasp of the efficient cause of a phenomenon, which thereby is grasped as "the nature" of the phenomenon, of it may be the grasp of the intelligible a reason (formal cause) that explains demonstratively the phenomenon, which thereby is grasped as "the essence" of the phenomenon. In both cases, though, an intelligent grasp is involved, in that the object is abstracted from existence, reflected upon, and understood in its universality and necessity. This grasp is, therefore, even more than the grasp of the factual general rule, beyond the ken of animals and it involves more than experimentum or experientia.

Because of the intelligible character of what is known in the case of ars, "Habens artem potest disputando obviare his quae contra artem dicuntur, non autem habens experimentum. Illi qui sciunt causam propter quid, scientiores sunt et sapientiores illis qui ignorant causam, sed solum sciunt quia. Experti autem sciunt quia, sed nesciunt propter quid. Artifices vero sciunt causam, et propter quid. Signum scientiae est quod potest docere, quod est scientiam in alio causare. Artifices autem docere possunt, quia cum causas cognoscant, ex eis possunt demonstrare. Demonstratio est syllogismus faciens scire, ut dicitur primo Posteriorum . Experti autem non posssunt docere, non possunt ad scientiam perducere, cum causam ignorent. Et si ea quae experimento cognoscunt aliis tradant, non recipient per modum scientiae, sed per modum opinionis vel credulitatis." *

* This speculative knowledge or sapientia is called  by Aristotle "Ars" or "Scientia," the former being more closely connected with action or production than the  latter.

If scientia is superior to experimentum or experientia in that it grasps the universal and necessary and can therefore demonstrate to others or teach, experientia is superior to scientia in practical terms. In other words, those who know the rule objectively, or even the reason for the rule, but not on the basis of personal experience-practice but on the basis of being taught, are inferior in practice to those who have personal experimentum , that is, who know the rule applicatively rather than objectively. "Unde si aliquis habet rationem artis sine experientia, erit quidem perfectus in hoc quod universale cognoscat: sed quia ignorat singulare cum experimento careat, multoties curando peccabit." Consequently, the superiority of scientia over experientia is only of the speculative, not of the practical order: "Unde praedicta differentia erat in cognoscendo tantum."

N.B. In others of his writings Aris seems to distinguish between ars and scientia. Ars is connected with production through action; scientia with contemplation through intellection of what is universal and necessary.

Prudentia

Another point dealt with by Aris in the  first Lectio of the Metaphysics concerns " prudentia ." Prudentia is defined as "ex praeteritorum memoria de futuris providentia," and "aestimatio de convenientibus prosequendis et fugiendis nocivis."

Definitely, then, insofar as prudentia is knowledge, it is interested and practical knowledge, connected with action.

* I think that prudentia is the only 'knowledge' there is--not as faithful image or reproduction of object but as disposition to respond.

Notice the parallelism in the difference between experimentum in animals and people and in the difference between prudentia in animals and people. This indicates that Aris is speaking about the same in both cases, for animals definitely know rules only applicatively and prospectively. On the other hand, if there is no such a thing as speculative knowledge, how is the difference between the exactness of speculative knowledge, in the case of episteme (universality and necessity) and the lack of exactness in ethical matters to be explained? I think that this question, which refers exclusively to people, is related to the fact that people, unlike animmals, are linguistic creatures.

It seems that experimentum, scienti a, ars ... belong with episteme or exact, that is, universal and necessary, speculative knowledge --Aristotle's  Platonic heritage--whereas prudentia, connected with value and action and goals, belongs with inexact, applicative, practical knowledge --Aristotle's pragmatic naturalism.

It seems, too, that if knowledge consists in resemblance or any other correspondence of that kind, then practical knowledge is not knowledge. If, on the other hand, the relationship between 'subject' and 'object' that obtains in appropriate or adequate response is the right model, then speculative knowledge as

understood by Aristotle under Plato's influence is not knowledge and doesn't exist.

Speculative AND practical knowledge , knowledge for its own sake AND interested knowledge, episteme AND phronesis-wisdom, knolwedge as intentional identity in the case of intuition and as necessary resemblance in the case of inference AND knowledge as disposition to respond, contemplator's AND exposed agent's knowledges-- all these pairs have different and disparate origins . The former pairs originate in Plato , who deals in what is stated and its subjective certainty and objective validity, in order to distinguish knowledge from opinion, and who subordinates action to knowledge. The latter originate in pragmatic Aristotle , who deals in exposed agents and in intentional, goal-directed, responsible action. However, Aristotle is not only unaware of these disparate and incompatible origins. He positively assumes that the two pairs belong under the same genus 'knowledge' understood as reproduction or intentional identity, attempts to reduce or subordinate practical to speculative knowledge as if practical knowledge consisted in resemblance, and ends up considering practical knowledge as a less perfect sort of knowledge, but knowledge nonetheless.

These two pairs and their confusion are reflected in the flotation between the reproductive and the instrumental or perlocutionary views of language.

   Prudentia, however, differs in people and in animals. There is prudence in people "secundum quod ex ratione deliberant quid eos oporteat agere; unde dicitur sexto Ethicorum quod prudentia est recta ratio agibilium." In animals, however, the practical judgment that terminates in action (iudicium de rebus agendis) arises not out of rational deliberation and personal choice but rather automatically, out of natural instinct and experience. "Unde prudentia in aliis animalibus est naturalis aestimatio de convenientibus prosequendis et fugiendis nocivis, sicut agnus sequitur matrem."

As Martha Nussbaum observes, animals do not deliberate because their goals are not chosen by them but are passively received; animals always act according to their strongest inclination in the given situation, that is, their actions pursue or avoid objects as their instinct dictates, because instinct determines their ends and either instinct or past experience determines their means. For instance, captive pigeons who have never observed other pigeons automatically build nests when exposed to pieces of straw. People, by contrast, genuinely deliberate as to their goals, when they are in control of their action, and as to their means, when different courses of action are possible--be it because because different objects of desire or repulsion are involved, and people have a choice as to which end to pursue, or because different ways of achieving their goals are available.

* Animals do not deliberate about goals. They receive their goals passively through heredity, and the goal to be pursued at any moment is determined by natural , that is, inherited or passively received desire or fear, as Nussbaum says. As to the means to achieve their natural goals, superior animals, guided by desire and fear, learn what is to be done through practice, not through invention, and know it only applicatively, not linguistically.

The above refers to responding to the surrounding world with which one is involved in interaction. In the case of people, however, it is not simply a matter of response to environmental stimulating conditions. As Marx argues, people reverse their dependence on the surrounding world, that is, their passive responsiveness (because they have prospective imagination of the possible), through "work," and in this way they begin to call the shots on the environment. People truly invent their surrounding world, their instruments,  and their own actions.

People begin with natural desires too. But being creatures of nurture far more than of nature, they learn from their culture far more than from their nature what objects are desirable or undesirable, and how to act not only in order to pursue or avoid those objects, but also to please and respect other people through their actions. Of course, like the superior animals who enjoy locomotion, memory and phantasia, people also learn through experience, as indicated above. Reversing and complementing Aquinas' expression, it may be said that people "non nisi parum participant" in animal learning.  

CONNECTION WITH DESIRE THAT RESULTS IN KNOWLEDGE-ACTION,

AND DELIBERATIVE PRUDENCE.

What I have in mind is Aris's distinction, as expounded by Nussbaum, beteween knowledge that results in action and knowledge that doesn't, which could be another way of distinguishing speculative from practical knowledge.

* Perhaps what creates the illusion of speculative knowledge in the case of people is that people, being linguistic--but performatively so, not contemplatively, because language is performative [WITH THE EXCEPTION OF LANGUAGE ABOUT LANGUAGE, WHICH DOESN'T FUNCTION AS LANGUAGE]--can choose their conduct rather than being merely prompted by their environment. In  the presence of meat the hungry dog will necessarily try to eat meat. Hungry people will have a strong inclination to eat the meat, but not all of them will necessarily eat the meat.

In De motu animalium Aristotle remarks that not all knowledge terminates in action, since not all knowledge is connected with desire. This seems to suggest that another way of distinguishing speculative from practical knowledge is whether it is meant to terminate in assent or in action. Desire, however, is not self-subsistent and independent of object but intentionally constitutive or co-constitutive of 'object' . For instance, in the case of the British captain, the grasp of the fire as fugiendum rather than as extinguendum was not an objective fact but the result of a decision. A more 'heroic' captain could have chosen to die fighting the fire. And a captain with more time and lucidity could have chosen, as argued by the judges of the captain, to fight the fire and save the ship. There is, however--to employ Sartre's language--a coefficient of adversity or favorableness in things. The captain had other choices, but, if he 'knew', that is, if he was really 'prudent,' he couldn't possibly, for all his desire, have chosen as a goal to save his ship if the fire was too strong for the ship to be saved with the available means. This passivity to things is the 'objective' element in the real by contrast to the imaginary and in the formation of projects by contrast to mere dreams.  

Now, what does Aris have in mind when he says that not all knowledge terminates in action? He means that some knowledge terminates in mere knowledge-assent, whereas some other knowledge terminates in action. The former is speculative, the latter practical. When the former involves universality and necessity, it is episteme . When not, then, for Plato, it is not knowledge at all, whereas for the more pragmatic Aristotle it still is knowledge rather than belief-opinion, though merely factual knowledge, an intermediate between opinion-belief and essential or causal knowledge or episteme . Such, in Aristotle's view, is the knowledge of the sensible, of the particular, of the temporal, of the existent.

Nussbaum's example of practical knowledge, of knwoledge that through desire results in action. If I know that John is in Corinth and I want to see John (assuming that there is no other means of seeing him), then I will necessarily go to Corinth. The knowledge of John being in Corinth together with my desire to see him 'necessarily' results in my going to Corinth.

Notice how the passivity to things intervenes here. My desire to see John can only become an end if there are means available. Otherwise it is mere dream, not project. In other words, we choose our ends under constraints that do not depend on us, though our capacity to 'work" in Marx's sense can change the situation, within limits, and make possible what for the present is impossible.

The necessity involved here is said by Nussbaum to be conceptual. If someone understands through his or her experience as an agent what desiring something as a goal is , which involves grasping that it is connected with using the available means or making the means available or inventing the means, he or she will know (that is, assent necessarily to the conclusion) that I will go to Corinth to see John. And if this person wants more than anything else to see me and has the means, this person, too, will necessarily go to Corinth to see me. (Except, of course, that this person cannot know with absolutely compelling evidence that I want to see John more than anything else).

There seems to be some confusion on Nussbaum's part between the knowledge of the contemporary contemplator-witness or of the not contemporary historian and that of the agent. The agent doesn't know anything in the sense of assenting to any proposition. The agent deliberates and chooses and, if successful , brings about the future desired state of affairs. Only in reflective retro -spect on the past as his own witness will he or she assent to propositions, with factual necessity. The contemporary witness makes applicative judgments on my surmised, interpreted conduct and, if his goal is to catch me, will himself go to Corinth and likely catch me there. But if he has no desires-goals in relation to me, then his grasp of my being in Corinth is applicative by default, in that his decision won't be to go to Corinth. Only the historian makes statements about the past to which others are expected to assent, and the historian's knowledge, being particular and factual, is never knowledge in the sense of episteme . *

* Remember how strenuously Kierkegaard argued this point in order to show that faith is not a species of knowledge, of belief-that, whjich has to do with assent to propositions. And de Lubac rightly stresses that faith is prospective.

Nussbaum's confusion originates in Aristotle's own, who assimilates the practical to the speculative, both under the genus knowledge as image of the real or intentional adequation to the real. Hence, because he sees the domain of the practical as a domain of knowledge and sees knowledge as necessary assent prompted by the grasped necessity of the object as judged, he assimilates the necesity of the practical to the necessity of the speculative, as if the agent's action in pursuance of a goal followed the choice of the goal with a necessity comparable to that with which assent to the conclusion follows the understanding of the premises.

That there is no necessary, causal connection between the decision and the action decided was made clear by Sartre in his analysis of anguish, for the anguish manifested in the "But will I?" would be impossible if the action necessarily, with causal necessity, followed the decision. Moreover, Aristotle himself showed that there is no such necessity in his analysis of "AKRATIA" (when the temptation of a conflicting desire wins the day and one acts regretfully against one's decision--video meliora, proboque; detriora sequor), and when one acts 'voluntarily' but under

coercion, and when he grants that luck is necessary for a virtuous-happy life. In his own way, Kant affirmed the same when he said that the success of the action, that is, the achievement of the goal intended, is not essential for virtue (which of course Kant didn't identify with happiness), for success doesn't depend on the agent alone, and consequently that good will (not merely desiring but actually doing whatever is within the agent's power to achieve the goal) is sufficient.

Nussbaum's mistake concerning 'practical necessity" consists in attributing to the concrete, temporally and spatially situated agent who makes a decision the characteristics of a decision or choice of a goal considered by itself or abstractly. Nussbaum rightly says that it is of the concept of choosing a goal to pursue it. But the concrete agent who chooses a goal under concrete circumstances may not achieve it and may not even pursue it to the bitter end, either because of a change of mind, or because of akratia , or because of bad luck that changes the circumstances. In  short, action is always contingent both because it is 'free,' that is, because it depends on the decision of a weak agent, and because its being carried out also depends on the circumstances, which do not depend on the agent. And if action is always contingent, which is the opposite of necessary, there is no way of knowing it with certainty before it takes place, that is, of assenting to it as necessary.

God supposedly knows the future by willing it, for his will necessarily brings about the correspondence between the known as projected and the realized project. God doesn't depend on circumstances for the realization of his projects, for the circumstances themselves depend on him. God's action involves no coefficient of adversity, no passivity to unwilled conditions, for He himself determines the condtions by willing them. By contrast, the human agent is, at best, prudent, with all the contingency of the future rather than the necessity and exactness of episteme . Even in the domain  of scientific technology, when the conceptual 'axiom' abstracted from past experience of observed regularity is actually applied , the result is never sure. Hence the necessity of redundant systems (for instance in planes, so that if one system fails another takes over). Considered abstractly, the technology 'dictated' by the 'axiom' may be sure; but it cannot be sure when applied.

The same in matters of behavior. Instinct and past experience succeed only most of the time. The same in matters of chosen conduct, and also in the case of production by even the most skilled agents, be they physicians or musicians. The same in the case of the ethical agent, be he Christian or atheist (for Christian and atheistic prudence may be very different in certain significant respects, as shown by the differences in their respective catalogues of virtues). This is the "inexactness" in matters of action, particularly of ethical action, acknowledged by Aristotle. What Aristotle failed to acknowledge is that there is no "exact" knowledge, that is, no speculative episteme , for all knowledge has its origin in human practice and, what is more important, is for the sake of practice, intrinsically performative. Abstract knowledge is exact, but it is purely conceptual, conceptual 'cum fundamento in re,' as the scholastics would say. But, as such, it is not a corresponding image of the real, for the real is particular and contingent-temporal, and anyway the function of 'knowledge' is not to portray what is the case.           

Perception in the ordinary mode of circumspection and care, is, as Sartre realizes, instrumental. The agent's space is instrumental, "hodological." I perceive what is in imminent effective presence in time and space in relation to me or to us, that to which I or we are vulnerable and which is exposed to my or our action. When my needs and desires seem to be taken care of, as is the case with Descartes in his meditation by the chimney, I don't genuinely perceive anymore, for I don't perceive objects of exposure, and desires-goals are excluded from consideration.
