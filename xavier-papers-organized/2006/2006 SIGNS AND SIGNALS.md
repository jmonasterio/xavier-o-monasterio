Tacit Knowledge

In The Tacit Dimension, Michael Polanyi introduced the notion of “tacit knowledge,” by which he became known, to refer to those cases in which a speaker “knows more than he can verbalize.” He exemplified this situation with the experiment conducted by psychologists Lazarus and McCleary in 1949.

“These authors presented a person with a large number of nonsense syllables, and after showing certain of the syllables, they administered an electric shock. Presently the person showed symptoms of anticipating the shock at the sight of ‘shock syllables’; yet, on questioning, he could not identify them. He had come to know when to expect a shock, but he could not tell what made him expect it. He had acquired a knowledge similar to that which we have when we know a person by signs we cannot tell.” ^(F2)

For his part, John Searle would say that  the very notion of tacit knowledge (from now on TK), as characterized by Polanyi, is nonsensical. Indeed, it seems to amount to an unconscious knowledge, for the subject who supposedly knows is not aware of what he knows, since he cannot say even to himself what he knows . Now, an unconscious knowledge is an oxymoron. Why? Because the very notion of knowledge, or at least the ordinary notion of knowledge that common sense seems to impose on us, implies on the subject’s part the awareness of what he knows. He knows what he knows thanks to the awareness he has of what he knows, so that he would not know what he knows without that awareness. And that awareness is expressed in enunciative language.

As an example of this ordinary notion of knowledge, let us consider the knowledge of which Searle spoke in the preceding chapter, since he used the word ‘knowledge’ in its ordinary meaning. According to Searle, in linguistic communication the hearer who understands what the speaker says thereby acquires the knowledge  –that is, becomes aware– of what the speaker intends to communicate to him. And it would be inconceivable that the hearer knew what the speaker intends to communicate if the hearer had no awareness at all of what he knew. In other words, what a subject knows becomes object known to him by the very fact of his becoming aware of it. Hence no knowledge of object without awareness of what is known, because knowledge implies and demands the awareness of the object known. Thus, when Polanyi defines TK as a knowledge in which the subject cannot say what he supposedly knows , we have to conclude

– Searle would argue– that TK is a knowledge in which the subject is not aware of what he knows, which is a contradiction in terms.  

For his part, Polanyi would reply to this objection to what he calls “tacit knowledge” by referring us to the undeniable facts of the experiment he described: the subject of the experiment cannot say which are the shock-producing syllables because he does not know them, that is, is not aware of them. Yet the subject’s behavior in reaction to those syllables shows that he knows them in a certain way which does not involve verbalization –and what is involved in this certain way of knowing is what Polanyi calls TK.

Polanyi calls it “tacit knowledge” not only because what is known is not formulated or verbalized, but because, contrary to common belief, linguistic formulation of what is known plays no essential role in knowledge nor in the communication of knowledge. According to Searle, l Linguistic formulation expresses what is understood by the speaker and is meant to terminate in understanding of the same on the addressee’s  part, whereas TK is meant to terminate in action. Polanyi maintains that, contrary to what Searle assumes, the knowing process does not terminate in  representational awareness of object, which  supposedly is enunciated linguistically either internally or externally. Rather, the knowing process terminates in action without the (necessary) mediation of predicative or enunciative verbalization.

In this process, through INTERIORIZATION, the so-called ‘object’ plays the role of instrument from which one acts, as is the case with maps, not of representation. Interiorization is practical in the sense that it is achieved and becomes fixed  or habitual through  practice of the game of baseball, not through verbal formulation of the rules . A child learns to play baseball, not by formulating the rules repeatedly (for he doesn’t  know them), but by ‘following’ them, that is, by imitating again and again in his own practice of the game the practice of those who play the game competently until following the rules becomes habitual. (Isn’t this the way Pascal thought that children of Christian parents learn their faith, by praying with their parents?) ^(F3). Later, through reflection, the adult may be able to formulate the rules of the game. However, neither is this formulation necessary for the correct performance by the child, nor is the formulation equivalent to the performance.

Polanyi’s alternative conception of knowledge

Searle assumes that the consciousness of object in which knowledge is ordinarily deemed to consist is an awareness of the object’s characteristics, an awareness that can be expressed linguistically in enunciative sentences. The speaker’s awareness of object is, indeed,  what is enunciated when verbalization occurs. Polanyi objects that the subject’s knowledge, in the case of TK, is not of this type, for it does not consist in a consciousness of the characteristics of the object, or in the verbal enunciation of those characteristics. What occurs in the subject of the experiment at the sight of the shock-producing syllables is not an awareness of the object’s characteristics, for he does not know the syllables. It is, rather, a blind fear of certain syllables that prompts or causes the subject to brace himself for the shock as if to protect himself from it. Now, the subject’s bracing himself  is not a verbal enunciation, not even a silent one; it is a non-linguistic conduct or behavior motivated by the affection of fear – a conduct whose reason is totally obscure to the subject, since he doesn’t know which syllables produce shocks.  

Thus, the subject of the experiment makes no linguistic enunciation. Rather, he behaves in a certain way when the shock-producing syllables appear on the screen, and this behavior is observed by those who conduct the experiment. Actually, the observed behavior of the subject is precisely the basis on which Polanyi grounds his claim that  the subject ‘knows’ in one way or another, and without that behavioral expression at the sight of the shock-producing syllables and not at the sight of the other syllables Polanyi would have no basis for his claim of tacit knowledge. Now, this connection of what Polanyi calls TK with action, in the absence of verbalization of the subject’s awareness –which is why the knowledge is said to be tacit rather than explicit –  is an important clue. Indeed , it is a first indication that knowledge may consist in action of a certain kind rather than in  consciousness of the object’s characteristics enunciated in verbalization, as it has been traditionally assumed. In which case Polanyi’s notion of tacit knowledge far from being as oxymoronic as it initially appeared, may even open a new epistemic avenue. Let us, therefore, review in detail Polanyi’s views on TK, with special attention to the role played by action instead of enunciation of the characteristics of the object of which the subject is aware.

The contrast between Searle’s and Polanyi’s respective conceptions of the way in which language functions in terms of  the place action occupies therein will prove enlightening.

Knowledge and action

In distinguishing the illocutionary from the perlocutionary effects, Searle attributes conceptual primacy to the illocutionary effect, which consists in knowledge, over the perlocutionary effects, which for the most part consist in actions. The underlying reason for this unique primacy attributed by Searle to the illocutionary  speech act over all the others is that the illocutionary effect consists in knowledge, and knowledge of what the speaker means is, in Searle’s opinion, the conceptually necessary condition for the success of all the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker; By contrast, the success of the illocutionary intentions of a speaker does not depend on the success of any of the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker. To put Searle’s point concretely, a speaker cannot say anything without intending to convey to the hearer the knowledge-understanding of what he means, but he can say something without producing or intending to produce any other effect than this knowledge. Correlatively, the hearer cannot fulfill the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker without knowing those intentions, that  is, without the illocutionary effect obtaining, but the hearer can know the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker, thereby insuring the success of the communicative act, without necessarily fulfilling the speaker’s perlocutionary intentions. Still more concretely: if I do not know what you mean when you ask me to lend you  my pen, I cannot lend you my pen, but I can know that you ask me for my pen, and not lend it to you. Moreover, you can produce in me an illocutionary effect without intending any perlocutionary effect. For instance, in Searle’s interpretation of saluting,  when you salute me, all you intend  to produce in me is the knowledge that you mean to salute me, and the communicative exchange succeeds if only I gain that knowledge. So, according to Searle, knowledge is independent from action, for it may obtain in the absence of any action,  but action is dependent on knowledge.

Polanyi, for his part, construes the relationship between knowledge and action in a totally different way. He does not ignore that knowledge involves a relationship to action; on the contrary, he sees action as an intrinsic component of knowledge. Indeed,  he identifies tacit knowledge with disposition to act and with the actual appropriate action when the right opportunity arises. Given this intrinsic relationship of knowledge to action, Polanyi would necessarily deny that knowledge is independent from action, as Searle claims. In other words, for Polanyi, the knowledge involved in the oral communicative exchange is ESSENTIALLY practical, that is, is meant to terminate in action, not in a purely theoretical awareness of a fact, as it is for Searle. For example, knowing an order received does not consist, according to Polanyi, in becoming aware of the order as an objective fact, but in the practical disposition to obey the order received, and in actually obeying it at the appropriate time. Similarly, according to Polanyi, knowing a promise received does not consist in becoming  aware of that fact, as one becomes aware of a color when one sees it in the course of an experiment in a laboratory, but in actually bracing oneself for the fulfillment of the received promise at the appropriate time. Thus, if you promise to repay me the $100.00 you owe me when I come to pick them up at your office at 3.00 P.M., the appropriate understanding of your promise does not consist in my registering that fact and then doing nothing, as Searle contends,  but in expecting the fulfillment of your promise and in the action of actually coming to your office at 3.00 P.M. to collect my money.

Knowledge and action in the case of animals

It will be useful to realize that the Polanyian notion of knowledge as meant to terminate in action rather than in consciousness of an object’s characteristics and/or its linguistic expression is not new at all. Spontaneously, that is, when free from epistemological prejudices, most of us believe that the members of at least the higher species of mammals, say, dogs, possess the ability to know. At the same time, most of us believe that dogs are non-linguistic agents, that is, that they do not posses the linguistic ability we possess, for they do not and cannot express in words what they know. But this shows that we are implicitly assuming that knowing is not identical with the consciousness of an object’s characteristics or with the consequent linguistic enunciation of what one knows or is aware of.

On what basis, then, if not linguistic expression, do we attribute to dogs the ability to know? On the basis of their observed actions. Certain of their actions are, we say,”intentional.” That is, they  reveal an agent’s purpose , in that the dog’s agency is not uniform but adapted by the dog to different circumstances for the sake of achieving a desired goal that satisfies a natural need of the dog. For instance, a dog may change the way it follows to reach its food, depending on the place in which it finds itself at the time it wants to eat. By contrast, a marble’s fall down an inclined plane is a uniform action, not an adaptive purposeful  action, because it is caused by the environmental circumstances (hence its uniformity), and thus is not intended by the marble for the sake of the satisfaction of a need and, therefore, not modified accordingly by the marble . For that very reason, we have no temptation of attributing knowledge to marbles: their action is not intentional, not for the sake of a goal intended-desired  by the marble, and the uniformity of their action shows no ability on the marble’s part to adapt the action to the circumstances in view of the realization of a goal. In a dogs’ actions, by contrast, we grasp the intention of a goal and, with their increased experience, an increasing ability on their part to adapt their actions to the circumstances and thereby make their actions more efficient for the realization of their goals. Hence we conclude that dogs know.

This analysis may be refined, but as it is it suffices for my purpose. For my immediate purpose is simply to show that knowledge is not necessarily to be conceived, as Searle among many others conceives it, as an awareness of object that mirrors or reproduces in a factual way the object’s characteristics. Rather, my analysis of what – without any epistemological preconceptions – we recognize as knowledge shows that what we identify as knowledge, at least in the case of animals, involves intentional actions for the sake of desired goals. It shows,

furthermore,  that verbalization of what is known is not a necessary component of knowledge, for animals are incapable of verbalization. In short, knowledge is not identified, at least in the case of animals, with a factual awareness of the characteristics of its object, as implied by the ordinary,  commonly accepted notion of knowledge shared by Searle, but with an agent’s ability to achieve a desired goal through intentional or purposeful action *,  that is, through action adapted by the agent to the realization of the desired goal.

* Intentional action demands the intention of a goal. However, the goal needs not  be the object of an explicitly formulated project.

But may not animals know enunciatively, though silently? In other words, may they not possess an internal or cerebral linguistic ability, and only lack the vocal organs to express externally in words that of which they are conscious and verbalize internally? Or are we , rather, implying that animals know in a non-enunciative, tacit way? The correct answer is the latter. And the difference between the two answers shows in the different relationship in which knowledge stands to action in each of the two conceptions, which implies two different notions of knowledge irreducible to each other. In one of them –the one I reject, together with Polanyi, and attribute to Searle– knowledge is conceptually or intrinsically independent from action. In other words, knowledge can obtain in  the absence of any action (remember that, according to Searle, the illocutionary effect is the only essential effect, the only effect that cannot fail to obtain if the communication succeeds). Action, however, is dependent on knowledge, for representational knowledge of object is deemed to be a necessary condition for appropriate action. In the second version – the one I (together with Polanyi) consider correct–   knowledge is not representational of object but instrumental for the realization of a desired goal, for we do not perceive “phenomena” but are involved in events .  Knowledge is therefore intrinsically or “conceptually” ^(F4) related to action in that it is meant to terminate in action, and will actually terminate in action unless prevented by circumstances independent of the agent.  

Knowledge as awareness of object

The notion of knowledge as awareness of its object’s characteristics has its roots in Plato and Aristotle and was adopted as canonical by the classical epistemological tradition. Searle’s philosophy of language reflects his own allegiance to that tradition. But, as we shall now see,  precisely therein lies the radical difference between Searle and Polanyi.  For the latter parts way with that tradition, since he sees knowledge as “ tacit. ” This means that knowledge does not consist in possessing or gaining an implicit or explicit awareness of its object’s characteristics, actually expressed or at least susceptible of being expressed in enunciative language. Rather than consisting in an awareness of an object’s characteristics and in a consequent verbal enunciation of the object’s characteristics,  knowledge functions as a sign FOR action, as the “from which” we act.

Another way of expressing the difference between Polanyi’s and Searle’s conceptions of knowledge is that the former thinks that knowledge functions as a SIGNAL FOR ACTION, whereas Searle thinks that it functions as a (verbal) representation of its object’s characteristics or of that which is signified . ^(F5) ‘nature.’ ^(F6)

Knowledge and desire

Having distinguished the common or traditional conception of knowledge, shared by Searle, from  Polanyi’s own, I should now make clear that the two conceptions are not only different but are mutually incompatible. In other words, the two cannot be correct. Why not? If for no other reason, because of the essential role desire-affection plays in tacit knowledge, whereas desire is positively excluded in the traditional conception of knowledge as representation, for desire is deemed to militate against the ‘objectivity’ of the cognition, that is, the fidelity of the representation to the original. The reason is that the consciousness of  object, in which knowledge supposedly consists, as well as its linguistic expression, have to represent the object factually or ‘objectively’– that is, without addition or subtraction. Not for nothing, in his close commentary on Aristotle’s treatise on linguistic interpretation, did Aquinas contrast what Aristotle sees as the genuinely enunciative speech of the scientist, which is dictated exclusively by the object and leads to knowledge of the truth, with the non-enunciative speech of the rhetorician that appeals to the desires of the hearer and, therefore, distorts the object to be known. Aquinas’s passage is such a succinct formulation of the disturbing function  desire plays in matters of cognition that it deserves to be quoted in full:

“Then Aristotle says, Let us therefore consider enunciative speech, etc. Here he points out that only enunciative speech is to be treated; the other four species [to wit, deprecative, imperative, interrogative, and vocative] must be omitted as far as the present intention is concerned, because their investigation belongs rather to the sciences of rhetoric or poetics. Enunciative speech belongs to the present consideration and for the following reason: this book is ordered directly to demonstrative science, in which the mind of man is led by an act of reasoning to assent to truth from those things that are proper to the thing; to this end the demonstrator uses only enunciative speech, which signifies things according as truth about them is in the mind. The rhetorician and the poet, on the other hand, induce assent to what they intend not only through what is proper to the thing but also through the dispositions of the hearer. Hence, rhetoricians and poets for the most part strive to move their auditors by arousing certain passions in them, as the Philosopher says in the Rhetorica. ^(F7)

Notice the contrast between the mind being led to knowledge exclusively by the observed object (“those things that are proper to the thing” independently of the dispositions or desires of the hearer), which Aristotle sees as the mark of genuinely enunciative-cognitive speech, on the one hand, and the mind being led to knowledge by the desires or affections of the knower (“through the dispositions of the hearer”). As long as knowledge is conceived as a factual replication of the object by the representational consciousness of it the subject has and/or by the linguistic expression by the subject himself  of what is known to him, then desire can only be seen as a disturbing element in the cognitive process. When, however, as is the case with TK, the point of cognition is not to re-present the object but to function as an instrument for the agent’s  conduct in order to achieve a desired end, then the desire of the end to be realized through action becomes an essential component of cognition. ^(F8)

Knowledge and linguistic enunciation

IN HIS PRINCIPLE OF EXPRESSIBILITY, THAT “WHATEVER CAN BE MEANT CAN BE SAID,” SEARLE DEMANDS EXPLICIT ENUNCIATION OF WHAT IS MEANT, BECAUSE HE ASSUMES THAT ENUNCIATIVE LANGUAGE SAYS OR DESCRIBES OR REPRESENTS LINGUISTICALLY WHAT IS MEANT. FURTHERMORE,  AS WE ALREADY KNOW, HE, SEES ACTION AS DEPENDENT ON SAYING. POLANYI, BY CONTRAST, INSISTS THAT KNOWLEDGE IS TACIT BECAUSE ITS FUNCTION IS NOT TO SAY OR ENUNCIATE WHAT IS THE CASE BUT TO TERMINATE IN ACTION BY BEING A SIGNAL FOR ACTION ON THE PART OF A DESIROUS AGENT .

It will be useful to preface my examination of Polanyi’s views on TK by remarking that, unlike Searle, Polanyi never wrote a treatise on language, nor was he primarily interested in language. Actually, his central idea is that knowledge is tacit and functions as a sign FOR, implies not only that knowledge does not consist in the linguistic enunciation of its object’s characteristics, but that linguistic enunciation of the object’s characteristics is not even required . Indeed, Polanyi’s shift FROM consciousness of the object’s characteristics and its consequent linguistic enunciation TO action in the absence of linguistic enunciation implies a different conception both of the way in which language functions and of its role or lack thereof in cognition.

TK is intrinsically connected with action, not with enunciation . Herein lies a radical difference between the ordinary notion of knowledge, shared by Searle, and Polanyi’s own notion , represented by TK. The ordinary notion of knowledge involves the consciousness of object as purely theoretical vision of its object, with no direct relationship to action.

The instrumental role of TK by contrast to the theoretical vision or revelation of object in which the ordinary notion of knowledge consists may be best illustrated by means of a comparison to the way in which eyeglasses function. When the eyeglasses function as such – that is, when they are worn by someone, not when they lie on a table and are looked at– the person who wears the eyeglasses does not see the eyeglasses, that is, has no awareness of them as object of knowledge, as is the case when they lie on a table and are looked at. In other words, when the eyeglasses are worn, they do not function as object known but as an instrument. As an  instrument for what? Polanyi answers: as an instrument for action , that is, as an instrument that guides the agent’s action in the achievement of his purpose. Such is the role played by TK, according to Polanyi, and therein lies the radical difference between our ordinary notion of knowledge and TK. However, in order to fully grasp this difference we have to follow in some detail Polanyi’s analysis of TK in which the knowing subject of the classical epistemological tradition is replaced by the agent .  

In his initial analysis of the notion of TK, in The tacit Dimension ,  referring to the experiment with the shock-producing syllables,  Polanyi’s emphasis was on the fact that the subject knows more than he can say . Let us remark in this connection, in the first place, that in affirming that the subject knows more than he can say Polanyi was by the same already taking his distances from the epistemological tradition that equates knowledge with the awareness of its object’s characteristics and with  the consequent verbal enunciation of those characteristics, for in that tradition the awareness of the object’s characteristics is equated with the ability to enunciate what is known as to be understood . Secondly,  Polanyi shifts the emphasis from verbal enunciation of what is known to action. Indeed, given that the subject knows more than he can express in words, his knowledge is not manifested  in the verbalization of what is known by the subject, but in the subject’s actions. This is why, as we shall see, all of Polanyi’s examples of tacit knowledge refer to an agent actually engaged in action to achieve a desired goal, not to a contemplating consciousness that expresses its knowledge through enunciations of the characteristics of the objects contemplated. Grasping this shift from the consciousness of the epistemic subject of classical epistemology to the acting agent  is fundamental for understanding Polanyi’s conception of knowledge. Knowledge, for Polanyi, is the deed of an agent exposed to the action of the surrounding world, not the deed of a  contemplating consciousness invulnerable to the surrounding world. Unfortunately, this displacement of the epistemic subject of the classical epistemological tradition by the agent exposed to the action of the surrounding world is insufficiently underscored, and perhaps even insufficiently grasped, by Polanyi himself. It will therefore by my task to bring this all-important point to the fore.

From awareness OF object to agency

Let us begin with the example Polanyi employs to introduce the notion of tacit knowledge. The subject in the experiment with the syllables knows tacitly which syllables produce shocks. His knowledge is tacit because it does not manifest itself  in words that express his awareness of those syllables. Indeed, the subject cannot say even to himself , much less to others,  which are those syllables; rather, his knowledge of the shock-producing syllables manifests itself in his behavior of bracing himself for a shock when those syllables appear on the screen. Thus, in the sense of being aware of the characteristics of the shock-producing syllables and of being able to say which are the shock-producing syllables, the subject of the experiment does not know, for both the awareness of object and the linguistic enunciation of the object characteristics, in which knowledge is ordinarily supposed to consist, are absent in this case. Yet, Polanyi claims, the subject of the experiment knows the syllables, not in the sense of being aware of the observable characteristics of the syllables, but in the sense of acting in response to those syllables , first by bracing himself for the shock to come when those syllables appear on the screen, and then, when possible by acting in such a way as to avoid the shock INTENTIONAL OR PURPOSEFUL Action, therefore, rather than linguistic enunciation, is the way in which knowledge manifests itself .

Signs-OF and Signs-FOR

To grasp the difference between knowledge as verbal enunciation (be it internal or external) of the characteristics of an object known, on the one hand, and, on the other hand, knowledge as instrument for action, let us reflect on what goes on in our everyday experience with  SIGNS, which themselves function as instruments for action, as we shall now see. As a concrete example, consider our experience with traffic lights . We think of red and green lights as signs. And we are not wrong, for they function as signs. Where we go wrong is in thinking that signs are primarily signs OF something  to be known-understood, which can be verbally enunciated, when in fact signs do not function as signs OF something to be known, but as instruments FOR ACTION. Thus, red, in the case of a traffic light, is not necessarily a sign OF the fact that a car is coming in the transversal street –something that can be enunciated. Rather, it is  a sign FOR PERFORMING the action of stopping. Similarly, green is a sign FOR PERFORMING the action of moving ahead. And the same is the case with all signs. An arrow on a road  is a sign FOR performing the action of moving in the direction pointed at by the arrow. Consequently, the correct comprehension of signs is not to be manifested in understanding what is said nor linguistic enunciations of what is signified, for there is nothing signified as to be known or understood. The correct comprehension of a sign is rather it to be  manifested in the performance of the action signified, for that is what signs signal or signify .

Natural and artificial signs

Traffic lights are examples of artificial signs, that is, of signs invented by people for the purpose of guiding or regulating their conduct in certain circumstances. But artificial signs are just an extension of the so-called “ natural signs,” which are not invented but inherited; they are characteristic of each species, and they function as rules for the conduct of  animals . A certain sound by a mother sheep  functions as a not learned but inherited signzl for her ewe – and only for her ewe, not for other sheep– to come and gather around her. This is a natural , that is, inherited sign. There are, however, in addition, non-inherited, acquired or learned signs. For instance, with a certain amount of training, certain shepherds manage to direct their dogs with the sounds of whistling to behave in a way that controls the direction in which their flock is supposed to move. Now, the sound of whistling is not a natural sign for the dogs to direct the flock, for it is not inherited but acquired through special training. In this sense, not only is it an acquired rather than inherited sign, but it is an artificial sign for the trained dogs for behaving in a certain way. MOREOVER, IT IS A NOT LINGUISTIC, SIGN, FOR  IT INVOLVES NO CONCEPTION OF ANYTHING SIGNIFIED. RATHER THAN A SIGN, ITIS A SIGNAL, FOR IT   DIRECTLY CONNECTED WITH ACTION, IT IS A SENSORI-MOTOR SIGNAL, IN THE SENSE THAT IT IS CONNECTED WITH ACTION WITHOUT THE MEDIATION OF AN UNDERSTOOD MEANING.

All artificial signs are modifications and extensions of natural signs. That is, new conducts are learned or acquired on the basis of inherited or natural sign. Correlatively, we people can invent or teach new signs only for those species that have the natural ability to respond to natural signs as signs.

The mechanism through which an acquired sign becomes and remains a sign for behaving in a particular way, in the case of animals, is complex. (In the case of people, the fundamental mechanism is language that sanctions conducts). Many theories have been proposed to explain the phenomenon. The reason for this is that there are different kinds of signs: natural, artificial, symbolic and not symbolic, communicative and not communicative, linguistic and not linguistic, .... What is certain is that all genuine sings are SIGNALS or signs-FOR, and that the range of conducts that can be elicited through signs varies widely from species to species..

Now, to come to humans, the most salient way in which we socialize or domesticate the young members of our own species and teach them certain conducts that we consider preferable, once they have reached a certain level of developmental maturity, is through the semantic system we call language. Very few species, comparatively, can become genuinely domesticated by humans, that is, to behave as domestic animals, even under the best trainers, because a certain inherited need is prerequired. Unfortunately we are as confused as to the way in which language itself as a system of signs functions as we are concerning the way in which other signs function. This is manifested by the multiple explanatory theories of language, most of which ignore the intrinsic connection of language with action.

Previously I mentioned that all our acquired and artificial signs are “extensions” of natural signs .  By that I meant that the objects that function as natural signs have to be  related, at least indirectly, to the satisfaction of the natural  needs of the species for which they function as signs . Now, the human species seems to be the most malleable or adaptable species by far , in that it can be domesticated –or domesticate itself– to the most varied “domi” or houses (domesticate comes from the Latin word “domus,” which means “house,” “home”). I think that the answer to this so important question has to do, on the one hand, with (1) the fact that our human  needs do not function as instincts, as they do in the case of animals, that is, they do not dictate, or are signals-For, definite, unmodifiable, uncontrollable conducts. So animals neither need nor can plan their conduct, in the sense that they cannot choose their ends.. On the other hand, the answer has also to do with two other interrelated facts, to wit: that we are linguistic creatures and, as such, are capable of (2) deliberative reflection on their future conduct,   and also (3) of applicative knowledge of the objective conditions on which successful action depends .

Let us begin with (1 )  instinctive conducts , which is the simplest element. An instinctive conduct is like a response in the S-R model of behavior, only more complex than a response. A reflex response is an inherited, automatic or not planned, uncontrollable  and  unmodifiable behavior prompted by a certain feature of the environment of a living creature. If there is intense light in my environment, my eye responds by contracting  the pupil and producing tears. If there is a certain intense scent in my environment, my stomach responds by vomiting. Well, an instinct or instinctive conduct is equally inherited or not learned, equally automatic, uncontrollable and unmodifiable as a response is, but is much more complex conduct than a response, for it is not just a behavior but an entire pattern of behavior, which is what I call a conduct by contrast to a behavior .. Examples of this are the complex pattern of behavior of bees in building hives, of birds in building their nests, of spiders in building webs, etc. These conducts vary from species to species. They are complex and highly skilled, unlike responses. Now, we humans, by contrast to animals, inherit some simple  responses or behaviors but no instinctive conducts. We build our houses in the most different styles. Moreover, our houses may not have the extremely sophisticated ventilating system of a hive, which seems to be a marvel of engineering, according to the experts. Thus, as indicated by Marx, the difference between the house built instinctively by bees and the house built by an architect, is not that the human builds better than the bee, but that the human builds in a totally different way, for the human invents and projects and chooses what he is going to build and how and for what purpose, whereas the bee just executes blindly the plan of nature as dictated to it by instinct. ^(F10)

REFLECTION is often mischaracterized in the epistemological literature, by contrast to perception , as a second-order activity , in that it is about a past or remembered moment of perception-interaction and presupposes it, whereas perception confronts us with the present and presupposes nothing. For instance, if you tell me that you offer me a job, I may answer that I will reflect on your offer. I can reflect on your offer because you already have made me an offer, but I cannot possibly  reflect on something which has not taken place yet and of which I did not become previously aware at the perceptual moment.

Wherein lies the MIScharacterization of reflection? In that perception does not confront us with the present as an object known or to be known; rather what is perceived functions as a sign  FOR action and  refers us to a desired future . For instance, concerning your offer of a job, which takes place at the interactive moment of perception, I am not objectively aware of your offer, but only tacitly, as a sign For action . Consequently,   I will accept your offer, if I want to have a job, or reject it, if I don’t want a job. And this will show in my actions subsequent to your offer. By contrast, the very comparison of the perceptual moment with  the past object of reflection wrongly presupposes that we perceive in the way in which we grasp the object of reflection, that is, objectively, as a Kantian phenomenon. Under the weight of the representational epistemology characteristic of the Western philosophical tradition, enunciative language is deemed to function as a (descriptive) sign OF what is enunciated. My conduct in response to your verbal offer of a job presupposes, it is argued by Searle,  that I am OBJECTIVELY  aware of your offer. However, that is not the case, for I am not aware of your offer as object known, but only tacitly and practically or instrumentally, as a sign-For, as the “from where to act.”    

But there is, in addition to this objectifying reflection, a different conception of reflection, which unfortunately occupies a very secondary place in our philosophical tradition, obsessed as that tradition  is with objective representational  knowledge. Let us call it deliberative reflection by contrast to the spurious objectifying reflection whose impossible purpose is to comme to know “the real” through linguistic representation . The purpose of deliberative reflection is  not to know objectively what is the case, but to determine how to act – the way the British captain reflected or deliberated to determine whether to abandon ship or fight the fire. Deliberative reflection, which may take place during the perceptual-interactive moment, presupposes the capacity to neutralize or put in abeyance the inherited signaling force of a sign-FOR, thereby putting in abeyance one’s own conduct in response to it, instead of responding  automatically to the signal, as animals do under the pressure of the prevailing desire. It also presupposes the capacity to know the world and its signs in an only potentially instrumental way through its regularities in order to adapt the means to the desired-chosen ends. Both of these capacities depend on our linguistic ability as their condition. For –by itself and in the perceptual absence of the objective conditions for action–  language refers us directly to concepts, not to real objects .

Now, animals do not reflect in any sense . All their dealings with their surrounding world are participatory or applicative, immediately practical. Why? Because they are not linguistic creatures capable of conceptualizing not only ”objects” but events and situations . The conduct of animals is predetermined by heredity and previous training or learning; they neither have to, nor can, plan their conduct nor the consequent modifications of the surrounding world. That is, they cannot invent new goals. That is why each species of animals, aside from the evolutionary modifications that happen to them, lives in approximately the same way as their ancestors lived. Not so the human species. Based on observed regularities, we invent means to transform the world in order to adapt it to our needs (agriculture is a case in point),  and in the process transform ourselves to adapt ourselves to the transformed world. But what has this to do with language?

The judges of the British captain mentioned above determined that he could, and therefore should, have saved his ship instead of abandoning it to the fire. Yet they were not present at the time of the events. This shows that conduct in response to a situation can be planned –“on paper,” as it were– while the planner is not actually involved in the situation and is not being pressured to act by the urgency of the situation. This is a variety of deliberative reflection, that involves putting the world in abeyance and, by the same token, putting oneself in abeyance before acting.  However, to put the world in abeyance in a complex situation like the one just described one needs language to enunciate (based on past experience or experiment), the regular reaction to fire of the different materials of which the ship was made, under the climatic conditions that prevailed at the time. Abstract enunciative scientific language makes possible these complex, ideal objectifications or conceptualizations of regularities in the activity of our surrounding world..Only a linguistic species, like ours, can do that, for enunciative language renders possible to think of, or conceptualize, objective regularities in their absence, in pure hypothesis in terms of actual action..    

Notice that these judges were not deliberating as to their own action, they were considering whether the desirable action of fighting the fire could be successful given the objective conditions, because the realization of projects through action depends on natural regularities that are objective in the sense of being independent of desire. Now, these conditions can be very complex, as Searle indirectly suggests ^(F11), and only the use of language –particularly the WRITTEN language of science , which, as a problem-solving activity is ultimately applicative– makes possible their knowledge and the storage of that knowledge IN WRITING, TO BE READ-CONCEPTUALIZED IN ABSENCE , AND APPLIED HYPOTHETICALLY ON PAPER,  RATHER THAN HEARD-TO BE RESPONDED TO IN PRESENCE, destined ultimately to the satisfaction of those desires concerning which the stated information will be pertinent at one time or another.

This is the activity to which Aristotle referred by the name of episteme, in Latin scientia. He understood it as yielding necessarily true knowledge, because it was INTELLECTUAL  knowledge per causas , which were intelligible rather than perceptible and, therefore, not contingent but necessary. However, unlike myself, Aristotle was an objectivist (hence his “categories” of the objects known), an intellectualist (hence his intelligible  “substances”), and a realist concerning “really existing natures tthat corresponded to what the subject had in mind. ” But the very notion of sign-FOR skips the Serlean and Aristotelian-Thomistic step that requires theoretical representational verbal  understanding as a condition for action As indicated by Wittgenstein, following a rule or obeying an order is not an interpretation or understanding of the rule as an ‘object” of knowledge ^(F12); rather, associated with our needs and desires, it is a result of the inherited tendency to respond to signs-FOR. The Aristotelian “causes,” which are regular, conjoined with desires, are signs-For, because the laws can be manipulated as instrumental means to satisfy our desires . If I know that cold air, under certain conditions, regularly causes an undesirable cold, I can invent the means to raise the temperature in my home, or to cover myself to avoid the cold.

However, what the activity we call “science” does is not, as Aristotle thought, is to grasp intelligible and necessary laws or objective “natures.’ Rather. through careful observation and measurement , it grasps regularities of potential action in the world which, at the perceptual-interactive moment of application and under the appropriate conditions of desire, will function as signs-instruments FOR problem-solving action. (Notice that the important or remembered regularities enunciated by science are related to common, permanent human desires, such as health, survival, security, etc).

To consider now Aquinas own example in I Meta, Lect. 1 , as developed by Peghaire in his article, “A Forgotten sense, The Cogitative,”

Plato has been sick; his pulse was rapid, his temperature too high, his tongue coated – these are so many external sensations whic h I have made and noted in my memory. Some doctor, as I have seen for myself or been told, gave him a dose of a certain herb – more external sensations which I have simply noted. Now the sick man’s  pulse is back to normal, his temperature is lower, his tongue is cleared, and he is cured – a third series of external sensations also noted in my memory. Thereupon I said that Plato was cured of his fever by this medicine. I have made the same observations in the case of Socrates, Phaedo, Critias. and so forth... – These various observations concerning Plato, Socrates, Phaedo, Critias, were successively recorded in my memory, perhaps at widely different times. But now, I place them together in my actual, present consciousness; I remember them. Then, going from one to the other, I note the concrete similarity of concrete symptoms in the case of each of the sick men; I note that the four doses of medicine which cured them show  a similarity of concrete characteristics;  I see that the concrete effect in the four cases was the same.  I have therefore a concrete knowledge of these similar instances under a common nature . Now this last named knowledge is what St. Thomas calls experimentum . He sees it as a collatio , that is to say, a gathering together, a collection of singular data going to make up a singular whole. - In this knowledge, the first thing I have is a series of what St. Thomas calls judgment of

the senses.” ^(F13)

So far, Aristotle would say, we are on the level of perception, of what Aquinas calls “judgments of the senses.” We still do not know why the herb cures the illness.

Now, the physiological  regularities with which physicians deal are more complex and less exactly measurable than the physical regularities with which physicists deal. The former cannot be enunciated in a mathematical statement, like the law of falling bodies on inclined planes. The mathematical law of physics, however –contrary to Aristotle’s assumption about “natures”– doesn’t explain or make intelligible why bodies fall the way they do on inclined planes, it only renders their fall exactly predictable at the time of application, thanks to the measurements involved in the law. As a consequence, the law makes possible the instrumental use of the grasped regularity to achieve our human purposes.

Searle suggests that language makes possible complex conceptual objectifications that combine a number of observed regularities under one enunciation. ^(F14) These are based on remembered regularities of the world, which are conceptualized in an enunciation and, when and if conjoined with a particular desire, will become signs FOR action. In the absence of actual desire, the registered regularities are only concepts,  potential signs FOR. Anyway those complex regularities couldn’t be remembered if they were not linguistically registered not as statements of fact to be understood and assented to,  but to serve as maps For action at the right moment when a desire can be  realized. Thus, in conjunction with our human ability to plan action, instead of acting immediately and automatically according to the strongest desire, as animals do when perceptually confronted with signs, enunciative language makes possible complex delayed actions through non-representational conceptualizations of regularities.

But doesn’t this show that, after all, enunciative language is representational OF what is enunciated?   No, not in the only important sense of being objectively representational of something to be understood . The enunciative language of science refers to regularities to be made relevant by desire, and what is referred to will function as a signal FOR action under the appropriate perceptual-interactive conditions . As indicated by KUHN’S problem-solving, instrumental, applicative conception of science (by contrast to the representational “ontological” conception involved in what he calls “textbook science”),  this is the way in which scientific enunciations mean and in which their meaning is to be cashed.  If cashed as if they were a representation of what is the case in reality, it results in the metaphysical conundrum that has infected and afflicted Western philosophy – and, to an extent, Western culture–  from Plato and Aristotle through Kant and beyond. - That is what philosophical reflection has traditionally attempted to do. (From it resulted Aristotelian metaphysics, a well as traditional metaphysical doctrinal theology, both of which are hopeless, misleading ventures). ^(F15)

This reflection on signs may help us to grasp the fundamental difference between Searle’s and Polanyi’s respective conceptions of the way in which language functions. Searle thinks that language functions as a system of signs OF something to be understood and formulated linguistically, for “THE CHARACTERISTIC INTENDED EFFECT OF MEANING IS UNDERSTANDING.” ^(F16) For his part,  Polanyi thinks that it is meant to function as a sign-FOR, that is, a SIGNAL FOR the actual performance of an action, with no understanding or enunciation of the action to be performed necessarily mediating. Thus, according to Searle, in order to obey a command I have first to understand the command. In other words, the verbal command is a sign OF something to be understood theoretically. According to Polanyi, by contrast, the verbal command is meant to function directly as a sign FOR performing the action commanded, under the appropriate perceptual conditions and conditions of desire. The “understanding” here involved is PRACTICAL, NOT INTELLECTUAL, in that it is meant to be manifested in the actual performance of the action commanded, not in theoretical understanding of the action commanded.

An important observation is pertinent in this respect. Contrary to Searle’s conviction, enunciative language is no exception for it does not function  as a sign OF something to be known rather than as a sign FOR performing an action. It too functions as a sign FOR, as all signs do. Indeed, in its original usage, that is, in ORAL, face-to face communication among agents who share the same situation, even enunciative language does not function as a sign OF A MEANING to be understood, namely, what is said. Rather, as is the case with all other signs, it, too, functions as a sign FOR performing an action. HOWEVER , WRITTEN complex enunciative speech acts of the type exemplified by Searle in note 14 with their sedimented conventional meaning to be activated at the appropriate applicative time, also mean perlocutionarily rather than illocutionarily.

Ironically, this shows in Searle’s own example of the American soldier in Italy during World War II. I say “ironically,” for the very purpose of the example is to show that enunciative speech acts function illocutionarily, as representations of reality. But that is not the way in which the American soldier intends to be heard. In that example the enunciation “Ich kome...” is intended to substitute for “I am a German soldier,” AND, conjoined with the desire to please his allies, this enunciation, pace Searle, was meant to elicit from the Italians the action of liberating the American, rather than, as Searle assumes, to terminate in the theoretical or purely objective awareness that the American was a German soldier.  

The reason why we get easily confused on this point, as Searle himself does, is that in the case of enunciative language –different in this from real speech acts possessing an explicit perlocutionary force– the action signaled is not specified, because (to return to the same Serlean example) the speaker rightly assumes that, given the circumstances, the addressee’s asssumed desire to treat well his German allies will automatically convert the enunciation into SIGN FOR or instrument for action at the appropriate time. (Thus, all the signs on roads presuppose a desire on the drivers’ part to reach one or another of the signaled places, and are relevant as signs only to those who desire to reach a particular destination and according to t he destination each of them wants to reach).

When, thanks to our human linguistic ability, the project is conceived in advance, in thought or “on paper,” in THE ABSENCE of the concrete circumstances in which it is to be realized , the agent has to determine when is the appropriate perceptual moment to act and realize his project. In the case of perceptual co-presence of speaker and addressee who share the same situation, as is the case in Searle’s example of the American soldier with the Italians, there is no problem, for the perceptual co-presence of speaker and hearer in the same situation functions itself as the sign For immediate action. (ORAL COMMUNICATION).  However, in the case of a so-called “scientific law,” as, for instance, the application of the abstract enunciation of the WRITTEN law to the solution of a concrete problem, it is through repeated practice of the application either in the laboratory or on paper that the student learns, under the teacher’s guidance, how and when to apply the regularity enunciated by the law to solve a particular problem to which the law applies. (Incidentally, this is the way one learns to use the map of a region to find one’s way to any place within the region).  

This is the opportunity to consider the essential role desire plays in the case of Signs- For. For something to function as a signal, that is as a sign For performing an action, it has to be conjoined with a desire or an inherited need on the agent’s part. Water perceived signals the action of drinking it, but only to an agent who needs water and is actually thirsty. The perceived water functions not as a picture or representation of what is the case,  to become aware of, but as an instrument for action, as a map-instrument that guides the agent’s action to satisfy his need or his desire. (Cf above the note that refers to the way in which signs of destinations in a road function for different people, depending on the destination each of them wants to reach).

The difference between a map conceived as a picture of a terrain and a map actually used as an instrument for action is the difference between a sign-OF and a sign- FOR “to be acted from” . To return  to the experiment with the shock-producing syllables, it is the difference between observing the syllables that appear on the screen to become aware of their observable characteristics, and using the experienced sensations produced by the syllables as instruments to guide one’s action toward the realization of the desire to escape the expected shock. Much as the blind person who carries a stick to find his way uses the sensations produced in his hand every time the stick hits an object on his path as an instrument  or map to guide himself toward the place he wants to reach. To a desirous agent, these sensations are not representations OF anything but signs FOR action to satisfy his desire. The same instrumental role is played by the verbal enunciations made by one agent to another in a commonly shared situation . If I tell you “Your sister is on the phone,” I expect you, not to become THEORETICALLY aware of that fact, that is of the conventional meaning of my words , but to use what I enunciate as an instrument similar to a map to guide  you  to the telephone and speak to your sister . In short, Searle is wrong in attributing to verbal enunciations the representational role of signs OF what is said of  what is meant, for –in conjunction with the project that as signs FOR they CONTRIBUTE TO MAKING POSSIBLE–  they are meant to function as instrumental signs FOR action, as Polanyi implicitly claims.

Like all other animals we come to the world ‘wired’ to act in certain ways in response to certain objects in order to satisfy our needs. The objects that satisfy  needs vary from species to species, as well as the needs and the response. The complexes object-need-response characteristic of each species are hereditary. Both chicken and ducks instinctively follow the first animal that takes care of them at birth, but ducks follow it into the water, whereas chicken stop at the border of the water thanks to a stronger inherited need. Instincts are complex, teleological  forms of behavior, inherited rather than learned . They are teleological in that they are meant to fulfill the purpose of satisfying a vital need of the animal, but they are not intentional, in that the animal doesn’t choose the behavior nor intends a purpose. A step lower in terms of complexity than the just mentioned “conducts” are those inherited automatic “movements” characteristic of a species that we call “reflexes” . Such are the sucking reflex in newborn children at contact with the nipple, the vomiting reflex in response to certain scents or tastes, the toad’s reflex of sticking its tongue in the direction of whatever comes near its mouth and is of a size that can be eaten by the toe. Reflex movements or “responses” are simple reactions to a stimulus, which obtain automatically whether or not they are appropriate to satisfy a need. Instinctive conducts are complex and not purely automatic, in that the animal may adapt its conduct, according to the perceptual signs-FOR,  to obtain the desired result. In contrast to reflex movements and instinctive conducts, in which the goals to be obtained are inherited, stand those complex conducts in which both goals and means are invented. Agriculture is an example.

Language and signs

For all their very significant differences, what Searle and Polanyi have in common is that both think that language is a semantic system, that is, a system of signs.

Let us begin with Searle. He thinks that language is primarily a system of signs through which a subject expresses to himself or herself and to others that of which he or she is aware. Indeed,  through the same signs  through which the subject enunciates silently to himself what he is aware of, the subject can communicate and make known to others to others, if he decides to do so, that of which he is aware. If, by understanding what the speaker says, the hearer becomes aware of what the speaker means, the speech act is successful, for the essential function of language consists in making known what the speaker intends to communicate. This is what Searle calls the “illocutionary effect,” the effect that the speaker cannot fail to intend whenever he says something, for the speaker necessarily intends to make known or understood to the hearer what he means

In addition, however, to the illocutionary effect, sometimes the speaker may also intend  to produce a “perlocutionary effect” which does not consist in knowledge,  but either in an action of the hearer intended by the speaker (for instance, the action of obeying in the case of a verbal  order) or (a possibility that Searle does not explicitly consider– in an affection of the hearer intended by the speaker (for instance, the affection of feeling threatened in the case of a verbal threat, or the affection of being amused in the case of a verbal joke). However, to the difference of the illocutionary effect, which is essential to the success of the speech act as such, the perlocutionary effect is accidental, because it is not necessarily intended by the speaker in every communicative action, and because the success of the  communicative action is insured if only the hearer gains the knowledge of what the speaker intends to communicate, independently of whether the speaker intends a perlocutionary effect in addition to the illocutionary effect, and independently of whether the perlocutionary effect, if intended, actually obtains or not.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Thus Searle thinks that the essential function of language is to make known to the hearer what the speaker means by saying or enunciating what is meant as something to be understood. , for “the characteristic effect of meaning is understanding” (p. 47). Indeed, that is why:

(1) Searle explains that the difference between linguistic signs and all other signs is that linguistic signs mean by saying or enunciating what is meant.  On this precise point Searle sees the dysanalogy between linguistic enunciations( which say what they mean) and games. ^(F17) For instance, a certain move in the game of chess means “checkmate” but doesn’t say it , while the verbal enunciation “checkmate” means and says it ^(F18). Again, a red traffic light means “stop,” but doesn’t say it, whereas the verbal enunciation “stop” says what is meant.

(2) The underlying idea of Searle’s “principle of expressibility, that whatever can be meant  can be said,” is that the speaker ideally should say exactly what he means, because only by enunciating exactly what he means can the speaker make known to the hearer exactly what he means. ^(F19)

(3) In his farfetched but revealing example of the American soldier captured by the Italians during World War II, whose point is ”to illustrate the connection between what the speaker means and what the words he utters mean” ( SA, p. 44), Searle would deny that the effect of the Italians believing that he was a German soldier was linguistically obtained, for the words the American uttered mean “I come from the land where the lemon-trees bloom,” not “I am a German soldier.” A fortiori Searle would deny that the effect of being set free by the Italians was linguistically obtained, for Searle cannot even conceive that statements mean as signs-For, that is, as signals, rather than as signs-OF.

====the words he utters mean die Citronen bluhen” obtained linguistically the effect of being set free (assuming that  he had actually been set free by the Italians), for what he said doesn’t mean in German “I am a German soldier” (which Searle himself assumes is what the speaker intended the Germans to unerstand and believe) but “I come from the land where the lemon trees flourish.”

Searle’s view that what is said by the speaker must express or describe what the speaker intends to make known to the hearer, and that the hearer gains that knowledge by understanding what is said, rests on the assumption that enunciative language is a system of representational signs. What is said must represent linguistically, as faithfully as possible, by making  known what the speaker intends to communicate, and what the hearer understands must be as faithful a representation as possible of what the speaker intends to communicate. Thus what is said by the speaker is a sign OF what the speaker means because it is a verbal representation OF the speaker’s communicative intention, and the same goes for what is understood by the hearer.

Totally different from Searle’s is Polanyi’s conception of the way in which language functions, because Polanyi thinks that signs –verbal signs included AND, PARTICULARLY, STATEMENTS INCLUDED—  do not mean by representing or describing in any way what is meant . Indeed, n either a red nor a green traffic light is in any way a representation or a description of what is signaled. Rather, each of them signals an action to be performed, not a meaning to be understood . Similarly an arrow on a road signals the kind of action to be performed by the agent who encounters the sign, but does not signal the action to be performed by representing or enunciating or describing the action signaled. Hence the appropriate understanding of the arrow encountered on the road consists in the actual performance of the action signaled, not in a mental or verbal representation of the action signaled.  

But is not language a unique case among signs? Is not Searle right in thinking  that the uniqueness of language among signs is that it means or signifies by saying what is meant by the speaker? Not so; in Polanyi’s opinion. According to Polanyi,, Searle is wrong on this point. The problem with Searle’s view of language is that he thinks of all “saying” as enunciating, and he thinks of what is enunciated as a piece of information to be understood or to become aware of by the hearer, rather than, as is the case, as a signal to be responded to by  performing the action signaled .. Precisely because Searle is wrong on this point, he  claims that the saying involved in a request, such as “Lend me your pencil,” succeeds as a speech act if only the hearer becomes informed of the speaker’s desire, as if in making a request the speaker intended to inform the hearer of his desire rather than having the hearer –or rather, the addressee–  comply with his desire by actually lending him his pencil.

I have already argued against this Serlean view which ignores the grammatical difference between the subjunctive or the imperative in which desires are expressed and the indicative in which pieces of information are imparted. This is the reason that, as I argued in the preceding chapter, if Searle were right on this point, language would function primarily in an illocutionary way, that is,  in the bastard way English functions in a course of English for foreigners, where the request “Lend me your pencil” is understood as a piece of information to be acquired by the hearer rather than as a request to be complied with. In that artificial situation, all the sayings of speakers function as Searle wrongly thinks that sayings normally function – as mere factual enunciations. Clearly, however,  this is not the way in which competent users of English understand requests, nor the way in which requests actually function in ordinary linguistic, face-to-face, oral exchanges, nor the way in which a person who makes a request intends his or her requests to be understood. However, rather than merely repeating the arguments already given against Searle’s views on language what we need is to explore the alternative conception of language furnished by Polanyi.

Polanyi’s alternative conception of language : The primary function of language is, what is meant to be heard as a sign or rule for action, not as a description of the action meant.

To begin with,  both Searle and Polanyi agree that language is a semantic system, that is, a system of signs. They disagree, however, concerning the way in which signs function when they are actually experienced as signs, that is, when we encounter them at the perceptual moment and are actually referred by them to what they signal and are referred to what they signal precisely in the way in which they signal it.

-----

Searle and Polanyi have totally  different and even disparate conceptions of language..

SEARLE THINKS

that linguistic signs are essentially different form all other signs in that, like all other signs, linguistic signs mean by referring to something to be understood – “the characteristic effect of meaning is understanding.” – However, linguistic signs are unique and superior in their signaling capacity in that they make explicit what they mean by saying or enunciating what they mean so as to make unambiguously  understood what they mean.

There are two classes of speech acts: (a) purely enunciative or “illocutionary,” whose exclusive purpose is to make understood what is enunciated, and (b) perlocutionary, whose purpose, in addition to making understood what they enunciate, is to elicit an action on the addressee’s part.

FOR HIS PART, POLANYI THINKS

that (a) No sign is meant  terminate in understanding, Rather. All signs are meant to terminate in the performance of an action – the action whose performance is signaled. And

(b) That linguistic signs are no exception. They too, like all other signs, are meant to terminate in the performance of the action signaled , not in understanding a meaning to be understood.

A few precisions are required concerning the character of language, of which linguistic signs are components:

- A language is an instrument of communication that exists as such, t hat is, as an act of communication, only when actually used by linguistic agents for purposes of communicating with other linguistic agents.

- The unit of linguistic usage is a speech act addressed by a speaker to a particular hearer or hearers in a particular situation. Words by themselves do not mean. This idea comes from the prevalence of reading over speaking and hearing language in face to face communication , but both reading and writing are very late developments in the history of languages. People are the ones – and the only ones–  who mean . And this they do through speech acts. (See Speech Acts , pp. 16-19).

-----

Now, the first thing to realize regarding signs is that being referred by a sign is not the same as observing, and becoming aware of, the characteristics of the thing that is a sign. In the case of written language this is obvious. If I have a book in a language I do not know, I see perfectly the signs on the page, but I do not experience them as signs , for  they do not refer me to what they mean. Nor, pace Searle, is being referred by a sign meant to terminate in theoretical understanding what is meant. In short, Searle is wrong in thinking that the characteristic effect of meaning is understanding. The characteristic effect of meaning is action, the performance of the action signaled.  

Secondly, regarding the experience of signs as signs Polanyi claims that when a sign functions as a sign, the sign is not itself observed or perceived as such,  for it signals “tacitly” or practically rather than explicitly or verbally . This is why, in the case of the experiment with shock-producing syllables, Polanyi claimed that the subject didn’t know –in the sense of observing or becoming explicitly aware of the syllables that produced shocks; consequently, he could not say which were those syllables. Actually, the subject ‘knew’ those syllables only in their referring function, only in his actually being referred by them to their shock-producing effect ,in the sense of expecting to suffer that effect .

Thirdly, not only was the subject’s knowledge of the shock-producing syllables “tacit,” in that he was not able to say which were those syllables, but the very verbal identification of the syllables would have been  superfluous, according to Polanyi. The reason is that, contrary to Searle’s assumption, the syllables did not signify something to be understood, that is, the knowledge of the verbalizable fact that those syllables produced shocks;  rather, they signaled to the subject to perform  an action– the action of protecting himself from the shock to come ..

Generalizing his misinterpretation of linguistic signs, Searle thinks that signs in general function as signs OF something –a “meaning”–  to be understood and thereby known by the hearer. For his part, Polanyi claims that all signs, linguistic signs included , are signs FOR performing an action – the action signaled, that is. In other words, signs are meant to terminate in an action on the subject’s part, not in what Searle calls understanding of something signaled.

Searle’s own farfetched example of the American soldier in Italy shows that –contrary to his own view– what is meant by statements is the performance of an action. For what the American soldier intended by saying “ Ich kome...,” was not that the Italians understand what the German sentence means, or what the speaker meant by using the German sentence, but that they perform the action of setting him free . In short, even in the case of linguistic enunciations , enunciations do not mean as a sign OF something to be known by the hearer, namely what is enunciated, for the purpose of face-to-face enunciating is not making anything known (in the sense of understood), as Searle claims, but to elicit an action. In other words, according to  Polanyi, saying is a sign FOR performing an action.  Which is to say that, pace Searle, enunciations mean, so to say,  perlocutionarily, not illocutionarily . The only difference between enunciations and other perlocutionary acts is that enunciations do not specify the action meant (as, for instance, commands and requests do),  but leave its determination to the linguistic agent according to his desires or needs in the circumstances in which he experiences the sign as a sign. As to why this difference, the reason is that genuinely l inguistic agents, unlike animals, are not inescapably led by their needs or desires but are capable of deciding how to act by cutting the automatic connection between sign and action and inventing the means. (Cf. Nussbaum)

Far from being what is signified by a red traffic light, the linguistic enunciation –if it occurs at all–  is only meaningful as a  practical direction or rule for the agent to follow in the performance of the conduct signified. This is obvious in the case of an order or of a request – that is, in the case of what Searle calls perlocutionary acts– , for an order is to be obeyed and a request is to be granted by performing the action requested, not by conceiving or enunciating or even assenting to the supposedly representational statement of the action to be obeyed or of the action requested.. And the same is the case with oral enunciations and statements. For, contrary to what Searle assumes, saying is not a sign OF what is said, and so it is not meant as a sign to conceive or imagine (as a copy of)  what is said, but a sign FOR linguistic action in response . Much as is the case with a music partition, the appropriate comprehension of which consists, and is manifested in, the actual performance of the musical piece. ^(F20)

After this long introduction, we are well prepared to understand Polanyi’s own presentation of his views, by contrast t o Searle’s.

As Polanyi’s analysis of tacit knowledge progresses, his emphasis shifts from the inability to express in words the characteristics of the object to what he calls “INTERIORIZATION ” is always practical .

Interiorizing something, Polanyi explains, is to identify oneself with it by making it function as the immediate term of TK IN THE WAY WE USE OUR OWN BODY WHEN WE ACT, thereby transcending it toward the realization of the desired goal.

What, precisely, is interiorized here? The stick. Rather than the stick being perceived itself as an object in the external  environment,  it is assimilated to the agent’s body  to the point that the stick ceases to count  as an external object and functions, rather, as an extension of the agent’s body, much as is the case with eyeglasses . When worn by an agent, the eyeglasses are not seen or perceived 0themselves, but are assimilated to the agent’s body and function as an extension of the eyes of the agent to guide him in his action. Think of an agent in total darkness. His situation is comparable to that of a blind person. Instinctively, this person, in order to find his way, extends his arms so as to touch the objects that are on his way before hitting them, thus being able to avoid them, not by seeing them, but  by using the sensations in his hands as instruments to guide his steps towards the place he wants to reach.

In connection with internalization Polanyi  uses such terms as “inhabiting” or “indwelling.” Now, “inhabiting” or “dwelling in” are different from “perceiving” or “knowing,” which refer to the object perceived or known.

“ The identification of tacit knowing with indwelling involves a shift of  emphasis in our conception of tacit knowing. We had envisaged tacit knowing in the first place as a way to know more than we can tell. We identified the two terms of tacit knowing, the proximal and the distal, and recognized the way we attend from the first to the second, thus achieving an integration of  particulars to a coherent entity to which we are attending. Since we were not attending to the particulars in themselves, we could not identify them; but if we now regard the integration of particulars as an interiorization , it takes on a more positive character. It now becomes a means of making certain things function as the proximal terms of tacit knowing, so that instead of observing them in themselves, we may be aware of them in their bearing on the comprehensive entity which they constitute. It brings home to us that it is not by looking at things, but by dwelling in them, that we understand their joint meaning.. By concentrating attention on his fingers, a pianist can temporarily paralyze his movement. ^(F21)

Polanyi’s notion of tacit knowledge would have gained much in precision if, instead of speaking of the proximal term of  knowledge he had specified that “inhabiting”, “indwelling,” etc., which  appear always in relation to interiorization,  refer to instruments or means used by an agent, not to objects known. Thus, the stick in the hands of the blind person in which the blind person “indwells” to find his way is not what the person knows but the instrument he uses to guide his steps to the place he wants to reach. Similarly, the eyeglasses worn by a myopic person. are not what the person who wears them  knows or sees, but the instrument he uses to guide his action to achieve the purposes he desires.

But why does Polanyi use the word ‘interiorization’? Because a shift takes place, as it were, from an object perceived in the subject’s environment to the agent’s body as instrument for action . As already indicated, this shift may be illustrated by the example of eyeglasses . When a pair of glasses rest on a table and I look at them, they do not function as eyeglasses but as object of knowledge, as object known. I see the eyeglasses. When I wear the eyeglasses, however, I do not see the eyeglasses, I “inhabit’ them, that is, I use them as instruments to find my way as an agent in the realization of my projects. The eyeglasses are “interiorized” in the sense that they now function as an extension of my eyes, which I do not see but which I use as instruments for my action. My eyes only function as object known when I look at them in the mirror. But those are not my eyes that see , but my eyes seen as any other observed object. When my eyes see they  function as the instruments I use to act efficiently in the realization of my projects. Polanyi calls interiorization this shift of an object  from object known in the environment of the agent to its functioning as an extension of the agent’s own body when actually used as an instrument. When worn, the eyeglasses are not known themselves; they are not seen, anymore than our eyes are seen when we use them as instruments to act in the world. ^(F22)

            When a blind person uses a stick to find his way to the place to which he wants to go, he is guided in his action by the sensations he experiences in his hands every time that the stick hits an object. However,  these sensations are not known in themselves by the blind person; rather, they are felt by the agent (for they are affections), and they  they function as “ that from which ” the person finds his way towards the place he wants to reach . Thus, if the blind person is asked to what he pays attention, he won’t mention the sensations he experiences in his hand, for he has no objective awareness of them. Yet he is aware of them in the sense that he uses the sensations transmitted by the stick as instruments to find his way. He ”inhabits” them, or “dwells in” them, much as when we walk we use the sensations we experience on the plants of our feet at each step we make; we do not know them, rather we transcend them as “that from which” we make the next step. The blind person doesn’t know the sensations he experiences in the palm of his hand, he knows them only tacitly in that he uses them to make his next step.  He ‘inhabits” them as we inhabit our body when we use it as an instrument to realize our projects. Thus, when  we walk we don’t pay attention to the sensations we experience on the plants of our feet every time they touch the ground; we “dwell” in those sensations as that from which we make the next step, we pass through them as “that from which” we make the next step, much as right now I pass through the sensations I experience at the tips of my fingers every time I type on the keyboard toward the word I want to write.

Polanyi had initially explained TK as knowing more than one can say. His conceptual shift to the “interiorization” involved in TK helps him to become more precise regarding the nature of  TK. When playing the piano, the pianist is using it as an instrument to produce the desired music. In that case, the consciousness he has of the keys he touches is instrumental – “that from which he acts,” and even that consciousness is merely habitual.. If the word knowledge is to be employed in this context, it has to be described as “tacit,” for it is totally different from the explicit consciousness of object with which the ordinary notion of knowledge (and saying-enunciating) is identified. So much so that, as just explained by Polanyi,  if, in the process of playing, that is, of having tacit knowledge of his fingers and of the keys they touch, the pianist turns his attention to his fingers and observes them, thereby turning them into objects of what we take for knowledge, automatically his activity of playing becomes impaired. Similarly, if, while reading a text, we shift our attention from the meaning we are getting through reading to the letters that compose the text in order to know them as such, our reading becomes impaired. The reason for this is that while reading we only have a tacit awareness of the written signs, for we are not attending to them as such but using them as instruments to get their meaning. If we turn our attention to the signs, we gain what we ordinarily take for knowledge of their characteristics, but they cease to function as instruments that convey a meaning. The two forms of consciousness are not only different from each other but incompatible with each other, which is the reason why we cannot get the meaning of a text if we try to know the letter themselves.  

To summarize, Polanyi calls “interiorization” the special, non-objective, quasi oblique consciousness we have of what we actually use as an instrument to achieve a purpose through action . And he calls it “interiorization,” because we assimilate it to our own body, since the consciousness we have of our own body when we act to achieve a  purpose is itself instrumental and the ultimate model of tacit knowledge:

“But our awareness of our body for attending to things outside it suggests a wider generalization of the feeling we have of our body. Whenever we use certain things for attending from them to other things,  in the way in which we always use our own body, these things change their appearance. They appear to us now in terms of the entities to which we are attending from them, just as we feel our body in terms of the things outside to which we are attending from our body. In this sense we can say that when we make a thing function as the proximal term of tacit knowing, we incorporate it in our body or extend our body to include it –so that we come to dwell in it.” ^(F23)

The generalization of the awareness of our body at the time of action as the model of the interiorization involved in TK allows Polanyi to discover the mechanism of agency, which he exemplifies with  a large number of particular examples of ACTION. In the first place with the case of acquired bodily skills, such as playing the piano or typing, and in the case of those more complex skills such as driving a car.  These skills are fundamentally similar to the way a blind person ‘drives, ’so to say, his own body with the  help of a stick. These skills  are acquired as a result of along practice of interiorization; thanks to repetition, the agent transforms the stick, the typewriter, the piano, the car into extensions of his own body by using them as he uses his own body. At the end of that long practice of interiorization the use of those objects becomes as natural to the agent as the use of his own body in, say, the action of walking, which doesn’t require any other instrument than his own body. When a child learns to walk, he is guided by the sensations he experiences on the plants of his feet every time he makes a step; however, he does not focus his attention on them, he does not observe his  sensations, they are only “that from which ” he makes the subsequent steps towards the place he wants to reach..

The typewriter, the piano, the car are examples of material instruments – the technology invented and created by human agents to multiply the efficiency of their agency and extend its reach in order to satisfy first their elementary needs and then the desires that emerge as the transformation of the surrounding world allows the emergence of such desires. However, in addition to the invention of material instruments is the invention of symbolic (that is, semantic, signifying)  instruments , that is, signs. among which the most important are spoken and written language, because they make possible the invention of all other symbolic instruments . such as mathematical symbols, scientific theories, etc.

After the case of acquired bodily skills as examples of TK , Polanyi refers us to the use of   maps by an agent who, with their help, attempts to find his way in an unfamiliar territory. Before analyzing that case, let us remark that the reading and actual use of maps are far more complex operations than they seem to be to those who have already mastered them. Actually, a child, even a child who has already acquired the skill of reading written language, is not thereby automatically capable of using a map to find his way in unfamiliar territory, much less of drawing a map to be used by others. The reason for this difficulty is that a map is not a picture of a territory, any more than a name is a picture of the object named. A map is a symbolic SIGN of a territory, but not of a territory to be contemplated or observed but of a territory to be walked through . In other words, not only is a map to be deciphered or ‘read,’ as all symbols are, but to be deciphered in relation to the action of moving through the territory to which the map refers, in order to reach particular places within that territory. In other words, a map is a symbolic instrument to be used by an agent to achieve his goal. That is why, if somebody claimed to understand a map but proved himself incapable of using it in practice, he wouldn’t know what he is saying. Indeed, since the map is an instrument for action, understanding it amounts to being able to manifest that understanding in one’s action. The mistake of the person who claimed to understand a map while being unable to use it would be comparable to the one Searle makes when he claims that a person who is given an order understands it if only he grasps objectively the meaning of the words, without realizing that an order is to be obeyed, not to be understood objectively. In short, the genuine understanding of a map consists in the performance of the conduct that the map is meant to render possible, that is, in the conduct of reaching the desired place within the territory to which the map refers, as the genuine understanding of a verbal joke is to be manifested in the affection of being actually amused by it.

Polanyi’s view of the reading of maps of the kind we are referring to goes as follows . The agent who reads the map must “inhabit” the map as he inhabits his body when he acts to realize a project, for instance, when he is nailing a nail to hang a picture. In other words, he must make the nail function not as an object to be looked at and observed in itself, but as “that from which” he must act in order to arrive at the desired place, that is, as a SIGN FOR performing the action of pounding. In short,  TK is always practical, for it involves the use of an instrument an agent to achieve an intended goal, not a representation of object. ^(F24)

Actually, as already explained, all perception by an acting agent is of this kind.

-------

After these remarks we will not be surprised by Polanyi’s  idea that scientific theories function instrumentally for the solution of  problems rather than representationally. In other words, we tend to consider the statement in which, for instance, a physical law is expressed as a symbolic representation of the intelligible ‘nature’ of the particular phenomenon to which the law refers. For his part, Polanyi argues that such an interpretation is wrong. In his view, the statements of scientific laws are meant to be used by the scientific agent as instruments to guide his conduct in the solution of concrete problems related to the portion of the surrounding material world the law refers to, much as a map is meant to functions as an instrument for the person who uses it to find his way within the mapped territory.

In the following text,  Polanyi explains how theories function in the actual practice of science, that is, when the theoretical instrument is actually used rather than talked about :

“To rely on a theory for understanding nature is to interiorize it. For we are attending from the theory to things seen in its light, and we are aware  of the theory, while thus using it, in terms of the spectacle that it serves to explain. This is why mathematical theory can be learned only by practicing its application: its true knowledge lies in our ability to use it.” ^(F25)

This text, quoted  in isolation from its context, may be too dense and too cryptic to make us understand how, in Polanyi’s view, scientific theories function. What is clear, however,  is that Polanyi’s view contradicts the prevailing conception of science as an exact, or at least an increasingly approximative, representational  view of reality. Thomas Kuhn , who on this fundamental point  repeatedly indicates his agreement with Polanyi, expressed it forcefully:

“A scientific theory is usually felt to be better than its predecessors not only in the sense that it is a better instrument for discovering and solving  puzzles but also because it is somehow a better representation of what nature is really like. One often hears that successive theories grow ever closer to, or approximate more and more closely to, the truth. Apparently generalizations like that refer not to the puzzle solutions and concrete predictions derived from a theory but rather to its ontology, to the match, that is, between the entities with which the theory populates nature and what is ‘really there.’ Perhaps there is some other way of salvaging the notion of ‘truth’ for application to whole theories, but this one will not do.... the notion of a match between the ontology of a theory and its ‘real’ counter part in nature now seems to me illusive in principle” ^(F26)

What Kuhn is pointing out here is that the statements in which scientific theories are expressed are not meant to function as representations of reality but as symbolic instruments to be used by agents to solve practical problems. In this regard, though, scientific statements constitute no exception. Indeed, if Polanyi is right, no statement –whether scientific or other– is  meant to function as a theoretical representation OF its referent; rather, all statements are meant to function as instruments or signs  FOR action at the appropriate time, as maps to be guided by in  the performance of an action at the appropriate time.

-------

The above indicates Searle’s conception of knowledge. By replicating in his own mind what the speaker intends to communicate,  thanks to the hearer’s understanding of what the speaker says, the hearer gains the knowledge of what the speaker means or wants to communicate. Thus, according to Searle,  at least in the case of linguistic communication, knowledge consists in the correspondence between the object to be known and what the knower comes to have in mind thanks to his knowing activity. Such, needless to say, is the ordinary notion  of knowledge, which the Scholastics, following Aristotle, defined as adaequatio mentis ad rem –  the conformation of the knower’s mind to the object. What Searle does is to extend to the field of linguistic communication the ordinary  notion of knowledge, that refers primarily to the conformity of the  knowing subject’s mind to the object in the perceptual world, that is, to “reality.” Now it is precisely regarding  this conception of knowledge that Polanyi parts ways with Searle. For Polanyi knowing is not a matter of becoming aware of the object to be known by acquiring a sort of reproduction of it that corresponds to the object, and which is true in the measure in which it corresponds to the object. Rather, knowledge is tacit because it is essentially practical, not theoretical. It does not aim at reproducing the object in the mind, in which case the knowledge of the object would be explicit rather than tacit. Knowledge consists, rather,  in a practical  disposition to ACT in the way appropriate to achieve the knower’s goal. Thus, in the case of the shock-producing syllables, the subject has no awareness of the individual characteristics of the syllables; for that very reason the subject cannot say or express in words which are those characteristics of which he has no visual awareness. What the subject acquires, thanks to his past painful experience of being shocked, is not an image of the syllables, but the practical disposition to avoid the shock, which manifests itself in his bracing himself for the shock when the shock-producing syllables appear again on the screen, in order to protect himself as much as possible from the shock.

Whose conception of the relationship of knowledge to action is right: Searle’s or Polanayi’s?

Regarding those acts in which a perlocutionary effect is intended by the speaker, I have already shown how unpersuasive I find Searle’s view that in every act of communication the speaker intends to produce the knowledge of his communicative intentions –that is, the illocutionary effect–  and that this knowledge suffices to insure the success of the communicative act. For this means, concretely, that when I give you an order, what I intend primarily is that you understand and thereby be informed or acquire factual knowledge that I give you an order, not that you obey my order. Searle is so convinced that in the case of an order what the speaker intends is that his order be understood rather than obeyed that he considers the communicative exchange successful in the case in which the order is not obeyed as long as the order is understood by the hearer.

Why do I think that Searle is wrong on this point? Because this aspect of Searle’s characterization of linguistic exchanges is at odds with the practice of all competent linguistic agents. It is true that I cannot disobey an order without understanding it and thereby knowing it. But what is in contention is what constitutes the appropriate “understanding” of an order and, in general, of all those linguistic acts that possess perlocutionary force, such as threats, warnings, promises, demands, etc. According to Searle, the primary intention of the speaker in all of  them is to inform the hearer of what he means, and only secondarily and accidentally to achieve the perlocutionary effect typical of the particular perlocutionary act, such as obeying it in the case of an order, expecting the fulfillment of the promise in the case of promising, etc.

But the linguistic facts contradict Searle’s opinion. Indeed, Searle’s opinion does not do justice to the practice of linguistic agents concerning the use of the indicative mood, on the one hand, and of the subjunctive and of the imperative moods, on the other. If  Searle were right, there would be no difference in actual use between the grammatical imperative, used in the case of orders, and the grammatical indicative, properly used only when one wants to inform of a fact. Furthermore, the imperative mood  would be totally superfluous,  for imperatives should be understood as  indicatives. Indeed,  if Searle were right, giving an order (in the imperative) would be exactly equivalent to saying or enunciating (in the indicative) that one gives an order. But such is not the case. As I have already argued, when I give an order, I do not say – in the sense of enunciating– something to be merely understood, to wit: that I give an order; rather, I say the order I give in the sense of giving an order to be obeyed. When I say something in the sense of enunciating, I refer my hearer to what I enunciate as something to be understood, that is, I intend to produce in my interlocutor the knowledge in which, according to Searle, the illocutionary effect consists. Therefore, if in giving an order I were enunciating that I give an order and the order I give, I should be satisfied if the addressee responded  “I understand your order.” But that is not the case. When I give an order –and this is why I use the imperative, not the indicative–  I expect and intend my order to be obeyed, which is the appropriate ‘understanding’ of an order by a competent linguistic agent.

Moreover, Searle’s opinion is also at odds with the practice of linguistic agents even in the case of statements. For, as I have already argued, contrary to what Searle thinks, in a real-life situations, by contrast to the artificial classroom situation in which people are learning a second language, linguistic agents do not consider their linguistic action successful if a purely theoretical  understanding of what is said obtains; success, for linguistic agents, is identical with linguistic action. Cf. GEOMETRY AS APPLICATIVE, AS MAP-READING.           

Why does Searle identify saying with enunciating?  Because his linguistic characterizations and, hence, his entire conception of language,  is based not on his own experience as a linguistic agent who actually participates in communicative interactions, but as a curious philosopher who overhears OR READS the words said by those who participate in communicative interactions, or who reflects after the fact on the words said by himself and others when he participated in communicative interactions. I underline “the words said,” to indicate that Searle’s characterization of language, in spite of his disclaimers, focuses on the words said, independently of the conduct and the intentions of those who say or hear them as actual participants in linguistic exchanges. This amounts to identifying linguistic meaning with saying in the sense of enunciating something, and, consequently, it amounts to considering the conduct of the linguistic agents as accidental to linguistic meaning, rather than integral to, or constitutive of, linguistic meaning. Clearly, however, this is not the kind of interiorization through agency through which children learn to play baseball, without being able to enunciate the rules they supposedly follow.

Thus Searle excludes from linguistic meaning anything that in a linguistic exchange is not  words in which something is said or enunciated as to be known. Such, however, is the case with  the actions and affections intended by the speaker in the communicative exchange, which consequently are excluded by Searle from being constitutive elements of linguistic meaning. That, indeed, was the reason why, in his example of the American soldier captured by the Italians, Searle would deny that the action by which the American tricked the Italians into believing that he was a German soldier by uttering the sounds “ Ich komme von dem Land wo die Zitronen  blühen ” was a linguistic action, since the words expressed did not enunciate the action intended.

The exclusion from linguistic meaning of whatever is not explicitly said in words is reflected in the separation Searle postulates between the illocutionary and the perlocutionary effects, as well as in the secondary and merely accidental role he attributes to the latter . Searle himself , in explaining his principle of expressibility that  “whatever can be meant can be said,” implicitly acknowledges that whatever is not said in words cannot be linguistically meant, for he excludes from meaning the perlocutionary effects. ^(F27)

In short, because he identifies linguistic meaning with what is expressed in words that supposedly say or enunciate something to be understood or known, Searle automatically excludes from linguistic meaning those no less linguistic effects that do not consist in words that express something to be understood, namely, the perlocutionary effects. No wonder, then, that  the way in which language functions in the artificial context of a course of a language for foreigners, in which only understanding  what is said counts as linguistic, to the systematic exclusion of any of the affections or actions intended by the speaker, ends up appearing to Searle as the model linguistic situation. Indeed this corresponds to Searle’s defective philosophy of language.

-----

It will not be superfluous to realize that Searle’s linguistic philosophy amounts to an application to the field of language of the Platonic-Aristotelian epistemology inherited by the Western philosophical tradition. In that tradition,  knowledge consists, on the subject’s part,  in the representational conformity of his mind to reality, and, on the object’s part, in the ontological correspondence or conformity to what is in the subject’s mind. By contrast,  Polanyi’s conception of tacit knowledge constitutes an alternative to that tradition, for tacit knowledge is to be used instrumentally by the agent to achieve a desired goal, not a representation of what is the case.

As we shall now see, Polanyi does not contend that the Searlean perlocutionary effects as Searle conceives them, that is, as separated from the illocutionary effect, are a constitutive element of linguistic meaning, for the Serlean conception of the illocutionary element as different from the perlocutionary presupposes the legitimacy of his conceptual distinction between them. Polanyi contends, rather, that this Serlean distinction reflects the separation between the knowing subject and the agent that is inherent to the epistemology inherited from Aristotle and Plato, which begins by reflecting on knowledge as different from and separable form action. The Searlean distinction between illocutionary and perlocutionary effects is possible only on the wrong assumption, common to Aristotle and Plato, that knowledge and action stand to each other in the relationship presupposed in the epistemology inherited from Plato and Aristotle by the Western philosophical tradition.

^(F22) As for the close connect ion of vision with action, that is, of the eyes as instruments for action, nothing is more enlightening than what happens in the case in which a person wears inverting glasses. Typically, the world remains inverted for about two to three days. And then, all of a sudden, in spite of the fact that the person still wears the inverting glasses,  the world seen –against what is to be expected–  redresses itself, and normal action becomes again possible and easy for him. The visual sensations of the subject have shifted to their normal function of instruments for action by being only tacitly known.
