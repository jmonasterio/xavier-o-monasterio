LA COUPURE EPISTEMOLOGIQUE

CRITICAL REASON

Philosophy started in Greece as a quest for certitude, as an attempt to escape from the ongoing circus of conflicting opinions and to control the manipulation of people by the sophists. In the interesting sense of the word, for it is the sense in which philosophy has deeply shaped our culture, philosophy began with Socrates and Plato. The discourse of the pre-Socratics was about the world, and it was innocent, that is, uncritical. The discourse of Socrates, prompted by the desire to act in the way that befits a human being, was critical; it was about and against the products of innocent discourse--the cacophonous variety of conflicting opinions. It opened the way for Plato, whose discourse was about discourse itself, about the conditions of scientific or justified discourse. Plato's discourse produced the first philosophy ever, that is, a view of the totality according to rational conditions and, therefore, rationally justified, that is, certainly true. Such, at least, was the ideal aimed at, though it is abundantly clear from his own evolution that Plato did not see his own philosophy as fully successful.

Later I will deal with the circularity of the project of rational discourse and with the further history of philosophy, which coincides with the historical development of the Greek project across time. For the moment, however, I will proceed to a rapid analysis of the kind of discourse inaugurated by Plato, that is, of philosophical discourse, for it involves the coupure epistemologique that concerns me here. Indeed, Plato's discourse, which includes his philosophy as a product, opens the space of what we have come to identify as "reason," the space within which all the further figures of discourse have to be inscribed in order to qualify as rational, as rationally justified, as rationally acceptable. Now, this 'space of reason' is, precisely, opened thanks to the coupure epistemologique.

Philosophy started with Socrates as a preoccupation with the products of discourse, that is, opinions. It continued with Plato as a preoccupation with discourse itself and the justification of its legitimate products. The examined life is, for Plato, the rational life, the life lived according to the legitimate products of discourse. With Plato, the practical preoccupation of Socrates about the good life remains alive and central, but it is mediated by philosophical discourse. And philosophical discourse is contemplative. It yields the vision of the true world. Human action is based on and follows vision. Discourse, which is contemplative, grounds action. There is, therefore, a primacy of vision over action, of discourse over life, and it involves a possible bifurcation between contemplation and action. Philosophers may come to be the ones who know and who devote their life to the supreme activity of contemplation, while others devote their lives to the inferior task of living and know only in the measure in which philosophers share with them the product oftheir rational discourse. Philosophers are meant to be kings.

This bifurcation between contemplation and action, obscurely but surely involved in Plato's move, together with the necessary primacy of philosophical contemplation is intrinsic to the philosophical project of the Greeks. It is, therefore, intrinsic to the space of reason opened by them, to the implied conception of the universe and of the human situation within it of those who, wittingly or unwittingly, inhabit the space of Greek reason. The consequences of this bifurcation and this primacy will be examined later.

I have spoken of a coupure, of a rupture. Rupture indicates a radical discontinuity. Rupture with what? Certainly with the innocent, uncritical attitude about discourse and its products that prevailed before Socrates. What is important, however, is to realize that the rupture with uncritical discourse actually consecrated discourse--or at least the variety of discourse that philosophers conceived as rational--with an absolute primacy. Indeed, discourse became, for them, our absolute opening to reality. Other people, the Brittish empiricists, would later shift this weight to perception. Variations will abound. What will remain will be the primacy of contemplation--knowledge conceived as contemplation as our absolute opening to reality, as our way of access to reality. Rationality would be identified with knowledge, knowledge with a contemplative activity, and critical (by oposition to innocent) living with criticism of the conditions of contemplation and of its products.

The rupture cannot be fully appreciated but by contrast with what preceded it. To say that rational or critical discourse was preceded by irrational or uncritical discourse amounts already to an adoption of the philosophical perspective within which discourse occupies the central place. Once this perspective defined by the central place of discourse is adopted, then the mode of life by which the philosophical perspective was preceded and in reaction to which it emerged has to be conceived and evaluated in terms of discourse--of the peripheral place occupied by discourse within that mode of life, and of the irrational character of its discourse by contrast to philosophical discourse. What is in contention, however, is the very philosophical perspective within which discourse occupies the place and plays the role it occupies and plays within the philosophical perspective. In other words, I am suggesting that the epistemological rupture to which I am reffering consisted in the radical discontinuity between the mode of life that preceded the critical moment inaugurated by Socrates, on the one hand and, on the other, the space of reason opened by Plato, within which discourse--and philosophical discourse in particular--occupies the central place and plays the absolute role Plato attributed to it.  

The Greek polis was what I call 'a mode of life.' Of course, it involved discourse, in the general way in which I have been using this term. That is, the members of the polis thought, talked to each other, discussed with each other, had conflicting views, attempted to persuade each other, contested the validity of each other's views, rejected the truth of certain statements and accepted the truth of certain others, etc. If this is what we mean by 'discourse,' discourse occupied a place within the Greek mode of life, as it does within any mode of life. But it did not occupy the central place. It was one among many human activities, such as working, waging war, making love, eating, raising children, celebrating religious feasts, playing, etc., etc. Undeniably, all these human activities were pervaded by language and thus, in the very large sense in which I am using the term, were 'discoursive.' But the fact that they were such was not thematized as object of special attention, much less was discourse as such thematized, still less was the validity of discourse itself questioned, and even less was discourse conceived as the supreme, all-embracing, absolute activity to which every other activity was particularly subordinated and by reference to which reality itself was to be gauged, as was to be the case with Plato. By Plato's philosophical standards, those people were naive, unreflective, uncritical, irrational, full of illusions and mere "opinions," and led an unexamined life not worth living. Needless to say, those people lived nonetheless. Furhtermore, they might have argued, not without reason, that the members of the Platonic academy engaged in many other activites apart from discoursing or philosophizing, such as eating, falling in love, making love, attending the theater, exactly as the naive people; that the fact that philosophers accorded such a central place to contemplation neither suppressed, nor dispensed them from, such other activities; and that such other activities were other precisely because they were neither reducible to, nor susceptible of being substituted by, the contemplative activity.

Whatever the value of this naive argument may be, it has at least the merit of suggesting that arguments are possible within, and by reference to, a 'mode of life,' not only within, and by reference to, the space of reason opened by Plato. To the same extent the argument at least indicates the possibility of a conception of rationality that constitutes an alternative to the philosophical conception  of rationality. In fact, or so at least it seems to me, this is the kind of arguments Aristotle uses to 'refute' scepticism as well as those who deny the principle of contradiction. But the point, for the moment, is not to demonstrate anything, but to explain the epistemological rupture by means of a contrast between what preceded it and what followed it. What preceded it was a mode of life, the Greek mode of life in the particular case; what followed it was the space of reason defined by the absolute centrality of contemplative discourse. The rupture lies in the radical discontinuity, in the total heterocliteness (to coin a barbarous neologism) between a mode of life and the space of philosopical reason.   

On purpose do I use a word derived from the word "heteroclite," which indicates a combination of elements which belong not simply to different genera but to radically different frames of reference. Indeed, of such a kind is the difference between a mode of life and the space of philosophical reason. For, a mode of life takes place within an inhabitable space, by definition, since a mode of life is a mode of life of people, whereas the space of philosophical reason is as uninhabitable as, say, a bidimensional space would be to tridimensional figures, the reason for this being that the space of philosophical reason is the space of an explanatory system, and people cannot inhabit such space. To make myself clear, though, on such complex matter, a roundabout way is required.

A rapid sketch of the history of philosophy as I understand it might help us. What Socrates was really in search of was the good life, the life that befits a human being. He wanted to live as a human being ought to. Unfortunately, even tragically, he mistook his own search for a theoretical search. He thought that it was a matter of finding the secret of true definitions and, then, of reaching the true definition, that is, the knowledge of, the good life. For Socrates, then, the good life was--of course, in a very obscure way--either identical with the good life or the immediate, automatic result of the knowledge of the good life. In his view, the person who knew the good life would live the good life. As for himself, he only knew that he knew nothing, since he did not know the good life. The perilous confusion of reality with the knowledge of reality was already at work.

Plato, his disciple, continued the Socratic search for the good life, with the same passionate desire of coming to live it in full certainty and unerringly and of making the same possible for others. For him, too, it was a matter of reaching the knowledge of the good life. But for Plato knowledge was not anymore simply a matter of definitions but of rational justifications, of rational grounds. And the good  life itself had to be placed within the totality of which it was an element. Each of the elements had to be rationally grounded and its connections with the other elements had to be made intelligible so that the totality itself be fully intelligible. Thus the quest for the good life which had begun as a quest for the knowledge (definition) of the good life had become a quest for the knowledge (justification and intelligibility) of the whole of reality. Despairing of the full intelligibility and reality of the changing visible world, Plato grounded it on a tenuous participation in the hierarchical, fully intelligible universe of the ideal forms, the contemplation of which was reserved to the lovers of wisdom and made them the kings of a polis that replicated in the tenuous, derived way possible in the temporal world the eternal order of the world of the forms.

Aristotle was less ambitious. He discarded the universe of ideal forms and replaced intelligibility in the world of perception which he made nevertheless eternal in order to avoid total unintelligibility and unreality. He took for granted, as a given, the reality of the world. His search was for the intelligibility of the given universe, a universe which needed no foundation as to its reality anymore than Plato's universe of ideal forms needed a foundation. More pragmatic than Plato, he accepted the limitations imposed by the nature of the multiplicity of objects to the explanatory project of Plato that was to reveal the intelligibility of the totality. Thanks to his theory of abstraction and its degrees, he found room for necessity and universality--the true marks of intelligibility--within the field of physical and mathematical objects as well as within the field of bieng (the "theoretical" sciences. He found room for a universality "for the most part" within the field of human action the "practical" sciences), that is, of the good life understood as the good agent--the person who knows how to act rightly by acting rightly, that is, by actually pursuing the good in the given circumstances. He found room for a certain universality and rational necessity of still another kind within the field of the "poetic" sciences, the sciences of inventive or creative discourse--poetic and rhetoric. And he found no room whatsoever for universality and necessity within the field of history--the realm of the purely contingent and changing without regularity. To all this Aristotle added, apart from his own excursions into the field of the factual or positive (by contrast to "what we say"), a Platonic theory of the ideal science or certain knowledge (an achieved science) strongly inspired by geometry, in the Posterior Analytics.

A few remarks on Aristotle are important. (1) The Platonic dream of full intelligibility, of a unified deductive system embracing all reality is almost completely gone under the weight of the conditions imposed by the objects of the different fields. The dream survives only in the theory of the ideal science in which episteme presides. Aris realizes that such ideal is realizable, if at all, only within the field of the theoretical sciences and abstracting from concrete matter and, therefore, from actual existence, though its verification is furnished by actual existents. (2) Whether or not Aris realizes it, his three different sciences are radically heterogeneous in that they belong in different and unrelated levels of discourse. For instance, his theory of abstraction does not apply at all in the field of action or ethics, which is the field of exposure and within which the good is the genuinely desirable. Nor does the theory of abstraction apply to the poetic sciences, the sciences of creative discourse. There is, therefore, no possibility of reducing Aristotle's views to a unified, hierarchical, intelligible system, much less to a system that corresponds to his ideal of an achieved science. (3) The bifurcation between contemplation and action is already clear. There is no theoretical or purely contemplative knowledge of action. Action has to do with the genuinely good to be done, not with the true to be assented to. At the same time, by considering the good agent from a sufficiently general point of view to render possible the universality "for the most part" that is required for scientific discourse to obtain, Aris's Ethics is theoretical. It is, at best, knowledge of orthopraxis, not knowledge that results in orthopraxis. This Aris realizes, since he grants that only the good agent would assent to his views on the good and the good person, and, on the other hand, the good person is the one who does not need the theory and from whose very praxis the theory originates.

I think that the Platonic dream of a unified system of reality is already outspent with Aristotle. This may have been realized by the practical scepticism and sophistry that continued unabatted after him in the pagan world. But it was not realized by the medievals who in the thirteenth century, thanks to the solidity of their Christian faith, resuscitated and reinvigorated Aristotle. They readopted, at least in theory, Aris's Platonic ideal of the completed science, that is, the rational philosophical project of the Greeks. At the same time, by the very nature of their Christian undertaking, they had to coordinate faith and reason. Since their project was primarily theological, they objectified their faith as statements to be assented to, without realizing that on the level of the stated they had submitted their faith, which was reduced to statements, to the level of mere propositions to be attained either through faith or through reason or through both. With their naive realism, which was shared by those who submitted themselves fully to the all-embracing character of the Greek philosophical project and of the Platonic Aristotelian ideal of the achieved science reached through apodictic evidence, they condemned themselves to lose the battle against rationalism.
