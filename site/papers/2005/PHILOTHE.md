Philotheo Chantilly,May 25, 93

Faith and Reason: A Medieval Debate

and the devaluation of narrative literature

In his highly influential study La theologie comme science su XIIIe siecle M.-D.Chenu followed step by step the development of theology as a scientific discipline from the twelfth to the thirteenth century. Whether theology could indeed qualify as a science emerged explicitly as a question around 1230, in connection with the recently rediscovered Aristotelian notion of scientific knowledge. The question was hotly debated among theologians for a number of years, all of them inclining to a negative answer. In fact, if we are to believe Chenu, it was only some thirty years after the explicit formulation of the question that Thomas Aquinas for the first time answered it affirmatively and for reasons that ended up in the long term rallying the agreement of most everybody to his answer.

It is not my present purpose to dispute whether Chenu is right in attributing this achievement to Aquinas or to evaluate the merits or demerits of an achievement for which Chenu has only praise. My very limited project consists in rendering intelligible the negative position and the objections of those predecessors of Aquinas who found it not only impossible but positively undesirable to transform theology into a scientific endeavor according to the Aristotelian model. After all, whether they were right or wrong, their strong reluctance to move in the direction that would end up by prevailing should prove enlightening as to a conception of the world that the ultimate triumph of Aristotelianism has rendered practically invisible to us.

An impressionistic taste of the nature of the medieval debate

Unlearning to see the world as we have learned to see it is extraordinarily difficult. Yet, this is the exercise in which we have to engage if the world inhabited by Aquinas' predecessors is to become visible and comprehensible to us. For this extraordinarily difficult purpose I will attempt something meant to give us, in an impressionistic way, a taste of the real nature of the medieval debate.

What we generally understand by thelogy is something that has very little to do with what the medievals understood by the same name. For us, theology is primarily an academic discipline that is, a field of study among the many others that find a place in our universities, with its own relatively well defined subject-matter and its more or less distinctive methods. At least, then, in this very general sense we are "Aristotelians," for precisely what was at stake in the dispute whether theology was a science--" an theologia sit scientia " was whether theology, given its nature, could be an academic discipline among others that deserve a place

in institutions that see themselves as scientific, a place that according to most of us today alchemy or astrology, for instance, do not deserve. In other words, in that Aquinas' predecessors answered negatively the question whether theology was or even could be ascience, they were "non-Aristotelians," unlike Aquinas and unlike us.

Obviously, then, the people who interest us here had a very different idea of theology from ours. Indeed, they did. In fact, the most obvious meaning that the word " theologia " as they understood and used it, and as it had been previously understood and used for centuries, was "the Bible." Indeed, the most common way in which they raised the question whether theology was a science was " an sacra pagina sit scientia ," which roughly translated amounts to asking "whether the Bible is a scientific book." Clearly, nobody today would stretch the meaning of the word 'scientific' to embrace the Bible within it. We might well ask whether the Bible may be studied scientifically, or from the scientific point of view, but not whether the Bible itself is a scientific book, that is, a book (or a collection of books) that deals with matters in a scientific way, or that intends to convey scientific truths. Yet, this is the question that the irruption of the Aristotelian scientific model forced the medieval theologians to confront.

Needless to say, our contemporary notion of what constitutes "a science" and of the method or methods that can be properly called "scientific" differs very significantly from Aristotle's own. In this sense, most certainly, we are not Aristotelians. Suppose, however, that we took the word "science" to mean a field of study and a subject-matter that deserves a place among the subjects studied in a university, and the word 'scientific' to mean both an academically acceptable method to proceed in that field as well as the results achieved through the use of that method when applied to that subject-matter. Then the question whether theology is an academic discipline would be equivalent to the question whether theology is a science. And, given that theology unlike, say, astrology or alchemy, has a generally recognized place in our universities, we would answer affirmatively the question whether theology is a science. Assume now that Aristotle meant by 'scientific' what we mean by 'academic,' and that he meant by 'science' a field of study and a subject-matter worthy of being studied in universities. Then we would be "Aristotelians."

So far we have been understanding the word "theology" roughly in the way in which it is understood today in our world, namely as a particular field of study and a subject-matter that may have its particular method but which shares with all the other disciplines worthy of being studied in a university its

concern to attain the truth on the basis of evidence rather than of authority, or of mere emotion, or on any other basis than evidence. Certainly, the rules and the nature of evidence is not the same in physics, in economics, in history, and in theology.

However, in that each of these disciplines, unlike astrology or alchemy, is concerned to attain the truth on the exclusive basis of evidence, we consider them 'scientific' and therefore worthy of being studied in universities.

But now suppose that we use the same word "theology" to designate the field of what has to do with the God of Christianity and with the Christian religion in general, and this field not exclusively as field of study for purposes of attaining the truth but also of religious commitment and of devotion, and the method to be employed in this field to be religious faith grounded on God's authority rather than evidence. To complicate matters a little more, let us assume that this pseudo-discipline called theology is such that by its very nature it demands these of the Bible as its fundamental textbook because it represents concretely God's revelation. Moreover,this pseudo-discipline demands that its practitioners take the word of the Bible as indisputably true because infallible and as normative in matters of truth, so that whatever assertion that either explicitly or implicitly contradicts the word of the Bible is to be judged as automatically false. Obviously, not only would this pseudo-discipline be totally different from the one practiced in our universities, but we would banish such "theology" from our universities because it is not a 'scientific discipline,' whatever else it may be.

Though this is a gross oversimplification, I believe that the preceding description gives an approximately correct idea of what the medievals practiced as, and  more or less obscurely understood by the name of, "theology" before the study of Aristotle's works confronted them with the inescapable problem they articulated under the question " an theologia sit scientia ."

The nature of their problem will remain insufficiently defined, even for my merely impresionistic purpose, until we form some idea of the way in which the encounter with Aristotle's works impinged on their notion of "theology." Before we come to that, however, it will be profitable to get some taste, as it were, of the problem they were to encounter. As I have said, we would expel their "theology" from our universities as a pseudo-discipline and as pseudo-scientific, much as we would consider completely out of place in a course of theology a strictly fundamentalist approach to the study of the Bible, mixed with moments of religious contemplation on biblical pasages and of mutual encouragement to observe the ten commandments. That we would be right in so doing is something that for us is beyond dispute. And right we may well be, for all I care now. But my point is that the kind of theology they practiced at the beginning of the eleventh century in their best schools, the very schools that were in the process of becoming the Universities of Paris and of Oxford, corresponded roughly to the description I

have given, and that they would have expelled from their schools

our kind of theology with at least the same passion and the same unshakable conviction of being right in doing so as we would expel theirs from our modern universities. If we are incapable of accepting this fact, we will be unable to appreciate the nature of the problem with which the medieval theologians were confronted with the irruption of the corpus of the Aristotelian works.

Now to their problem, as they lived it, given their notion of theology.

Applying Frei

Writing about 1250 (?), the Franciscan Friar Roger Bacon bitterly regretted the deep changes that had taken place in the theological schools during his own lifetime.

Not much later (?), Thomas Aquinas, by contrast, celebrated and defended the same changes as an enormous progress in the theological practice.

Clearly, what was in contention between these two men was the nature itself of theology, of which they had two very different conceptions. For Bacon, the fundamental textbook in the schools of theology ought to be the Bible. Genuine theology is primarily a commentary of the Bible read as a narrative book or a sequential collection of narrative books. Such is the way in which the Bible used to be read in the schools of theology, a way still to be found in certain places that consitute a rare exception. Thus, the substitution of the Bible as a textbook by commentaries on Lombard's Liber Sententiarum is a pernicious corruption. For, though such commentaries and Lombard's own book of Sententiae appeal to the Bible as their ultimate and indisputable authority, they deal with questions which have nothing to do with the biblical narratives as such, for they are merely speculative questions independent from the biblical text itself and whose answer contributes nothing to the understanding of the world into which the text itself projects--and is meant to project--the reader.

Aquinas, by contrast...

Where Aquinas sees progress, Bacon sees a regrettable deviation. These are normative terms that reflect their different conceptions of theology. Each of the two men sees the changes in function of his own notion of theology. If theology is indeed what Bacon believes it to be, then the changes in the theological schools are a pernicious deviation. On the contrary, if theology is what Aquinas believes it to be, then the changes represent an undeniable progress. But is there not a neutral, superior, non-evaluative poit of view from which the genuine nature of theology can be discovered and by appeal to which their difference can be settled? In fact, there is no such a point of view, for any point of view taken implies an assumption of the nature of its object and of the appropriate way of dealing with it, which is precisely what is at stake. Indeed, only because Aquinas assumes that theology is what he believes it to be does he dismiss the narrative reading of the text itself of the Bible as the appropriate method for theology, and only because Bacon assumes that theology is what he believes it to be does he think that modus historicalis is magis proprius theolgiae. There is, however, an important difference between Aquinas and Bacon, between the Augustinian and the Aristotelian. Aquinas positively thinks that there is such a superior, value-neutral point of view, namely, the point of view of "reason," and that his own conception of theology is correct because it corresponds to that point of view, whereas Bacon does not even suspect that both his own and Aquinas' positions imply points of view, that the implied points of view are totally different, and that the supposed neutrality and consequent superiority of Aquinas' point of view are in fact a myth. Curiously enough, the shift from Augustinianism to Aristotelianism in theology rests on the assumption that the point of view of "reason" is value-neutral and ultimate , and such shift is only one among many others--the distinction between philosophy and theology and the birth of the schools of philosophy being one of them--of the acceptance of that myth as true. Once that myth is adopted, however, since it is ultimate and all-embracing, it becomes the tribunal by whose standards the theological practice defined by Bacon's point of view as well as the point of view itself will be judged and ineluctably condemned , since it is different from Aquinas' theological practice defined by the point of view of reason.

Does this mean that relativism and, ultimately, scepticism are in order, since there is no possible appeal to a superior tribunal? No, because, contrary to the postulate of Aristotelianism, contemplation is subservient to practice and meant to enhance practice--something which "reason" cannot do.

But this is a further issue with which I do not want to deal now.   It is my thesis that the transformation undergone by theology between the eleventh and the twelfth centuries can be best understood as a passage from what Frei calls "a narrative reading" of the Bible to a reading of a totally different kind. In other words, Augustinian, that is pre-Aristotelian theology, was grounded in a narrative reading of the Bible, whereas Aristotelian theology was grounded in what might be called a scientific reading of the Bible. Now, if this is the case, and if Frei is right--as I believe he is--in asserting that such a passage entails the loss of the central meaning of the Bible as a narrative, then we have to conclude that whatever else theology may have gained by becoming scientific it lost nothing less than the possibility of dealing with the central meaning of the Bible.

If my thesis that Frei's interpetive grill can be rightly applied to the case at hand is to hold, I have to show that the adoption of Aristotelianism brought about: (1) A detachment of the world (the real world, independently accessible through reason) from the biblical world; this made possible--even imperative, when Aristotelianism gained full sway--to contest the truth of biblical stories understood as statements of historical "facts". (2) A consequent loss of the historico-dramatic unity of the canon. (3) A consequent loss of the possibility of situating oneself within that dramatic history.

According to Frei, there is a totally different comprehension of the Bible by the end of the eighteenth century, that makes impossible what was possible and normal (normative) before, and makes possible and normal what was impossible or inconceivable before. In the process, the narrative reading of the realistic portions of the Bible got lost, which Frei attempts to retrieve from their eclipse.

A very similar event took place between the XII and XIIIth, except that the incompatibility between the two comprehensions was only obscurely sensed by the participants and provisorily hidden by a so-called synthesis of faith and reason.

What prompted the passage from one to the other was the encounter of the Christian with the Aristotelian comprehension of the world. Different from the former in this, the latter included a self-justifying, explicit theoretical bagage concerning the nature of reality and of the knowledge of reality, and ended up prevailing, initially in the schools and later in the Western culture with the Enlightenment.

Whatever the gain brought about by such cultural revolution may have been, one of the things that (if Frei's thesis is right, as I think it is) got lost in the process was the significance of a realistic reading of the realistic narrative portions of the Bible. Because truth became identified with the truth of statements and reality with the formally intelligible, a consequent devaluation of 'literature' took place (of which Plato's banishment of the poets as liers is an echo). Frei focuses in particular on the effects of this shift in the area of realistic narrative literature, of which significant portions of the Bible are examples. After the shift, those portions were devalued as conveyors of truth. They could, at best, convey truth once their meaning was cashed in terms of statements, for only under the form of statements could their truth enter the Aristotelianly shped theology. Yet, if Frei is right, realistic narratives convey a meaning that is inseparable from its narrative form, so that the transformed truths that entered theology were not the truths of the biblical narratives. (In fact, realistic narratives, when historic, do not call for intellectual assent). [By "realistic" meaning and reading Frei does not mean "historically true" meaning and reading. In fact, he brackets the question of historical truth or falsity concerning those passages, much as one might methodically bracket the historical truth or falsity of such a realistic novel as Madame Bovary, which would amount to bracketing the question whether the Bible means and is to be understood as a realistic novel or as a fundamentally historical book. (WHETHER THIS BRACKETING ITSELF CAN BE PRACTICED AT ALL, OR CAN BE PRACTICED WITHOUT SIGNIFICANT LOSS IN THE PARTICULAR CASE OF THE BIBLE IS HIGHLY DISPUTABLE, THOUGH I WANT TO LEAVE THE QUESTION OPEN FOR THE MOMENT)]. Such loss, according to Frei, amounts to missing the the meaning of Jesus as a human character, for it is conveyed by narratives in which the depiction itself and what is depicted are inseparable. If this is correct, then the transition from Augustinianism to Aristotelianism entailed the same loss for the medieval theologians, who were Christians and could not have failed to sense such loss.

Was that the case actually? I think so. This paper attempts to show that it was. Without being able to put his finger as clearly as Frei on the significance of the loss, Bacon's lamentation syntesizes the loss and its significance. However, Bacon was by no means the only one to sense the loss. In fact, all of Aquinas' predecessors seem to have sensed the loss that was about to occur, that was already occurring before their eyes, and refused Aristotelianism.

This paper attempts to show that the objections raised by the Augustinians make full sense and are worthy of serious consideration when couched in terms of Frei's theory of narrative meaning, that they are in fact obscure appeals to the narrative meaning and its irreducibility to Aristotelian meaning, that they constitute a protest against the validity of a theology that substitutes Aristotelian meaning for narrative meaning.

Their objections may be classified as follows : speech acts, genre of passages (narrative genre in particular), and horizon of the reader. Or, rather, they manifest implicitly their customary reading of the Bible and the effects it produces on them and is meant to produe on them as theologians.

Defining my question

So far I have attempted to explain the problem encountered by the medieval theologians. As we know, all of them before Aquinas rejected Aristotelianism. Their fundamental reason was religious in character, deeply connected with their Christian faith. If the Aristotelian notion of science, inextricably correlated with his notion of reason, was correct, then their faith was wrong. True, Aquinas became convinced and came later to convince most other people that a harmonious coexistence and even a mutual complementarity between faith and reason was possible thank to his synthesis. But this came later. For the people we are concerned with it was a matter of either-or. Given this, their choice was clear: Aristotle had to go.

The opposition's resistance

For the moment I want to do justice to the people we are concerned with abstracting from theological matters. In other words, my point is that, on the level of the humanities , their objections carry more water than they were given credit for at the time and are given credit for today.  

According to Frei, in the eighteenth century a whole way of reading-understanding the Bible was lost, or at least got forgotten, which he intends to rescue because he considers it fundamental in a certain way. (Though it got never quite lost, for it remained in the practice of the Protestant churches). Why it is fundamental he never explains.
