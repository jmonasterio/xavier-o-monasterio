November 24,1992

Dear Father Jim:

I just received your letter on my paper together with your paper on Moral Development of College Students. Thanks for both.

I already read both, but I want to reread, and reflect a little more, on your paper before reacting to it. For the moment, all I want to say concerning the latter is that you and I are closer in the way we think than it might seem. Part of what I was reacting against in my paper on CIT is a version of it at whose heart is the already consummated divorce between the intellect and the heart and which< think, contributed to lead us to the Enlightenment and the sequels that you acknowledge and regret in your paper.

I sincerely hope that, as you say in your letter, "we shall talk about all this and more the next time we meet." In other words, I hope that there will be such a time, for I have the impression that the pressures of your job and other obligations have cut you too much from the world of friendship--of informal, unguarded, confident sharing and reflection with those who love you--and that this is doing you no good.

Before I finish, I want to let you know that today, not unreluctantly, I accepted to receive the Lackner award. Not unreluctantly, because the mantle is entirely too large for my small size. I feel more humbled than honored, but Brother Matthews argued that I should accept the award for the good of the University. Since I am sure that in one way or another you were very instrumental in this business, I suppose I should thank you for this friendly treason. There!

With love,
