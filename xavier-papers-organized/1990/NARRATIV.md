READING A NARRATIVE

Scio cui credidi.

Se en manos de quien estoy.

I blame the Scholastics for having read the Bible as a series of statements, not as a narrative. What do I mean thereby, and what was lost in the process?

Aquinas--to take him as representative of the Scholastics who interest me here, namely the ones who carried out the revolution of paradigms that took place between the XIIth and the XIIIth centuries--believed in the Bible as God's word. What the Bible said was, for him, true. But the particular notion of truth with which he worked as a theologian was Aristotelian. Truth was a predicate or quality of "judgments," whose natural expression is statements. If, therefore, the Bible was true, it had to consist in statements or in expressions translatable into statements. An instance of the latter would be a parable; though itself couched in narrative terms to facilitate its understanding by the rude or to make possible the understanding of what lies as such beyond our human capaicity, its message is convertible into a statement.

Correlative of this understanding of the Bible is Aquinas' understanding of revelation as God's knowledge--God's judgments to be assented to by us-- and of faith as assent to God's statements .

That the Bible, since it was true, had to be, and actually was, a series of statements expressing judgments was taken for granted by Aquinas, under Aristotle's influence. But what went unquestioned by Aquinas and seemed to him unquestionable is quite questionable for me. And I want to question it.

The Bible as a narrative

On its surface, at least, the Bible is a narrative. I do not mean that each and every portion of the Bible is narrative, which would be a foolish claim. But I will later contend that the Bible is fundamentally a narrative, in that only by reference to its narrative portions can its not narrative ones-- legislative, exhortative, didactic, etc.--be properly understood.

As my primary example of what I mean by narrative I will take the portion of Genesis that tells of Isaac's sacrifice by Abraham.

The episode told has a certain unity, namely, the unity of a dramatic episode that moves temporally and dramatically to its final outcome. What confers to the passage its unity is the dramatic connection of each of its elements, all of which are there in function of the outcome. Furthermore, whether or not the reader believes in the historicity of the passage, the reader is moved by it, suffers with Abraham and is relieved with him by the final outcome of the episode. Now, suppose that to someone who has never read the Bible and has no idea of the biblical God I make the statement "Abraham, a Jewish, was willing to sacrifice his son to his God." To me, who am familiar with the Bible, the statement is an allusion to the whole story, though I am not moved by the statement in the way I am moved by the narrative, much as I may be moved by a movie I see but not by a summary of, or an allusion to, the movie. My only point, for the moment, is that, although a statement may summarize a story, it is a poor substitute for the story in two ways: First, the summary works as a summary only for someone who is familiar with the story itself. (Indeed, for someone who is totally unfamiliar with the story, it gives only one aspect, however important, of the whole story, and an aspect that is by itself compatible with very different stories. For instance, that Abraham was willing to sacrifice his son is compatible with a story in which Abraham refused to sacrifice Isaac when he realized that he had to eat his son). Second, that the statement lacks the moving character of the story itself.

I could, however, by means of a series of statements give a fairly complete summary of Abraham's story to someone unfamiliar with it. One finds this sort of summaries of 'the argument' or plot of a play or a novel in certain books of history of literature. Still my summary would be compatible with a number of very different stories which would have the same 'argument' or plot. One can think, for instance, of the endless series of very different films about Anna Karenina that would result from the knowledge of a mere summary of Tolstoy's book. Even in the case of a fairly complete summary, though, the summary, that is, the series of statements, would not move the reader as the actual story does. This is to say, that the moving quality of narratives, their capacity to move our emotions, depends on the narrative genre itself.

I can hazard, in passing, a hypothesis concerning the moving quality of narratives. Narratives move us because of their dramatic character, and they possess such a character because they present the dramatic experience lived by a character or characters, where the drama lies in the tension between the outcome or outcomes desired by the character or characters and the actual, final outcome. Let this stand as a hypothesis to be developed later or somewhere else. What is important now is that no statement or series of statements can, as such, provoke the emotional response characteristic of narratives. In this sense, at least, statements are not only a poor substitute for a narrative, but no genuine substitute--unless we assume that the moving quality of narratives is accidental to their 'message.'

But can we make that assumption safely at least concerning the biblical narratives? Let us see.

The dramatic character of narratives

As I have explained elsewhere, the author of a narrative does not have to decide to confer a dramatic quality to its work. If he narrates, his work is dramatic, unavoidably. Why? Because it tells the experience of an agent who, confronted with the world, desires a particular outcome, his ' peripecies' ( peripatheia ), and the actual outcome which may or may not be the one desired by the agent. If you have a sensitive being, that is, capable of being affected by what happens to it, surrounded by a world on which he makes demands and which demands his response, you have a dramatic situation. And if, as in the case of an adult human being, you have a definitely intentional agent, that is, an agent who pursues or actively tries to bring about through intentional action the desired outcome, you have the dramatic situation typical of narratives. Because narratives deal with such agents as they confront the surrounding world, narratives are essentially and unavoidably dramatic. And because they are so, they are essentially and unavoidably moving, that is, provoke the emotional response of the reader. This is what statements cannot do. Because statements, by their nature, are not presentifications of the actual encounter of an agent with the surrounding world. Statements are 'about,' they are not presentifications, as narratives are .

Let us now make the not necessarily farfetched assumption that part and parcel of what the author of a narrative wants to communicate to the reader is the experience lived by the character or characters involved in the narrative. Can this be communicated otherwise than through a narrative, that is, a dramatic presentification of the events in which the character participated and their outcome?

Certainly, the author may have his or her personal agenda. He may narrate for didactic purposes (as in a parable), or for purposes of persuading (a lawyer making a vivid narration of a crime to have the accused condemned, and so on). Narratives can indeed be used and produced for many different purposes. But whatever his personal purposes, the author narrates, and good narratives move. The emotional response of the readers or hearers is precisely the means used by the author to achieve his own purposes, for those purposes could not be achieved without such a response . There is an ad on TV, in which a girl who uses a special perfume is followed admiratively by all the boys she comes across. Obviously, the purpose of the add is to make the girls who watch it desire to use the perfume advertised. In a way, the ad is a statement about the desirability of the perfume because of its appeal on boys. But the point of the ad is to make the viewer desire the perfume, to provoke actual desire of the perfume on her part by presentifying its effects on boys . Now, what is presentified? The emotional response of the boys to the girls who use the perfume. By emotional response I mean an action which is the expression not only of what an agent perceives or thinks, but of what an agent feels and wills in response to an object .

We are so used to the traditional distinction between perceiving and acting that we may have trouble reconciling ourselves with the notion of emotional response. We tend to conceive of perceiving as identifying an object by its sensible qualities: what can be touched, seen, smelled, heard, and tasted. The perceiver identifies as a dog what has the shape, the solidity, and the smell of a dog, makes the sounds dogs make, and moves the way dogs move. Then come the emotions: certain objects perceived are accompanied by or produce certain emotions in the perceiver. Thus, a dog that approaches me barking furiously makes me  feel fear. Finally, concerning action, certain actions follow certain perceptions which are accompanied by certain emotions. Thus, my perception of dog accompanied by the feeling we call fear is followed by the action of running. But this picture hardly does justice to the facts.

There are not three neatly distinct things: perceiving, feeling, and acting. The three are internally and dynamically interrelated and flow, so to say, into each other. I do not first perceive and identify something as a dog according to its sensible qualities. I perceive a furious dog running toward me. I do not just have an emotion that feels like fear and is different from other emotions. My fear is fear of this furious dog; it is not just a feeling, but a feeling referred to the dog and to nothing else else than the dog. And my action is not just something that happens to follow the presence of a certain feeling in me. It is action in response to the furious dog; it expresses both my fear of the dog and my desire to escape from it and it incarnates my project of finding myself safe from the dog's attack. Together with the final outcome (in this case, of my attempt to escape from the dog) perception-emotion-action-project-outcome constitue an episode.

What a narrator narrates is an episode or a series of episodes interconnected by a final outcome. The narrator does not talk about characters, events, and outcome. He renders them present by witnessing them post-factum. Thus, the narrator already knows the outcome, which the participants did not know.

Since the reader does not know the outcome either, the reader becomes, through the narrator, a contemporary witness.

It is important to unpack the notion of witness. Consider the legal witness. He is both, someone who witnessed the action contemporarily, and who gives witness about the witnessed. As a contemporary witness, he makes no statements. He just understands  and empathizes with the agents on the basis of  their intentional actions, their effects, and the agents' further actions in response to those effects, and so on, until the final outcome. The contemporary witness understands the character through his actions, their effects, and his further actions. Unerstanding the character amounts to understanding the outcome pursued by the character, given the situation. What is understood by the witness is the "event," which is constituted by the actions-character on his surrounding world, and the actions-character-or-non-character of the surrounding world on the agent, through the anticipation of the character's goal and the counterfinality of the surrounding world . The counterfinality of the world acquires its counterfinal character in relation to the goal(s) pursued by the character.Thus, there is no dramatic understanding by a witness that is not agent-centered, though the center of finality may in a narration shift successively from one agent to another. The witness as such understands dramatiocally.

Now, while the character is understood, the purpose of the character is not understanding but realizing, bringing about the desired outcome, making the desired outcome real. In other words, the character as participant is not a "to be understood" but "a to be realized through action." This is important because it will help us to avoid the dangerous confusion between reality and text. Reality to be understood functions as text and is object of hermeneutics, but reality as lived and encountered neither is nor functions as text contemporarily. More precisely, perhaps, the surrounding world of an agent is for the agent, as subject and object of exposure, object of practical understanding, of what can be done through practical transformation of it, whereas a text is merely to be understood, not to be transformed. We might appreciate this in the shift of a witness into a participant, where reality as a present state of affairs acquires the shape of to be transformed.- As for the witness, not as contemporary but post factum, as giving witness, he is a historical narrator, or at least a furnisher of pieces to be integrated in a narrative, for only through the medium of a narrative can the  event be understood and theagent judged. Insofar as the witness witnesses before others and for others, his narrative becomes a statement of what happens. But that a narrative becomes a statement is not inherent to the nature of narrative; it results from the particular purpose for which the narration is made.

I return to Aquinas , whom I grudge for having misread the biblical narratives as statements, which they are not.To make my claim persuasive I need to make it a lot more precise.   

There seem to be three different, though interconnected, aspects to my grudge: (l) Aquinas reduced the Bible to Aristotelian statements, which narratives are not. (2) Insofar as the biblical narratives are statements of witnesses (not of contemplators) they are susceptible of truth, but their truth is not the truth of judgments, as Aquinas assumed. (3) Because narratives are primarily narratives--dramatic narratives--rather than statements, because they presentify rather than stating about, their essential element is the presence of the exposed agent , not of the unexposed witness. This implies that Aquinas is wrong in taking his unexposed presence as a pure knower for the exposed presence of the agent, which leads him to confuse faith as consent with faith as assent. I will consider these points in inverse order, starting with the last.

(3) Narratives presentify the exposed agent .

What distinguishes the narrative genre from all others is that it presentifies the agent exposed to the world and the world to which he is exposed. All the statements about the agent and the setting are subordinated to that presentification and acquire their proper meaning only in terms of that presentification. The presentification is dramatic in that it moves from a desired outcome to an actual outcome (given the situation) which may or may not coincide withe the one desired. The spontaneous liking we all have for stories is due to the fact that its dramatic structure coincides with the structure of our life as agents (though the agent's concern often becp,es. on the reacder's part, mere curiosity aboput the final outcome. Though a narrative expresses the understanding of an agent and his world by a post-factum witness, a narrative as such is not a statement but a dramatic presentification. In this sense, narratives of imaginary and of real events do not differ. Only when the narrator becomes witness in the legal sense does the narrative become a statement. Even then, though, a narrative is dramatic statement, not Aristotelian statement.

Before coming to the issue of statements, however, we need to consider that which is presentified by a narrative, namely, the exposed agent's agency and passion. The agent is not narrating; he is acting, inter-acting with his world. When God asked Abraham to sacrifice Isaac, He did not state anything; He made a demand to which Abraham was not expected to respond by assenting--after all, there was nojudgment to assent to--but by consenting. The faith that made of Abraham the father of the faithful did not consist in assenting to the statement that God had demanded him to sacrifice Abraham, for there was no such statement, but in consenting to the God who demanded that sacrifice. By consent I mean, in this case, Abraham willingness to obey God's command, incarnated and manifested in his actions. As underscored in one of the Gospels, the son who says "No" to his father's demand but executes the demand is closer to consent than the son who says "yes" and does not execute the demand.

This brings us back to Aquinas. For him, faith is assent,

an intellectual act based on God's authority as a knower. Certainly, this was not Abraham's faith. But is it ours? Was it Aquinas' faith?

Undoubtedly, if asked after the events, Abraham would have assented to the statement that God commanded him to sacrifice Isaac, and that he obeyed God's commandment. Exactly as we believers do. But that is not the faith that saved Abraham or that saves us. That is just a contemplator's (or perhaps a post-factum witness's) judgment. The faith that saves us is consent to the same demanding God' of Abraham, the same willingness (at least in aspiration) to obey to Him whatever His demands on us. This consent is, therefore, of the order of exposure, the willingness to obey of someone who lives exposed to God--on whom God has made demands and will make demands in the future. And Aquinas' faith as a faithful was of this kind too.

Think of Clamance, in Camus' The Fall . His case might enlighten us as to the nature of consent. He heard the cry of the falling girl as a call, that is, he realized that he was called to help. It was not a matter of first hearing a cry as a mere fact and then realizing that it was a call; it was in his very experiencing himself called by the cry of the girl that Clamance understood the cry as a call. Thus, consent is not blind; it involves understanding, but the understanding involved is not of the purely intellectual order, but of the order of exposure and action. And it does not terminate in this experiential, practical understanding but in active response; understanding it as a personal call and refusing to respond appropriately to it is denying practically one's own understanding, making the call not to have the reality-value of a call but of a mere cry.

Aquinas' faith as a faithful was consent too, not assent; it was of the order of exposure, not of mere contemplation. But could Aquinas have acquired that faith without direct, or at least indirect, reference to the biblical narratives? In other words, COULD AQUINAS HAVE REACHED FAITH BASED ON STATEMENTS RATHER THAN NARRATIVES? (I already  set the basis to answer this question in "Theology as a Science, II, pp. 7-16) .

This has to do with GOD'S SELF-REVELATION THROUGH INTERACTION, NOT THROUGH STATEMENTS.(On this point see my "theology as a Science, II, pp. 7-16) .

To clarify further the two issues just mentioned in the two preceding paragraphs I will return to the point of THE PRIMACY OF CONSENT OVER ASSENT. On p. 29 of "Assent and Consent" I wrote:

"When the medievals read the Bible, they took what the Bible said for statements, that is, for objects (of consideration in view) of assent , not of consent. Most certainly, however, when Abraham was called to sacrifice Isaac, he was not related to God as a thinker to an object of thought; he was exposed to God. This is why Abraham did not take God's words for a statement; he heard them as an order to be obeyed, and his faith consisted in obeying them. Abraham did not assent to the statement that God had called him; he believed in God calling him. We might put this in another way. The medievals believed that God had called Abraham; they assented to 'that' on the basis of God's authority under whose inspiration the author of the biblical book had written. But surely they already believed in God, in the sense of consenting to God, of being willing to live by God's authority. Otherwise, why would they assent to what God said through the biblical author? Thus their willingnes to live by God's authority, that is, their consent, preceded and made possible their assent to what (they thought) the biblical author had stated (rather than narrated). Well, Abraham's faith in God was a consent of the same kind as the medievals', whereas the medievals imagined that Abraham's faith was of the same kind as their assent to the statement that God had called Abrahm. As for Abraham's consent, it was, for the medievals, just an act of the will preceded by, and grounded upon, his assent to what God had stated. ( See also what I wrote about the primacy of consent over assent in "Assent", pp. l2-l4 )

But the medievals, like us, were not Abraham. Their access to faith was not through direct interaction with God. Abraham would have assented to the statement "God demanded Abraham to sacrifice Isaac" on the basis of his previous consent to God's demand. The medievals assented (as agents) to the statements they extracted from biblical narrations on the basis of their previous consent. But how was that first consent reached, since it was not reached through direct interaction with God? What brought them, and us, to the interaction of faith in which we live?

In the first place, not through assent to an Aristotelian statement, for those statements lead at best to assent, not to consent. I say "at best," for I doubt that assent to reality can be reached on the basis of evidential "proof."

Secondly, it might have been through the statements of a trusted agent who already consented to God. But these were statements of an agent, not a contemplator. In other words, these were statements of an agent who was not involved in actual interaction with God, who consented to God 'in absence,'who while not actually involved in responding to God was braced for future response to God. Take the case of Abraham after his consent to God. How did his family reach consent to God? By trusting Abraham's  statements. But if these statements were statements about, they were statements about God as object of consent, not of assent. And to be such they had to be based on the narrative of his encounter with God . This amounts to saying that, insofar as there is 'evidence' of an object for response, the evidence  cannot be an object of consideration in view of assent, but a dramatic narrative in view of consent .

By now we should be able to answer the question: WHAT WAS LOST WITH THE MEDIEVALS' SHIFT FROM NARRATIVES TO STATEMENTS?

Let us take as an example Clamance's situation . He heard a cry for help, which is the same as he felt called by someone in distress. Suppose that somebody else heard the same cry but as an insignificant sound. In the latter case, there would have been no narrative, no novel called The Fall . As there would have been no Bible without Abraham. Now we are the judges of both. We listen to the narrative of Clamance and to the non-narrative of the other. What we have from Clamance is a foundational story --foundational because it changed and determined the course of his life. ( What we have in the case of the Bible is a shared foundational story ). At the time of the sound heard so differently by our two characters, only the future could tell who was right. If we had arrived on the scene two minutes after the sound, we would have had to take our risks with one or the other, as they themselves had to take their risks as agents. Later, on the basis of "traces' and reports or the lack thereof, we would have an evidential basis on which to agree with one or the other. But what would be the nature of this agreement? Suppose we come to agree with Clamance. We take his story for history. We agree with all the statements made by Clamance. The source of intelligibility for all those statements is the story of Clamance.  Without it, all the statements would be disconnected and insignificant, as insignificant as the sound heard by the second of our characters, and we would have no reason to bother about them.  Agreeing with Clamance would amount to saying "You should have pursued the same outcome so that now fundamentally the same story as the one you have told us could be told. And if I had been in your shoes I would have pursued the same outcome so that I could tell now the same story." Disagreeing with the other would amount to saying "You should have heard the sound as a cry for help, so that you could have pursued the same outcome Clamance did and told now fundamentally the same story Clamance tells." Thus, it is by reference to the outcome pursued by the agent and to the story in which it is later reflected after the outcome that agreement or disagreement are possible. And the agreement on particular statements is important and possible on the basis of agreement to the story. (Except for the case of pure 'traces,' which may be part of the setting or of the events of different stories). All this amounts to saying that the nature of agreement, in the case of stories, consists in consenting with the agent, that is in consenting to the same to which the agent consented to .

Let us return to the Scholastics and the use they made of the Bible. Because they believed the biblical narrative as history, that is, because--to encapsulate--they consented with Abraham by consenting to the same God who demanded the sacrifice of Isaac, they consented with all the agent-statements in the narrative. But they mistook them for Aristotelian statements and transferred them as such to the context of demonstration.
