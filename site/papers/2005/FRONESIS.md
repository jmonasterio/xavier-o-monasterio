FRONESIS AND SOFIA

This paper continues the reflection I started in \wp\chantill\theofilo. Unless otherwise specified, my references here are to Gauthier, L'Etique a Nicomaque: Introduction, Traduction et Commentaire (Publications Universitaires, Louvain, second edition, 1970).

I expressed my guiding intuition in "Philoteo," particularly in the section that precedes immediately the data from the Nicomachean Ethics , and in the concluding section that follows it. It seems to me that fronesis is not knowledge in any sense, if by knowledge we are to understand what Aris following Plato understands. It can therefore neither be compared to it nor much less subordinated to it. Much of what Aris says in his analysis of fronesis is correct, if purified from his confusion between what might be called scientia moralis and the agent's moral or fronetic action.

La decision c'est le jugement meme, non pas en tant qu'il enonce une affirmation, mais en tant que sous la motion du desir il est moteur.

Fronesis is not knowledge in any way

What I mean is not only that that fronesis is not episteme, which Aristotle makes abundantly clear, but that it is not knowledge in any way but something totally different.

Let me suggest what I mean by this. Speaking of fronesis, Aris calls it deliberative desire. Another way of saying the same would be to call it intelligent desire. Now, desire is appetitive of the good. Intelligence connotes cognition of the truth. The satisfactors or 'objects' of an intellective and of an appetitive potency are totally heterogeneous. One can as little satisfy one's hunger by knowing an apple as one can satisfy one's desire to know by eating an apple. To speak, therefore, of an intelligent desire is nonsensical, for an appetitus neither can nor is meant to understand and an intellectual act nor can nor is meant to achieve or possess its object.

Consider it from another angle. The cognitive faculty is, in Aristotle's anthropology, immaterial and "almost divine." The appetitive faculty is material and resides in the body. How can possibly both faculties be satisfied by the same object, and what kind of object can that be which satisfies two totally heterogeneous faculties? Later in the De Anima Aris will distinguish the intellectus speculativus from theintellectus practicus. Now, the intellect is definitely a faculty of the soul, and its function is to understand. How can the intellectus, however practicus, desire? Hor how can the appetitus, which is not a faculty of the soul, desire what the intellectus understand? (Even in Aristotle's later hylemorphic anthropology, where the soul is the form of the body and they are substantially related, the problem seems to remain and is reflected in the 'mystery' of the possibility of such union which is beyond any possible explanation and which creates insoluble problems of the kind I just mentioned. This forced the Scholastics to invent a rational willing faculty of the soul). - In the Nicomachean Ethics the problem is reflected in another way. According to Aristotle, sapientia (which in some poor way we share with the gods) is one, but there are different froneses for people and for different species of animals, and no virtue and no action for the gods. - A last way to suggest what bothers me. In the De Motu Animalium Aris moves within the world of exposure where the concrete distinction between knowledge and desire is not possible. In fact, it does not appear until Aris comes to 'l'acte humain' and the 'practical syllogism,' where Aris analysis is marked by Plato's unique concern with knowledge as episteme. On the other hand, in the Ethics Aris's is centrally concerned with scientia moralis as knowledge. Thus he does not move within the world of exposure directly, and Plato's intellectualism gets the better of him at this level. On the other hand, he moves within the world of exposure as far as the agent is concerned, where horexis (as neither knowledge nor desire but something else, totally original, which has its place only within the dimension of exposure), but under Plato's intellectualistic influence he ends up having the intellectus practicus directing desire ( SEE GAUTHIER, P. 205).

There is an internal contradiction here. For all his efforts to escape Plato's intellectualism, Aris cannot quite escape Plato's intellectualism. Intellect and desire remain separated, though he speaks of deliberative desire. What I need, in the first place, is a more clear account of this internal contradiction. COMMENTARY:

To speak of "une connaissance originale," of 'jugement prudentiel" (24), of "un type nouveau de connaissance qui n'est plus une pure connaissance mais une connaissance penetree de desir" is still to conceive, as Aris does, the human horexis, human action, as a form of knowledge. But human action is not in any way a species of knowledge (as Aris conceives it after Plato) anymore than it is a species of desire (as Aris conceives it after Plato, for whom desire is an animal, purely passive, irrational capacity). What is it, then? How can it be characterized in a way that distances us from Plato? This is the question. And my hunch is that the approach used in the De Motu Animalium contains the solution. Why and how?

As soon as we objectify the movement toward the future in which action consists to analyze it we are in trouble, for that is not movement anymore and is not what the agent does. That is, at best, contemplated, petrified movement one of whose fixed elements is a purely nominal future.

A piece of meat for a hungry dog is a piece of meat only because and inasmuch as it is hungried for and intended actionally and actually eaten. A piece of meat that does not function as such for this dog is not a piece of meat for the dog.

"Action" is the process through which a desired piece of meat becomes  striven for  and hopefully eaten piece of meat. The difference with human action is that , on the assumption that there is no conflictive desire, the goal of eating the piece of meat is not invented by the dog and, under the appropriate circumstances, is to be inescapably pursued. Instinct enriched by past experience of success and failure will imperatively dictate and determine the behavior of the hungry dog. By contrast, a hungry human being can invent his/her goal and, therefore, his behavior (which is what Aris calls action, the immanent action that interests him) and, in the case of non-natural objects, create his or her object and even the means to bring it to existence.

How can the human being invent his goal? It is an invention that is not creation ex nihilo. There are general predispositional desires (called human needs) characteristic of human beings. The world of an herbivorous elephant contains no meat and contains only as many different herbs as an elephant is capable of appetizing. An herb that is not appetibile for an elephant does not exist as an herb in the elephant's world. If the elephant were capable of distinguishing species, neither meat nor species of meat would be part of the elephantine vocabulary, of the 'natural species' of the elephantine world. An elephant makes an herb exist as an herb by appetizing it, and the herb makes the elephant behave eatingly by being appetized, by act ivting and specifying the general need of the elephant. This is to say that within the field of its appetizing general pulsions the elephant carves the herbs that populate its world by responding to the activating-specifying. Only those 'objects' that fall within the general appetitive inherited fileld of an elephant can appear within its world, and only as to be pursued, only as appetibilia to the elephant, as appetenda, not as mere objects.

If humans were contemplative angels, if they were not needful, if they had no field of general natural desires to be activated by singular appetenda, they could not invent goals. It is in this sense that humans do not create their goals ex nihilo but on the proximate or remote basis of their inherited, characteristically human general desires. Yet, they invent their goals. What does this mean?

Animals have to find their appetenda already made, or at best make them "according to the standards of the species to which they belong," whereas people can make appetenda which they do not find ready made, as Marx observed. People originally lived in natural caves, then perhaps in artificial caves, then they built houses, while birds have always lived in nests, except for the transformations effected by natural evolution. This presupposes a natural pulsion to a sheltered life, which originally was satisfied by natural caves. Without this original pulsion they would have never recognized and appetized caves to be lived in. But the interesting fact, for our purposes, is that they ceased to live in caves, that they built houses. In other words, humans can be motivated to behave by ideal objects , which become goals (rather than mere dreams) when the co-determination between goal and available means obtains. These ideal, not previously existing objects, like houses, become goals and codetermine means. These goals are invented as well as the means to realize them, though the goals are invetned as the desired by itself and the means only as the desired for the sake of the goal.

What is so important about this? For one, it inaugurates history, by contrast to mere changes passively suffered, and it inaugurates the change of the world with its correlative change of the human being. But my purpose here is connected with what Aris calls "deliberative desire." A goal that is invented, carved out within the field of general human pulsions but which heightens and extends the limits of the field, cannot be given by nature; it involves the invention of the goal, of the means, and of the behavior needed to bring about the goal by the application of the means as means. By inventing and realizing artificial desiderata people cease being mere creatures of nature and start inventing themselves through their invented world.

Invented goals presuppose or imply the at least limited ability to interrupt the circuit of passivity to the natural objects that are, under the right conditions, automatic activators of human behavior. Again, a hungry dog, is unavoidably activated by a piece of meat to attempt to eat it, but a hungry human being may abstain from such behavior by inventing his/her own goal, say, weight reduction, an ideal goal to be actually realized or brought into existence by means of a self-designed behavior of abstention from eating. They also presuppose or imply an at least limited 'logistiké' ability, that is, the ability to understand now to use the observed 'causal connections' instrumentally, that is, in view of their effectiveness for an invented goal.

Later I will pursue this line further. Now I return to the problem at hand, which has to do with Aris's reduction of the deliberative human capacity to knowledge. Now, Aris strongly distinguishes practical knowledge (and, later, practical intellect) from theoretical knowledge (and, later, speculative intellect). That is quite a progress over Plato and even over Aris's own earleir thinking in the Protreptikon. The fact remains that his idea and even his ideal of knowledge (however modified) is Plato's idea, which identifies knowledge with theoretical knowledge, and that theoretical knowledge remains for Aris the princeps analogatum by reference to which practical knowledge is identified as knowledge. My position, however, is that action is not knowledge and cannot be reduced to knowledge plus behavior without misunderstanding.

Let us ignore pure physical action of objects and pure physiological reflexes in living creatures. Through skillful operant conditioning I can teach my dog not to bark when a certain person knocks at my door. But I cannot bring my dog not to bark at that person by means of purely verbal instructions. For my dog, before any training, whoever smelling 'stranger' knocks is to be barked at, to be responded to as enemy. For the dog behaving threateningly is the same as knowing as enemy. A more pointed way of saying what I mean: for my dog behaving threateningly is identical with perceiving as enemy; there is no neutral, 'objective' perception for a dog, and its perception is identical with practical response. The perceiving as of my dog is evaluative through and through and is identical with behaving threateningly. We may explain its behavior causally by means of mental images and feelings. Whatever the causal explanation, howver, what the dog responds to is the stranger as enemy. The dog does not bark at its mental image, it does not attack its mental image, it attacks the stranger who knocks at my door. I may try to protect the stranger from my dog, but not the hypothetical mental image of my dog, which needs no protection. And, unless I am an idealist as a result of much intellectual hypothesizing (notice that even idealists protect the stranger rather than the dog's hypothetical mental image of the dog), and unless I know the causal mentalistic explanation for its behavior alluded to above, I would have no reason to mention mental images in connection with my dog's action. Barking, attacking and, if not prevented, destroying the stranger is my dog's action. Notice that I am neither affirming nor denying that my dog has mental images and/or feelings, I am just characterizing its action, and for such characterization mental images and feelings are irrelevant. Furthermore, if my dog happens to have such mental entities which cause its behavior rather than the stranger's presence, there is no reason to think that he knows that it has them and that they cause its behavior. In short, I am simply characterizing my dog's action (which is a behavioral response to the presence of a certain object). And I have to characterize it in its own terms, which are very different from characterizing what we call thinking, for thinking is not what we call acting.

Now to the human's case. In infancy our actions may be characterized in the same way as the dog's. But we are lingusitc creatures. With other humans we establish successful linguistic reciprocal relations that sometimes bring to existence the desired state of affairs, and we undergo or suffer rleations in which verbally we are made into instruments of the realization of other people's desired state of affairs, as when the child is forced by her mother's verbal order to go to bed against her will. Like animals, we develop expectations about natural and artificial objects, and like them we use them instrumentally to achieve our naturally given--not invented--purposes . To that extent, we are creatures of desire, like animals. Thanks, however, to our linguistic ability, that gives us names on which to hang our interactive experience and to enrich it with other people's experience, and to our behavior being linguistically directed in its responses by other people's interactive experience, we learn to behave contrary to our immediate natural desires and to delay our responses. The fact that we are not born with instincts, complex, automatic and species-uniform patterns of behavior that lead to the satisfaction of our needs, forces us to discover, initially, and to invent, later, our way of satisfying them. Initially the goals are given, only the means are to be discovered: vgr. a primitive man learning how to catch birds through observation of the birds' behavior and trial and error. This is the beginning of deliberation about means, which later will become invention of goals and of means. Do I want that this involves 'knowledge'? No, what I want to deny is that it involves speculative knowledge. But doe not Aris concedes this by distinguishing, initially implicitly, later explicitly, between speculative and practical knowledge? Not quite, because his practical intellect is the same faculty as his speculative intellect, only applied toa different, contingent kind of object.

And what I hold is that practical intellect is a desiderative faculty correlative to its object which is desideratum, object of desire in a hodological, dramatic space.

Hume was right: it is impossible to move from facts to values, from mere object to object of desire. And since the passage is impossible, once we start there we necessarily end up reducing desire to a mere subjective, blind feeling caused perhaps by the object but not directed to the object, desirously intending the object as desirable.

Unavoidably, however, when we reflect, as I am doing now, we abstract in order to analyze: speaking of object of desire and of desiring subject is an abstraction from the actual conditions of encounter.
