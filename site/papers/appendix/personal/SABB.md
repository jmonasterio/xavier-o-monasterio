5. DESCRIPTION OF PROJECT:

For a number of years I have been working on a book which might still be completed some day. I was prompted to undertake this investigation by my discomfort with the way in which philosophy of religion is generally conceived and taught.

The issue has shown itself to be far more complex than I originally imagined, particularly because of its deep historical

roots and ramifications. As a result, my investigation has taken me in such different directions as the distinction between theoretical and practical reason as it evolved from Socrates to Aristotle, the encounter of the Catholic intellectual medievals with the bulk of the Aristotelian corpus, the conceptions of faith and of reason that resulted from that encounter, and the way in which both the curriculum of the medieval schools and the scholarly reading of the Bible were shaped by those conceptions.

In the last year or so the way to put together the different strands of these apparently unrelated or very tenuously interrelated subjects has taken a more definite shape in my mind. As a result my research has become more clearly focused. I would like to put this to the test during my sabbatical term by writing an article on two of the most original and articulate philosophers in the contemporary American scene: Martha Nussbaum and Alasdair MacIntyre.

The interest that these two philosophers have for me is that both of them share a notion of reason that has its basis on Aristotle's "practical reason," rather than, as it was the case with the medievals and has been for the most part the case with Western philosophers, on the Greek view of "speculative reason." As a consequence, both  Nussbaum and MacIntyre appeal, as Aristotle did in the Nicomachean Ethics , to a conception of what being human amounts to, which in its turn enlightens the agent as to how to lead a virtuous--or excellent or thoroughly human--life. Yet the respective results reached by these thinkers on what is apparently the same basis differ in very significant ways. How is this possible, if both of them are supposedly proceeding rationally?

My hypothesis, in a nutshell, is that what makes the difference between them is that they differ in their religious convictions, MacIntyre being a Catholic and Nussbaum a secular humanist. For my purposes this is important in at least two regards. First, it is an indication of the way in which religious faith or the absence thereof irremediably permeate and shape our views, so that at least in practical matters, that is, in matters of decision and action, there is no possible appeal to a neutral standard of reason. (By the same token, the teaching of philosophy of religion, particularly when taught--as it should be--as a matter of decision and action rather than as a matter of speculative intellection, is inescapably and substantively influenced by the teacher's convictions). Second--and on this point, though not on many others, MacIntyre is more perceptive than Nussbaum because of his awareness of the explicitly traditional and communitary character of the Catholic faith--, this opens a new perspective for the understanding both of religious faith as something other than intellectual assent and of rational knowledge as something other than the result of compelling neutral evidence.

I cannot explain more the substance and the significance of the article I want to write without actually writing it. As for the steps to be followed, all that needs to be said, since what I propose to write is only an article, is that it will be grounded in the following bibliography:

Nussbaum, Martha Craven:

- Aristotle's De Motu Animalium: Translationm, Commentary, and Interpretive Essays (Princeton University Press, 1978).

- The Fragility of Goodness : Luck and Ethics in Greek Tragedy and Philosophy (Cambridge University Press, 1986).

- Love's Knowledge : Essays on Philosophy and Literature (Oxford University Press, 1990).

- "Saving Aristotle's Appearances," in Language and Logos , edited by M. Schofield and M. C. Nussbaum (Cambridge University Press, 1982).  

- "Recoiling from Reason" ( The New York Review of Books , December 7, 1989, pp. 36-41).

MacIntyre, Alasdair:

- After Virtue (University of Notre Dame Press, 1981).

- Whose Justice?, Which Rationality? (University of Notre Dame Press, 1988).

- Three Rival Versions of Moral Enquiry: Encyclopaedia, Genealogy, and Tradition (University of Notre Dame Press, 1990).
