Data - Chapter Six

Points to be considered

- Revelation and Faith

- God reveals his knowledge ("teaches as a master who has perfect knowledge" (l7, art 7), "the knowledge of faith was imparted to the Fathers who were instructors in the faith" (ibid.). The knowledge of faith was delivered by the early Patriarchs to those who came later; the Apostles were more fully instructed about the mysteries than those who came early (Ibid.).

Man acquires a share of this learning a little at a time. In many respects faith perceives the invisible things of God in a higher way than natural reason does proceeding from its creatures (21, 3).

- Knowledge of God's essence is by grace (11, 12) and belongs only to the good, whereas the natural knowledge of God belongs to good and bad (12, 12).

- Through revelation we do not know what God essentially is, in this life ; thus we are united to Him as to one unknown . However, through reason we have some knowledge of Him through his effects, and through revelation we attribute to Him certain things that reason cannot know (vgr. Three in One). Through revelation given in images a fuller knowledge is received (12, 12)

- Faith will be succeeded by vision (13, 2). The object of heavenly vision will be the First Truth seen in itself, according to John III, 2. Hence that vision will not be by way of a proposition, but by way of simple understanding (14, 2). THE CHIEF OBJECT OF FAITH CONSISTS IN THOSE THINGS WHICH WE HOPE TO SEE IN HEAVEN (16, 6).

- Faith is of the unseen : Faith signifies the assent of the intellect to that which is believed. Those things are said to be seen which, of themselves, move the intellect or the senses to knowledge. Thus neither faith nor opinion can be of things seen (14-15, 4). It is impossible that one and the same thing should be believed and seen by the same person . But something may be object of vision for one, and believed by another. Nevertheless, that which is proposed to be believed by all is equally unknown by vision. Such are the things that are of faith absolutely . Consequently faith and science are not about the same things (15, 5). Things which can be demonstrated are reckoned among what is of faith , not because they are believed absolutely by all , but because they are a necessary presupposition to matters of faith; so that those who do not know them by demonstration must possess them at least by faith (15, 5). The formal object of faith is the First Truth; but from our point of view, the formal object is that it is something unseen; and from this point of view there are distinct articles (16, 6). Some kind of belief in Incarnation (or at least a Mediator) was necessary at all times (23, 7). Actions are meritorious if they proceed from free choice. So is faith. Scientific assent is not meritorius (23, 8). The demons are compelled to assent by the evidence of the signs by which they recognize that the teaching of the Church is from God, but they do not see the things themselves; however, they do not have faith, for their will is not ordered toward the Good (26, 2).

Fear is of the appetitive power; its principle is apprehended evil. Through faith there is apprehension of certain penal evils. Fear of God cannot precede faith, because if we knew nothing about Him with regard to rewards and punishments, we would not fear God (26, 1).

- Scripture

- Certain things in Scripture are proposed to our belief, not chiefly on their own account (as those which are absolutely of faith), but for the manifestation of the latter: like Abraham had two sons, Eliseus resuscitated a man; they manifest the divine majesty or the Incarnation. These do not form distinct articles (16, 6). The Scripture is the rule of faith to which no addition should be made. But the Scripture contains the truth diffusely, in various modes of expression, and sometimes obscurely, so that one needs long practice to gather the truth from it; many have not the time for that study; the articles are a clear summary of Scripture proposed to the belief of all (19, 9).

- Object of faith

- God : The object of faith is the First Truth  (12, 1).

- material : Not only God but also many other things contained in the Scripture; but they come under the assent of faith only as related to God and because they lead us to the final enjoyment of God; hence we assent to the First Truth (13,1).

- formal : The First Truth, for faith does not assent to anything except because it is revealed by God (12-13, 1). The formal aspect of the object of faith, on the part of the thing believed, is the First Truth ; but from our point of view, the formal aspect of a matter of faith is that it is something unseen ; and from this point of view there are distinct articles (l6, 6).

- primary or essential : Things proposwed to be believed by all are unseen by all; such are the things that are of faith absolutely (15, 5). Some things proposed to our belief are in themselves of faith (the articles), while others are of faith, not in themselves, but only in relation to others . Since the chief object of faith consists in those things which we hope to see in heaven, those things are in themselves of faith which order us directly to eternal life. Such are the Trinity, the Incarnation, and the like (16, 6). To faith those things belong essentially the sight of which we shall enjoy in eternal life, and by which we are brought to eternal life . The two things proposed to us in eternal life to be seen are the secret of the Godhead, and the mystery of Christ's Incarnation (18, 8).

- secondary : Preambles (see below). Certain things in Holy Scripture: (see above).

- The direct object of faith is that whereby man is made one of the Blessed, while the indirect and secondary object comprises all things delivered by God in Scripture (that Abraham had two sons, that David was the son of Jessee...). The secondary points must be believed at least implicitly , to be ready to believe whatever is in the Scriptures. Explicitly , only when it is clear to o ne that they are in Scripture (22, 5).

- of the intellect : To believe is an act of the intellect insofar as the willl moves it to assentl. The object of faith can be considered either on the part of the intellect or of the will. On the part of the intellect, the material object is to believe in God, for nothing is proposed to our believ but insufar as it is referred to God. The formal object , that is, the medim because of which we assent to such and such point of faith, is to believe that God is the First Truth , so as to assent for its sake to what one believes. If the object of faith be considered insofar as the intellect is moved by the will , an act of faith is to believe in God, for the First Truth is referred to the will through having the nature of an end . Thes are not three acts of faith, but one and the same having different relations to the object of faith ((20=21, 2). Man's happiness consists in a supernatural vision of God . To this vision man cannot attain unless taught by God (21, 3).

 - of the will : The intellect of the believer is determined to one object not by the reason but by the will, and so assent is taken here for an act of the intellect as determined by the will (20, 1). To believe is an act of the intellect insofar as the will moves it to assent (20, 2). The act of faith is directed to the object of the will, i.e., the good, as to its end; and this Good which is the end of faith, namely, the Divine Good, is the proper object of charity . Therefore charity is called the form of faith. Even obedience, hope, and whatever other virtue might preced the act of faith is prompted by charity ((24-25, 3).

- propositional object :

- We know through propositions --the simple through a certain complexity. The act of the believer terminates in a thing, not in a proposition , for we form propositions in order to have knowledge about things through them (14, 2).

- Symbols and articles of faith :

- The Symbol does not contain propositions but things , for it does not state that God is almighty, but I believe in God almighty (13, 2). The Symbol mentions the things about which faith is, as the act of the believer is terminated in them, as is evident from the manner of speaking about them . The act of the believer does not terminate in a proposition but in a thing. (14, 2). The articles of faith stand in the same relation to the doctrine of faith (THEOLOGY?) as self-evident principles to teaching based on natural reason. Among the principles there is a certain order, so that some are contained implicitly in others. All the articles are contained implicitly in certain truths of faith, such as God's existence and his providence over the salvation of man. The being of God includes all those things which God dispeneses in time for man's salvation and which are the way to happiness. In this way, Redemption includes implicitly Incarnation, Passion, etc. The substance of faith has not increased , for the early Patriarchs believed implicitly (17, 7). Athanasius drew up a declaration of faith as an exposition, not as a symbol; it became a Symbol (a rule of faith) through the authority of the Sovering Pontiff (19, 10).

- Faith and reason :

- Preambles of faith : What is of faith cannot be demonstrated . The existence of God and other like truths about God are not articles of faith, but are preambles to the articles; for faith presupposes natural knowledge, even as grace presupposes nature and perfection the perfectible (10, 2).

- Obj. 1: It can be known by demonstration that there is one God . Therefore it should not be an article of faith. Reply to obj. 1: By faith we hold truths that exceed reason, vgr. his providence, omnipotence, that He alone is to be worshipped, all of which are contained in the article that God is one. Since certain people erred about the three Persons, articles were needed for the problematic points (18, 8).

- But a man can accept by faith what can be demonstrated but he is unable to grasp through demonstration (10, 2)

- The knowledge of God's essence is by grace (11, 12).

- Natural knowledge begins from sense; thus, it cannot be led by sensible things to the knowledge of God's essence ; but through God's effects can be led to His existence and what must necessarily belong to Him (11, 12).

- Faith has not that inquiry of natural reason which demonstrates what is believed, but an inquiry into those things whereby a man is induced to believe; for instance, that such things have been uttered by God and confirmed by miracles (20, 1).

- Obj.: Unbelievers can be said to believe that there is a God; so it is not object of faith. Reply: Unbelievers cannot be said to believe IN a God , as we understand it in relation to the act of faith, for they do not believe that God exists under the conditions that faith determines (21, 2). See demons above/

- Obj.: Science and faith are not about the same object: thus, if all things which can be known through natural reason are object of science, it is not necessary to believe them. Reply: It is necessary for man to believe not only those things which are above reason, but also those known by reason :(a) to arrive more quickly at the knowledge of divien truth; (b) to have that knowledge available to all; to have full certitude. True, science and faith cannot be in the same subject and about the same object; but what is an object of science for one can be an object of faith for another ((21-22, 4).

 - Those who teach others have to believe explicitly more things.

- The believer has sufficient motive for believing , for he is moved by the authority of divine teaching--confirmed by miracles and by the inward instigation of divine grace; so he does not believe lightly. But he has no sufficient reason for scientific knowledge , hence he does not lose the merit (23, 8).

- Human reasons may be consequent to the will of the believer ; so the merit remains . The reasons in support of the articles are not demonstrations; the articles remain unseen. Reasons in support of the preambles may be demonstrative; but the merit is not diminished, for the will to believe them remains even if they were unseen; so the merit remmains (23-24, 10).
