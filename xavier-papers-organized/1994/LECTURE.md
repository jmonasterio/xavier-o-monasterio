ABSTRACT

The issue at stake is not whether there is a Catholic tradition but whether there is a Catholic intellectual tradition.

In part one I explore the synthesis of faith and reason commonly attributed to Aquinas both because it seems the best candidate for the title  of Catholic intellectual tradition and because of the impact it has had on the Western culture. My assessment is that the synthesis never worked, that it could not possibly work, and that by its very character it was bound to create the conflict between faith and reason in the very form which the conflict actually took historically.

My question in part two is whether the historic triumph of reason over faith in the conflict between the two constrains us Catholic intellectuals to choose between being Catholic and being rational. My answer is that both the notion of faith and the notion of reason which create the conflict are defective, and that the dilemma appears pressing only because and insofar as we remain uncritically committed to those notions.

In a future article I will attempt to show that the notion of reason as a neutral or impartial  "faculty", as a tabula rasa to be passively shaped by a contemplated world, much as the surface of a lake in which the surroundings are reflected, does not make sense. This, however, is the notion that the Thomistic synthesis of faith and reason bequeathed to the Enlightenment and which the latter uncritically accepted and passed on to us.

I also will have to offer an alternative understanding of faith as a special variety of practical knowledge, that is, as desirous-knowledge of exposed agent.
