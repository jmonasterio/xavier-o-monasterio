      "To be sure, the encounter between St. Thomas  and  Aristotle  is  for
most of us a far away event. The passage of time has  buried  it  deeply  in
history and recreated it inextricably in myth and prejudice.  We  have  been
told often enough that St. Thomas  learned  the  nature  and  principles  of
philosophy from Aristotle and thereafter reported what he learned as  though
Aristotle was philosophy personified. In point of fact, did not  St.  Thomas
repeatedly say': "secundum veritatem et Aristotelem: according to truth  and
Aristotle"? Moreover,  since  philosophy  is  on  everyone's  admission  the
native work of man's intellect. it would seem perfectly natural  to  suppose
that the philosophy St. Thomas used in  his  theology  was  Aristotelianism,
which is to say, the work of the intellect belonging  to  an  ancient  pagan
philosopher. Aristotelianism was philosophy pure and simple and  St.  Thomas
so understood it" (Anton C. Pegis, Thomism as a Philosophy, in  The  McAuley
Lectures, 1960: St. Thomas Aquinas and Philosophy, St. Joseph College,  West
Hartford, Connecticut, 1961, p. 17).

      "The Fathers of the Church had discussed at length the errors and  the
sins of paganism, its pride and its superstititions. But in  the  thirteenth
century ancient paganism marched into the Christian world  by  a  new  road,
philosophy itself, and chiefly the philosophy of a man who was to be  called
for centuries thereafter The Philosopher, the great Aris. No century  before
the  thirteenth  had  ever  felt  the  full  force  of   the   philosophical
intelligence of the ancient world. Now, in the new age of the  universities,
Christian theologians were undergoing a new experience.  They  were  reading
in the Metaphysics and the Physics of Aristotle a complete  account  of  the
nature and destiny of the physical universe as seen  by  a  human  intellect
that had never heard of the Xtian  revelation.  They  were  reading  in  the
Ethics and the Politics of the same Aris a pagan ad  pre-Christian  view  of
the nature and the destiny of man,  including  discussions  on  the  end  of
life,  the  nature  of  virtue,  and  the  relations  between  goodness  and
beatitude. Moreover, these pagan views had been set forth by a man  who  was
one of the acknowledged founders of European philosophical thought, who  had
created a technical vocabulary to express it, and  who  had  already  taught
the civilized world the nature of intellectual evidence and demonstration.
      "As they entered the thirteenth-century Latin world  the  writings  of
Aris were accompanied by great Arabian commentaties that left  little  doubt
as to his philosophical stature but  that  often  hardened  his  ideas  from
something that was originally pagan and pre-Christian  into  something  that
became specifically anti-Christian. Thus, assuming the eternitiy of  matter,
Aris had argued for the eternity of motion, time and  the  order  itself  of
the world, and he had done so in an entirely negative way: he had  not  seen
any other or higher  mode  of  origin  in  the  universe  than  by  physical
change... In  the  twelfth  century,  however,  Averroes  deliberately  used
Aris's arguments for the eternity of motion to fight  against  the  doctrine
of creation as taught by Jewish, Mohammedan and Xtian  theologians."  (Ibid.
20-1).

      "One may or may not agree with its doctrines,  one  cannot  but  agree
that St. Thomas considers no notion as  part  of  philosophy,  whatever  its
source, if the human intellect cannot reach it by its own light.... We  must
say that St. Thomas believed unreservedly in  free  intellectualism  as  the
charter of philosophy; but we must add that he espoused  and  cherished  the
role of the Xtian religion in promoting the greater freedom  and  purity  of
the intellect in philosophy" (Ibid., p. 19).

       "When  Aristotle  became  the  philosophical  teacher  of  the   mid-
thirteenth -century  Paris,  the  Xtian  West  entered  that  phase  of  its
development commonly called "Scholasticism." The age of  the  Fathers,  from
St. Augustine to the Cistercian and Victorine contemplatives,  who  occupied
themselves with the memory of God and the rich symbolism of Xtian  devotion,
was succeeded by the age of the philosophical theologians, the  "schoolmen."
Scholasticism was an intellectually sophisticated but short-livged event  in
medieval culture. Emerging toward the middle of the thirteenth  century,  it
ran its full doctrinal cycle by the middle of the fourteenth  and  survivied
as a static edifice into  the modern world. But  scholasticism  was  both  a
crisis and a struggle, testing whether, on the occasion of  the  meeting  of
the intellectual picture of the universe  framed  by  the  pagan  Aristotle,
Xtianity could develop within its own religious vision of time and  eternity
a purely human  and  rational  view  of  the  same  universe  in  which  its
religious teaching was planted. Scholasticism  was  thus  an  arena  of  the
lasting intellectual conflict between Athens and Jerusalem" (Ibid. 20).

      "St. Thomas set out  to  christianize  paganism  by  saving  what  was
genuinely human within it. If there are Xtians for whom  such  a  task  must
fail because they believe that to undertake it is already to be captured  by
a pagan Aristotle, I can only say  that  this  was  not  St.  Thomas's  view
either of the nature of Xtianity or of what was human in paganism.
      "The distinctive feature in St.  Thomas's  attitude  toward  Aristotle
was that he neither condemned  Aristotelianism  neither  capitulated  before
it" (Ibid., p. 21).

      "This meant,  moreover,  establishing  the  philosophy  of  St  Thomas
outside his theology, where it was born, and giving  to  it  the  completion
and general structure that it did not have within  the  Thomistic  theology.
There was no doubt that the thing could be done and the idea that St  Thomas
was both a tehologian  and  a  philosopher  reinforced  what  surely  was  a
widespread conviction down to the second world war. In practice, we  derived
St Thomas' natural theology and  psychology  from  the  Prima  pars  of  the
Summa, his ethics  from  the  Prima  Secundae,  and  we  supplemented  these
fundamental texts with materials from the Disputed Questions. Books  on  the
philosophy of St. Thomas were written in  this  way,  with  a  philosophical
order of exposition that was part of the modernization of  St.  Thomas,  and
few doubted that the result was the philosophy of St  thomas,  uptdated  but
still his philosophy.
      "It is fair to say that  this  attitude  represented  more  the  late-
nineteenth-century view of Scholastic or Thomist philosophy  than  the  view
emerging among the historians of medieval philosophy from 1920  on.  Let  us
here limit ourselves to the essentials of that development, and  let  us  do
so by concentrating our attention on the development of Gilson as a  student
from the early twenties to the present day. He took a leading  part  in  the
breakup opf the notion of a common scholastic philosophical synthesis  as  a
medieval reality. With his book on St. Bonaventure  ((1924)  he  proved  the
existence of a Bonaventurean  philosophy  that  was  no  pale  or  imperfect
imitation of Thomism,  but,  from  first  to  last,  a  synthesis  that  was
Platonic in metaphysical inspiration and that accepted  Aristotle  from  the
time of  the  Sentences  on  the  condition  of  subordinating  him  to  St.
Augustine. From this point on Gilson moved steadily toward  a  second  idea.
Not only was 'medival philosophy" the work of several independent  thinkers,
each going his  own  way  and  dreaming  of  his  own  synthesis,  buth  the
syntheses in question were the work of  theologians  who  intended  them  as
theologies. In other words, medieval philo was part and parcel  of  medieval
theology, living within tehology and serving its purposes.  Thomistic  philo
is part of Thomistic theology: there  is  no  Thomist  philo  outside   that
theology, there is only a Thomistic philo that is identical  with  the  wine
of Thomistic theology. Born as theology, thomistic philo  cannot  be  recast
into a pure philo; its permanent signature is to be the philo of  and  in  a
scholastic theology, serving as its human  body.  That  is  why  Gilson  has
systematically refused to present the philo of St  Thomas  in  any  but  the
theological form in which  it  was  born;  that  is  why,  too,  instead  of
engaging in the modernizing of Thomism, he has been  concerned...  with  the
metaphysical principles that are the ultimate foundation of the  philo  that
St Thomas used in his theology." (Anton C. Pegis,  "Thomism  1966,"  in  The
McAuley Lectures, 1966: In Search of St. Thomas, St.  Joseph  College,  West
Harford, Connecticut, l966, pp.20-21).

      "What other conclusion than this has Maritain reached? Neither he  nor
Gilson believes in a philo  pieced  together  from  fragments  of  theology.
Gilson has said more than once  that  a  scholasticism  so  created  in  the
sixteenth and seventeenth centuries  has  been  a  sterile  reality  in  the
modern world; and Maritain has added that a philo derived  in  this  way  is
nothing more than dead theology" (9bid., p. 22).

      "How can a theology of creation and man, living  within  the  movement
of  the  divine  love  as  known  from  revelation,  function  outside  that
movement? It cannot, except  in  the  lifeless  sort  of  way  in  which  an
instrument that has lost its original force and purpose  can  be  made  into
something else" (Ibid. 23).

      "What is clear is that St Thomas's Aristotelian option was to  explain
at the level of metaphysics the unity  of  the  human  person  as  the  main
Christian tradition behind it him had developed it  on  religious  grounds."
(Ibid., p. 26).

      "The most continuous and extended use of his own  philosophy  that  St
Thomas has made is in the Summa Contra Genmtiles, a theological treatise  so
full of philosophy that there are not  wanting  interpreters  for  whom  the
first three books of this work are  philosophy  pure  and  simple.  But,  in

christianae that faith professes and reason  is  competent  to  investigate"

      "The foundations of St Thomas's theology of man  are  the  immortality
of the soul and the unity of man  in  existence.  But  these  two  doctrines
cannot by themsevles act as a basis for a coherent phil.losophy of man,  and
still less can they organize one. I do not  think  that  St  Thomas  himself
believed this, even though as a theoplogian he limited himself to  enforcing
these two doctrines.
      "To be sure,  if  there  is  such  a  being  as  man  composed  of  an
intellectual soul and body   [my  emphasis],  then  St  Thomas'  existential
explanation is perfectly  adequate  to  insure  that  the  fact  of  man  is
maintained and in this sense explained. But to say so much is  to  have  man
before you as an object of philosophical investigation, it is  not  to  have
explained him. For the true problem in the unity of man still remains to  be
considered. How is man possible? What unity of nature does he have, so  that
he is an integral whole, one in essence and therefore  intelligible  in  the
internal  structure  of  his  being?  How,  then,  do  soul  and  body,   in
constituting one being, constitute and posit one essence? It  is  here  that
St Thomas has said much  less  than  his  principles  contain  or  require."
(Ibiud. 28).

      "Man is part of nature for St Thomas Aquinas, he belongs by nature  in
the world of bodies, for he is himself a living body. If he is  living  with
an intellectual life even as a body, this does not unsay that to be  a  bddy
is part of his nature; it simply points to the  entirely  unique  conditions
under which he is such a body. Here begins the  truly  Thomistic  philosophy
of man. Man is part of nature because by nature  the  human  intellect  does
not have the power to live anywhere except in the world of matter.  And  yet
the human intellect is living in the world of matter  with  an  intellectual
existence, it is giving the human body the same intellectual existence  that
it is itself exercizing, and  though  it  is  living  within  the  world  of
material nature, there is also a deep sense  in  which  material  nature  is
itself living within the higher world of human intelligence. Man is part  of
nature, therefore, because in the very intellectuality of his  constitution,
nature is part of him. I say that a Thomistic philosophy of man begins  here
and nowhere else. The immortality of the soul and the existential  unity  of
the human composite are no more than presuppositions of such  a  philosophy.
They are, so to speak, the preliminary considerations which, by  making  man
possible as a  being,  give  us  a  human  person  and  a  human  nature  to
investigate and to study.
      "Such a Thomistic philosophy if man does not yet exist.. It  does  not
exist, in the main, becausde instead  of  trying  to  discover  by  our  own
resources and in our own world, we have tended to  piece  together  what  St
Thomas  said  as  a  theologian,  and  for  theological  purposes,  in   the
thirteenth century." (Ibid., pp. 29-309).

      "The first point to understand is that being or not  being  a  Thomist
has not much to do with being or not being  a  Catholic.  Among  those  that
philosophize, many Catholics... refuse to  subscribe  to  the  teachings  of
Thomism. And the worst of it is that their  refusal  can  be  based  on  two
contradictory grounds: either because Thomism is a  philosophy,  whereas  it

      "Far from finding fault with philosophy, when  that  which  philosophy
says is true, Thomas thinks that theology which is  not  faith  itself,  but
our understanding of faith, as far as such understanding is possible, has  a
duty to appeal to philosophy and indeed to all the  sciences,  in  order  to
open the mind to the turth of divine revelation" (ibid., p. 35).

      "Whether or not there ever was one single philosophy that is  innocent
of all admixture of non  strictly  rational  elements  is  a  problem  whose
discussion would extend to infinity. But let us concede that such  at  least
is  the  commonly  received  idea  of  philosophy,   which   obtains   among
philosophers. On the basis of that notion, many  philosophizing  Christians,
laymen and priests, insist on teaching a  philosophy  wholly  separate  from
theology and untainted by belief in any religious revelation.
      "... in order to  reconcile  their  own  brand  of  Thomism  with  the
current notion of a philosophy free  from  theological  entanglements,  they
divide it into  two  distinct  parts:  a  theology  exclusively  based  upon
revelation and faith on the one hand, and on the other  hand,  a  philosophy
exclusively derived from the natural light of reason.
      "Whether that is a correct notion of both theology and philosophy,  is
in itself a problem with which I am not prepared  to  deal.  The  discussion
has beein going on for centuries and  it  is  still  going  strong.  My  own
question is: do such notions of philosophy and theology fit the thought  and
practce of Thomas Aquinas? He certainly would  agree,  in  fact  did  agree,
that  religious  faith  in  the  truth  of  the  divine  revelation  differs
specifically, nay, generically from philosophical  knowledge  obtainable  by
reason alone. Either I believe or else I know. Thomas was not interested  in
the distinction of the sake of philosophy and reason alone, but at least  as
much for that of faith. For faith is about  the  word  of  God,  itself  the
necessary means to our salvation, which is  a  supernatural  religious  end,
obtainable only by  religious  and  supernatural  means.  Salvation  through
reason and philosophy was precisely the fundamental error  denounced  by  St
Paul; not indeed that reason and philosophy are  wrong  in  themselves,  but
rather, that they are the wrong means of salvation...
       "From  the  fact  that  theology  derives  its  own  principles  from
revelation, not from reason, it does  not  follow  that  the  theologian  is
forbidden to resort to philosophical arguments in  order  either  to  refute
error  or  to  show  that  there  is  a  measure   of   agreeement   between
philosophical knowledge and religious belief. Thomism is not  a  philosophy;
it is a theology. But since theology is faith in quest of understanding,  it
must needs appeal to the methods of philosophical knowledge  in  order,  not
at all to justify and to confirm faith, but to achieve, to the  extent  that
is possible, some understanding of it...
      "When an unbeliever tells me: your philosophy  would  not  agree  with
the teachings of Christianity if  you  did  not  first  believe  that  those
teachings are true, I have  nothing  to  answer.  At  least  nothing  simple
permitting to settle the question in a few words. Just now, I shall  content
myuself with observing that, when Christians  want  me  to  adopt  the  same
attitude, on the ground that it has been  the  genuine  attitude  of  Thomas
Aquinas, I cannot subscribe to their opinion. It  to  be  a  Thomist  is  to
philosophize as though one were not a Xtian,..., then I can only  sayt  that
to be a Thomist is to be a very different man from St. Thomas  Aquinas;  for
indeed a long time before being  made  a  saint,  he  was  a  Xtian  and  he
certainly made no bones about it.
      "This is perhaps the worst way of misunderstanding Tomism, namely,  to
conceive it as a philosophy wholly separate from religion and, so to  speak,
impermeable to all supernatural influence... I have no objection  to  anyone
philosophizing in that way, provided only he does not pretend that  this  is
a Thomistic way to philsophize...
      "Theologian Thomas is inhabited by a philosopher  Thomas  and  in  him
the philosopher lives on the best possible terms with the theologian.  There
are philosophical reasons why it should be so....
      ... Man has been  created  intelligent  in  order  that,  whith  God's
grace, he might share God's blessedness in another life; that is the  proper
end of man's understanding. As to revelation, it has for its purpose to  let
man know certain truths without  which  a  finite  understanding  could  not
possibly attain its supernatural end; yet faith is not itself its  own  end;
it is in us, Thomas says, in order to lead us to an  understanding  of  that
which we believe: fides est in nobis, ut perveniamus and intelligendum  quae
credimus (In Boettii De Trinitate, Proem., q.  I,  a.  I  ad  2m,  6m,  8m).
(Ibid. pp. 36-39).

      "... concerning Thomas and his theology. It was born,
in  the  mind  of  Thomas  Aquinas,  of  his  effort  to  achieve  a  better
understanding of the Xtian faith in the light of his  own  metaphysics.  The
fruit of that effort is  found  in  his  Summa  Theologiae,  where  all  the
rejuvenated metaphysical principles are set to work and  are  seen  to  bear

      "True wisdom, Thomas once  said,  does  not  consist  in  knowing  the

      "Here was a  new  world  of  philosophical  intelligence,  a  new  and
technical language, a new  and  sometimes  barbarous  vocabulary,  indeed  a
whole new map of reality, extending  from  man  to  God  and  including  the
foundations of philosophical and scientific method, as well as a vast  array
of technical questions that Christian  thought  had  never  asked  from  the
beginning of its existence. Here was a new challenge and  a  new  adventure,
here was Lady Philosophy
making its appearance not as a syumbol in Boethius' book  but  as  a  living
reality, personified in the Latinized Aristotle as the  Philosopher  and  in
Averroes his great commentator. Here  also  was  human  intelligence  itself
such as Christians had never seen before, procalaiming its  rights  and  its
privileges  as  intelligence  by  an  appeal  to  the  unassailable  bar  of
judgment: its own principles  and  demonstrations,  the  guiding  lights  of
human rationality as well  as  the  guardians  of  the  integrity  of  human
intelligence itself. Here, in  sum,  was  a  philosophiy  pure  and  simple,
creation and image of the human intellect, teaching what man could know  and
how far his knowledge could extend and was not to be denied.
      "... Here the Parisian theologians recognized that,  in  the  face  of
Aristotelian philosophy, they had to  formulate  an  intellectual  synthesis
that suited ont only Aristotelianism but also themselves;  in  other  words,
they had to formulate their own intellectual view of  the  universe  in  the
presence of the philosophical one they found in Aris and  his  commentators.
They had to formulate a doctrinal synthesis that was  religious  because  it
was Christian and that, at the same time,  could  meet  and  assimilate  the
philo  of  Aris  in  an  authentic  way.  The  name  of   this   new   Xtian
intellectualism, first formulated at the University of Paris,  is  theology,
or rather, scholastic theology..."  (Anton  C.  Pegis,  "In  Search  of  St.
Thomas Aquinas," in The McAuley Lectures, 1966, p. 8).

      "The U of Paris created an intellectual Xtianity in order to meet  and
assimilate  the  intellectualism  of  the  philosophers  and,  chiefly,   of
Aristotle... this new Xtian reality before us, namely, scholastic  theology,
fruit of the transformation  of  patristic  and  monastic  Xtianity  into  a
Xtianity that, in addition to speaking the traditional  language  of  piety,
meditation and devotion, now spoke the  great  truths  of  Xtianity  in  the
scientific language discovered  and  formulated  by  human  intelligence  as
such. Consider a few examples.
      "Where, up to the thirteenth century, God had been  a  Creator  and  a
Father, He now became also the Prime Mover, the Pure Act of Being,  and  the
Necessary Being. Where the human soul had been the center of man, the  image
of God that St.  Augustine  had  explored  so  persistently  and  with  such
anxiety, it now also became a physical reality,  the  form  of  matter,  and
somehow  a  citizedn  of  the  world  of  physical   nature.   Where   human
intellligence had been the servant of God, seeking Him beneath  the  surface
of every truth and every good in creation, it was  no  also  the  scientific
student of nature, caught within the intellligibile  structures  of  things"
(Ibid. p. 9).

      "Had the theologians of the U of Paris  merely  embarked  upon  asking
whether man the believer could  try  to  understand  the  divinely  revealed
truths he believed, they  would  have  been  continuing--but  no  more  than
continuing--the best tradition of the Fathers of the  Church.  What  had  St
Augustine, St Jerome and St Hilary, whom St Thomas quotes  on  this  precise
point, said except that man should press on  within  his  faith,  reverently
and humbly, seeking to understand  divine  truths,  but  always  remembering
never to measure his faith by his human understanding? [My  emphasis].  This
patristic  tradition  of  faith  seeking  understanding,  that  is,  of  the
believing reason seeking some understanding of what it  believed,  lived  on
in the thirteenth century; and it continued to  animate  then,  as  it  does
today, the spirit of Christian theology. But  the  thirteenth  century  went
much farther and added dimension to Christian theology. That is why  we  can
distinguish  between  patristic  theology  and  scholastic  theology.   (THE
DISTINCTION CAN BE SEEN IN TWO SUCCESSIVE ARTICLES OF ST tHOMAS'  COMMENTARY
ON THE DE TRINITATE OF  BOETHIUS  :  EXPOSITION  SUPER  LIBRUM  BOETHIII  DE
TRINITATE, Q. 2, AA. 1-2; ED. b. dECKER, lEIDEN; e. j. bRILL, L955, PP.  80-
90. THE FIRST OF THESE ARTICLES ASKS THE  PATRISTIC  QUESTION  UTRUM  DIVINA
LICEAT INVESTIGANDO TRACTARE, P. 80; THE SECOND ASKS A SCHOLASTIC  QUESTION:
UTRUM  DE  DIVINIS  POSSIT  ESSE  SCIENTIA  ,  P.  85).  Confronted  by  the
Aristotelian world of demonstrative knowledge, the schoolmen asked: can  the
Christian faith itself be put into the form of  a  human  science?  Can  God
speak to main in the demonstrative form of exposition  useld  by  the  human
intellect in human knowledge? Of  course,  man  as  man  cannot  presume  to
demonstrate within the world of revelation, or  otherwise  faith  will  lose
its transcendence and its merit... But God can demonstrate  what  is  to  be
found in revelation  since  He  sees  with  his  intelligence  what  He  has
revealed. Man himself cannot see it, but he can believe it on the  authority
of God, and his faith in a divinely revelaed world  of  truths,  present  in
him as a divine gift, links him directly to what God sees.  Hence  what  God
sees  and  man  believes  can  act  as  the  starting  point  of  a  humanly
constructed science, divine in origin and nature, depending  on  the  divine
mind for its possibility, and residing in the  believing  intellect  of  man
himself. This was, to put the point very briefly,  the  origin  of  what  we
call scholastic theology, a divinely-anchored  science  of  revealed  truths
whose human residence was a genuine vessel for these  truths  but  still  no
more than a vessel, that is to say, an intelligence  serving  through  faith
the human incarnation of a vision not its own...
      "... Scholastic atheology  contains  three  characteristics  that  are
immediately  important  for  our  purposes:   the   transcendent   role   of
revelation, the absolute harmony between revelation and human  intelligence,
the instrumental status and role of philosophy." (Ibid. 10-11).

       "But,  while  fully  aqdmitting  that  the  intellect  often  reasons
erroneously, though it need not, St Thomas  has  just  as  clearly  asserted
that, at the level of principles, from which reasoning begins and  in  which
it terminates, the intellect has an  infallible  understanding"  (Ibid.,  p.
11).

      "In the world of St. Thomas, only a genuine and free intelligence  can

      My reflections on the above: Faith, Opinion, and Reason

       -  "All  Aris's  works  are  full  of  interpretive  problems."   The
medievals' interpretation was a possible one among others. What   fascinated
them above all was  the  idea  of  science  presented  particularly  in  the
Posterior Analytics.

      - As such, the problem of faith and reason  arises  from  the  contact
between  Xtianity  and  Greek  philosophy.  What  Xtians  had  until   then,
inherited from Israel, was a historical understanding of themselves  and  of
the world. Their mode of existing the world was historical. What  they  came
to adopt--and to adopt  as  paradigmatic  of  knowledge--was  a  theoretical
understanding of the world. And a "foundational" one, at  that,  since  they
read the Posterior  Analytics,  as  it  is  possible  to  do,  as  based  on
intuitive and deductive evidence.
      The problem of faith and reason reached the level of full
awareness only in the twelvth-thirtheenth  centuries,  with  the  medievals'
encounter with Aris.
