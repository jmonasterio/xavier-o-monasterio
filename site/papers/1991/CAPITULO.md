                                     CAPITULO?
                             Epistéme and

      Aristotle  distinguishes  two  kinds  of  knowledge:  theoretical  and
practical. One of the points of distinction is that the former  is  for  its
own sake, the latter for the sake of application, of a practical  result  to
be obtained. Another point of  distinction  concerns  the  universality  and
necessity of the knowledge obtained; he who knows  something  but  does  not
know why has no epistéme; but does he who  knows  without  knowing  why  can
thereby be said to possess practical rather than theoretical  knowledge,  or
can one possess theoretical knowledge without
possessing epistéme? Furthermore, whether one can reach epistéme is said  by
Aristotle to depend on  the  object,  for  certain  objects  (for  instance,
"action," or "singular events," or probably even existents as such)  do  not
lend themselves to epistéme, but are not thereby considered by Aristotle  to
be  mere  beliefs  (for  instance,  his  refutation  of  scepticism  by  the
practical response of the sceptic. Is the knowledge of  what  is  impervious
to epistéme thereby practical rather than theoretical? In  other  words,  it
mikght be the case that epistéme is  an  equivocal  word  that  stasnds  for
different things: for knowledge for it own sake rather  than  for  practical
purposes, for knowledge of essence (universal and  necessary).  (And  notice
that   "essences"   are   connected   with    "definitions,"    (explanatory
definitions), but that there are definitions of meanings  of  words  and  of
nature of objects). If this is the case, knowledge of  action--the  kind  of
knowledge of which Aris speaks in his two Ethics--is not episteme in  either
sense, for it is both practical and contingent  (and  singular),  but  there
may be other forms of knowledge that are not epistéme oinly for one  or  the
other reason. Finally, at least in the  Posterior  Analytics,  there  is  an
opposition or a distinction between deductive and indictive  knowledge  that
might impinge on the way in  which  epistéme  and  practical  knowledge  are
understood by Aristotle.
      As if the above confusion were not sufficient,  there  is,  or  so  it
seems to me, a lack of distinction in Aristotle between the  perspective  of
the pure contemplating mind and the standpoint of the  exposed  agent  which
makes his distinction between  epistéme  and  practical  knowledge  suspect.
Epistéme seems to imply the  contemplating  mind,  the  pure  intellect,  to
which the notions of "facts" and "explanations of facts"  correspond,  while
practical knowledge seems to involve emotive and behavioral response.
      A further source of confusion for me is Aristotle's  understanding  of
appearances,  of  "what  we  say."  For  it  indicates  that  Aris   is   no
foundationalist, that his "principles" are not  grounded  on  intuition  and
beyond  dispute,  as  at  least  a  passage  from  the  Posterior  analytics
suggests. Does he mean what we say as members  of  a  community  of  exposed
agents?
      Al this has  repercussions  on  the  different  implicit  Aristotelian
taxonomies of science or knowledge, which are made from different points  of
view. It also has repercussions on the relationship  between  the  logic  of
statements, the logic of discussion or dialectic, the  logic  of  rhethoric,
and the logic of dramatic  literature.  Moreover,  it  may  imply  that  the
notion of truth as adaequatio mentis rei is spurious.
