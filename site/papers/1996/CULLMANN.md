CHAPTER  1:  THE  SIGNIFICANCE  OF  THE  NEW   TESTAMENT   TERMINOLOGY   FOR
TIME.

      Primitive Christian faith and thinking do not start from  the  spatial
contrast between thne here and the beyond, but  from  the  time  distinction
between formerly, now, and then. The visible -invisible course of events  is
completeley subjected to time. The essential is the distinction faith  marks
between tiemes. Faith is the assurance of thing hoped for--time.

      This is connected with Judaism, but for the first time  revelation  is
given in the NT an anchorage in time: the time line is consistently  carried
out in its central significance for salvation and faith.

      In this respect the terminology of the NT is characteristic:
emera, hora, kairos, chronos, aion (age) and aiones (ages).,  together  with
the emphatic nun (now) and semeron (today).

      The two ideas that most clearly elucidate the NT  conception  of  time
are usually expressed by kairos (a point in time  which  in  the  NT  has  a
fixed content) and aion (age, in the NT a defined  or  undefined  extent  of
time). Both are used to  characterize  the  time  in  which  the  redemptive
history occurs.

       Kairos  (opportunity):  the  moment   especially  favorable  for   an
undertaking--D-day. In secular usage human considerations cause  this  point
in time to be  specially  favorable.  The  NT  usage  refers  to  redemptive
history and the considerations are not human  but  a  divine  decision  that
makes this time a kairos for the divine plan of salvation because chosen  by
God.

      From the human point of view this  divine  choice  is  arbitrary:  the
kairoi which the Father in  his  soverign  power  has  fixed,  which  people
cannot  know  in  advance,  like  the  kairos  (decisisive  moment  of   the
eschatological drama: in the Apocalypse, in the Synoptics, in the Epistles.

      It is not merely in the faith of the Church after Xt  that  this  role
of a central kairos in the divine plan of savlation is ascribed to the  word
of Xt, but Jesus himself characterizes his passion as  his  kairos,  as  the
completion of his own work. And the   decisive  kairos  of  Xt's  death  and
resurrecttion and victory achieved over the  world  and  demons  is  already
announced in the (minor) kairoi of his earhtly career and miracles.

       Clearest  expression  in  John  7,  3:  "My  kairos  (*to  go  up  to
Jwerusalem) has not yet come; your kairos is always ready (for you there  is
no kairos in the redemptive history, you can go to Jerusalem any time).

      Upon the deed of Xt, there also exists in the present  period  of  the
Primitive Xtian Church a divine kairos for the believer--the present  kairos
whose significance is known to believers upon the basis of the  past  kairos
of Xt's death and resurrection.

      The line of divine kairoi--special points in time at  which  salvation
is effected, past, present and future--constitutes the redemptive line.

      Other words equivalent to kairos: "the  day  of  the  Lord"  (Jewish).
"The day and the hour" (John's Jesus's "hour".). The emmphatic  "now"  (nun)
employed in the NT  to  stress  the  fact  that  the  present  time  of  the
Apostolic age berlongs in an outstanding way to the redemptive  history  and
is thus distinguished from all other times.

CHAPTER 5: THE NEW DIVISION OF TIME FROM THE CENTRAL POINT OF
             REDEMPTIVE HISTORY

81-83: For Judaism, the decisive mid-point,  the  arrival  of  the  Messiah,
lies in the future. For Christians, the mid-point, the historical  life  and
work of JC, since Easter no longer lies in the future. This  recognition  is
of immense importance. In the latter, the part that  follows  the  mid-point
already belongs to the new age, although the final period starting with  the
Parousia has not yet come. Hence the entire attitude of the Primitive  Xtian
writings is radically different from that of  Judaism.  This  is  also  true
from the point of view of Jesus, for whom  Satna  has  already  fallen  from
heaven and he heals the sick and checks the power of death.

84: In Xtianity there is a tension "already-not  yet."  There  is  a  future
expected, as in Judaism, but the future event is no  longer  the  center  of
the redemptive history. The center has been reached but the end is still  to
come. D-Day has occurred, but the war goes on.  The  revelation  of  the  NT
consists in the proclamation that the event in the  cross  and  resurrection
was the already concluded decisive battle.

85-88: So Martin Werner and Albert Schweitzer are both  wrong;  they  regard
as the mid-point the future coming of the Messianic age.
      But though Xtianity is eschatological, toward a future expected,  that
future is not the center. The blind already see, the lame already  walk.  Vs
consistent  eschatology,  the  primary  thing  is  not  the   eschatological
expectation but the mighty fact of the resurrection. Hence whether there  is
a long or a short time till the Parousia is not primary. Since the "already-
not yet" is present with Jesus, it is imposible to represent  the  Primitive
Christian solution, in contrast to  the  eschatological  attitude  of  Jeuss
himself, as a "solution  inspired  by  embarrassment."  Xtianity  is  not  a
Jewish sect. The itensification of hope in Xtianity is to  be  explained  by
the fact that the center of time is not the object of hope  but  an  already
occurred historical fact. Hope in the future is supported by  faith  in  the
past. It is not true that the faith in  the  fulfillment  that  has  already
taken place in JC is a "substitute" for the unfulfilled expectation  of  the
immedioate coming of the Kingdom; on the contrary, this faith  produced  the
expectation, and the error about the immediacy is already  corrected  in  II
Peter, 3,8. And the hope of Paul suffered no loss in intensity or  anchorage
when he realized that he was going to die before the Parousia,  because  his
fixed point of orientation lied in the past. And the  "already-not  yet"  of
the judgment, in Saint John, is not a contradiction.

90-91: From the light of the mid-point  illumination  also  falls  upon  the
line that  extends  backward.  In  Judaism  the  entire  history  of  Israel
receives its  ligth  from  the  future  and  is  purely  eschatological;  in
Primitive Xtianity the history of Israel is now illuminated from the  (past)
new center. Even  the  entire  time  before  Creation  is  now  regarded  as
preparation for the redemption in JC. The  revelation  that  the  Crucified-
Risen is the center of the process permits us to recognize the  lordship  of
God over time, for in that point of time is gathered all that takes place.

92-93: Bultmann therefore is wrong in  reinterpreting  the  mid-point  in  a
metaphysical sense. It is mid-point in time, by  reference  to  what  really
precedes and really is still to come. Neither are the concepts
"sin" and  "redemption"  to  be  substituted  for  temporal  realities.  For
redemptive history, the calendar continues in force after Xt. It  is  not  a
new time that is created, but a new division of time.

CHAPTER  6:  THE  CONNNECTION  BETWEEN   HISTORY   AND   PROPHECY   (HISTORY
AND MYTH)

94-96: The  same  redemptive  line  includes  both  historically  verifiable
[Historical?] occurrences and things beyond the reach of historical  testing
[not historical?], such as sagas [Abraham?], which are set in  a  historical
framework, or myths, which deal with the processes of creation and nature.

      The NT makes no distinction between these: Adam  is  placed  upon  the
same plane as Jesus in Luke's genealogy. Complete lack  of  historical  [our
historical?] sense.

      We can get rid of the outmoded world view of the  Bible.  But  in  the
case we are considering there exists a positive theological ground  why  the
redemptive line must by its very  nature  unite  both  things,  history  and
myth. The failure to distinguish between history and  myth  belongs  in  the
outmoded world view of the primitive  Xtian  writers;  but  the  placing  of
history and myth  upon  one  common  line  of  development  belongs  to  the
essential core of the pritimitve xtian conception  of  salvation.  The  fact
that a myth is not historical  does  not  imply  that  the  happening  whose
account it preserves is not 'temporal.' If  the  connecting  of  primal  and
eschatological history with historical events were only the  result  of  the
inability of the primitive writers to separate the two then  the  connection
would be dispensable. But then the history of the primal  beginning  and  of
the eschatological drama would have to be loosed as timeless from  the  line
of time development. This is what Bultmann tried for  all  myths,  even  the
part that concerns actual historical events. Thus,  from  Genesis  only  the
'superhistorical' substance is retained: human "sin."  But  those  histories
are robbed of their NT significance when  stripped  of  their  character  as
redemptive history and of their connection with the  remaining  development.
It is essential in the figure of Adam that the second Adam comes after  him.
Bultmann is  wrong  in  seeing  all  redemptive  history,  NT  included,  as
dispensable, outmoded framework. But at least he  implicitly  concedes  that
in the NT and in primitive Xtianity the connection  between  historical  and
non-historical is so close that the difference betweken history and myth  is
unimportant, not because primitive Xtianity possesses no  historical  sense,
but because there is a positive theological outlook  kwhich  transcends  the
contrast between history and myth.

      Our question: How does the association of history and  myth  upon  the
redemptive line retain its validity when we know how to distinguish  between
them? Answer: The bond that unites both is prophecy in the mythical  and  in
the historical sections, for even the latter deal  not  only  whith  history
but with history viewed from the prophetic  point  of  view.  And  even  the
primal and eschatological processes of creation and  new  creation  are  not
understood in primitve Xtianity as timeless mytholgy,  but  spoken  only  in
connection with the historical process of the redemptive line.

97: The redemptive history as a whole is 'prophecy.' Here is the point  that
transcends the contrast between history and myth.
There is difference between historical and non-historical events;  but  from
the pont of view of prophecy this differemce  is  to  be  otherwise  defined
than it would be from  the  viewpoint  of  the  modern  distinction  between
history and myth. Both are prophecy, but primal and eschatological are  only
prophecy, while the middle section is open in part  to  historical  testing,
is prophecy of a  kind  that  refers  to  facts  that  can  be  historically
established, and it makes these facts an object of  faith.  And  even  these
are not presented as history, but as revealed  prophecy  concerning  history
[as divine PROMISE object of faith?]. Thus many  facts  of  the  history  of
Israel are historically testable, but not that it is  God's  chosen  people.
Same with the Virgin birth, firmly embedded in the midst  of  the  prophetic
presentation of the facts open  to  historical  verification  and  meant  to
bring out in a special way the prophetic character  of  this  very  history.
And the connection between both is  so  close  in  the  Gospel  that  it  is
impossible  to  distinguiksh  between  only   prophecy   and   prophetically
interpreted history (see 99: distinction between historical  and  verifiably
historical). Thus the important thing is that both are  prophecy,  both  are
revelation, which appeals not to historical tests but to faith.

100: Because the mid-point enlightens the whole line, the  whole  line  must
have the character of a development in time:  primal  history  tends  toward
Xt, eschatological proceeds from Xt. All nature, all things  are  reconciled
in Xt.

103: Important close connection in primitive Xtianity between the  invisible
powers or being who are at work behind all  visible  occurrences  and  which
are part of and subordinated to the history of salvation.

104-105: Even the Apocalypse  which  is  only  prophecy  and  in  which  the
angelic powers operate in a nonhistorical framework is  full  of  historical
references  to  the  Roman  Empire;  hereby  it  is  indicated   that   this
eschatological drama as well as the initial process of  creation  is  to  be
thought of as adevelopment in time.

105-106: If nonetheless we want to formulate  the  difference  between  only
prophecy  and  prophetically  interpreted  history  without  breaking   with
Primitive Xtianity, then we shall say that the mid-point  must  be  history,
for the reality of the Incarnation is at stake; the events that  lie  close,
both  backwards  and   forwards,   are   history.   The   primal   and   the
eschatological, in sofar as they don't belong in the  same  measure  to  the
incarnation, may be distinguished from the remaining process of  revelation.
But the chief fact is that the NT faith extends the  historical  incarnation
to the primal and the  eschatological.  History  and  myth  are  essentially
united by the comon denominators of both prophecy and development  in  time.
By contrast, where the  primal  and  the  eschatological  are  raised  to  a
timeless plane, then we have to  do  with  a  transformation  of  the  Xtian
faith.

CHAPTER 7: THE REDEMPTIVE LINE AS CHRIST LINE. THE MANNER OF
             PRESENTING IT

107:  Redemptive  history  was  not  revealed   to   the   early   Chrstians
chronologically. The starting point of  primitive  Xtian  understanding  was
the mid-point, backward and forward of which did the divine  plan  open  up.
This shows in our reckoning of time:  BC  and  AC.  Later,  however,  Xtians
understood that redemptive history had a beginning and an end,  that  is,  a
chronological sequence that we will describe now,  precisely  to  show  that
the entire line is actually constructed from the mid-point. For it is  a  Xt
line from the beginning, so that it is justified to speak of  Xt  and  time.
If we spoke before Xt's birth of  God  without  reference  to  Xt  we  would
destroy the line in its unity of content.

109: It is a Xt line: -  Xt  the  mediator  of  the  Creation  -  Xt,  God's
suffering servant as the one who fulfills the election  of  Israel  -Xt  the
Lord ruling in the present - Xt the returning Son of  Man  as  the  one  who
completes the entire process and is the Mediator of the  New  Creation.  The
Pre-existent One,  the  One  who  yesterday  was  crucified,  he  who  today
exercises hidden lordship, he who returns at the turn of the ages--they  are
all one and the same Xt, but in  the  execution  of  his  functions  in  the
successive  stages  of  time  in  the  redemptive   history.   Though   this
chronological sequence is seldom explicitly  sketkched  in  the  NT,  it  is
everywhere presupposed and it gives the key  to  the  understanding  of  all
that is said. Whether the discussion is of theological  or  ethical  nature,
it is always to be understood  by  reference  to  that  line.  The  line  of
redemptive history is thus not drawn completely every time a new subject  is
treated. Often it forms only the unspoken presupposition.  The  aim  of  all
primitive xtian preaching is to assign  to  every   concrete  happening  tht
occurs in the Church its place in the entire redemptive process.

110-112: The Gospels report the life  of  Jesus  only  in  the  interest  of
fitting it into its place in redemptive history. Mathew's  genealogy  starts
with Abraham,: fulfillment of the entire history of Israel. Luke  with  Adam
and the Creation. Mark and Luke go to the Baptist, who points  backward  and
forward. John starts with "in the beginning" in order to  show  that  He  is
the same who took flesh  and  will  return.  The  entire  NT  presupposes  a
unified conception of the redemptive process.

112: That the entire line is Xt line, that Xt did not merely appear for  the
first  time  at  a  definite  point  of  the  line  shows  in  the  earliest
confessional  formulas  of  the  NT.  The  majority  of  them   are   purely
Christological; only a small number also mention God the Father--unlike  the
threefold division of the Creeds that  are  first  attested  in  the  second
century, which obscures the  original  orientation.  The  faith  was  always
trinitarian, but the sharp  separation  of  the  three  articles  creates  a
perspective that does not correspond to the way in which  the  first  Xtians
summed up the fundamental features of their faith. The  latter  is  a  later
systematization. Xtians did not derive their faith in Xt  from  their  faith
in God the Creator, but had faith  in  the  Creator  in  the  light  of  the
Christocentric  line  of  salvation.  And  the   sections   concerning   the
forgiveness of sins were more closely connected to  Xt  than  in  the  later
three-part confessions. Most of the earliest confessions  contain  only  one
article--the Christological one. When the Father is mentioned  the  creation
is related to both the Father and the  Son;  the  subsequent  separation  of
functions is not yet sketched.

CHAPTER  8:  THE  DOUBLE  MOVEMENT  OF   THE   REDEMPTIVE   LINE   ACCORDING
       TO THE PRINCIPLE OF REPRESENTATION

114: That the redemptive line is not a dispensable framework of
Xtian preaching but belongs to its innermost nature is shown
finally in the further fact that in  movement of this  development  time  is
determined by a notably theological principle, namely, that of election  and
representation.

114: The fall into sin makes necessary redemptive history, for the curse  is
not God's last word. He bring about a further  time  process  which  removes
the curse of sin and death and  reconciles  man  with  himself,  and  brings
creation to a new creation in which death exists no more. The  principle  of
this process is that of election of a minority for  the  redemption  of  the
whole. Otherwise expressed, representation.

116: Man represents creation, which is  therefore  involved  in  the  curse.
Principle of selective representation that  becomes  ever  more  clear:  the
entire  creation  depends  on  the  action  of  man,  one   people   becomes
determinative for the salvation of  all.  A  further  reduction:  since  the
people of Israel as a  whole  do  not  fulfill  their  assigned  mission,  a
remnant appears as representative of the  people.  The  remnant  is  further
reduced to one man, the Servant  of  Yaweh  of  the  Deutero-Isaiah.  Hence:
mankind - people of Israel, remnant of  Israel  -  the  One,  Xt.  Here  the
redemptive history has reached its center. From  that  point,  an  important
change in the principle of movement. Still election and representation,  but
no more reduction. Now from the One to  the  many:  Xt  -the  Apostles  -the
Church as the Body of the One and fulfills for   mankind  the  task  of  the
remnant.

118: This double movement  of  redemptive  history  comes  most  clearly  to
expression in the sketch that Paul gives in Gal, from 3,6 to  4,7.  This  is
symbolically reflected in the year 1, which is the year of Xt's birth,  with
steadily increased numbers toward both ends.

       PART  TWO:  THE  UNIQUE   CHARACTER   OF   THE   REDEMPTIVE   EPOCHS:
"EPHAPAX"

CHAPTER  1:  THE  UNIQUE  CHARACTER  OF  THE   CHRIST-DEED   AT   THE   MID-
POINT

121: It is of the nature of the kairoi that constitute the  redemptive  line
that each of them corresponds to a unique, unrepeatable fact, once  and  for
all decisive. From the already demonstrated continuity  of  the  line,  this
nature applies to  every  point:  each  has  its  unique  redemptive  value.
Nonetheless, the mid-point has a unique significance.

121-122: The mid-point, Xt., is ephapax in a  primary,  founding  way.  But,
for instance, baptism is also something  decisive  and  whose  benefits  are
given once an for  all.  This  points  to  the  problem  of  "Scripture  and
Tradition."  It did not appear in these terms to  the  first  generation  of
Xtians, since no Scripture. But it is already implicitly given in  the  fact
that the mid-point is decisive for all periods, and yet the now between  the
mid-point and the parousia has its own unique value for redemptive  history.

122-125: The inter-relationship history-prophecy becomes clear in ephapax  +
once and  for all or decisively unique  for  salvation.  For  the  primitive
Xtians who had known Jesus and his relatives and  lived  in  the  same  land
this was particularly scandalous. In Judaism, since  the  mid-point  was  in
the eschatological future, it could be only the object of prophecy  and  not
of history in the past. The  NT  writers,  Hebrews,  Paul,  John  (the  Word
became flesh) put this scandal in the center.

125-129: We understand now why DOCETISM was the great theological heresy  of
ancient times, already opposed in the Epistles of John and his  gospel.  The
Jewish atachment to the Law shows that for them the death of Xt is  not  the
central event. Docetism (gnosticism) did not first appear  in  the  area  of
Hellenism outside of  Palestine.   Docetism  has  to  do  with  a  heretical
Christology found at diverse tiems and in the most varied hererical  systems
In its narrow sense, docetism maintains only an apparent body in Xt. The  NT
knows only a Christology in which the work and person of  Xt  are  connected
and which thus does not  consist  in  abstract  speculation  concerning  his
nature  (126).  A  less  narrow  variety  of  docetism  (and  its   meaning)
recognizes the real flesh of Xt, but among  the  events  of  the  Gospels  a
selection is made and only certain of them are recognized as  normative  for
salvation. Thus the crucifixion is denied on the basis  of  an  already-made
ideal view of the Redeemer. The common  denominator  of  both  varieties  of
docetism is the failure to respect the historically unique character of  the
redemptive deed of Xt (127). This is the germ of the  modern  position  that
what is decisive for salvation stands altoghether apart from history  (127).
The docetists of both types already know  everthing  in  advance.  For  this
reason, docetism was for primitive xtianity the  life-and-death  issue.  All
speculation on Xt's natures is un-Biblical as soon  as  it  ceases  to  take
place in the light of  the  great  historical  deeds  of  redemption  (128).
Docetism continues in all theological explanations which  make  a  selection
in the events narrated in the gospels, in order to recognize  only  some  as
theologically normative. This  choice  is  made  on  an  independent,  ideal
picture; it has nothing new to learn from the Xt who  appeared  in  history.
Here the prophetic, the "once for all" aspect is  privileged  in  a  docetic
manner.

129-130: Rejecting the docetic attitude does not exclude  distinction  among
facts in the Gospel.  From  the  beginning  there  was  distinction  between
central and peripheral facts. But the  center  can  be  found  only  if  one
derives the distinction from the NT  itself.  Our  reference  to  the  early
confesions of faith shows how this is possible.  In  the  short  confessions
there is only what was central to the primitive Church.

CHAPTER   2:   THE   PAST   STAGES   OF   REDEMPTIVE   HISTORY   AND   THEIR
 RELATION TO THE CHRIST EVENT AT THE MID-POINT

131: In successive chapters we shall show that past and future  and  present
stages in redemptive history are unique  redemptive  occurrences,  and  that
they are such only when related to the mid-point.
      The past from  Creation  to  Christ  as  set  in  the  OT  is  already
redemptive history, but  its  orientation  to  a  historical  event  is  not
recognized. Primitive Xtianity keeps everything from the OT;  it  only  adds
that this entire event of the Creation is to be intkerpreted from that  mid-
point. Paul writes that a veil lay over the books of Moses as long  as  this
understanding was not revealed. Thus the primitive Church did  not  do  away
with the OT, as Marcion claimed.

132-133: It was a problem for primitive  Xtianity  why  the  OT  after  Xt's
advent continues to exist as a xtian norm, as a  xtian  book.  Barnabas   in
his epistel seeks justification for that, but does it through unlimited  use
of the allegorical mekthod. All  the  details  of  the  life  of  Jesus  are
prefigured. Thus the OT becomes  a  book  of  riddles  and  its  content  as
revelation of redemptive history  that  moves  toward  Xt  as  its  goal  is
deprived of value. The OT as redemptive history is abandoned.  Time  has  no
importance: mere hidden timeless truths. And since all  the  truths  (rather
than actual events) are already in the OT, the NT becomes superfluous.  Thus
the Epistle of Barnabas is docetic.

134-136: That Epistle shows us negatively how the past stages of  redemptive
history toward Xt are not to be understood: the concept  of  fulfillment  is
not taken seriously; OT and  NT  are  interchangeable.  Right  reading:  the
course of  events  of  the  OT,  which  has  its  own  significance  in  the
redemptive history, constitutes in its distinctive character  a  preparation
for Xt, i.e., it prepares for the  interpreting  of  the  mid-point  of  the
entire redemptive process. (Thus the OT is not a  parallel  presentation  of
the same thing in another form. Probably this originates before the  NT  was
written, when Xtians wanted  to  see  preserved  the  contemporay  facts  of
redemptive history. The recognition that the entire  redemptive  history  of
the OT tends toward the goal of the incarnation is  the  understanding  that
is now possible in Xt.: preparation-fulfillment. From our knowledge  of  the
incarnate and crucified Xt we know how to  understand  the  past  events  of
redemptive history as preparation for incarnation and cross.  Only  now  can
it be seen how sin leads to incarnation and cross. In the OT we do not  find
the cross itself but the temporal preparation for the  cross.

137-138:  Objection:  How  can  a  temporal  development   which   is   only
preparation continue to have meaning for the believer in Xt after the  thing
prepared  has  already  been  realized  in  time?  Answer:  Because  the  OT
illuminates  the  Xt  event,  after  it  itself  has  been  illuminated   as
preparation by that very mid-point. The death and resurrection of Xt  enable
the believer to see in the history of Adam and of Israel a  preparation  for
Jesus. But ony in thus understood history of Adam  and  of  Israel  can  the
believer fully grasp the work  of  Xt,  the  crucified  and  risen  One,  in
connection with the diviene plan of salvation. Thus the OT continues  to  be
revelation even for the Xtian Church. As Son  of  Man,  Second  Adam,  Jesus
fulfills the destiny of the man created byu God;  as  Servant  of  Yaweh  he
fulfills the history of his people.  But  the  succession  in  time  is  not
obliterated. There is no identity  between  the  two  Testaments.  Primitive
xtianity takes time seriously both towards past and future.

CHAPTER  3:  THE   FUTURE   STAGES   OF   REDEMPTIVE   HISTORY   AND   THEIR
RELATION TO THE CHRIST-EVENT AT THE MID-POINT

139: We must remember that in primitive xtianity the  future  plays  a  very
different  role  from  that  it  plays  in   Judaism.   The   eschatological
oriientation of Xtianity is very different: the norm is not what is to  come
but what has already come. Eschatology  is  not  suppressed,  but  dethroned
both  chronologically  and  essentially.  But  this  is  connected  with   a
hightened expectation for the future. The "when"  does  not  have  the  same
meaning, and that is why Xt rejects it as a question.

140: Our salvation does not depend  on  what  is  to  come.  Then,  in  what
relation does the future stand to that which has already been done  for  our
salvation? How does the future complete the already done?
      The future is not, as it is in Judaism, the telos that  gives  meaning
to the whole. The end is JC who has already  appeared.  However  eschatology
continues to possess its own significance for redemptive history.  Primitive
xtianity thinks eschatologically, but no longer in  a  "consistently",  that
is, exclusively, eschatologgical, manner.

141: This is not to devalue the eschatological future. It possesses its  own
uniqueness as redemptive. Just as V-Day presents something new,  so  the  V-
Day, though the latter depends on the  former.  The  new  thing  that  V-Day
brings in addition is that the Holy Sppirit lays hold of  the  entire  world
of the flesh, of matter. According to primitive Xtianity only Xt  rose  from
the dead. Other glorious bodies do not  yet  exists.  But  at  the  end  the
Sppirit will lay hold of our mortal bodies.  Thus  Xtianity  does  not  fall
into a purely otherworldly sphere; the final setting is the earth,  and  the
Spirit creates anew the whole of matter.

142: Till that final completion happens the Spirit penetrates the body  only
temporarily: only temporarily are sickness  and  death  repelled.  Lazarus's
resurrection was not final. Only in the final  resurrection  will  the  dead
really rise to live in a spiritual body. And  the  completion  willl  affect
both heaven and earth.
      When the D-Day has already occurrred, it is only understandable  human
curiosity  to  know  the  day  and  the  time  of  V-Day.  The  question  is
theologically unimportant.

142-143: The expectation of the new creation still to  come  roots  entirely
on the already fulfilled fact of Xt's resurrection., as emphasized by  Paul.
(I Cor, ch. 15).
CHAPTER   4:   THE   PRESENT   STAGE   OF   REDEMPTIVE   HISTORY   AND   ITS
RELATION TO THE CHRIST-EVENT AT THE MID-POINT

144-146: The problem of the  significance  of  the  present  for  redemptive
history and of its relation to the midpoint is more complex  than  the  case
of the past and the future, for it lies in the  middle  of  the  final  time
before the end and, thus, stands in a diferent relation: it is  already  the
time of the end, and yet it is not the end.. This tension  finds  expression
in the entire theology of primitive  Xtianity.  The  tension  of  this  time
between D-Day and V-Day is the silent fundamental presupposition of  the  NT
books, whose dialectic is not between the world and the Beyond,  or  between
time and eternity, but between present and future.

146-147: The present has its proper significance, contrary to  Irenaeus  who
tends to make everything jump from the mid-point  to  the  end.  In  another
way, Kierkegaard with his "contemporaneity'  mistakes  the  significance  of
the present for redemptive history. He is wrong: we do not go  back  to  the
time of Xt, Xt crucified and risen comes to us, and He  sits  at  the  right
hand of God now, today. Thus Catholics have  a  point  in  absolutizing  the
time of the Church, but in not subordinating tradition  to  Scripture;  they
tend to attribute to  the  present  what  primitive  Xtianity  says  of  the
future. Also  in  later  Catholicism  the  relationship  between  tieme  and
eternity is not determined in the same way as in the NT.  As  Paul  says  in
Ro. 13,11, "our salvation is now nearer than when we first believed."

148-150: A preliminary question: Did the Jesus of the Synoptics expect  such
an intermediate period between his death and the Parousia?
      Schweitzer denies this. For him, in the first part of his ministry  Xt
expected the end in his lifetime; in the second part, to coincide  with  his
death;. BUT no trace of such distinction in the Synoptics. Only  the  latter
part--that Xt expected his death to  be  decisive  for  redemptive  history,
even if nowhere is it expressely said that  his  death  coincides  with  the
final end--is in the Synoptics. In fact we find  texts  that  support  both:
that the end coincides with his death, and that the Son  of  Man  will  come
only at a later time, though perhaps a short one. See Mk  14,  62;  and  13,
10, and 9,1; and  12,  18;  and  14,  28.  Thus  Xt  did  indeed  expect  an
intermediate period between his death and the end.

150: Given this, He had to expect a role for his disciples to  play  in  the
intermediate: this is what  the  early  Church  clearly  understood  as  the
period of the Church,  of the earthly Body of  Christ,  which  begins  right
after the Ascension (Acts 1, 11). It is the period of  the  Kingdom  of  Xt,
which will end with the  Kingdom  of  God,  when  Xt  shall  have  subjected
himself to God (I Cor., 15, 28). In numerous texts  this  "Xt's  sitting  at
the right hand of God" is expressed. This shows that primitive Xtianity  was
particularly interested by this intermediate period.
152: Against consistent eschatology, this present time  interests  primitive
xtianity far more than  the  end.  The  desire  for  the  Parousia  is  only
expressed in prayer, not in  the  confessions  of  faith:  Maranatha  is  an
imperative.

153-154: The simplest confession of  the  present  Lordship  of  Xt  is  the
formula "Kyrios  Christos":  Xt  rules  as  Lord--uttered  at  the  time  of
martyrdom, in worship, in exorcisms. See Rom  10,9,  where  Paul  designates
every confession with the mouth as a confession of Kyrios Xtos., i.e.,  that
Xt sits at the right hand of the Father. All enemies  are  subject  to  him.
This subjection involves the tension already-not yet. Most of the  developed
confessions (Phil 2, 6 ff; I Peter 3, 22: the  confession  of  Polycarp,  ch
21; the confession of Ignatius in To the Trallians, ch. 9,1) emphasize  that
invisible powers are subjected to Xt.
154: Church and Kingkdom of Xt coincide in time: the Church is  the  earthly
center from which the full  Lordship  of  Xt  becomes  visible;  it  is  the
crucified and risen body of Xt. The Church is the  place  where  the  spirit
performs miracles typical of the eschatological period, and  yet  the  flesh
still rules. The Church is God's gift of salvation, yet it  is  composed  of
sinful  people:  tensions  between  Peter  and  Paul,  Paul  and   Barnabas,
murmurings of the faithful.

155: The eschatological miracle of the Church is above all realized  in  the
assemblies of worship crowned by the Supper  celebrations.  Maranatha  means
both "Come to us who are assembled in thy name," and "Come  finally  at  the
end."

156-157: The missionary proclamation of the Church,  its  preaching  of  the
Gospel, gives to the intermediate period its meaning for redemptive  history
through its connection with  Xt's  present  lordship.  This  is  the  unique
character of the present. It is the preaching of what has  already  happened
at the mid-point. The Holy Spirit  makes  the  preaching  understandable  to
people of different languages.  Ye  shall  be  my  witnesses  in  Jerusalem,
Judea, Samaria, and uibnto the end of the world (Act 1, 8).
158-160: The end will come only when the gospel shall  be  preached  to  all
peoples (not when all shall be converted): the having been preached  becomes
the sign of the end. (Prechristian judaism is  familiar  with  the  mission,
but not as a condition of the coming of the Messiah).

160-167: NT's texts concerning the preaching of the gospel as  a  condition-
sign of the end. Particularly Paul with his compulsion to preach the  gospel
everywhere, even in Spain. But the Reformers were wrong in saying  that  the
gospel had already been preached everywhgere.  "It  belongs  rather  to  the
nature of the sign that to the very end it appears in every generation  that
belongs  to  the  present  intermediate  period...  Every  generation   must
proclaim the gospel to the nations of their time, without being troubled  by
the question as to whether their ancestors had already done that.

167-168: Now we can understand the  lofty  consciousness  of  the  primitive
Church  of  carrying  forward  redemptive  history  as  an   instrument   of
redemptive activity: "Reigning together  with  Xt"  (I  Cor  4,  8),  "royal
people (Rev. 1, 6; I Peter 2, 9). This is different  from  the  mission   of
Judaism, because the Christ-event as the mid-point no  longer  lies  in  the
future but is known as an historic fact.

168: On the basis of this understanding of the present, we  have  to  reject
all those transformations of the  primitive  Christian  conception  of  time
that play with the concept of "eternity  invading  time."  And  reject  also
Kierkegaard's contemporaneity, which overlooks  the  fact  that  Christ  now
reigns in heaven and earth, and works visibly in  and  through  the  Church.
This does not detract from the fact of the supremacy of  the  mid-point  and
its foundational character. The ecclesiastical "tradition" is not  of  equal
importance as the mid-point. The Church cannot be  absolutized  without  its
place in time being deformed. The center is  neither  the  present  nor  the
future (parousia) but the earthly work of Christ, which in certain  Catholic
explanations of the eucharistic presence is  devalued  by  contrast  to  the
present.

170: At about A. D. 150 the Church created the canon as a  norm  to  subject
all further tradition to the Scripture's  control.  The  Church  showed  her
understanding that the distance from the mid-point was  such  that  the  now
could not anymore be considered  as  part  of  the  mid-point.  The  obscene
legendary narrative of Judas's death by Papias,  compared  to  the  same  in
Math and Acts, shows  how  reasonable  was  the  Church's  distrust  of  the
tradtion taken alone.
The ancient tradition was codified  as  Scripture,  and  the  now  beginning
further tradition was to be  cotrolled  and  judged  on  the  basis  of  the
earlier one  as  the  rule  of  faith.  "In  opposition  to  the  Protestant
narrowing of the Primitive Xtian faith, however, it must be emphasized  that
the ancient Church, by thus setting up the canon, did not intend to  prevent
completely the rise of a further Church  tradtion:  the  redemptive  history
would continue to develop, but only from the fixed orientation point at  the
mid-point  could  one  recongize  where  this  redemptive   history   really
continues. Thus the Scripture itself must, so to spek, be regarded  as  also
belonging to the center. The fact that  thes4  books  were  written  in  the
apostolic age was at a later time necessarily  included  in  the  redemptive
happening in the mid-point. From the second half of the second  century  the
entire apostolic age was regarded as the time of the  unique  foundation  of
the Chrch. Since this is so,  a  saying  that  in  ther  NT  refers  to  the
apostolic calling cannot be refered without  further  ado  to  the  bishop's
office. The apostolic calling is unique  (epaphax),  non  transferable:  the
Apostle in the NT is the  witness  to  the  resurrection  of  Xt.  Preaching
continues, but the foundation of the  apostles  who  "have  seen  the  Lord"
cannot continue. "Therefore even the famous saying of Jesus  concerning  the
Church (Mth. 16,18) cannot furnish  biblical  justification  of  the  papacy
(172).
The saying is authentic and is directed to the person of Peter, but only  to
him as the once-for-all established foundation of thre Church.

173-174: In opposition to a Catholic absolutization of the  Church  we  have
underscored  the  subordination  of  the  present  to  the   mid-point;   in
opppostion  to  a  cramped  Protestantism  we  have  emphasized   that   the
redemptive history has continued  to  advance  from  the  Ascencion  to  our
present period.

      PART IV: REDEMPTIVE HISTORY AND THE INDIVIDUAL MAN

CHAPTER  1:  THE  INDIVIDUAL  MAN  AND  THE   PAST   STAGE   OF   REDEMPTIVE
HISTORY (FAITH AND ELECTION)

217: One distinguishing feature of the NT writings is that, by  contrast  to
Judaism which concerns itself with mankind as such, it  enhances  the  worth
of the individual man. How is this connected with the entire   structure  of
redemptive history?
217-219: The revelation of the development  of  the  great  divine  plan  or
salvation makes a personal claim  on  each  one;  it  determines  his  life:
hidden in Christ, having died with Christ,  having  risen  with  Christ  who
sits at the right hand of God, and to be revealed with Him  in  glory  (Col.
3, 1-4). Dependence of the individual in a process  that  unfolds  in  time,
not in a mystical-mythical process. Faith, in the NT sense  is  the  way  by
which the past phase of redemptive history becomes effective for me.  It  is
the acceptance that the entire process concerns me  personally  and  that  I
have to participate through my remorse and repentance in the guilt  and  sin
of mankind. Without sin, my sin, no redemptive process.  The  NT  shows  how
the redemptive process  affects  the  individual.  The  connecting  link  is
faith: the redemptive process is for my  sake.  Faith  in  my  own  election
implies that I as an individual belong from  the  beginning  in  the  divine
providential administration.

220: In the NT predestination means both personal and collective  salvation.
In Rom Paul speaks ofpredestination in reference to  the  people  of  Israel
and the Gentiles and also  of  his  own  predestination  as  Apostle.  Every
single memeber of the Church is elected "from the beginning  of  the  world"
(Eph. 1, 4) and has his own charisma in the body of Xt (I Cor., 12,  4  ff).
And the election of Xt is spoken in exactly the same terms as  the  election
of the believers: "loved before the foundation of the world"  (Jn  17,  24),
"foreknown" (I Pet 1, 20).

221: Thus the individual stands related to the past phase of  redemption  in
that the entire processs occurred for him, and in that he  is  foreordained.
Of course the active participation emerges only in the  Church  and  at  the
present time, during the individual's life.

CHAPTER  2:  THE  INDIVIDUAL  MAN  AND  THE  PRESENT  STAGE  OF   REDEMPTIVE
HISTORY (THE GIFT OF THE SPIRIT AND THE COMMANDMENT OF         GOD)

222: The man who lives in the present  naturally  lives  in  a  particularly
close relationship to the  present  of  redemptive  histroy  marked  by  the
Lordship of Xt and by what takes place in  the  Church;  he  therefore  also
shares in the temporal tension characteristic of the present.

222: The present is the time of the Church. Through baptism  the  individual
finds his exact place in it. The Church mediates for him the forgiveness  of
sin and the Holy Spirit--the gift that marks the present and future  stages.
The individual gifts or charismata of the individual  are  destined  to  the
service of the Church. Thus the individual's significance  emerges  only  in
connection with the Church.

223-224:  Paul's  strong  consciousnees  of  his  predestination   for   the
Apostolate as his mode of personal participation in the  redemptive  process
is a lofty example, but even the most moedest service in the  Church  of  Xt
belongs in the redemptive history.

224: Thus the divine calling of the  individual  is  determined  by  the  Xt
event. Hence in Primitive Xtianity ethics  without  theology  is  absolutely
inconceivable. The imperative 'ought' is anchored in  the  indicative  'is.'
We are holy, means  we  should  sanctify  orselves.  We  have  received  the
Spirit, means we should walk in  the  Spirit.  This  is  an  aspect  of  the
tension fulfilled-not yet fulfilled.

225: Because the  believer  knows  that  he  is  on  the  way  between  Xt's
Resurrection and the Parousia, the fulfillment in which all imperatives  are
removed and the consummation still to  come,  the  Primitive  Xtian  'ethic'
does not consist in new commandments but in the demand  that  the  believer,
oriented toward past and future, should recognize ever anew at  each  moment
the  commandmment  that  the  situation  at  that  time  presents.  He  thus
'fulfills' the old law, knowing that each of his actions is  connected  with
the advance of redemptive history.

226: The Old T deals with a situation different from that of  the  New,  and
the ethical task, according to the NT, is that in every  new  situation  one
is to fulfill the OT in the light of the New.

226-227: In the sermon of the Mount there is  the  "but  I  say  onto  you,"
which sets the Old T decalogue in the light of the imminent Kingdom of  God.
Thus it would be a dangerous error to make the concrete  examples  given  by
Jesus as a new general law. When the ethical instructions of Jesus  are  not
seen as paradigmatic, contradictions in the sayings  of  Jesus  arise.  Each
word of Jesus is to be understood,  not  as  a  law,  but  in  its  concrete
application.

228: This is also  Paul's  position  concerning  the  ethical  question:  no
ethical demands but concrete applications in the light of the indicative  of
redemptive history. In Rom ch 6 the walking in the Spirit is not defined  in
terms  of  new  commandments  but   in   the   testing   or   discriminating
(dokimazein), i. e. the  capacity  of  forming  the  correct  Xtian  ethical
judgment at each given moment. In Phil 1,9 ff, and 2, 13,  Paul  prays  that
we attain that dokimazein.
229: It is characteristic that Paul does not reach his conclusions by  using
sayings of Jesus but adds his own insturctions,  for  all  instructions  and
examples are only paradigms. Paul's instructions (even concerning  marriage,
I Cor., ch 7), are of the form of Jesus' teaching concerning the Sabath:  if
this condition obtains, do this, but if not act otherise.
This does not exclude the  fact  that  from  the  indicative  of  redemptive
history a principle of application--the principle  of  love,  in  which  the
entire law is fulfilled--emerges. John's epistles have as their  theme  this
imperative; they are the catechism of the ethic of the  NT.  The  NT  itself
sets no actual catechisms, no general ethical rules. This will be  done  for
the  first  time  by  the  Apostolic  Fathers  in  whom  the  indicative  of
redemptive  history  and  of  the  atoning  work  of  Xt  recedes  into  the
backgorund or vanishes. In the NT, by contrast,  there  are  only  rules  of
faith--indicatives--which summarize the work of Xt.

CHAPTER  3:  THE  INDIVIDUAL  MAN  AND  THE  FUTURE  STAGE   OF   REDEMPTIVE
HISTORY (RESURRECTION FAITH AND RESURRECTION HOPE)

231-233: The Greek belief in the immortality of the  soul  as  well  as  the
opinion of the individual's resurrection immediately after his death  before
the entire creation is released from the power of  death  are  not  part  of
Primitive  Xtianity.  In  it  the  starting  point  of  hope  is   not   the
individual's concern for his individual happiness nor can the Xtian hope  be
derived  solely  from  the   egocentric  striving  of  man  for  blessednes.
(Protests against  Barth's  interpretation  of  individual  resurrection  as
under philosophical influence). Here  the  tension  fulfilled-not  fulfilled
remains. The eschatological drama is no longer  the  center  but  keeps  its
significance. We have resurrected with Xt but will still arise only  at  the
end in the new creation. We shall show that those who have died in Xt,  even
as dead, do not yet belong in the future stage of redemptive history but  in
the present stage, whose characteristic is the Holy Spirit as "earnest"  and
as "firstfruits."

                             ..............

233-234: Death is seen in the NT in the light of redemptive history as  "the
last enemy" and in all its horror  as  the  price  of  disobedience;  Xt  is
seized with trembling and horror in Gethsemane. Only a Greek  like  Socrates
could see it as a friend, connecting it with the immortlity of the soul  and
its liberation from the body. In Xtianity the resurrection hope  presupposes
faith in creation. Because God is the creator also of the  body,  therefore,
by contrast to Hellenism, there must be bodily resurrection.

234-235: Even in the OT, by Jesus' time, the hope of resurrection  had  been
reached. But it was only 'hope' and had no relation  to  the  faith  in  the
past and  the  present.  By  contrast,  in  the  NT  resurrection  hope  and
resurrection faith are not added to faith but constitutive of it,  based  on
Xt's resurrection which has already occurred. This is new:  resurrection  is
spoken not only in the future but in the past and  in  connection  with  the
past. The omnipotence of death is once and for all ended.

235-236:  Since  Xt's  resurrection  has  already  taken  place,  the   Holy
Spirit(the firstfruits) has already entered into the realm of  the  physical
at least in the case of  one  body.The  Holy  Spirit  is  operative  in  the
present as the power of the resurrection. Thus baptism  is  a  "rising  with
Xt." (Rom. 6, 3-5). The fact that our full resurrection is future  does  not
mean that for now the resurrection power of the Spirit is only in our  inner
life. He operates upon the physical: healings and resurrections narrated  in
the gospels and in the Acts. Though not yet the final  victory  over  death,
in Christ the resurrection power is already at work.  It  is  the  messianic
time in which the NT places us: "the blind see..."

237-238: The resurrection hope, anchored on Xt's resurrection: this  is  the
uniquely new thing that the future will bring for the  individual.  Even  in
John's gospel, which  most  strongly  emphasizes   the  present  reality  of
salvation, places the raising of the body in the last day.

238-242: Does this mean that those who have died before  the  last  day  are
excluded for the time being,  unlike  us  who  are  still  alive,  from  the
resurrection power? No. The NT says that they are with Xt immediately  after
their death: "Today shalt thou be with me in  paradise" (Luke 23,  43).  And
see Phil, 1, 23; and II Cor 5, 1-10). But  these  passages  do  not  suggest
that the dead have alredy received the spiritual body. Both the  now  living
and the dead are under the resurrection power of the Spirit. Only  thus  can
it be understood Paul's desire to be with Xt (Phil. 1, 23; I Cor. 5, 8).  In
the Apoch (6, 9 ff) the souls of those who "were slain for the word of  God"
are already found "under the altar," especially near  to  God.  "Being  with
Xt" does not yet signify resurrection of the body, but a  closer  connection
with Xt effected through the resurrection power of the  Spirit.  Thus,  even
for the dead, the tension between present and future  still  exists.  As  to
how this intermediatre state is to be conceived, the  NT  never  speculates.
Saying  something  more  definite  and  speaking  of  purgatory  has  no  NT
foundation, and this concern for the how shows little faith.  The  same  has
to be said concerning the "how" of the resurrection of the body at  the  end
of days. All we know is that it will be a  "spiritual  body,  of  which  the
Spirit will not only be the principle but also  the  material  (I  Cor.  15,
35).
