The impossibility of reconstructing Pascal's own plan

a) The original documents at the time of Pascal's death

      In the preface to the Port-Royal edition Etienne Périer tells us  that
they found a number of packages  of texts ("liasses") not ordered  and  very
poorly written. The first thing they did was to make  a  copy.  As  for  the
autopgraph, the thoughts were glued as in an  album  but  not  before  1711.
There is also a hand-written copy  that  comes  from  the  Benedictine  Jean
Gerrier, who received it form Marguerite Périer, which probably was used  at
Port-Royal for the preparation of the first edition  in  its  original  plan
(1662-1669).

b) External authorities about the Apology

      - Filleau de la Chaise, "Discours sur les  pensées  de  M.  Pascal  où
l'on essai de faire voir quel était son dessein," published  in  1672  under
the name of Dubois de la Cour. It was meant to be the preface to  the  first
edition, but was too long (and perhaps opposed by Pascal's sister).
      -- Plan de l'Apologie d'après Mme Périer, sa  soeur,  tel  qu'elle  le
rapporte dans la vie de son frère.

c) The first edition--the "Port-Royal edition."

      - First attempt: to make a clean copy and publish the pensées  exactly
as Pascal left them. Disappointing result.
They thought of giving  up  the  idea  of  publishing.  This  was  the  idea
supported by Mme Périer, P's sister.
      - Second attempt: M de Roannez, P's close  friend,  who  had  attended
P's lectures on his Apologie de la religion chrétienne à Port-Royal,  wanted
to complete the work and to publish it  as  Pascal  had  dreamt  of  it.  He
submitted the project of his work to a committee: Arnauld,  Nicole,  Filleau
de la Chaise... The project is  stopped  by  the  stubborn  refusal  of  Mme
Périer, who wanted Pascal's text as he had left it.
      - The edition of Port-Royal (1670): Alterations of P's  text  to  make
it suitable to the new religious climate after kthe signature of  the  bull.
Omission of many thoughts and changed order.

d) Impossibility of reconstructing Pascal's own plan

      - Un relieur malencontreux [for the autograph] (Br. LI)
      - Many fragments don't belong: Provincials, Style...
(Br. LI).
      - Risk of attributing "to P himself some remark taken from  Montaigne,
some fragment or sentence... in which P is reporting a  saying  not  of  his
own, but one of the participants in his imaginary conversations  or  one  of
his characters" Steinman, 225).
      - Pascal gives us only general  indications,  and  he  changes  pieces
from one place to another, while he is aware of the importance of order  and
of the not ordinary order or disposition of the material he  wants  for  his
work: "I could easily have treated this discourse in  this  kind  of  order:
show the vanity of all kinds of conditions;, show  the  vanity  of  ordinary
lives, and then the vanity of  philosopher's  lives,  whether  sceptical  or
Stoic, but the order would not have beken kept. I know  something  about  it
and how few people understand it. No human science can keep it.  St.  Thomas
did not keep it. Mathematics keeps it , but it goes sofar as to be  useless"
(P. 246:694). And "Let no  one  say  that  I  have  said  nothing  new;  the
arrangement of the material is new. In playing tennis both players  use  the
same ball, but one player is better. - ... As if the same thoughts  did  not
form a different argument by being differently arranged, just  as  the  same
words make different words when arranged differently."
      - Filleau de la Chaise: Discours  sur  les  pensees:  Those  who  have
tried to follow it are in disagreement. He wrote 8 years  after  P's  death,
and 12 years after Pascal's speech; thus this speech  did  not  include  the
thoughts written between 1658-1662. Big discrepancies  with  Mme.  Perrier's
version, probably written  three  years  after  Pascal's  speech,  and  thus
refers to a more recent view of the Apology by Pascal. Finally, P,  who  was
the genius  who  wrote  the  Provinciales,  wanted  a  dramatic  order  (not
analytic or logical order but persuasive order that motivates) and does  not
seem to have reached a final decision.
      - The order of the  first  edition  by  the  Port-Royal  people  lacks
authority: they themselves gave up the idea of a Pascalian  order  that  the
duc de Roannez wanted, they had  to  deal  with  the  protestations  of  Mme
Périer, and their final option is a third order that satisfied nobdoy.

CONCLUSION, as summarized by Steinman:

      "The 'dialogue' or 'dramatic' form which the  finished  Apology  would
have had has become more and more evident. Commentators have  realized  that
we have to be highly circumspect... (Steinman, p. 225).
      "All that reaches us from the navigator  of  the  infinite  are  words
which are sometimes as  disconnected  as  the  SOS  of  a  shipwrecked  man"
(Steinman, 226).
      "It is impossible to discover the order in  which  they  were  written
for the very good reason that not  all  his  notes  were  intended  for  the
Apology  and that the plan of the work had not reached finality at the  time
of his death" (Steinman 227). [Also the order and  the  form...  were  under
consideration and different possibilities studied].

The axes or links

      "But if it is impossible to discover the thread  of  the  Apology,  it
must be recognized that links exist between the fragments of the  Pensées...
the role of the atheist, the idea of  the  infinite,  human  contradictions,
the meaning of the Bible, the nature of the Church...--such are the axes  of
Pascal's thought" (Steinman 227).

      THE LINKS AND AXES  MENTIONED  BY  STEINMAN  ARE  TOO  VAGUE.  CAN  WE
MENTION OTHERS, PERHAPS MORE IMPORTANT ONES? (Brunschwicg)
      - Considerations on order, style, type of argumentation.
      - A psychological study of man without God: "Misère  de  l'homme  sans
Dieu."
      - Philosophy leads nowhere: between scepticism and dogmatism.
      - One has to make a decision (not deciding is impossible).
      - Christianity (to the exclusion of Jews  and  Muslims)  is  the  true
religion: Perpetuity, Figuratifs, Prophecies, Jesus-Christ, Miracles.

Pascal's authors

      "Montaigne, Charron et Méré d'une part--de l'autre,  Jansenius,  saint
Augustin et l'Ecriture, voilà,  semble-t-il,  toute  l'Apologie  que  Pascal
méditait" (Br. XCIV).
      Educated at home by his father, Pascal had escaped Scholasticism,  had
no philosophical or theological formal culture, and had read  at  most  some
Stoics.
      He knew well the Augustinus of Jansenius.

      (a) Augustinus:
            Title: Saint Augustine's teaching on  the  health,  the  illness
and  the  'medicine'  of  human  nature,  against  the  Pelagians  and   the
Marsilians  [semi-pelagians],  in  three  sections.  (An  appendix  makes  a
parallel between the Marsilians and  some  moderns--three  jesuits:  Suarez,
Vasquez and Molina).
            From the beginning Jansenius identifies "the ultimate  roots  of
Pelagius' heresy and  of  all  the  enemies  of  grace  [with]  philosophy":
Through Origen who borrows from the Stoics, a trust in human nature  and  an
exaltation of freedom that renders salvation by a Mediator superfluous.

      (b) The Bible:
            P read it constantly and knew it almost by heart.
            The figurative character of the Old  Testament  comes  from  the
New Testament, and is underscored by Paul and Augustine.
(According to the Bible of Royaumont which  Fontaine  put  together  for  Le
Maître de Saci, the Old Testament contains 183 figures).
            The Pugio christianorum  ad  impiorum  perfidiam  jugulandam  et
maxime judaeorum by Raymond Martin,  a  Dominican  from  the  XIII  century,
reprinted   in   1650,   quotes   in   Hebrew   and   translates   Rabinical
interpretations; Pascal reads it as a textbook of Rabinical exegesis.

      (c) Apologists:
            Charron:
            Grotius,  Traité  de  la  religion  chrétienne  (P  borrows  his
polemic against Mahomed.
            Raymond Sebond (through Montainge)
            Balzac, Socrate chrétien

      (d) Montaigne: Borrows from him his  experience  of  the  human  being
(See Entretien avec Monsieur de  Saci):  "First  Part:  Adam  by  Montaigne.
Second Part: God by JC."

The novelty and appeal of Pascal

      "Pascal produced a complete upheaval inside  apologetics  [WHAT  ABOUT
THEOLOGY, A NEW WAY OF THEOLOGIZING (?) GROUNDED IN  A  "PRACTICAL"  9RATHER
THAN THEORETICAL0 UNDERSTANDING OF FAITH],  a  revolution  as  important  as
that of Descartes [in philosophy], but in the reverse sense which  consisted
in detaching the proofs of  Xtianity  from  their  traditional  metaphysical
foundations and replacing philosophy by  psychology  and  history  [EXPOSED,
VULNERABLE,   PRACTICAL,   DELIBERATIVE   PARTICIPANT'S   THINKING]...,   an
apologetic that would pursue the atheist  into  his  lair,  into  the  place
where the roots of  his  affections  and  his  feelings  reached,  into  the
unconscious depths of his heart... It is in fact in  this  mysterious  crypt
[the human heart] that according to Pascal the drama of salvation is  played
out. The rest--proofs, refutations,  metaphysical  discourses--were  without
any power to convince" (Steinman, 175).

      "Rationalists... have accused P of scepticism or  fideism"  (Steinman,
248).

      "La religion doit être vraie pour l'homme, et elle ne peut être  vraie
que contre l'homme. Cette  contradiction,  que  les  contemporains  essaient
d'attenuer ou de  dissimuler,  apparaît  a  P  comme  la  raison  d'être  du
christianisme. C'est trahir  Jésus  que  de  le  mettre  à  la  remorque  de
Descartes, comme la  theoilogie  de  l'école  l'avait  mis  à  la  remmorque
d'Aristote paien ou de  Philon  juif:  'Pour  les  religions  il  faut  être
sincère: vrais païens, vrais juifs, vrais chretiens.' -  On  comprend  alors
la deestinée de Pascal dans l'histoire de l'esprit humain. Il ne  s'est  pas
contenté de nier la vérité de la philosophie moderne; il a refusé  d'aborder
le problème de la vérité dans les termes nouveaux où  cette  philosophie  le
pose. Tandis que le cartésianisme inaugure la méthode  critique  qui  devait
transformer les conditions de la pensée,  Pascal  emprunte  à  Montaigne  le
cadre du dogmatisme antique...  (Br  p.  XCIX).  -  "...  certains  penseurs
catholiques ont tenté de réédifier l'apologetique sur la base  psychologique
et morale que Pascal lui avait assignée, ils  ont  tracé  entre  l'homme  et
Dieu une voie de continuité  qui  contredit  expressément  la  transcendance
radicale des  ordres  pascaliens  [la  méthode  d'immanance  de  Blondel  et
Laberthonnière]. Pour entendre des  accents  qui  pourraient  être  rappelés
après les Pensées de Pascal, on est réduit a sortir de son église..."  ["...
on songe à Lamenais et aux Paroles d'un Croyant, à Tolstoï et  à  la  Lettre
au Synode,  on  songe  surtout  à...  Soren  Kierkegaard.  Tout  dans  Soren
Kierkegaard manifeste cette remarquable parentée, depuis les titres  de  ses
ouvrages: La maladie à la mort, l'Exercice dans le  christianisme,  jusqu  à
ses âpres attaques contre ceux qui sont en vêtements  longs"]  (Brunschvicg,
pp. C-CII)
      As for "academics," Pascal had no high regard for them: "... there  is
nothing to be said to them, not out of contempt, but because  they  have  no
common sense. God must touch them. - People  of  that  kind  are  academics,
scholars, and that is the nastiest kind of man I know" (p. 275:432).

      "Not the false  symmetry,  the  mathematical  order  of  Descartes  or
Spinoza. Human reason is not the measure of the world: P seeks a true  order
which is not only resigned to mystery, but loves it" (Steinman, 227).

      According to Filleau de la Chaise, Pascal finished by  pointing   that
"Not only do the proofs of xtianity in their own order depend on the  heart,
but the same is true of their efficacy. 'Not only the things  that  must  be
felt depend on the heart, but also those belonging  to  the  mind  when  the
heart may have some part in them..'.. Since God is personal,  the  relations
between hate, indifference or love towards him place all  the  proofs  in  a
different light." (Steinman, 176)

      "In The Art of Persuasion he shows that there exists a whole order  of
truths which depend less on demonstration than on an  effort  of  the  will.
Everything which causes us displeasure demands, in  order  to  be  believed,
the consent of our hearts" (Steinman, 182)

       "It  is  not  barbara  and  baralipton  which  form   our   reasoning
process..." (Steinman 184). Cf. Newman on logic.
      "P sets great store by the intuitive  knowledge  of  the  heart...  It
places us in contact with things and beings, while reason  apprehends  ideas
and concepts...  In his analysis of the highest form  of  thought  P  opened
the way for the profound aperçus of  Newman.  The  heart  is  the  'illative
sense' of The Grammar of Asssent" (Steinman, 246).

      "P's  atheist  admires  and  enjoys  Montaigne  more  than  Descartes.
Montaigne is his chosen master. P approves of his choice" (Steinman 231).

       "Is  dogmatism  any  more  solid  in  its   unbending   pride   [than
scepticism]? It, too, comes into conflict with an  unsurmountable  obstacle:
our uncertainty 'whether man is created by a good God, a wicked devil or  by
chance...' (Br. 434).  Descartes  bases  his  whole  system  on  the  divine
veracity. Belief in reason ultimately means belief in God. The  atheist  who
is a disciple of Montaigne regards this as begging the  question:  Cartesian
dogmatism implies the autonomy  of  philosophy  and  a  possibility  of  its
grasping the Sovereign Good, a thing to which  P  cannot  give  his  assent"
(Steinman 244).

      Enlightenment: "The splendid burst of optimism of the Renaissance  and
the 18th century collapse before the spectacle of the empty, inhuman  world.
The divine and beneficent watchmaker dreamed  of  by  Voltaire...  does  not
exist anymore than Descartes's metaphysical God" (Steinman 256).
      "If Christendom had survived with all its medieval  panoply,  P  might
have been less well understood today because he professed  the  xtian  faith
in all its starkness. He isolated it from the social and  metaphysical  ties
to which it seemed to have become irrevocably  committed  under  the  Ancien
Régime. Unlike Aquinas and Descartes, Pascal was no metaphysician; there  is
no infantile cosmology in his work, no throne supporting the altar as  there
is in Bossuet" (Steinman 298).

The content of the Pensées

       Reason and heart:
      - P. 58: 110: Reason, heart, and faith.
      - P. 145:394: ???!!! "Two sorts of persons know  him:  those  who  are
humble of heart and love their lowly state, whatever  the  degree  of  their
intelligence, high or low, and those who are intelligent enough to  see  the
truth, however much they may be opposed to it."
      - P. 122:298: "Against  the  objection  that  there  is  no  order  in
Scritpture. - The heart has its order, the mind  has  its  own,  which  uses
principles and demonstrations. The heart has a  different  one.  We  do  not
prove that we ought to be loved by setting in  order  the  causes  of  love;
that would be absurd. - JC and St Paul possess the order of charity, not  of
the mind, for they wished to humble, not  to  teach.  -  The  same  with  St
Augustine. This order consists mainly in digressions upon each  point  which
relates to the end, so that this shall be kept always in sight."
      - P. 154:423: "The  heart  has  its  reasons  of  which  reason  knows
nothing..."
      - P. 154:424: "It is  the  heart  which  perceives  God  and  not  the
reason. That is what faith is; God  perceived  by  the  heart,  not  by  the
reason."
      - Pp. 210-212:512--514: Contrast between  mathematical  and  intuitive
minds.

      Weakness of reason:
      - Pp. 38-42: 44: reason and imagination.
      - P. 216:530: "All  our  reasoning  comes  dopwn  to  surrendering  to
feeling. - But fancy is like and also unlike  feeling,  so  that  we  cannot
distinguish between the two opposites. One person says that  my  feeling  is
mere fancy, another that his fancy  is  feeling.  We  should  have  a  rule.
Reason is available but it can be bent in kany direction. And  so  there  is
no rule."
      - P. 60: 119: Passions (concupiscence) makes  our  decisions  for  us.
N.B. DECISIONS: A MATTER OF PRACTICAL REASON.

      Reason, the proofs, and faith:

      - P. 168-170:449: The God  of  philosophers:  "...  deism,  almost  as
remote from the Christian religion as atheism... But let them conclude  what
they like against deism, their conclusions do not appply to  Christianity...
Knowing only one of these points  leads  either  to  the  arrogance  of  the
philosophers, who have known God but not their own wretchedness, or  to  the
despair of atheists, who know their own  wretchedness  without  knowing  the
Redeemer... And that is why I shall not undertake here to prove  by  reasons
from nature either the existence of God, or the Trinity or  the  immortality
of the soul, or anything of that kind: not just because I  should  not  feel
competent to  find  in  nature  arguments  which  whould  convince  hardened
atheists, but also because such knowledge, without Christ, is  sterile...  -
The Xtian God does not consist  merely  of  a  God  who  is  the  author  of
mathematical truths and the order of the elements. That is  the  portion  of
the heathen and Epicureans. He  does  not  consist  merelyu  of  a  God  who
extends his providence over the life and property of men so as  to  grant  a
happy span of years to those who worship him. That is  the  portion  of  the
Jews. BUT THE GOD OF ABRAHAM, THE GOD OF ISAAC, THE GOD OF  JACOB,  THE  GOD
OF THE CHRISTIANS is a God of love and consolation; he is a  God  who  fills
the soul and heart of those whom he possesses..."  - P. 227:588:  "Faith  is
a gift of God. Do not imagine that we describe it as a gift of reason..."
      - P. 34: 7: Faith different from proof, but proof may be  instrumental
for faith.
      - P.  244:687:  "I  had  spent  a  long  time  studying  the  abstract
sciences... When I began  the  study  of  man  I  saw  that  these  abstract
sciences are not proper to man, and that I  was  straying  further  from  my
true condition by going into them than were  others  by  being  ignorant  of
them...."
      - P. 179:463: "It is a remarkable fact that no  canonical  author  has
ever used nature to prove God. They all try to make people believe  in  him.
David, Solomon, etc., never said: 'There is  no  such  thing  as  a  vacuum,
therefore God exists.' They must have been cleverer than  the  cleverest  of
their successors, all of whom have used proofs from  nature.  That  is  very
noteworthy."
      - Pp. 263-264:781: "Preface to the second part...  "I  marvel  at  the
boldness with which they presume to speak of  God.  -  In  addressing  their
argument to unbelievers, their first chapter is the proof for the  existence
of God from the  works  of  nature.  Their  enterprise  would  cause  me  no
surprise if they were addressing their arguments to the faithful... to  give
them no other proof of this great and weighty matter than the course of  the
moon and the planets... That is not how Scripture speaks... On the  contrary
it says that God is a hidden God, and that since  nature  was  corrupted  he
has left men to their blindness, from which they  can  escape  only  through
JC, without whom all communication with God is broken..."
      - Pp. 78-80:149: "'I do not demand of you blind  faith'  but  a  faith
that is somwehat persuasive because it  explains  your  wretched  state  and
your good desires and offer you cure.
      - P. 83:167: "Submission and use of reason; that is  what  makes  true
Christianity." See pp. 83-86.
      - P. 271:808:  "There  are  three  ways  to  believe:  reason,  habit,
inspiration. Christianity, which alone has reason, does  not  admit  as  its
true children those who beliefe without  inspiration.  It  is  not  that  it
excludes reason and habit, quite the contrary, but we must  open  our  minds
to the proofs,  confirm  ourselves  in  it  through  habit,  while  offering
ourselves through humiliations to inspiration, which alone can  produce  the
real and salutary effect."
      - P. 85-86:189: "We know God only through JC...  All  those  who  have
claimed to know God and prove  his  existence  without  JC.  have  only  had
futile proofs to offer...
      - P. 86:190: "The metaphysical proofs for the existence of God are  so
remote from human reasoning and so involved that they  make  little  impact,
and, even if they did help some people, it would  only  be  for  the  moment
during which they watched the demonstration,  because  an  hour  later  they
would be afraid they had made a mistake. - What  they  gained  by  curiosity
they lost through pride (Saint Augustine). - That is the result  of  knowing
God without Christ, on other words communicating without a mediator wqith  a
God known without a mediator. - Whereas those who have known God  through  a
mediator know theiur own wretchedness."
      - P. 87:192: "Knowing God without knowing our own  wretchedness  makes
for pride. - Knowing our own wretchedness  without  knowing  God  makes  for
despair. Knowing JC strikes the balance because he shows  us  both  God  and
our own wretchedness."
      - P. 99:219: "Other religions... are more popular,  for  they  consist
in externals, but they  are  not  for  clever  men.  A  purely  intellectual
religion would be more appropriate to the clever, but would be no  good  for
people. The xtian religion alone is appropriate for all, being  a  blend  of
external and internal. It exalts the people inwardly, and humbles the  proud
outwardly..."
      - P. 101:234:"God wishes to  move  the  will  rather  than  the  mind.
Perfect clarity would help the mind and harm the will"
      -  P. 137:377: "What a long way it is between knowing God  and  loving
him!"
      - P. 138:380: "Do not be astonished to  see  simple  people  believing
without argument. God makes them love him and hate themselves.  He  inclines
their hearts to believe. We shall never believe, with  an  effective  belief
and faith, unless God inclines our hearts, and we shall believe as  soon  as
he does so."
      - P. 138:381: "Those who believe without having  read  the  Testaments
do so because their inward disposition is truly  holy  and  what  they  hear
about religion matches it. They feel that a God made them,  they  only  want
to love God... It takes no more than this to convince men whose  hearts  are
thus disposed  and  who  have  such  an  understanding  of  their  duty  and
incapacity."
      - PP. 138-9:382: "... Christians without knowledge of  the  prophecies
and proofs are no less sound judges than those who possess  such  knowledge.
They judge with their hearts as others judge with their  minds.  It  is  God
himself who inclines them to believe and  thus  they  are  most  effectively
convinced. - It may be said that his way of  judging  is  not  certain,  and
that it is by following such a  method  that  heretics  and  unbelievers  go
astray. - 'the reply to that might be that heretics and unbelievers say  the
same thing.' To that I answer that God truly inclines those  whom  he  loves
to believe in the Xtian religion, that the  unbelievers  have  no  proof  of
what they say and therefore, though our propositions employ the same  terms,
they differ in that one lacks any proof while  the  other  is  very  solidly
proved... - I feeely admit that one of  these  xtians  who  believe  without
proof will perhaps not have the  means  of  convincing  an  unbeliever,  who
might say as much for himself, but those who do know the proofs of  religion
can easily prove that this believer is truly inspired by  God,  although  he
cannot prove it himself."

The wager (Pp. 149-155:418-426)

       "The  meditation  on  the  infinite  has  driven  the  atheist   into
agnosticism. The wager is a whole cluster of  arguments  designed  to  force
the agnostic to live to all intents and purposes in  conformity  with  Xtian
morality. That is all. It is frankly absurd  to  try  to  turn  it  into  an
argument intended to prove the existence of God" (Steinman,m 258).
      "For the unbeliever is imprisoned in a  vicious  circle.  On  the  one
hand, his egoism prevents him from perceiving the force  of  the  proofs  of
the existence of the Xtian God, but, on the  other,  his  ignorance  of  the
Xtian  God  prevents  him  from  ridding  himself  of  his  egoism  and  his
passisons" (Steinman 259).
      "... the agnostic has lost all rational objection to the existence  of
the xtian God; in this respect he differs from the atheist.  He  is  in  the
dark: 'Nature offers me nothing that is not source of doubt and anxiety.  If
I saw nothing in it which was a sign of the divinity,  I  should  answer  in
the negative; if I saw everywhere the signs of a Creator, I should  live  at
peace in the faith.  Butseeing  to  much  for  denial  and  too  little  for
certainty, I am in a state which inspires pity'" (Br 229) (Steinman 259).
      "To be sure, replies Pascal, 'you have to wager; you are  not  a  free
agent; you are committed' (Br. 233). Life involves choice. If you refuse  to
act as though the xtian God exists, you are acting  as  though  he  did  not
exist. You think like an agnostic,  but  live  like  am  atheist"  (Steinman
260).
      "The unbeliever desires it without being able  to  attain  it.  'I  am
made in such a way,' he admits, 'that I cannot believe' (Br. 233). This  was
the admission for which P was waiting. The whole aim of  the  wager  was  to
provoke this inadvertent, this despairing appeal... P... wanted to  make  an
end... of the frivolity of a  Renan  and  his  countless  followers.  -  The
agnostic claims that he is incapable of making an act of faith.  'Take  holy
water, have masses said,' P retorts brutally. 'That will naturally make  you
inclined to believe and will stupefy you' (Br. 233)... Do the  externals  of
faith shock you? Do you think they are stupid? Ought we to be  cunning  with
the Infinite? Is it less stupid to drink water than to put our fingers  into
it? To play tennis, go hunting and be present at his Majesty's petit  lever,
than to go to mass?" (Steinman 263)
      "For the wager opens the way to something  else.  The  gamble  is  not
sufficient in itself; we must look at the  other  side  of  the  cards.  The
atheist is ready. Pascal at last opens his Bible" (Steinman 265).

The Five Theses of Jansenism

1. Some of God's commandments cannot be followed by the righteous  with  the
help of those powers available to them in the present  state  even  if  they
want to follow them. Neither do they have the  grace  which  would  make  it
possible for them to be followed.

2. In the state of fallen nature inner grace never meets resistance.

3. In the state of fallen nature merit or demerit  does  nor  require  man's
freedom from inner compulsion; freedom from outer constraint is sufficient.

4. The Semi-Pelagians admitted the necesssity of antecendent grace  for  the
individual acts, also for  the  beginning  of  faith.  Their  error  was  to
maintain that human will could resist or obey this grace.

5. It is Semi-Pelagian to assert that Christ has died or shed his blood  for
all men.

      The Bull Cum occasione, in May 1653, condemned the  five  propositions
which it ascribed to Jansen, the first four as heretical, the fifth  one  as
erroneous.
      In 1654 Arnauld wrote that while the  propositions  with  a  heretical
meaning were justifiably  condemned  by  Innocent  X,  they  were  in  their
heterodox meaning not ascribable to Jansen.
      Arnauld expelled from the Sorbonne in January 1656. He  was  supported
by a young theologian from Chartres, Pierre Nicole (1625-1695).
      In January 1695 Arnaudld and Nicole invite Pascal  to  lend  them  his
sharp penn, for they were theologians but not publicists. The first  of  the
Provinciales appeared in January,  the last on March 1657.
       In  October  1656  Alexander  VII's  Bull  Ad   sacram   renews   the
condemnation of the theses.
      Between April and  August  1657,  in  several  writings,  Arnauld  and
Nicole agree with the  Pope  on  the  quaestio  juris  (the  heresy  of  the
propositions), but not on the quaestio facti (the  actual  presence  of  the
propositions in the Augustinus). Division at Port-Royal: some  refused  what
they saw as a condemnation of Augustinianism, some found  Arnauld's  tactics
unacceptable, some wanted to submit.
      In 1666 Isaac  Lemaistre  de  Sacy,  Mother  Angélique's  nephew,  was
jailed till October 1668. The nuns, in opposition to Arnauld  who  wanted  a
conditional signature, refused to sign.  Urged by Sacy, they finally  signed
on 15 February 1669.
