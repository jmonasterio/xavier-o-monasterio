Inglis on the medievals

The relation between philosophy and theology is one very important aspect of the problem of the relation between faith and reason. Inglis is right in denying that the medievals solved it. I would add, however, that Aquinas himself thought that he had actually solved it. Indeed he proposed a definite solution to it, which by the same token defined  the respective fields of faith and of reason, and the interrelationship of philosophy and theology. Aquinas’s solution was widely accepted for centuries. That is the reason why, as Inglis says, historians thought that the issue of the relation between philosophy and theology had been settled. It was on the basis of the very solution proposed by Aquinas to the problem of the relation between faith and reason  that Stockl and others, in the nineteenth century, rightly attributed to Aquinas a particular view of the relation between philosophy and theology.

Now, Inglis is right in affirming that Aquinas’s actual practice doesn’t correspond to the neat distinction between philosophy and theology attributed to him by Stockl. But the reason for this is not, as Inglis thinks, that Aquinas never thought of distinguishing philosophy from theology in that way, but that Aquinas’s own solution to the problem of the relation between faith and reason was wrong. This is reflected in the fact that Aquinas own practice couldn’t correspond  to his theory. Indeed as Inglis observes, if philosophy supposedly functions on the basis of pure or strict reason and evidence, whereas theology functions on the basis of revelation and authority; and if, furthermore, the object of study of theology supposedly is God and the things directly related to God, whereas the object of philosophy is nature, then Aquinas is doing neither philosophy nor theology.

Which concretely is Aquinas’s view of the relation between faith and reason? There are, according to him, two sources of truth: reason and revelation. There can be no contradiction between the truths of the two sources because God is Himself the Truth and the origin of all truth. Thus, in this sense, the truth is one. My reaction      

          Criticizing Aquinas, de Lubac speaks of “the process of analysis proper to scholasticism.” He could have said, more pointedly, “proper to philosophy as practiced by the Greeks and adopted by the scholastics.”

The Greeks, starting with Socrates, wanted to know before acting and as the basis for acting in the right way – therefore independently of acting. (Remember Descartes’ Discourse on Method : to know before acting). Hence analysis or consideration of what appears to be the case before committing oneself to its truth – ideally its absolutely necessary truth-- and as a condition for appropriate action. This was the Greek way, the philosophical way adopted by Aquinas in his program for a scientific theology . * This option was theoretically unavoidable, once he had identified revelation as the communication of a doctrine, a teaching to be understood and assented to.

* See Chenu on the transformation of the biblical reading in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries.

The above involved the assumption that knowing how to act –practical knowledge– was no different from theoretical or speculative knowledge, or at least presupposed as its condition theoretical knowledge, whose truth consisted in conformity between mind and thing. Moreover, since this conformity can only be known through reflection on what appears to be the case, what appears to be the case to the agent has to be objectified and submitted to analysis. Aristotle would eventually distinguish practical from theoretical knowledge, but would not change his mind about truth as conformity of the mind to reality, nor would he reverse the priority of speculative over practical knowledge and the subordination of practical knowledge to speculative knowledge as its condition. This is the confusion inherited and perpetuated by Aquinas, who will try to submit the practice of his Christian faith to the strictures of the Aristotelian epistemology in order to place it on the map of knowledge at least as a quasi knowledge..

Like the Greeks, and following Aristotle for the most part, Aquinas applied the analytic way in the realm of reason, that is, of what is knowable to us by our natural powers. In this way he got much from Aristotle concerning the knowing subject, the human being, the agent and the world. But he also applied the analytic way to the field of revelation and faith.

Now analysis establishes a particular relationship between the subject and the object: curious observer to curiously observed --without any relationship to the subject’s ‘subjective’ interests (“ex his quae sunt propria rei,” not “per dispositionem audientis,” Aquinas would say)-- in order to, first , know the object (and then , if appropriate, to act on the basis of that certifiedly correct understanding). Like Socrates in respect of piety, who is convinced that he needs to know theoretically*  –and even scientifically or demonstratively-- what piety is in order to be pious (cf Euthyphro).

* “Theoretically” seems to mean objectively, that is, what the object is or appears to be once the subjective, not purely gnoseological interests of the subject have been neutralized. Which is to say that the relationship of the particular object to the exposed, vulnerable subject –and therefore to the possible response of the subject to the object– is deemed irrelevant and, therefore, neutralized. No distances of exposure, no hodological or situational or dramatic space. Desire is systematically separated and excluded from knowledge.

I was going to say that Aquinas agrees with Socrates in that he thinks that he needs first to know theoretically God’s revelation in order to act as a Christian, for he finds a fundamental  analogy between the doctrines of the philosophers and revelation, which he understands as a doctrine or doctrines.* But in fact Aquinas is of two minds in this respect. On the one hand, he grants that the humble vetula who has faith genuinely  knows God, though I add, she knows God the way Euthyphro knows piety, by responding to the gods in the appropriate way, not at all in the way the learned theologian knows God as theoretical object, not as the adoring knows the adorable by the very act of adoring Him. On the other hand, Aquinas’s own theory is that what God reveals is a doctrine to be known theoretically, that is, to be appropriated by understanding and assenting to it and, therefore, that the way to know God is the theologian’s way. Obviously, Aquinas cannot have it both ways. Actually, he has confused the practical knowledge involved in the practice of faith with the theoretical knowledge of the Greek philosophers (which demanded certainty and was a necessary, though perhaps not sufficient condition for appropriate action), and has reduced the former to the latter –not the latter to the former. In so doing, Aquinas put the Western intellectual tradition on the wrong track not only concerning our self-understanding as Christian agents but also our self-understanding as human agents. Because the Greek philosophers from whom Aquinas uncritically took his cue were themselves confused in the same way: they, too, assimilated the agent’s practical knowledge to what they conceived knowledge to be, to wit: theoretical knowledge involving certainty of correspondence with what is the case as the necessary condition for appropriate action.

* A doctrine is a teaching by a teacher, expressed in words . It is not first order (as the agent’s practical knowledge –or, rather, practical judgment -- is) but second order, reflective on what the agent supposedly knows theoretically. Galileo couldn’t have taught his law of falling bodies without first having learned it by practicing it in the prediction of numerous not previously observed falls. And we are supposed do understand Galileo’s law not as a doctrine to be assented to, but as a map to be used. But this is not the way science is understood by those who reduce it to doctrines taught. According to them, the teacher teaches what he or she has previously  found and judged to be true, that is, to correspond to what is the case, either by the authority of others or by personal experiment or by demonstration. Thus, before every doctrine, we can hear implicitly, “It is true that ...”, as supposedly we can implicitly hear it before every stating speech-act, divine revelation included . (For Aquinas, “real and true are convertible,” though, in fact,  true is a property of judgments not of reality. Indeed, one can say “Santa is not real,” but one can not say “Santa is not true”) . A doctrine is meant to be appropriated by assenting to it after due consideration of whether there is sufficient reason to assent to it. Euclid in his demonstrations furnishes demonstrative, rationally compelling reasons, to assent to his second order statements, which are his theorems. In this view,  geometry terminates in assent, as do all doctrines. Euclid, the teacher, teaches what he previously has felt bound to assent to on the basis of sufficient or even compelling reason for assenting. It is a matter of ‘seeing’ what is the case, what is objective.

This model is the model of someone who wants to know what is the case. There is, however, another model: the agent who wants to achieve an end.  This may happen under conditions that already obtain, in which the agent finds himself and within which he has to respond, or as a project to create the right conditions where the means for the achievement of the end are available

Philosophy and theology are second-order discourses.* In other words, they are reflective, discourse about the agent’s discourse, for the purpose of knowing, or about what the agent knows. But the agent does not discourse about , for the agent is not “analyzing.” The agent is not attempting to know theoretically what is the case; the agent is engaged in action and wants to act successfully in achieving his ends. Furthermore, the agent doesn’t need to know theoretically what is the case before acting, doesn’t need to teach himself a true doctrine; all the agent needs in order to act intentionally is to ‘know,’ that is, to judge practically.

* I mean: philosophy and theology, IF understood as doctrines to be assented to, t hey are second-order discourses comparable to the demonstrations of geometrical theorems. But they can also–and more appropriately—be understood as ‘maps’ of the respondendum , based on the repeated experience of an agent or a community of agents. “ Iesus est Dominus” is a good example. It appears to be second-order in that it is the result of reflection; but it is agent’s reflection, not knower’s reflection . Jesus is the name of a singular concrete historical person to whom the agents-believers are exposed, not a concept like triangle or an abstract object as the fall of bodies. According to Lindbeck, “Lonergan... persuasively argues that these [the Nicaean and Chalcedonian doctrines] are the products of systematic reflection on the confused multiplicity of presystematic symbols, titles, predicates applied to God and Jesus-Christ” ( The Nature of Doctrine , pp.9 3-94). From this Lonergan concludes that the doctrines of Nicaea and Chalcedon primarily express second-order guidelines for Christian discourse rather than being first-order [that is, ontological] affirmations about the inner being of God and Jesus Christ ( (Ibid.). (Neither Lonergan nor Lindbeck had given up on the “ontological” as the “factual” mirrored by the mind –that is their confusion). A Christian doctrine like “Iesus est Dominus” can be second-order in the sense of being reflective without thereby ceasing to be first-order in the important sense of referring to a respondendum (not to a “fact,”as Lonergan concedes that the Nicaean and Chalcedonian doctrines secondarily came to refer).

Textbook geometry and Kuhn’s textbook science

A good illustration of the mistake I am criticizing is found in textbook geometry , when the content of the statement of a theorem is taken for what is to be known theoretically, assented to, and is even demonstrated as necessarily true. (Much as textbook scientists may think that Galileo’s formula of falling bodies on inclineds as a result of experiment is what is to be known, and to be known theoretically). This is   positivism’s understanding of knowledge, whose ideal is, therefore,  to bring the object to intuition, a la Kant. However, people can know that (in the sense of assenting firmly to it as a result of demonstration) without knowing how to apply the theorems, in which case they are rightly deemed not to know geometry. Indeed, they confuse a ‘map,” an instrument to be appropriated through use, a genuinely practical judgment by a desirous agent, with a picture of the object, without realizing that its value is as a map, not as a picture, and that it is meant to be used as a map by an agent at the appropriate perceptual time of application. This is why, as is the case with all other sciences, in order to be learned geometry is to be practiced in its application.

The understanding or misunderstanding of geometry I am criticizing corresponds to the understanding of science in what Kuhn calls “textbook science,” which is a second order discourse, not a desirous agent’s judgment but a statement about the object judged,  a doctrine written by people who discourse about what practicin g geometricians state. Kuhn is very good at explaining real problem-solving science by contrast to textbook science. What the experiment yielded in Galileo’s case of falling bodies on inclined planes, when taught by Galileo to others, was a statement to be read  applicatively, as a map to deal with the object at the moment of interaction, not as a mirroring picture of a reality to be contemplated. The same with  perceptual statements. “There is fire in the theater,”  meant for those who are in the theater and have to decide whether to stay and fight the fire or get the hell out of there. On the realistic assumption that their goal is to survive and depending on the circumstances, they are expected to get the hell out or to fight the fire . Thus the fire is a respondendum , depending on the way people are prospectively affected, which of course depends on their situation and on their goals. As observed by Sartre, Carthago delenda est romanis sed servanda carthaginensibus.

       A good example of the fascination with textbook science is Aristotle’s so influential analysis of science, in which he presents his ideal of science in the Analytica Posteriora .  He analyzes the statements of an already constituted science like geometry and its demonstrative procedures, which he proceeds to canonize as the ideal of science, that is, the genuinely scientific science in which every statement is either an intuitive first principle or one logically derived from a first principle. ( See Heidegger’s criticism of logic as part of philosophy ). This, in its turn, was the model after which Aquinas designed his ideal quasi science of  theology in the first question of the Summa Theologiae , thereby equating the revelata with doctrines taught by God. Which is fine, Kierkegaard would say, in case  God had taught doctrines which are infallibly true rather than addressing people as an agent with His own goals to exposed agents in particular historical circumstances. Which is why the Bible was, and had to be, a fundamentally storico-dramatic work composed of successive episodes constituted by the way in which people respond to God.

The practical knowledge of science and the practical knowledge of faith

My guiding intuition is that Kuhn has begun the process of making science fully independent of the Greek ‘scientific’ tradition by placing it where it belongs, to wit: on the side of practical, “tacit” knowledge. And that a parallel move needs to be undertaken concerning the Christian faith by placing it on the side of practical knowledge, and placing practical knowledge on the side of decision and action rather than on the side of mirroring knowledge.

My point is not, however, that faith is entirely like Kuhnian science. Science has an ‘objectivity’ (by contrast to ‘subjectivity’) that faith neither has nor should have. In the properly scientific moment, that is, in the moment of inquiry, the scientist invests only his intelligence; all other interests are hushed, and properly so, because they are irrelevant to ea quae sunt propria rei . Which is to say that the object of science is object to be merely understood practically in relation to an ideal, non-singular,  non-situated agent, as was Galileo when he checked experimentally his hypothesis by applying it to theoretical particular cases previously unobserved. Not so in the case of faith, where the relation of the Christian agent to that to which he has to respond  is singular and as varied and complex as the relation of the agent to the perceptual world, and cannot be mapped in advance but is to be determined by deliberation and decision of the involved agent, depending on his goal.  This is why Aristotle concluded that ethics couldn’t be a science. Moreover, the purpose of science is to know practically in order to master, which is not at all the purpose of faith . Thus the analogy between faith and Kuhnian scientific knowledge concerns only the fact that both are forms of practical knowledge; however much they may differ in all other respects . This may be clarified by paying attention to the systematic scientific paradigms, on the one hand, and, on the other hand, to the narrative paradigm of Christianity within which the Christian finds himself situated as an agent.

But let us move systematically, step by step. To begin with, science, in its inquiring moment, is reflective or second order, whereas action is first order. In other words, the exposed agent is not guided by the desire to know but to respond appropriately in order to achieve his or her goal in the given concrete circumstances. Thus the agent’s thinking is essentially self-regarding, interested thinking. For it is situated thinking, thinking of the object of exposure  in relation to the singular agent’s own desires and strengths and weaknesses.* . For instance, the captain of my typical example wanted to save his ship, if possible, or at least his crew and his own skin. His estimated strength in relation to the particular fire didn’t allow him to save the ship, so he decided to go for -or chose the goal of– saving his crew. Neutralizing his exposure with all the particularity of the situation, by means of some kind of abstraction as it is done in scientific observation which is ex iis quae sunt propria rei , would defeat the purpose of the agent’s thinking,  which is deciding appropriately , for the scientist as such is not exposed and doesn’t have to deliberate and decide. Much as neutralizing one’s exposure would be out of place  in a game of chess, which demands situational thinking for similar reasons, though of course a game doesn’t involve the same sort of exposure.

* This reference to the strengths and weaknesses of the  singular agent involved in a situation is forgotten by those who from the fact that it is possible for a certain agent to, say, jump six feet argue that it is possible for the human being to do that. Well, I am a human being, but it is impossible for me to do that.

In sum, the scientist’s case is very different from the agent’s ,  for the scientist as inquirer is not exposed, or at least not immediately exposed. Science is an (abstractive) problem-solving activity, as remarked by Kuhn. There is a certain intellectual pleasure in problem-solving, even when the problem is a trivial puzzle. (To this extent, Aristotle is right in saying that there is a pure desire to know). Hence the scientist is a curious objective observer of the object’s activity and passivity, without any situational relationship to himself.. A practical interest, like the desire to cure in the case of medicine, may be the motivator of the observation. Even in that case, though, and even if it is observation of the symptoms by which a human being is affected for whom the investigating scientist cares, the observation as such is purely objective in the sense that it is guided by the pure desire to know (understand) the object ex iis quae sunt propria rei , and in that the other desires and possible goals of the scientist don’t count as far as the result is concerned. Here, then, the observation is, and has to be, ex iis quae sunt propria rei, whether or not the resulting knowledge will be applicative. Moreover, the observation is objectifying and abstractive, for it aims at the ‘universal’. It is observation of an ideal, universal object by an ideal, universal, non-situated subject.

In the case of scientific thinking, the investigating thinking and the actual applicative thinking may be separated. The biologist who discovers how a bacillus acts may not be the same person who discovers a cure for the bacillus or the doctor who actually cures the patient. Only the latter has to think applicatively by means of deliberation and decision. And even then his thinking is not situational in the sense of having to calculate his strengths and weaknesses in relation to the object. The reason is that the applicative scientist is involved in the situation as a scientist with a ready-made, proven map-formula . Of course, he has to determine whether the case is one in which the formula is to be applied, and how, but the conditions of the actual application are usually controlled and don’t depend on the strengths and weaknesses of the singular scientist. Scientific thinking is exclusively about ea quae sunt propria rei as such, without any particular relation to the scientist. That is an aspect of the abstraction characteristic of science.* Moreover, as remarked by Aristotle, science studies phenomena that occur with regularity, which explains the ‘universality’ of its laws.

* On scientific abstraction, which is typical of analysis, see Lonergan, Verbum , pp. 39-45.

To return now to the agent’s knowledge, I said that it is situated thinking in relation to the exposed agent’s desires and individual strengths and weaknesses. However, this relationship is tacit; it is lived, not known . At the moment of interaction, the agent doesn’t focus on his strengths and weaknesses as the scientist focuses on the object. Rather they are given to him in his very exposure to the object as the sedimented result of his past experience of encounters. Much as when I am running I do not focus on my changing situation in respect of the objects in front of me, but, as Sartre says, I am only indicated to myself, thanks to my past experience, by the objects themselves. The same happens with my implicit or tacit assessment of my strengths and weaknesses in respect of the respondendum . In this sense, the agent is always the tacit component of practical knowledge; as Sartre says, I am the instrument that I don’t use because I am it, because I am the user of instruments indicated by all instruments. If in my running I come across an obstacle, the very configuration of the obstacle, given my past sedimented experience, indicates me to myself as capable or incapable of jumping it, assuming that my goal demands that I overcome it, which precisely is why it surges as obstacle. Now, this tacit indication of myself to myself as capable or incapable agent by the respondenda I confront as tools or as obstacles when I am in pursuance of a goal, this index of instrumentality of things whose correlate is my indicated self as capable or incapable, is totally absent from scientific thinking, which is nonetheless  practical thinking too. The reason for this is that scientific thinking, in the moment of inquiry, is second order, reflective, and therefore analytic and abstractive or ‘objectifying,’ The space and time of scientific thinking at the moment of inquiry are not lived space and time, not the space of action of the exposed agent, which is not observed but participatory and hodological. They are lived space and time only at the moment of actual application. (See above Lonergan’s quotation on the different way in which the scientist and the “ rusticus” know an eclipse).  However, as already said, the scientist (the engineer, the physician) comes to that moment with a ready-made map which is comparatively precise because of the regularities of the causal order. Moreover, the scientist is not ordinarily in situation of personal exposure where there is room for akrateia, for the scientist as such is not exposed and is not indicated by the objects of his practical field as capable or incapable.

Thus, though the scientist and the exposed agent both use practical thinking, their practical thinking is very different. This, I think, is reflected in the differences between their respective “paradigms” in the sense of “vision of their respective practical fields” by which their action is underpinned or supported. According to Kuhn, the most fundamental sense of ‘paradigm’ is paradigmatic examples or exemplars of problem-solving activity, not a totalizing vision. The students are introduced to those exemplars by their teachers on the basis of authority and they interiorize them by practicing problem-solving. This is what Kuhn refers to as “learning how to see.” When the exemplars are combined they are seen as the same (pendular movement). When internalized, this leaves as a sediment a sort of unified  vision (in the form of habitual practical dispositions to act) of the practical field of physics, or of chemistry, or of medicine, etc. Often, however –and wrongly, since the vision is a practical interiorized map, and only a map of the particular field of the scientist–, this vision is mistaken for a mirroring representation of reality, much as Searle understands statements. Which is why Kuhn attacks the idea that Einsteinian physics is a vision,  a more accurate representation of reality than Newtonian physics. Nonetheless this totalizing realistic move is common . For instance, scientists turned philosophers often talk of ‘the scientific view of the universe,’ referring to the physical universe starting with the big bang, if the scientists are physicists ( a la Sagan), or referring to the evolutionary character of the world if the scientists are anthropologists ( a la Darwin ). Of this kind of pseudo-paradigmatic scientific vision Kuhn doesn’t speak –and rightly so, since what an internalized paradigm generates is more of a practical pre-comprehension than a vision, and the pseudo-paradigmatic vision is not genuinely scientific. Rather it is a spurious side- effect of the  unavoidable totalizing effort of the agent who tries to figure out his own situation in the universe by figuring out the total universe in which he finds himself. The pseudo-paradgimatic vision presupposes, however, that the partial, segmented, practical ‘visions’ of each of the scientific special fields mirrors reality and that they can be combined into one total picture. Contrary to this view, Kuhn shows that the practical vision of such close fields as physics and chemistry are different, for the same names generate different practices. Moreover, since the analytic approach of science is essentially objectifying, its resulting “visions” cannot contain subjects; thus the resulting universe would necessarily be without subjects.

But don’t we Christians have a particular pseudo-paradigmatic vision –the biblical vision of history, which opens from the present to the beginning by creation, and from the present to the future end of history by the second coming of Christ? We do have a vision, except that we don’t claim that it is scientific or even that it is true in the Greek sense of corresponding factually to what is in the mind of Christians. It results from the unavoidable totalizing effort of the imagination of the agent to situate himself in the universe, by taking stock of his culturally-shaped individual practices. It is, therefore, primarily, a practical pre-comprehension that makes the past intelligible and delineates the limits of conceivable expectations and projects for the future. It can only be cashed in terms of living accordingly within it, not in terms of seeing it. And, then, a science is not a natural culture that prepares agents to deal with the world –the natural world, the world of the “rusticus.”

Unlike the unidimensional practical expectations of problem-solving science, whose time and space are abstract rather than lived, here the time-space is dramatic, constituted by past narrated episodes. * In the case of Christianity, the ultimate meaning of episodes is defined by reference to the paradigmatic biblical stories , repeated again and again, heard by the agent and reflected in the practices of his community, by reference to which stories all singular stories are understood

--in the first place, the ongoing story of the agent himself. These stories define the time-space inhabited by the Christian agent, constitute his own cultural home and his family’s –his family being constituted by all the ‘us’ whose practices are recognizably Christian, whereas the rest who do not inhabit this “home” is ‘them.’

* According to Lindbeck ( op. supra cit., p. 64), In xtianity the system is constituted not in purely intellectual terms by axioms, definitions and corollaries, but by a set of stories used in specifiable ways to interpret and live in the world. The mistake of a primarily cognitive propositional theory of religion, from the cultural linguistic perspective, is to ignore this difference. A religious system is more like a natural language than a formally organized set of explicit statements, and the right use of this language, unlike a mathematical one, cannot be detached from a particular way of behaving. (See ope. cit., pp. 65-66).

All cultures are ‘storical ’ and shaped by shared stories. Science is not storical, for it is not a natural culture. Its space-time are not lived and therefore not dramatic. The human agent needs a narrative identity to be an agent, for without that he doesn’t have an inhabited past and an inhabitable future. Hence the awesome significance of the eclipse of narrative, not only of biblical narrative of which Frei speaks, when the scientific space-time absorbed the storical space time.

. Searle on speech acts

But how could such outstandingly intelligent people as Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle fall into that confusion and make such a fateful mistake which Aquinas didn’t correct?  Because, once you become an epistemologist and attempt to legitimize knowledge a priori, that is, before action and independently of action, you objectify what is ‘known’ by the agent and identify what you know as a pure contemplator with what is supposedly known by the agent. But rather than theorizing on this point, let me show how easy it is to fall into this confusion by referring to Searle’s view of statements as speech acts, which happens to be close, though not identical, to Aristotle’s views in Peri hermeneias .

What is characteristic of language is, says Searle, not that it means, but that it means by saying what it means. A dog’s barking may mean (in the sense of being a sign) that there is a stranger in the house, but the dog’s barking doesn’t say that there is a stranger in the house. Now all speech acts say what they mean. If I tell you, “Go to the kitchen,” I am not only giving you an order but saying precisely what my order is.

Speech acts are communicative acts. What the speaker communicates is what the speaker says. All speech acts say what the speaker  means by them, and what they mean is something to be understood by the addressee. This being understood Searle calls the illocutionary effect intended by all speech acts, if indeed they are speech acts.* In addition, however, some speech acts (such as begging, commanding, etc.) intend to produce a further effect, be it an effect on the addressee or an action on the addressee’s part. If I threaten you I intend to make you fearful, If I give you an order, I intend my order to be carried out by you. If I beg for your for bread, I intend you to give me bread. This effect in addition to understanding is called by Searle the perlocutionary effect .

* Thus “ Ich komme von dem Land wo die Zitronen bluhen” (Searle’s example) is not a genuine speech act, for it doesn’t say what is actually meant by the speaker, which is “I am a German soldier.”

Here we arrive at the important point for my purposes, which regards the connection between knowledge and action . The illocutionary effect is the essential linguistic effect intended by all genuine speech acts. This effect is always understanding. In other words, all speech acts intend to convey information about what the speaker intends or means, which is actually conveyed when the information intended by the speaker is understood by the addressee. A speech act is successful if the information is conveyed to the addressee. Any other effect intended by any other speech act is accidental, in that the speech act is essentially communicative of information to be understood. All other effects may or may not obtain without thereby detracting from the success of the speech act, and they are parasitic on the illocutionary effect. Thus, I may or may not obey your order, and this doesn’t detract from the success of  the speech act as such, as long as I understood your intention of communicating to me such an order. Moreover, I can obey your order only if I understand your order, which means that the perlocutionary effect is parasitic on the illocutionary effect but different from it..

Information, as Searle sees it, is something intelligible, something to be merely understood. However, in those speech acts like begging, promising, threatening, in which a perlocutionary effect is intended, something other and more than conveying objective information to be understood is intended. If I order you to go to the next room, I intend you to go to the next room, which is not itself an act of understanding, but something other and more than understanding. However, according to Searle, my speech-act is fully successful as speech act if only you understand that I intend you to go to the next room., for that is the essential linguistic or communicative effect; whether or not you go to the next room is not essential to the success of the speech act as such..

Now, the only speech act in which the illocutionary effect is exclusively intended by means of what is said is the statement, for in this case what is said is something to be merely understood and nothing else and nothing more. (In addition to statements, Searle would probably include salutations, but, at least for the moment, I will ignore this point, which I think wrong). This amounts to saying that, in Searle’s view, statements are not essentially meant to be believed but just to be understood. I may want what I state to be believed, says Searle, but I may not. Which amounts to saying that it is not of the ideal nature of statements to convey true information, that is, information believed to be true by the speaker. From this it would follow that, the addressee has no reason to expect that I am not lying when I make a statement, or that I expect him or her to believe what I say.

In my opinion, this is to ignore a fundamental difference between statements and all other speech acts, which has resulted in the privileged place they have occupied in the history of philosophy, to wit: that statements are the only speech acts that can be true or false. A command may be appropriate or inappropriate, but it cannot be true or false. To repeat: according to Searle, when I make a statement I necessarily intend to communicate what I state by its being understood, but I don’t necessarily intend what I state bo be believed. Searle distinguishes, then, between understanding and believing. I can understand what someone affirms without believing it, and the speaker cannot affirm that without the intention of its being understood, but can affirm it without the intention of its being believed. In other words, believing a statement is a perlocutionary effect in addition to understanding it, whereas understanding the statement is the illocutionary effect of statements. In short, though statements are the only speech acts that can be true or false, and therefore believed or disbelieved, their illocutionary effect is identical to that of all other speech acts: they communicate something to be understood.

In sum, Searle agrees with Aristotle’s view that statements are the only speech acts that can be true or false and that can be believed or disbelieved, for only through them does the speaker affirm or deny something. But while Aristotle thought that genuine statements affirmed what the speaker believed to be the case or denied what the speaker believed not to be the case, and that the speaker intended the same to be believed by the addressee, Searle reduces the meaning of statements to conveying a piece of information, independently of whether or not it is believed or not believed to be true by the speaker, and independently of whether or not it will be believed by the addressee. In short, exactly like in the case of any other speech acts, the intended effect of statements is to communicate information to be understood by the addressee. However, whereas in all other speech acts what is said is something that the speaker wants apart from its being understood, namely, a certain perlocutionary effect different from understanding, this is not the case with statements, which may or may not intend a perlocutionary effect desired by the speaker, but do not necessarily intend any other effect than being understood and are therefore fully successful as speech acts by just being understood. In short, according to Searle, all speech acts are to be understood, but statements are the only speech acts in which the what is said expresses something merely to be understood, whereas all other speech acts express a speaker’s intention that is not only to be understood but something more and other. This is to say that, as such, statements demand no mental or behavioral response from the addressee other than being understood.

We move now to the connection between knowledge and action, beginning with the case of statements.

We imagine that having beliefs is having in the mind doctrines-statements to which we assent. Nonsense! I don’t have the belief-statement-judgment that this is a door. My belief consists in the practical disposition, of which I am not even focally aware, to treat it as a door by using it as a door rather than as a desk. Which is exactly what my cat does. For my cat doesn’t first understand that the object it sees is a door, and then actually believes that, and then uses it as a door by going through it rather than through the chimney.

Since we imagine that having beliefs..., we objectify them and try to demonstrate them, if possible, in order to have absolute certainty. That is what the Greeks did, thereby separating vision from action, on the mistaken conviction that knowledge is correspondence and truth is certainty of correspondence between the content of a belief and what is the case.

Back to Inglis and my reservations on Aquinas

“Few problems have been more persistently discussed through the centuries than the relationship between faith and reason. The prominence of this problem is due, in great part, to the general acceptance of Greek philosophical categories as the basis for theological reflection during the first millennium and beyond. Greek philosophy, on the one hand, was an effort to achieve certified, permanent, and universal truth by human intellectual effort. It aimed to go beyond opinion and appearances and gain definitive insight into reality itself. Biblical faith, on the other hand, is based upon oracles spoken in the name of God, and accepted on the authority of the speaker – an authority confirmed, in some cases, by miracles ” (Dulles, Avery, S.J., The Assurance of Things Hoped For: A theology of Christian Faith, N.Y., Oxford UP, 1994, p. 204).

John Inglis and my reservations on Aquinas

Inglis wrote a fascinating book on the understanding of the mediaevals by a group of nineteenth century German neo-scholastics and by Stockl in particular. I’ll use Inglis’book as a convenient foil to clarify my reservations on Aquinas.

I agree with Inglis that the way in which Aquinas practiced philosophy and theology has little to do with the way in which he was understood by a number of well-meaning post-Kantian, nineteenth century Catholic thinkers who had an apologetic interest in presenting Aquinas’s supposed philosophy as an alternative to Kant’s epistemology. Inglis shows that the view of these thinkers is wrong.

What then was Aquinas’s own reasoning? What was his project? Inglis thinks that even when Aquinas explored philosophical issues in a philosophical manner, that is, not on the basis of revelation, he did it for theological purposes and in a theological context. Hence, if doing philosophy is what Stockl thought it was, Aquinas was not a philosopher. But was he therefore a theologian? At times Inglis sounds as if that is what he feels. But that is not, in fact what he ends up feeling, for he concludes that Aquinas didn’t separate theology from philosophy .

Inglis’s work is defined as merely historiographical. It rightly debunks a particular interpretation of the medievals, and of Aquinas in particular, by showing that they were not doing what the nineteenth century thinkers thought they were doing. So, Inglis concludes, “In order to understand the project of medieval writers, we need to clarify the reasoning of the medievals themselves” (p. 328). What, then, was Aquinas’ own reasoning? That is not Inglis’s concern in this particular book.

Inglis should have nothing to say regarding the actual value of Aquinas’s work, since he avows that we do not understand the reasoning behind the project of the medievals. In fact, however, at least once,  passing by, does Inglis allow himself a value judgment. Indeed, from the fact that the medievals left us in the lurch concerning “the relation between philosophy and theology, the very problem earlier historians thought that they had settled,” he concludes that “Aquinas, Scotus and Ockham do not seem so much to have reconciled faith and reason in their accounts of human understanding, as to have coopted reason within accounts of faith” (p. 307).

For my part , I am not an historiographer. I am a Christian who reflects on Aquinas’s work with the definite intention of evaluating his contribution. And I have serious reservations on Aquinas in this very regard. My conviction is that Aquinas, under the influence of Aristfotle’s epistemology, positively put us on the wrong rails concerning faith, reason and their relationship, with disastrous consequences for our self-understanding as human agents in general and as Christians in particular. For, contrary to what Inglis indirectly suggests, Aquinas had a definite idea of the relation between faith and reason, on the basis of which idea Aquinas thought that he had solved the problem of that relationship. Now, It is my opinion that Aquinas was wrong on both accounts, and that  the fact that we Christians have been unable to escape from the very paradigm bequeathed to us by Aquinas has hampered our self-understanding as Christians and as intellectuals.

Most certainly, the issues involved in this whole question are highly complex. I will try however to deal with some of them in a way that renders somewhat persuasive the reasons for my reservations on Aquinas.  

Inglis writes:

.

In explicating medieval discussions of human understanding we bump our heads against the relation between philosophy and theology, the very problem earlier historians thought they had settled. Aquinas, Scotus and Ockham do not seem so much to have reconciled faith and reason in their accounts of human understanding, as to have coopted reason within accounts of faith (Inglis, p. 307).

Inglis is right. He could have added, however, that among these people at least Aquinas thought that he had found the way to reconcile faith and reason, and that though his way didn’t work proved nonetheless to be enormously influential, we ourselves, not only the medievals, are still bumping our heads concerning the relationship between faith and reason to the great detriment of our self-understanding as Christian intellectuals.  

The following  is my argument reduced to its bare bones . For me, the question is not, as it is formulated by Inglis, whether Aquinas in his main works was functioning as a theologian or as a philosopher. I am willing to grant that he was functioning as a Catholic theologian. The question is which were the intellectual categories with which Aquinas approached his work. Now, even if Aquinas was doing theology rather than philosophy, he certainly was an Aristotelian theologian, with certain elements of Platonism. Which is to say that, as a thinker, he functioned within the mainstream of the Greek tradition that began with Socrates and continued with Socrates’ disciple, Plato, and then with Plato’s disciple, Aristotle. This doesn’t mean that all of Aquinas’s positions can be found as such in Plato or in Aristotle. Certainly not. Aristotle, for instance, came to have very important disagreements with Plato, and yet the two were members of the same intellectual tradition –what I will call here the Greek tradition. My claim is that Aquinas belonged to the same developing tradition. But what does this mean? It means that his whole general outlook, not each of his particular positions, was Greek, that is, possessed a recognizable kinship with the outlook of Socrates, of Plato and of Aristotle.

 To take a particular instance. As a Christian thinker, Aquinas raised such questions as the difference between theology and philosophy and the relationship between faith and reason. But what did he mean by philosophy? Not what the Hindu thinkers had done but what the Greek thinkers had done. In fact, if Aquinas sensed a possible tension between faith and reason, it was because he  realized that the Greeks had attempted to reach the truth by their own intellectual effort, rejecting the way of authority, and that his own Christian faith attained the truth by what appeared to be the way of authority. In other words, had Aquinas’s intellectual outlook not been Greek, he would have felt no tension between what we nowadays call faith and reason. In other words, it was because, as a thinker, Aquinas thought as a Greek whereas he was a Christian that he found himself in a bind concerning the relationship between faith and reason . Indeed, since he was a Christian, Aquinas could simply have decided to leave the Greeks to their way and continue to live and believe as a Christian, whatever the Greeks may have thought. After all, if there was any merit to the way of the Greeks and if they had reached some truth, their truth would have to rejoin the Christian truth, or at least couldn’t be in conflict with it. But that was not at all what Aquinas did. He did, rather,  what Plato and Aristotle conceivably would have done if they had been Christians, to wit: he raised the question of the relationship between faith and reason, and consequently, between philosophy and theology, and he attempted to answer this question the Greek way, that is, through a rational exploration, not simply by relying on the authority of his faith. In fact, he was so much in admiration of the rational way of the Greeks that the entire first question of the Sumnma Theologica is whether theology can be a scientific discipline that reaches necessarily true results comparable to those achieved by Aristotle in his Metaphysics. In short, the way in which the very first question of the Summa is formulated shows that Aquinas works on the assumption that the Greek way to reach the truth is the indisputable model for the attainment of knowledge and, consequently, that theology, that is, the Christian reflection on faith, has to follow the Greek model and its results are to be evaluated by comparison to the highest results achieved by the Greeks.

“... what Lindbeck calls intratextual theology, in which one ‘redescribes reality within the scriptural framework rather than translating Scripture into extrascriptural categories. It is the [scriptural] text, so to speak,  that absorbs the world, rather than the world the text” (Bruce D. Marshall, Aquinas as Post-liberal Theologian,” in The Thomist (1989, pp. 353-402), pp. 400.

In recent years the question whether in his works Aquinas is doing philosophy or theology has been hotly disputed. At least among Christians, the consensus seems to be that Aquinas was functioning as a theologian, not as a philosopher. (See Serge Thomas Bonino, OP, Revue Thomiste 99 (1999), 595-655, sur “la reaction contre la lecture philosophisante... de l’oeuvre de l’Aquinate” p. 595). The problem with this approach is: according to which definition or conception of philosophy and of theology?. After all, if Inglis is right –and I think he is– Aquinas is doing neither, if we go by the definitions of philosophy and theology of the post-Kantians (such as Stockl), and in order to determine what Aquinas was actually doing we would have to determine what his project was on the basis of an analysis of his works and of those of other medievals. In short, nothing is gained, in my opinion, by saying that Aquinas was a theologian rather than a philosopher.

A more promising direction of inquiry is to determine whether --to use Lindbeck’s expression, which could be echoed by Frei in slightly different terms– in his works Aquinas “redecribes reality within the scriptural framework rather than translating Scripture into extrascriptural categories . ” To put it in a slightly different way, Aquinas is definitely a Christian thinker deeply influenced by the Greeks, particularly by Aristotle. Hence the question: even granting that Aquinas thought of himself as a theologian, are whether his basic interpretive categories are biblical or Aristotelian. And, if they happen to be Aristotelian, does this fact necessarily involve a radical misunderstanding of the Christian faith because the Aristotelian categories employed by Aquinas just don’t fit Christianity?

It is evident that Aquinas’s project is, not to do away with the Greek conception of reason and its valuable achievements, in the name of his Christian faith, but rather to find room for the tenets of his Christian faith side by side with the tenets of Aristotelian philosophy and, for this purpose,  to open the Aristotelian conception of knowledge so as to make room for his Christian faith if not quite within that conception, at least in continuity with it. This amounts to saying that Aquinas planted his feet firmly within the Aristotelian epistemic tradition and proceeded  from there to interpret his Christian faith. Or, to use a different image, Aquinas brought his Christian faith within the gravitational field of Greek reason, rather than placing Greek reason within the gravitational field of the Christian faith .

When I said that Aquinas’s basic categories are Aristotelian I had in mind the epistemological categories or molds that he spontaneously and unquestioningly brings to bearing in his works.

To give a concrete example , the very first question of the Summa Theologica is whether theology -- Christian theology, that is, “ Sacra doctrina”-- can be a science. Now, Aquinas was not the first to ask such a question. In fact, it had become the traditional first question of all the Summae Theologicae long before Aquinas. From the detailed answers given to that question by all of Aquinas’s predecessors we can easily gather the nature of the problem considered. The Bible, the word of God in which divine revelation concretely consists, doesn’t present itself as a scientific treatise attempting to elicit assent by demonstrative means. Yet it is infallibly true, for it is God’s word. Cannot it, therefore, be systematized into a theological treatise?.

The analogy between faith and resason, or between  the knowledge of faith and the knowledge of reason

To take another example . In the first question of the Summa Theologiae , in the very first article of the question, Aquinas matter-of-factly, unquestioningly, and without any argument in support of his position, affirms an analogy between what God reveals to us for our salvation – this is what Aquinas understands by “revelation” and “faith”– and the knowledge yielded by the philosophical disciplines, that is “reason.” Now you have to be an intellectual who spends his life among books, as Aquinas actually was and did, to think of God primarily as a teacher who possesses knowledge and transmits it, which is what the philosophers studied by Aquinas did, and to compare God’s revelation to the doctrines or teachings of Aristotle or Plato. Of course, after smuggling this analogy, Aquinas took infinite care to distinguish between the teachings of philosophers and what God reveals. For God’s teachings are infallible, which is not the case with the doctrines of philosophers. Furthermore, God’s teachings are authoritative, whereas philosophers teach by way of argumentation and demonstration. Consequently what God reveals is to be assented to not because of its evidence but because of God’s authority. In this vein, Aquinas distinguishes the knowledge of faith from the knowledge of reason. But the fundamental analogy remains and pervades Aquinas’s works: the nature of what God reveals to us is like what philosophers teach, namely,  His own knowledge, to be appropriated by us through faith. Consequently faith has to consist in intellectual assent to what God reveals. In short, the entire nature of Christianity and of the relationship between God and us is determined by the initial assumption on which the analogy rests, to wit: that what God reveals is truths to be assented to.

To give a hint of the all pervasive consequences of the analogy, consider what follows from it concerning the relationship between faith and reason, as presented by Aquinas in his explanation of the problem of faith and reason and his proposed solution. Since both revelation and reason are sources of knowledge, they can complement each other. For what God reveals is only the knowledge that is necessary or useful for our salvation. [SEE MY LECTURE ON AQUINAS ON FAITH AND REASON].

Now assume that Aquinas’s fundamental analogy is mistaken either because what God reveals is not information about reality, so that it cannot possibly be in conflict with the knowledge achieved by human reason, or because the very conception of knowledge that Aquinas inherited from the Greeks and on the basis of which his analogy between the truths of revelation and of reason is predicated is wrongheaded. What then? What is the nature of faith, if what God reveals is not a doctrine to be assented to? And what is the nature of science if it doesn’t yield knowledge of the sort the Greeks and Aquinas had in mind?

Why do we accept so naturally Aquinas’s analogy? Because we ourselves have been colonized, as he was, and partly thanks to him, by the Greek view of knowledge as correspondence or conformity of the mind to what is. This view, however, which Aquinas took for granted and to whose strictures he attempted to submit revelation, is nowadays in crisis both on the side of revelation and of reason. On the side of revelation, less and less are we Christians convinced that revelation is a matter of knowledge. On the side of reason, less and less are we convinced that even in the hardest of the natural sciences, which supposedly are the prime exemplar of rationality, knowledge as correspondence obtains at all (see Kuhn).

  

The analogy goes more or less like this. What God reveals to us is that portion of the infallible, infinite knowledge he possesses that is important for our salvation. This has traditionally been called the view of the two sources of knowledge, namely, human reason and divine revelation. Now the analogy is based on the fact that both the philosophical disciplines and divine revelation yield knowledge, that is, something to be assented to because it is true. Here we have a glaring example of the unquestioning, spontaneous application by Aquinas of a basic epistemological Aristotelian category.
