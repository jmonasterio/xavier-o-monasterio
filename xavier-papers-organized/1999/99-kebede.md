January 12, 2000

Dr. Paul Morman

Dean

College of Arts and Sciences

Dear Dr. Morman:

The philosophy department convened on January 7 of this year for the purpose of evaluating for the second time Dr. Messay Kebede’s progress toward tenure. On January 10 the department convened again to take its vote. The two questions voted upon were: (1) Do you judge that the appropriate progress is being made toward tenure? and (2) Do you recommend that a probationary contract be continued? The results were: 8 affirmative answers to both questions and one negative answer to both questions. The present letter, written on behalf of the department and reviewed by its tenured members, is meant to convey the department’s reflection on Dr. Kebede’s progress towards tenure, as it was expressed at the meeting of January 7.

Concerning Dr. Kebede’s teaching , the department heard the evaluations of the three

faculty members who had previously made the class visitations. All the evaluators found Dr. Kebede’s lectures philosophically substantive, clear and very well organized, intense and, at times, engaging. He completes his lectures with detailed handouts, and is constantly available to his students through electronic mail. The general sense of the evaluators is that Dr. Kebede is a competent teacher who fosters critical and creative talents in his students, although he is less suscessful, in the lower level courses,  in provoking questions from the students and in engaging them in discussion. He also uses the computer technology in his courses to considerable advantage.

Dr. Kebede’s ability as a teacher shows in the evaluations he received from the students in his 300-level course on African Philosophy, where his cumulative average was the same as the department’s, which itself is very high. Yet, the department remarked that the numbers in the students’s evaluations of his other courses remain comparatively low. The department feels that this is due, in part, to the unwilligness of a number of students to make an effort to understand Dr. Kebede’s accent (a repeated complaint that appears in his student evaluations). Since the three faculty members who visited his classes unanimously found Dr. Kebede’s English  perfectly understandable, indeed polished, it seems that, for all the University’s efforts, the level of acceptance of diversity by the students is not yet as high as it should be. However, the department thinks that the lower evaluations  by some students also depend, in part, on certain features of his teaching style which is deeply marked by the French system of education – a system in which the teacher is a figure of authority and in which personal interaction with the students in the classroom is highly uncommon. T he department expects that, being made aware of the American students’ expectations, Dr Kebede will gradually adapt to the more congenial and interactive American style in the classroom, given his warm personality outside the classroom. The Department, and especially his class visitors, suggested that Dr. Kebede greet his students as he enters the classroom and in general try to be less distant in his manner in the classroom, that he share with his students in his lectures some of his so rich personal experience, that he take advantage of the latitude offered him by the concrete character of the themes of general education to add more of the human  dimension to his courses, that he select and actually use textbooks that incorporate this human or more existential dimension and, finally, that he develop techniques to promote student interaction and ways for the students to follow his lectures with greater ease.

Concerning his research , the department noted with satisfaction Dr. Kebede’s accomplishments. His new book, Survival and Modernization, Ethiopia’s Enigmatic Present: a Philosophical Discourse (460 pages), was published in 1999. On the occasion of this publication Dr. Kebede was named “Author of the Year” by the Ethiopian News Headlines . His article, “Development and the African Philosophical Debate,” appeared in the refereed Journal of Sustainable Development in Africa . In addition, Dr. Kebede read a paper at a philosophical conference on Peace and Global Issues, and has submitted for publication a paper entitled “Between Evolutionism and Pluralism.” He is a solid scholar with broad interests.

The preparation of two new courses and his teaching and research activities have absorbed most of Dr. Kebede’s energies. However, in the area of service he has already begun to contribute within the university as a member of a subcommittee of the Faculty Board. As for the community at large, he published an article entitled “On the Wavering of the Opposition” in the Ethiopian Register.

In conclusion, with the exception of one person, as indicated by the vote, the department feels that Dr. Kebede is making good progress and that, when the time comes, he will be fully qualified for tenure.

Sincerely,

Xavier O. Monasterio, Ph. D.
