Mimesis-2

64:  With mimesis-2 opens the kingdom of the as if . The latter expression is preferred to "fiction" because it ignores the distinction between fictional and historical narrative and covers the common structural characteristics of both. Whatever the scope of the revisions that the distinction between the fictive or imaginary and the real must undergo, a difference will remain between fictional and historical narrative that will have to be reformulated in vol. 2. In the meantime I will use "composition" or "configuration" to designate the configuration of a narrative for which emplotment is the paradigm, regardless of the truth claims of the two classes of narrative. This is the meaning of muthos ("organization of events") in the Poetics . I will use "fiction" and "history" when the problems of reference and truth are involved.

I now propose to disengage the configuring activity from the limiting constraints the paradigm of tragedy imposes on the concept of emplotment for Aris. Further I want to complete my model by an analysis of its temporal structures which has no place in the Poetics . The model of emplotment that will be tested in this book responds to one fundamental requirement. Mimesis-2 has an intermediary position because it has a mediating function which derives from the configurating operation that has led us to prefer the term emplotment to plot and ordering to system. The plot exercises within its textual field an integrating and, in this sense, mediating fucntion which allows it, beyond thisfield, to exercise a mediation between the preunderstanding and the postunderstanding of the order of action and its temporal features.

65: Emplotment-Plot is mediating in at least three ways:

(1) It is a mediation between the individual events or incidents and a story taken as a whole. It draws a meaningful stroy from a diversity of events, transforms them into a sktory.

An event gets its definition from its contribution to the story. Thus the plot mediates between events and narrated story.

(2) It also brings together all the heterogeneous features of action: the "what" of tragedy: plot, characters, and thought .

By including pitiable nd fearful incidents, sudden reversals, recognitions and violent effects under plot, Aris equates plot with the configuring we have characterized as concordant discordance. This is the feature that, in the final analysis, constitutes the mediating function of the plot.

(3) By its temporal characteristics, ignored by Aris, plot 'solves' the discordant--the Agustinian paradoxes--not in a specultative but in a poetic way. The configurational arrangement transforms the succession of events into onemeaningful whole. The narrative tiem mediates between the episodic and the configurational aspect. Second, the plot emposes the sense of an ending on the indefinite succession of incidents. Theeventslead to an end. A new quality of time emerges from this understanding that appears clearly when the story is already known and retold.

Finally, the repetition of a story, governing as a whole by its ending, offers an alternative to the representation of time as flowing from the past toward the future. We learn to read the story backwards, as the recapitulation of the initial conditions of a course of action and its terminal consequences.

68: Two complementary features that assure the continuity of the proces that joins mimesis-1 to mimesis-2 had to be added to this analysis of the configurational act. More visibly than the preceding ones, these two features require the support of reading to be reactivated: schematization, and the character of traditionality characteristic of the configurational act, each of which has a specific relation to time .

(1) Schematization : The "grasping together" characteristic of the configurational act are comparable to Kant's act of judgment. The categories of the understanding are first schematized by the productive imagination which has a fundamentally synthetic function: it connects understanding and intuition by engendering syntheses that are intellectual and intuitive at the same time . Emplotment, too, engenders a synthesis between the point or theme or thought of thestory and the intuitive presentation of circumstances, characters, episodes, and changes of fortune that make up the denouement. In this way we may speak of the schematism of the narrative function (whose typology is elaborated by Fry in Anatomy of Criticism ).

(2) Traditionality : The schematism is constituted within a traditional history. A tradition is constituted by the interplay of innovation and sedimentation. Sedimented history is hidden under the genres accepted within a culture. But there are also individual works. The genres (illustrated by paradigmatic particular works) constitute only the grammar that governs thecomposition of new works the composition, the rules by which innovation in new particular works is made possible. Two poles: servile application and calculated deviation. Thus the contemporary novl is an antinovel. Anddeviation may come into placy in relation to the types, the genres, even to the formal principle of concordant discordance. It is this variety of application that confers a history on the productive imagination and that, in counterpoint to sedimentation, makes a narrative tradition possible. This is the final enrichment by which the relationship of narrative to time is augmented at thelevel of mimesis .

Mimesis-3

70: It is only at the end of our traversal of the cycle of mimesis that the thesis stated at the beginning of this chapter will receive a concrete content: narrative has its full meaning when it is restored to the time of action and of suffering in mimesis-3.

This stage corresponds to what Gadamaer calls "application."

Aris is not much concerned with the audience in the Poetics . But he speaks of poetry "teaching" the universal, of tragedy "in representing pity and fear... effecting the purgation of these emotions." Thus in mimesis-3 lies the complete fulfilment.

71: Generalizing beyond Aris I shall say that mimesis-3 marks the intersection of the world of the text and the world of the hearer or reader, of the world configured by the poem and the world wherein real action occurs and unfolds its specific temporality. I shall proceed in four steps represented by each of the next sections of this chapter .

(1) The Circle of Mimesis. (Reply to the objection of circularity).

72: That the analysis is circular, granted. That it is viciously circular, rejected. It is an endless spiral.

(a) First objection: Narrative puts consonance where there was only dissonance. Hence it is just fiction, a lie to console us in the face of death. Our nostalgia for order is met by the pseudo-solution of narrative.

Answer : The objection dramatizes the discordant concordance attached to the relationship between narrative and time. But the argument misses the properly dialectical relationship by placing the consonance on the side of nattative and the dissonance on the side of temporality.

Firstly, our experience of temporality cannot be reduced to simple discordance. Distentio and intentio mutually confront each other at the heart of ourmost authentic experience. The objection is the product of a fascination of modernitywith the unformed and does awaywith the paradox of time.

73: Secondly, the consonance characteristic of narrative opposed undialectically to the dissonance of our temporal existence is exaggerated. Emplotment is never the simple triumph of order. Even Greek tragedy makes a place for the upsetting role of peripeteia, the contingencies and reversals of fortune that solicit horror and piety. The same with other paradigms that have governed the sense of ending in the Western tradition, such as the apocalyptic. Even the antinovel stems from a history of 'concordance'. The latter could be another case of imposing by force discordance on our experience of time.

Thus reflection on the limits of concordance does not lose its legitimacy. It appliues to every instance of concordant discordance and discordant concordance both at the level of narrative and of time. But in ever yinstance the circle is inevitable without being vicious.

74: (b) Second objection: How can we speak of a human life as a story in a nascent state, since we do not have access to the temporal dramas of existence outside of stories told about them by others or by ourselves?

Answer : I will present a series of situations that constrain us to accord already to experience as such an inchoate narrativity as a genuine demand for narrative. Experience is prenarrative.

Are we not inclined to see in a given sequence of episodes of our lives as yet untold stories that demand to be told?

The patient in psychoanalysis offers bits and pieces. The goal of the sessions is to arrive to a narrative more supposrtable and intelligible. It seems that a life story proceeds from untold and represed stories in the direction of actual stories the subject can take up and hold as constitutive of his personal identity. It is the quest for personal identity that assures the continuity between the potential or inchoate story and the actual story we assume responsibilityfor.

75: A judge can be seen as undertaking to understand a course of actions, a character, by unraveling the tangle of plots the subject is caught up in. This prehistory of a story is made up of the living imbrication of the lived story with other stories. Told stories emerge from this background, and with it the implied subject, the character. "The story stands for the person." Of course, the narrator has to choose the beginning. The consequence of this analysis of human beings as "entangled in stories" is that narrating is a secondary process--thestory becoming known. Telling, following, undersanding stories is the 'continuation' of the untold stories. In fact, if there is no untold story, the artificiality of the told story (Aris so it as produced by the artifice of the narrator) becomes unintelligible .

76: Thus we should see here a healthy circle in which the arguments offered about each side of the problem aid one another.

(2) Configuration, Refiguration, and Reading

The transition between mimesis-2 and mimesis-3 is brought about by the act of reading. Reading actualizes the plot's capacity to model experience by taking up again and fulfilling the configurational act in a sort of 'judgment' that 'grasps together' the details of action into the unity of the plot. Schematization and traditionality in mimesis-2, that break down the opposition of an 'inside' and an 'outside' of the text and shape the structuring activity of emplotment are from the start categories of interaction between the operations of writing and reading. The received paradigms structure readers' expectations and aid them in recognizing the formal rule, the genre or the type exemplified by thenarrated story. In short, they govern thestory's capacity to be followed. To follow a story is to actualize it by reading it. Furthermore, it is the act of reading that accompanies the interplay of the innovation and sedimentation of paradigms that schematizes emplotment The reader plelays with the narrative constaints, brings about gaps, takes part in the combat between novel and antinovel, enjoys the pleasure of the text. The written work is a sketch for reading, and the reader completes the work. In thecase of Joyce's Ulisses the reader carries the burden of emplotment by configuring what the author takes malign delight in disfiguring. The act of reading is thus the operator that joins mimesis-3 to mimesis-2--the refiguring of the world of action under the sign of the plot.

As we shall see in vol. 2 , what Wolfgang Iser's theory of reading and Robert Jauss' theory of reception have in common is seeing in the effect that the text produces in its receiver, whehter individual or collective, an intrinsic component of the present or actual meaning of the text. For both, the text is a set of instructions that the individual reader or the reading public executes in a passive or a creative way.

(3) Narrativity and reference

What is communicated by a narrative is, beyond the sense of a work, the world it projects and that constitutes its horizon that intersects with the horizon of the readers or listeners or spectators. The definition of mimesis-3 as the intersection of the world of the text and that of the reader is close to Gadamer's notion of "fusion of horizons". This definition rests upon three presuppositions which underlie, respectively, acts of discourse in general, literary works among these acts of discourse, and narratives among these literary works. The order that ties together these three presuppositions is thus one of increasing specification .

(a) Concerning acts of discourse in general :

78: With Benveniste rather thand de Saussure, we take the sentence as the unit of discourse. The sentence says something about something. The complete event is not only that someone speaks and addresses himself to an interlocutor, it is also the speaker's ambition to bring an experience to language and share it with someone else. It is this experience that has the world for its horizon. Reference and horizon are correlative as are figure and ground. Language does not constitute a world for itself. It is not even a world. Because we are in the world and are affected by situations, we try to orient ourselves in them by means of understanding;; we also have something to say, and experience to bring to language and to share. This is the ontological presupposition of reference, a presupposition reflected inside languate itself as a postulate lacking any immanent justification. Language is for itself the order of the Same. The world is its Other. The attesttion of this otherness arises from language's reflexivity with regard to itself, whereby it knows itself as being in being in order to bear on being. This ontological attestation would remain an irrational leap if the externalization it required were not the counterpart of a prior and more originary notion, starting from ourexperience of being in the world and in time, and proceeding from this ontological condition toward its expression in language.

An ability to communicate and a capacity to refer must be simultaneously posited. A reference is coreference, dialogical reference. What a reader receives is not just the sense but, through its sense, its reference, the experience it brings to language and the world and the temporality it unfolds.

79: (b) Concerning literary works : Literary works, too, bring an experience to language, just as all discourse does. The dominant theory of contemporary poetics rejects reference in the name of the strict immanence of literary language in relation to itself.  When literary texts contain allegations of, for instance, truth, that bring back the dialectic of being and appearance, kthey create a 'referential illusion.' But this does not abolish the problem of thje relation of literature to the reader's world. Reading poses again the problem of the fusion of two horizons. If we take the question of the impact of literature on everyday experience as not pertinent, we simply forget that fiction is precisely what makes language the supreme danger. From ideological confirmation of the established order to social criticism and even derision for everything 'real.' The shock of thepossible is no less than that of the real.

80: If we accept the intersection between the world of the text and that of the reader, we have to find in the very functioning of poetic language the means to cross from one to the other.

In The Rule of Metaphor I tried to demonstrate that language's capacity for reference was not exhausted by descriptive discourse and that poetic works referred to the world in their own specific way, that of metaphorical reference. ("Poetic" includes lyrical and narrative). Poetic works speak of the world, though theymay not do so in a descriptive fashion. The effacement of descriptive reference is the negative condition for freeing a more radical power ofreference to those aspect of our being in the world that cannot be talked about directly. This is metaphorical reerence.

Metaphorical [vs. descriptive] reference, by means of a new pertinence, establishes at the level of sense, on the ruins of the literal sense. It metaphorizes the verb 'to be' itself and makes of "being as" the correlate of 'seeing as'. This 'being as' brings my second presupposition to the ontological level of my first and, at the same time, enriches it. The concept of horizon and of world does not just concern descriptive references but also metaphorical references, those of poetic diction. For me the world is the whole set of referneces opened by every sort of descriptive or poetic text I have read, interpreted,and loved. We oiwe a large part of the enlarging ofour horizon of existence to poetic works. Far from being Platonic shadows of reality, they augment reality.

(4) Narrated time

83: For the interweaving reference between history and fiction it is important to sketch the temporal features of the world refigured by the configurational act.

A theory of refigured or narrated time necessitates a conversation already begun among the epistemology of history, literary criticism applied to narrativity, and phenomenological philosophy. The dialectic of time and narative is the ultimate stake of this conversation. Augustine's phenomenology of time was a first step. My thesis will be that the discoveries of the phenomenology of time cannot be removed from the aporetic realm that so strongly characterizes Agustine's theory of time. One major step in vol. 2 will be to prove the in principle aporetic character of the pure phenomenology of time, which shows the validity of my thesis that the poetics of narrativity responds and corresponds to the aporetics of temporality . The rappprochement Agustine-Aris offered a limited and circumstantial verification. But so far historiography and the philo of history and literary criticism have yet  had not their say about historical time and the games fiction plays with time. I will not deny the properly phenomenological originality that Heidegger's description of temporality owes to its anchorage in the ontology of Care. Heidegger's Geschichtlickeit (historicality) are closest to each other at this level, before diverging radically--at least in appearance--as the one directs himself toward Pauline hope, the other toward quasi Stoic resolutneness in the face of death. Nonetheless, theontology of Dasein remains tied up withj a phenomenology that poses problems analogous to those raised by Augustine's and Husserl's phenomeonologies and engenders problems that augment the aporetic character of pure phenomenology. The aporia has to do with the relations between the phenomenology of time and the human sciences, particularly history aand narratology. The paradox is that Heidegger failed in deriving the concept of history familiar to professional historians, as well as the thematic of the human sciences received from Dilthey, from thehistoricality of Dasein. Furthermore, how do we pass from a phenomenology so privatized by being towards death to that common time that requires interaction among multiple characters in every narrative and, all the more, to the public time required by history? Vol. 2 will show that, in spite of the abyss that seems to lie between the two poles, narrative and time simultaneously and mutually arrange themselves in hierarchies. Sometimes the phenomenology of time provides the key for the hierarchization of narratives, sometimes the disciplines concerned with historical and fictional narrative allow us to resolve poetically the speculatively intractable aporias of the phenomenology of time.
