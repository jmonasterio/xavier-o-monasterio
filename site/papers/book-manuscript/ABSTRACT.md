## Central Thesis

This manuscript argues that Thomas Aquinas's attempt to synthesize Christian faith with Aristotelian epistemology resulted in a fundamental *denaturation of the Christian understanding of faith*. Dr. Monasterio contends that the Aristotelian framework, with its emphasis on propositional knowledge, intellectual assent, and scientific demonstration, is **radically incompatible** with the narrative, relational, and holistic character of Biblical faith as understood by the Church Fathers.

The work challenges the common view that Aquinas successfully "baptized" Aristotle, proposing instead that Aquinas unwittingly subordinated Christian faith to Aristotelian categories, transforming faith from a total personal relationship with God into a primarily intellectual act of assent to propositions.

## Chapter Overview

### [Chapter 1: Determinatio Distrahens](/#/writings/book-manuscript/chapter-1.md)
Examines whether Aquinas recognized that submitting the Bible to Aristotelian science distorted the nature of revelation. Introduces the concept of "determinatio distrahens" (distorting determination) and argues that Aquinas failed to acknowledge the radical difference between Biblical narrative and theological demonstration.

### [Chapter 2: Aquinas's Synthesis: Half Aristotelian, Half Christian?](/#/writings/book-manuscript/chapter-2.md)
Analyzes the historical context of the Aristotelian revolution in medieval theology. Questions whether Aquinas's synthesis was genuinely harmonious or whether it represented an impossible mixture of incompatible worldviews that would eventually split.

### [Chapter 3: From Christian Meditation to Summa Theologica](/#/writings/book-manuscript/chapter-3.md)
Traces the transformation from Patristic meditation on Scripture to Scholastic systematic theology. Examines Anselm's pivotal role ("credo ut intelligam") and the emergence of the "quaestio" method that subordinated revelation to rational inquiry.

### [Chapter 4: On the Scientific Character of Theology](/#/writings/book-manuscript/chapter-4.md)
Surveys the 13th-century debate on whether theology qualifies as an Aristotelian science. Examines the "reluctant non-Aristotelians" (de Hales, d'Auxerre, Kilwardby) and "anti-Aristotelians" who resisted the assimilation, documenting their prescient objections.

### [Chapter 5: The Notion of Faith](/#/writings/book-manuscript/chapter-5.md)
Contrasts the Patristic understanding of faith as a holistic personal relationship ("credere in Deum") with Aquinas's intellectualist reduction of faith to assent. Argues that Aquinas's analysis relegates the will to an "extrinsic" role, fundamentally distorting traditional teaching.

### [Chapter 6: The Object of Faith](/#/writings/book-manuscript/chapter-6.md)
Analyzes Aquinas's treatment of what is believed. Critiques the displacement of focus from God Himself to "truths about God," and exposes tensions between Aquinas's claim that faith and knowledge cannot coexist regarding the same object and his admission that some revealed truths are demonstrable.

### [Chapter 7: Revelation, Theology, and the Bible](/#/writings/book-manuscript/chapter-7.md)
Compares Bonaventure's and Aquinas's approaches to the relationship between Scripture and theology. Critiques Aquinas's equation of revelation with knowledge and his treatment of the Bible as fundamentally a quasi-scientific doctrinal work.

### [Chapter 8: Biblical Categories and Aristotelian Categories](/#/writings/book-manuscript/chapter-8.md)
The culminating philosophical chapter. Develops an alternative theory of faith grounded in narrative rather than proposition. Drawing on speech-act theory, argues that Biblical narratives possess an irreducible "narrative sense" that cannot be captured by Aristotelian propositional logic.

## Key Arguments

### Against Aquinas's Model of Faith

- Aquinas reduces faith to intellectual assent prompted by the will, whereas for the Fathers faith was an inseparable unity of cognition, volition, and action
- The Patristic formula "credere in Deum" (believing INTO God) differs fundamentally from "credere Deum" (believing propositions about God)
- Aquinas's model treats God primarily as "the First Truth" (source of reliable statements) rather than as the personal God of relationship
- The paradigmatic case of Abraham's faith cannot be explained by Aquinas's model: Abraham did not assent to propositions but obeyed a command

### On the Nature of Scripture

- The Bible's fundamental literary genre is *narrative*, not doctrinal or argumentative
- Aquinas wrongly equates the "literal sense" with the "historical sense" and reduces both to propositional content
- Narratives possess an irreducible "narrative sense" that cannot be translated into propositions without loss
- The Bible moves the heart and calls for total response; it does not merely address the intellect seeking assent

### Philosophical Innovation (Chapter 8)

- Critique of Searle's distinction between illocutionary and perlocutionary effects
- All speech acts are fundamentally performative, not informative
- Understanding is not separable from appropriate response; meaning is constituted by perlocutionary force
- Narratives make the reader present as an "ideal witness" who is moved and must take a stance

## Scholarly Significance

This manuscript represents a sustained, philosophically sophisticated critique of the Thomistic synthesis from within the Catholic tradition. It anticipates and engages with themes later developed in:

- **Hans Frei's** *The Eclipse of Biblical Narrative* (explicitly acknowledged)
- **Narrative theology** movements emphasizing story over proposition
- **Speech-act theory** applications to religious language
- Contemporary debates on **faith and reason** in Thomistic scholarship

The work challenges scholars to reconsider whether the medieval Aristotelian synthesis, often celebrated as a triumph of faith-reason harmony, may have fundamentally altered Christianity's self-understanding in ways not yet fully appreciated.
