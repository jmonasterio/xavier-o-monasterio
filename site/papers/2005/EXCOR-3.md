A REFLECTION ON THE APOSTOLIC CONSTITUTION EX CORDE ECCLESIAE
      [THIS PAPER CONTINUES \WP\CHANT\EXCORDE AND EXCOR4-2]
      "It may appear, then, that the result of my arguments in this paper
      have been to make Aquinas more distant from contemporary
      epistemological views and less interesting to contemporary
      philosophy... I believe, however, that it is the differences between
      ourselves and past philosophers which are as illuminating as the
      similarities, and that great philosophers of the past--of which
      Aquinas is certainly one--become really interesting when their
      philosophical distance from ourselves is recognized and acknowledged"
      (John Jenkins, "Aquinas on the veracity of the intellect," The Journal

                                  I

                             THE CHASM
      I will begin this reflection on Ex corde Ecclesiae by pointing out a
fact which, I think, will prove enlightening. Although the name of Aquinas
is nowhere mentioned, this document relies very obviously on the view of
the relationship between faith and reason which was worked out
progressively in the course of the thirteenth century and which Aquinas
brought to its fully mature and definitive articulation.* [*The classical
historical study of the formation of this view of the relationship between
faith and reason remains Chenu's La théologie comme science au XIIIe
siècle].
      To make clear how closely Ex corde Ecclesiae follows Aquinas's
thought, or at least is in perfect hakrmony with it, a short reminder of
the latter's position will be helpful before we examine the Apostolic
Constitution. Concerning, then, the reason-faith relationship, it was
Aquinas' view that there are two sources of truth, namely, divine
revelation and human reason, which complement each other. The domains of
faith and of reason are, for the most part, different, so that both faith
and reason are autonomous in their respective domains. There is, however, a
limited region in which the two domains touch each other and which is
common to both, because certain revealed truths are also discoverable by
reason left to its own devices. Now, even in this limited region common to
faith and to reason no genuine conflict is possible between their
respective truths, because God, the author of revelation, is also the
creator of reason and cannot contradict Himself. There is, nonetheless, in
this region, the possibility of apparent conflict between the truths of
faith and the truths of reason, because reason is fallible and can make
mistakes. However, since divine revelation is infallible, it follows that
in case of apparent conflict between a truth of faith and a truth of
reason, it behooves reason to allow itself to be corrected by faith and
hence to assent to the truth revealed, in the certainty that reason's own
mistake will in time be detected and that the truth of faith will prevail.

      With this summary of Aquinas' views in mind, we may now pass to Ex
corde Ecclesiae. The purpose of this document is to determine the
distinctive way in which Catholic universities, precisely as Catholic, are
meant to fulfill their function of "universitas magistrorum et scholarium
[which] is dedicated to research, to teaching and to the education of
students who freely associate with their teachers in a common love of
knowledge" (#1). As we are reminded in a note, this was the definition of
the university used by Pope Alexander IV in his letter to the University of
Paris of April 14, 1255 (note 1). By the way, that year Thomas Aquinas
began delivering his four-year cycle of lectures on Peter Lombard's
Sentences in Paris.* [*See James A. Weisheipl, O. P., Friar Thomas
d'Aquino: His Life, Thought and Works (Doubleday & Company, New York 1974,
p. 67].
      This matter-of-factly reference in the opening paragraph of the
document to the university as it existed in the thirteenth century is a
revealing indication of the frame of mind in which Ex corde Ecclesiae is
conceived. Indeed, there may be a certain continuity, as far as the
identity of the university as an institution is concerned, between the
middle of the thirteenth and the end of the twentieth century. There is,
however, between these two moments a rather long stretch of time in which
much has happened that has deeply affected the way in which the university
functions today. For instance, today there are secular universities,
something which was practically inconceivable in the thirteenth century,
when the university was born as an essentially Catholic, confessional
institution. Now, facts of this nature may likely have a bearing on the
appropriateness of thinking of the university as a timeless institution,
unaffected by the world in which it is inserted. Yet, Ex corde Ecclesiae
speaks as if the change from the homogeneously Catholic world of Western
Europe in wich the university was born to the present day's situation were
totally irrelevant. Perhaps this lack of historical sense is inherent to a
transhistorical notion of faith whose legitimacy the author or authors of
the document think is to be taken for granted. Even so, if the document is
to speak to today's people, it has to meet them as they are and where they
are. Yet, as we shall presently see, Ex corde Ecclesiae shows no
significant awareness that the confessional character of the university,
which appears to its author or authors as natural as it doubtlessly
appeared in the thirteenth century to the inhabitants of the homogeneously
Catholic portion of Western Europe in which the university was born, has
become highly problematic today for the people to whom the Apostolic
Constitution is addressed.
      But let us be precise. Actually, there is in the very first paragraph
of Ex corde Ecclesiae a hint that the idea that revelation and reason as
sources of knowledge are mutually exclusive rather than complementary, as
the medievals contended, is nowadays widespread. However, the hint is
rather obscure and merely incidental. At any rate, instead of taking this
idea seriously enough as to pause to consider why it would seem so
persuasive to people in our days, the document simply puts it aside without
further ado:

      "A Catholic university's privileged task is 'to unite existentially by
      intellectual effort two orders of reality that too frequently tend to
      be placed in opposition as though they were antithetical: the search
      for truth, and the certainty of already knowing the fount of truth'"
      (#1).

      Of course, once the incompatibility between the two orders of
reality, namely, revealed reality known through faith and natural reality
accessible to reason, is excluded, it follows that, as Aquinas claimed, the
knowledge of the whole of reality can emerge only out of the respective
complementary contributions of revelation and of reason:
      "... Catholic universities... are called to explore courageously the
      riches of revelation and of nature so that the united endeavor of
      intelligence and faith will enable people to come to the full measure
      of their humanity..." (#5).
      In addition, and thanks, to their acceptance of both sources of
knowledge, Catholic universities have the distinctive privilege of aiming
at a fuller, more complete knowledge than is accessible to their secular
counterparts:
      "It is the honor and responsibility of a Catholic university to
      consecrate itself without reserve to the cause of truth... a Catholic
      university is distinguished by its free search for the whole truth
      about nature, man and God" (#4).

      Moreover, because Catholic universities include both the truths
revealed by God and the truths accessible to human  reason, it is possible
in them to aspire to a superior synthesis that embraces the whole of
reality and fully satisfies the human being:
      "'It is necessary to work toward a higher synthesis of knowledge, in
      which alone lies the possibility of statisfying that thirst for truth
      which is profoundly inscribed in the heart of the human person.'"
      (#16).

      The difference as well as the complementarity of revelation and
reason as sources of knowledge ground their respective autonomy and insure
their harmony. This idea is forcefully expressed, in words that Aquinas
would gladly have signed, in an allocution of Pope John Paul II to a group
of scientists, quoted in a note of the document:
      "... while reason and faith surely represent two distinct orders of
      knowledge, each autonomous with regard to its own methods, the two
      must finally converge in the discovery of a single whole reality which
      has its origin in God" (note 20).

      Consistent with this fundamental trust in the ability of reason,
certain segments of the Apostolic Constitution describe the Catholic
university as an institution devoted to the "free search for the whole
truth" (#9), in which "each academic discipline retains its own inntegrity
and has its own methods" (# 17), and whose members are guaranteed the
possibility of "conducting an impartial search for truth, a search that is
neither subordinated to nor conditioned by particular interests of any kind
" (#7).
      Alas!, the relationship between faith and reason is more complex than
it would be if the two orders of knowledge were entirely different.
However, as underscored by Aquinas, there is also that limited region
common to both in which certain revealed truths are independently
accessible to reason on its own strength. Now, reason, as Aquinas argued in
this connection, is prone to error, particularly in difficult matters.
Thus, the trust in reason has to be tempered with distrust. The way in
which this point is made in Ex corde Ecclesiae is less straight-forward
than in Aquinas' texts, but it is made nonetheless:
      "... a specific part of a Catholic university's task is to promote
      dialogue between faith and reason, so that it can be seen more
      profoundly how faith and reason bear harmonious witness to the unity
      of all truth. While each academic discipline retains its own integrity
      and has its own methods, this dialogue demonstrates that 'methodical
      research within every branch of learning, when carried out in a truly
      scientific manner and in accord with moral norms, can never truly
      conflict with faith. For the things of the earth and the concerns of
      faith derive from the same God" (#17).
      In a barely perceptible form, a significant shift has taken place
here from an enthusiastic trust in reason by "the Church, which has 'an
intimate conviction that truth is [its] real ally... and that knowledge and
reason are sure ministers of faith" (#4), to a far more reserved attitude
toward it. The shift is barely perceptible because the motive for it,
namely, the weakness of reason, is only alluded to very indirectly. Indeed,
in the very process of arguing that research can never truly conflict with
faith when carried out in a truly scientific manner and according to moral
norms the document is implicitly acknowledging that apparent conflict may
arise when the conditions are not fulfilled.
      The way in which this shift occurs may be subtle, but its practical
consequences are highly visible and significant, for, aks a consekquence,
the freedom of research and the impartiality which the document announced
in its early paragraphs as characteristic of the Catholic university become
strongly qualified. Thus, we read that a Catholic university "guarantees
its members academic freedom, so long as the rights of the individual
person and of the community are preserved within the confines of the truth
and the common good" (#12. My emphasis). As for the previously announced
impartiality of the search for truth, "not conditioned by particular
interests of any kind," we are now told that
      "Every Catholic university, without ceasing to be a university, has a
      relationship to the Church that is essential to its institutional
      identity... One consequence of its essential relationship to the
      Church is that the institutional fidelity of the university to the
      Christian message includes a recognition of and an adherence to the
      teaching authority of the Church in matters of faith and morals" (#27)



      And lest any doubt might linger as to the areas where these
limitations and conditions apply, the document singles them out one by one.
Naturally, theology is  treated in particular detail:
      "... it is intrinsic to the principles and methods of their research
      and teaching in their academic discipline that theologians respect the
      authority of the bishops, and assent to Catholic doctrine according to
      the degree of authority with which it is taught"  (#29).
      Philosophy and the human sciences are also given definite guidelines
as to the way in which they are to be practiced:

      "A specific priority is the need to examine and evaluate the
      predominant values and norms of modern society and culture in a
      Christian perspective, and the responsibility to try to communicate to
      society those ethical and religious principles which give full meaning
      to human life. In this way a university can contribute further to the
      development of a true Christian anthropology, founded on the person of
      Christ, which will bring the dynamism of cration and redemption to
      bear on reality and the correct solution to the problems of life"
      (#33).
      Finally, even the natural sciences are mentioned in this connection:
      "Because knowledge is meant to serve the human person, research in a
      Catholic university is always carried out with a concern for the
      ethical and moral implications both of its methods and of its
      discoveries. This concern, while it must be present in all research,
      is particularly important in the areas of science and technology"
      (#18).

      With this we have completed our overview of the way in which the
identity of the Catholic university is authoritatively defined in Ex corde
Ecclesiae. What can be said about it?
      I must confess that, after a first reading of this Apostolic
Constitution, for all my sincere reverence for the Holy See I felt ashamed.
How could its author or authors, after all the conditions and limitations
specified in the document, honestly believe and maintain that academic
freedom and the autonomy of reason are respected in Catholic universities?
Given those conditions and limitations, would it not be the honorable thing
to do to avow before the face of the world, so as to be clearly understood
by everybody and by us Catholics in the first place, that faith and reason
are mutually exclusive, and that one has to opt between the two? Certainly,
that option would be painful, but much preferable to the duplicity of
taking away with one hand what one is giving with the other while
maintaining that nothing is taken away, as this Apostolic Constitution
does.
      Such was my first reaction. But then, on second thought, it dawned on
me that there is no duplicity whatswoever in the document, that its author
or authors are genuinely convinced that in the Catholic university as they
define it freedom of research, the integrity of the academic disciplines,
and the autonomy of reason are truly preserved. What perhaps neither they
nor most of us realize, at least not fully, is that, as thinkers, they and
we live in literally different worlds. As thinkers, they inhabit the late
medieval universe of discourse, whose language Aquinas' perfected, whereas
we think in post-Enl,ightenment categories.
       Most certainly, Aquinas was not dishonest when he affirmed the
autonomy of reason at the very moment he was limiting its freedom to what
had not been preempted by faith and was demanding its submission to faith
in those matters. He was not dishonest. He simply lived in world in which
faith was the undisputed initial given to which all the rest had to be
accommodated, much as reason is the undisputed initial given in the world
we inhabit. Aquinas, therefore, thought as someone who takes faith for
granted, and this necessarily shaped his conception of reason and its
limits and limitations. In fact, if one were to characterize in Kantian
terms Aquinas' project as a thinker, he was making room for reason within
faith. The project he carried out made sense and rallied the minds as long
as, and in the measure that, people shared Aquinas' initial posture.
However, with the triumph of heliocentrism and the development of the
quantitative experimental sciences, a paradigmatic shift took place. The
clash between the two parties involved in Galileo's trial dramatized the
shift from one world to another. Hence, Kant was no less honest than
Aquinas when he demanded the submission of faith to reason--"religion
within the limits of reason" was his exact expression. Indeed, this demand
which Aquinas would have found incomprehensible came as naturally to Kant
as the inverse demand had come to Aquinas, because Kant, as a child of the
Enlightenment, thought, so to say, from the opposite end than Aquinas, as
someone who takes reason for granted, and this unavoidably shaped his
conception of faith. Accordingly, the project that came naturally to him as
a thinker was precisely the inverse of Aquinas' project, namely, to make
room for faith within reason.
      Two centuries after Kant, in the vortex of post-modernity, we
ourselves may be far less sanguine about reason than people were in the
immediate aftermath of the Enlightnement, but our categories of thought,
reflected in our institutions of learning, owe much to the Enlightenment.
For us, culturally, the university is by nature the institution where
reason is autonomous and reigns supreme. No doubt, this creates thorny
problems for those  of us, members of the university, who are Catholic and
not totally unreflective, as well as for those universities which in our
highly secularized world still declare themselves Catholic without any
definite idea of what this might concretely mean or involve. Clearly, Ex
corde Ecclesiae is meant to respond to this crisis. Yet, for all practical
purposes, this document amounts to a call made in a language that is
foreign to us to inhabit a world that to its author or authors may appear
as natural as it appeared to the medieval thinkers who created it in their
vastly different circumstances but that to us is hardly understandable and
totally unlivable.
      Now, this is done by the author or authors of the document matter-of-
factly, in total innocence of the chiasm that separates them from their
intended audience, since there is not the slightest attempt on their part
to explain themselves to us. And, if my first reaction can be taken as
typical of that of their audience, the innocence of the chiasm is no lesser
on our part, for there would be no room for the suspicion of duplicity in
the authors of the document if we recognized that we and they inhabit
different worlds, so that what is normal for them is inconceivable for us,
and viceversa.
      It seems to me that the first constructive step is to attempt to heal
ourselves, on both sides of the fence, of this innocence, for without this
the suspicion either of duplicity on their part or of lack of faith on
ours, with the consequent total rupture in communication, is unavoidable.
It is obvious, though, that this first step, however necessary, cannot be
sufficient to suppress the chiasm. What is worse, it seems highly unlikely
either that the author or authors of Ex corde Ecclesiae and the people of
the mentality they represent will come to inhabit our world, a step which
they cannot see otherwise than as a renunciation of faith, or that we,
their audience, will move back to their world, even assuming that it were
within our power to make that shift, for that is a step that we cannot see
but as a renunciation of reason. The situation seems hopeless. At least for
those of us for whom the Christian faith and the membership in the Catholic
Church are of primary importance this is tragic. But is the situation as
hopeless at it seems? Only if the conceptions of reason and of faith from
which the chiasm between the two results are incorrigible. But are they?

                                  A WAY AROUND
1) Preliminary consideranda
      The world in which we actually function and the universe in which, as
a result of our explanatory theories, we think we function are sometimes
very different. For instance, theories of language built on the basis of
written language are fairly common, though they are at odds with the way
language actually functions when spoken. When this happens we continue to
speak as we always did, though the way we speak does not correspond to the
theory held as correct and valid by intellectuals. However, when this
happens, certain things occur. First, the separation between theory and
practice, between culture and interpretation of the culture, is likely to
create a tension between ordinary people and intellectuals.
      Second, since in scientificized cultures like ours intellectuals hold
the monopoly of scientific publication and are submissively listened to as
experts, that is, as authoritative arbiters, the more their theories are at
odds with the practice the larger the rift between the self-understanding
of people implicit in their practice and the more the disparagement by the
arbiters of the popular practice. As for the intellectuals themselves, who
tend to be reflective and consequently to notice the rift between the
scientific theory and the cultural practice and who wear two hats as both
members of the culture and intellectuals, the rift takes the proportions of
an unbearable conflict, in which often the intellectual side will carry the
day with the consequent abandonment of the disparaged practice.
      The example of theories of language may not be the best, and in any
case it is not well developed. But the point remains.
A theory may be at odds with cultural practice as well as being wrong, and
nonetheless be held as authoritative by the experts and even by the
reflective members of the culture. When this happens, the cultural practice
is endangered--and unwarrantedly so--, and the reflective members of the
culture are forced into a conflict of profound loyalties in which something
precious of their culture may be sacrificed to the illegitimate demands of
scientific-theoretical reason, or the legitimate demands of reason be
disparaged as systematically incompatible with life. The point seems to me
important because it expresses in a relatively simple, understandable way,
the present conflict between faith and reason with which we want to deal.
What complicates the conflict between faith and reason is the fact that in
this particular case the wrong theory does not come only from the side of
reason, independently from the side of faith, as an interpretation from
outside by contemplators of the culture, but from the side of faith as a
"theological" theory, as a "rational" theory of "faith", wrongly covered
with the authority of faith, an interpretation made as contemplators by the
very supporters and practitioners of the practice.
      After this proemium, a few other prefatory points.
      Firstly, Scientific Theory is contemplative, objectifying,
explanatory of uniformity and, therefore, universal. Scientific theory may
be wrong, but when it covers only objects, it cannot be in conflict with
the implicit self-interpretation or self-comprehension or self-
understanding of the subject.
      Secondly, since scientific theory is explanatory "per causas", there
can be scientific theory only of what is caused and is uniform because
proceeding from the 'same' cause. Thus, of what is not uniform and repeated
there can be no strictly scientific theory. Hence, as suspected by Aris in
the NE, there can be no strictly scientific theory of adult human conduct
because it is not caused but freely chosen. Yet, scientific theory can be
wrongly attempted of human conduct, including adult human conduct, (for
reasons that we will soon explore) without obvious contradiction. In that
case, however, not only will the theory be wrong but it will be in conflict
with the implicit self-understanding of reflective adults. And, insofar as
made by adults, it will be "self-stultifying."
      Thirdly, not only free subjects are subjects, but also non-free
people and even animals, whose conduct, even if caused, is mediated by
desire of. Thus by "adult human conduct," in the second paragraph, is to be
understood free, self-motivated conduct. They are the only "subjects" of
which there can be no strictly scientific explanation (cf. first
paragraph). Hence, of animals and of non-adults or non-free human beings
there can be scientific explanation (causal, universal), though an
explanation mediated in different degrees of complexity by desire-of.
However, since desire-of is a satisfying but unverifiable hypothesis in the
case of animals (how can we possibly know whether migratory birds feel
desire or are unconsciously programmed to migrate) and a hardly linguistic
verifiable hypotheis in the case of people (because the difference between
free and non-free people moved by culture and heredity is itself
unverifiable), the explanation  cannot be strictly scientific and, at the
limit, even not quite scientific in the case of people and even of higher
animals.
      Fourthly, even aside from considerations of applicative understanding
even in the realm of scientific explanation, the distinction between
scientific and phronetic Nous and between theoretical and practical
judgment or understanding as well as between or among their corresponding
objects is messy rather than tight. This might be due to the following
causes. (1) There is always possible, concerning people, to take the first
person or the third person perspective; and the third person perspective
can be on exposed subject or on object of exposure or on contemplated
object for explanation. (2) No one of this perspectives can be a priori
preferable, for we people are subject-agents, subject patients, objects
exposed to other subjects,  cultural animates and things among things.

2) Impartiality and the Medieval's conception  of reason
      Whether or not this demand of allegiance to faith is compatible with
the claim of impartiality is highly doubtful, and there is not in the
document the slightest attempt to allay the doubt with an explanation. In
any case, it is my opinion that that the two are incompatible and,
therefore, that an either-or choice has to be made. Secular universities
have made their choice and defend and demand impartiality and,
consequently, reject faith, which presupposes commitment to the truth on
the basis of authority and even against scientific evidence.
      From this document one gathers that the Church attempts to avoid
making a choice between the two, since it purports to guarantee
impartiality (that is, pure objectivity, non-commitment) in the acquisition
of knowledge, while demanding a commitment to faith that is obviously
partial and imposes limits on reason.  Actually, by trying to win on both
scores, Ex corde Ecclesiae is radically vitiated, for it is simply
incoherent to promise freedom from interference and guarantee impartiality,
and to demand allegiance and impose limits.
      THE QUESTION IS WHETHER THE DEMAND FOR IMPARTIALITY IS
ITSELF REASONABLE. I do not think it is. But, obviously, most academicians
do, and even the author or authors of Ex corde Ecclesiae seem to do too,
since they promise it in Catholic universities as a sine qua non for the
attainment of truth.
      How is this surprising agreement between two parties otherwise so
distant to be explained? To put it in a nutshell, they share in common the
very view of reason introduced by the medievals under the influence of
Aristoteles' theoretical works, such as the two Analytics, particularly the
Posterior, and rendered canonical by Aquinas. Now in this view reason is
not an appetitive faculty, it is meant to direct the rational appetitive
faculty and, therefore, desire can only interfere with the right exercise
of reason. So reason--that is, theoretical, not practical reason--is meant
to be impartial (without any other desire than the desire to know). SEE
BELOW LONMG QUOTATION OF AQUINAS'.
      As suggested in the first part, although in a slightly different
language, Ex corde Ecclesiae merely replicates as prescriptive the
medieval position that has not worked in the past and that, if anything,
has led to the erosion of the confidence in faith. In particular, 1) two
sources of knowledge and truth (2) but the truth is one, (3) and its
entirety is constituted by the truths revealed and by the truths discovered
by reason left to its own devices, (4) so that faith and reason are
complementary; indeed, (6) there is no possible contradiction between faith
and reason, (7) faith determines the limits within which reason is
autonomous.
      Underlying assumptions of the medieval conception: (1) knowledge is
achieved in the intellectual act of judging through assent of the mind
justified by the grasp of sufficient reason for assenting, that is,  either
by evidence or by God's or another knower's authority, (2) knowledge
consists in (intentional) conformity of the judging mind to reality, and
(3) reality has priority over and is independent from action and desire--
hence knowledge is the correlate of the disinterested desire to know.
      The view of reason that became canonical thanks to Aquinas is
constituted by a constellation of a number of interconnected notions, such
as knowledge, truth, certainty, etc.
      A good starting place is the very first question of the Summa
Theologica where Aquinas explains faith and reason by contrast to each
other in order to find out whether theology can be a scientific, rational
discipline. Both reason and faith are forms of knowing reality. They
differ, however, in the first place by their object. The object of faith
are divinely revealed truths, whereas the object of reason are naturally
accessible realities or aspects of reality. Secondly, they differ by their
motive. Whereas the motive that prompts reason to assent is evidence, the
motive of the assent of faith is God's authority.
Thirdly, they differ by their certainty. Since faith rests on God's
authority, who is absolultely veracious and infallible, the certainty of
the knowledge of faith is absolute in a way in which the certainty of even
the most evident piece of rational knowledge cannot be. As for the
relationship between reason and faiith, since God, the author of
revelation, is also the creator of reason and of the order of reality
accessible to it, there can be no contradiction between revealed and
rational truths; on the contrary, as knowledge of reality, reason and faith
complement each other, for through faith we have access to knowledge that
would be inaccessible to us without divine revelation.
      From the preceding contrast between faith and reason it follows that,
as knowledge, faith is, in one sense, inferior to reason in terms of the
evidence with which their respective objects are known, for the assent of
faith is based on authority whereas reason's assent is based on the
evidence of that to which it assents; in another sense, though, faith is
superior to reason in terms of certainty, for faith rests on God's
authority, who is  absolutely veracious and infallible, whereas reason
which rests on its own appraisal of the evidence is not itself infallible.
      According, then, to Aquinas, both faith and reason lead to knowledge.
But how does Aquinas conceive of knowledge?
      In his view, knowledge and action are entirely different, though not
unrelated, things or realities. They are different in that one can know
without acting. Knowledge is a matter of the intellect, exclusively,
whereas action certainly is not. Yet, knowledge and action are not
unrelated, in that both intentional and systematically successful action is
conditioned by knowledge. Thus, to allude to the first chapter of
Augustine's Confessions, in this view one cannot praise God (which is an
intentional, object-directed action) without knowing God, whereas one can
conceivably know God without actually praising Him. Furthermore, only on
the basis of knowing can one act successfully on a systematic basis. For
instance, if I am walking blindfolded in an unfamiliar room which has a
door, I can once by chance pass through a door without hitting the wall,
but it is on the basis of my knowing where exactly the door is situated
that I can systematically suceed in passing through the door without
hitting the wall.
      Another characterstic of this conception is the knowing intellect is
passive to the reality to be known. Indeed, knowledge is defined by Aquinas
as adaequatio mentis ad rem, conformity of the knowing mind to the reality
known, not the other way around. In other words, knowledge is pure
discovery, not inventive at all. It follows from this that real is what can
be known by the intellect, what can legitimately be assented to by the
intellect as being the case.
      A further, important characteristic of this conception of knowledge
is that it presupposes that knowledge is a matter of resemblance of the
knowing mind to the reality known. Aquinas calls the adaequatio or
conformity of the knowing mind to reality "intentional, that is,
"cognitional" rather than real, for the intellect does not really become
the table whose existence it affirms. But what does "intentional" mean,
concretely?  For to what aspect or 'formality' of the reality to be known
is the mind passive in knowledge? Not to its goodness or badness, its
attractiveness or repulsiveness to the subject, but only to what is
objective, to what the object is in itself, to what in the object is
capable of satisfying the subject's pure, disinterested desire to know.
      Knowledge is a matter of the intellect, which is a cognitive, not an
appetitive faculty. Furthermore, the intellect, by nature, automatically
and necessarily affirms or denies only when the evidence is sufficient.
Hence this conception of knowledge implies that the only appropriate
attitude to reach genuine knowledge is impartiality as to the goodnes or
badness of the reality to be known. In other words, the disinterested
desire to know must be primary and independent from any other desires or
interests, for such desires might prevent the object from showing itself as
it is rather than as it is wished to be, and might induce the intellect to
respond to desire rather than to evidence. In other words, real or
objective is what on the side of the object corresponds to the pure,
disinterested desire to know the object as it is in itself (not in relation
to the exposed subject) on the side of the subject. This is why
impartiality is essential for knowing, for any motivation other than the
disinterested desire to know (that is, to understand the object as
phenomenon, as visually appearing without distances, not as showing in the
hodological, dramatic space inhabited by the exposed subject) would distort
the reality to be known to make it conformable to the subject's desires.
Therefore the subject who pursues knowledge has to make a deliberate effort
to hush or neutralize any interests or desires that would interfere with
the disinterested desire to know, that is, with sheer curiosity. [SEE BELOW
AQUINAS' LONG QUOTATION AND "kNOWLEDGE AS JUDGMENT"].
            To sum up this conception of the knower, of the object known,
and of knowledge. Real or objective is what is to be known by a subject
guided by the disinterested desire to know, that is, by an impartial knower
who neutralizes all other desires and adequates itself passively to the
purely objective aspects of its object. Knowledge is the result of this
adaequatio mentis ad rem, and is the condition of possibility of genuinely
intentional and systematically successful action.

3) A criticism of the Medievals' notion of reason
      But does this notion of reason make sense? There are reasons to doubt
it, even leaving aside
the fact that the complementarity between reason and faith has actually  not
worked the way it was supposed to.
      One reason for suspecting this conception is that IT SEEMS  TO  IGNORE
THAT, WHEN REASON BEGINS TO FUNCTION, IT IS  ALREADY  CULTURALLY  COMMITTED.
WHAT ARIS WOULD CALL "THE APPEARANCES," "WHAT IS SAID,"  ARE  NOT  THE  SAME
FOR THE GREKS AND THE  JEWS.  FURTHERMORE,  SCIENTIFIC  REASONING  BASED  ON
EVIDENCE IS A SPECIALIZED AND SECONDARY  USE  OF  REASON,  GROUNDED  ON  THE
PRACTICAL REASON REFLECTED IN  ORDINARY  LANGUAGE.  -  In  this  conception,
reason is taken both as a faculty (the knowinbg faculty, the intellect)  and
as the product of the work of that faculty  by  its  own  strngth  (what  is
known, the truths of reason). Now, as a faculty reason  may  conceivably  be
neutral, but as a collection of truths which are assented to  or  taken  for
true it is not.
      A second consideration for  suspecting  this  conception  is  that  it
presupposes  a relationship between  knowing and acting that is  impossible,
given  the  way  in  which  knowledge  is  conceived.  According   to   this
conception, knowledge is a  necessary  condition  for  intentional  and  for
systematically successful action. Yet knowledge is understood in such a  way
that it cannot have an intrinsic relationship to action and be  a  necessary
condition for intentional and systematically successful  action,  much  less
be a sufficient condition for action. The reason is  that,  as  remarked  by
Aristotle, there is nothing in knowledge as so  far  understood  capable  by
itself of motivating the knowing subject to act (Cf. De Motu  Animalium,  p.
168).   Indeed,   knowledge   supposedly   is   purely   objective,   purely
'informative' about the  object  itself,  purely  revealing  of  the  object
itself independently of its goodnes or  badness  for  the  subject,  or  for
other subjects, or for  the  human  subject.  (Cf.  Aquinas  in  the  coming
quotation, who nonetheless maintains that action (and, presumably,  emotion)
are "consequent" to knowledge, not, as Aris maintains  concerning  practical
knowledge, constitutive of knowledge: See The Fragility of Goodness, pp.307-
317, and 378-391).  Such  knowledge  finds  its  expression  in  the  merely
informative  statement  (be  it  particular  or  explanatory)--what  in  his

quo est verum et  falsum"  by  contrast  to  other  "orationes"  which  "non
significant ipsum conceptum intellectus in  quo  est  verum  et  falsum  sed
quemdam  ordinem  ad  hoc  consequentem".  The  entire  paragraph   deserves

4) Speculative and practical reason: Nihil volitum quin praecognitum
      This scholastic dictum can be understood in two ways: (1) Nothing can
be pursued or averted that is not first theoretically known, that is,
merely assented to, object of mere assent. (2) Nothing can be pursued or
averted without being practically known, because pursuing or averting
something are constitutive of knowing something practically.
(1) Here "knowledge" terminates in assent, is different from pursuing and
averting (that is, from dispositional emotion and actual action), and can
exist without the subject pursuing or averting. Pursuing or averting,
however, cannot obtain without knowledge of this kind.
      Theoretical knowledge can be scientific (episteme--explanatory,
universal and necessary) or non-scientific (non-episteme but also non-
phronetic--particular, factual rather than int elligible).
      Theoretical-scientific knowledge, according to Aris, is not connected
with action and cannot be connected with action because it is knowledge of
universal and merely intelligible, whereas action involves particular
object desired or feared either by itself as end or for the sake of
something else as means.
      Thus  theoretical non-scientific knowledge is the only theoretical
knowledge that can conceivably correspond to the above specifications,
namely: what is so known is a particular that can be known (object of mere
assent) without being desired or averted, but cannot be desired or averted
without being known. Now, this latter trait presupposes that the object
sometimes desired is the same as the object previously known, that is,
known theoretically, that is, known independently of its goodness as end or
of its instrumental goodness in the particular situation. [In other words,
in this view,the God of philosoophers, who is neither adored, nor feared,
nor loved, is the God of Jesus-Christ].
      Is this first version of the scholastic dictum acceptable to Aris?
      There are reasons to doubt it.
      One, that is perhaps not quite Aristotelian in that Aris does not
refer clearly to it, is that the object desired--the object that moves, the
object of practical reason (chosen as end or selected as means?) has not
only to be particular but singular--this here and now--and singular with
distances of exposure, not without distances, that is, only definitionally.
(Situational Nous, dramatic space). [Yet Aris speaks in  NE of species-
goods that are ends, or at least potentially ends, dispositionally ends,
'natural' ends but deliberatively chosen nonetheless ...].
      Second, and related to the former, Aris introduces practical reason
apart from theoretical reason, because theoretical knowledge does not
motivate. In other words, the object of theoretical knowledge speaks to the
intelligence and prompts assent, but does not speak to the passions, does
not provoke desire or aversion. So, how could the cognitum or praecognitum
in this way be related to the volitum, and what need would he have of
practical reason if desire were purely extrinsic to the object? Would Aris
say that death is not fearful, that it becomes fearful for me only if, and
because, I arbitrarily choose to fear it (à la Sartre), and that it is not
more reasonable for a human being to act out of fear of death than out of,
say, agoraphobia? (After all, are not emotions, dispositions and actions
reasonable and unreasonable, appropriate and inappropriate, according to
Aris?). Or would he rather say, as I assume he would, that death is
fearful, but that under certain circumstances it can and even ought to be
preferred rather than...?
      But why does not the knowledge of theoretical reason motivate, and
why does the object of practical reason motivate?
      To start with, the object of episteme, being explanatory and
universal, speaks to the intelligence alone. Furthermore, being purely
abstract, there are no possible distances of exposure involved; and being
purely intelligible rather than imaginable, there is no possible desire of
it involved.
      Secondly, the non-scientific but non-phronetic object.
There are two possible approaches--curiosity and concern--, two possible
situations--exposure and non-exposure--, and four possible kinds of object--
desirable and non-desirable, subject (of possible concern) and thing. To
give an example: a lion is an avertendum, but only in situation of
exposure; in situation of non-exposure it may be object of curiosity. The
object is non-phronetic, be it desirable or not, subject or thing, whenever
it is approached as object of curiosity. The object of curiosity is the
object considered by itself, not as desirable and regardless of its being
subject of concern. - Aquinas' long quotation and, in general, Aris' notion
of knowledge, judgment and truth in the Posterior Analytics  and in the
Metaphysics takes no account of these different possibilities. It levels
down the knowing subject's attitude to curiosity, the situation to non-
exposure, and the object as non-desirable and non subject. And it makes of
such knowledge the antecedent of action. But what exactly is the possible
connection between such knowledge and action, so that action may
intelligibly be said to be a consequent of such knowledge?
      In the first place, since the object so known is not intended as
subject of concern, its subjectivity cannot motivate action. Then, since
the situation is of non-exposure, desire and aversion are excluded as
motivators of action. Finally, since all that is involved is the mere
desire to know, concern is excluded as motivator of action. Hence there can
be no intelligible connection between this knowledge and action in terms of
motivation.
      But cannot there be a connection in terms of "calculation" of action?
After all, 'scientific' knowledge is applicative. Knowing how "Aids" works
allows us to invent a cure. And knowing how "Aids" works is a matter of
curiosity and explanation, not of concern. So "Nihil volitum quin
praecognitum" may mean "Nothing can be intended as an end unless the means
to attain it are theoretically preknown so that they be available as
means."
Yes. But this is very different from saying that everything needs to be
theoretically prekonown in order to intend it otherwise than as object of
knowledge, that is, averted or desired and acted towards accordingly. For
here the means are known practically only when they are applied.........
      Let us clarify. Desiring or averting are neither modes of knowing
theoretically, nor mere feelings, nor a combination of the two, namely,
theoretical knowledge + feeling. They are desirous or aversive practical
knowledge terminating not in assent to predication but in action in
response that attempts to bring about a state of the world. Since they are
action in response, the object responded to can be abstracted or
extrapolated from the desire-response. For instance, from my adoration of
Xt in the eucharist the eucharistic presence may be abstracted and
affirmed. But it is abstracted and affirmed as object for potential
response, meant and to be heard applicatively, not as object of predication
for assent. Hence only those who respond, or are habitually disposed to
respond, emotionally-actively way know the object practically. For example,
fire within distances of exposure is object for response, object for
deliberation: to be afraid of, to be extinguished, to be averted,  to be
fled from, unless otherwise demanded by the circumstances and the character
of the agent in an ongoing history (think of martyrs). A response in terms
of mere assent would be inappropriate and unintelligible. In other words,
theoretical assent to fire in situation of exposure is neither appropriate
nor required. Not appropriate, for fire as dangerous and harmful is not
object to be contemplated but object for response. Not required, for
theoretical assent would add nothing useful to the practical knowledge.
      Yet, fire can be theoretically known more and more. And the
properties of fire theoretically known are properties of the fire
practically encountered. It is extinguishable by water and, more
effectively, by chemical foam. And this gives me, the exposed agent,
possibilities of extinguishing the fire that I woluld not otherwise have,
so that I can have as my goal extinguishing the fire as a satisfactory
state of affairs when previously I would have have to fly. Thus, I repeat,
there is a connection between theoretical and practical knowledge. But let
us not get things backwards the way Plato did (and Aris too, to the extent
that he remained Plato's disciple) because of his reduction of the good to
the true and of practical to theoretical knowledge: theoretical ignorance
is the only sin. That there is a connection between theoretical and
practical knowledge and that in cases of invented goals theoretical
knowledge conditions practical knowledge does not necessarily imply that
there is a constant and de iure primacy of theoretical over practical
knowledge, much less that theoretical knowledge is a picture of reality
(the eternal ideas) and that theoretical knowledge is knowledge by itself,
not applicatively in situation of encounter.
      THINK OF THE REASON WHY, IN SPITE OF HIS REFLECTION ABOUT HUMAN GOODS
IN THE ETHICS, THEY ARE NOT ENDS, AND THE WISE PERSON IS THE ULTIMATE
ARBITER AND MODEL. CULTURE GIVES US A GENERAL CONCEPTION OF THE HUMAN GOOD,
BUT THE WISE PARTICULARIZES AND SOMEETIMES EVEN CHANGES THE CULLTURE, AS
NUSSBAUM'S HECUBA CHANGED OR AT LEAST REJECTED HER CULTURE'S VIEW OF THE
GODS.
       Now, Aquinas maintains that theology is a theoretical and practical
science, though theoretical for the most part. This shows that he does not
realize what Aris, and later Hume, came to realize, namely that if
knowledge and reality are what they are supposed to be as conceived in the
Posterior Analytics  (and, Aris would have to say, in the Summa Theologica,
which replicates the Posterior Analytics), then there is no passage from
knowledge to action. In other words, there is no connection either way
between action and knowledge--there is no automatic action in response, no
teleological action, and no free teleological action. Yet, Aquinas assumes,
as do all those who unlike Aris and Hume do not realize that knowledge by
itself is not motivating, that desire and action is "consequent" to
knowledge (see end of long quotation above), that one can know God without
loving Him, although one cannot love God without knowing Him. (One can have
faith without charity and, as de Lubac observes, charity for Aquinas--or at
least the job of the will--is in a certain way extrinsic to faith). This is
why Aris introduces the practical intellect or "nous," which is the
deliberative intellect of the exposed agent, and practical knowledge, which
together constitute the realm of "practical reason." Unfortunately Aris did
not take the next step of reworking the relationship between speculative
and practical knowledge, if there is one, and left the impression that
theoretical and practical knowledge are two species of the same genus, and
that practicval knowledge is an inferior kind of knowledge. In other words,

7) The incompatibility of Aris' notions of theoretical and practical reason
      Plato, following Socrates' lead, collapsed practical reason into
theoretical reason, whereas it was Aristotle's merit to realize that
practical reason cannot be subsumed under theoretical reason, though
perhaps it can be subordinated to the latter as far as human happiness is
concerned. The contemplation of the immutable essences, though not of the
eternal ideas, remains the telos even in the Ethics, however precariously
combined with the life of action.
      Aristotle was on the way to becoming independent from Plato and
redressing his mistake. (De Motu Animalium, the Rhetoric, the Nicomachean
Ethics, partly De Anima). But Aris never completed his independence from
Plato. He never gave up the notion of episteme, and with it the works of
theoretical reason, very specially the Metaphysics, as his ultimate point
of reference.    Yet, he came close, or at least opened the way for us to
escape from Platonism. The practical syllogism, underpinned by desire and
need, can lead to the realization of the primacy of action over assent. And
deliberation introduces practical reason, a form of intentional action
(actus humanus) of which animals are incapable. However, Aris did not
undertake the debunking of theoretical reason and of "scientific knowledge"
and its implied primacy (given his conception of knowledge and truth), nor
does he seem to have even suspected the need to debunk it. He thought that
the two were compatible. As a result, he bequeathed us, through the
medievals, the philosophical mess in which we have been ever since. The
scepticism of post-modernism is the result of the failure of theoretical
reason, in which we had placed our hope, to live up to its promises, as
well as of our failure to replace it with practical reason.
      The fact is that Aristotle has two different and mutually exclusive
conceptions of knowledge: knowledge as theoretical judgment, and knowledge
as practical judgment (which in animals is practical knowledge without
judgment). To these correspond two different conceptions of the subject,
that is, of the human being, not distinguished by Aristotle: the pure,
invulnerable contemplator, and the exposed, needful, vulnerable agent-
patient.    "Reason" is the natural ability to know characteristic of the
human being. However, since Aris views on knowledge correspond to two
different and incompatible conceptions of the human being, he consequently
has two different and incompatible conceptions of reason: theoretical
reason, whose ideal is episteme, and practical reason, whose ideal is
appropriate action.
      Aris did not notice the incompatibility between his notions of
theoretical and practical reason. In fact he kept alive both the primacy of
theoretical reason and contemplation as the human telos. This is due to the
fact that he placed truth in judgment and considered practical judgment
capable of truth and falsity as if it were a matter of theoretical evidence
and assent, whereas in fact it is a matter of affection and action. It is
at this point that the fact that we are linguistic animals misled him, for
his interest was concentrated in statements written and read by
('scientific') contemplators rather than spoken and heard by exposed
agents. (This without taking into account his Platonic interest in essences
and essential truths.(Notice that Aris, like Plato, denies or at least
distrusts the truth of statements of particular facts because their truth
ceases to be--cf. Suzanne Mansion. This presupposes an idea of truth as
intentional conformity of the mind to the real that comes very close to
picture).
      In practical judgments there is no evidence (in the sense of Kantian
theoretical intuition), and therefore no possibility of proof, because the
hearer supplies in part with his or her own evaluative situation in
relation to the object what would count as evidence (in other words, the
evidence is not objective), and anyway the point is not to assent to what
is stated or can be stated, but to respond appropriately.
      Practical reason is the involved exposed agent's reason, or the
witness's reason who may become involved in the future. Therefore practical
reason is always toward the future and potentially deliberative, since the
agent supplies his or her own situation (but on the basis of his or her
comprehension of a factual situation taken for granted).
      However, history is possible, and stories (novels) too, when told

      As far as theoretical knowledge goes, Aris denies that animals know
for two interconnected reasons: (a) because they cannot escape the sphere
of practical interest, that is, the dimension of exposure, so that they
only memorize and generalize what is useful for their future action; (b)
because they cannot abstract and conceptualize the object to convert it
into object of predication, so that they remain within the field of the
imaginable. . The two reasons are interconnected. Indeed, to escape the
sphere of practical interest, abstraction and conceptualization are
required, for only in this way can the object be considered by itself,
independently of its usefulness for the agent. But, conversely, to abstract
and conceptualize the object one needs to cease considering it in terms of
its usefulness for the agent, to want to grasp it by itself. Which is to
say that people belong in an entirely different order than animals because
they are linguistic agents and this renders possible for them what is
impossible for animals, namely, Knowledge with a capital K.

in his Ethics, namely an agent capable of decision and action, that is, an
agent who can determine himself or herself to desire and  who can therefore
act not according to spontaneous desire but according to reason. Two
questions need to be answered in order to understand this point. First, how
and why does this differ from the way animals act? Second, what is involved
in acting rationally?
      (1) How does this differ from the way animals act?

knowledge is that it is desirous knowledge, that is, knowledge informed by
desire.     Now, the action of the higher animals is not, according to
Aristotle, purely mechanical, for it involves application based on past
experience; thus there is in the higher animals a sort of knowledge
inspired by desire that results in not purely mechanical actions but in
actions guided by the passively received goal. Compared to human action,
though, the action of even the higher animals has a uniformity that human
action overcomes. And this in at least two ways. In animal action the
concrete desired goals are determined by instinct and are determinant of
action, though the means can be improved by experience. As Nussbaum says,
"animals follow the appearance of the moment" (MA, 193). Now, people cannot
only delay their response; they can abstain from responding in the way that
instinctoid spontaneous desire suggests, that is, people can actively
invent their goal. A hungry person may abstain from eating when food is
available: hunger strike. The appearance--desired food--is not followed;
instead, another appearance is followed which is not passively received but
invented as goal to be followed , as mihi desirandum et adipiscendum. For
this kind of action the means are, objectively, as available to animals as
they are to people, since the means are mere abstention; but they are not
available subjectively, in that it is not possible for animals to avail
themselves of abstention. To animals the desiderandum et adipiscendum when
the means are available is perceptually given in the apupearance. By
contrast, people invent sometimes, or at least choose, the appearance to be
realized. And if they just respond to the given appearance as animals do,
they do it by choice, since they could abstain from responding and choose
their own response.
      Thus, for Aris, there are two kinds of desires and two kinds of
objects: animal desire and human desire; object of animal desire and object
of human (either rational or irrational, but in any case freely chosen as
an end, or at least freely adhered to) desire. In other words, man invents
and brings about reality, though not arbitrarily but on the basis of the
existing material (and cultural?) conditions. Here "real" is not object
known (or knowable) but object feared or desired (or desirable), that is,
what is capable of satisfying need or desire or of preventing the
satisfaction of need or desire, and what is desired or avoided not for its
own sake but for the sake of something else.
      Why, then, does Aris speak of the practical syllogism only in
connection with deliberation and as if it were an exclusively human conduct
or way of knowing? He speaks of it in connection with deliberation because,
in the particular context of the Ethics, Aris is interested not in desire
but in rational desire, not in action but in action regulated by a
conception of happiness that is object of rational desire. But does Aris
speak of practical judgment only in connection with deliberation? I think
so, for animals perceive practically, but they do not judge practically.
What I above characterized as "non-linguistic enthymemes" of animas are
really not enthymemes, which presuppose a linguistic agent who judges and
combines judgments, but practical perceptions.
      Thus people can invent and choose goals, because they can abstain
from responding according to instinctoid desire. And, as we shall see, they
can also invent means that are not presently available, so as to render
possible the realization of a goal that was previously impossible. But why
can they, whereas animals cannot? Is this a surd brute fact?
      No. Here their linguistic ability intervenes and makes possible what
is impossible to animals. People can speak about that to which they respond
even in its absence and when they are not exposed to it. This
objectification creates a distance between the object and the response
within which the appropriateness of the response habitually prompted by the
object may be considered. In addition, most of the responses of linguistic
subjects are learned and culturally conditioned, not biologically
inherited. This cultural induction by which objects for response are
defined and responses reinforced positively or negatively is for the most
part linguistically done, so that the object itself is perceived as
demanding or at least justifying the culturally induced response, and
correlatively the culturally induced response is automatically taken for
appropriate. Because the responses themselves, as well as their correlate
objects, are informed by culturally induced attitudes which are not the
result of irresistible and unmodifiable instinctive pulls, these acquired
attitudinal desires and aversions are, at least in principle--because the
force of habit combined with inherited predispositions may be overwhelming--
controllable and modifiable. The human agent shaped by culture has,
therefore, a lived experience of conflict between spontaneous desire or
aversion and induced attitudinal desire or aversion that informs actual
action and curves spontaneous desire and aversion and even supplants them
through habituation. (Remeber Civilization and its Discontents, with its
principles of reality and desire). (REMEBER THAT "ANIMALS DO NOT BECOME
AKRATIC" - Nussbaum, MA 201).     Moreover, as part of his or her
acculturation, the human agent not only experiences linguistically the
reality to be responded to, but through the explanatory and justificatory
process through which acculturation is reinforced is used to talk about
objects and responses to objects objectively, by themselves, and to think
of responses in terms of appropriateness.
      But what is the standard by which appropriateness is adjudicated?
There is not one single standard but a combination of them. In general,
however, the relevant considerations have to do with the purely technical
adequacy of the action to realize the goal pursued or  with the
appropriateness of the goal chosen.
Let us consider the former first, then the latter.
      The former has to do, in the first  place,  with  the  possibility  of
realization  of  the  goal  chosen.  And  this,   in   its   turn,   depends
dialectically on the availability of the means to the agent who chooses  the
goal for his or her action. Only a goal  that  appears  concretely  possible
because the means appear to be available to the agent can  be  chosen  as  a
goal. But in certain cases  the  means  have  not  only  to  be  implemented
because already available but invented, like agriculture  for  an  instance,
or the cure of aids. This invention depends on 'the  nature  of  the  object
itself,' independently of desire. Here is where  objective  thinking  enters
and with it what might be called theoretical reason.
      The desire of a cure for aids leads to looking for how  and  why  aids
produces its ill effects, and to what--and how and why--it reacts.  If  aids
were not an illness to be done away with (observed syndrome),  aids  as  its
to be known cause to be suppressed would not exist for us. Thus  the  search
is teleological and interested,  informed  by  desire.  But  the  search  is
objective, that is independent  of  desire,  and  therefore  presupposes  no
desire in the researcher other than the desire  to  understand  the  object,
for here the subject is totally passive to the object. The fit in this  case
is world to word, and there is no deliberation-declaration possible.  It  is
only when the object is known and the means to do away  with  it  are  known
and made available, that the action-conclusion of  the  practical  syllogism
will obtain, and it will be  implemented  not  by  the  researchers  but  by
physicians. In other words, the objective knowledge of the cause  understood
applicatively as to be suppressed led to  the  objective  knowledge  of  the
means, which understood applicatively led to their  making  them  available,
all of which understood applicatively led to the action by the physician.
      What then is theoretical knowledge, at least in a case of  this  type?
It is knowledge of the object  by  itself  abstracted  from  all  irrelevant
relations and knowledge of the means to be employed  to  do  away  with  it,
which renders possible choosing doing away with aids as  an  end.  Certainly
animals are incapable of this knowledge  for  they  do  not  discover-invent
ends (their ends are passively received), or means  to  ends  not  passively
received.
      But what is the  status  of  theoretical  knowledge  when  it  is  not
applied? Is it a picture of aids and of the means to do away  with  it?  Let
us say that the conclusion of research is that aids is a  virus  of  such  a
kind and that it responds favorably to such a dose of X.  Notice  that  this
kind of technical knwoledge is only knowledge (that is,  actually  functions
as knowledge) for the  technically  prepared  people  capable  of  using  it
applicatively, capable of 'understanding' it so as to hear it  applicatively
under  the  appropriate  conditions,  not  for  us  laymen  who  hear  these
statements as pictures of reality. Saying that aids is a  virus  means  that
it is to be treated as a virus  rather  than  as  something  else,  much  as
saying to you that your mother is on the phone is saying that she is  to  be
so responded by you now. But is not saying that your mother is on the  phone
something true, independently of whether you respond to it or not?
      If by something true we mean that that is where your mother  is  right
now exposed to action, so that what knowledge  yields  and  is  supposed  to
yield for the  knower  is  objects  for  response  and  that  the  truth  of
statements is to be heard applicatively and cashed in terms of action,  then
saying that your mother is on the phone is saying something true.  In  other
words,  there  is  no  purely  theoretical  knowledge,  for  even  the  most
theoretical knowledge  is  ultimately  to  be  understood  applicatively  by
exposed agents. Theoretical knowledge,  then,  is  a  sort  of  purposefully
built pair of glasses that makes possible appropriate response  to  what  is
known. In short, theoretical reason is subordinated to practical reason.
      A sticky point remains obscure.  Concrete  objects  for  response  are
always known as to be responded to surrounded by other objects and known  by
a subject who is also surrounded by  other  objects.  In  other  words,  the
object of perceptual encounter is always a figure  in  a  background  for  a
subject-agent  who  also  acts  from  a  particular  situation,   that   is,
surrounded  by other objects as subject-figure in  a  background.  Now,  the
background of the object for response is  perceived  but  only  subsidiarily
(that is, is not known), and the subject's background is not  known  either,
perhaps not even perceived but only subsidiarily sensed.  Yet  we  attribute
factual reality to all these traits. For instance, when "your mother  is  on
the phone," I perceive the phone by the table at  a  certain  distance  from
the chair and from me. This is another way of saying that perception is  not
only perspectival but practically-perspectival, that is,  situational,  that
the object to be responded to is  in  a  concrete  hodological  space  where
practical  distances  to  be  cleared  unfold  from  the  agent,  where  the
background  objects  possess  a  coefficient  of  instrumentality  that   is
favorable or adverse to the realization of the subject-agent, and  that  the
subject-agent is himself exposed in  the  world.  This,  precisely,  is  the
reason why, knowledge being essentially practical  and  by  reference  to  a
project to be realized by an exposed subject, that is, by  a  subject-object
of exposure, knowledge is essentially relative and  cannot  be  hypostasized
and absolutized in itself, for  what  is  object  for  response  for  me  is
background for you whose project  is  different,  and  what  is  object  for
response for me now can become background for me in a moment without  itself
changing except in relation to my project.
       Appearances--that  is,  "contents'  of  experiences  of  objects   by
subjects--are obviously relative.  They  are  appearances  of  this  exposed
object to this subject so situated, and they are not to be  contemplated  in
themselves but to be instrumentally transcended toward action. In fact  they
are so 'read' when the subject is involved in action, not as pictures to  be
contemplated but as guiding maps. To attempt to identify  the  reality  with
its appearances in subjects does  not  make  sense,  much  less  to  try  to
identify the reality with a selected series of  its  appearances,  which  in
general tend to be visual appearances.
      I have begun to fall into the very trap I am  criticizing.  I  am  now
reflecting as a contemplator. I have isolated the knowing activity as if  it
were our only relationship to the surrounding world,  and  an  instantaneous
one at that. I have forgotten that we know as exposed subjects, that  is  as
subjects-objects of exposure to other subjects-objects of  exposure  to  us,
and that our exposure is more extended  than  our  knowledge  and  that  our
awareness of it, for we are no less exposed when asleep and exposed to  what
we do not know. Action is temporal interaction, that is,  involves  passion.
Successful action is not the one that  succeeds  immediately  only  to  fail
subsequently, but the one that has the best chances  of  succeeding  all  in
all in the  temporal,  finite  flux  and  reflux  of  reciprocal  relations.
Successful action across time demands successful  passion  across  time,  as
successful a lived history as it can be in an imperfect  world  and  insofar
as it depends on me. My project of which you are part should involve  taking
you as subject affected, not as mere object instrument. So  knowledge  which
is always embedded in a project is, to an extent, a matter  of  choice.  The
point is not to interpret the world but to build the world.

9) Faith as knowledge
      Faith, in  a  general  way--hoi  pistoi--is  the  ability  to  conduct
oneself in the appropriate Christian way, that is, to  respond  to  God  and
other people as a Christian in the concrete circumstances of  the  responder
ought to respond. It is Christian faith because  the  central  axis  of  the
conduct, its motivating framework,  so  to  say,  is  Jesus-Christ  and  His
teaching (much as one would say that the central axis of the conduct of  the
protagonist of Kafka's The Castle is the Master of the castle). The  more  a
life is structured around the Jesus-Christ  axis,  that  is,  the  more  the
whole conduct of a person can be understood by reference to that  motivating
framework, the more it is Christian, the more it is the life of  faith.  The
saints are the best of the faithful. In the  Ancient  Testament  Abraham  is
the model, for his entire  life  was  defined  by  obedience  (as  an  actus
humanus, as a decisdion resulting from deliberation) to God.
      In relation to Jesus-Christ, faith is knowledge, for in a  way  Jesus-
Christ is known. But what kind of knowledge?
      Not theoretical knowledge. Theoretical knowledge is the  knowledge  of
a pure contemplator of contemplated object, of an intelligent  observer  who
may well be a morally evil  person,  not  of  an  exposed  subject.  It  is,
therefore, purely objective, merely informative  about  the  object  itself.
Furthermore, it abstracts the object from all relations and objectifies  it,
that is, considers it by itself, which amounts  to  conceptualizing  it.  By
the same token, this conceptualized-known object demands no response on  the
knower's part, for neither is the knower exposed to it nor is  an  abstract,
purely intelligible  concept  the  sort  of  object  that  can  motivate  to
respond. It is characteristic of theoretical knowledge  to  terminate  in  a
judgment whose expression is a  statement,  a  mere  enunciation  about  the
object of the kind spoken of by Aquinas in the long quotation above.
      By contrast, the knowledge in which faith consists  is  both  personal
and affective. Insofar, therefore, as faith is knowledge, it has  to  be  in
one way or another personal and motivating knowledge. Personal not  only  in
the sense that all knowledge is a subject's knowledge, for knowledge is  not
something that floats around by itself, but  personal  in  the  sense  of  a
relationship of exposure of the subject to the object in which  the  subject
is at stake, which makes the knowledge affective.
      However, we should not think of  knowledge,  much  less  of  practical
knowledge, as a picture of reality. Plantinga argues that there are  beliefs
that are more foundational than others. In a similar way, there are, in  the
case  of  practical  knowledge,  objects  for   response   that   are   more
foundational than others. In the case of practical  knowledge,  though,  the
standard for comparative foundationality arises from the  history  in  which
the agent is involved, not from a system. (Cf. The Plan of the Summa).
      [Concerning faith: Insofar as faith  is  knowledge,  it  is  knowledge
whose subject is the exposed agent, practical knowledge. The mistake of  the
medievals was to assimilate faith to theoretical knowledge, and  to  try  to
establish the truth of the statements of the faithful, which  are  practical
statements  to  be  heard  applicatively,  as  if  they   were   scientific,
theoretical statements of pure contemplators.]

10) Logic, judgment, and system
      "Our knowledge of God's existence is just our knowledge of  the  truth
      of the judgment, 'Deus est'." (Lonergan, Verbum, p. 8).
      Nussbaum says that  the  medievals,  perhaps  AQUINAS  in  particular,
mistook  the  view  of  scientific  knowledge  presented  in  the  Posterior
Analytics  for  Aris'  view  of   the   heuristic   procedure   to   acquire
(scientific?) knowledge, which  does  not  possess  the  characteristics  of
Platonic episteme, when in fact Aris is speaking there of  the  presentation
of already acquired knowledge.    In other words, what Aris expounds in  the
Analytics about  scientific  knowledge  is  not  a  logica  inventionis  sed
expositionis, for what Aris calls scientific knowledge  is  closer  to  what
Plato  calls  "opinion"  than  to  what  he  calls  "episteme."   Scientific
knowledge in the sense of strict episteme is  possible  for  the  Plato  who
believed in the intuition of Ideas, but impossible for Aris  who  does  not.
For Aris scientific knowledge is less stringent, more empirically  grounded,
not absolutely necessarily true, and thus  there  are  different  grades  of
(scientific?) knowledge as far as certainty is concerned.   The  implication
is that the medievals took literally the idea  of  scientific  knowledge  as
consisting of universal and necessarily true propositions (some  intuitively
grasped by the intellect, some  deductively  connected),  whereas  Aris  did
not. And he did not because he was not a foundationalist  intuitionist  like
Plato, but empirically-minded.
      BUT THIS DOES NOT TAKE INTO ACCOUNT THE DISTINCTION BETWEEN
SPECULATIVE AND PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE. Nussbaum realizes that Aris'
scientific knowledge is closer to opinion than to Plato's episteme. In this
sense, she argues that there is no difference between speculative and
practical knowledge. But she seems to preserve the distinction between
speculative and practical knowledge, whereas I think that all knowledge is
practical.
      E. Weil, "The Place of Logic in Aris' Thought," in Articles on
Aristotle: 1. Science, edited by Jonathan Barnes. Malcolm Schofield,

prejudice in their favour in as much as they encapsulate the common
experience of mankind" (p. 93). "Nor should  we talk of 'probable'/ or
'plausible' theses in connection with Topics, but rather translate endoxon

the meaning Aris gave it: 'endoxa are what is accepted by everyone or by
most men or by the wise--either all of them or the most celebrated and
endoxoi of them' (Top. I, 1, 100bb1-3)."

      The Aristotelian theory and ideal of knowledge and truth is drawn
from the reading of written examples of universal stated judgments already
assented to or to be assented to, that is, from the stated 'content,' from
the 'picture'. THIS WAS MY FIRST HYPOTHESIS, CONTRADICTED BY LONERGAN
CONCERNING THE ACQUISITION OF KNOWLEDGE, NOT BY NUSSBAUM OR WEIL. SO I HAVE
TO CLARIFY FURTHER OR MODIFY MY INITIAL HYPOTHESIS.
       My  hypothesis  needs  clarification.  It  is  my  hunch   that   the
Aristotelian conception of knowledge expounded in the  Analytics,  that  is,
the conception of speculative knowledge by contrast  to  that  of  practical
knowledge expounded in the DMA and NE,  is  based  on  the  analogy  between
knowledge and reading and, correspondingly, between reality  and  a  written
text to be understood and assented to, namely,  a  written  statement,*  for
only statements aim at  assent  and  can  be  assented  to.  And  Lonergan's
explanation of the Aristotelian-Thomist theory of knowledge (which does  not
deal at all with practical knowledge) seems to confirm my point.

      Speculative knowledge is motivated exclusively by the desire  to  know
and is, therefore, guided in each case by a question of  mere  curiosity  to
be satisfied by an answer that prompts mere assent. This is what  a  written
statement aims at exclusively, particularly a  'scientific'  statement  that
abstracts uniformity (not a concrete object) and explains it.  Now,  in  the
original process of knowing by the discoverer, as  described  by  Aristotle,
the real appears as a text to be understood, not as somethinbg to which  one
is exposed and which  has  to  be  responded  to.  The  simplex  apprehensio
represents a tentative, prospective  reading  which,  if  confirmed  by  the
reflective act that looks for sufficient reason,  terminates  in  the  firm,
committed reading represented by the  judgment.  This  original  process  of
discovery is in part replicated in the communication  of  the  discovery  by
the teacher. The teacher presents the content of the judgment,  the  stated,
as a hypothesis, and the sufficient reason for it, in the hope of  prompting
the disciples' assent--that is, the same reading. The  difference,  then  is
that in the case of the discoverer the simplex apprehensio confirmed in  the
judgment is an uncharted discovery, whereas in the case of the disciple  the
simplex apprehensio is received as already formed (which demands  much  less
intelligence and tends to prevent a fresh hypothesizing).  Notice  that,  as
Lonergan argues, the hypothesis or simplex apprehensio is a  synthesis,  for
it is prospectively predicative. And what the judgment judges  directly  and
affirms is the synthesis, the reality as known, the reality as stated,  that
is, as object of assent, not the reality  as  encountered  in  situation  of
exposure  and  to  be  responded  to.  Without  apparently   realizing   the
implications of his point, Lonergan puts it very  well:  "Our  knowledge  of
God's existence is just our knowledge of the truth of  the  judgment,  'Deus
est'."
      The Analytics, then, deal with  the  speculative  "Nous,"  the  "nous"
that achieves knowledge that does not move (to emotion and action),  not  at
all with the practical knowledge with which the DMA and  the  NE  deal.  Its
rhetoric, which is not literally a rhethoric since it aims at  demonstration
of purely  intelligible  and,  therefore  certain,  object  rather  than  at
persuasion of concrete, emotive object of  decision  is  the  'rhetoric'  of
written scientific papers (and  of  Aquinas'  Summa  Theologica)  which,  if
successful, terminate in "It is true that..."  as  object  of  mere  assent.

      IN CONCLUSION: It becomes increasingly  clear  to  me  that  Aris  has
indeed  two  different  and  mutually  incompatible  views   of   knowledge.
Speculative and practical knowledge are not two species of the  same  genus,
but two genera. Moreover, even speculative knowlege--for there is  something
to Aris' analysis of speculative knowledge--is not knowledge as  a  picture,
but as an applicative picture, for it is potentially and actually  knowledge
only when it can function and actually functions applicatively.
