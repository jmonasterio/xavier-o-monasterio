                 DIRECT AND REFLECTIVE KNOWLEDGE

      The ‘judgment’ of which Aristotle speaks in connection with episteme
or “scientific knowledge,”  –the intellectual act to which alone he
attributes absolute certainty as knowledge–is a reflective act. It
presupposes abstraction from the sensible phantasm, a concept or
definition, and the sentence passed on the truth of the concept on the
basis of sufficient reason for affirming or denying the conformity of the
defined to reality, in which judgment properly called consists. For
Aristotle, for whom as a philosopher the certainty of scientific knowledge
(see II Posterior Analytics, lectio 19) is of fundamental importance in
order to distinguish knowledge from opinion, the reflective character of
judgment is essential for it is through it that the conformity of what is
conceived to what is the case is ascertained. Indeed, the mind may or may
not conform to reality; the judgment determines whether or not it actually
conforms by finding or not finding sufficient reason for affirming.

      In the strictly scientific judgment, the case of the truth of the
judgment is straightforward and simple, at least for Aristotle, because he
takes it for granted that the object of such judgment is a  reality that is
universal and necessary –a genuine epistaton or object of epsiteme, that
is, of necessarily true knowledge, rather than a mere doxaston or object of
opinion. However, the case of a judgment whose object is not intelligible
but sensible and, therefore, not scientifically knowable because of the
contingency of sensible objects, is thornier. Aristotle seems to solve it
by accepting the possibility of the material truth of the perceptual
phantasm and making this the object of the reflective judgment as to the
conditions under which the perception obtained. If, on reflection, the
conditions for correct perception appear to have been adequate, this will
be taken for sufficient reason for affirming and the affirmative judgment
will automatically follow. The judgment consists in assent to what is
judged as being  true or conform to reality. Thanks to the reflective
character of judgment on the sufficiency of the reason for affirming or
denying, formal truth is attained: that is, not mere resemblance to or
conformity with the object, but knowledge of such conformity. In this case,
not only does the subject know, but knows that he or she knows, knows with
rational certainty, possesses genuine knowledge by contrast to opinion or
belief.

      Whatever the merits of the Aristotelian view on the scientific
judgment, his view on the perceptual judgment –if  the summary I just gave
of it represents indeed Aristotle’s view– is definitely unsatisfactory in
that it implies that animals are incapable of knowing, which is definitely
contrary to Aristotle’s conviction. The reason for this implication is that
judgment is an intellectual act and a reflective one, at that, and animals
possess no intellect and are incapable of reflection. Moreover, this view
on the perceptual judgment is unsatisfactory even concerning the case of
people, in that the intellect is only capable of knowing what is
intelligible and therefore universal, whereas the objects of perception are
singular or individual –sensible rather than intelligible. Now, Aristotle
has no doubt that we people know not only the universal but also the
individual. To use Aristotle’s own example, we know Callias not only as a
human being, that is, as a member of the human class, but also as Callias,
that is, as this individual.[1] Moreover, there is every reason to
grant–and this did not escape Aristotle– that a dog recognizes his master
as this individual. Lastly, as an intellectual act dealing with the purely
intelligible, the judgment is by itself necessarily theoretical. Indeed, it
lacks all possible connection with action, for action presupposes affection
and evaluation. If so, there would be no possible room for practical
knowledge either in the case of people or of animals!

      This aporia, however,  is perhaps less intractable than it appears to
be. It arises from focusing exclusively on the notion of judgment presented
by Aristotle in connection with scientific knowledge, when he is explaining
how the scientific judgment because of the universality and necessity of
its object is necessarily true. This knowledge is explanatory in that it
grasps the reason why, and  is accessible only to the intellect. By the
same token, such knowledge is inaccessible to animals and cannot be
practical, for practical knowledge is always particular. Now, one of the
merits of Devereux is to show that,  precisely in connection with practical
knowledge, Aristotle indicates the existence of another kind of universal,
aside from the explanatory universal whose knowledge is strictly
intellectual and not directly connected with action. This non-explanatory
universal furnishes the solution to the mentioned aporia.

      Aristotle attributes practical knowledge to both animals and people.
One of the limitations of Devereux’s analysis is that, though his focus is
on practical knowledge by contrast to theoretical knowledge, he fails to
deal at all with the practical knowledge animals possess. The question is
whether animals can actually be capable of practical knowledge as it is
understood and defined by both Aristotle and Devereux.

      Two aspects of Devereux’s conception of practical knowledge give me
pause as to whether animals are capable of practical knowledge if it is to
be conceived in that way. (1) First, the kind of grasp of universality
supposedly involved in practical knowledge. If the grasp is theoretical
rather than practical, even if it is said to be non-explanatory, it is
beyond the ken of animals. (2) Second, the absence of any mention of need
and desire in relation to practical knowledge. As forcefully underscored by
Nussbaum in relation to other Aristotelian texts concerning practical
knowledge common to people and animals, without desire knowledge cannot be
practical, for it cannot terminate in action. Now, Devereux chooses to
analyze only those texts in which there is no mention of need and desire.
That is, those texts in which Aristotle model of knowledge is Platonic
resemblance or identity, and no intelligible connection with action. As a
consequence, in those texts Aristotle tends to assimilate practical to
theoretical knowledge, and therefore to amalgamate the abilities of animals
with those of people, rather than assimilating the abilities of people to
those of animals. The reason for this is that, being still too much under
Plato’s influence, Aristotle’s notion of knowledge is still theoretical
correspondence  between the object in the mind and the real object

      (1) First.
      To start getting some idea of what Aristotle is talking about when he
refers to a knowledge of the universal that is not intellectual but
sensible it will be useful to refer to a shrewd distinction that Devereux
finds implicit in Aristotle doctrine, and which for lack of explicitness
has led to confusion. One thing , says Devereux, is the universal as
knowledge of a general fact; another thing is the universal as explanatory
knowledge of a general fact. Unfortunately, Devereux  fails to distinguish
strictly explanatory knowledge, that is, knowledge that renders
intelligible a fact by grasping the formal cause or reason that renders it
intelligible, from knowledge that is not strictly explanatory, in that it
simply relates one general fact known by sensible observation to another
general fact as its cause, without in any way rendering it intelligible.
[Devereux’s distinction is irrelevant to my point: even factual, non-
explanatory knowledge is objective rather than applicative]. To quote
Devereux’s analysis of an example used by Aristotle,

      “The awareness that chicken is wholesome is given as an instance of
      particular knowledge. An untutored empiric may know this fact, but he
      will not know why chicken is wholesome. The person who has
      investigated the matter scientifically will have discovered that all
      light meats are wholesome, and that it is in virtue of being a light
      meat that chicken is wholesome. According to the terms of the example,
      this knowledge that light meats are wholesome is universal. But as
      Cooper points out, the content of the particular knowledge is also
      universal in the ordinary sense of the term ; it contains no reference
      to specific individuals. The important difference seems to be that the
      universal knowledge is explanatory in a way that the particular is
      not” (Devereux, p. 491).

      Devereux is on the right track in saying that what distinguishes
universal knowledge from particular is that the former is explanatory in a
way that particular knowledge is not. However, Devereux thinks that the
investigator who discovers that light meats are wholesome, and that
chicken is wholesome  because it is a case of light meat, has attained a
piece of scientific knowledge, for scientific knowledge is explanatory.
What Devereux fails to notice,  however, is that the way in which this
discovery is explanatory is by relating a general fact to another general
fact. Now this second fact is not explanatory, for it is not a reason that
explains the first fact. What is crucial is that Devereux thinks that this
is the kind of explanation involved in what Aristotle calls scientific
knowledge, that is, in knowledge that is universal and necessarily true.
This is a serious mistake, as I will explain in a moment.

      This confusion is not totally Devereux’s fault, as Aristotle himself
fell victim of the same confusion. Suzanne Mansion remarked long ago that
Aristotle was the victim of several important confusions concerning the
necessity and the universality of the epistaton or object of scientific
judgment, which is intelligible, and the particularity and contingency of
the doxaston or object of opinion, which is sensible, and from which the
scientific judgment originates.[2]

      The latter is purely intelligible, whereas the former is accessible
to sense and, therefore accessible to at least certain kinds of animals.
[But it is accessible not objectively but applicatively]. The strict,
explanatory universal is the one Aristotle has in mind when he speaks of
scientific knowledge, whereas the general fact, being sensible, cannot
yield necessarily true knowledge but only opinion. Devereux may not have
grasped all the implications of his distinction between the two senses in
which Aristotle uses the word “universal,’ but he is perfectly right
concerning the ambiguity. [Again, however, this ambiguity is irrelevant to
my point].
