CONNOTATION AND DENOTATION

The primary meaning of words are concepts, not real things. To put it otherwise, words are primarily signals for conceiving what is said. If I say “Napoleon” to you, you don’t salute Napoleon or bow before him as you might in his presence; rather, you ask “what about Napoleon” of whom you have conceived a concept, prompted by what I said. By contrast, if a dog’s master is named Napoleon and I pronounce his name before the dog, the dog wags its tail, because the sound Napoleon is for the dog  not a signal to conceive or think of Napoleon but a sign of his imminent presence, much as the smell of Napoleon.

The primary meaning of a word is the concept connoted by it; not the object denoted by it. The perceived smell of Napoleon denotes his presence to the dog. Perception signifies or denotes or refers to the object perceived. A pronounced name signifies or connotes the concept of the object named, it is a signal to think of the object named.

For a name to connote the concept of its object, the presence of the object is not required, for the object can be conceived as well in the presence as in the absence of the object connoted. For a name to denote its object, the presence or imminent presence of its object is required, for denotation—as well as perception –

is a signal for acting, not for conceiving or thinking.

What is the difference between conceiving and perceiving? That the object conceived is an abstract concept, not a concrete object. The triangle of which geometers speak is a notion, a thought, not a particular perceived triangle. It has no color or extension. It is neither here nor there. We can think of it and talk about it whether not it is actually present before our eyes. For it is an object of thought, a concept.

Searle says that words differ from all other signs in that they say what they mean. But there are two ways of meaning or signifying something, to wit: denoting a real object and connoting a concept. Searle does not distinguish explicitly these two ways of signifying linguistically. Implicitly, however, he reduces linguistic meaning to connoting a concept, for he claims that “the characteristic effect of meaning is understanding,” that is, deciphering the meaning of what is said by conceiving it. Now, this applies to the case of linguistic meaning, but not to the case of perceiving. For what is perceived is a signal for acting, not a sign of a meaning to be deciphered and verbally enunciated. Linguistic meaning terminates in verbal interpretation of what is said. That is what the translator of a linguistic text does and is meant to do. But that is not what a perceiver of a perceptual signal does or is meant to do. A certain noise in my house in the middle of the night is not a sign of a meaning to be interpreted; it is a signal for an action to be performed – be it calling the police or attacking the uninvited guest. But of this way of meaning, which is characteristic of what is perceived, Searle has no notion, since he confuses it with linguistic meaning.

Wittgenstein protested against this kind of confusion when he said that “there is a way of grasping a rule which is not an interpretation, but which is exhibited in what we call obeying a rule and going against it in actual cases.”  By that he meant that rules are not meant to be interpreted or deciphered but to be obeyed, and, as far as our case is concerned, that perceptual signs are meant to function as rules for action, not to be read and deciphered as linguistic texts are.

Searle is far from being alone in having fallen into this confusion. Aristotle did, too, when he spoke of the practical syllogism which is supposed to terminate in action rather than in assent, and so did Newton when he conceived  of nature as a book in which his laws were written as to be read and interpreted or understood rather than followed.. Actually, Kuhn saw this Newtonian confusion involved in the misinterpretation of science in what he called “the scientific textbooks”, and he undertook to correct it.

To return to Aristotle’s confusion, his logic is composed of purely intelligible – that is, not perceptual but conceptual propositions. It is supposed to lead from the intellectual assent to two propositions of the mentioned kind to a new assent to the linguistic –that is, conceptual–  meaning of a third proposition. It is based on the analytic notion that two tings that are identical to a third one are identical to each other. If A = B, and B = C, the conclusion that A= C necessarily follows. However, the assent is, in this case, to the conceptual or connotational rather than to the denotational or referential meaning of the propositions. The concluding proposition constitutes what Kant calls an analytic proposition because it suffices to understand what is meant by the premises –without needing any perceptual experience–to know that what is said in the conclusion is true. Analytic truths are purely conceptual because they are about linguistic meanings, not about the world.

Perceptual signals are actionable. They are given to us by actually perceived  objects that we encounter in the course of our acting to achieve a desired purpose . If I want to cross the road in order to reach my destination, and I see a car coming in my direction, the car signals to me (experienced agent) not to cross the road. It is not a sign of a linguistic meaning to be understood, for it is meant to terminate in an action – an action appropriate to satisfy my innate need to survive.

Contrary to what Aristotle thought, science is not after theoretical truths but after ways of solving practical problems, that is, finding the means for the realization of our desires through action. As suspected by Husserl, geometry originated in Egypt as a means to solve such practical problems as finding an equivalent surface of land after the periodical inundations in the delta of the Nile.
