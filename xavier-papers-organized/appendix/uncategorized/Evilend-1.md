CAMUS' MEDITATION ON THE CHRISTIAN FAITH

Curiously for somebody who in his childhood had only a very superficial contact with Christianity and had very early totally distanced himself from it, Camus never ceased to explain his own stance by contrast to that of Christians.  Even more curiously, while always maintaining between himself and the Christian faith an unbridgeable chasm, Camus progressively developed a sympathetic understanding of Christians and their faith.  This understanding was reflected in his novel, The Plague , in which there is to be found what, at least in my opinion, amounts to a fascinating meditation by an atheist on the Christian faith.  I intend to explore that meditation in some depth, after placing it against the background of Camus' initial view of Christianity, without which it cannot make full sense.

The suicidal leap of faith

Before World War II Camus' view of Christians was totally negative. He condensed it in "An Absurd Reasoning," the most substantial of the essays by which the English edition of The Myth of Sisyphus is composed. ^(E1)  

Belief in God, according to Camus at that stage of his philosophical evolution, amounts to a sheer betrayal of reason, for there is no rational path to God.  Thinking and believing, in other words, are mutually exclusive. One can be a thinker or one can be a believer, but one cannot be both at the same time. As to what leads certain thinkers, of whom Kierkegaard is an example, to kill themselves as thinkers and become mere believers, Camus is peremptory: it is their desire to escape from the absurdity of the pointless universe into which we are thrown by the event of birth, the absurdity of which they themselves had a glimpse when they reflected as genuine thinkers.

Certain men, starting from a critique of rationalism, have admitted the absurd climate...

Now to limit myself to existential philosophies, I see that  all of them without exception suggest escape.  Through an odd reasoning, starting out from the absurd over the ruins of reason..., they deify what crushes them and find reason to hope in what impoverishes them. That forced hope is religious in all of them. ^(E2)

Christians, therefore--for Christians in particular are the believers Camus has in mind--are people who commit "philosophical suicide."  Thereby he means "the movement by which a thought negates itself and tends to transcend itself in its very negation." ^(E3)  God is affirmed, but only "through the negation of human reason" ^(E4), through an irrational "leap". ^(E5)  God, in other words, is the Santa Claus adults invent through the leap of faith in order to elude the threatening truth that death is final and life pointless.

Being fair to Christians

Such was Camus' totally negative idea of Christians and their faith around 1940.  A few years later the theme of the Christian faith reappears in The Plague , in a more concrete form, incarnated this time in Father Paneloux--the only developed Christian character in all of Camus' novels and plays. ^(E6)  Father Paneloux, however, is an enigmatic figure, at least at first sight, so that grasping what Camus may have intended to convey through him about Christians is a problem in itself.  Indeed, after a spiritual evolution that clearly involves a crisis of faith, Paneloux dies with a crucifix in his hands but rejecting the help of physicians.  What are we supposed to make of this?  Is Paneloux's crisis of faith on the occasion of a child's death meant as a suggestion of the radical vulnerability of the Christian faith in the presence of suffering and death?  And are Paneloux's inflexible refusal of a doctor's help and his exclusive trust in God meant as a suggestion of the ridiculous irrationality of those who stubbornly cling to the Christian faith?

Such hypotheses are not intrinsically absurd, so they cannot be dismissed without argument.  In addition, it cannot simply be taken for granted, as if it were self-evident, that Camus intended to express in The Plague through Father Paneloux his own views on Christianity.  Precisely because we are dealing with a novel we have no right to identify automatically the authorial voice with Camus' own.  After all, not without reason, Camus himself protested at least once against this facile confusion. ^(E7)  Given these not inconsiderable problems of interpretation, we will be well advised in taking a roundabout way and beginning by paying attention to whatever external clues Camus may have left as to his intentions in this novel.

As is well known, Camus wrote The Plague under the German occupation of France, and he meant it as an anti-Nazi work. Obviously, however, given the circumstances, he had to disguise his intentions not only for the sake of his own safety but to have the hope that his novel would be eventually published and read.  Thus the Nazis appear in the novel under the symbolic form of the rats that unexpectedly bring the plague to a happy city, and those who fight the plague represent the members of the underground Resistance.  That we are not unwarrantedly reading such symbolism into the novel is confirmed by Camus himself, who commented:

The Plague , which I wanted to be read on a number of levels, nevertheless has as its obvious content the struggle of the European resistance movements against the Nazis.  The proof of this is that although the specific enemy is nowhere named, everyone in every European country recognized it.  In a sense, The Plague is more than a chronicle of the Resistance, but certainly it is nothing less. ^(E8)

It is, therefore, a fact beyond dispute that Dr. Rieux and those who join him in his fight against the plague represent the resistants.  Thus in eventually joining that group Paneloux is actually becoming a member of the Resistance.

A second clue. "In my novel," Camus declared, referring to The Plague , "I had the obligation to be fair to my Christian friends, for I had met them during the occupation engaged in a fight that was just." ^(E9)  This text clearly suggests that, as a result of personal acquaintance, Camus felt that his previous totally negative appraisal of Christians had failed to do them justice.  Thus we are entitled to expect that Christians will be presented in The Plague in a rather positive light.  And since Father Paneloux happens to be the only Christian of any consequence in the novel, we may be sure that, however enigmatic this character may be at first sight, it is through him that Camus will be doing justice to Christians.

Finally, a third clue lies buried in a fragment of 1948.  That year Camus was invited by the Dominican Friars of Paris to give a lecture in their convent.  The subject selected was, "What do unbelievers expect from Christians?"  In the course of that lecture Camus said the following:

I want to state that, as I do not possess any absolute truth or any absolute message, I will never affirm that the Christian truth is an illusion.  All I can say is that, as a matter of fact, I have been unable to believe...  I share with you Christians your horror of evil.  But I do not share your hope. And I will go on fighting against this universe in which children suffer and die. ^(E10)

This fragment is revealing in several regards.  In the first place, it shows unequivocally that Camus had travelled a long road in his appraisal of the Christian faith since The Myth of Sisyphus .  Evidently, in that early work Camus had assumed that he was in possession of the truth with a capital T, for he definitely had considered the Christian faith as an illusion.  Now, more humble, he doesn't dare to go that far; all he can say is that he has been unable to share the faith of his Christian friends.  A second interesting point, suggested by Camus' own formulation, is that the objection against the Christian faith  to which he personally thinks there can be no satisfactory answer is the problem of evil.  A universe in which innocent children suffer and die appears to him absolutely incompatible with the faith in the supposedly loving and all-powerful God of Christianity.  Finally, the very words in which Camus expresses his rebellion against a world in which children die echo very closely, as we will see, a statement that Dr. Rieux makes in the novel to characterize the fundamental difference between himself and Father Paneloux.  We will therefore be entitled to assume that on that particular point Rieux definitely speaks for Camus.

Guided by these clues, we may now move with some confidence to the text itself of The Plague .

Paneloux is better than his sermon

The moment in the novel at which Paneloux joins the group around Rieux to fight the plague constitutes a watershed, as far as the presentation of Camus' new idea of Christians is concerned.  Before and after that moment, Paneloux represents two different sorts of Christians.  Those of the first kind, as it will turn out, Camus finds silly in their obtuse self-complacency and close to repulsive in their lack of human sensitivity. Perhaps it was Camus' acquaintance with Christians of this kind--a kind which is by no means unheard of--that had led him to his purely negative appraisal of the Christian faith in his younger years.  The Christians, by contrast, whom Camus met in the Resistance engaged in a fight that was just and to whom he feels the need to be fair are the ones of whom Paneloux will become a prototype later in the novel, after the watershed.  It is, therefore, to this "second" Paneloux that we will have to pay attention in order to figure out Camus' new view of the Christian faith.  However, we will be wise to follow Camus' own strategy and begin by focusing on the "first" Paneloux, for the grasp of the type of Christians the "second" Paneloux represents is partly dependent on the contrast between the two.

Father Paneloux is introduced early in the novel as a bookish intellectual, self-sufficient in his faith, with little genuine human compassion--"a learned and militant jesuit," in Camus' words. ^(E11)  These traits are reflected in Paneloux's first sermon, in which the first type of Christian is epitomized. What does Paneloux have to say, in their horrible distress, to the forlorn people whose city has recently been invaded by the rats that carry the plague?  It comes to this.  My brothers, you have been naughty children; you have sinned, and God is displeased with you. He is, however, a good God.  He has sent you the plague in order to bring you back to your senses.  And, since this is the reason for the plague's eruption, you have only to repent and pray, leaving the rest to God.

Though cruel in its irony, I do not think that Camus would have protested against my summary of Paneloux's first sermon. Indeed, Rieux obviously speaks for Camus when he answers Tarrou's question as to what he thought of that sermon:

I've seen too much of hospitals to relish any idea of collective punishment....

Paneloux is a man of learning, a scholar.  He hasn't come in contact with death; that is why he can speak with such assurance of the truth--with a capital T.  But every country priest who visits his parishioners and has heard a man gasping for breath on his deathbed thinks as I do. He'd try to relieve human suffering before trying to point out its excellence. ^(E12)

One assumes that after his sermon Paneloux followed his own advice and prayed, waiting for God to do the rest.  Whether he himself had anything to repent of depends on whether he truly considered himself a sinner, for, as Camus will point out later, in this first sermon Paneloux didn't include himself among the people to whom he preached.  Be it as it may, the plague, contrary to Paneloux's assurances, continued unabated.  This fact opened Paneloux's eyes, for it made him realize that he knew much less about God's plans than he had presumptuously implied in his sermon and that, at any rate, it was very much the business of Christians to do something about the plague rather than leaving the task entirely to God. Tarrou brings the news to Rieux:

"I've just asked Paneloux to join us."

"Well?" asked the doctor.

"He thought it over, then said yes."

"That's good," the doctor said.  "I'm glad to know he's better than his sermon." ^(E13)

God Himself can't part us now

On one occasion Rieux and some of the people who are fighting the plague with him, Paneloux included, find themselves by the bedside of a young child in a hospital.  Since his case seems hopeless, a new promising serum will be tried on him.  They watch expectantly.

They had already seen children die--for many months now death had shown no favoritism--but they had never yet watched a child's agony minute by minute, as they had now been doing since daybreak.  Needless to say the pain inflicted on these innocent victims had always seemed to them what in fact it was: an abominable thing.  But hitherto they had felt its abomination in, so to speak, an abstract way; they had never had to witness over so long a period the death-throes of an innocent child. ^(E14)

The abominable thing is described by Camus in detail, lest the so-called "problem of evil" be taken by his readers for a philosophical abstraction.  The child's agony goes on for hours, unremittingly.  At a certain moment Paneloux murmurs imploringly, "'My God, spare this child!'.  But the wail continued without cease." ^(E15) It was only many hours later that, at last!,

it was over... His mouth still gaping, but silent now, the child was lying among the tumbled blankets, a small, shrunken form, with tears still wet on his cheeks. ^(E16)

The encounter between Dr. Rieux and Father Paneloux, as both leave the child's room just after his death, constitutes the first moment in the novel at which, in his attempt to be fair to them, Camus articulates through Rieux his new view of Christians.

Horrified by what he had just witnessed, "Rieux felt like shouting imprecations--anything to loosen the stranglehold lashing his heart with steel." ^(E17)  In his fury, he turns against Paneloux: "Ah! That child, anyhow, was innocent, and you know it as well as I do." ^(E18)  Hadn't Paneloux, indeed, in his first sermon, affirmed that the plague was God's way of calling sinners to repentance?  But how can Paneloux's explanation fit the case of this child who didn't even have the opportunity to sin?

Obviously, it can't.  However, the Paneloux with whom Rieux is now talking is not anymore the Paneloux of the first sermon.  The new Paneloux is now fully aware that he cannot in any way account for human suffering and death.  He tells Rieux that he finds the agony and the death of the child as "unbearable" as Rieux does, but instead of attempting to explain this evil to soften its brutality he now openly confesses that "it is revolting because it passes our human understanding." ^(E19)  What, then, is the difference that separates Christians and atheists?  Faith and the lack thereof.  Paneloux calls it "grace."  Because of it, he finds himself capable of trying to "love what he cannot understand."  To which Rieux impetuously replies that he has a very different idea of love, and declares --in the words which Camus, speaking in his own name, was to echo later in his lecture to the Dominicans--that "until my dying day I shall refuse to love a scheme of things in which children are put to torture." ^(E20)

For an explanation of what Paneloux means by implying that Christians love the suffering they cannot understand we will have to wait for Paneloux's second sermon.  However, already at this point Camus makes clear that he does not mean to say that because Christians believe in God they reconcile themselves in a simplistic way with suffering.  On the contrary, what Camus wants to emphasize is that what characterizes genuine Christians is that they do not give in tamely to the plague but fight it. Actually, it is on this very point that Camus will show his new fairness to Christians.  Evidently, he had assumed that, because they believe in an all-

powerful God, Christians, like the Paneloux of the first sermon, are people who pray but leave to God what needs to be done.  Now, however, after having met a number of them engaged in the Resistance, Camus does them justice.  Far from preventing them from caring about the plague, their faith motivates them to fight it with all their strength. In this regard, then--and this new understanding is so important for Camus that he will repeat it twice in a row in roughly equivalent terms--there is no difference between the best of Christians and the best of atheists.  True, on the level of faith there is an abyss between the two, but this does not do away with the very real kinship which unites them.  This is what Rieux forcefully points out at the end of the conversation with Paneloux to which I have been referring:

"[Grace] is something I haven't got; that I know.  But I'd rather not discuss that with you.  We're working side by side for something that unites us--beyond blasphemy and prayers.  And it's the only thing that matters." ^(E21)

And, as if to insure that the reader does not miss such a central point, Camus makes Rieux repeat it in reply to Paneloux's expression of regret that he has been unable to communicate his faith to him:

"What does it matter?  What I hate is death and disease, as you well know.  And whether you wish it or not, we're allies, facing them and fighting them together."  Rieux was still holding Paneloux's hand. "So you see"--but he refrained from meeting the priest's eyes--"God himself can't part us now." ^(E22)

A universe in which children suffer and die

Even if Christians care for what happens in this world as much as those who have no hope of an afterlife, there remains, nevertheless, the serious charge Camus had made against them that they make the leap of faith in order to avoid facing life's absurdity.  Does the accusation of philosophical suicide stand, or does Camus owe fairness to Christians on this point, too?  To make up his mind on such a complex and difficult issue Camus embarks on an exploration of the way in which Christians actually cope with the problem of evil.  This is what Paneloux's second sermon in the novel deals with.

Witnessing hour after hour the horrible agony and death of the child shook Paneloux very deeply.  Till then it seems that, as Rieux had said, the scholarly Paneloux hadn't quite come in contact with death.  That is why, in his first sermon, he had so volubly theorized about the plague being almost a divine gift to bring people to repentance.  What was worse, he had by the same token explained away human suffering and death by conferring on them a purpose that seemed to justify them.  However, the death of the child had put him in the wrong, for an innocent child has nothing to pay for or repent of.  This forced Paneloux to face for the first time in all lucidity the problem of evil and what it implied for the Christian faith in general and for his own personal faith in particular, which was being so severely tested.  The results of this critical reconsideration will be the subject of his second sermon, which is prefaced by Camus by a detailed sketch of Paneloux's spiritual evolution until then.

Since joining Rieux's band of workers Paneloux had spent his entire time in hospitals and places where he came in contact with plague.  He had elected for the place among his fellow workers that he judged incumbent on him--in the forefront of the fight.  And constantly since then he had rubbed shoulders with death.  Though theoretically immunized by periodical inoculations, he was well aware that at any moment death might claim him too, and he had given thought to this.  But from the day on which he saw a child die, something seemed to change in him.  And his face bore traces of the rising tension of his thoughts.  When one day he told Rieux with a smile that he was working on a short essay entitled 'Is a Priest Justified in Consulting a Doctor?' Rieux had gathered that something graver lay behind the question than the priest's tone seemed to imply.  On the doctor's saying he would greatly like to have a look at the essay, Paneloux informed him that he would shortly be preaching at a Mass for men, and his sermon would convey some at least of his considered opinions on the question.

"I hope you will come, Doctor. The subject will interest you." ^(E23)

Camus introduces Paneloux's second sermon with a pointed contrast to his first one:

He spoke in a gentler, more thoughtful tone than on the previous occasion, and several times was noticed to be stumbling over his words.  A yet more noteworthy change was that instead of saying 'you' he now said 'we'. ^(E24)

How Paneloux has changed! In his first sermon he had spoken as if his being a priest placed him above the rest of Christians and opened to him a unique access to God.  He had then explained everything--why the plague had come, and what God would do if people turned to Him--with the utmost assurance, as if he had had breakfast with God that very morning and discussed His intentions with Him.  None of that now.  Paneloux has been humbled and has become humble.  Now he is just one Christian among others, with no ready-made explanations to be eloquently passed down; he is painfully groping for his way through the maze, stumbling over his words.  Unsurprisingly, what he now has to say is very different, too.

Paneloux's first point is absolutely crucial.  Taught by the child's death, he now begins by acknowledging openly that Christians have no explanation for suffering and death:

... there were things we could grasp as touching God,

and others we could not...  The difficulty began when we looked into the nature of evil, and among things evil he

included human suffering.... we see no reason for a

child's suffering.  And, truth to tell, nothing was more

important on earth than a child's suffering. ^(E25)

Paneloux is fully aware of the dire consequences of this admission and confronts himself and his audience with them.  Without the protective cushion of fallacious solutions to the problem of evil, Christians find themselves "with their backs against the wall." ^(E26)  Indeed, as long as they take seriously the explanations given for suffering and death, Christians fail to realize the extent to which the presence of evil in the world renders problematic the existence of the supposedly omnipotent and loving God in whom they believe.  Now, however, for the first time, they are constrained to confront the problem in all its starkness.

An example may help us to appreciate, in a concrete way, the quandary in which Christians find themselves once they admit, as Paneloux honestly does, that they have no explanation for the presence of evil in the world.  There is mention in The Plague of the case of a priest (perhaps Camus met that priest in real life) "who had lost his faith during the war, as the result of seeing a young man's face with both eyes destroyed." ^(E27)   Understandably, this priest found such a terrible tragedy incompatible with the existence of the supposedly good, all-powerful God in whom he had until then believed and, as a result, he lost his faith.

Of course, had this priest had available an explanation that showed that this event was compatible with the existence of the God in whom he believed, he would have had no reason to lose his faith.  It is clear, then, that rational explanations of this type constitute a protective cushion of the faith of Christians. This is why, in fact, those who don't believe in the God of Christians see in the undeniable reality of suffering and death the most powerful argument against the existence of such a God, and why we Christians have desperately attempted through our entire history to convince ourselves and others that we possess a rational solution to the problem of evil.  For the fact is that, without such a solution, our faith is as vulnerable as the faith of the priest of Camus' story.  Such, precisely, is the situation in which, at least according to Paneloux, we Christians find ourselves.  "My brothers, a time of testing has come for us all. We must believe everything or deny everything." ^(E28)

Confronted with the unexplainable tragedy of the blinded young man, the priest to whom Tarrou alludes denied everything. Confronted with the unexplainable suffering and death of the innocent child, Paneloux chooses to believe everything.  

... he, Father Paneloux, would keep faith with that great symbol of all suffering, the tortured body on the Cross; he would stand fast, his back to the wall, and face honestly the terrible problem of a child's agony. ^(E29)

Having refused to lose his faith and having thrown his lot with the Crucified, Paneloux is now forced to draw in all honesty the implications concerning the faith he has opted for. And this is what he will do in the rest of his sermon.

The immediate, unavoidable consequence is that the Christian faith involves consenting to the suffering and death of innocent children, for that happens in God's universe and because of God's permissive will, and Christians consent to God's will.  Instead of softening it, Paneloux stresses this consequence forcefully.

He was saying that the total acceptance of which he had been speaking was not to be taken in the limited sense usually given to the words; he was not thinking of mere resignation or even of that harder virtue, humility.  True, the agony of a child was humiliating to the heart and to the mind.  But that was why we had to come to terms with it.  And that, too, was why--and here Paneloux assured those present that it was not easy to say what he was about to say--since it was God's will, we, too, should will it.  Thus and thus only the Christian could face the problem squarely and, scorning subterfuge, pierce to the heart of the supreme issue, the essential choice.  And his choice would be to believe everything, so as not to be forced into denying everything. ^(E30)

Paneloux, of course, is a fictional character.  The one who is putting words in the mouth of this Christian is Camus, who himself is an atheist.  However, Camus thinks that his  interpretation  of Christians is correct.  Of course, he knows that there are Christians who, like the Paneloux of the first sermon, imagine that they can explain away the problem of evil; but Camus thinks that the truly reflective Christians realize, like Paneloux after the child's death, that they would only be fooling themselves with those "simple devices" to escape the problem. ^(E31)  At the end of the day, Christians are constrained to admit, like Paneloux, that they have no rational solution to the problem of evil. And when, using the Lord's prayer, they say "thy will be done," they set no restrictions to God's mysterious will.  There is no hint that they will not accept the suffering and death of those who are innocent, if God permits it.  Perhaps Christians are not always fully aware of what they mean when they make the Lord's prayer theirs.  But Camus is aware and doesn't think that Christians, at least the genuine ones among them, are playing a comedy.  He takes them at their word, and he assumes that they mean what they say.  In saying "thy will be done" they are extending a blank check to the God in whose universe, mysteriously, suffering and death are permitted by Him to occur, and they are consenting to His will.

Paneloux is aware that those Christians who have never reflected on what they say when they recite the Lord's prayer will be horrified and balk at his conclusion.  However, the Christian faith is not a menu from which one chooses the dishes one likes and dismisses the ones one dislikes.  At any rate, Paneloux will have no recourse to such an escape.

It was wrong to say: " This I understand, but that I cannot accept"; we must go straight to the heart of that which is unacceptable, precisely because it is thus that we are constrained to make our choice. ^(E32)

Paneloux has made his choice and is willing to face this fact.  However, he has one important clarification to make as to the way in which Christians consent to God's will.  It is not a matter of "fatalism," which would imply that Christians say yes in advance to whatever God may will and, as a result, wait passively for it to happen.  That such is not the case Camus is sure, for otherwise he would have met no Christians engaged in the resistance against the Nazis under the excuse that it was God's will.  Furthermore, Camus has already acknowledged in his novel that, as far as fighting the plague (that is, suffering and death) is concerned, genuine Christians and the best of atheists are "allies."  How, then, is the Christians' consent to God's will to be understood?  Paneloux explains:

He made no doubt that the ugly word "fatalism" would be applied to what he had said.  Well, he would not boggle at the word, provided he were allowed to qualify it with the adjective "active". ^(E33)

Christians, therefore, are--however paradoxical this may sound--"active-fatalists."  They consent to God's will, yes, but they are not passive; they fight the plague.  As Paneloux explains,

There was no question of not taking precautions or failing to comply with the orders wisely promulgated for the public weal in the disorders of pestilence.  Nor should we listen to certain moralists who told us to sink on our knees and give up the struggle.  No, we should go forward, groping our way through the darkness, stumbling perhaps at times, and do what good lay in our power.  As for the rest, we must hold fast, trusting in divine goodness, even as to the deaths of little children... ^(E34)

The doctrine of certain Christian sects that prevent their members from availing themselves of the services of physicians and hospitals because that would amount to interfering with God's will is not at all a component of the Christian faith, in Paneloux's eyes.  That is the very "fatalism" he opposes as un-Christian.

Nor should one even think of acting like those Persians who in time of plague threw their infected garments on the Christian sanitary workers and loudly called on Heaven to give the plague to these infidels who were trying to avert a pestilence sent by God... For the[m] a child's agony did not count... ^(E35)

But how is this activism of Christians to be reconciled with the full consent to God's will of which Paneloux spoke earlier in his sermon?  Though Camus does not enter into this explanation, the fact is that, while Christians extend a blank check to God, they never know with certainty God's will in advance.  They interpret and discern God's will according to their best lights.  And according to their best lights suffering and death are evils to be fought as long as, and insofar as, they can be prevented.  As Paneloux had pointed out, "among things evil he included human suffering," and, for him, "nothing was more important on earth than a child's suffering." ^(E36)  Genuine Christians, therefore, do their best to contain what they see as evil; they are not people for whom a child's agony does not count.  That is why Paneloux had concluded that the place incumbent on them--and such was the place he himself had resolutely taken throughout the plague, once he escaped from the muddled thinking of his first sermon--was "in the forefront of the fight." ^(E37)  That is where Camus, to his own surprise, had met his Christian friends; not merely praying and waiting passively for God to do the rest.

Paneloux has now said all he had to say and brings his meditation on the Christian faith and the problem of evil to a close:

My brothers..., the love of God is a hard love.  It demands total self-surrender, disdain of our human personality.  And yet it alone can reconcile us to suffering and the deaths of children, it alone can justify them, since we cannot understand them, and we  can only make God's will ours.  That is the hard lesson I would share with you today.  That is the faith, cruel in men's eyes, and crucial in God's, which we must ever strive to compass. ^(E38)

That is what he meant to say

In all this we seem to have forgotten that Paneloux had informed Rieux that the sermon he was about to preach would convey some at least of his considered views on the question on which he had been writing an essay, to wit: "Is a priest justified in consulting a doctor?" ^(E39)  Curiously, this issue never arose in Paneloux's sermon.  It might be thought, however, that the question was addressed and answered indirectly, for we just heard Paneloux dismiss as un-Christian the position of those who think that availing themselves of the means to cure the plague would amount to interfering with God's will.  As it stands, however, such an answer is unsatisfactory, for at least two reasons.  First, Paneloux's own problem was whether a priest is entitled to use the services of physicians, and in his sermon priests were never even mentioned; there was question only of Christians.  Second, and more important, Paneloux's answer in his sermon is that Christians should avail themselves of the services of physicians, but Camus will tell us that Paneloux died shortly thereafter refusing repeatedly to be attended by doctors. ^(E40)  Thus we cannot simply assimilate the case of priests to the case of common Christians concerning the issue at stake.  On the contrary, Camus himself seems to suggest that we shouldn't assimilate the two cases, for we are told that Paneloux had already finished his essay before his sermon, and that the main idea of it was "That it is illogical for a priest to call in a doctor." ^(E41)

All this is very puzzling.  After all, aren't priests Christians?  Why then, if Christians should avail themselves of the services of doctors, because otherwise they would be mere "fatalists," which is un-Christian,  wouldn't it be un-Christian for priests to refuse the services of doctors, as Paneloux does?  Does Camus want to imply that Paneloux became mentally deranged by the illness that took his life?

The solution to this puzzle is furnished by Camus in a very compact, even cryptic, passage by which the events related to Paneloux's death are prefaced.  Referring to Paneloux's conclusion in his essay that it's illogical for a priest to call in a doctor, Camus writes:

Tarrou, when told by Rieux what Paneloux had said, remarked that he'd known a priest who had lost his faith during the war, as a result of seeing a young man's face with both eyes destroyed.

"Paneloux is right," Tarrou continued. "When an innocent youth can have his eyes destroyed, a Christian should either lose his faith or consent to having his eyes destroyed.  Paneloux declines to lose his faith, and he will go through to the end.   That's what he meant to say."

It may be that this remark of Tarrou's throws some light on the regrettable events which followed, in the course of which the priest's conduct seemed inexplicable to his friends.  The reader will judge for himself. ^(E42)

It is certain, then, that Paneloux did not become mentally deranged, since his disconcerting conclusion that a priest shouldn't avail himself of the services of a doctor has a perfectly rational explanation.  What Paneloux meant is not what the sentence literally means .  In other words, in real life (not as a character in a novel, used by its author to make a point symbolically), Paneloux would have followed his own advice to Christians and would have availed himself of a doctor's services. What, then, did Paneloux mean to say, if not what the sentence means literally?  Something that refers not to priests exclusively but to all Christians: "When an innocent youth can have his eyes destroyed, a Christian should either lose his faith or consent to having his eyes destroyed.  Paneloux declines to lose his faith and he will go through to the end.   That is what he meant to say ."

When the child whose agony Paneloux followed impotently to its bitter end died, that is, when Paneloux couldn't do anything anymore to prevent that horror, then he accepted in faith or 'loved' what he could not construe anymore but as God's actual will. But-- and this is Camus' point --a Christian who consents to the suffering and death of others is also implicitly consenting in advance to his or her own suffering and death, when that becomes Gods' will.  In short, as Camus sees it, the acceptance of God's will by Christians is not a merely edifying gesture. When, instead of losing their faith they accept a young man's loss of sight, they are deadly serious, for they are also consenting in advance to having their own eyes destroyed if ever that happens to be God's will.  That is what Camus meant to say symbolically by having Father Paneloux refuse the doctors' services.

Such is the faith of Christians, which Camus finds it impossible to share.  He shares with them their horror of evil and sees them as his allies in the fight against it, but he does not share their ultimate hope.  Like Rieux, until his dying day Camus would refuse to love a scheme of things in which children are put to torture.

Fair or unfair?

What precedes amounts to a meditation by an atheist on the Christian faith and the problem of evil as Camus came to understand that issue through his personal contact with Christians.  That his intention to be fair to them was sincere is beyond doubt.  However, one is entitled, as a Christian, to ask whether Camus' interpretation is indeed fair in the sense of corresponding to the Christians' own understanding of their faith. After all, the old priest who in Camus' novel commented on Paneloux's second sermon may have been right:

He paid tribute to the preacher's eloquence, but the boldness of thought Paneloux had shown gave him pause.  In his opinion the sermon had displayed more uneasiness than real power, and at Paneloux's age a priest had no business to feel uneasy. ^(E43)       

For my part, I fully agree with Camus' first point, to wit: that we Christians have no satisfactory explanation for the existence of evil in this universe subject to God's loving providence.  When confronted with the real thing--be it the agony and death of a single child or of the innumerable victims of the Holocaust--all our attempts to scale the wall of the problem of evil prove to be "simple devices."  In fact, however well-

intentioned, such attempts end up detracting from the importance of the suffering they are supposed to explain; yet, nothing that detracts from the importance of the suffering and death of real people deserves to be taken seriously.  What is perhaps worse, from the point of view of owning up to our Christian faith, by fostering in us the illusion that we can explain these evils such attempts allow us to elude facing the harsh fact that we Christians actually trust a God who permits these evils while keeping us totally in the dark as to why He permits them.  In short, not only do I agree with Camus's first point, but I think we should be grateful to him for reminding us Christians of the absolute trust which our faith involves and which remains easily ignored as long as we think that the wall can be scaled.

A trust of this kind, though, is so stark that we would do almost anything rather than facing the fact that such is the trust that the Christian faith demands.  Thus, in recent years and in connection with the Holocaust in particular, one of the simplest devices to scale the wall against which the problem of evil puts us seems to have gained some popularity.  It consists in maintaining that God has nothing to do with, and can do no nothing about, what happens in the universe He created.  In this way God is neatly absolved of all involvement with our suffering and death and, what is even more attractive, we Christians are taken off the painful hook of having to acknowledge God's mysterious participation through his permissive will.

Unfortunately, from the point of view of the Christian tradition which sees God as the loving and all-powerful Lord of the universe, a God so involved in the universe He created that not even a hair of our heads falls independently of His will, this is utterly disingenuous.  Actually, if this proposed escape from the problem of evil represented the typically Christian attitude, what would have been the point of the huge apologetic Christian literature across the centuries concerning the problem of evil? Why would we Christians have felt particularly concerned by that problem? And why would those who do not share our faith have consistently raised the problem of evil as perhaps the most powerful objection against us?  To put it more concretely, why would the priest of Camus' example have had a problem of faith at all when he saw the young man's eyes destroyed?  Could he really, in all consistency with the faith in which he had been educated, have told himself that event had no relevance whatsoever to his faith?  For my part, as a Christian, I find his loss of faith on the occasion of that event perfectly intelligible.  After all, if we Christians give thanks to God for the good things that happen in the world, as we do, then we cannot honestly use the simple device of absolving God from having anything to do with the evils that happen in the world.  Better to lose our faith honestly than to keep it dishonestly.  Camus is right: the Christian faith is not a menu.

This brings us to Camus' second point.  If Christians keep their faith and do not fool themselves, they have to confront the fact that their faith involves positive consent--not mere resignation before the unavoidable--to God's will.  And in this blank check they extend to God is included, in the first place, the consent in advance to their own suffering and death.  What can I say but that, as a Christian, I recognize my faith in Camus' analysis?  Like Camus, I do not see what else we Christians could mean when we say "Thy will be done," unless we do not mean at all what we say.  Furthermore, as far as I can see, this is what Jesus' prayer in the garden obviously meant, and this was the quality of his terrified but total consent to his Father's incomprehensible will, just before his own suffering and death.  In short, I do not see how we Christians could grant that God permits the evils that happen in the world of which He is the Lord without drawing with Camus the inference that we do consent to what He permits.

Finally, I cannot but agree with Camus' so perceptive clarification that, if we Christians do consent to the suffering and death that God permits, our Christian consent paradoxically demands, nonetheless, that we do whatever is in our power to prevent those evils, while being willing to accept them when our efforts fail to prevent them.  Indeed, we Christians are not fatalists, but "active fatalists."

In sum, I find Camus' meditation on the Christian faith and the problem of evil not only fair but extraordinarily lucid. In fact, I find it positively enlightening, certainly more enlightening than so many well-meaning but shallow treatments of the problem of evil by Christians who, in their attempt to escape from that awesome problem only manage to hide from themselves and from others what the Christian faith demands and involves.

A doubtful case

Yet, there is at least one point left on which we haven't figured out Camus' position.  Does he still think that believing in God automatically amounts to committing philosophical suicide?

Paneloux acknowledges that the problem of evil, once the hope of simple devices to scale the wall has been given up, puts  Christians with their backs against the wall.  When the priest of Tarrou's story realized that his faith demanded consent to the God who had permitted a young man's eyes to be destroyed, he refused to consent to such a God and lost his faith.  Needless to say, this is the reaction that Camus, who felt so strongly about a universe in which children suffer and die, found understandable.  Rieux most certainly speaks for Camus when he comments:   

After all..., since the order of the world is shaped by death, mightn't it be better for God if we refuse to believe in Him and struggle with all our might against death, without raising our eyes toward the heaven where He sits in silence? ^(E44)

This is to say, however, that for all his willingness to be fair to Christians Camus couldn't possibly understand why certain Christians who confront the problem of evil without subterfuges do not lose their faith.  Now, such precisely was Paneloux's case. Thus, the most Camus could do in his novel was to state without giving any reason for this fact that Paneloux refused to lose his faith and "would keep faith with that great symbol of all suffering, the tortured body on the Cross." ^(E45)

But if Camus couldn't understand why Christians do not lose their faith in the presence of evil, he couldn't anymore say as, he had in The Myth of Sisyphus , that Christians cease thinking and become believers in order not to face the absurdity of the human situation.  Indeed, not only didn't Paneloux use his faith as a screen from the absurdity of a world in which innocent children are tortured but, if anything, his faith compounded for him the depth of the absurdity, for he had to conjugate that fact with his belief in a loving, all-powerful God.  Thus, at the end of his exploration, Camus finds himself caught between the horns of an impossible dilemma.  On the one hand, he cannot accuse the lucid, honest Christians represented by Paneloux of committing philosophical suicide, for they do face up to the absurdity.  On the other hand, trusting a God who permits suffering and death is something Camus cannot understand, and this seems to suggest that after all they keep their faith for no reason and against all reason.  Completely baffled, Camus' final word in The Plague on those Christians of whom Paneloux is the symbol is precisely that he is incapable of really understanding how they can believe. "Against his [Paneloux's] name the index card recorded: 'Doubtful case.'" ^(E46)  This, of course, echoes Camus' own final judgment of Christians, previously quoted: "... I will never affirm that the Christian faith is an illusion. All I can say is that... I have been unable to believe."

Hardly could even the most sympathetic atheist be expected to go any farther in his understanding of Christians than giving them the benefit of the doubt.  That, and nothing less, is what Camus did. Remarkable fairness!
