2005-October 30-Moral theory

                                    CATHOLIC MORAL THEORY

Whatever the case may be concerning the intellectual tradition of the Catholic Church in epistemic matters, which is strongly influenced by Aristotle and

Aquinas, the thinking of the Church in matters of morality is not directly derived from a definite identifiable doctrine or view of the human being and God’s urpose in creating him. Rather it has developed piecemeal across the centuries according to the emerging needs of the Chrsitian community or communities. Thus we kmow through the Acts of the Apostles that moral doctrine was developed in an ad hoc way. Yet the cumulative reflection of Catholic moralists has resulted in a number of theoretical positions which have acquired a sort of authoritative life of their own as practically indisputable, and are reflected in the contemporary positions taken by many members of the Catholic hierarchy on  abortion. I want to consider and dispute a few of the most important ones.  

- Certain actions, of which abortion is a prime example, are intrinsically evil. Meaning; whatever the circumstances and the intention of those performing them,  these actions are morally evil and wrong.

Now, in terms of what are actions as such, independently of the intentions of the performer, deemed to be wrong? In terms of their consequeneces. Indeed, an action that entails no consequences, or whose consequences are good or indifferent, is deemed to be morally acceptable for Catholics. Abortion is unacceptable because in every case, no matter what the circumstances or the intetnion of the person peforming it, produces as its consequence the death of an innocent and defenseless child. This consequence is unavoidable because it is intrinsic to the action. Intrinsic, because it is the consequence by which the action itself of aborting is defined. In other words, this consequence is part and parcel of the nature itself of the action.
