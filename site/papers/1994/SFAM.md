            Did Sartre Ever Cease Being an Existentialist?
      Existentialism is a notoriously vague label. In the  present  context,
though, I will take the term not as the name for the  curiously  titillating
view of the human condition popularized mainly by Sartre and  Camus  in  the
early Forty's (characterized by such tenets as God is dead, life is  utterly
absurd, morality is a matter of arbitrary preferences,  etc.),  but  rather,
as Sartre himself came to understand the term since Search for a Method  on,
as the name for a philosophical  current  that  proclaims  and  defends  the
centrality of the free human agent.
      The subject I have chosen seems to me to be particularly  timely.  For
Sartre's long and intense involvement with marxism and  Communism  (one  has
just  to  remember  his  passionate  cry   in   the   early   Fifty's,   "Un
anticommuniste est un chien") coupled with the recent demise of marxism  and
communism, may suggest that Sartre as a philosopher is as  obsolete  as  the
causes he espoused. Now, exploring  whether  Sartre  ever  ceased  being  an
existentialist  furnishes  us  an  opportunity  to  reexamine   the   actual
relationship  of  Sartre's  philosophy  to  marxism  and  communism  from  a
perspective that is not usually taken, because  it  is  commonly  taken  for
granted that Sartre himself settled this issue forever and in  no  uncertain
terms.
      The basis for this view, it has to be conceded, is rather  persuasive.
The  core  of  Search  for  a  Method  is  a  discussion  of   marxism,   of
existentialism, and of their relationship, with  the  purpose  of  grounding
the  project  which  Sartre  intended  to  carry  out  in  the  Critique  of
Dialectical Reason,  and  Sartre's  own  diagnosis  in  that  work  is  that
existentialism finds itself in an advanced stage in  the  process  of  being
superseded by the only genuinely valid philosophy  for  our  times,  namely,
marxism. It would seem, then, beyond dispute that Sartre ceased  considering
existentialism philosophically viable and became himself a  marxist,  though
never a member of the Communist Party. Is not the case closed?
      The case would, indeed, be closed if we  had  to  take  Sartre  for  a
reliable interpreter of himself and of his own works. It  suffices,  though,
to be even  vaguely  familiar  with  the  history  of  Sartre's  successive,
mutually  incompatible  self-interpretations  to  learn  to  distrust  them.
Actually, our subject happens to be a case in point,  as  indicated  by  the
text of one of Sartre's last and most probing interviews  about  his  entire
career:
      "- Q.: [In Question de méthode] you said that Existentialism was  only
      an enclave of Marxism.
      "- A.: Yes, but that was my mistake.
      "...
      "Q.: If you had to choose between the two labels, that of  Marxist  or
      that of existentialist, which would you prefer?
      "A.: That of existentialist.That is what I just told you."[i]

      There are, then, two incompatible interpretations  by  Sartre  himself
of the relation in which his own philosophy  stands  to  existentialism  and
marxism.  Hence,  the  issue  whether  or  not  he  ever  ceased  being   an
existentialist cannot be settled by mere reference to one or  the  other  at
will, but demands and deserves further study. Let us undertake it.
The  emergence  of  the  issue   in   Sartre's   philosophical   development
Sartre started his philosophical  career  as  an  existentialist.  In  other
words, in his early period of  which  Being  and  Nothingness  is  the  most
significant  representative  work,  he  definitely   was   closer   to   the
Kierkegaard he speaks  of  in  Search  for  a  Method  than  to  Marx.  More
concretely, at the core of Sartre's philosophy during that  period  was  the
human  being--what  he  later  called  "human  subjectivity"--to   whom   he
attributes a uniqueness among, and a superior  importance  to,  all  beings.
This on the basis of a freedom  that  makes  possible  for  humans  to  make
themselves into the ones they will become and,  consequently,  to  bear  and
assume responsibility for their being.
      Now, thanks to his contact with French marxists and  with  Marx's  own
works from around 1945  on--of  which  the  long  article  "Matérialisme  et
révolution" is the first important textual trace[ii]--Sartre went through  a
self-corrective process from which his early conception of the  human  being
emerged profoundly modified. He realized,  for  instance,  that  his  famous
Café waiter could project himself as an engineer  or  as  a  physician,  but
only in dreams, for in real life he was trapped in his role of  Café  waiter
by the particular mode of production of his society.  Sartre  realized  too,
as a second significant instance of the profound change he  underwent,  that
the  inescapably  conflictive  relations  among  individuals  which  he  had
previously  taken  for  an  ontological  structure  of  the  coexistence  of
subjects  were  due,  rather,  to  historical  factors   of   socio-economic
character. And,  as  this  alienation  of  people  from  each  other  was  a
historical, not an ontological, trait, it could in principle be overcome  in
the future under a different socio-economic structure.
      It is undeniable, then, that by the end of the Forty's Sartre was  not
anymore the kind of libertarian existentialist he had been at the  beginning
of the decennium. But had he ceased being an existentialist?  Had  he  fully
become a marxist? These, precisely, are the questions  taken  up  by  Sartre
himself in chapter 1 of Search for a Method.[iii]
Sartre's self-interpretation
      Let us listen to Sartre himself:
      "... since I am to speak of existentialism, let it be understood  that
      I take it to be an 'ideology'. It is a parasitical  system  living  on
      the margin of Knowledge, which at first  it  opposed  but  into  which
      today it seeks to be integrated.[iv]

      "It is Marx with whom we claim kinship..."[v]

      "... a philosophy remains efficacious so long as the praxis which  has
      engendered it, which supports it, and which  is  clarified  by  it  is
      still alive... there is the moment of Descartes  and  Locke,  that  of
      Kant and Hegel, finally that of Marx. These three philosophies become,
      each in its turn, the  humus  of  every  particular  thought  and  the
      horizon of all culture; there is no going beyond the historical moment
      which they express."[vi]
      Hardly could Sartre have been more pointed and explicit. He is not  an
existentialist anymore. He has become a marxist in that he  sees  in  Marx's
position, with which he claims kinship, the only living  philosophy  of  our
time. He now sees existentialism as just a parasitical  view  that  survives
provisionally on the margin of Knowledge with  a  capital  K,  that  is,  of
Marx's interpretation of the world,  and  is  destined  to  be  absorbed  by
marxism.
      But why, if existentialism is a mere ideology that fails  to  engender
the efficacious praxis which characterizes genuine philosophies, has it  not
simply been absorbed by marxism, "into  which,"  Sartre  claims,  "today  it
seeks to be integrated"?[vii] The rest of the essay is devoted to  answering
this question.
      Sartre's explanation is that contemporary marxism, contrary to  Marx's
intentions, has become a sort of dogmatic,  dead  Scholasticism.  "The  open
concepts of Marxism have closed in. They are no longer keys,  interpretative
schemata;  they  are  posited  by  themselves  as   an   already   totalized
knowledge."[viii]  Contemporary  marxists--the  members  of  the   Communist
Party, that is, or at least its  leaders  and  its  guiding  theoreticians--
imagine that on the basis of Marx's teaching they  can  know  the  future  a
priori. They do not  anymore  feel  constrained  to  pay  attention  to  the
concrete  situation  in  order  to  be  able  to  foresee   outcomes.   Such
aprioristic  Marxism,  declares  Sartre,  does  "not  satisfy  our  need  to
understand."[ix] Existentialism, by contrast, affirms the role of the  human
subject as a projective agent, not as a  mere  and  blind  patient,  in  the
development of history. Hence the temporary survival of existentialism as  a
required thorn in  the  flesh  of  contemporary  marxism  until  the  latter
recovers its genuine, original identity.
      "It [existentialism] puts nothing into question except  a  mechanistic
      determinism which is not exactly Marxist and which has been introduced
      from the outside into his total philosophy. Existentialism, too, wants
      to situate man and his class in the  conflicts  which  oppose  him  to
      other classes, starting with... the  relations  of  production...  But
      into this very Knowledge and into the  universality  of  concepts,  it
      wants to  reintroduce  the  unsurpassable  singularity  of  the  human
      adventure.
       "Thus  the  comprehension  of   existence   is   presented   as   the
                            foundation of Marxist anthropology."[x]
The weakness of Sartre's interpretation
      One wonders, of course,  why  "contemporary  Marxism"  has  introduced
from the outside  a  mechanistic  determinism  into  Marx's  philosophy  and
closed in its open concepts. Oddly enough, Search for  a  Method  offers  no
explanation for this mysterious infidelity on the part of Marx's  passionate
followers, anymore  than  it  furnishes  any  evidence  in  support  of  the
significant assumption that contemporary Marxism has betrayed Marx's views.
      In my opinion, Sartre showed himself  more  perceptive,  or  at  least
more complete, in a former article,  "Faux  savants  et  faux  lièvres,"  in
which he explicitly related the fact that  contemporary  Marxism  "does  not
give its due place to human subjectivity"[xi] to  the  fact  that  "Marx  is
ambiguous [about the human being],"[xii] which  led  Sartre  to  the  highly
significant conclusion of the article: "We have got to rethink  marxism,  we
have got to rethink the human being."[xiii] It follows, however, if Marx  is
indeed ambiguous about the human being, that  the  deviation  for  which  in
Search for a Method  Sartre criticizes contemporary Marxism  may  not  be  a
deviation  after  all,  since  Marx's  own  works  do   not   exclude   that
interpretation as possibly reflecting Marx's own meaning.  In  other  words,
if Marx is ambiguous about  the  human  being,  then  far  from  introducing
determinism from the outside contemporary   marxism  simply  dispels  Marx's
own ambiguity in one of the  two  possible  ways  implicitly  authorized  by
Marx's works,  namely,  in  the  naturalistic,  deterministic  way.  As  for
Sartre, he settles down from  Search  for  a  Method  on  to  dispel  Marx's
ambiguity about the human being in  the  direction  he  assumes  to  be  the
genuinely  Marxian  one,  namely  in  a  way  that  makes  room  for  "human
subjectivity" within marxism and preservers "the  unsurpassable  singularity
of the human adventure."
      Indisputably, this is how, for a long time, Sartre himself  understood
the philosophical task he  was  carrying  out,  and  how  his  late  works--
particularly  the  Critique  of  Dialectical  Reason--have  generally   been
understood, namely, as a retrieval and full elaboration  of  the  humanistic
anthropology implicit in Marx's works, which would  render  superfluous  the
parasitic role of existentialism. I want to argue, however,  that  there  is
evidence that in the very process of carrying out that task Sartre  came  to
realize  that,  contrary  to  his  initial   assumption,   the   determinism
characteristic of contemporary marxism  was  not  a  deviation  from  Marx's
thought,  that  Marx's  philosophy  itself  was  irredeemable,   and   that,
consequently,  his  own  (that  is,  Sartre's)  philosophy  as  a  sort   of
existentialist alternative was called to replace marxism as the  efficacious
praxis of the future.
      My interpretation finds its origin on the pointed confession  made  by
Sartre of his own mistake about marxism, about the provisional  function  of
existentialism, and consequently about the character of his own  philosophy,
in the interview to which I referred at the beginning of this paper. Let  me
quote again, but now more completely, what Sartre had to say about  his  own
philosophy in relation to Marx's own.  Reminded  by  the   interviewer  that
"you said that Existentialism  was  only  an  enclave  of  Marxism,"  Sartre
replies:
      "Yes, but that was my mistake. It cannot be an enclave because  of  my
      idea  of  freedom,  and  therefore  it  is  ultimately   a   different
      philosophy. I do not think that ultimately this philosophy is Marxist.
      I cannot ignore Marxism; it is linked  to  it...  But  now  I  do  not
      consider it at all a Marxist philosophy... [It is] a  philosophy  that
      would be on the same level, a  mixture  of  theory  and  practice,  as
      Marxism--a philosophy in which theory serves practice, but which takes
      as its starting point the freedom that seems to me to  be  missing  in
      Marxist thought."[xiv]

      Significantly, the attempt to distinguish between
contemporary marxism as a deviation, on the one hand, and Marx's marxism  as
the genuine philosophy for our times, on  the  other,  has  disappeared.  No
less significantly, all allusion to an  ambiguity  in  Marx's  own  marxism,
which  would  render   intelligible   the   misunderstanding   incurred   by
contemporary Marxism while denying its legitimacy is absent, too. Now  there
is question of one marxism only, namely, the marxism of Marx himself and  of
contemporary marxism. And this unique marxism is rejected by Sartre  because
it has no room for the human agent as  free,  which  is  the  very  starting
point of Sartre's own philosophy. And precisely because  of  the  centrality
of the human being as free  in  his  own  philosophy,  Sartre  considers  it
closer to the existentialism he had criticized in Search for a  Method  than
to the marxism to  which  he  had  declared  his  unqualified,  enthusiastic
fealty.
      There is, however, an obvious objection to the view I just  presented.
As  I  myself   pointed   out   the   cautiousness   with   which   Sartre's
interpretations  of  his  own  work  are  to  be  approached,  I  am  highly
vulnerable to  the  criticism  that  I  am  making  much  of  one  of  those
interpretations--and this one in an  impromptu  interview--over  a  previous
one in a text that has become a classic for  the  intelligence  of  Sartre's
late work and which Sartre himself chose as the guiding introduction to  the
Critique of Dialectical Reason.
      Obviously, more than a short  paper  would  be  needed  to  render  my
interpretation fully persuasive. All I can do here is  sketch  very  briefly
the reasons that, in my  opinion,  led  Sartre  to  believe  initially  that
Marx's own Marxism rendered existentialism  superfluous  by  preserving  and
promoting human freedom and, later, to  foreclose  that  possibility.  These
reasons, I think, are to be found in the character of Marx's own  philosophy
itself.
      In  "Faux  savants  et  faux  lièvres"   Sartre  gives  voice  to  his
considerate conviction that Marx  himself  is  ambiguous  "about  the  human
being.," This view of Marx's philosophy  is  not  epoch-making.  After  all,
long before Sartre, particularly since the publication of the  Economic  and
Philosophical Manuscripts  and  The  German  Ideology,  the  possibility  of
rescuing Marx's philosophy from the  official  deterministic  interpretation
of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union had  already  been  proposed  and
gained credence. Thus, if Sartre added something to  this,  it  was  only  a
clear awareness of  the  ambiguity  of  Marx's  own  texts  as  far  as  his
anthropology  was  concerned,  though  this  awareness  was  accompanied  in
Sartre's case by the purely intuitive conviction that the  logic  itself  of
Marx's  philosophy   required  the  affirmation  of  the  genuiness  of  the
humanistic interpretation over the  deterministic  one.  If  my  reading  of
Sartre's evolution is correct, though,  what  Sartre  subsequently  realized
was that the ambiguity of  Marx's  philosophy  concerning  the  human  being
ultimately had to be  dispelled  in  the  way  in  which  the  "contemporary
Marxism" he had criticized had dispelled it, whereas his own  humanistic  or
existentialist dispelling was ultimately excluded by the internal  logic  of
Marx's philosophy.
Why?
      Here is my reconstruction  of  what  the  increased  familiarity  with
Marx's thought led Sartre to realize. Indisputably,  the  praxis  engendered
by Marx's thought--and meant by  Marx  to  be  engendered--is  revolutionary
praxis,  that  is,  the  overcoming  of  the  capitalist   order   and   its
substitution by the new, communist order. Now,  the  justification  of  this
revolutionary praxis rests itself on two absolute certainties, namely,  that
capitalism  is  by  its  very  nature,  that  is,  necessarily,   bound   to
dissolution, and that it will necessarily be substituted by communism,  that
is, by a mode or production in which  each  receives  according  to  genuine
needs and contributes according to  genuine  abilities.  Do  away  with  the
necessity of  the  former  event  and  you  have  opened  the  door  to  the
possibility of a corrected capitalism,  a  capitalism  with  a  human  face,
which would justify  the  praxis  of  the  social  reformers  so  hated  and
despised by Marx, rather  than  the  revolutionary  activity  he  considered
imperative. Do away with the necessity of the latter  event,  and  you  have
opened the door to the reconsideration of  the  extent  to  which  a  highly
problematic, perhaps even unrealizable future, justifies  the  sacrifice  of
generation after generation of people to bring about that future.  Now,  the
only way to avoid imperiling the  necessity  of  both  of  these  events  is
historical determinism, to which corresponds  a  naturalistic  view  of  the
human being as a mere component of the historical  process  itself.  And  as
there is no doubt as to where Marx stood  concerning  revolutionary  praxis,
without which his entire philosophy would  become  unintelligible  and  lose
its ability to engender any  praxis  whatsoever,  it  cannot  ultimately  be
claimed  that  contemporary  Marxism  introduced  determinism  into   Marx's
philosophy from the outside. But then, as Sartre came to realize  it,  there
really is no logical space for  free  human  agents  in  Marx's  philosophy,
which therefore falls into  the  sort  of  unintelligibility  which  finally
prompted Sartre to take his distances from it.  And,  whether  or  not  Marx
himself  realized  it,  this  very  threat  of  unintelligibility  is   what
constrained him to introduce from the outside into his 'scientific' views  a
conception of human  agency  which  does  not  square  with  his  historical
determinism. Thus Marx is indeed ambiguous about the human being because  he
needed to be ambiguous, for the revolution  could  not  take  place  without
being the project of free agents who needed to be persuaded by Marx  of  the
necessity of their participation in the  historical  process,  and,  at  the
same time, the revolution had to be  historically  predetermined  so  as  to
exclude both the acceptance of reformism and the  unavoidable  despair  that
would result from waiting for a never coming godot.
      In sum, it is my opinion that Sartre owes to Marx, as a number  of  us
do, to have overcome the  silly  individualism  foisted  by  the  praxis  of
capitalism. Thanks to Marx,  Sartre  came  to  the  realization  that  human
beings are, primarily (though not exclusively), subjects of material  needs,
that they reproduce their  lives  not  through  pure  thinking  but  through
working, and not as isolated, self-sufficient individuals but as members  of
a society underpinned by a mode  of  production.  Because  Sartre's  initial
form of existentialism happened to coincide rather  closely  with  the  very
sort of the  individualism  in  which  capitalism  finds  its  philosophical
justification,  Sartre  himself   thought   that,   having   overcome   that
misconception thanks to Marx, he had  become  a  genuine  marxist.  What  he
failed initially to notice is that from his early form of existentialism  he
had also brought with himself a notion of  the  human  being,  not  as  mere
effect, but as initiator of effects, which is ultimately  incompatible  with
Marx's revolutionary praxis. For having failed to realize this  soon  enough
Sartre was, I  believe,  all  the  poorer.  For,  on  the  one  hand,  as  a
theoretician, he obscured his  own  way  and  made  it  more  difficult  for
himself to make his own contribution to its fullest. On the other  hand,  as
an activist, he tended to give too easily  and  indiscriminately  his  loyal
support  to  any  movement  which  appeared  to  be  Marx-inspired,  without
seriously considering its chances of actually  making  ever  more  room  for
ever less alienated  people.  Yet,  for  all  his   human,  all  too  human,
shortcomings, I believe there is in Sartre's thought  a  richness  of  which
his own words may be taken as the veiled promise:
      "Q.: If you had to choose today between two labels, that of Marxist or
      that of existentialist, which would you prefer?
      "A.: That of existentialist. That is what I just told you."
