THE HORROR AND THE CHARM: SARTRE’S DISCOVERY AND ‘INVENTION’ OF

PHENOMENOLOGY

A COMMENTARY ON CHRISTIAN DUPONT’S PAPER

Xavier O. Monasterio

University of Dayton

Dupont’s paper explores the relationship between phenomenology as Husserl invented and practiced it and Sartre’s so-called phenomenology–the extent to which Sartre can legitimately be classified as a phenomenologist.

On the one hand, there are certain undeniable facts, such as, Sartre’s enthusiasm the first time he heard of Husserl’s method when mentioned to him by Raymond Aron, his having spent a year in Germany studying phenomenology, his having wriutten Being and Nothingness as “an essay in phenomenological ontology,” and, in Dupont’s correct estimation, his having contributed “more to stimulate French awareness and interest in phenomenology than more knowledgeable contemporaries such as Georges Gurvitch and Emmanuel Levinas” (p. 12). On the other hand, however, the careful analysis Dupont himself carries out in his paper shows little, if any, allegiance on Sartre’s part to a number of  Husserl’s fundamental theses. Actually, in Dupont’s opinion, Sartre’s “bold attack on Husserl’s theories regarding the transcendental ego, his beguiling description of the psychological ego as a thing in the world, and his provocative assertion that the phenomenological reduction represents a spontaneous irruption of absolute consciousness into mundane life, all stem from a dualistic and strongly polarized theory of consciousness that is essentially Cartesian” (p. 11).

Now, I don’t know whether the fact that before his encounter with Marx’s materialism

Sartre was definitely Cartesian in his mind-body dualism would suffice by itself to disqualify him as a phenomenologist of the Husserlian persuasion, for Husserl himself was profoundly Cartesian, as shown in his Cartesian Meditations . I think however, that Dupont is quite on the right track in trying to exterminate the notion that Sartre was ever a genuine phenomenologist. True, Sartre studied Husserl intensely and saw himself as a phenomenologist of sorts still at the time he wrote Being and Nothingess. However, in the last analysis, or so it seems to me, all Sartre borrowed from Husserl is a certain vocabulary into which, without fully realizing it, he poured entirely new wine.

    

A concrete example of what I am talking about is indirectly suggested by Jeffrey Wilson in his paper. Sartre saw Being and Nothingness as an essay in phenomenological ontology because he truly believed that his ontology was purely descriptive, as it should indeed have been according to the Husserlian prescription. Yet, as Wilson rightly observes, very little would have remained of Being and Nothingness if Sartre had remained true to his descriptive diet, for all his belief that he had observed it.

A few pages after the quotation by Dupont of the famous passage of The Prime of Life in which Simone de Beauvoir described Sartre’s first enthusiastic contact with phenomenology through Raymond Aron’s mediation, she gives a very enlightening characterization of Sartre’s philosophical temperament:

“Sartre had declared that my grasp of philosophical doctrines, including that held by Husserl, was quicker and more precise than his own. The truth was that he tended to interpret them according to his own hypotheses; he found great difficulty in jettisoning his own viewpoint and unreservedly adopting anyone else’s” (p. 178).

This is, exactly, what happened concerning Husserl and his phenomenology. What attracted Sartre to Husserl and his method was Aron’s view that “‘if you are a phenomenologist, you can talk about this cocktail and make philosophy out of it!’ Sartre turned pale. Here was just the thing he had been longing to achieve for years” ( The Prime of Life, p. 112). Now Sartre may have had a point in wanting to bring philosophy closer to cocktails than it had been for its entire history. But if the reason why he was attracted to the austere and dry Husserlian phenomenology with its search after essences was that you can  talk about a cocktail and make philosophy out of it, I think he was attracted for a very wrong reason, for that  was not to be found at all in Husserl’s phenomenology. However, as suggested by Simone de Beauvoir’s reading of Sartre’s philosophical temperament, he was to hear very selectively what Husserl had to say, paying attention only to what could be profitably assimilated to his own views, no matter how transformed or deformed.

Sartre’s understanding of intentionality in the text alluded to by Dupont--an understanding that Sartre straightfacedly attributes to Husserl--is a glaring case in point. According to Sartre, with his notion of intentionality “Husserl has restored horror and charm to things. He has reinstated the world of artists and prophets: terrifying, hostile, dangerous, with havens of grace and love.” Obviously, when Sartre wrote this he hadn’t yet gone beyond his first impression that Husserl’s phenomenology had directly to do with the abundant cocktails he drank.  For my part, however, I can hardly think of anything more at odds with Husserl’s phenomenology, in which even our everyday life enters only as object of curious examination rather than of genuine concern than his having restored horror and charm to things and his having reinstated the terrifying, hostile, dangerous world of artists and prophets, with havens of grace and love.

In summary: Dupont is quite right, in my opinion, in his view that one of Sartre’s greatest contributions was to “reconnect philosophy with literature, with psychology, with ethics and politics.” I only hope Dupont had been more clear that this was not a contribution to phenomenology but only, and by accident , to its reception in France and philosophy generally.

Indeed, not only, as Dupont writes, “it would be an exaggeration... to claim that Sartre ‘invented’ a new species of phenomenological philosophy” (p. 11); it would be simply wrong. For good or bad–for good, in my opinion--Sartre never thought in phenomenological categories.
