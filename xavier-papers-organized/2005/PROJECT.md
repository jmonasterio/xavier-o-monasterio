4) THE MEDIEVALS' OBJECTIONS TO ARISTOTELIANISM

An epistemological rupture.

In "The Eclipse" X analyzes the loss of biblical narrative, which he places in the seventeenth century with Spinoza and Cocceius. An aspect of my project is to show that this loss had taken place in the 12th and 13 centuries with the adoption of Aristotelianism. Till then the Bible had been read not only as a story or even as a book of history but as history tout court, as the history of the Christian reader's past by relation to which the reader situated himself in the present and toward the future,and in which he was himself a participant. The history of the world was, in the Augustinian sense, sacred history. In the 13th century, however, under the pressure of Greek philosophical thought, particularly of Aris's scientific methodology, this reading of the Bible ceased to be, in the intellectual life of the Church. From being read as history the Bible became object of scientific inquiry. This is the epistemological rupture that concerns me.

How and why this epistemological rupture occurred and the consequences it involved is my quarry.

X stops short, not only in time but in breadth, because his purpose is to study biblical hermeneutics. My project is different. I am not primarily interested in the reading of texts, not even in the reading of the text of the Bible. What interests me is the Christian faith. It is my conviction that the epistemological rupture of which I am speaking entailed a wrong self-understanding on the faithful's part and a correlative wrong understanding of the object of their faith. Of such wrong understanding we are still victims, with disastrous consequences. Our self-understanding and our understanding of the world, not only as Christians but simply as people, is at odds with our actual life. Yet we stubbornly continue to pursue the impossible project of situating ourselves as people and as faithful within the wrong paradigm--the philosophical paradigm inherited from the Middle Age--which we take as the standard of truth and validity by which our faith is to be understood and judged and to whose exigencies it cannot live up. This creates for us, as believers, the quandary of the rational legitimacy of our faith. The problem is even larger in that the same philosophical paradigm prevents us from understanding ourselves and our world not only as believers but as human beings.

The point is to recover reflectively the implicit paradigm by which, unavoidably, we live.

Before assimilating the Greek paradigm the Medievals lived innocently in what, following Husserl, we might call "the natural attitude." This is to say that they thought, spoke and acted as people spontaneously do, as we all do in our everyday thinking, speaking and acting when we are not engaged in an intellectual or technical task, that is, as 'specialists' but just as plain adult human beings. The universe in which they lived was three-dimensional and populated by other beings as real as themselves.

They had questions of truth and falsity, but their questions were not philosophical questions, at least not epistemological questions. Descartes'methodical doubt would have been unintelligible to them because it presupposes a form of questioning which was alien to them. And even those few intellectuals who, through their contact with the writings of the Greek and Roman sceptics, could have understood Descartes' project, the project itself would have looked as a purely intellectual game rather than as a serious philosophical undertaking for the simple reason that they found their actual contact with reality indubitable. The reality of God was for them as indubitable as the reality of the computer on which I am typing is for me in my everyday life, that is, in "the natural attitude."

Why do I speak of the natural attitude in connection with the medievals? Because the epistemological rupture that took place with the assimilation of Aris consisted, precisely, in the shift from the natural attitude to the unnatural attitude introduced by the Greek philosophical undertaking. And this is the critical point not only for the loss of the biblical narrative but, no less important, for the loss of the paradigm within which alone can we find our bearings both as believers and even as human beings. My point, therefore, is to explain this precise loss and its consequences.
