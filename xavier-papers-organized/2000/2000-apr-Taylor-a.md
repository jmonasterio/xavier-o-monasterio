- There is no transcendent paradigm prevailing. Perhaps not transcendent
and perhaps not in society at large. But what about superior education? Why
such opposition to Christianity, no to be found in the same measure in (the
American) society at large? Because Christians are not neutral and
objective but committed to certain ‘truths’. And they are not committed on
the basis of evidence but of authority.

- The scientific, Greek paradigm. - Truth as objective representation. -
First knowledge, then commitment and action. - Universalism vs the
individual, the temporal, the contingent, the historical, which are not
objects of knowledge but, at best, of true opinion. Objectivism vs
sentimentalism.

- How to find room for Christian experience within an institution devoted
to knowledge, which in the name of detached objectivity and of the unity of
truth –“Relativism: The central problem for the Church Today” (Ratzinger,
p. 59)--, renders illegitimate or irrelevant any commitment that is not
purely intellectual, exclusively motivated by the natural desire to know
immanent to the subject?

- The Socratic Plato accepted only the universal and necessary as
knowledge; therefore he eliminated the temporal and particular, and reduced
moral error to lack of knowledge. However confused, Aris had at least two
conceptions of reason –theoretical and practical.

- “A striking example of this preshaped agenda is the aspect of moral
theory... I argued.... that a key issue for out times is that of moral
sources, whether for instance, we can maintain the high level of
philanthropy and solidarity we now demand of ourselves without these
degenerating into their opposites: contempt, the need to control. THE ISSUE
HERE IS THE QUALITY OF OUR MORAL MOTIVATION –IN MORE OLD-FASHIONED TERMS,
THE QUALITY OF OUR WILL AND THE NATURE OF THE VISION THAT SUSTAINS IT.
Plato and Aris would have understood what I was talking about... But modern
moral philosophy, particularly in the analytic world, has undergone a
drastic foreshortening. These issues just fall of the agenda. For those
thinking in the wake of utilitarians and Kant, for instance, the principal
moral question is, What ought we to do? (as against what is good to be? or
What should we love?), and the principal task of moral philosophy is to
find the principle or principles from which we can derive what we ought to
do (the greatest happiness, or universalization, or whatever).... But one
wants to protest, don’t you see that it also matters whether people can
actually bring themselves to do the right thing? But then your interlocutor
looks at you blankly and says” of course, but that is not moral philosophy;
how people get actually motivated, that’s in the domain of psychology, or
sociology, or whatever” (Taylor, pp. 119-120).

- “... for Plato, the very definition of justice requires that we identify
a higher and a lower and distinguish our love of one from our love of the
other... Augustine speaks explicitly of two loves. Recognition that there
is a difference in us between higher and lower, straight and crooked, or
loving and self-absorbed desires opens an intellectual space in which
philosophy has a crucial role. I mean philosophy not in any narrow
‘disciplinary’ definition but as the attempt to articulate and define the
deepest and most general features of some subject matter– herE our moral
being.... Now this distinction between higher and lower fell under a deep
cloud of suspicion in the 18 century and was expunged altogether in certain
strands of the exclusive humanism that arises at that time - ... the
revolution consists in a rehabilitation of ordinary, untransformed human
desire and self-love, previously seen as an obstacle to universal justice
and benevolence, which now is cast either as innocent or as a positive
force for good. - ... The innocentizing strategy paints human motivation as
neutral; always a mode of self-love, it can be well or badly and
irrationally or rationally directed. Guided by reason, it leads to justice
and mutual aid [... the positive strategy is to paint original, unspoiled
human motivation as including a bent toward solidarity with all others]...
There is something here, analogous to the move from narrow, irrational
brutish, unenlightened ways of seeing the world to enlightenment and
science. SCIENCE BY ITS VERY NATURE INVOLVES OUR TAKING AN OBJECTIVE AND,
IN THIS SENSE, UNIVERSAL PERSPECTIVE ON THINGS. TO SEE HUMAN LIFE IN THE
VIEW FROM NOWHERE... FROM THE STANDPOINT OF THE IMPARTIAL SPECTATOR IS TO
THINK IN UNIVERSAL AND NO LONGER PAROCHIAL TERMS. BUT THIS RISE IS NOW
CODED AS EXCLUSIVELY IN THE REGISTER OF THE UNDERSTANDING, THE WILL REMAINS
CONSTANT... the issue of higher versus lower... is just off the agenda, and
the very attempt to raise it sounds undescribable quaint and dated... - All
this leads me to believe that the most important thing Christian scholars
should attempt is to change the agenda, open it up.... We have to think of
the ways in which our whole debate with modernity., our friendly and
adversarial exchange, is impeded and stifled by a drastically foreshortened
intellectual agenda and then move to widen it” (Taylor, pp. 122-123).

















Turning around the pot

      Taylor mixes two things that are not identical. One is the Western
culture, which has generated the tradition of human rights, another is the
academic culture. The former may be secular for the most part, and
inhospitable to Christianity, but is not totally dechristianized. Indeed,
many people in the USA and Canada declare themselves Christians. The
latter, however, breathes an atmosphere of unbelief and shapes not only the
answers but the very questions in ways that positively exclude the concerns
of Christians. Taylor declares that the investigation of the reasons why
things are so in the academic world is one of the most important
intellectual tasks of our age. I want to give it a stab.

      The purpose of academia is to acquire and disseminate knowledge. Now,
knowledge
is, by definition, accessible to human reason left to its own devices, and
what characterizes knowledge is that it is reached by way of evidence.
Knowledge takes the concrete form of judgements expressed in statements to
which assent is given because of their evidence, as is the natural wont of
reason. Now the Christian faith is similar to knowledge in that it takes
the concrete form of judgments expressed in statements. However, it differs
from knowledge in that the assent to the statements of faith is given not
because of their evidence but because of God’s authority who reveals those
truths. Furthermore, some of the truths of faith are not accessible to
reason left to its own devices, though some others may be accessible to
reason left to its own devices and can be reached by way of evidence.

      On the basis of what precedes, one can understand the reluctance of
academia to accept theology as an academic subject. Indeed, not only does
this discipline include as knowledge statements which are not reached by
way of evidence but assented to because of divine authority, but, when
there is disagreement between the statements reached by way of evidence and
those reached by way of authority, it attributes truth to the latter and
declares the former false on a basis which is obviously extrinsic to
academia.

      Knowledge is, therefore, not just a matter of content but also of
procedure. Much as the sentence in a judicial trial is not necessarily
just, but is taken as just if the appropriate procedure was observed, so if
a statement is assented to on the basis of authority rather than of
evidence it cannot qualify as knowledge and has no validity whatsoever
within the map of knowledge. Within academia reasson is the only and
supreme authority. Hence the fierce defense of academic freedom from all
authoritative aggressions, be they on the part of the Church and in the
name of God, or on the part of the State or of any other agency.

      Given the above, is there any reason to be surprised by the fact that
the academic agenda shapes not only the answers but also the questions, and
that insofar as it only acknowledges the validity of assent based on
evidence it breathes an atmosphere of unbelief?

      What I have characterized so far is the ‘officially’ accepted myth of
knowledge-truth in academia. And, given that academia is the institution
devoted to knowledge, the way in which knowledge is conceived plays an
important role in the life of its members. That the myth of knowledge by
contrast to faith is alive shows, for instance, in the rationale of the
defense of academic freedom against any interference, and in particular
against the interference of the Church. It shows, too, in the rationale for
the Church’s interference: the truth is one –otherwise relativism would be
in order, when it is in fact the greatest danger of the Church in the
present– and revealed truth because of its certainty has precedence over
human truths (see the recent document    ).

      That, on the other hand, the myth is no longer fully operative and is
breaking down shows, for instance, in the open acceptance of the feminist
agenda in the curriculum (which is inconsistent with the objectivity and
the unity of knowledge), in the attacks to the literary cannon as sexist
and whitist and heterosexualist in the departments of English, in the
acceptance oand in the unofficial but clearly rampant epistemic relativism
everywhere but in the most conservative departments of physicis and
chemistry.
