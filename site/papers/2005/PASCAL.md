BLAISE PASCAL

"Ainsi, si l'on avance ce discours: 'Le temps est le mouvement d'une chose créée', il faut demander ce qu'on entend par ce mot de temps, c.a.d. si on lui laisse le sens ordinaire et reçu de tous, ou si on l'en dépouille pour lui donner en cette occasion celui de mouvement d'une chose créée. Que si on le destitue de tout autre sens, on ne peut contredire, et ce sera une definition libre, ensuite de laquelle, comme j'ai dit, il y aura deux choses qui auront ce même nom. Mais si on lui laisse son sens ordinaire, et qu'on prétende néanmoins que ce qu'on entend par ce mot soit le mouvement d'une chose créée, on peut contredire. Ce n'est pas une définition libre, c'est une proposition qu'il faut prouver, si ce n'est qu'elle soit très évidente d'elle-même; et alors ce sera un principe et un axiome, mais jamais une définition, parce que dans cette énonciation on n'entend pas que le mot de temps signifie la me chose que ceux-ci: le mouvment d'une chose créée; mais on entend que ce que l'on conçoit par le terme de temps soit ce mouvement supposé.

"Pascal resists the parceling out of God into separate functions the discharge of which is required for nature and society to be what they are. So he writes: 'The God of Christians is not a God who is merely the author of mathematical truths and the order of the elements; that is the point of view of heathens and Epicureans' ( Pascal's Pensées , tr. "Dans les preuves que mon frère devait donner de Dieu et de la religion xtienne, il ne voulait rien dire qui ne fût à la portée de tous ceux pour qui elles etaient destinées, et où l'homme ne se trouvât intéressé de prendre part , ou en sentant en lui-même toutes les choses qu'on lui faisait remarquer bonnes ou mauvaises, ou en voyant clairement qu'il ne pouvait prendre un meilleur parti, ni plus raisonnable, que de croire qu'il y a un Dieu dont nous pouvons jouir..." ( Ibid ., p. CCXLV).

The grudges of Pascal against the philosophical proofs for the existence of God and against the God that such proofs would yield if they were successful are multiple and diverse, so multiple and diverse that one suspects that Pascal's own position in this regard is not firm.  Does he consider the proofs invalid, or just too complex to be of use to ordinary people or even to intellectuals, or too abstract and intellectual to be satisfactory (remember Saint Anselm's lamentation after his own ontological proof!), or merely futile because people do not believe or cease to believe in God due to the proofs of philosophers?  ( See my Pascal, p. II, 20; and my notes on Brunschvicg: Fils de Madame Périer, Madame Périer, et Filleau dde la Chaois ). We shall never know for sure. One reason for this, I suspect, is that Pascal himself was not sure, and that his scattered reflections on the traditional proofs are not necessarily consistent with each other. He seems to be taking stabs at the traditional proofs, without knowing whether his stabs are mortal or not, or even whether each of the stabs actually attains its intended victim. One thing, however, seems clear. Pascal is, to put it mildly, unsympathetic to the traditional--he calls them "metaphysical"--proofs, and personally has no use for them, since he himself does not use them. His apology for the Christian religion follows a different route, not the route of the traditional proofs. What that route is we shall later explore. For the moment, I want to concentrate on the following questions: Can we, partly on the basis of what Pascal thinks of the traditional proofs, make more sense than Pascal did of his lack of sympathy for them, and show that he was right in rejecting them?

God is not a concept: The traditional proofs

One of Pascal's grudges against the traditional proofs for God's existence is that they do not make God's presence felt (See Lafuma III, 90-91, quoted above). At best, if at all, they yield a purely conceptual (not even concrete notional) God, incapable of motivating us to love and adore and fear Him, a God with whom a personal, religious relationship is as impossible as a personal relationship with the triangle of Euclidean geometry. Why? My answer (not necessarily Pascal's; see second part of De l'esprit géometrique ) is: because the traditional proofs appeal to a pure intellect, aim at mere assent and, correlatively, speak of a God who is object of mere assent, a God-idea that corresponds to pure minds confronted with a spectacle or even with a datum, not to vulnerable agents exposed to the surrounding world. In Pascal's language, the people who offer the traditional proofs think with their reason, not with their heart, and appeal to people's reason, not to their hearts. Their purpose is to communicate their understanding of a datum by giving an explanation of it to be understood and assented to by the recipient, not to bring the recipient to respond appropriately to the God whom they adore . Let me spell this out in more detail.

The primitive man who falls on his knees before the god Sun is in the effective presence of his god, exposed to his action as sufferer of the effects of this action. I mean that this man is the beneficiary of the sun's bounty, the victim of its absence, fully dependent on his god. The sense of owe which the primitive man experiences before the Sun comes from his experience of being at the overwhelming, vital mercy of the Sun, in need of him and without any power on him. Indeed, the exposure, in this case, is not in any way reciprocal. There is nothing the primitive can do to his god, whereas the Sun has on him power of life and death.

Something more akin to the god-Sun in relation to the  primitive man than to the God of the traditional proofs in relation to the one who offer the proofs or to the one to whom the proofs are offered is, I think, what Pascal was obscurely alluding to under the name of the God of Abraham and Isaac when he contrasted the God of philosophers with the God of Abraham and of Jesus-Christ. Not, of course, that Pascal would have equated the God of Abraham with the Sun, but he would have underscored that both are for their respective faithful object of adoration, not object of mere assent as the god of the proofs is. *

* And yet, confused on this point, in the second part of De l'esprit géometrique , Pascal himself states that ideally all persuasion should appeal to the intellect alone, not to feeling, and confuses the effect of divine revelation with persuasion--that is, with demonstration--rather than identifying it with knowing practically .

There is, indeed, a striking contrast between this god-Sun and the God of the traditional proofs as well as between the way this god and the god of proofs come to be known. As Pascal suggests at the beginning of De l'esprit géometrique , one should not identify knowing and coming to know with persuading and coming to persuade. * According to Pascal, one does not come to know by demonstration (by what Aquinas after Aristotle calls syllogismus faciens scientiam ), for in that case the method to discover the truth would be the same as the method to demonstrate the truth, and Pascal says that he will not deal with the former. The one who demonstrates already knows applicatively (in a 'tacit' way), if not in geometry (and perhaps even there) then at least in practical knowledge. Demonstration isolates the propositional content of practical, applied knowledge, which coincides with its abstract object, and this is what it demonstrates to a pure intelligence.

* "On peut avoir trois principaux objets dans l'étude de la vérité: l'un, de la découvrir quand on la cherche ; l'autre de la démontrer quand on la possède ; le dernier de la discerner d'avec le faux quand on l'examine. - Je ne parle point du premier: je traite particulièrement du second, et il enferme le troisième" (Pléiade, Gallimard 1964, p. 575).

To move now to the god of philosophers, let us consider the God of Aquinas' proofs in the Summa Theologica . Contrasting precisely Aquinas and Pascal, Steinman remarks that there is no emotion involved in Aquinas' proofs of God's existence. Indeed, there is none--not in the demonstrator, not in the process of demonstrating, and not in the result of the process (in the effect on the person to whom the demonstration is addressed). * By contrast, there is much emotion--and appeal to the reader's emotion--in Pascal Pensées , and the intended effect cannot be achieved without emotion in the persuader that provokes emotion in the persuadendus-a . If instead of Aquinas, who at the end of each of his proofs concludes "And this is what we call God," the one who made this remark were a computer addressing another computer, we would not be surprised. After all, having no needs and desires and not being exposed, computers cannot be emotional.

* The demonstrator, as Aquinas says in the Summa Theologica concerning the modus argumentandi appropriate to scientific

theology (notice that theology is argumentativa-demonstrativa , that is, about proving, not about knowing or coming to know), is ARGUMENTATIVUS (?) . And in his commentary to Aris' De Interpretatione Aquinas explicitly distinguishes this modus from all other modi , in particular the modi employed in the Bible (See Summa Theologica ), and declares it the only legitimate one in scientific theology.

It is important to notice that when Aquinas is engaged in proving God's existence he is not acting, he is, reflecting. He has put the world in abeyance, not in order to decide, but in order to understand the object in itself. In short, he is thinking scientifically, that is, theoretically: he is thinking in a way structured by a question for mere understanding which will lead to a proposition for mere assent . Consequently, the contingent beings to which Aquinas refers in his argument are not the sky, the hills, the trees, the sun that he sees through the window of his cell and with which he interacts in his daily life and which carry a coefficient of favorableness or adversity. His arguments do not refer to the scorching sun from which Aquinas has to look for protection under the trees, nor to the hills through whose narrow paths he has to make his way to the valley on the other side. The distances in his world are not distances of exposure but, if at all, of contemplation. Because Aquinas is thinking scientifically, that is, theoretically about the world rather than interacting with it, the world on which he reflects and which serves as the supposedly experiential basis for his arguments for God's existence is just a datum, an abstract given to be thought about, much as the triangle drawn by the geometrician on a piece of paper is not a concrete triangle but just the abstract basis for the geometrician's deductions. If instead of seeing from his window a fertile valley Saint Thomas saw a desolate desert (both of which terms express the agent's emotion), his argument from contingency would not change a bit, for the world on which his argument is based is not the concrete world in which he and we live; it is an abstract, uninhabitable datum. If there were no water in the contingent universe of which Aquinas speaks in his argument on contingency it would not matter a bit, as it would not matter a bit if the triangle the geometrician uses as the basis for his/her thinking were ten times bigger or were drawn in a different color. How distant from Saint Thomas are the hills in the contingent world to which he refers? It does not matter, for the distance, if there is any, is not a distance to be travelled. Or, rather, no distances unfold from the demonstrator to the world. How hot is the day in which Thomas writes? It does not matter, assuming that Aquinas' universe coud have a temperature, for the world to which Aquinas' argument refers is not the world through which he has to sweat his way to move from one place to another. In short, the objects to which Aquinas' arguments for God's existence refer are objects as they would be for a pure, bodyless "thinking substance" situated nowhere, to use Descartes' expression, in relation to which objects are neither close nor far away, neither pleasant nor unpleasant, neither attractive nor dangerous--just data to be understood.

If in saying "And this is what we call God" at the end of each of his proofs Aquinas had meant " we philosophers " or " we philosophical, that is, scientific, theologians ," he would have been right. Pascal would argue, though, that, precisely because of this, the proofs (both the metaphysical and the 'from nature'?) do not refer to, or deal with, the God to whom the faithful talk and are exposed. * Similarly, the primitive man would not recognize his god-Sun, who is concrete and to whose action he is exposed, in Aquinas' Necessary Being whom "we call God." And Pascal is on the side of the primitive man who would not call god this Necessary Being, for Pascal himself does not recognize his own God, the God of Abraham, the God of Jesus-Christ, in Aquinas's God.

* Remember what he says in De l'esprit géometrique about the change of names when a word is defined which needs or can have no clearer definition than what people refer to under that name is defined and, therefore, ceases to mean when it means when undefined and begins unwarrantedly and without proof to refer to something else.

In contrast to Aquinas' way let us consider how the primitive mans comes to know (not to demonstrate to himself or to others) his god. Unlike in the case of Aquinas' God, his Sun-god is not the result of an inference. The primitive man does not first attain his god through thinking, through theologizing-scientifically or otherwise, and then adores him; he finds himself exposed to his god just by being a member of his Sun-adoring culture. Rather than first knowing his god (in the sense of naming his god with a name in the nominative ) and then adoring or praying him, he first prays (calls his god in the vocative) and adores him, knows his god practically (in the sense of responding to him--proportionate actus humanus--and calling him in the vocative) , and only later, reflectively, gives to the one he prays and adores the name of god in the nominative. * The inverse is the case with the god of the proofs (assuming that such a god could be prayed and adored, which Pascal rightly denies, at least concerning religious adoration).

* Even the name in the nominative is not used scientifically by the Christian, for it does not refer to a subject of theoretical predicates but to a character in a story in which the believer participates.

Before the primitive man raises the question of his god's existence, the question has already been answered by his culture, and thus the question does not arise. Insofar as there is for the primitive man an answer to that question, the answer did not come to him through a process of thinking, for there is no inference from the primitive man's experience of the sun to the sun being god. By contrast, Aquinas does use his grasp of the unintelligibility of contingent realities without a necessary reality as a logical support for the inference that there has to be that which we call God, which becomes the definition of the name in the nominative. And, indeed, that is how demonstration has to proceed, which differs from ordinary coming to know and, at any rate, from the coming to know of the god-Sun by the primitive man.

Closely related to the difference just considered concerning the way in which the god-Sun comes to be known in the nominative as the subject of predicates is the fact that Aquinas' recognition of his God comes primarily in the form of assent to a proposition, namely, that there is a Necessary Being, whereas the recognition by the primitive man of his god-Sun takes primarily the practical shape of a religious relationship with It, namely, prayer, ritual, adoration, that is, of a conduct towards the Sun. Now, assent, as explicitly acknowledged by Aquinas, is an intellectual act, in the case of the proofs prompted unavoidably by the purely intellectual grasp of the connection established by the middle term between the existence of a contingent and the existence of a necessary being. Indeed, as suggested above, scientific theology is a matter of theoretical understanding.
