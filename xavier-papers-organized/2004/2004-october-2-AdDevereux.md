“ Perception is influenced by motor activity from the outset, just as the latter is by the former. This is what we, for our part, have asserted when speaking of sensori-motor schemata in order to describe the simultaneously perceptual and motor assimilation which characterises the behavior of the infant.” (Piaget, Psychology of Intelligence, (Littlefield, Adams, and Company, Totowa, N.J., 1966), p. 87.

“... as though intelligence were an extension of those mechanisms which in their automatic form appear as habit . In connection with habit, we once again find the genetic schemata of association, of trial and error, and of assimilatory structuring .” ( Ibid. , p. 88).

“The formation of habit always involves a fundamental means-end relation; an action is never a succession of a mechanically associated movements, but is directed towards a satisfaction, such as contact with food or release, e.g. Limena, when placed upside down return more an more rapidly to their normal position” (p. 89).

“The more we analyse the formation of habit as opposed to the automatic exercising of it , the more we realise the complexity of t he activities that come into play at the outset” (p. 89).

“...just as intelligent insight only slightly modifies a perception (knowledge has little influence on an illusion) ...so intelligence only slightly modifies an acquired habit, and, above all, the formation of a habit is not immediately followed by the development of intelligence. There is actually an appreciable break in the genetic series between the appearance of the two types of structures. Piéron’s sea-anemones, which close up at low tide and thus store the water they need, are not evidence for a really mobile intelligence and, in particular, they retain t heir habit in the aquarium for several days before it is extinguished. Goldsmith’s gobii learn to pass through a hole in a sheet glass to reach food and keep to t he same route after the glass is removed: we may name this behavior subcortical intelligence, but it is still considerably inferior to what is ordinarily called intelligence without qualification” (p.90)

“But if the existence of conditioned behaviour is a fact..., its interpretation does not imply the reflexological associationism with which it is too often identified. When a response is associated with a perception there is more in t his connection than a passive association (i.e. becoming stamped in as a result of repetition alone) meanings also enter into it, since association occurs only in the presence of need and its satisfaction ... a conditioned reflex is stabilised only as long as  it is confirmed or reinforced; a signal associated with food does not give rise to an enduring reaction if real food is not periodically presented together with the signal. Association thus comes to be part of a complex piece of behaviour, which starts from a need an finishes with the satisfaction (actual, anticipated, or make-believe, etc). This amounts to saying that it is not a case of association in the classical sense of the term, but rather a of the constitution of a complex schema bound with a meaning ” (p. 81).

“Thus ... the conditioned  response is not the simple substitution of responses originally due to a simple reflex, but new behavior which arrives at stability only by a structuring of the whole environment ” (92).

“Adaptation to reality means an assimilation of real;uity to the subject’s schemata... In its finished state, a mental experiment is the reproduction in thought, not of reality, but of actions or operations which affect it ” (p. 93). [THE SAME APPLIES TO “PROJECT”].

“Claparede thus goes so far as to interpret the conditioned reflex in terms of implication. Pavlov’s dog salivates at the sound of a bell after having heard it at the same time as he saw food, because then the bell ‘implies’ food. This gradual reversal of the trial and error theory [from blind activity] is worth careful examination” (p. 96).

“ ... does a black cat, seen by an infant, involve immediately from its first perception the relation ‘cat implies black’?... This is not a necessary kind; it is the beginning of a possible schema which, however, will only engender relations felt to be necessary as long as they form a genuine schema through transposition or generalisation (i.e. an application to new elements), in short, by introducing an assimilation. The assimilation, then, is the source of what Claparede calls implication. .. The subject will not arrive at the relation ‘A implies x’ on first perceiving an A with quality x, but will be led to the relation ‘A implies x’ inasmuch as he assimilates A2 to the schema (A), this schema being created precisely by the assimilation A2 = A. The dog that salivates at the sign of food will not salivate in this way at the sound of a bell unless he assimilates it, as a sign or a part of the total act, to the schema of this action. Claparede has good reason to say that repetition does not engender implication but that it appears only in the course of repetition, since implication is the internal product of the assimilation that ensures the repetition of the external act. Now this necessary intervention of assimilation further supports the reservation that Claparede was himself induced to make with regard to the general role of trial and error. Firstly, it is obvious that when trial and error occurs, it cannot be explained in mechanical terms. Mechanically, that is to say with t he hypothesis of simple traces, error should be reproduced as often as successful trials. If such is not the case, if the law of  effect ‘ holds, it means that the subject anticipates his failures and successes. In other words, each trial operates on the next, not as a channel opening to new responses, but as a schema enabling meanings to be attributed to subsequent trials. So trial and error in no way excludes assimilation. But this is not all. The very first trials are difficult to reduce to simple chance. In maze experiments, Adams finds responses directed from the outset. Dennis and Dashiel lay stress on the continuation of the sets initially adopted. Tolman and Krechevsky even speak of ‘hypotheses’ in describing the behavior of rats... As for Tolman, he brings out the role of generalisation in the formation of habits themselves. Thus, when an animal is placed on a new maze different form the one known to it, it perceives general analogies and applies to the new case the behavior that met with success in the previous  case (particular routes). So there is always complex structuring, but, for Tolman, the structures concerned are not simple configurations in the sense of Kohler’s theory; they are sign-gestalts ,, i.e. schemata provided with meanings... Thus from elementary learning to intelligence, there seems to be involved an assimilatory activity which is necessary to the structuring of the most passive forms of habits (conditioned responses and associative transfers) as it is to t he unfolding of the visible manifestations of activity (directed trial and error... Just as perceptual activity is not identical with intelligence, but links up with it a soon as it is freed from centering on the immediate and present object, so the assimilatory activity   that engenders habits is not the  same as intelligence but leads to the later as soon as irreversible and isolated sensori-motor systems are differentiated and coordinated in mobile articulations. Besides this, the affinity between these two kinds of activity is obvious, since perceptions and habitual responses are constantly united in complex schemata, and since the ‘transfer’ or generalisation characteristic of habit is the exact equivalent, on the motor side, of ‘transposition’ in the domain of spatial figures, both involving the same generalized assimilation” (pp. 97-99).

I want to explore Devereux’s and Aristotle’s mistake in considering as “practical

sciences or arts,” medicine, ethics, politics, poetics, and rhetoric when both of them are referring  not to the actual practices of agents but to the written treatises or textbooks on those subjects, all of which are the result of reflection guided by the desire to “know,” to ‘understand,” to “explain.” In fact, however,  the study of the corresponding treatises does not result in action, which is what makes different the theoretical from the practical sciences. Aristotle confuses the statements he makes about ethical matters in his Ethics , when he reflects, with the practical judgments of the agent in his ethical practice. In the agent’s case, these ‘judgments’ are practical for they terminate in actions, not in statements. My purpose is to investigate the origin of this confusion.

Mygut-hypothesis is that Aristotle confuses knowledge with the content of statements and believes that statements, when true, correspond to what is the case. The De Interpretatione seems to confirm both points. Indeed, he focuses exclusively on enunciative language because it is the only linguistic form capable of being true or false, since it claims to express what is the case in reality;  hence a statement is true and therefore, constitutes genuine knowledge, when it corresponds to reality (much as Searle’s conception of enunciative saying and its truth). In addition, Aristotle’s fixation with “scientific” knowledge, which is possible only of what is intelligible by contrast to what is perceptible, leads him to conceive the real as constituted by intelligible substances whose action is accidental.

That Aristotle considered his Ethics a practical science in the sense of resulting in action is certain, for he himself declared that explicitly when he said that the aim of his treatise was not to obtain the assent of his readers to his knowledge of what virtue is , (knowledge of virtue as an entity), but their virtuous action. ^(F1)  For his part, Devereux himself aptly distinguishes between the practitioner of medicine, who learned his trade through practice, through actual attempts to cure,  from the medical student who learned it from textbooks (composed of statements) that are analogous  to Aristotle’s treatise on ethics. The latter’s knowledge his purely theoretical (knowledge of what the thing is, knowledge of ‘entity’), for he has had no practice of medicine, whereas the former’s knowledge is gained through experience in the sense of  practice. But what does this mean, concretely?

Let us consider the case of the person who has learned to react to the syllables that come accompanied by an electric shock, but is incapable of telling which are those syllables. The instrumental knowledge he has acquired allows him to avoid those syllables, and consequently the shock. This is practical knowledge, in that it results in the action appropriate to achieve the desired goal, and this not by accident or chance but systematically. Compare this to the knowledge possessed by those who conduct the experiment. They can identify verbally the shocking syllables. Suppose, however, that these people, like totally inexperienced children,  have never experienced an electric shock and have no idea that electric shocks are painful. Will their verbal knowledge result in the action of avoiding the shocking syllables, or in any action at all? Of course not. Their knowledge is purely theoretical, that is, without any direct connection with action, with practical conduct. Moreover, one can wonder whether their verbal statements actually constitute any knowledge whatever, since they themselves do not understand what they mean when the say that the syllables produce shocks. In other words, their supposed knowledge of the shocking syllables does not refer them to anything, since they themselves have no idea of what they are referring or being referred to by the words. If they know syllables, they do not know them as shock-producing, which is what their language portends. They themselves do not understand what they say.

Let me backtrack. In de De Motu Animalium Aristotle raises the question why sometimes knowledge leads to act ion –that is, is practical– and why sometimes not. His answer, according to Nussbaum, is that sometimes the knowledge is informed by desire, and sometimes not. But this is too vague. Desire of what? And  why only sometimes rather than always? And how does this relate to the difference between the tacit or not verbalized knowledge of the people exposed to the syllables and the verbalized knowledge of the designers of the experiment?

As a first approximation, what is the role played in knowledge by language –that is, by verbalization in statements? It seems that in practical knowledge its role is not essential, since animals do not possess language and yet they are capable of practical knowledge. Indeed, animals would react to the syllables in a similar way as those who recognize the shocking syllables without being able to identify them linguistically. It seems, then, that practical knowledge is tacit, non-linguistic; it terminates in action. Theoretical knowledge, by contrast, is essentially linguistic, for it terminates in statements .

But is this answer sufficient? Are not all the knowing activities essentially linguistic? Is not Aristotle right in saying that only judgments and statements are susceptible of truth and falsity, for only they refer to something as being or not being the case? Let us be precise. For something to refer to what is the case, what is the case has to be explicitly formulated. Now, a desire is intentional but not cognitive-objective, for it refers the one who is desirous (but not others, unless it is expressed verbally to them –and not being desirous themselves, they are not referred the way the desirous agent is) to what is desired, but not as being or not being the case, but as desirable. As observed by Nussbaum, a desire can function as desire without being expressed linguistically (a baby desires milk), but a judgment cannot unless it is expressed verbally either internally or overtly. Animals have desires, but they neither do nor can nor need to express them linguistically in order for their desires to function as desires, for desires do not function as referring to what is the case but as informing the conduct of the desirous agent for him to achieve the desired goal.

To see this point more clearly, let us consider the case of animals or of babies, both of which lack language. A newborn baby wants milk. That is, it is born with an active disposition to drink milk. Psychologically, this disposition takes the form of the baby’s feeling of hunger. But the baby doesn’t know that it desires milk, of which it has no previous experience, and doesn’t need to know that enunciatively in order to desire milk. Thus desiring milk and knowing enunciatively that one desires milk are two different things. Ideally the baby’s hunger should result in the action of procuring milk. But that is impossible for the baby. It will eventually get milk with somebody else’s help. Now, as the baby grows in experience of the surrounding world and of itself because it has had its hunger satisfied by sucking the mother’s breast, it learns to actively procure the breast (in order to satisfy its hunger). But this learned or acquired activity is a purely bodily disposition genetically programmed, a disposition that the baby’s body acquires without, so to say, in any way passing through the knowing mind or objective consciousness of the baby. In other words, instinctively guided by its past experience of hunger, of procuring the breast, and of being satisfied, the baby will look for the breast whenever hungry, without in any way knowing-that looking for the breast and sucking it are means to achieve the goal of satisfying its hunger. At least at this early stage, then, practical or instrumental knowledge is tacit and totally different and independent from linguistic or enunciative knowledge. ^(F2)

If so, however, why call these two different things by the same name, ‘knowledge,’ which originally means correspondence between what is enunciatively judged by the knower and what is the case – none of which applies to practical knowledge? This, I think, is at the origin of the confusion that has plagued Western philosophy, compounded by the primacy attributed to theoretical knowledge and the subordination of practical to theoretical knowledge, as well as by the Greek idea of scientific or necessarily true knowledge –all of which should have been destroyed by Kant’s critique, if he himself had been logical instead of sparing scientific knowledge under the pretext of its supposed intuitive character–, and by Aristotle’s invention of formal cause-nature-substance and accidents, existence and essence.

In his Ethics, which supposedly is a practical discipline (composed of statements)  bound to terminate in action, Aristotle confuses the way in which desire functions in a desirous agent who is pursuing a goal with the objective statements that refer predicatively or objectively to the desire or the conduct of the desirous agent as entities out there. But the objective statement of or about a desire is not a desire and doesn’t function as a desire anymore than, as Sartre observes, the eye-entity seen by the philosopher is, and can be identified with, the agent’s seeing eye, or anyu more than the body spoken about by the physician – the body “entity”–  can be identified with the agent’s body, the body I am, “my body for me.” A desire actually functions as a desire when, under the appropriate circumstances, it guides or informs the conduct of the desirous agent to obtain the desired goal. That is why Monsieur de Rollebon ^(F3) instilled fear of hell in the dying person by speaking of hell in a way that appealed to the sensitivity and the imagination of the situated agent, thereby  provoking fear that motivated th e action of confessing his sins, rather than by making objective statements about hell.

Carthago delenda est romanis, sed non carthaginenisbus .  For a given state of affairs to reveal its coefficient of adversity or favorableness, a project is required on the part of the agent.

In short, there are ways of referring others through statements otherwise than referring to what is objectively the case. All those who are exposed to the surrounding world as affectible and  vulnerable because they have needs and desires that can be frustrated or satisfied by the world to which they are exposed, can be referred by others, and refer themselves, to the world in a different way than in the cognitive way conceived and privileged by Aristotle. And this is the case of all agents.

But let me return to the question: what is the role of language in practical knowledge?

I said that language is not essential, since animals do not possess language and yet are capable of practical knowledge. But we need to be precise. Animals do not possess enunciative language –  what Aristotle calls “enunciative speech” – (by contrast to communicative language?). They cannot enunciate to themselves or to others what is the case, objectively. That is, they are incapable of making what Aristotle calls ‘judgments’ and even simply communicative ‘statements’ to others. Certainly they are incapable of making judgments in the strict Aristotelian sense of the word, that is, as reflective acts that result from finding sufficient reason for believing and affirming or denying something. Actually, they don’t even know the something that is to be believed or disbelieved, since they are incapable of enunciating it to themselves. Nonetheless Aristotle grants them the capacity to perceive, and even admits that their acts of perception may be materially true, that is, actually correspond to what is the case. This indicates that, according to Aristotle, without being able to verbalize it, animals possess theoretical knowledge. I think this is nonsensical and shows how confused Aristotle is by language – not the language he uses but the language he observes with curiosity and reflects upon as a philosopher. It is true that animals perceive, but not they perceive objectively; they perceive ‘signally,’ instrumentally and tacitly, in connection with action. What they perceive is, for them, a signal for action, depending on their inherited and acquired reflexes. They grasp what they perceive in the way Susanne Langer says that animals understand names – with practical denotation but with no connotation – conceptual or otherwise.

To give a concrete example, fear  is the way in which the agent was referred to hell by Rollebon. The reference was practical, related to action. Hell was not meant by Rollebon predicatively or objectively, as object of mere assent, that is, as object to be known theoretically, as object without any particular existential relation to the subject, but as timendum, as to be responded to with fear which disposes the agent to act so as to avoid it.

Aquinas distinguishes two ways of being referred or related to the object by means of statements: ex his quae sunt propria rei and ex dispositione agentis. ^(F4) In the former case, the object named is considered in itself and by itself, abstracted from all concrete existential relation to the subject. This objectifying abstraction is made possible by the connotational character of language. In the latter case, by contrast , the existential relations of the affectible subject to the object, which are tacit, are taken into account by the exposed, desirous agent. Science limits itself to the former relationship of the object to the curious subject. But not, as Aristotle does, by concentrating on the concept of what the thing is and moving from there to entities as substances and their natures, but by concentrating on how the thing acts and on the effects it produces, which refers indirectly to the way the agent’s dispositions enter or may enter into play, and which renders possible and even necessary the return to the world of agency. Actually, this concentration on action by contrast to entity is the shift that inaugurates modern science with Galileo and separates it from Aristotelian science . Aristotelian science studies the entities that populate the world, Galilean science studies the regular modus operandi of things. Aristotelian science looks for the nature or substance of the object conceived as the intelligible entity that supposedly corresponds necessarily to what is the case. Modern science looks for the regular activity of the object in the world , which is the same world in which the agent finds himselfs exposed to the action of what surrounds him, and vulnerable to it.  

The preceding considerations concerning language are not precise enough. What is what language makes possible?  Langer, as Aristotle in his Metaphysics, concentrate on names, not on verbs in sentences.   Names, in the case of people, can function connotationally or denotationally. Connotationally , names or ‘substantives’ lead to ‘conceive’ an object–an object-concept– , what they name, which cannot simply be equated with an “entity”, be it real or imaginary . Denotationally or signally , when completed by a gesture or another contextual indicator, names (which usually appear within sentences, not alone) announce the immediate or imminent presence of an object to a vulnerable, needful agent exposed to it. Dogs understand only perceptually-denotationally. Because words connote, we people,   in the case of names, ‘conceive’ “objects,” i. e., what they connote. But, if the names do not appear in sentences, we –we people, by contrast to animals– conceive them by themselves, as concepts , without any direct relation to the subject or to the surrounding perceptual world. The objects-concepts evoked by names may be concrete or abstract, singular or universal, real or imaginary or purely conceptual. However,   we, the users of language, are within the world, and connected to our immediate surroundings by perception .  Now, in ordinary perception, we are potentially exposed to what we perceive, and we are tacitly indicated to ourselves, by our repulsions and attractions, by our fears and desires (which always are of something in particular), as exposed to what surrounds us. I.e., we normally perceive ex dispositione agentis,, whereas the conception evoked by a name is neutral as to the relation of what is perceived to the subject and opens the way to think of the object ex his quae sunt propria rei, in the mode of curiosity rather than of concern.

Names normally appear in sentences. The only sentences that are important to us for the present are statements. By the very character of the objects connoted by the names contained or  involved in the statement (singular and concrete-perceptible or general or universal, concrete or abstract, etc.), as well as by the circumstances in which the statements are made, statements ^(F5) indicate the possible relations of the hearer or, – if in co-presence to the speaker, of the addressee–  to the object. In the case of reference to perceptible objects in ORAL STATEMENTS, which are always in immediate or imminent presence of both  the speaker and the addressee to the object(s) named, the names of the object(s) are always singular and concrete. The  exposure of the experiencing-judging subject, who is an agent, to the object’s action is tacit, indexical, for all consciousness or experience of object, be it immediately acquired or linguistically received, involves a tacit consciousness (of) the subject in relation to it (and, in the case of communication, a tacit understanding or awareness by the speaker of the tacit relation of the addressee to the object named). This tacit consciousness depends on the agent’s inheritance, on his culture, and on his previous experience of his own interaction with objects as well as the observed interaction of other subjects with objects and of objects among themselves. As a consequence of the exposure of t he needful-desirous subject, the actual relationship of the experiencing agent to the object named and referred to in the sentence that refers to the present perceptual world  is hodological, that is, instrumental, involving the past experienced relations of power of the subject in relation to the object and vice versa: “ I can,” or “I cannot .” In other words, past experience indicates and limits the kind of projects (by contrast to mere dreams) that the subject can conceive and realize in relation to the present or imminent object, given his desires and projects.       

However, the sentence may not refer to the immediate perceptual concrete world in which the hearer or reader finds himself in a particular situation, tacitly indicated to himself in his exposure. In that case, the subject is referred only connotationally, to a conceived or imagined ‘object,’ not denotationally or signally to an immediate or imminent concrete respondendum with distances of exposure. Such is the case in conversations, in narrations, etc., and also in oral discussions (scientific or other), in scientific writings, etc. Animals, lacking language, cannot be referred in this way even by names, much less by sentences. For them, the smell or their masters or their characteristic ringing of the bell signal the presence or imminent presence of their masters to be responded to. But these –smell, ringing of the bell– are not words and do not mean as language means. Thus  we can say that not only names but complete sentences and collections of sentences (such as narratives, essays, etc.) can function connotationally, i.e., as vehicle for   th e conception or imagiantion of objects,  not only denotationally or signally, in that they lead only to conceive what is said, without any immediate connection with action .

Thus language can function in very different ways. More concretely, the same sentence can function, and be meant to function, in very different ways. The reason is that much of the meaning of language is not explicit but tacit. Such being the case, being a competent user of language or linguistic agent involves the practical ability to ‘understand’ or ‘sense’ how sentences are meant to function in the concrete circumstances in which they are read or heard, and in which they are said or written. A lot of this ‘understanding’ or ‘sensing’ depends on cultural customs and on accepted literary genres, but also on the context in which the sentence is uttered and heard, written and read. And for this there are no rules. Think of the Bible and its Christian ‘realistic’ reading by contrast to the realistic but non-historical reading of novels (Cf. Frei, The Eclipse of Biblical Narrative). Think, too, of “the Martians have landed”, and of the way in which language functions in a class for foreigners where all that survives of the language taught is its customary meaning. Add to this, in connection with perception, the case of inverting lenses and the re-inversion of the perceived through action and not without action, which indicates the practical connection of perception with action.

 

There is a correlation between perception, action, and language .
