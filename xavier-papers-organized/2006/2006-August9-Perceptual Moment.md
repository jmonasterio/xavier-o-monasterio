2006-August-9-Perceptual Moment

THE PERCEPTUAL MOMENT

Unlike Plato, who systematically distrusted perception because of the particularity of its object, and therefore the contingency of perceptual knowledge, the more pragmatic Aristotle gave perception a place of choice for at least two reasons: because all kowledge begins with perception, and because of the quasi intuitive character of perception due to the presence of its object. True, Aristotle did not therefore deem perception infallible or absolutely certain. Like Plato, he reserved certainty for the intellectual judgment, whose primary object was the intelligible – but for Aristotle it was the intelligible in the perceptual object.

However, the judgment itself was a second order or reflective operation, not a perceptual one, for it was preceded by abstraction. My point is that the supposed rehabilitation of perception in Aritotle’s epistemology by contrast to Plato’s, in other words, Aristotle’s empiricism, is a misconstruction of his views, for Aristotle never gave its due to the perceptual moment. We have to begin all over again at the beginning, at the perceptual moment. But not like Husserl, who focused in an abstract consciousness of object, acccording to the principle that all consciousness is consciousness OF something.

This obsession with the object of consciousness arises from the fact that we attain that consciousness through reflection, which is indeed objective.
