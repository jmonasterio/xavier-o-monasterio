De Lubac on Faith in Aquinas

(Taken from H. de Lubac, (Essai sur la Structure du Symbole des Apotres , 1969, Ignati The Christian Faith us Press , San Francisco, 1986).

Chapter 9

1) Augustine

Faith is a dynamism toward God of the whole person. Belief (which the demons have) is of the intellect, but not faith . The dynamism in faith is not extrinsic to faith. For him "there is no difference between the impulse of faith and that of charity" (300). "Ille enim credit in Xtum qui sperat in Xtum et diligit Xtum."

2) Aquinas

He also knew that the realization of God's existence "as an assent to revelation made to us, is entirely different from that which philosophy can provide, because it is a step leading to beatific vision" (Gilson, 303).

Repeats the threefold credere that the act of faith includes.

Adopts Auxerre's definition: "perceptio divinae veritatis, tendens in ipsam.

Faith is adherence to a Person, not to concepts or things: "What would seem to be most important, and which in some way has the value of the end, in every act of faith, is the Person to whose word one adheres" (Summa, q. I, art. 1).

Through faith Xt is received into the heart.

Distinguishes dogmas from mysteries (under different words) .

Compares the adherence of faith with a marriage with God, following Hosea.

Faith is ordained toward the goods one hopes for.

By means of enuntiabilia, the believer's intellect, tends toward its sole object, which is the primary Truth .

This impulse of faith is also of charity.

However

two characteristics indicate the beginning of a change in orientation which was gradually brought about in the theology of the schoolmen:

(1) The process of analysis proper to scholasticism eventually led to a categorical distinction between intellect and will .- Aquinas makes the will move the intellect, and what is accidental to the intellect may be essential to faith. Still, the supplement which charity brings to the act of faith is accidental 'secundum genus naturae'.

(2) At least in certain texts Aquinas opposes "seeing" and "believing" so as to consider faith as imperfect knowledge between opinion and science. In this way the Augustinian impetus of faith does not belong to faith anymore; it is now the effect of desire which urges the believer to seek something else, i.e. to move beyond faith toward vision . And this vision is a sort of knowledge itself.

" The intellectualist concept which underlies these reflections seems rather distant from the spiritual dynamism of the fathers." (307)

" Calvin, ignorant as he was of the teaching of the great Scholastics, should have included them too in the reproaches he addressed to certain decadent theologians of his time. The 'frigida Parisiensis opinio', 'mortua opinio', 'scholastica fides’ he criticizes . And, if he had preserved in his explanations the intermediary term 'credere Deo', he might have avoided his antithesis (between credere Deum esse and credere in Deum) which, assuming the appearance of a dichotomy, dangerously opened the way to promoters of a faith-trust emptied of all doctrinal content" (308) . Same with Melanchton (309). "This polemical depreciation of 'credere Deum' did not develop to the profit of the movement of faith 'in Deum' . A change of orientation was taking place, the consequences of which would appear later; the direction was no longer toward God but toward man.

De Lubac : "Faith is its content" (Guardini, 311).

The movement of faith belongs to the order of foresight, whereas the objective recognition of its intellectual content belongs to retrospection . The former mode of knowing is direct and concrete, one's whole life is projected forward. Retrospection discovers and itemizes (incompletely) under an abstract form ( Blondel). (310) .

Faith is its content. Is determined by what it believes . Is the living response to the call of the One who announces himself in revelation.

Contrary to a misguided exegesis, faith will remain. Such exegesis "allowed itself to be ruled by a theological prejudice, i.e. by an idea of faith which is at least incomplete. Faith "in itself is not non-seeing , even now it is a sort of familiarity, a seeing; just as it is commitment and, indivisibly, even knowledge: "my own know me". This is what Aquinas recognized: "faith is a habitus of the mind by which eternal life has begun in us" (312-3). Sort of 'vision through faith" .

Cajetan and Banez :

In faith man does not progress toward an impersonal knowledge which some day he may possess perfectly . The 'knowledge of the blessed' of which Scholasticism speaks is not that of which some theologians have dreamed since the days of Cajetan and Banez; it is not a 'scientific' theology like the one that is evolving in our schools, and which would eventually satisfy the mind entirely because it would rest on principles based, not on the obscurity of faith but on the clarity of vision .in And if theology must remain and grow deeper beyond death, it is not that kind of science (of which St Thomas said at the moment of his death that it seemed only a straw to him), but theology in its most ancient and noble sense, which is the movement of faith, adoration, ecstasy in God. That theology will never end because God is inexhaustible. God is not a spectacle; it is something that is always happening anew (313-14).

From chapter 4: Belief and Faith

Many things we have to believe on other people's testimony or out of practical necessity. These are 'beliefs". We believe them, but that is not our faith. (145).

Ensemble of characteristics that distinguish faith from belief (145-8) .

Yet, though distinct from simple belief, it does not exclude but includes it, requires it, and cannot do without it . "The intimate union, in a single act, of what we designate as belief and as faith is essential to Xtianity and indissoluble. Faith makes the beliefs which it includes share in its own characteristics . It is not enough to entertain beliefs, to adhere to truths, in order to be a Xtian, but it is necessary to integrate them in an act which involves the whole being. The obedience of faith necessarily includes an act of intellectual adherence to a series of beliefs (148-150).

l63-170: Faith can only be a response to God.- Faith is what makes one a Xtian. For St Paul "believer" is synonym with 'Xtian" and "saint". But pistis can only be translated by faith, not by belief . The verb credere followed by the preposition in applies only to persons. One cannot believe in things; but one cannot have faith but in God; we believe in solum Deum. To believe with the total commitment of faith is not possible unless one believes in that personal, unique Being we call God . To give such faith to a man would be idolatry and enslavement. Newman's obedience of faith is absolute obedience .

Because of a false scruple, in the English edtion of Mouroux's "Je crois en Toi" the tile was shortened into "I believe"--so great, in certain theological circles in the middle of this century, was the fear of a personalistic attitude, suspected of undermining the intellectualism of belief and the objectivism of truth" (170) .

From chapter 7: The unity of faith

"Ecclesiastical faith" in the specialized language of theology in recent ceturies : Opposed to "divine faith" which imposes its object on us directly by divine revelation. Was to be the assent given to declarations of the Magisterium not in matters of doctrine but of fact. A supplementary kind of faith of a lower degree based on the authority of the magisterium.

Weak doctrinal foundation; cheap trick to insure the unity of the faith; extrinsecistic mentality, against which Blondel protested.

It produced a chiasm between the faith of the 'majores" and the simple faith of the 'minores" . St Thomas on this distinction (231). But  St Thomas's distinction became less acceptable as his doctrine on the substance of faith became blurred, leaving only the series of 'articles" or "statements"; loss of the personal character of faith, fragmented now into multiple beliefs . The distinction drew new strength or gained a new basis in more recent theories about "natural faith" or 'scientific faith" .

Having reached, thanks to study and without the need of supernatural enlightenment, a rational certitude concerning divine revelation, the majores were more like learned men than like faithful; the latter were the lower caste. Rousselot , because of his hatred of natural faith opposed this distinction (232).

Another form of this extrinsecistic theory came from the distinction between the teaching and the taught Church. Clearly there are among Xtians many differences in their manner of believing; but they are all one in proportion as they participate in a single faith, which is the faith of the Church into which they were initiated equally. (234). Criticism of pretentious theologians--" the magisterium of the experts" criticized by Congar.

Objectum fidei is quid incomplexum. Nor does it suffice to say that the object of faith derives its unity from divine revelation, meaning merely that all the truths it contains are believed en masse on the formal authority of God; these truths really make up an internal unity, they hold together, they refer to one another (237). Each one is an aspect of a global and unique truth, one which shares in some manner in the very unity of  God in three Persons. Cf. Newman (237) . Because  it adheres to the primary and final unity of its object, the obedience of faith, wherever it exists, remains substantially the same, in the most varied situations. (238-9).

The objective unity of faith is one of both dogma and mystery.

The notion of dogma : Connected with that of authority. Decree, ordinance, in the NT. Later refers to the rules which are addressed first of all to reason, to the truths which demand intellectual assent (241) . Disuse in Middle Ages and St Thomas. Later it became again customary to speak of Xtian dogmas and of the collective dogma, that is, the ensemble of Xtian truth insofar as it is accepted by faith on God's authority, transmitted to us by the authority of the Church . "The character of dogma is the same whatever may be the relative importance of the truth in question" (Danielou), and every truth is equally obligatory. [Extrinsecism?] . However, if we considered only the dogmatic or authoritarian character of Xtian truth, we should form only a superficial and finally  odious notion of it, as did Canon Muzzarelli (who reminds me of Aquinas, to an extent) (243)

The supposed unintelligibility of mysteries completed the authoritarianism of dogma, in this view. But the notion of mystery which cannot be separated from dogma without distortion invites us to look beyond the authority which our faith recognizes to the object itself which is proposed to us. Moreover, just as one can legitimately enumerate dogmas, one can also enumerate mysteries, and these are in both cases the same truths. But if the dogmas find their unity in their origin, the mysteries find their unity interiorly , through silence (243).

The total truth which the Creed encloses is a living and life-giving truth. An indefinitely fruitful truth. And also the principle of a twofold progress: on the one hand, its objective implications lend themselves to endless clarifications; on the other, it can be more and more deeply studied by the interior gaze of the believer. Development of dogma and deepening of mystery (246).

The development of dogma is an objective phenomenon unfolded through the centuries. It does not lead us any farther than the Creed. Through analysis and increasing precision, whose driving force is the elimination of error or protection against it, and also through selection, purification, assimilation. This is the activity which constantly renews the perspectives of theology .

But this 'multiplication' and 'refinement' of truth  would be out of balance without the deepening of mystery (252-3).
