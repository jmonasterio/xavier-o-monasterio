The God of philosoophers

                 "Do you believe in God? - If belief in God means  speaking
           about Him in the third person, then I don't believe in God.  But
           if belief means being able to speak to Him in the second person,
           then I do believe.
                 "But Buber's idea that we can converse  with  God  without
           having any notion (independent of the  conversation  itself)  of
           who it  is  we  are  speaking  with  is  no  less  dubious  than
           Leibowitz's idea that we can  worship  God  without  having  any
           notion (independent of the act of worship itself)  of  who  this
           God is that we are worshipping" (NYRB, Nov. 4, 1993)

      There is a point to Buber's view of belief in God, as well as  to  all
those who think that the God of  philosophers  is  not  the  God  of  faith.
Indeed,  insofar  as  the  God  of  philosophers  is  a  God  known  through
propositions assented to, and assented to on the  basis  of  evidence,  then
between the God of  philosophers  and  the  God  of  Jesus-Christ  there  is
nothing in common but a name.
      This does not entail, however, that the God of faith cannot be  spoken
of. Fides ex auditu. How else did Buber come  to  believe  in  the  biblical
God, to appropriate what he himself saw as "the  authentic  biblical  belief
in God"? Pace Buber, those who love a person can  speak  lovingly  and  even
knowingly--though  non-scientifically,  and  this  is  the  valid  point  of
Buber's remark--of the person they love.
      According to Buber, "it was  Paul  who  'Helenized'  biblical  belief.
Paul replaced 'belief  in'  by  'belief  that',  thus  chaning  belief  from
encounter to a doctrine with a Church" (ibid.). Buber makes this  accusation
because he does not see any alternative between  the  objectifying  language
of science and the I-Thou language of encounter.  But  he  is  wrong.  Paul,
like the authors of the Gospels, speaks of the God he  has  encountered  and
as encountered or to be encountered by others.
The Greek conception of truth and belief-that
      For the Greeks, at least for  the  Greeks  who  concern  us--Socrates,
Plato, Aristotle--truth  is  a  property  of  language,  be  it  the  silent
language of the  knower  talking  to  himself  or  herself,  or  the  spoken
language of the  communicator  talking  to  others.  If  nothing  is  either
silently or audibly said, there is neither truth  nor  falsity.  Concretely,
truth is a property of statements, for only statements mean to express  what
is the case, and truth is  precisely  the  conformity  (adaequatio)  between
what is said to be the case and what is actually the case.
      That truth, for the Greek philosophers,  consisted  in  conformity  is
clear from what was problematic for them. That the sensible was relative  to
the perceiver was a problem for them because it threatened their  notion  of
truth as conformity between the stated and the object.  How,  indeed,  could
different statements be conform to one and the same reality?  The  sensible,
and the merely contingent, and the temporal lacked the  kind  of  permanence
required for the conformity between the said and the object. (If all  is  in
flux, as Plato argues in the Theaetetus, the name white (which is a name  of
the sensation of white in the mind) cannot be conform to the  object).  Thus
only the unchanging was object of truth--of episteme, of  scientific  truth,
rather than of mere--even right--opinion. And  only  the  intelligible--what
had a "nature" grasped in the "essential" concept or  definition  seeked  by
Socrates and affirmed in the said--not the  contingent  singular,  possessed
the required necessary fixity to be object of episteme, that is,  object  of
necessary true statements.
      Certain traits of this conception of reality,  of  knowledge,  of  the
knowing mind, and of truth deserve attention. First of all,  if  no  'said',
that is, 'stated' by the mind, then no truth.
[As Searle observes, if I tell you "It is true that your house caught  fire"
I am referring to a previous statement, not to the reality itself]. Does  it
follow from this that if no truth no  reality?  In  other  words,  were  the
Greeks idealists? Did they hold the primacy of knowledge over  reality?  Did
they believe that the  conformity  between  the  said  and  the  object  was
conformity of the object to the said rather than the other way around?
      Certainly Aristotle was no idealist,  for  he  relaxed  pragmatically,
however illogically, his epistemic constraints. For  instance,  he  came  to
grant that what is regular can be object of episteme even  if  it  does  not
have an essence, like eclipses. Also, what is singular or transient  can  be
object of right opinion (of common opinion by "us"), even though  it  cannot
be proved. Finally, though the  good  is  not  object  of  episteme,  it  is
nonetheless object of practical (true?) knowledge.  (Hence  the  distinction
between theoretical and practical reason. But  do  these  two  "reasons"  or
faculties have anything in common except the name? Is not  practical  reason
totally non-Platonic, naturalistic,  the  reason--that  is,  the  evaluative
intelligence--of the exposed cultural agent who is not  an  animal,  whereas
theoretical reason is the Platonic reason of an angelic  contemplator  whose
objects  are  if  not  eternal  ideas  at  least   abstract,   intelligible,
immutable natures?).
      However much the pragmatic Aristotle may have, under the  pressure  of
unacceptable consequences, moved away from the notion  of  an  angelic  soul
buried in an animal body and from the consequent reduction  of the field  of
knowledge to practically nothing, the fact remains that behind his  original
Platonic  conception--progressively  modified  but  never   fully   abjured,
inherited by the medievals and integrated into their  conception  of  faith,
of revelation, of theology, of philosophy, and of the  relationship  between
faith and reason--lies a commonsensical,  mirror-theory  of  knowledge  that
continues to exert on us a powerful fascination. (See,  however,  concerning
Aris to the difference from Descartes on the  mirroring  mind,  PH  and  the
Mirror of Nature, pp. 46 ff). It is or appears to  be  commonsensical,  that
is, 'allant de soi', because thinking on the nature  of  knowledge,  of  the
mind, of truth, etc., is REFLECTIVE. In other words, we do not reflect  when
we are knowing exposedly, but afterwards, either when  others  are  knowing,
or when we already know and are cashing our knowledge into statements to  be
assented to by others. Thus it seems to us that knowers do what we  see  and
state them to do--we who are not exposed and whose only  distances  both  to
the exposed knowers and to the objects which  we  contemplators  think  they
contemplate are contemplative.
      But let us return to the Greek view of knowledge and truth  as  it  is
reflected in their at least implicit view of  language  in  general  and  of
statements  in  particular  .[As  an  instance,  consider  Aquinas   telling
commentary on Aris' Peri hermeneias.]
Constative language
      The Greek view of knowledge and truth is reflected in a conception  of
language according to which constatives  (or  statives)  are  the  epistemic
basis of meaning; they have a foundational priority. Constatives, which  are
referential, mean to  say  what  is  the  case  and  express  the  assentful
judgment of a subject to be assented to by  all  those  other  subjects  who
consider that statement a picture conform to reality.
      According to this view, statements, which  express  silent  judgments,
are pictures, and true statements, which express silent true judgments,  are
genuinely resemblant pictures. Thus what  people  do  in  knowing  is  build
pictures of reality, and what they exchange in constative language are  such
pictures. [This presupposes the primacy and independence of  knowledge  over
practice]. Accordingly, when there is disagreement  among  people  proof  is
needed that  the  picture  is--in  the  ideal  case  of  demonstration,  has
necessarily to be--resemblant, that is, that the object has  necessarily  to
be as stated.
       What  is  curious  is  that  the  theory  of  resemblance  is  purely
hypothetical  and  cannot  be  verified,  since  nobody  can  know   reality
directly. This is what led Kant  to  drop  the  hypothesis  and  effect  his
Copernican epistemic revolution. (However,  the  foundational  character  of
the language of statements is parasitic on  the  hypothesis  of  knowing  as
seeing, and of seeing as having resemblant pictures  in  the  mind,  and  of
judging as vErifying rationally the resemblance. One has supposedly to  move
to that level of language to dissipate disagreements,  though  disagreements
cannot be  dissipated  through  objective  discussion.  And  the  appeal  to
scientific statements as the ultimate tribunal is based on the same  theory.
And so is the appeal to the  supposedly  evidential  statement  language  of
academia, of "the experts).
      It is part and parcel of this conception that knowledge  is  objective
in the sense of dictated by, and referring  to,  the  object  as  it  is  in
itself independently of  the  desires,  preferences  and  interests  of  the
knower, and therefore that  desires,  preferences  and  interests  interfere
with the knowing  process  (for  they  are  non-cognitive,  passional  blind
forces or tendencies) and are therefore sources of error  in  the  cognitive
process, and sources of wrong conduct when not  directed  by  the  cognitive
faculty.
      An important characteristic of this view of language is that it  makes
of statements a unique species of speech. Only  statements  are  referential
through depicting object. [See Aquinas on Peri hermeneias]. Only  statements
affirm or deny the resemblance of the picture  to  reality  and,  therefore,
only they are true or false. Only statements  are  dictated  by  the  world.
[Only statements are world-subject directed; all others are  subject-world].
Other speech acts mean, but they do not refer to what is the case  as  being
the case. Meaning is to be understood, reference is to be assented to.  Thus
in the case of all other speech acts understanding  is  sufficient  for  the
speech  act  to  be  successful,  but  only  in  the  case   of   statements
understanding is not  sufficient,  for  mere  understanding  without  assent
fails to efficiently refer the hearer.
      Now, this unique status of statements, which determines their  primacy
and basicality is dependent on taking as paradigmatic the view  of  language
and of reality of a hearer of language who 'understands' as  a  contemplator
situated nowhere but neither speaks nor responds,  an  angelic  contemplator
of reality who neither desires nor is exposed, who has nothing to  gain  and
nothing to lose.
      As a way of characterizing this  view  of  language  we  can  usefully
think of the way in which 'language' is 'spoken' and 'heard' in a course  on
a foreign language. Thus, in a class of French the following  sentences  may
be uttered  by  the  teacher  and  heard  by  the  students:  "Ma  mère  m'a
téléphoné." "J'ai un chien blanc."  "Je  te  promets  mille  dollars  si  tu
traduis bien cette phrase-ci." "Le bâtiment dans lequel nous  nous  trouvons
a pris feu."
       Any  competent  user  of  the  French  language   understands   these
sentences. However, they would not be 'taken seriously' by  competent  users
of language, given the context in which they are uttered.  And  rightly  so,
for although they sound like pieces  of  language  in  that  they  could  be
meaningfully spoken and heard by linguistic  agents  under  the  appropriate
circumstances, here they are not meant linguistically,  that  is,  they  are
not meant to function as language anymore than a faked bill of  ten  dollars
is meant to function as money.
      Suppose, however, that somebody, based on the  facts  that  the  above
utterances are grammatically correct and semantically meaningful,  and  that
all competent users of French would identify the  same  meaning  in  hearing
them, developed a theory of language on that basis.  [Probably  the  Greeks,
particularly  Plato,  thought  of  language  along  these  lines,  and  most
certainly--though not explicitly, Kant].
      To be consistent with its  basis,  the  theory  would  have  to  place
meaning in the words themselves (be it in single  words  or  in  strings  of
words), and to define meaning  in  terms  of  mere  intelligibility  of  the
uttered (that is, written) sentences.   However,  since  our  theorist  also
uses language, he or she would notice that only statements refer to  reality
in the sense of explicitly affirming or denying what is actually  the  case,
and that not all statements can be assented to by those who hear them.  Thus
only statements  have  the  property  of  being  true  or  false  pieces  of
language. Furthermore, our theorist would relate this feature of  statements
to his or her experience as a knower. For she  would  remark  that  she  can
utter sincerely the words "J'ai un chien blanc," or assent  to  these  words
when uttered by somebody else, only when she knows that the  case  is  such,
which makes the truth  of  statements  dependent  on  the  truth  of  silent
judgments, and the truth of silent judgments  on  their  conformity  to  the
reality stated.
      One feature of this  view  of  statements  that  it  presupposes  that
knowledge  is  totally  independent  from  action.  Speaking  is   conveying
meanings to be understood by the hearer, except in the case  of  statements,
which do not only convey a meaning but also refer to  reality  (to  what  is
the case) and are, therefore, to be not only understood but assented  to  by
the hearer if they are true or at least judged to be true.
Speech-Acts theory of language
      As already mentioned, this view of language takes language not  as  it
is experienced by those who use the  language  in  their  very  use  of  it.
Rather, it considers language as an object already there, as  an  instrument
to express the meanings one wants to express  and  convey.  The  person  who
knows and who wants to convey the  meanming  that  her  mother  is  present,
takes the appropriate words, combines them into the correct and  appropriate
sentence that means that, and says "My mother is present."
       Now,  for  multiple  reasons  this  view  of  communicative  language
(whatever the case may be for the silent language  of  the  thinker),  seems
unsatisfactory. A very important among them  is  that  this  view  does  not
correspond to the experience of  competent  users  of  language.  Linguistic
utterances do not speak or hear themselves nor do they mean  by  themselves;
they are uttered by speakers and heard by hearers, and they are  uttered  by
speakers to convey their own intentions, for words  and  sentences  have  no
intentions, and these intentions in many  cases  aim  at  producing  effects
that are translinguistic.  How  do  we  know  this?  By  reflecting  on  our
experience as users of our native language, that is, of being  speakers  and
hearers of a language as natives, not as learners of a second language,  not
even as readers  of  our  native  language.  Though  Speech-Acts  theory  of
language  arises  as  an  alternative  to  the  Greek  or  constative   view
oflanguage, it  remains  nonetheless,  at  least  in  its  Serlean  version,
remains contaminated with the Greek view, and this is reflected in at  least
three ways: (1) Linguistic meaning is something to be understood, not to  be
transcended in application--which amounts to the  separation  of  truth  and
action; (2) Constative language, that is, statements, is  foundational;  and
(3), truth is conformity between the meant and reality.
      Let us listen to Searle himself.
            "Human communication  has  some  extraordinary  properties,  not
      shared by most  other  kinds  of  human  behavior.  One  of  the  most
      extraordinary is this: If I am trying to tell someone something,  then
      (assuming certain conditions are satisfied) as soon as  he  recognizes
      that I am trying to tell him something and exactly what  it  is  I  am
      trying  to  tell  him,  I  have  succeeded  in  telling  it  to   him.
      Furthermore, unless he  recognizes  that  I  am  trying  to  tell  him
      something and what I am trying to tell him, I do not fully succeed  in
      telling it to him. In the case of illocutionary  acts  we  succeed  in
      doing what we are trying to do by getting our  audience  to  recognize
      what we are trying to do. But the 'effect' on  the  hearer  is  not  a
      belief or response, it consists simply in the hearer understanding the
      utterance of the speaker. It is this effect that I have  been  calling
      the illocutionary effect.
            "The characteristic intended effect of meaning is understanding,
      but understanding... is not a perlocutionary effect...
            "Understanding a sentence is  knowing  its  meaning...  And  the
      intention will in general be achieved if the  hearer  understands  the
      sentence, i.e. knows its meaning, i.e. knows the rules  governing  its
      elements.
            "Let us illustrate these points with a very simple  example...--
      an utterance of the sentence "Hello."...
      The intentions will be achieved in general if the  hearer  understands
      the sentence "Hello", i.e. understands its meaning,  i.e.  understands
      that under  certain  conditions  its  utterance  counts  as  greeting"
      (Speech Acts, pp. 47-49).

            "I wish to argue that saying  something  and  meaning  it  is  a
      matter of intending to perform an illocutionary act. First,  it  could
      not be the case that in general intended effects of  meant  utterances
      were perlocutionary because many kinds of sentences  used  to  perform
      illocutionary acts have no perlocutionary effect associated with their
      meaning. For example, there is no associated perlocutionary effect  of
      greeting. When I say "Hello" and mean it, I do not necessarily  intend
      to produce or elicit any state or action in my hearer other  than  the
      knowledge that he is being greeted. But that knowledge is  simply  his
      understanding what I said, it is not an additional response or effect"
      (Ibid. p. 46).
            "In the utterance of 'Hello' there is no  propositional  content
      and no sincerity condition. The  preparatory  condition  is  that  the
      speaker must have just encountered the hearer, and the essential  rule
      is that the utterance counts as a courteous indication of  recognition
      of the hearer" (pp. 64-65)

      [No, there is no indication or  notification  independent  from  being
      constituted as curteously recognized (and this  being  constituted  is
      not knowledge or mere understanding), anymore than  there  is  on  the
      speaker's  part  a  recognition  and  a  statement  notifying  of  the
      recognition; there is only linguistic recognition, no statement!].
            "...  there  is  a  close  connection  between  saying  and  the
      constative class of illocutionary acts. Saying fits statements but not
      greetings. Indeed, Austin's original insight  into  performatives  was
      that SOME utterances were not sayings, but doings of some other  kind.
      But this point can be exaggerated. A man who says "I  hereby  promise"
      not only promises but says  he  does.  That  is,  there  is  indeed  a
      connection between saying and constatives, but it is not as  close  as
      one might be inclined to think" (p. 68).
      [Clearly, according to  Searle,  a  person  who  utters  "Hello"  says
      nothing but performs a salute by uttering what counts as saluting].
            "Wherever there  is  a  psychological  state  specified  in  the
      sincerity condition the performance of the act counts as an expression
      of the  psychological  state.  This  law  holds  whether  the  speaker
      actually has the specified psychological state or not. Thus to assert,
      affirm, state (that p) counts as an expression of belief  (that  p)...
      To request... (that A be done) counts as  an  expression  of  wish  or
      desire (that A be done) (p. 65).
            "If your house is on fire, I do not rush up to you and announce,
      'It is true taht your house is on fire'; rather, I  simply  say  'Your
      house is on fire.' The former locution I use only when the proposition
      that your house is on fire is already under consideration,  where  the
      question has already  been  raised  prior  to  my  announcement...  we
      characteristically use the word 'true'  only  when  a  proposition  is
      already  under   consideration,   and   because   a   proposition   is
      characteristically put under consideration by the performance of  some
      such perlocutionary act  as  asserting,  stating,  or  hypothesizing--
      because of these two facts--calling something true will place us in  a
      certain relation to that initial illocutionary act (a relation,  f.e.,
      of agreement or endorsement and conversely in the case of 'not true' a
      relation  of  disagreement.  All  of  this  tells  us  what  sorts  of
      illocutionary acts we might be performing (among others) when we utter
      the sentence, f. e., "it is true that your house is on fire'. But  for
      reasons already stated, it still does not tell us the solution to  the
      philosophical problem of truth" (pp. 153-4).
      (APPLY THIS TO REVELATION AND  THEOLOGY--AND  POHILOSOPHY,  WHICH  ARE
      REFLECTIVE NOT ONLY IN THE SENSE OF NOT TAKING PLACE IN THE MOMENT  OF
      EXPOSURE, BUT ALSO IN THE SENSE OF BEING ABOUT VERBAL  PICTURES,  THAT
      IS, STATEMENTS).
      Notice that in the very first quotation Searle identifies  the  notion
of language with the intention of telling someone something. (Now  "telling"
is most of the time taken by Searle in the sense of saying something  to  be
uderstood, but in certain cases it is taken in the sense  of  counting  as).
Of course, if the fundamental  linguistic  intention  is  to  say  something
rather than doing something or having something  done  linguistically,  then
sheer understanding of what I tell in the sense of knowing what I intend  to
tell makes the act, as speech act, successful. And, in that  case,  "Hello,"
which says nothing to be understood, has no illocutionary meaning.  But,  as
we shall see, it is the very notion  of  illocutionary  meaning  independent
from and foundational of the perlocutionary that is under  dispute,  for  it
is an illusion of the REFLECTIVE UNDERSTANDING OF  LANGUAGE,  that  is,  the
understanding of language not by the users themselves  but  by  philosophers
who reflect.

      Searle's view of meaning is wrong.  In  the  actual  use  of  language
(with the possible exception of statements, which we  will  consider  below)
we do not exchange  meanings  to  be  understood.  Let  us  consider  a  few
examples.
      Take a question: "Are  you  coming  tomorrow?".  I  am  not  saying  a
question; I am questioning you concerning your coming tomorrow.
According to Searle, if you understand that  I  am  asking  you  about  your
coming tomorrow--and in the  above  quotation  he  identifies  understanding
with knowing--, you are a competent user of language, for you  grasped  what
I wanted to communicate. I deny that. Understanding  a  question   addressed
to me is becoming questioned linguistically, being constituted as  answerer.
If I do not become questioned, your speech act of questioning me failed.
Otherwise, AND THIS SEEMS TO BE THE CASE FOR  SEARLE,  the  pure  hearer  of
language who is not questioned but understands your asking a question of  me
is the paradigm of understanding language,  rather  than  the  participating
hearer involvekd with the speaker. In fact, as a competent user of  language
in concrete circumstances, a witness who hears the question and  understands
it  applicatively  and  from  his  situation  as   a   witness,   has   been
linguistically put in the state of not being questioned, and his conduct  of
not answering my question is his real grasp of my meaning.
      "I promise to pay you five dlls. if you do this job." On p. 61  Searle
states that promising involves that "S intends that the utterance of T  will
place him under an obligation to do A," and that "S  intends...  to  produce
in H the knowledge (K) that the utterance of T is  to  count  as  placing  S
under an obligation to do A." But how can the promiser have  more  than  the
knowledge,  namely,  the  obligation  to  do  something  (even  if  we  take
obligation in a purely institutional, quasi-legal sense), if the heraer  has
only the knowledge that S has an obligation to do A,  not  the  right  to  A
unless it becomes  impossible?  Is  there  not,  on  the  hearer's  part,  a
correspondent to the sincerity condition on the speaker's  part,  namely,  a
"taking-seriously condition," that is, an applicative hearing incarnated  in
action?  The lack of symmetry in the speakekr and the  hearer,  in  Searle's
view,  is  suspect,  for  being  under  obligation  on  the  S's   part   is
perlocutionary, not mere illocutionary understanding.  PERHAPS,  INSTEAD  OF
ILLOCUTIONARY  AND  PERLOCUTIONARY,  WE  SHOULD  SPEAK  OF  APPLICATIVE  AND
CONTEMPLATIVE UNDERSTANDING OF LANGUAGE]. If it  were,  there  would  be  no
difference between an insincere promise and a sincere one. And if  the  fact
that a promise is insincerely made dispensed the  utterer  from  obligation,
except in the legal sense, then H  would  have  no  right  in  the  case  of
insincere promises, since his or her right is not a  linguistically  created
effect. (The case  of  insincere  promises  is  interesting  concerning  the
possible reduction of promising to true statement; see p. 62).
      Apart from the fact  that  this  view  ultimately  reduces  lingusitic
meaning, as far as the hearer is concerned, to saying  something  said  (the
propositional [?]) plus an intention, it leads to  a  confusion  of  written
with spoken language and to a paradigmatization of  the  former--the  silent
language of the thinker--as will be shown when we come to statements.
      As for the statement that "Hello" or saluting  has  no  perlocutionary
characteristic effect, it is the other way around: it has  no  illocutionary
effect distinguishable.
      If I threaten you with the words "I will kill you if you do  not  give
me your money," I do not expect you simply to catch the meaning of my  words
as somebody who is learning  a  foreign  language,  and  not  even  just  to
understand my intention of having your money or killing you. (This  is  what
a pure non-situated contemplator of language would  understand,  exactly  as
those learning French as a second language). I expect, and mean you,  to  be
and act scared, to actually give me your money or  to  counterattack  me  in
words or deeds, or at least to  explain  to  me  why  you  are  not  scared.
Correlatively, you the hearer do not expect me  to turn  around  and  go  my
way after saying that, but to atempt to kill me if I  do  not  give  you  my
money.
      Similarly, if I tell you "Come here," I do not expect  you  simply  to
catch the meaning of my words as someone who happens to hear my  words,  but
to come to move from where you are toward me. And if you tell  me  that  you
are a competent user of English and that the central if not the whole  point
of my telling you "Come here" is to  have  you  understand  what  the  words
"Come here" mean, I tell you that you  are  no  competent  user  of  English

being--has dangerous points of correspondence with the  Greek  view.  I  say
dangerous, because what the participants in linguistic communication  do  is
not what the theory of language  built  by  a  contemplator-hearer  imagines
they do.
      My view is different from Searle's. WE SPEAK AND HEAR AS WE  PERCEIVE,
NAMELY, APPLICATIVELY, for we are exposed agents; we do not speak  and  hear
as contemplators, as Searle thinks  we  do.  Language  is  communicative  of
practical intentions  of  exposed  agents-patients.  Communication  succeeds
when  the  practical  or  perlocutionary  effect  obtains  and  the   action
continues  to  flow.  Only  when  communication  fails  does  the   supposed
illocutionary effect distinctive of language appear.  But  such  effect,  if
taken as the paradigmatic case, leads to a mistaken theory of  language.  As
any other instrumental activity,  language  is  to  be  understood  when  it
succeeds, not wehn it fails. Language means to achieve practical effects  by
saying. A linguistic piece that fails to achieve the  practical  effect  its
peaker means to achieve is a defective case. Thus, illocutionary  meaning--a
merely understood intention of the speaker--is parasitic  on  perlocutionary
meaning, not the other way around.

results--as in the Greek view--in  their  foundational  character.  Let  us,

The origin of the contemplative illusion
       Suppose  that  the  hearer  understands  without  believing,  without
assenting, and contests the S's statement either for lack of  confidence  in
the speaker's sincerity or competence, or because of  positive  evidence  to
the contrary. Thus, "It is not true that my house is on fire."
      When I see my house on fire  I  see  applicatively,  since  I  have  a
vested interest in my house. SEEING IS SEEING AS, AND SEEING  AS  IS  SEEING
AS APPLICATIVELY, depending on my being-relationship to the seen. Seeing  my
house on fire amounts to responding practically to my house  on  fire.  When
you see my house on fire as a situated, concerned witness  (not  addressee),
you see it applicatively. Your seeing it  amounts to running to  notify  me.
But if a pure contemplator saw my house on fire with no vested  interest  at
all, he or she would see assentfully--Newman would say  "notionally."  Would
this contemplator be speaking of the same seeing and of the same  object  as
we exposed agents do?
      When you pass to me the piece of information--you who  function  as  a
witness who has the experience of exposed agent--you 'read'  the  object  as
object for response for me and pass it to  me  as  object  for  response  by
someone who understands my addressee's situation as the owner of the  house.
That this is so is clear, for you would be totally disconcerted  and  expect
an explanation if I assented to what you say and  did  nothing  in  response
concerning the house. So you are  speaking  the  language  of  exposure  and
expect me to hear it accordingly. So you do not expect assent but  assentful
action, APPLICATIVE UNDERSTANDING, if something can be  done,  or  at  least
assentful reaction if nothing can be done. In other  words,  statements  are
not pictures to be believed of how objects are in themselves.
      Yet, they seem to be. Indeed, it is argued  that  the  fact  that  the
house is on fire is purely objective, whether or not you or I  or  both  are
concerned about it, and that we cannot even be concerned about  it  but  for
the fact that it is objective. This is the common argument to show that  the
real object is the contemplator's and that it has  precedence  over  emotion
and action in that it makes them intelligible, rational.
      My answer is that all the  terms  here  are  terms  of  exposure  with
distances  of  exposure  to      speakers  and  hearers--and  this  is   the
dimension captured by ordinary language on  which  all  technical  languages
are parasitic. The terms  are  spoken  and  heard  tacitly  from  the  world
presently inhabited by speakers and hearers as vulnerable agents.  The  very
fact that I would have to give an explanation to a  puzzled  speaker,  if  I
assented that the house is on fire and would neither act or  react  further,
manifests this.
      The rejoinder is that instrumentality,  no  less  than  factuality  or
objectivity, is dependent on the physico-causal. RETURN
      "Your house is on fire." If I disagree with you,  I  am  not  referred
applicatively to the house  as  object  for  response.  Yet  I  am  referred
nominally,  by  the  meaning  of  the  words  (Searle's   understanding   of
language). Hence the illusion that cognitive judgment  is  reflective,  that
is,  based  on  sufficient  reason  that  counts  as  evidence,   that   its
propositional content is an image of the object, and that it is a matter  of
mere assent. [In fact, your situational exposure  (witness  called  to  act)
constituted the object for response, and the propositional  content  is  not
belived by  you,  it  is  instrumentally  spoken  and  to  be  heard  by  me
applicatively. Only  when  there  is  disagreement  does  the  propositional
content become disfigured into object of belief. But since both S and H  are
involved agents, if you convince me that the house is burning I will  regain
my  applicative  understanding].   Then  you  and  I,  the  discussants  who
disagree, are apparently placed as pure contemplators  in  relation  to  the
propositional object, the content of your assentful  judgment  and  of  your
statement for which you want my agreement. Now we have--at least  apparently
and  provisorily--transformed  the  object  for  response  into  object   of
inquiry, of detached  curiosity.  And  we  are  going  to  give  each  other
arguments that are purely 'objective,' regardless of interest. But  what  we
are considering here is a propositional, conceptual object  to  be  affirmed
or denied.
      Now, if you 'demonstrate' to me that the house which  is  mine  is  on
fire (for after all we both live in the same society in  which  such  things
as  ownership  are  institutional  facts),  at  that  very  moment  I   will
understand applicatively as vulnerable agent, because that is the way  I  am
situated. In other words, our discussion  is  not  theoretical  inquiry  but
deliberation deliberation  is  not  detached  or  objective,  it  is  merely
postponed action. No, let us be more precise. The point I was going to  make
is  that  philosophers  (not  common  people),  who  attribute  foundational
primacy to the propositional  discourse  of  inquiry  and  consequently  see
truth as a quality of judgments consisting  in  correspondence  to  reality,
are going to be in the position of disagreement you and I are concerning  my
house on fire, and are going to divorce forever truth from action.
      But, to return to deliberation and be more  precise,  deliberation  is
for the purpose of appropriate [according to who the  subject  is,  even  if
for the most part the subject has been made] action and involves not only  a
more desirable future state but also its possibility. As Aris  says,  we  do
not deliberate  about  what  will  necessarily  happen,  or  about  what  is
impossible, or what does not depend on us, be it because there are no  means
or because the means are not available. In other words, deliberation is  not
only constrained by the principle of desirability but  also  by  what  Freud
called "the principle of reality." Thus, there is a sense in  which  reality
has to be taken into account. And is not reality independent from desire?
      That reality  is  independent  from  and  conditions  desire,  whereas
desire is dependent upon and does not condition reality seems to  me  to  be
one of the sacred presuppositions made  possible  by  the  independence  and
primacy of scientific, purely inquisitive, non-deliberative inquiry.  But  I
want to discuss the point.
      For starters, my instinct tells  me  that,  at  least  in  one  sense,
reality and desire are dialectically related and without logical  precedence
over each other. Thus, in realizing our desired  technical  projects--for  a
splendid instance, agriculture--, which would not have become real  if  they
had not been previosuly desired and projected, we bring about  a  previously
non-existing reality which will make possible projects wich were  previously
impossible, like the project of staying in a place instead of migrating.
      Secondly, the philosophical or  contemplative  mode  of  discourse  as
paradigmatic, against which I am arguing, has the insurmountable problem  of
the passage from facts to values, from reality to  desired  reality  brought
about through action, for it  divorces  truth  from  action.  THIS  MODE  OF
DISCOURSE IS NOT OF EXPOSED AGENT BUT CONTEMPLATIVE,  AND  IT  MAKES  ITSELF
FOUNDATIONAL, INDEPENDENT OF INTERESTS OF EXPOSURE.
      Thirdly--and this is the  thorniest  part--even  the  hardest  of  the
sciences do not yield THEORETICAL reality,  for  their  practitioners  speak
and hear applicatively, both  in  the  particular  cases  and  even  in  the
general laws. Indeed, the general laws take advantage of natural--strict  or
less strict regularities--and are spoken and  heard  applicatively  to  deal
with particular  cases.  Even  in  geometry,  the  theorems  are  understood
applicatively to theoretical problems  (remember  that  Kuhn  realizes  that
'the same' principles are not understood exactly in the same way in  physics
and chemistry), which themselves develop  practical  attitudes  to  confront
perceptual givens applicatively and bring about desired states of  affairs--
for instance, the equitable, peaceful redistribution  of  owned  land  after
the yearly inundations by the Nile. The sciences are technical,  specialized
languages that make possible technical,  specialized  practices,  and  which
are developed for the sake of the practices.
      The issue remains however whether reality  constrains  and  conditions
desire.  Is  there  not  a  basic  constraining  passivity  of   desire,   a
fundamental, brute, surd coefficient of adversity or favorableness  in  what
we might call "matter," and even in living human matter, what we might  call
"the body"? Is this not reflected, for instance, in the  difference  between
discovering in the sciences, relatively freely inventing in the  novel,  and
freely inventing in the arts, between discovering the past  in  history  and
inventing the past in a novel,  between  dreaming  and  projecting,  between
knowing and imagining?
      That there is a passivity seems beyond  doubt,  and  the  agent  would
ignore this passivity in practice at one's own risk. If I think that  having
water and imagining that I have water to drink are no different, I  will  be
in trouble. If I  do not believe that my house is  burning  when  it  is,  I
will lose my house. However,  the  real  question  is  whether  or  not  the
passivity  is  fundamentally  constraining  in  the  significant  sense   of
separating truth from practice and making  possible  the  discovery  of  the
truth independently  from  practice  and  practical  interests  and  as  its
necessary grounds is the important question. What is the answer?
      Some lessons  may  be  learned  from  Sartre's  attempt  to  make  the
passivity  non-constraining  and  dependent  on  the  agent.  First  of  all
passivity is a term that refers primarily to the agent, not to the  thinker,
so that it cannot  be  even  conceived,  much  less  determined  as  to  its
character and extent, by a mere contemplator.  Secondly,  the  passivity  of
the agent to what surrounds him or her, which is the other side  of  his  or
her activity on what surrounds him or her, both hinders and  makes  possible
the having projects rather than mere wishes as well as  the  realization  of
the projects rather than the mere imagining on  the  agent's  part.  So  the
passivity of the agent to the activity of his or  her  surroundings  is  not
constraining  in  the  sense  of  the  objection   which   relies   in   the
disinterested desire to know and  conceives  of  reality  as  immutable  and
simply  there  to  be  discovered.  If  there  were  not  certain   constant
environmental features agriculture could not be possible, and knowing  those
features is a condition for making agriculture into an  end,  but  if  there
were  not  certain  desired  states  of  affairs  the  features  that   make
agriculture possible would have  never  become  known.  Thus,  there  is  no
primacy of knowing over acting  or  of  acting  over  knowing;  there  is  a
dialectic, and the movement toward realization  in  a  particular  case  can
start indifferently either from knowing or from desiring-dreaming.
