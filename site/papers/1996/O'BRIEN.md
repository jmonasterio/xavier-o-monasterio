David J. O'Brien, From the Heart of the American Church:

Catholic higher Education and American Culture (Orbis Books, Maryknoll, New York 1994).

Ch. 1: Autobiography and Catholic Higher Education

- pp. 4-7: Rather than beginning with the abstract ideas of university and of Catholic university, he prefers to begin with the concrete experience he has of the Catholic university in America.

- pp. 5-7: Pope John XXIII distinguished between the unchanging fundamental truths of faith and the manner in which they were expressed, which could and should change. This was a liberating distinction for the author and for many other Catholic intellectuals, who wanted to engage as Catholics the issues of their times: racism, poverty, nuclear weapons. He became a Catholic liberal, a historically conscious believer who was aware that the fact and the content of belief is always in part culturally determined, and who has to carry out "a balancing act between faith and history," between absolutism and the abandonment of faith .

- pp. 7-11: He made his "discovery of the liberating utility, and the hidden dangers, of historical consciousness at the U of Rochester," where he went after four years at Notre Dame. After fifteen years of absolutistic Catholic education, this was a liberating discovery; it was also a disturbing discovery, for if all ideals are culturally conditioned, where is the ground on which to stand firm? Anyway, he decided to cling to faith and reason, to "search for a middle ground..., one with enough power and moral force to enable us to play a significant role on the historical stage." He came to see himself as historian, writer, and college professor in the setting of the Catholic social movement, and he has spent his professional life studying and reflecting on Catholic universities .   

- pp 7-11: His discovery of the historical dimension of faith (by contrast to his previous a-historical, a-temporal conception. Catholicism, and its attendant problem of relativism-skepticism.

Ch. 2: Catholicism, American Style

- pp. 16-20: Debates about Catholic higher education in the USA tend to ignore the Americanness of American Catholicism. There was a time when "God, Country, and Notre Dame" appear to him, and to most other Catholics, an unproblemmatic conjunction. The taken-for-granted blend of Catholic and American righteousness which once shaped our church and our universities was shattered by the Vietnam war and the earthquakes of the 1960.

Catholic higher education became a vaguely uneasy and strangely confused culture in search of an identity.

- pp. 20-22: A triple pattern of change: (1) Improved income, education, and status of American Catholics, amply documented by Greely. (2) Vatican II blurred the boundaries with nonCatholic Christians  and with all persons, and it recentered the people of God in the midst of human history. (3) The cultural upheaval of the sixties: new religious pluralism, sexual revolution, racial crisis, war in Vietnam, all of which conspired to undermine the innocent conjunction of Catholicism with patriotism. New critical distance between Church and Country.

- pp. 22-24: The combination of these three streams of change left in their wake a new and more complicated American Catholicism: consensus and community are harder to find, self-conscious choice has become the norm, people have been thrown on their own religious and spiritual resources--what Greely has called "do-it-yourself" Catholicism, and others less sympathetic have called "cafeteria Catholicism." In order to understand Catholic higher education  one has to make sense of this new, very diverse, sometimes conflicted American church.

- pp. 24-28: Historians have made sense of this in different ways. Most of them agree that the Catholic Church has moved toward assimilation in America, but not all of them are sure that the Church has kept "its own tradition, its own social memory, its own special understanding of reality. It is fair to say that this pessimistic view, once confined to a small minority, has now become a near consensus. Adaptation to American culture has gone too far." But this pessimistic view is not indisputable. People like Timothy L. Smith and the author himself see the present, americanized state of the Church as a progress. Living in isolation from the rest of America was not normal. "Perhaps Catholics were seduced by secularism or capitalism or materialism. But I suspect that Catholics also wanted America, and they wanted and needed the openness and honesty made possible by Vatican II. They broke out of the subculture more than they watched it disintegrate.

- pp. 29-31: After Vatican II, faced with emerging global pluralism, Catholics now seek a balance between 'indigenization' within diverse cultures, with its risk of fragmenting the universal Church, and the unity of the Church, achieved by the authority of Rome. In the USA this effort to come to terms with pluralism is intensified by our distinctive emphasis on individualism and freedom, which makes Rome nervous and Catholic identity especially precarious. According to Avery Dulles, Vatican II was a compromise between the church as a hierarchical divine society oriented toward eternal life and a newer version of the church as servant of the human community. In his view, the latter position weighed too much and endangered the very existence of a distinctive Catholic church and community. People as diverse as Dulles, Daniel Berrigan and Ralph Martin agree that the Church and the Catholic university, as well as most middle-class Catholics, have been compromised by immersion in the American culture. To become again true disciples, they should take a resolute stand against many commonly accepted axioms. Subject to the conflicting pressures of  those who salute Americanization as a progress and those who lament it, Catholic schools are beset by uncertainty about their mission.

pp. 31-34: Americanization is not easily reversed, and Americanism should not be easily discarded. It is one thing to insist upon the independence of the church, it is quite another to make that church the sole or dominant center of value in any particular culture. The church is at the same time a community of faith and a constituent element of its particular world. Hence the church and its members must necessarily be bilingual and bicultural, capable of forming communities of conscience and conviction but capable as well of sharing with others responsibility for neighborhood and nation.

Ch. 3: Ex Corde Ecclesiae Americana

- pp. 36-7: In 1884 the bishops moved toward Catholic schools, because public education encouraged indifference and pluralism (not secularism, at the time) . (Reinforced by the triumph of ultramontanism in Europe).

- pp. 42-43: The radical difference in the Catholic college curriculum was not theology or history but philosophy, which was defined as the only 'Catholic philosophy.' Religion was rarely taught for academic credit. Only in 1939 did reformers led by JJ. C. Murray begin urging the introduction of theology courses as the basis for the undergraduate curriculum . All agreed with Murray that neither philosophy nor theology admitted any error.

- p. 44: From the start, colleges and universities changed the curriculum  to meet the demands of changing constituencies, especially to meet admissions requirements of secular professional schools . Adoption of credit hours, introduction of social sciences , schools of education . Only strong programs in neo-scholastic philosophy allowed continued emphasis on Catholic culture and curriculum integration . More lay faculty formed in non-Catholic professional schools.

- p. 47: The drive for excellence. "We brought back more than credentials. We also brought those structures of specialized learning that had worried academics from the start. The triumph of the graduate school . For most of us, our intellectual homeland was our discipline... papers and conferences. We preferred graduate courses and teaching undergraduate majors. Other courses should provide introduction to the methodology of our discipline rather than examine its place in our culture or in relation to other knowledge. We saw ourselves as apostles of serious intellectual work against antiquarian, amateurish, moralistic, mediocre teaching .

- p. 48: Theology to the center . The drive for excellence meant imitating the prevailing academic models: departmental divisions and academic specialization. This threatened to leave philosophy and theology attached to the academic body like a vermiform appendix, a vestigial remnant . These departments were supported by Catholic administrations. Before long the newly diverse philosophy departments lost their way and theology was left alone to provide the diminishing intellectual component of Catholic higher education .

Ch. 4: The Catholic Academic Revolution

- p. 52: In 1967 St Louis and Notre Dame led the way to separate incorporation . Two  distinct entities: university and religious community. Property and charters were turned to boards of trustees.

- pp.53-55: Curran and other lost battles ( Dayton ) against academic freedom in theology and philosophy.

- p. 56: Conclusion: " The dynamics of appointments, rewards and faculty development reinforced the values of academic professionalism while not supporting goals more appropriate to the Catholicity of a university. Thus rather than developing a synthesis of academic professionalism with concern for questions of ultimate moral and social importance, Marquette has allowed academic professionalism to displace Catholicism as the dominant value orientation within the faculty . " There was a gap between what universities were doing and their explanation of what they were doing .

- pp. 57-9: Separate incorporation meant that decisions would from now on be made within the institution, through its own procedures, and that external agencies would have no role. Thinking the relationship to the Church came after the incorporation: autonomy, academic freedom, but not secularization because of voluntary choice to study theology as a link to the Church . Only Rome was not happy with this arrangement that left the "Catholic character" a matter of a voluntary policy .

- p. 62: The case Curran concluded in 1989.

Ch. 5: What is Catholic about Catholic Higher Education

- 69-72: Statistics about Catholic colleges.

- p. 72: Three publics: Like the hospitals, the Catholic college and university stands at the intersection of three overlapping publics--professional, political and religious--within each of which it has responsibilities.

- pp. 73-76: RESULTS: Catholic colleges render significant  services to each of these publics. They continue to provide quality education to Catholics, they provide intellectual and cultural resources for the American Church, and they often provide special services to the local churches in which they live. They have not yet made major intellectual contributions to American academic life . The intellectual preparation of priests, religious, and lay ministers is inadequate; networks of Catholic intellectuals are weak, and there have been sharp drops in circulation of intelligent Catholic journals. Arguments about multiculturalism and political correctness are as common on Catholic campuses as on on others; so are problems of drug and alcohol abuse and sexual harassment. Catholic schools struggle, as others do, to do the right thing in areas of race and gender. From wrestling with intercollegiate athletics to lobbying government agencies, Catholic schools are part of the academic mainstream. A century of social Catholic teaching provides unique resources for developing social and business and professional ethics. So far Catholics have not been particularly creative or even energetic about incorporating these resources into curriculum .

Ch. 6: From Secularization to Americanism

- p. 97-100: The slippery path . Catholic schools are on the same path as protestant schools were: "from denominational to generic xtianity, then to vaguely defined religious values and finally to total secularization" (Avery Dulles) . Historian Philip Gleason has been the most consistent exponent of this critical position.

James Burtchaell, the Notre Dame theologian and former provost, working on the experience of Vanderbilt U, argues for a Catholic majority of teachers and close ties with the Church in theology.

- pp. 100-2: Michael Buckley,, S.J. puts the emphasis more on the intellectual life. The Catholic U is one of the communities which are integral to the universal church, in which the Church strives to relate all human culture to the gospel. Theology, when it presents itself as one discipline among others has already given the game away . But Buckley's views cannot easily be translated into policy.

- 111-186: Americanism reborn. Turner maintains that Americanization has meant secularization. William Shea, the most americanizer, practically promotes the absorption of faith by the American way and ideals. Robert Imbelli opposes him. Vatican II  led to chaos and religious promiscuity; it "deprived Catholics of the language with which they had habitually ordered their religious universe and through which they had articulated their own self-understanding... its loss created a widespread sense of anomie, a loss of personal and corporate religious identity." The post-conciliar quest is a quest for a new universal language , "a comprehensive language which combats our subtle tendencies to partiality, to the erection of our immediate and often distorted experiences into the measure of the whole." Shea speaks of dual loyalties, but Imbelli denies that they are of equal value: "For among the loyalties of the Xtian, is there not a paramount, indeed identity-defining loyalty: top Xt and his gospel as proclaimed by the Church?" Shea maintains that American pluralism  is good . When students realize that a fellow student is not Xtian, they revise their previous conviction that the other religion is inferior. And by taking seriously the other's belief, they come to see their own for what it is--belief--whereas previously they had thought it was religious knowledge.

Shea writes: "Catholic higher education is dealing with American culture as it in fact is in  all its plurality, and it has chosen to take that plurality into itself in terms of students and faculty."

Ch. 7: A Case Study: Holy Cross and Its Mission Statement

- 124-26: Holy Cross' brief Mission Statement

- 127-130: Background for mission statement. In 1976 the President and the Board of trustees overturned a number of faculty decisions on tenure ad insisted on their ultimate authority, required to protect the Jesuit and Catholic character of the college. They won by exhaustion of the faculty . Later President and trustees agreed that their most important goal was to make H Cross an excellent liberal arts college, comparable to the best, but they insisted that it should remain visibly Catholic and Jesuit . - In the 1950s the Jesuits and laypeople were dedicated teachers. Later the new faculty found their greatest satisfaction in publications and public presentations. This sense of identity was reinforced by the College's criteria for hiring and tenuring, where research and publication weighed more than teaching. The growing professionalization of the faculty endangered any notion of institutional mission. Trustees left departments alone for hiring and tenuring. In 1986 Dean Schroth, S.J., directed the economic's department to fill an open post with an economist interested in Catholic social teaching, then prominent because of the Bishop's letter on the U.S. economy. A majority of the faculty signed a letter of protest and Schroth resigned; he cited this as an example of the erosion of Catholic identity.

- 130-33: "Liberal arts," "Jesuit," and "Catholic" lacked authoritative definition, we opted for an inclusive conversation about them. We found that there were fewer 'religious exclusivists' and 'secular exclusivists' than we had expected.

- 136: The draft of the statement reached the faculty at the end of the 88-89 academic year. Long debate and conversation. Vote in 1991, after more debate. Most notable was the absence of a strong voice calling for a clearer commitment to Catholic identity

One of the trustees argued: "To get faculty approval, you must have watered this down. F.e., I found no reference to God here. Why is that?" My first reaction, which almost slipped out, was "I never thought of God."

- 137-140: Some strengths of the statement : Approved by faculty. Is honest: facing up to the lack of agreement about liberal arts education. Makes conversation and dialogue central. Repeats themes drawn from the recent history of higher education: diversity, toleration, dialogue . Highlights shared responsibility. For those who care about tradition the statement is promising. Almost no member of the faculty surrendered to what Dupré has called the twins temptations of unrestricted freedom of choice or practical vocational education. For Dupré, "humanist education has from its inception consisted in conversation," with 3 central purposes : (1) to transmit "the spiritual legacy in which our culture has found its identity." (2) the critical examination of "those questions which every thoughtful person spontaneously asks." (3) to provide moral education. According to him, religious instruction does not consist only in communicating the essentials of a doctrinal tradition, but "even more, in assisting the student in extending the religious attitude based on that tradition into all areas of existence . This goal requires contemplation as well as study and "liberation from the manifold concerns and petty desires that obstruct the way to the other."

More problematic areas : No serious concern for th e Catholic-ecclesial (as distinct from the intellectual and cultural) elements of the college's life, to say nothing of the God question. Much complacency about the departmental/disciplinary structure that dominates higher education . Few resources for interdisciplinary programs. Hiring done by departments, departments tenure people  on the basis of publication, theology departments included. That system shortchanges general education, including education for effective citizenship. In religion it contributes to an a-intellectual pietism, in politics to a culture of complaint and irresponsibility .

Ch. 9: Catholics as Intellectuals

- 157-61: Ambivalence. Despite its sacramental heritage, modern Catholicism in practice assigned religious value only to specifically religious activities, which embraces intimate relationships and family values, but not 'secular activities' like politics or work . In colleges this resulted in the subordination of academic to pastoral considerations, and the lack of appreciation of the achievement of academic excellence as relevant to the Catholic character of colleges. Religion confined to church and private life . The Americanists tried and failed.

- 161-165: Vatican II and Intellectual life. Reaction vs Enlightenment and ultramontanism in the 19th, reduced scholarship to apologetics and drove restless spirits out of the church or into a Catholic underground. Institutional intellectuals subordinated truth to ecclesiastical power. In the 20th the legitimacy of this anti-modernist ideology was undermined, but its organizational legacy of surveillance and fear remained intact until Vatican II with church-state separation, religious liberty, relations Xtians-nonXtians, openesss, dialogue, human solidarity, service of Church to world.

Intellectuals seemed to have a place in the Church, but there were problems :

(1) Vatican II and post-vaticanist reformers were not always aware of possible implications of moving from nonhistorical orthodoxy to a historical adaptive understanding of faith and life . People like Thomas Sheehan (1984) drew the consequence that Catholic theologians had undermined the foundational doctrine of the Resurrection and that it was time to acknowledge that fact. He argued for a religionless Christianity of the type advocated earlier by secular theology and echoing liberal Protestantism of the 19th. This was taken by some Catholic intellectuals as the logical outcome of the Council, as it had been ironically pointed out by diehard Thomistic philosophers and theologians.

(2) Defensive reaction within the Church. Charismatic renewal, scriptural and doctrinal fundamentalism, unreflective loyalty to 'traditional morality' have plagued modernizing reformers . The Catholic intellectual feels caught between doctrinaire ecclesiastical authorities and religious populism or evangelicalism. Add to this mix the sharp drop in religious vocations and the bishop-led retreat of seminary education from university centers: the gap between theology after 'the critical turn' and popular piety becomes enormous . As for campus ministry, it does a fine job with liturgy, community building, and pastoral counselling, but gets lower marks for communicating the idea that Catholicism makes intellectual claims . They often have strained relations with theology or religious studies departments, which often look upon pastoral ministers as second-class citizens within the university.

(3) Intellectuals, freed from roles defined by the church, naturally relocated in the university, where they contributed to upgrade learning but could as easily become prisoners of the institution as they were previously of the church. The danger is increased by specialized, professionalized knowledge. Thus Catholic sociologists no longer felt the need for their own organization, which suggests that they do not need to engage each other in reflection on the relationship between faith and scholarly work; religion, for sociologists as for physicians and bricklayers, was a matter of church and family . The supposed secularization of the schools had its counterpart in the apparent secularization of scholarship.

- 165-9: Theology and religious studies . Hessburgh says that when theology was expelled from universities in Europe it lost its public influence. Departments of theology and religious studies thrive today in Catholic universities with budgets that compare favorably with those of other departments. Yet, in a poll the majority of theologians felt that their department should not have special responsibility for the Catholic character of the institution. Theologians are highly critical of administrations' and Rome's attempt to assert control over the teaching of Catholic theology . Multiple, often conflicting assignments. Bishops see theology departments' main task "to make theology relevant to all human knowledge and all human knowledge relevant to theology." They are supposed to provide access to Catholic theology, establish theology as a respectable discipline, offer an intellectually coherent approach to fundamental religious and moral questions, encourage diversity by offering courses on world religions. All this trying to avoid privileging Catholicism and not to displease conservatives. At the same time, battle between theology and religious studies. And even where theology won, theology became an academic subject "just like any other subject in the sense that competence to teach it had nothing to do with the personal religious commitment of the teacher." Faculty in non-Catholic research universities disdain theology, which they regard as confessional, a subject appropriate for seminaries, not for universities. Faculty in private church-related universities are less hostile, but even there many favor an approach to religious studies modeled on the sciences, being descriptive and explanatory, except in ethics, where moral advocacy is encouraged . For religious-studies-minded people, a department that privileges xtianity or at least western categories for interpreting religion is unacceptable. Theologians in general do not return this animosity, but many of them are worried by the tendency of Catholic schools to hire faculty from secular universities and interdenominational seminaries with little knowledge of the Catholic tradition, classical languages, patristics, church history and history of Christian theology . A symptom of the still existing gap between Catholic religious scholarship and the academic mainstream: Major university professors did not include Theological Studies among the top 15 journals, while it is first among professors in Catholic schools; conversely, The Journal of the American Academy of  Religion , tops among the secular faculty, does not make the Catholic list.

C h. 10: Disciples and Citizens

- 193-95: Discipleship, yes; citizenship, not so sure. Many speak more passionately of Xtian discipleship than of responsible citizenship . Shortcircuit of conversation and persuasion : 'this is a Catholic school, this is what the church teaches, therefore we should have a program.' Radical language of evangelicalism, applying Gospel mandates directly to complex economic and political questions without filtering them through hard-headed social and economical analysis and assessment of political options. This direct move from faith to social judgment also limits their ability to find common ground with non-xtians or less fervent xtians . In matters of social justice and world peace, its central questions are posed not in terms of natural law or papal teaching but in terms of scripture: "What would Jesus do?" This leads to a stance of advocacy on behalf of the poor and of pacifism and nonviolent resistance to the arms race and nuclear weapons .

- 195-199: The claims of citizenship. If these 'extremists' are isolated, it is not their fault. Too often the Catholic college has allowed a segregation of academic and religious matters that goes far beyond the authentic respect for the autonomy of the secular recognized by Vatican II. It marginalizes religion and institutionalizes political and civic irresponsibility. Here, far more than in the area of supposed secularization, lies the real failure of nerve . Many theologians and also bishops in the religious sections of their pastorals speak of discipleship in countercultural terms, while in the middle sections call Catholics to participate in the common public life of which they speak in realistic, humanistic terms of natural law and human rights. Sort of duality between discipleship and citizenship . The bishops referred to the danger of the laity living a "spiritually schizophrenic existence," with the values of their faith bearing little relationship to their lives as workers, consumers, and citizens, yet they themselves were unable to integrate the seemingly conflicting demands of citizenship and discipleship. The same with colleges: Landy points out that Jesuits blame their schools for failing to embody Jesuit ideals when in fact 'we do not know how to bring them about.' Jesuits 'do not talk enough about what it will take as a group to construct the kind of institutions we want...' Few put the major share of their efforts into programs that could really shape institutions to embody faith and justice ideals . Only 8% of Jesuits in graduate studies are in fields related directly to social and political problems.

- 198-202: Christian bilingualism. The church that educates for discipleship must also educate for citizenship . Coleman, S.J. thinks that this has been done poorly. Xtians tend to make a direct move from faith to politics, with solutions that have little reference to social science and political reality. Hence cycles of intense moralism-activism followed by otherworldly defeatism . Active citizenship, seeking solutions to public problems amid multiple pluralism, diverse interests, and varying perspectives requires constant negotiation and compromise. Faith can provide, vision, motivation and norms to guide judgment; it can name an evil and demand action, but it cannot of itself prescribe solutions and mandate choices. Yet choices will be made, and in a democratic society everybody is responsible for those choices . Religion is crucially important for democracy because "it affirms those mores which alone anchor a republic and make democracy possible. Xtianity's greatest achievement was a human community beyond national societies, thus witnessing to the possibilities of a larger human family . But the duties of people to their country have been inadequately defined and promoted. Xtianity lacks a coherent, fully developed Xtian theory of citizenship, a specifically xtian sense of sacredness or vocational meaning of membership in a particular nation. It is hard for xtians to see their public role as disciples in a society marked by pluralism and expressive individualism. Hence Coleman's insistence in a "pedagogy for discipleship and citizenship."

- 202-203: In its origin, the American university was torn between three conflicting visions of its purpose: research for its own sake, the preservation of traditional culture, and public service. By World War I the commitment to service had disappeared except in the rhetoric; increasing emphasis on standards of entry, certification, internal modes of reward and status, intensified specialization, clienteles interested in security and advancement.

- 209: In colleges, the justice-imperative is usually connected with community service programs for students and courses in ethics in departments and schools. Limited impact of such programs. And there are problems with the courses in ethics as well. The ethics involved are usually personal and professional. They highlight the moral problems that people face in particular fields and locate the center in the person's conscience. They speak less about the institutional settings within which such decision-making takes place and rarely address the politics of decision-making in business, law, medicine, or society at large. The accent is more on avoiding evil than in doing good. A second problem is that ethics is philosophical, not theological; it tends to separate value questions from meaning or faith questions . In the process, decision makers (including professors and students) are abstracted from communities of meaning and value, churches, parties and movements. The person who makes ethical evaluations is not a Protestant, a Catholic, a Jew, a fundamentalist or a liberal, a republican or a democrat. Detached from communities of meaning, dropped into structures that are simply given, the abstract person finds that justice is a matter of choosing the best available options . One tends to adapt passively to changing historical circumstances.

- 212-213: So far, Catholic restorationists offer few interesting ideas about how Catholic colleges might do better than others in overcoming the fragmentation of disciplines or the exclusion of religion from most areas of academic research and teaching. If there is a consensus, it is that schools return to theology, to Catholic theology. But academic theology is as prone to superspecialization and methodological obsession as any other discipline, and shows no greater affinity for gral education and core curriculum than English or history; that much academic theology has lost almost all contact with the church's pastoral life, a chasm which on some campuses places theologians at odds with the campus ministry.

IMPORTANT SECTIONS

- 191: Remarkable uniformity of graduate instruction... Questions of value only rarely find their way into the classroom or comprehensive exam. Questions of meaning, religious or philosophical, never do . Here, in graduate education, research and disciplinary self-definition is the real battleground of modern higher education (at least for its theory; for its practice the battleground will be federal and state legislatures). Unless Catholic intellectuals confront this reality, there really will be no available third choice between confessional restoration and all-out integration into the seamless web of academic culture . Yet, oddly, critics concentrate almost entirely on theology, giving almost no attention to other disciplines, except for complaints about the absence of religion, and make almost no suggestion about the alternative graduate programs or faculty development .

- 182: The difficulty also resides in the fabric of contemporary American culture and, in particular, in the problem of pluralism. Talking about religion, especially about religious differences,

is no easy matter . - The basic dilemma of pluralism is always the same: how to create a society in which people 'who espouse widely different and incompatible conceptions of the good life' can 'live together peaceably... enjoying the same political status and engaging in the same economic relationships.' To sustain the American experiment, a number of idea clusters were once widely believed: social contract theory; the 'inalienable rights' of autonomous individuals; reason as a source of principles to govern the common life; and the common good served by relegating nonrational beliefs to private life. None of those idea systems has been rejected, but each is now in doubt. John Courtney Murray forty years ago found wide agreement on the formula 'we hold these truths.' Today, consensus is harder to locate.

- 183: The last idea--that the common good requires a secular, neutral public space--arouses deep concern among Christians:

'While a religious understanding of life naturally seeks cultural wholeness and expression in the public realm, the ideology of modern liberalism relegates religion to the private domain in an attempt to construct a public square devoid of religious conviction and independent of any particular conception of the good . In a liberal society, all sectarian beliefs will be tolerated of course--but only if those who hold them agree that all such beliefs are equally valid and equally irrelevant to matters of public concern . The common life is to be conducted on essentially secular principles to which we will all subscribe and agree by virtue of our common share in reason' (Lee Hardy, see note 37 of ch 9).

- 183: Three options then arise ... One is to reaffirm the particular values of particular groups: the church must be the church, the sectarian option that informs explicitly confessional schools. The second... is to live and let live and allow various marketplaces to adjudicate all claims; the church should concentrate on religion and heal the pains of personal and family life, leaving public and academic life alone. Theology and pastoral ministry may be welcome on campus, but they are options for individuals with little purchase on the real business of the university .

The third option... to take up the shared work of reconstructing the foundations of public discourse and thus the common life ... This latter, American Catholic option, was the goal of the Americanists, from Isaac Hecker to John Courtney Murray. The third voice--the voice of Murray and Hessburgh and Gaudium et Spes and the pastoral letters on war and economics--is muted, its advocates fewer and unsure of themselves and its place in the debate shrinking.

183-4: What is at stake here is not so much Catholic identity as religion's role in intellectual life and American culture. Both denominational and sectarian options, alert in their criticism of liberalism, surrender to its demands by abandoning the public task... Christian critics (though not only Christian critics) rarely challenge the structures of liberalism: the creation of religiously neutral institutions within which the concrete demands of the common life must be negotiated. What they do object to is liberalism's resolution of the religious question. By excluding particular religious views and thus particular views of the good that are at all contested, liberalism restricts religious concerns to private life. In doing so it drains public life of meaning and undermines its own need for civic virtue. This resolution depended more than was recognized in the past on a liberal ideology, perhaps even a liberal religion, rooted in the Enlightenment faith in reason. Thus, what was common--reason--could provide the foundation for truly common enterprises like education and, though the critics say this less often, war and preparation for war .

- 184: More and more, the secular, neutral resolution of the problem of pluralism seems inadequate. Mark Schwehn, f.e., revives the idea that the virtues of public life are nurtured best in communities sustained by a common narrative about life and history, so that society should nurture, nor marginalize, such communities. As many of the foundations of the scientific domination of the academy come into question , the notion returns that all learning is a 'generically human enterprise,' often carried out best within communities. Communities of conversation are an asset for the educational enterprise as well, in part because recent work suggests the communal character of human inquiry, but also because such virtues as faith, humility, self-denial, and charity make a positive contribution to the advancement of knowledge... 'I believe that the most promising argument for an integral relationship between religion and higher education can be made through a demonstration that the practice of certain spiritual values is and always has been essential to the process of learning, even within the secular academy,' Schwehn argues. Connecting faith and work, religion and real life, is the unfinished and usually unmentioned agenda.

PP. 104 to 111 : An epistemological revolution: a new faith in scientific method as the only path to truth, rendering denominational colleges an anachronism. This dissolved the Protestant colleges, sent theology out of the campus into seminaries, leaving the Church with Sunday schools as the only means of educating the laity. Increasing tendency to separate intellect from religious commitment, so that the latter lost theological coherence and was described in purely spiritual and increasingly psychological terms . Religion, to remain legitimate, allowed itself to be privatized .

The problems of Catholic higher education are part of the larger problems of contemporary culture: the fragmentation of knowledge and the subjectivism-relativism-skepticism  that infects much recent theory in the humanities and the social sciences. According to Turner, if we are ever to reestablish communication among the scattered realms of scholarship, ever to find common ground of discourse and build anew the lost unity of knowledge, we will do so only by constructing a new intellectual tradition. MY POSITION

1) A Unifying Core or Discipline

Supposedly, a Catholic university has a unifying, distinctive vision of the world (and of the way to respond to

it). But does it?

The German universities

The unifying core were the Geisteswissenschaften , hermeneutic disciplines, under the umbrella of philosophy as reflective and strictly cognitive . Theology, like medicine and law, was only one of the professional schools. However the latter two, as practically applicative of a number of hermeneutic disciplines, could be organically related to the secular knowledge characteristic of the hermeneutic disciplines (much as today philosophy and sociology are considered part of the basis for law studies),  whereas theology , because of its commitment to revelation as its basis, could not . Nonetheless, because of the religious Protestant character of the German state of which the university was an institution, the faculty of theology remained in most German universities, where it fulfilled the intellectual function of the Catholic seminary of forming candidates for pastoral duties, but where it could not function as the unifying discipline. In addition, under the positivistic pressure and the appearance of the human sciences in the nineteenth century, philosophy as such lost its unifying function and became one among many other disciplines without a clearly defined subject-matter and function . Thus the Enlightenment failed in its synthetic or unifying project, no less than Scholasticism .

The American Protestant colleges and universities

Under the pressure of the secular Enlightenment and the scientism of 19th century positivism, Protestant colleges ended up expelling theology to the seminaries, and at best kept secular Departments of philosophy as one among the many other Departments, and with a poorly defined subject-matter at that. Thus no unifying discipline. Institutionalization of fragmentation, professionalization, and secularization of knowledge . Secularization of the universitiesw themselves.

The American Catholic colleges and universities

When they began, by the end of the 19th, they followed for the most part the German model that prevailed in kthe USA. This corresponded roughly to the secular model of the Enlightenment. Theology , at the time Catholic colleges began (in the 1880s?), had already been excluded, or was in the process of being excluded, from the American Protestant universities, as it did not fit within the Enlightenment model of the university and of knowledge, which was supposed to be purely rational and secular (that is, to exclude revelation as such). Philosophy , therefore, not theology, became the integrating discipline in Catholic colleges, that is the discipline which was supposed to justify and transmit the Catholic distinctive vision.  Indeed, philosophy was understood in Catholic universities, following Aquinas, as subordinated to and limited by faith. Concretely, it consisted in an already elaborated, canonical system of metaphysical and ethical positions ("theses"), which were deemed to be rationally demonstrable and incontrovertible, though each of them was disputed by the "adversaries."

When philosophy became truly secular in the early 60s thanks to the education of teachers of philosophy in secular universities, that is, when what was taught in Catholic colleges under the name of philosophy was neither subordinated to nor limited by faith but fully autonomous and supreme, it could not convey the distinctive Catholic vision . By then, Departments of theology already existed in Catholic universities, and they were decreed to be the apt conveyors of the Catholic vision. But by then these Departments wanted to be scientific rather than committed to faith in order to have right of citizenship in the university as conceived by the Enlightenment, so they neither would nor could fulfill the unifying function assigned them.

Given this failure as well as the postmodern ethical skepticism, in recent times the humanities, under the guise of "general education," (the remainder of the German Geisteswissenschaften ) have been assigned to fulfill the unifying role, but only as places where ethical values are talked about and discussed, not practiced, and where they are talked about as idiosyncratic preferences, under a (positivist) view of reason and knowledge that subjectivizes values and makes the choice of goals purely arbitrary, with the consequent religious and ethical fragmentation among the members of the university, limited only and only to an extent by the shifting wind of political correctness. Clearly, under these conditions the humanities cannot fulfill a genuinely unifying role, much less can such uncommitted humanities convey a distinctive Catholic vision.

CONCLUSION: American Catholic colleges and universities are in no position, as universities, to convey a unified-unifiying vision, much less a Catholic vision, assuming that that would be part of their specific task. Furthermore, given the prevailing notion of knowledge bequeathed to us by the Enlightenment under which they work and which they contribute to perpetuate as institutions, in ethical and religious matters they cannot even claim to convey knowledge, but only beliefs or opinions which akre ultimately arbitrary.

The only alternative I can see (whether or not it can be practically realized is another matter) is to offer a persuasive conception of knowledge .  

2) Modernity and Post-modernity

Modernity is the Enlightenment: Universal Reason (common, rational religion, or at least, common rational mores).

Post-modernity is the failure of the Enlightenment's Reason, with the attendant fragmentation, relativism,  and skeptical cultural disarray.

3) The Enligthtenment is the counterpart of Scholalsticism

Both share the same notions of reason and of revelation and the same conception of faith, of philosophy, and of theology. For the Scholastics, theology is committed and the result of commitment; philosophy is not committed, purely rational assent.

For the Enlightenment people, reason leads to purely rational, objective assent.

4) Research universities and the Enlightenments notion of knowledge

Research universities incarnate the Enlightenment's idea of reason, modified by scientific positivism, nowadays softened and complicated by the  all-pervasive "textuality" of postmodern hermeneutics.

Such universities correspond to the Enlightenment's notion of knowledge as justified intellectual assent, that is, assent proportionate to the evidence (and experimentally confirmed?). This notion corresponds to the "Reason" universally shared by normal human beings, and leads to universal agreement on reality. Knowledge, therefore, is purely objective, that is, guided exclusively by the desire to know and the scientific methodology proper to each particular discipline, dictated by the nature of its subject-matter. Consequently, no questions of meaning or value have a legitimate place within the university as such, for those are questions of interested agents and can have no objective answers. Since the humanities (the humaniores disciplinae ) explore human conduct, that is, the conduct of involved vulnerable agents, and since this notion of knowledge knowledge filters down and excludes the emotions, there is no legitimate place for the humanities within the university. To carve a place for themselves as a discipline they have to mimic the natural sciences. Same with theology and philosophy. Theology has to play the part of a disinterested, uncommitted discipline.

As for Philosophy , assuming it has a subject-matter of its own left to it by the human sciences, it has to play the same part. Thus, professionally, no less than theology, it excludes questions of meaning, which are questions of interested, involved, vulnerable agents (and which are always raised by agents who belong to a culture in which certain answers to those questions prevail).

[Clearly, in Catholic universities, such questions have to be included. Partly, because it has been tradtionally and wrongly assumed that they are objective questions with objective, purely rational, answers. Partly, too, because to exclude these matters would be to exclude anything distinctly Catholic from the curriculum and from the field of knowledge. This is why, against the notion of knowledge that Catholic univkerksities as well as other universities perpetuate, the humanities, philosophy and theology are kept, in the hope that questions of meaning and of religious meaning will be raisedk, though it is understood--and according to the prevailing notion of knowledge has to be granted--that the answers to such questions are mere blind interjections like "Ouf!".

5) Catholic universities and Graduate Schools

Catholic colleges have a dual, often not clearly distinguished, and conflictive function: the acquisition of knowledge as understood after the environment within each discipline, and the education of human beings.

The division by departments, which corresponds to the division by disciplines, presupposes that departments are there to prepare students for graduate studies in the discipline --that is, to do research and enrich the knowledge in the field. This corresponds to the Enlightnement's conception of knowledge.

Consequently, Catholic universities, like all others, define excellence and organize themselves by departments, as Research universities do, in order to have their undergraduate students qualify for admittance to graduate programs in research universities. Reserach for its own  sake, disinterested acquisition of knowledge .

Hence, Catholic universities are indistinguishable from Research universities in their graduate programs, when they have them, except, for the most part, in economic resources and recongized quality of faculty and students. They receive their faculty from research universities, that is, poeople who are coopted by the Research universities' idea of knowledge and of the university, and they explicitly emulate and imitate Research universities.

( This is the "Complacency" with the current structure, critiziced by O'Brien. WHAT OTHER CHOICE, AT LEAST THEORETICALLY, ONCE THE ENLIGHTENMENT'S VIEW OF KNOWLEDGE IS TAKEN FOR GRANTED AND INSTITUTIONALIZED?).

However, the undergraduate programs of Catholic universities are schizophrenic. For they are not ONLY meant to prepare students for graduate research (that is, disinterested, professionaly limited knowledge) or for professions within the city in which some of the solving-problem abilities or the value-content-free skills acquired in the university find application. Indeed, Catholic universities, by virtue of their very tradition, much more so than their public or their secular counteparts, are under pressure to integrate knowledge with definite religious Catholic faith and morality. (HERE IS WHERE "THE CATHOLIC INTELLECTUAL TRADITION" ENTERS, AS FAR AS UNIVKERSITIES ARE CONCERNED).

Most of them, in one way or another, attempt to respond to this demand as well as they can. However, they do it within the institutional structure of research universities inspired by the conception of knowledge of Enlightenment-positivism, wich entails the separation of knowledge from practice and the subjectification of values (the choice of goals is individual and arbitrary).

To cope with the fragmentation of knowledge, they resort to the so-called general education , imitating in this many of the secular universities. The ability of general education to favor the integration of knowledge, given the prevailing conditions, is highly disputable, to say the least. In addition, since most of the burden of general education as far as values are concerned falls on the humanities (Theology, philosophy, literature, history) the faculty in these departments lead schizophrenic lives, feel overburdened, unprofessional, and unprepared for the sort of courses they are required to conduct, and for the most part avoid serious commitment to general education. In addition, their respective disciplines do not prepare them to deal with interdisciplinary subjects, much less to bring to bear on the subjects a Catholic tradition which may not be theirs at all or which they themselvkes have never seriously explored and to which many of them feel no strong loyalty.

When values are discussed , they are discussed haphazardly, with the discussion coordinated by professors who very often do not consider 'professional' or genuinely 'academic' such discussion, and who cannot help but favoring their own--often politically correct--priorities, such as feminism, multiculturalism, etc. Furthermore, as underscored by O'Brien(p. 209), values are discussed as matters of personal conscience, (that is, of personal preference), for unlike genuinely academic matters values are not susceptible of real knowledge; they are discussed freom the philoso-phical, not from the religious point of view; and the institutional settings are not discussed but taken for granted.

This is often complemented with sporadic extracurricular public lectures on religion and religious issues , and with the availability of religious services and of religious activities promoted by campus ministry , but   often without intellectual underpinning in support. As for students' conduct, there are no characteristically Catholic requirements, and, as notekd by O'Brien, Catholic colleges are indistinguishakle from their counterparts in this regard.

5) Does becoming American amount to becoming secular, for Catholic universities?

WHY DOES THE EXPRESSION 'CATHOLIC UNIVERSITY' SOUND TO MANY PEOPLE, EVEN TO CATHOLICS, AS AN OXYMORON? THE ANSWER IS: BECAUSE A CERTAIN NOTION OF KNOWLEDGE, NAMELY, THE ONE BEQUEATHED TO US BY THE ENLIGHTENMENT AND MODIFIED BY POSITIVISM, IS TAKEN FOR GRANTED.

The American universities with which the Catholic universities had to compete on their terms when they decided to abandon the ghetto and become "great" American universities were secular. The public universities were secular because of the religious neutrality of the American state. The Protestant universities had become secular in the very process of becoming great universities. Why?

The university was by definition, as a reaction against the previous dispensation, autonomous as reason was supposed to be, and exclusively devoted to the acquisition and impartation of knowledge. Knowledge was supposed to be objective, accessible to neutral reason. No wonder that, given this conception of knowledge and of the university, the expression "Catholic university" sounds as an oxymoron . So had "Protestant university" sounded--and not by accident. But this is to say that becoming a university by competing with the already existing American universities on their own terms amounted not only to becoming secular, but to becoming an institution that incarnated and perpetuated a conception of knowledge and of the university that made it impossible to be a Catholic intellectual, a Catholic knowe r .
