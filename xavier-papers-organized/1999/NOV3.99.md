Not only was there a separation of the theoretical from the practical, but also a subordination of the latter to the former. And even the subordination tends to disappear from Descartes on, when the theoretical, the purely epistemological, chases out the practical from the field of professional philosophy. Both Hume and Kant reject soft, unprofessional philosophy (Monasterio).

I don’t act appropriately toward X because I know X independently of acting appropriately. Rather I know X in acting appropriately in regard of it (Monasterio, “2000-feb-2-Skepticism.”

“How does it happen that thinking is sometimes followed by action and sometimes not, sometimes by motion and sometimes not?” (Aristotle, De Motu Animalium 701a7-8; Martha Nussbaum, p.185).

“Aris says two anti-Platonic things about practical deliberation. First, that it is not and cannot be scientific... Second, that the appropriate criterion of correct choice is a thoroughly human being, the person of practical wisdom... These two features ... are connected, clearly: for the reason why good deliberation is not scientific is that it is not the way this model good judge goes about deliberating... [ N.B. The very idea of considering whether or not deliberation is scientific indicates that Aris persists in viewing practical knowledge as knowledge , for only to knowledge can the predicates “scientific” and ‘not scientific’ be applied ]. - ... the Platonic aspiration to universality, precision and stable control is met and criticized in Aris’s more ‘yielding’ and flexible conception of RESPONSIVE PERCEPTION... an examination of Aris’s claims that practical deliberation must be anthropocentric, concerning itself with the human good rather than with the good simpliciter. “To rely on a theory for understanding nature is to interiorize it. For we are attending from the theory to things seen in its light, and are aware of the theory, while thus using it in terms of the spectacle that it serves to explain. This is why mathematical theory can be learned only by practicing its application: its true knowledge lies in our ability to use it ” (M Polanyi, The Tacit Dimension , Anchor Books, p. 17).

“In Metaphysics , Delta 7, Aris lists, among others, the following meanings of Being...: (a) Being is that which is divided by the ten categories [i.e. that which is either a substance or a quality (of a substance), or a quantity (of a substance), or a relation (of substances), etc.]. (b) Being is that which signifies the truth of a proposition. - Medieval thinkers (and I believe they were quite accurate in their reading of Aristotle) made this passage the basis for a distinction between (1) ens reale , real Being, and (2) ens rationis , conceptual being. The first term defines a real entity as that which has actual and positive existence as an object in the world –ultimately, a primary substance or one of its attributes or relations (2) The second sense includes entities that do not have real and positive existence in the first sense. Thus, if I can assert a true proposition about a non-existing thing, then in some sense it has Being, since it is not a pure non-entity. ‘A centaur is half man half horse’ is a true proposition; and obviously a centaur is an entity of some kind, though not a really existing one. A centaur is an entity about which at least one true proposition may be uttered. Since propositions do not exist without minds to interpret them, the centaur is an ens rationis – a conceptual or mental entity. - In the light of this distinction, the medieval tradition treats all negative entities (including privations) as entia rationis , conceptual entities. The example of privation used by St Thomas is blindness. Blindness is not an ens reale ; the eye is real and the cataract or other substance that may grow over it to cause blindness is real; but the blindness itself, the not-seeing, is an entity only in the sense that the proposition ‘The eye does not see’ is true –that is, asserts what is the case if we happen to be talking about a blind man” (William Barrett, Irrational Man , Doubleday 1962, Appendix I, pp. 286-287).

“The difficulties that philosophers have had with the fact of transcendence are due to the fallacy of  locating the existence of a thing strictly within its boundaries. The philosopher Whitehead aptly baptized this “the fallacy of simple location’: the fallacy of common sense that a thing is located just where it is, i.e., confined to its own skin. Whitehead locates the traditional source of this fallacy in the Aristotelian doctrine of ‘primary substance.’ A primary substance, for Aris, is a concrete individual thing, this chair or this table, in which qualities and attributes inhere. Primary substances are thus the ultimate supposita of reality. With the development of the new physics in the modern period Aris’s primary substances become transformed into material substances: bits of stuff extended in space. And there the difficulties with transcendence become acute. - This is as far as Whitehead takes the problem historically. Heidegger carries the historical interpretation from Aris’s theory of primary substance back to the way in which the Greeks posed the problem of Being itself. Instead of sticking with the question, what is Being?, in the sense of , what is the Is?, the Greeks replaced this by the question, what are the things-which-are? They forgot the verb in Being and asked only after the noun. Thus the question of Being became transformed into the question of reality, and reality is an object of some kind or other. Plato in his Republic makes the question of questions: What is really real? And the ‘really real’ things he takes to be the eternal objects, the Ideas. Aris brings Plato down to earth and takes as the really real things the primary substances. Being as the Is, as the enveloping presence within which things stand forth and are revealed, is forgotten. When things alone are real, then transcendence becomes a purely ‘subjective’ phenomenon, a curious quirk of the human mind, something altogether unique and inexplicable in a world of things. Hence Heidegger’s contention, which is borne out by the whole history of modern philosophy, that the problem of human transcendence can be adequately met only if we go back to the question of Being and pose this question in a radically different way from that of the tradition” (William Barrett, What is Existentialism? , Grove Press 1964, pp.160-161).

“Kierkegaard is not an ontologist who is attempting to describe the general structures of human existence. If he does happen to make clear such structures, as notably in The concept of Dread and The Sickness unto Death , it is incidental to his main purpose, which is religious. Where he touches upon the problem of human esixstence, which is irreducibly mine, it is to insist that this existence, which is irreducibly mine, can never be exhausted by any concept or system of concepts. This is the point of his famous polemic against Hegel in the Concluding Scientific Postscript , where he insists that thought and existence can never be identical. The living individual, incomplete and changing, can never be swallowed up in ay complete static system. Man, as Karl Jaspers puts it, is always more than the sum of his thoughts—and cannot, we may add, be summed up in the thoughts of any behavioral science. K is talking about the actual existing individual. Thought deals with universals, or possibilities , but the existence that we have to live, and not merely think, is one of gritty actuality . The speculative philosopher may deal with sheer possibilities as if he were, in Plato’s words, a spectator of all time and existence. This is the attitude of detached contemplation, the attitude of the aesthete, as K calls it. But in life, as we live it, we cannot sit as mere spectators; we have to be involved, and as soon as we have made the decision of involvement, we have passed from a purely aesthetic to an ethical stage. In so doing, we become, as the common phrase puts it, a real person ; we have put off childish things and become serious. ‘Reality’ for K is not a highfalutin metaphysical problem at all: it has to do simply with the seriousness of the existing individual who has become real by committing himself to some ethical and religious decision. - Certain key points emerge from this Kierkegaardian treatment of existence: - (1) Actuality is prior to possibility. Life is not an affair of hypotheticals and contrapositives, but of actual situations. I might have loved any number of other women, but the fact is that I love this woman and the hypotheticals vanish like vapors before this actual enmeshment. (2) The point of view of human existence is not that of the philosophical theorist, but of the ethical and religious teacher who seeks to summon other individuals to the task of becoming a self. In this sense Kierkegaard refers to Socrates as his model and hero: not Socrates the dialectician but Socrates the human gadfly who sought to sting his fellow Athenians into following the only life that is worth living by a man. Philosophy is not primarily a doctrine, but rather an appeal and summons to achieve an authentic life. - (3) Kierkegaard’s treatment of existence does not deal at all with the strictly philosophical problem of ‘transcendence’ that has bedeviled modern philosophy since Descartes; the problem of how man, circumscribed in time and space [like all things or primary substances], can nevertheless comprehend a world beyond himself. - On all these three points exactly the opposite, as we shall see, has to be said of Heidegger. - K’s treatment of human existence really turns on the traditional problem of universals and particulars. And his conclusion, if we may transpose it into more general and traditional language [which we may not, for K is not doing speculative metaphysics (my observation)] is that the individual cannot be reduced to a mere nexus or set of universals. This is a traditional position that goes back to Aris. K’s contribution is to dramatize the human  discrepancy between the universal and particular that makes up the pathos of life itself–the discrepancy between the law and the individual case, between moral obligation and individual need, between the abstract psychological explanation and the individual mystery that we ourselves are. - His insistence that actuality is prior to possibility is equally traditional and Aristotelian, having come down through medieval philosophy in the form of the scholastic axiom ‘ Actus prius and melius est quam potentia.’ According to the Aristotelian view, possibilities, or potentialities, exist only because there are actual entities with certain actual structures or characteristics that can operate in a certain way. For example, I can walk –walking is a possibility for me– because I do actually exist and do actually have a pair of legs that function. The Aristotelian position here is simply the lucid expression of common sense insofar as it takes its cue from things or objects and regards psotentialities as characteristic of things. - This traditional character of K’s views leads Heidegger to say that K is no ‘thinker’ –in the sense in which Heidegger himself is a thinker seeking to recast the traditional understanding of Being– but a religious writer; and, further, that K’s ontology, as ontology, never went beyond what he had learned from Trendelenburg’s commentaries on Aris. Heidegger does not mean to denigrate K by these remarks, but rather to indicate the limitations that really define his greatness. - Heidegger, on the contrary, is not a religious writer, and his philosophy is neither appeal nor exhortation but the strictly philosophical meditation of a thinker seeking to recast the meaning of the fundamental terms of Western metaphysics. How untraditional his thought is in comparison with K’s can be seen in his reversal of the dictum that actuality is prior to possibility. At the very beginning of Being and Time he tells us: possibility is higher than (prior to) actuality. Man is a creature whose being is constituted by possibilities. Human existence cannot be understood from actual objects, but only from possibilities which, immediately and as such, make that existence what it is” (William Barrett, Ibid. , pp. 151-155).   

“ The characteristic and the consequent problem, which Kant shares with all of his predecessors (save a small handful) is that of separating theoretical and practical reason, a separation which is simply Kant’s version of the Aristotelian separation of episteme and Sophia from phronesis . When Hegel characterized his attempt to complete the Kantian transcendental project in his Science of Logic as a domain of ultimate intelligibility in which ‘in the silent region of thought which has come to itself and communes only with itself, the interests which move the lives of races and of individuals are hushed, ” he simultaneously signaled the ultimate success and ultimate failure not only of Kant, but of the whole western philosophical tradition up to his time. The ultimate success was the presentation of the complete speech concerning the forms or categories which underlie the intelligibility of what-is. The project which had begun in sixth-century Greece and had dominated the tradition was complete. The ultimate failure was just this completion. The ultimate failure was the embodiment of the nihilism which had begun explicitly with Plato’s recognition of Socrates’ inability to determine how either the polis or he himself were to be made healthy again. The ideal of a unity is still present in the dialogues; that wisdom should be a guide and not knowledge for its own sake is still the principle which, in a Pythagorean manner, forms the principle of the dialogues. But it is only an ideal, and with Aristotle’s condemnation of dialectic and dialogue in the Topics and his discussion of knowledge and action in the Nicomachean Ethics , that ideal is laid to rest, enshrined but no longer active. Sophia is not phronesis. While Kant’s treatment of the relation between theory and praxis was different from Aristotle’s or Descartes’ or Hegel’s, it was nevertheless quite the same: a separation between what knowledge we can acquire once we are out of the cave and what can be done with it once we have come back into the cave. The fundamental conditions for the possibility of experience and knowledge are neither applicable to actions taken as actions nor do they lead to a theory which will be applicable.. . - ... Why?... In Kant there is a tyranny of the categories which, in the end, makes them, transcendent to experience... - It is not really the categories themselves which have tyrannized philosophy, but rather the Categories taken in the context of a very basic assumption, viz., that what is said about categories –including the cataloguing them, the discussion of their relations to each other and to reality, and the principle of their discovery and ordering– must be universally true for all reality without qualification. That is to say, whether ‘reality’ refers to experience as in Kant or to what-is as in Aristotle, predications must have the characteristic of being universally applicable to reality. - That is the assumption, so elegantly expressed when Hegel refers to the domain of the categories as one in which all interests are hushed; for interests mean relativity , and relativity means non-universality or, at best, a universality which must constantly and in different ways be limited through qualification. If there is to be intelligibility, this basic assumption reads, then it must be or derive from an intelligibility of the whole, without distinction . That assumed principle is at the heart of the Platonic nihilism: Euthyphro, for instance cannot bring himself to discard his interest in the particular, without distinction. - That serious, existential act in which he is at the moment deeply involved in order to discuss with Socrates, on a high level of abstraction, from that act, the meaning of piety. Socrates, on the other hand, utterly fails, in respect both to his own subsequent actions and to those of Euthyphro, to make sense of the eidos , to make sense of hosion kat’ auto , precisely because kath’auto has nothing to do with piety in relation to some particular instance. Euthyphro’s existential pragmatic definition of piety, on the other hand, is rooted in that instance. ‘If a man understands how to say and do, in prayer and sacrifice, what is pleasing to the gods, this is piety; and it is this sort of observance that preserves both private households and the public life of the polis . But the opposite of what is pleasing to the gods is impiety; and it is this that overturns everything.’ In a way in which Socrates’ ‘piety in relation to itself’ ( kath auto ) could never reflect a human concern for the good, Euthyphro’s notion of piety reflects and is determined by that: a concern for the good, healthy city and a good, healthy household which can be achieved only through piety... Euthyphro keeps this interest in the good before himself and Socrates; and through it he has a pragmatic principle of verification for the truth and goodness of his own actions . Euthyphro’s definition of and interest in piety reflects and is reflected in his recognition of the human condition, a condition which Socrates, by his own confession is seeking to flee and is incompetent to deal with; for Euthyphro’s pragmatic conception of piety entails that one cannot be certain a priori that one has acted piously, i.e., that there is no certainty at the heart of the human condition, but that we must await events if we are to come to know the truth about our actions. And Euthyphro has the courage to face this. - Euthyphro’s demand for a categorial discussion that will lead to a criterion useable to guide him in this action is opposed by the Socratic demand for a category which will make intelligible, a priori, all instances of such actions. The latter culminates at best in a spectatorial position with respect to human existence, the former in a participatory position . The marked inferiority of the spectatorial is clearly indicated because, by definition, the eide transcend the thisness and interests of a specific action, thus making the particular action indistinguishable from other actions, since it is in the thisness and interests that an action has meaning for the participant. And for the participant, who must decide and act, the specificity, not the generalities, is of the real essence of the act. We have thus come again to the distinction between knowledge of the whole and praxis ” (Joseph C. Flay, “Categories of Comportment,” in Categories: A Colloquium , The Penn State U. 1978, pp. 127-129).

Transcending truth as correspondence

I return to the view that our accepted idea of truth as (pictorial) correspondence --and the consequent idea of (pictorial) consistency and oneness of truth-- arise from considering judgments-statements made in the past, or presently made but not by the agent but by others, or from considering written statements, or from zeroing in on the content of statements when we disagree with others on perceptual matters. The correspondence assumed or looked for is between the ‘content’ of the statement and ‘reality.’ This idea of truth, when the epistemological theory of knowledge as correspondence becomes explicit, and of truth as knowledge of correspondence, of necessary correspondence, is also applied (at least by the scholastics, and probably by Aris too, given his view that animals know) to perception as vision (material truth) . However, this idea of judgments-statements as representational originates from a contemplator’s view of knowledge, but doesn’t fit the case of the agent who states something (for response) to others or to himself, or who is the addressee of a statement of that kind, or who is confronted with an object that demands a decision on his part .* Contrary to what the accepted idea of knowledge and truth assumes, where presence and what is present occupy the central place, the agent doesn’t observe what is the case, believes it to be the case, and then either acts or doesn’t, as if action were accidental to perception.  It would be accidental if indeed perception were the deed of a disinterested, unsituated observer. But such is not the case. In the ordinary mode of experience (by contrast to the observational or the reflective modes), perception is, so to say, structured by desire or repulsion and is an implicit project of action–a project which may or may not be changed by the deliberative process.

[* I make statements such as “The hammer is in the other room,” not out of the blue but only when they are relevant for action. Either I or the other need the hammer and is looking for it. And the perlocutionary effect of the judgment-statement is to go to the other room in search of the hammer. Only when there is disagreement or disbelief on the addressee’s part does the illocutionary effect of language appear as different from the perlocutionary and as a condition of it, and it is this supposed illocutionary effect that becomes primary and renders possible and persuasive the representational view of knowledge].

The needful, and consequently desirous, agent is exposed to the surrounding world and, on the basis of what is possible given his desires and the conditions imposed by his exposure to the object, deliberates and acts in view of the future.* In other words, in the ordinary rather than in the scientific mode, WE DON’T PERCEIVE OBJECTIVELY, OBSERVATIONALLY; WE ‘PERCEIVE-FROM’ ‘SUBSIDIARILY’(cf. Polanyi), APPLICATIVELY, WITH IMPLICIT REFERENCE TO OUR EXPOSED BODY’S POSSIBILITIES AND TRANSCENDING THE PRESENT TOWARD THE PROJECTED FUTURE TO BE REALIZED –much as explained by Sartre concerning my-body-for-me .

Yet there is room, too, for the observational, scientific knowledge of my body-physiological-thing, for there is room for the scientific, observational mode, independently of whether this observational mode is or is not underpinned by a practical interest (as  medical knowledge actually is). This knowledge amounts to the grasp of the objectively observed -explained regularities in the functioning of the body as a living thing (medical physiology) and in the way these functions are either favorably or unfavorably affected by internal and environmental factors. Hence pathology and preventive medicine. However, this knowledge, too, is practical and applicative, for it is in the applicative moment that the knowledgeable physician who desires the patient’s health uses ‘the map’ to attain the desired state of health.

There is room, too, for the observational, scientific knowledge of the inanimate world, as shown by physics, chemistry, engineering, etc . Here the objectively observed-explained regularities are stricter –more precise or “ akribos ,” as Aris would say-- because the activity of inanimates is more regular than the activity of animates, given that the latter’s activity is teleological and adaptive and, therefore, changes from situation to situation. Hence the precision of “the hard sciences” and the technology based on such regularities. Galileo placed this knowledge of the inanimate world on the scientific orbit. But his very experiments on predicting the fall of bodies on inclined planes already indicate the applicative moment, confirmed by the technology developed to attain the desired human ends as well as by the achievements of engineering when applying the knowledge. In short, the knowledge as it is expressed in mathematical formulae in books is no more than a sort of map of regularities to be used instrumentally at the appropriate time of exposure, not a reproduction; it, therefore, is knowledge only insofar as it is susceptible of being appropriated in the applicative moment by human beings, for only then does it function as knowledge is meant to function, namely, in successful action. As explained by Polanyi,

“To rely on a theory for understanding nature is to interiorize it. For we are attending from the theory to things seen in its light, and are aware of the theory, while thus using it in terms of the spectacle that it serves to explain. This is why mathematical theory can be learned only by practicing its application: its true knowledge lies in our ability to use it ” ( The Tacit Dimension , Anchor Books, p. 17).

My point is that what we call knowledge is applicative and is actual knowledge only when it actually functions as such in the moment of application; hence all knowledge is practical, where there is no room for correspondence, at least not as the pictorial, static, final product of the process of encountering. These alternative notions of knowledge and truth –if indeed such notions remain viable-- are what I want to explore here.*

[* One interiorizes (mostly by automatic imitation) not only scientific theories but cultural ways of responding to the world in typical situations. Actually, such is our original introduction to the world, which is linguistic and unavoidably tribal, since our original language is the language of our tribe. An example of this form of internalization is found in our changed attitudes in regard to blacks and women. We have learned, literally, to see them differently in order to respond to them differently, or –what is equivalent– to respond to them differently in order to see them differently . This seeing may be at odds with our previously customary way of seeing, so that a positive effort is required to act against our misshaped sensibility until a new habitual mode is developed].

At any rate, personal deliberation is never excluded, in principle. Obviously, the character of the deliberator, in part shaped by his or her culture, will be an enormously important factor in the deliberation and its outcome. (Only the young Sartre could be innocent enough to assume that when we reflect we take our distances not only from the world to which we have to respond but also from desirous, exposed self. If I am a hungry body in a concentration camp, the weight of my hunger in my deliberation whether or not I’ll steal the bread of my comrade is heavy and, at the limit, may even be determinant. Can I always neutralize my hungry self when I make my choice? Sartre ignores that it is this hungry self who deliberates and chooses, not the ideal, disengaged reflector he invents).

Aristotle on practical and speculative knowledge

According to Aris, animals are capable only of practical knowledge, whereas people are capable of both practical and speculative knowledge. (The following references are to Metaphysics I, 1, i, 1-35, as commented by Aquinas).

In animals : “Cognitio inest ipsis animalibus non propter ipsum cognoscere, sed propter necessitatem actionis” (14). Notice that this knowledge that inest animalibus seems to assume that it is something inside them or in them that corresponds to (or is intentionally identical with) the thing out there rather, than being a disposition to act. Be that as it may, in the case of animals knowledge is practical in that it is exclusively related to action rather than to assent-vision with a possible further connection with action. Moreover, since animal knowledge is practical, for it is propter necessitatem actionis, its span is limited to what is useful to the particular species and is acquired for the sake of action, unlike the speculative knowledge of people which aims at reality as such --the whole of reality, potentially--and is for the sake of knowing.

Thus, a first Aristotelian distinction is that speculative knowledge is exclusively guided by the disinterested desire to know, whereas practical knowledge is guided by other interested desires. As a consequence, practical knowledge terminates in action under the appropriate circumstances, whereas speculative knowledge terminates in knowledge and the natural satisfaction that knowing  produces.

In people , by contrast: Cum sensus ad duo in nobis deserivant , sc. ad cognitionem rerum et ad utilitatem vitae , diliguntur a nobis propter seipsos, in quantum cognoscitivi sunt, et etiam propter hoc, quod utilitatem ad vitam conferunt. Hence we can know not only what is useful to us and not only insofar as it is useful to us and not only when it is immediately useful to us, but whatever is, that is, whatever is real, that is, whatever actually exists and whatever is intelligible. ( Notice the confusion between real-existent and real-intelligible –two very different meanings of the word ‘est’ as used by Aris. That the intelligible, which is by nature not particular but universal, is real is a relic of Aris’s Platonism. For Aris, physical “gravity” would be a universal reality found in particular bodies, for otherwise there would be no correspondence and, consequently, no knowledge of gravity and no truth to the judgments concerning gravitational phenomena). For we not only have senses to grasp what is useful to us but also an intellect, which intuits and reasons and judges in view of attaining reality as such.

What is not inherited knowledge in animals, that is, a disposition to act in a certain way in the appropriate circumstances, which makes them act automatically ad utilitatem vitae, is acquired through experimentum . This acquired knowledge amounts, it seems, to regular dispositions to act in order to obtain their instinctive goals according to their instinctive needs. When superior animals are confronted with objects, they know, thanks to repeated past experience, how to act in their regard according to their natural fears and desires, for they already have developed definite expectations, much as the child learns which effects attend which causes,  internalizes that as an agent, and uses it applicatively. As Hume would say, from objects that look similar animals and people automatically expect similar effects. This becomes for superior animals a law for their action, which they are capable of accommodating to different circumstances according to the comparative weight of their needs, as shown by their animal ‘prudence’ which is not genuinely deliberative. This seems to be what Aris has in mind when he speaks of animal knowledge, which is manifested by the way animals act. However, the fact that he uses the term ‘knowledge’ for both the speculative and the practical, and that he thinks of animal knowledge as something IN the animal –something that “inest”– suggests that Aris conceives animal knowledge also as similar or identical to the object, although it is not known as similar or identical by the animal. Hence the ‘MATERIAL KNOWLEDGE’ of the Scholastics, which is manifested through action but which is –and inest in the animal as-- knowledge because of its resemblance to what is the case.  

In people , however, experimentum is of a different kind than in animals: “Supra memoriam autem in hominibus proximum est experimentum, quod quaedam animalia [superiora] non participant nisi parum. Experimentum est ex collatione plurium singularium in memoria receptorum. Huiusmodi autem collatio est homini propria et pertinet ad vim cogitativam quae ratio particularis dicitur ” (15) (see Verbum pp. 30-31, note 146) .  

What is the difference between experimentum in animals and in people? Don’t animals also ‘universalize’ through experience? Do they only know concrete particulars? Yes, animals ‘universalize,’so to say, but not in the same way as people, because animals know only propter necessitatem actionis and in relation to acting to satisfy their needs. Sensibus et memoria animalia ad aliquid consuescunt prosequendum vel vitandum , inde est quod aliquid experimenti, licet parum, participare videntur.  Experimentum leads to ‘universalization.’ It seems, however, that in animals, this universalization is purely practical in the form of expectation and disposition to act, whereas in people there is actual grasp (and sometimes formulation) of what is common to all the cases. (At least this is the only way in which I can clarify what Aris says). This grasp obtains in two ways: it is grasp of “ratio particularis,” of what actually happens that is common to all cases, and of “ratio universalis,” of the universal explanatory cause of what happens.

Homines autem supra experimentum, quod pertinet ad rationem particularem, habent rationem universalem, per quam vivunt, sicut per id quod est principale in eis” (15). “Sicut autem se habet experimentum ad rationem particularem, et consuetudo ad memoriam in animalibus, ita se habet ars ad rationem universalem” (16). Thus experimentum in people is different from experimentum in animals in that people grasp “ratio particularis” (the “quia”) through the comparison effected by the “vis cogitativa,” and also grasp the “ratio universalis” (the “propter quid” [through intellectual abstraction and judgment?].    

Thus animals know only those objects connected with the satisfaction of their needs, and they know them only in order to pursue or avoid them . What does this mean? To put it concretely, when a dog hears its master’s name, it hears it as the announcement of his imminent presence ( notice the reference to the future in all practical knowledge ) and, according to its desire, starts looking for him. Now, before her awakening to language, Hellen Keller knew like a dog. Her past experience of regularities, guided by her instinctive desires and fears, created in her definite expectations. She ‘knew’ that she would be taken out when her hat was put on her. This is what experimentum in animals amounts to. Afterwards, however, she reached experimentum in its full human sense, which in its lower form is knowledge quia on the basis of vis cogitativa, and in its higher form is knowledge propter quid on the basis of understanding.

Whether Aquinas thinks of “ratio particularis” in terms of expectations a la Hume or rather as something that “inest homini” as a resemblant image of what is expected, is not totally clear. As shown by the following text, he realizes that the grasp of ratio particularis contributes to successful action; in other words, that it is applicative. However, since it also constitutes the basis for grasping the universal reason which per se is specular or speculative but is also connected with action, as we shall see, Aquinas’ position is ambiguous. (Actually, Aquinas seems to make the same mistake as Aristotle: all knowledge is specular, a matter of correspondence, whether the correspondence is formally known or not by the agent (for even animals, at least according to Aquinas, possess “material knowledge”), but, in addition, certain pieces of knowledge are practical in that they facilitate successful action ). This is how Aris explains the connection of knowledge with action, in people :

“... ex memoria in hominibus experimentum causatur. Modus autem causandi est iste: quia ex multis memoriis unius rei accipit homo experimentum de aliquo, quo experimento potens est ad facile et recte operandum . Et ideo quia potentiam recte et faciliter operandi praebet experimentum, videtur fere esse simili arti et scientiae. Est enim similitudo eo quod utrobique ex multis una acceptio alicujus rei sumitur. Dissimilitudo autem, quia per artem accipiuntur universalia, per experimentum singularia ” (17). “Ex experientia in hominibus fit scientia et ars... experimentum fit ex memoria. Nam sicut multis memoriis fit una experimentalis scientia, ita ex multis experimentis apprehensis fit universalis acceptio de omnibus similibus. Unde plus habet hoc ars quam experimentum, quia experimentum tantum circa singularia versatur” (18). “... quantum ad actum pertinet, experientia nihil videtur differre ab arte. Cum enim ad actionem venitur, tollitur differentia quae inter experimentum et artem erat per universale et singulare: quia sicut experimentum circa singularia operatur, ita et ars; unde praedicta differentia erat in cognoscendo tantum. Sed quamvis in modo operandi ars et experimentum non differant, quia utraque circa singularia operatur, differunt tamen in efficacia operandi. Nam experti magis proficiunt in operando illis qui habent rationem universalem artis sine experimento” (20) N.B. These sentences whow Aris’s confusion of the particular in the case of knowledge (resemblant) and in the case of action (object for response transcewnded toward the realization of the project).

Though Aquinas doesn’t put it in these terms, it seems that experimentum in its full human sense, not the parum of it found in animals, is connected with the human linguistic ability, which allows us to objectify in words what we know –primarily  the object  and secondarily the expectations we have of it, what Hume called “the powers” of the object (the object for response). By conceiving-stating the object [reduced to theoretical unity through abstraction (?)] we convert the object for response into object of knowledge, whereas for animals objects are only objects for response, respondenda . (Cum homo accipit in sua coginitione quod hae medicina contulit Socrati et Platoni tali infirmitati laborantibus, et multis aliis singularibus, quidquid sit illud, hoc ad experientiam pertinet; sed cum aliquis accipit quod hoc omnibus conferat in tali specie aegritudinis determinata, et secundum talem complexionem, sicut quod contulit febricitantibus et phlegamticis et cholericis, id iam ad artem pertinet. - Ex experientia in hominibus fit scientia (experimentalis) et ars. Experientia facit artem, sed inexperientia casum. Quod enim aliquis inexpertus recte operatur , a casu est. Modus autem, quo ars fit ex experimento, est idem cum modo praedicto, quo experimentum fit ex memoria. Nam sicut ex multis memoriis fit una experimentalis sicentia, ita ex multis experimentis apprehensis fit universalis acceptio de omnibus similibus. Unde plus hoc habet ars quam experimentum; quia experimentum tantum circa singularia versatur, ars autem circa universalia ).

Hellen Keller’s experience of a regularity, retained thanks to memory, contributed, as in the case of animals, to her faciliter et recte operandum, for it resulted in definite regular expectations. In people, however, unlike in animals, their linguistic capacity and their curiosity that is not limited to the immediately useful allow them to objectify verbally in the indicative rather than in the imperative, and to cogitate on what they perceive. In this way the object becomes conceptual, object to be considered and known, not merely presential and respondendum. By naming “water,” Helen Keller converted it into subject of predicates to be enriched through her own experience and the experience of others who shared it with her. This permits the acquisition of further knowledge for its own sake, that is, without immediate utility,  through further experiment. All this, which falls under the power of the vis cogitativa and concerns the ratio particularis, is already beyond the animal’s ken. But experimentum also leads to the ratio universalis. For, thanks to linguistic objectification, the universal reason for the regularities specified in the knowledge quia, that is, the explanatory cause of the regularities, is looked for and sometimes comes to be known. This is the knowledge propter quid , which is the deed of the intellect (see previous quotation on Socrates and Plato).

However, it is not only the grasp of the ratio particularis, of the quia, that is connected with action, according to Aris, but also the knowledge propter quid : “... quantum ad actum pertinet, experientia nihil videtur differre ab arte. Cum enim ad actionem venitur, tollitur differentia quae inter experimentum et artem erat per universale et singulare: quia sicut experimentum circa singularia operatur, ita et ars; unde praedicta differentia erat in cognoscendo tantum” (20). Notice that in this text knowledge “in cognoscendo”

and “quantum ad actum pertinet”are distinguished as two different things. This suggests that, in Aris’s view, knowledge is complete in itself by being similar or identical to its real object, and that action results from knowledge but is something different from and in addition to knowledge . Be that as it may, experimentum et ars are not connected with action in exactly the same way , for  knowledge propter quid , that is, knowledge of the ratio universalis, of the intelligible reason for the observed constancy or regularity, is not as important for action as knowledge of the ratio particularis: “Sed quamvis in modo operandi ars et experimentum non differant, quia utraque circa singularia operatur, differunt tamen in efficacia operandi. Nam experti magis proficiunt in operando illis qui habent rationem universalem artis sine experimento” (Ibid.). “Cuius causa est, quia actiones sunt circa singularia... Unde medicus non sanat hominem nisi per accidents, sed per se sanat Platonem et Socratem...” (21) “Unde cum ars sit universalium, experientia singularium, si aliquis habet rationem artis sine experientia, erit quidem perfectus in hoc quod universale cognoscat; sed quia ignorat singulare cum experimento careat, multoties in curando peccabit: quia curatio magis pertinet ad singulare quam ad universalem, cum ad hoc pertineat per se, ad illud per accidens (22). [N.B. The person who learns from practical experience knows applicatively, while the person who knows the universal understands as resemblant,and therefore misunderstands. The latter wouldn’t understand negatities or lacks, for nothing outside corresponds to them and, if a medievazl physician, would quote Aquinas to a blind man roaring with anguish: ‘Caecitas not habet esse in rebus” (Cf. William Barrett, Irrational Man, appendix 1, p. 289).

Aris explains the difference in efficiency between experimentum and ars by saying that actions are about particulars and ars about universals. Ars is perfect in that it knows the universal;  however, it ignores the singular, because the singular is related to the universal only per accidens. Indeed, the universal itself is purely intelligible and the fact that it is realized in a singular is accidental in that it doesn’t affect the universal. In my view, however, as I just explained, the case is different. The pure knowledge of the universal of which Aris speaks doesn’t arise from internalization of the experience of particulars (the way it was explained above by Polanyi), which is kapplicative. It arises from an abstract consideration of a regularity–or even a necessary regularity that makes the predication necessary-- by an observer who imagines that a real universal corresponds to the grasped universal. Consequently, that grasp doesn’t result in appropriate response (applicative knowledge); it is like the purely theoretical grasp of a geometrical theorem without the application to problems and later to actual situations. It is purely verbal understanding of what is said, as if what is said were a picture of what is the case, when in fact it is meant applicatively, instrumentally, in reference to object for response. What the inexperienced  lacks is real understanding that is applicative and arises out of internalization. It is like learning how to drive. The ‘doctrine’ is meaningful only as to be applied, like a map.  

To repeat. I think that Aris is wrong concerning the connection of ratio universalis, as he understands it , with action. For he understands ratio universalis as the grasp of an essence or a nature, much in the way  Socrates tried to understand piety: he wanted an essential definition that made the essence of piety –the Idea itself-- present to his intellect . If such is the case, however, then the connection of knowledge with action disappears since essences, refer to the intellect but not to desire (only concrete particulars, objects for response, refer to desire), and then there is no reason why the knowledge of essence would lead to action.

There is, however, another way of understanding ratio universalis of which I spoke above in reference to the physical sciences –a way of which Aris had no inkling. That was Galileo’s discovery, from which the so called scientific method, which is applicative rather than contemplative , took its origin. Galileo didn’t reach the essential definition of the fall of bodies on inclined planes. He discovered the concrete constant relationship in which the different factors involved in that fall stood to each other. This allowed him, because of the constancy opf the relationship, to predict each particular possible case of fall and, when possible, by controlling those factors, to produce at will the particular fall he wanted to produce. The same is the case with physicians. That physician has knowledge quia who knows what to do, when actually possible, to cure a particular (kind of) headache. Similarly, that physician has knowledge propter quid who knows why the drug he uses to cure the headache works and can, therefore, manipulate the factors involved to apply the drug more efficiently. Or, also, who knows what causes the headaches of that type, and can therefore correct the problem.

Let us return to Aris. If, in terms of action, then,  scientia is inferior to experimentum, on the other hand, Aristotle continues, there is a superiority of ars et scientia (knowledge propter quid ) over experimentum (knowledge quia) , for three reasons: (a)“Illi qui sciunt causam et propter quid, scientiores sunt et sapientiores illis qui ignorant causam, sed solum sciunt quia, (for they know the universal). Experti autem sciunt quia sed nesciunt propter quid. Artifices vero sciunt causam, et propter quid” (24).  (b) Nam habens artem potest disputando obviare his quae contra artem dicuntur, non autem habens experimentum” (23). (c) “... signum scientiae est, quod possit docere, quod est scientiam in alio causare. Artifices autem docere possunt, quia cum causas cognoscant, ex eis possunt demonstrare; demonstratio autem est syllogismus faciens scire, ut dicitur primo Posteriorum . Experti autem non possunt docere, quia non possunt ad scientiam perducere, cum causam ignorent. Et si ea quae experimento cognoscunt aliiis tradant, non recipient per modum scientiae, sed per modum opinionis vel credulitatis. Unde patet quod artifices sunt magis sapientes et scientes expertis” (29).

Let us be precise: knowledge of the ratio particularis is knowledge of a regularity observed and retained thanks to memory. It is knowledge of a constancy, such as bodies fall down, or whenever the hat is put on H. Keller this is followed by being taken out for a walk, or a certain infusion of herbs does away with headaches, or smoke accompanies fire, etc. These regularities are a matter of sensory observation or affective experience and memory. Both in animals and people these regularities result in practical expectations and habits of action, many of which expectations are not explicitly noticed unless they are frustrated and the habitual action doesn’t work. For instance, if a step is missing in the ladder I use habitually, I am likely to fall for lack of attention. I implicitly expected the step to be there, and this is manifested in my habitual action. There is, therefore, both in animals and people, a connection between experienced regularities under the form of practical expectations and actions, on the basis of observed-retained quia .  In animals, however, the expectations developed through experience (experimentum) are limited to what is useful to them, given their needs and instinctive aversions, for they are not curious about reality , they have not the unlimited, disinterested desire to know that Aris imagines people have . On the basis of these expectations animal prudence, which is not deliberative but regulated by instinct, is developed. People, however, are different. Because they possess language, the regularities are not merely expected but objectified, stated. In this sense, they become observed regularities, not merely lived as practical expectations . Furthermore, because of language, regularities observed and shared with us by others enrich us, give us a sort of vicarious, enlarged experimentum.   

This linguistic objectification deserves attention, for it is at the origin of the specular view of knowledge. When verbally objectified, the object for response ceases to be object for response or deliberation, at least immediately, and becomes object of consideration or object of thought to which one is not immediately exposed,   as Descartes neutralized his exposure when he meditated by his chimney. Thus the immediate connection between object and response is neutralized by the same token. And, given that one is not immediately exposed, desire and fear are inactive . Now, when this shift from object for deliberation and response to object of thought obtains, and the former is assimilated to the latter, the damage typical of traditional philosophy is done: THE IDEA OF knowledge as correspondence or intentional identity emerges, and the division of knowledge into speculative and practical as well as the ASSIMILATION AND subordination of the latter to the former become unavoidable. EXPOSURE AND LACK THEREOF ARE THE ANSWER TO ARIS’S QUESTION: WHY NOT ALL KNOWLEDGE IS FOLLOWED BY ACTION?

Notice, however, that the universal of which Aris speaks when he refers to knowledge propter quid is strictly intelligible, not a matter of observation and memory, whereas there is nothing strictly intelligible in the fact that water satisfies desire. It is a merely factual regularity. Now, we may be capable of cutting the connection between object and desire, between water and need, and then observe water scientifically, that is, objectively. BUT THE ABSTRACTION FROM THE CONNECTION IS AND SHOULD BE ONLY PROVISIONAL. Galileo’s study of the fall of bodies is an example of the useful abstraction or mental neutralization of the connection. But the law, when grasped and enunciated, is really a law for the agent’s action, and the agent is responsible for determining the moment of application and for applying the action correctly.

 

So much for Aris himself, or for Aris as seen by Aquinas . As it is clear from what precedes, I don’t want to deny that there are differences between people and animals. What I  deny is that knowledge is a sort of vision in the mind and that what is viewed corresponds to (or is intentionally identical with) what is outside there.  This is the philosophers’ radical mistake, which arises out of objectifying and contemplating statements in order to discriminate what is knowledge from what is opinion. It is about what is already congealed or objectified, about ‘the content’ of the statement, that the discrimination is attempted. But there is not such a thing in the agent who encounters the world.

The beginning of the whole process is connected with desire or need and action. What perception gives to the ‘knower’ (it would be better to say to the exposed ‘agent’), be it animal or person, is not something that inheres (inest) to the animal or person and that potentially corresponds to or replicates what is outside there, but an encounter with a  respondendum, with something that is capable of affecting the agent. The objectification of which Aris thinks presupposes that we  and confronted by ‘objects’ on which desire or aversion will be or was previously grafted in a merely accidental way –pure ‘phenomena’ to be seen or contemplated (hence speculative knowledge) rather than that we encounter respondenda or desiranda and avertenda. Such objectification cuts irremediably and irretrievably the connection with the surrounding world which, once cut and once having resulted in what Kant called  ‘ Objekt ,’ cannot be bridged, as Hume observed and Kant acknowledged in his Copernican revolution.

Now, it is true that we people, thanks to our linguistic ability, can provisionally dissociate the object of thought from desire and can think of it in absence. But it is always in implicit relation to the object of desire or aversion and to the moment of application in presence.* Insofar, then,  as there is truth, the moment of application is the moment of truth . If so, however, truth is not speculative correspondence but temporary satisfaction of desire through the achievement of the goal thanks to appropriate action based in or correlated with the right instrumental conception of the object of desire or aversion (much as a map, underpinned by desire, is instrumental). (N.B. The action may be appropriate in the sense of efficiency, or in the sense of morality, or in both).

* All names in the singular, in ordinary (non scientific) language are names of objects of desire and aversion.

Let me be more concrete. The superior animals who have locomotion, memory, phantasia, etc., know “water” only as satisfactor of desire, as to be pursued, and their knowledge consists in desiring and pursuing and having it. We people, by contrast, thanks to the objectifying power of language reflected in the use of the indicative mode, are capable of considering the object in its absence, and of converting it into object of observation when exposure doesn’t dictate otherwise, and into object of consideration in its absence. Whether or not this ability of ours will result in the damage typical of traditional philosophy depends on whether or not we realize that the neutralization of the connection with action is merely provisional.

Defining my position

In order not to forget this all-important point, I want to begin by dealing with one of the elements involved here, namely, the agent –the one who responds, deliberates, etc.

As Sartre would say, we are centers of (temporal) perspective and centers of instrumentality –because we are exposed agents-patients. This means that, in any situation, be it of immediate exposure or of observation, there is an implicit reference to ourself. The pencil I want is far or near, within or without reach, by reference to me, the desirous agent . For me to deliberate on whether I will get the pencil, the pencil has to be within practical reach, given who I am and my situation as agent in relation to the pencil.

We don’t have to make the reference explicit because we are the one who desires, fears, thinks, deliberates, acts, suffers.... And who we are and conceive ourself to be weigh very heavily on our options, our choices and our actions. To give a concrete example, the alcoholic who deliberates whether or not he will have a drink is an alcoholic self , so that his choice will be heavily marked by his tendency to drink. This seems to be what Aristotle called “character.” In order to make the right choices, and perhaps even to conceive those choices as possible choices, the agent has to be a good character developed through the acquisition of good habits, for what the agent is will predefine his very field of action and, in extreme cases, his very action. Thus, for instance, adultery is not a conceivable choice-action for a genuinely Christian agent, while it may be a very natural choice-action for an atheist of a certain kind. In other words, these two agents, given what they are, have entirely different fields of action. Of course, there is such a thing as akrasia , which results from the conflict between what the agent sees himself supposed to be and what he actually is. (See my 99-aug-1).
