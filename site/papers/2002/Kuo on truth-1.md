Bob Kuo: On truth

Truth is a reflective concept;. It presupposes a stated objective reality of which what is stated or judged is affirmed or denied. You can say “It is true (or false) that God exists” only by reference to a statement made by you or somebody else concerning God’s existence. If there is no previous statement or at least silent judgment concerning God’s existence, its truth cannot be affirmed or denied because truth and falsity refer not to a reality directly but to the affirmation or denial of a reality. Thus when you say “It is true or it is false that my house burnt” you are referring not directly to your house (which is real, but neither true nor false) but to the affirmation or denial that your house burnt. I suspect that this is your notion of truth, which is conformity between what the mind judges and what is the case. Now, if this is indeed your at least implicit notion of truth, I don’t believe in truth. Yet I believe in reality –in your house burning or having burnt, if it is indeed burning or it did indeed burn.

You say that philosophers haven’t paid sufficient attention to emotions. You are quite right, and this is connected  with the issue of truth. We don’t stand in relation to what we call reality as invulnerable, objective observers, as  is presupposed by the notion of truth as objective correspondence. We stand in relation to what we call reality as part and parcel of it, as vulnerable, exposed agents-patients to whom things happen and who make things happen. Your house burning is something that happens to you. It is for you not a neutral spectacle but object of emotion. Emotion is an incipient project of action You suffer from your house burning, and you will do whatever you can to prevent your house from burning.
