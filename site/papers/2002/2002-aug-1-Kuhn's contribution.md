2002-aug-1-Kuhn’s contribution August 16, 02

     KUHN’S REAL CONTRIBUTION

(Cf. my <2001-April-1-, revised on March 2002, particularly pages 7-9>, of which the present paper is a further development).

The paradigmatic character of normal science and the development of science through paradigmatic revolutions seem to be the two most important contributions credited to Kuhn by his friends, while his enemies attack the value of both contributions. Furthermore, both his friends and his enemies see relativism, when not ful-fledged skepticism, as an unavoidable consequence of his views, if indeed they posses any validity.

For my part, I agree with his friends that the two contributions they credit to him are valuable. I think, however, that his most important contribution consists in a radically different view of science by contrast to the commonly accepted image of it, to which Kuhn refers as ‘the textbook view of science.’ I think, moreover, that, educated himself in the old view and baffled by the novelty of the new view he was championing, Kuhn himself failed to give full shape to the alternative he was proposing. I will try to pinpoint the novelty of Kuhn’s own idea of science. As for the objections of idealism, skepticism and relativism leveled against Kuhn’s position, I am confident that when  Kuhn’s alternative view of science is fully explained, it automatically takes care of such objections.

The textbook view of science

In the very first page of the introduction to The Structure of Scientific Revolutions Kuhn attacks “the image of science by which we are now possessed,” and specifies his own purpose:

“that image has previously been drawn... mainly from the study of finished scientific achievements as these are recorded in the classics and, more recently, in the textbooks from which each new scientific generation learns to practice its trade. Inevitably, however, the aim of such books is persuasive and pedagogic; a concept of science drawn from them is no more likely to fit the enterprise that produced them than an image of a national culture drawn from a tourist brochure or a language text. This essay attempts to show that we have been misled by them in fundamental ways. Its aim is a sketch of the quite different concept of science that can emerge from the historical record of the research activity itself” ^(F1)  

The view of science presented by the textbooks that teach beginners their scientific trade  focuses on the chain of the most salient achievements of the scientific activity in terms of observations, experiments, laws and theories, together with the names of those scientists to whom each of those contributions is to be attributed. That is usually the content of the introductory chapter of scientific textbooks. In the successive chapters, the scientific method is illustrated by the experimental techniques used in gathering the data pertinent to the particular subject-mater of the textbook, together with the logical operations involved in relating those data to the theoretical generalizations found in the textbook. In addition, across the textbook, a series of concrete problems is furnished, whose solution by the student will serve as experimental confirmation of the validity of the laws involved, and will contribute to increase the student’s facility in applying the laws.

Subjacent to this textbook view is the notion that science grows by cumulative accretion, much as a building develops by horizontal accretion of single bricks and of successive vertical layers of bricks.  To develop the image: each innovative scientist contributes a brick or two to the edifice, and each generation of innovative scientists contributes another layer of observed facts and laws and theories.

As is too well known to need elaboration, Kuhn’s idea of the development of science is radically different. “Normal science” appears after a primitive pre-paradigmatic period in which there is no fundamental agreement among the practitioners; each scientist moves on his or her own, according to his or her own best lights. Such continues to be the case until a “paradigm” emerges that gains general acceptance. “Normal science” is paradigmatic.  More precisely, it is a problem-solving activity characterized by a shared view of the nature of the entities that constitute the particular field and, correspondingly, by a shared manner of solving the problems typical of the field. The shared view of the nature of the entities that constitute the field, though, is open-ended and confronts irregularities that threaten the shared view. However, rather than renouncing the shared view because of the irregularities, the scientists who share the paradigm live with those irregularities and endeavor to accommodate them within it.  Some of those irregularities prove, in the long run, to be merely apparent, when a solution to accommodate them within the paradigm is found. However, some other of the  irregularities that threaten the paradigm because of their incompatibility with it  may in the long run provoke a crisis. The crisis may lead to the abandonment of the current paradigm when an alternative view of the nature of the entities that constitute the field and, correspondingly, a new manner of solving the problems typical of the field emerges and rallies new adepts among the practicing scientists. Such an event, that is, the shift of paradigms, constitutes what Kuhn calls a  “revolution.”

Herein lies the explosiveness of Kuhn’s position. According to the classical view of science, represented by the textbook image, science genuinely grows . The truths discovered  by each generation of innovative scientists are preserved, and the next generation adds to the previously known scientific truths. As a result, there is genuine scientific growth in scientific truth; science is asymptotically moving toward total possession of the truth. Not so, in Kuhn’s opinion. Within a given paradigm, there is  growth, indeed, in that the problem-solving activity progresses further and further. ^(F2) With the shift of paradigms, however, there is no preservation of the previous scientific knowledge and, consequently, no genuine addition to it. There simply is a revolution, a new scientific beginning – a new science, literally , defined by a new paradigm. What was previously taken for genuine knowledge ceases to be accepted. Similarly, what is presently taken for genuine knowledge under the presently prevailing paradigm will in its turn cease to be accepted when the present paradigm is eventually substituted by a new one. That, in Kuhn’s opinion, is what happened when the Copernican paradigm was substituted by the Newtonian, and again when the Newtonian paradigm was substituted by the Einsteinian. In other words,  Copernican, Newtonian, and Einsteinian physics are three entirely different physics in which the putative ‘truths’ of the previous physics are replaced by new and different, but no less putative, ‘truths.’ Hence the optimistic idea that science is asymptotically moving toward the total truth is dead. In Kuhn’s softer words to the same effect,

“We may... have to relinquish the notion, implicit of explicit, that changes of paradigm carry scientists and those who learn from them closer and closer to truth.” ^(F3)

Of course, nothing more is needed to understand the furious attacks of which Kuhn’s view of science has been the object, as well as the numerous accusations of relativism and even skepticism leveled against him. Before pronouncing ourselves on this matter, however, we should pause to reconsider the entire case.

Science as a problem-solving activity

I have already said that, in my estimation, Kuhn’s most important contribution is the image of science he proposes as an alternative to what he calls the textbook view. Now, the Kuhnian image involves the paradigmatic character of normal science, by contrast to pre-paradigmatic or primitive science, as well as the periodic shifts of paradigms, with the just mentioned threatening implication concerning the development of science through revolutionary changes. But these characteristics of the Kuhnian view of science are only consequences that flow from his real contribution that consists in the replacement of the traditional image of science as a knowledge-acquiring activity to the view of science as a problem-solving activity . Kuhn himself, however, never explicitly thought of this aspect of his outlook as his main achievement, all to his own detriment. Indeed, as I hope to show it, everything becomes considerably clearer when the matter is seen in this light.

We may gain a first insight into the difference between the textbook image of science as a knowledge-acquiring activity and Kuhn’s own view of science as a problem-solving activity if we pay close attention to the contrast between the two as sketched by Kuhn. In the following paragraph he identifies what is understood by knowledge in the traditional view as accurate representation of reality; consequently, he identifies the traditional view of science reflected in the scientific textbooks as a knowledge-acquiring activity, that is, as an activity in which there is progress in  the accurate representation of reality. For his part, it will be remarked, Kuhn denies that the scientific activity yields knowledge in the sense of an accurate or even ever more accurate representation of the world; what the scientific activity yields, when successful, is problem-solving ability within its proper field:  

“ A scientific theory is usually felt better than its predecessors... because it is somehow representation of what nature is really like. One often hears that successive theories grow closer to, or approximate more closely to the truth. Apparently generalizations like that refer not to the puzzle-solutions and concrete predictions derived from a theory but rather to its ontology, to the match, that is, between the entities with which the theory populates nature and what is ’really’ there. Perhaps there is some other way of saving the notion of ‘truth’ for application to whole theories, but this one will not do. There is, I think, no theory-independent way to reconstruct phrases like ‘really there’; the notion of a match between the ontology of a theory and its ‘real’ counterpart in nature now seems to me illusive in principle. Besides, as a historian, I am impressed by the implausibility of the view. I do not doubt, for example, that Newton mechanics improves on Aristotle’s and that Einstein’s improves on Newton’s as instruments for puzzle-solving . But I can see in their succession no coherent direction of ontological development. On the contrary, in some important respects, though by no means in all, Einstein’s general theory of  relativity is closer to Aristotle’s than to Newton’s. Though the temptation to describe that position as relativistic is understandable, the description seems to me wrong .” ^(F4)

In rejecting the notion of knowledge as correspondence with the real Kuhn is fighting against a long and deeply ingrained epistemic tradition in which we all have been steeped. Indeed, the commonsensical notion, based on a superficial reflection on  visual perception, a view extended by Aristotle to enunciative language in his Peri Hermeneias, to wit : that knowledge consists in the match or exact correspondence between what the knower sees or  knows and what is the case in reality , received its philosophical consecration from Plato. But how can one be sure that what one sees actually corresponds to what is the case? After all, everyone has had the experience of having been wrong after being convinced of being right. In other words, the notion of knowledge as correspondence to what is the case, coupled with the obvious fact that putative knowledge proves some times to be false, that is, not to be genuine knowledge, created the problem of discerning between merely apparent and genuine knowledge. To distinguish the two, at least in name, Plato called the former “opinion” (doxa) and the latter “knowledge,” and he tackled the problem of discerning the two in the following way. Only those realities can be object of “knowledge” which are absolutely unchangeable or permanent because they exist necessarily and are necessarily as they are. For if the knower grasps such necessity, then the knower grasps that what he or she knows necessarily corresponds to what is the case. Now, no sensible or perceivable object is of that kind, for all of them are contingent rather than necessary, that is, they are subject to change in the flux of time. Thus my opinions about them, even assuming that my perceptions actually corresponded at a certain instant to what is the case, ceases to be true as they change. Thus Plato felt constrained to relegate all the particular objects of sensible perception to the realm of opinion or merely putative knowledge. Furthermore,  he determined that only the intellect (as opposed to the senses), which was capable of grasping what is necessarily the case, could yield knowledge (as opposed to opinion), and only in the case of the intelligible (as opposed to sensible) realities, which being purely intelligible were not subject to temporal change, was genuine knowledge possible. The notion of “scientific knowledge,” that is, of a knowledge whose unshakeable, objective certainty is superior to the merely subjective feeling of what is ordinarily taken for knowledge, has its origin in Plato.

As to the avatars of the notion of knowledge as correspondence between the mind and  what is the case in reality, as well as the intense scrutiny and criticism to which it has been subjected for centuries, from Plato on, the story has been told a hundred times. For my purpose, it may suffice to say that Kuhn is not alone, by far, in finding such notion of knowledge defective. It is fair to say that Kant, for instance, who gave up on the notion of knowledge as correspondence with the real on the basis that, since we have no independent means of access to the real other than knowledge, we cannot possibly find out whether or not what we take for knowledge actually corresponds to “the thing itself,” is, in this particular regard, Kuhn’s direct predecessor. Whether, however, Kant proposed a viable alternative to the notion of knowledge as correspondence with what is the case, or simply left us in the abyss of skepticism, is not my question. Or, rather, my question is exactly the same, but concerning Kuhn. Does he, or doesn’t he furnish us with a viable alternative to the traditional notion of knowledge as correspondence between the mind and reality?

Practical knowledge is not representational: Ortho-doxy and ortho-praxis

Do animals know? To Plato, for whom genuine knowledge was a deed of the intellect, not of the senses, and who had an acute sense of the radical difference between animals and people, such question would have sounded trivial and uninteresting, if not plainly wrongheaded. Not so to Aristotle, who in time came to distinguish practical from theoretical knowledge and to see some kind of continuity between animals and people. The interest of his exploration of the issue whether animals know is that it seems to open the way to an  alternative to the traditional notion of knowledge as faithful mental representation of the real –the sort of alternative Kuhn was obscurely groping for in order to give full shape to his understanding of science.

In my <2002-April-3-Aris-Devereux : Practical Knowledge in Aristotle> I argued that Aristotle failed to distinguish cleanly practical from theoretical knowledge. The reason for that failure is that, under Plato’s influence,  he still could not help looking in practical knowledge for an element of universality characteristic of Plato’s conception of theoretical knowledge, though practical knowledge is necessarily particular. In Aristotle’s view, the distinctive trait of practical knowledge is its connection with action. Let us reflect on this important point in more detail.

The connection with action is not found in theoretical knowledge, whose intrinsic purpose is knowledge for its own sake, the mere grasp of the truth. Thus, animals look for, and are capable of attaining, only the knowledge that is useful to the preservation and well-being of the individual or of the species to which the animal belongs, while people are capable of both theoretical and practical knowledge. Now the fact that practical knowledge is connected with action does not detract from the fact that it is knowledge. If it is knowledge, though, it has to be grasp of what is the case, for that is what knowledge consists in. For instance, on the basis of their respective past experience, both lions and people may come to grasp the useful ‘universal’ fact that, say, eating such a plant has beneficial purgative effects, though lions only know that fact practically, that is, for the purpose of applying that knowledge to obtain the benefits of the practice, whereas people know it practically and also theoretically, as a truth independent of its practical utility.

Such  is Aristotle’s conception of practical and theoretical knowledge in the particular version to which we are presently referring. In this version, the difference between practical and theoretical knowledge is purely extrinsic. Indeed, the fact known by both the lion and the human is the same, though the lion knows it only because it is actually useful and only for the purpose of applying the knowledge, whereas the human knows the ‘universal’ fact also theoretically. There is, however, another possible version of practical knowledge. In the one we have so far considered, the connection between knowledge and action is extrinsic, in that the knowledge of the same fact may be applied or not, and it is knowledge whether or not it is applied. But think, in the first place, of the lion’s case. The lion can attain the knowledge of the purgative effects of the herb only through its own experience of those effects, while the human may come to know the purgative effects of the herb by being told of them by somebody else. Now, the lion’s experience is not only experience of the purgative effects, but also experience of eating the plant. In other words, it is experience of the action that was followed by the purgative effects. Thus the lion learns not only the objective side, so to say, namely, the purgative effects the plant produces, but learns also the subjective side, namely, what to do to obtain the effects. Eating the plant becomes the means to obtain the effects. And the two sides are learned together and cannot be learned separately.
