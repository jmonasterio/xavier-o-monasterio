Michael Polanyi: Linguistic action and Tacit Knowledge ^(F1)

“Je crois en Dieu, à laTrinité. à l’incarnation, à la redemption, à l’eucharistie, aux vérités de l’Evangile.

Tacit Knowledge            

In The Tacit Dimension Michael Polanyi initially introduced the notion of “tacit knowledge,” by which he became known, to refer to those cases in which a person knows more than he can verbalize. He exemplified this situation with the experiment conducted by psychologists Lazarus and McCleary in 1949.

“These authors presented a person with a large number of nonsense syllables, and after showing certain of the syllables, they administered an electric shock. Presently the person showed symptoms of anticipating the shock at the sight of ‘shock syllables’; yet, on questioning, he could not identify them. He had come to know when to expect a shock, but he could not tell what made him expect it. He had acquired a knowledge similar to that which we have when we know a person by signs we cannot tell.” ^(F2)

Two points are to be observed in this connection. (1) That it is assumed as a matter of fact that, in its ordinary form, knowledge is intrinsically related to language in that it involves the ability to enunciate in words what is known. Indeed what will differentiate what Polanyi calls tacit knowledge from ordinary knowledge is that the former is tacit precisely because it does not involve the ability on the knower’s part to enunciate in words what he knows. (2) It is also assumed that knowledge consists in the awareness on the knower’s part of the characteristics of object known, for that is what is expressed or enunciated in words; indeed, the reason why in the case of  what Polanyi will call tacit knowledge the subject cannot enunciate linguistically what he knows is precisely that he is not aware of the object known.      

If such is the case, it seems that the very notion of tacit knowledge (from now on TK), as characterized by Polanyi, is nonsensical. Indeed, it seems to amount to an unconscious knowledge, for the subject who supposedly knows is not aware of what he knows, since he cannot say what he knows. Now, an unconscious knowledge is an oxymoron. Why? Because the very notion of knowledge, or at least the ordinary notion of knowledge that common sense seems to impose on us, implies on the subject’s part the awareness of what he knows. He knows what he knows thanks to the awareness he has of what he knows, so he would not know what he knows without that awareness.

As an example of this ordinary notion of knowledge from which Polanyi here takes his distance let us consider the knowledge of which Searle spoke in the preceding chapter, since he used the word ‘knowledge’ in its ordinary meaning. According to Searle, in linguistic communication the hearer who understands what the speaker says thereby acquires the knowledge  – that is, becomes aware – of what the speaker intends to communicate to him. And it would be inconceivable that the hearer knew what the speaker intends to communicate if the hearer had no awareness at all of what he knew. In other words, what a subject knows becomes object known to him by the very fact of his becoming aware of it. Hence no knowledge without awareness of what is known, because knowledge implies and demands the awareness of the object known. Thus, when Polanyi defines KT as a knowledge in which the subject cannot say what he supposedly knows, we have to conclude that KT is a knowledge in which the subject is not aware of what he knows, which is a contradiction in terms.  

For his part, Polanyi would reply to this objection to his notion of TK by referring us to the undeniable facts of the experiment he described: the subject of the experiment cannot say which are the shock-producing syllables because he does not know them, that is, is not aware of them, has no consciousness of them. Yet the subject’s behavior in reaction to those syllables shows that he knows them in a certain way, for he braces himself for a shock –and this ‘certain way of knowing’ which does not involve the awareness of the object known is what Polanyi calls TK.

As the facts of the experiment seem undeniable, we cannot ignore them. Let us then try to see things more clearly. For this purpose, it might be helpful to compare Polanyi’s TK to what Sartre called “non-thetic or non-positional awareness” in an analysis that led him to his famous notion of “Bad faith.” ^(F3) According to Sartre, the “censor” of which Freud speaks and to which Freud attributes the task of preventing the psychoanalysand from acknowledging his own shameful impulses by rendering them unconscious , is nothing else than the psychoanalysand himself who prevents himself from acknowledging them precisely because he is somehow conscious or aware of those. How, indeed, Sartre reasons, could the Freudian censor censure only the shameful impulses and not others, if they were totally unconscious?

What happens actually, explains Sartre, is that the psychoanalysand, who is conscious of his shameful impulses, does not want to assume responsibility for them. Now, what renders possible this hypocrisy on the psychoanalysand’s part, who pretends to himself not to know at all the impulses he himself censures with such discrimination, is that he is conscious or aware of them only under the mode of not being explicitly aware of them, because he has not of them the kind of thetic or positional consciousness of object we normally have of what  we know. The psychoanalysand, in Sartre’s view, has only an implicit or not verbalized awareness of his shameful impulses, and his awareness is only implicit precisely because it does not reach the level of verbal formulation. In short, the psychoanalysand knows his shameful impulses because he is conscious of them, yet he can pretend not to know them because he is only non-positionally aware of them, that is, cannot verbalize what he is aware of. By the way, this indicates the relationship between knowledge – which consists in the awareness of what is known – and linguistic verbalization – which consists precisely in expressing in words that of which the subject is aware, what is known to him.

In order to gain as precise an idea as possible of Polanyi’s tacit knowledge, let us now inquire whether Polanyi’s TK is equivalent to  Sartre’s non-positional consciousness. Thanks to his notion of non-positional consciousness Sartre can show, against Freud, that the psychoanalysand is conscious of his shameful impulses, that is, knows them, and is therefore responsible for his refusal to assume responsibility for them – a responsibility of which he is discharged by Freud under the pretext that they are unconscious. Sartre’s reply to Freud is that in order  to be conscious of something and, hence, to know it, it is not required to verbalize explicitly that of which one is only non-positionally conscious. Consequently,  the psychoanalysand does not formulate to himself that of which he is implicitly aware simply because he does not want to. Indeed, that is why Sartre considers him responsible for his shameful impulses. But this very trait of Sartre’s non-positional consciousness, which he rightly equates with our ordinary notion of knowledge because it amounts to the consciousness of what is known (though not the explicit consciousness brought about by verbalization), cannot be equated with Polanyi’s TK. Polanyi does not mean that the subject of the experiment with the shock-producing syllables is tacit because it is not even internally verbalized by the subject. The subject who has TK of the shock-producing syllables does not know at all , either explicitly or even implicitly, which are those syllables. Different in this from the Sartrean psychoanalysand, the subject of the experiment does not identify verbally the shock-producing syllables, not because he does not want to render explicit his knowledge of his shameful impulses in order not to have to assume responsibility for them, but because the subject of the experiment literally has no knowledge of them.    

Let us be clear. Sartre does not reject the ordinary notion of knowledge as consciousness of object. He does not claim that there is knowledge that does not involve awareness of object. He simply claims that the consciousness of object in which knowledge is ordinarily deemed to   consist can take two forms in its relationship to language: the form of  positional consciousness of object, thanks to verbalization, as well as the less common form of merely implicit or non-positional consciousness of object. In other words, verbalization is not a necessary condition for knowledge, because the consciousness in which knowledge is ordinarily deemed to consist is possible without verbalization. Verbalization only renders explicit and communicable to others the implicit consciousness of object that the subject possesses before and without verbalization, and implicit or not verbalized consciousness is sufficient to constitute knowledge in the ordinary sense.

Thus, Sartre’s subject is initially non-positionally aware of his shameful impulses, so that, if he so decides, he can express in words the characteristics of the object of which he is non-positionally aware. The subject who possesses TK, by contrast, cannot say which are the shock-producing syllables because he does not know them at all. In other words, if TK is knowledge, it is not knowledge in the ordinary sense of the word, for it does not consist in either implicit or explicit awareness of object.

For my present purposes it is irrelevant whether or not one accepts Sartre’s view of implicit consciousness as pre-verbal consciousness of object. His analysis was only helpful to show that the commonly accepted notion of  knowledge, in whatever variety,  implies consciousness of object. TK, by contrast, does not involve consciousness of object. How, then, can Polanyi claim that what he calls “tacit knowledge” is knowledge at all? If it is, it has to be knowledge in a different sense. And can a different notion of knowledge make sense at all? How can knowledge be anything else than the knower’s awareness of the object known? A systematic contrast between Searle’s and Polanyi’s views on the way language functions will be helpful in this connection.

Polanyi’s alternative conception of knowledge

Searle matter-of-factly assumes and takes for granted the validity of the common conception of  knowledge as awareness of the object known, as well as the common conception of the relationship between the awareness in which knowledge consists and language. Indeed, in Searle’s view,  the awareness of object possessed by the knowing subject is what is expressed in words when verbalization occurs. “Saying,” in Searle’s view, is nothing else than expressing in words that of which the subject is aware, and it is by understanding those words that the hearer becomes aware or comes to know or is informed of that of which the speaker is aware . ^(F4)

According to Searle, the primary and essential function of language is to inform of, or make known to, the hearer, what the speaker intends to communicate to him. However, the speaker may have two different kinds of communicative intentions: either simply to make something known to the hearer, or, in addition, to have him do something the speaker wants. Thus,  I can tell my doctor “I have a headache,” to make known to him my state, and I can tell him “Give me something for my headache.” In both cases I make known to him what I want to communicate to him. In the first case, my intention is to produce in the hearer what Searle calls the” illocutionary effect”; in the second case, to produce in the hearer a “perlocutionary effect.”  

According to Polanyi, however, TK is not awareness of the characteristics of the object known. That is the reason that TK is tacit: since TK does not involve awareness of the object known, the knowledge in which TK consists cannot be expressed in words that make that awareness  known. What occurs in the subject of the experiment at the sight of the shock-producing syllables is not an awareness of the object’s characteristics, for he does not know the syllables, but a blind affection of fear that causes him to brace himself for the shock as if protecting himself from it. Now, this reaction of bracing himself  is not awareness of object, not even a silent or non verbalized one; it is a non-linguistic conduct or behavior caused by the affection of fear – a conduct whose reason is totally obscure to the subject, since he doesn’t know which syllables produce shocks.  

Thus, the subject of the experiment makes no linguistic enunciation either to himself or to  others. Rather, he automatically acts in a certain way, and this behavior is observed by those who conduct the experiment. Actually, this behavior is precisely the basis on which Polanyi grounds his claim that the subject ‘knows’ in one way or another, and without that behavioral manifestation at the sight of the shock-producing syllables and not at the sight of the other syllables Polanyi would have no basis for his claim of tacit knowledge on the subject’s part.

Now, this close connection with action of what Polanyi calls TK, in the absence of verbalization,  is an important clue. Indeed, it is a first indication that, in Polanyi’s view,   knowledge may consist in action of a certain kind independent of verbalization rather than in consciousness of the object’s characteristics expressed in verbalization , as it has been traditionally assumed. In which case Polanyi’s notion of tacit knowledge, far from being as oxymoronic as it initially appeared, may even open a new epistemic avenue. Let us, therefore, review in detail Searle’s and Polanyi’s views, with special attention to the place action occupies in each of them.

Searle and Polanyi on the relationship of  language to action

As we know, Searle distinguishes two kinds of linguistic effects: the illocutionary effect, which consists in knowledge of what the speaker intends to communicate, and the perlocutionary effects which for the most part consist in actions. It should be remarked, however, that in  distinguishing the illocutionary from the perlocutionary effects, Searle ipso facto subordinates action to knowledge. The reason for this subordination is that knowledge of what the speaker means appears to Searle as the necessary precondition for the success of all the perlocutionary intentions of a speaker, whereas the success of the illocutionary intentions of a speaker, that is, the hearer’s knowledge of what the speaker means, does not depend on the success of any of the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker. To put it concretely, a speaker cannot say anything without intending to convey to the hearer the knowledge of what he means, but he can say something without obtaining or even intending to obtain any other effect than the hearer’s knowledge of what he means. So Searle sees knowledge as independent from action. Correlatively, the hearer cannot fulfill the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker without knowing those intentions, that  is, without the illocutionary effect obtaining; but the hearer can know the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker, thereby insuring the success of the communicative act as such, without fulfilling the speaker’s perlocutionary intentions. For instance, if I do not know what you mean when you ask me to lend you  my pen, I cannot lend you my pen, but I can know that you ask me for my pen and not lend it to you. Moreover, you can produce in me an illocutionary effect without intending any perlocutionary effect. Thus, in Searle’s interpretation of saluting,  when you salute me, all you intend to produce in me is the knowledge that you mean to salute me, and the communicative exchange succeeds if only I gain that knowledge. So, in Searle’s view, knowledge is independent from action, but action is dependent on knowledge . Now, because the perlocutionary effects depend on the realization of the  illocutionary effect, but not the other way around, and because the speaker may say something without intending any perlocutionary effect but cannot say anything without intending to produce the illocutionary effect, which consists in the hearer’s awareness of  what the speaker means, Searle sees language primarily as a system of signs to communicate knowledge, and only secondarily and accidentally as a system of signs to effect action . ^(F5)

Polanyi, for his part, based on his own analysis of the way in which language functions, construes the relationship between knowledge and action in a totally different way. .For Searle, as just explained, action may or may not accompany knowledge. For Polanyi, knowledge without action is inconceivable, for action is a constitutive element or ‘moment’ of knowledge . Indeed, the knowledge involved in the communicative exchange is, in his view, essentially practical in that it is meant to terminate, and actually manifests itself in, a certain kind of action, not, as the case is for Searle, in a theoretical awareness of a fact whose natural manifestation is the linguistic enunciation of the object’s characteristics. For example, knowing an order received does not consist, according to Polanyi, in becoming aware of the order as a theoretical fact, but in the practical disposition to obey the order received, and in actually obeying it at the appropriate time, unless prevented by extrinsic causes. Similarly, according to Polanyi, knowing a promise received does not consist in becoming theoretically aware of that fact, as one becomes aware of a color when one sees it in the course of an experiment in a laboratory, but in actually disposing oneself for the fulfillment of the received promise at the appropriate time. If you promise to repay me the $100.00 you owe me, when I come to pick them up at your office at 3.00 P.M., the appropriate understanding of your promise does not consist in registering that fact and then doing nothing, as Searle contends,  but in expecting the fulfillment of your promise and in the action of actually coming to your office at 3.00 P.M. to collect my money.

Now, the idea that knowledge consists in a certain kind of  action rather than in the   consciousness of an object’s characteristics and its linguistic enunciation is neither outlandish nor even  new, however much it may differ from the ordinarily accepted notion of knowledge as awareness of its object characteristics – an awareness that is reflected in the enunciative expression of the object’s characteristics. Spontaneously, that is, when free from epistemological prejudices, most of us believe that the members of at least the higher species of mammals, say, dogs, possess the ability to know. At the same time, most of us believe that dogs are non-linguistic agents, that is, that they do not posses the linguistic ability we possess, for they do not express in words what they know. But this shows that we spontaneously assume that knowing is not identical with the consciousness of an object’s characteristics or with the consequent linguistic capacity to enunciate what one knows or is aware of.

On what basis, then, do we attribute to dogs the ability to know? On the basis of their observed actions . Certain of their actions are, we say,”intentional.” They  reveal an agent’s purpose , in that the dog’s agency is not uniform but adapted by the dog to different circumstances for the sake of a desired goal that satisfies a natural need of the dog.  By contrast, a marble’s fall down an inclined plane is a uniform action, not an adaptive or ‘intentional’ action, because it is caused by the environmental circumstances; moreover, the action is not intended by the marble for the sake of the satisfaction of a need and, therefore, it not modified by the marble according to the circumstances. For that very reason, we have no temptation of attributing knowledge to marbles: their action is not intentional, not for the sake of a goal intended-desired  by the marble, and the uniformity of the marble’s action shows no ability on the marble’s part to adapt its action to the circumstances in view of the realization of a goal. In a dogs’ actions, by contrast, we grasp the intention of a goal and, with their increased experience, an increasing ability on their part to adapt their actions to the circumstances and thereby make their actions more efficient for the realization of their natural goals. Hence we conclude that dogs know.

Of course, this analysis may be refined, but as it is it suffices for my purpose. For my purpose is simply to indicate that knowledge is not necessarily to be conceived, as Searle among so many others  conceives it, as an awareness of object that mirrors or reproduces the object’s characteristics in a way that is totally unrelated to action. Rather, our analysis of what – without any epistemological preconceptions – we spontaneously recognize as knowledge shows that what we identify as knowledge, at least  in the case of animals, involves intentional actions for the sake of desired goals, and that verbalization of what is known is not a necessary component of knowledge. In short, knowledge is not identified, at least in this case, with a purely factual, purely theoretical or non-practical awareness of the characteristics of its object, as implied by the ordinary or commonly accepted notion of knowledge,  but with an agent’s ability to realize a desired goal through intentional action,  that is, through action adapted by the agent to the realization of the desired goal in the given circumstances.

One way of expressing the fundamental difference between Polanyi’s and Searle’s respective conceptions of knowledge is that the former thinks that knowledge functions as an instrument FOR action, whereas Searle sees it as a purely theoretical representation OF what is known, without any intrinsic relationship to action. In order, however, to understand the instrumental function Polanyi attributes to knowledge we have to study his views in more detail and to contrast them with Searle’s own.

Knowledge as representation of object and knowledge as instrument for action

Let us return to the example Polanyi employs in The Tacit Dimension to introduce the notion of tacit knowledge. The subject in the experiment with the syllables knows which syllables produce shocks, but that knowledge does not consist in, nor is manifested in, internal or external  words that express his awareness of those syllables, for the subject cannot say even to himself which are those syllables;  rather the knowledge manifests itself  in the subject’s conduct of bracing himself for a shock when those syllables appear on the screen. Thus, in the sense of being aware of the shock-producing syllables and of being able to say which are the shock-producing syllables, the subject of the experiment does not know . Yet he knows the syllables in the sense of acting in response to those syllables by bracing himself for the shock to come. Action , therefore, rather than linguistic enunciation, is the way in which knowledge manifests itself in this case.

As an initial illustration of the instrumental role Polanyi attributes to TK, by contrast to the vision or awareness of the object’s characteristics that can be enunciated linguistically, as Searle construes it, let us consider the way in which, according to Polanyi, eyeglasses function. When eyeglasses actually function as such – that is when they are worn by someone, not when they lie on a table and are looked at–  the person who wears the eyeglasses does not see the eyeglasses. This is another way of saying that the person who wears the eyeglasses has no awareness of them as object of knowledge, as is the case when they lie on a table and are looked at. In other words, when the eyeglasses are worn, they do not function as object known but as instrument. Now, we have to resist the temptation to complete the preceding sentence by saying that when worn on the nose the eyeglasses function as instrument OF knowledge , that is, as instrument to acquire knowledge of the characteristics of the objects seen through the eyeglasses. For that is not what Polanyi thinks. He thinks that, when they are worn by someone, the eyeglasses function as an instrument FOR action , that is, as an instrument to guide the agent’s action in the realization of the goals he desires . ^(F6) If the agent wants to get a hammer and, thanks to the eyeglasses, he sees the hammer at the opposite end of the room as well as a table that is placed between himself and the desired object, that vision will not result in the awareness of the individual characteristics of the table and of the hammer; rather the vision will be instrumental in that it will allow him to move in the right direction toward the hammer while avoiding the interposed table. In other words, what is seen through the eyeglasses will function as an instrument that guides the person’s action. It is a matter of TK , not of what we would usually understand by knowledge of the table and of the hammer, that is. the awareness of the individual characteristics of the hammer and the table. Such is, according to Polanyi, the instrumental role played by the objects perceived, in every case of normal perception; the example of the person wearing eyeglasses is only a particular illustration of what Polanyi thinks is the general rule in perception.  

To gain a better understanding of the instrumental role Polanyi attributes to perception let us reflect on the way signs function when they are actually perceived as signs . This reflection is important for two reasons. (1) Because Polanyi thinks that the instrumental role of perception is closely related to the fact that, in ordinary perception, the objects perceived function as natural signs.  (2) Because reflection on the way signs actually function is required to correct a common   mistake about language as a system of signs  – a mistake exemplified, as we shall see, by Searle’s view of language.

                                                  

How do signs work? Think of what goes on in our daily experience with traffic lights. We think of the red and green lights as  signs. And we are not wrong, for they are signs. Where we go wrong –not when we perceive the traffic lights, but when we reflect on our perception of them–  is in assuming that signs are primarily signs OF something to be known by the subject who perceives the signs, something of which the subject who perceives the signs is aware, and which therefore can be made explicit when verbally enunciated by the subject. In fact, Polanyi thinks, signs, all signs without exception: whether natural or conventional, linguistic signs included– function as instruments FOR action, not as signs OF something of what one becomes aware and that can be made known to oneself and to others through linguistic enunciation. ^(F7)

If Polanyi is right, red in the case of a traffic light, is  a sign FOR performing the action of stopping at the sight of the red light. Similarly, a green light is a sign FOR performing the action of moving ahead at the sight of the light.  An arrow pointing in a certain direction on a road is a sign FOR performing the action of moving in the direction pointed at. In short, the correct comprehension of signs is manifested not in linguistic enunciations of what is signified, but in the actual performance of the action signified. for that is what is signified by the sign.  

Let me be more precise. In affirming that signs are signs FOR action rather than signs OF something, my point is that the signified is never something that can be linguistically expressed as ‘the content’ of purely enunciative speech act that specifies what is the case. Rather, the signified is always something desired or feared by an agent either for its own sake or as a means for something else. In other words, the signified is sign FOR performing an action because it is sign of something desired or feared either for its own sake or as a means for something else, and therefore it motivates the agent’s action. For instance, when I am walking and am about to cross a road, I look on my right. If I perceive  a car moving in my direction at a certain speed, the perceived car is a sign For stopping, because I have the goal of reaching alive the other side of the road. Thus, the sign is not a sign OF, if by that we understand sign OF object of ‘objective’ knowledge, that is, of purely theoretical awareness. But neither is it necessarily, as in the cases of inherited reflexes, a mere sign FOR a certain action causally produced by thee sign, without any reference to an object of desire or fearon the subject’s part. When I see a car moving in my direction at a certain distance and speed, my stopping is not a purely reflex action caused by the perceived car;  rather, it is an intentional action motivated by the car perceived as a sign of danger. Motivated –here is where the sign’s reference to the signification of a perceptual object enters. However, it is not reference to object merely understood, capable of being expressed in a purely enunciative speech act, for the object is object of desire or fear. My stopping is of the order of signification rather than of the causal  order not only because it implies reference to refers to a signified object, but also because the object being  feared its perception functions as instrument  for achieving my desired goal. In other words, my perception of the car signals my stopping as instrumental for achieving my goal. I perceive the car not objectively, that is, independently of my dispositions as an exposed agent; I perceive the car as to be avoided . Hence the emotion of fear that qualifies the car as perceived by me and makes me perceive the car as dangerous. As observed by Sartre regarding the practical nature of perception, given its relationship to the perceiving agent, Carthago delenda est Carthaginensibus , but only to the Carthagenians, because it is dangerous object to them and it functions as instrument for their action because it is perceived as sign of danger, not as ‘objective’ reality.  

In short, when I denied that signs function as signs OF, I was only denying that they function as signs of an object of knowledge in the way  knowledge is ordinarily conceived , that is, as something that corresponds in reality to what is conceived in the mind of a purely objective, that is, disinterested or non-desirous knower . I was not denying, however, that it is ‘known’ or to be ‘known’ as object of practical  knowledge, as something to be responded to according to its instrumental value for the realization of an agent’s desire or project . If I am hungry, the sight of a piece of ham within my reach functions as a sign for eating . Consequently, under ordinary circumstances, I read that sign as signs are to be read, that is, instrumentally– as an instrument that guides me in the realization of my desire of eating.  Thus, the perception guides me as a sort of map thanks to which I move toward the place where I perceive the ham, and take it in my hand,  and put it in my mouth.

---------------------

This assimilation of the object to my is body-for -me, to my instrumental body is what Polanyi calls “internalization,” meaning that from something purely out there I have appropriated  the ham into ‘my body for me’ and made it into an instrument to realize my project– the body I am because it is the only body I use and can use as mine to realize my projects. You can use your hands to take the ham if you have the project of eating, but I cannot use your hands; I can only use  my hands. Which is the reason that Polanyi sees my own body –the body I am and use as instrument for the realization of my projects– as the model of internalization, that is, of the process through which I assimilate certain things in the environment to my own body when I use them as instruments as I use my body.  

-------------------------

To formulate in other terms the general rule regarding the way in which signs signify: Perceptual objects, at the moment of actual encounter with them and when they are relevant for the satisfaction of a desire of the perceiving subject, function as signs that  mean performatively, not enunciatively . In other words, the signified is not an awareness OF, whose content neither can, neither  needs, to be expressed linguistically in an enunciation. For the signified is not an object to be aware of,  but an action to be performed. And because perceptual signs do not mean enunciatively but performatively, they mean tacitly by opposition to enunciatively . An important consequence of this general rule is that the perception itself of a sign as a sign, be it natural or conventional, is different from the observation of an object through which one becomes aware of the characteristics of the object observed. Precisely because a sign is a sign, not a thing or an object to be known, it does not point to itself  but, so to say, beyond itself.

The perceptual observation of an object is inspired by curiosity concerning the  characteristics of the object. But the perception of a sign as a sign does not consist in observing the sign, for that is not how signs function. This is what Polanyi was underscoring in his example of the shock-producing syllables. Because they function as signs FOR  action, not as objects OF objective knowledge capable of being adequately expressed in an enunciation, the subject for whom they function as signs does not thereby gain the awareness of the individual characteristics of the object-sign and, therefore, the subject cannot say which are those characteristics. So little are we aware of the objet characteristics when we perceive signs as signs that, as Polanyi observes, if I am fluent in both English and Spanish, sometimes I cannot remember whether something was said to me in English or in Spanish, for I am not aware of the signs themselves but of what they refer to. Which is the very reason why the knowledge involved in the case of signs is tacit , by contrast to enunciative , for it is not meant to terminate in awareness of its object’s characteristics nor, consequently, in words that enunciate those characteristics, but in the action signaled.

It is to be remarked, though, that for a sign to actually function as a sign and lead to the performance of the action signified a desire or the intention of a goal is required on the subject’s part, a desire for whose realization the action signified is relevant as a MEANS OR INSTRUMENT. For instance, the presence of water functions as a sign For drinking it only for an agent who is thirsty. And the action functions as the instrument through wihch the desire is satisfied.

That is what Aristotle had in mind when, as Nussbaum remarks,  he observed that only when desire is present does action follow perception, for only in that case does the object perceivd funcion as aign For the performance of an action. Now, it is in connection with action that Nussbaum underscores a difference between animals and people that will lead us to a grasp of the way in which language functions by contrast to all other signs. So far I have spoken of perceptual objects which function as signs. Since, aw we shall see in a moment, perceptual objets are not linguistic signs, their relationship to action is immediate. In the presence of desire or fear, they automatically result in action, for they function as signs FOR immediate action. However, unlike animals, we are genuinely  linguistic creatures, and our very perception is mediated by language. Now, what distinguishes linguistic signs from other signs is not that they are signs for action –for that they have in common with all signs– but that their connection with action is not immediate but mediated by the concept of the object signified.   What Nussbaum was pointing out in her remark of the contrast between animals and people is related to the fact that because the action of people is genuinely linguistic, their perception of objects is not immediately related to action.

Language, concepts, and the return to experience

The remark by Nussbaum* about a significant  difference between animals and people is that in their perception animals always act in immediate response to the object relevant to their most pressing desire at the time, whereas people may perceive objects independently of their immediate instrumentality for their projects. In other words, to use Aquinas’s language, animals only perceive ex dispositione agentis , while we perceive sometimes ex dispositione agentis but also, at other times, ex his quae sunt propria rei. For example, a hungry dog will automatically attempt to eat the piece of meat it perceives within its reach. For the dog, unless the dog has a more pressing desire at the time, the piece of meat automatically  functions as a sign FOR performing the action of eating, that is, the action that satisfies its most pressing desire at the time of the perception. People, by contrast, may not perceive the piece of meat in relation to their need for food and thus not eat it, because their action is not immediately tied to their perception of a desired object but to a conception of the object.  Unfortunately Nussbaum does not explore the reason for this difference, a reason which is highly relevant to our linguistic search.

The unexplored reason for this difference is, I think, that  we people are genuinely linguistic creatures. Unlike in the case of animals, our experience is mediated by language, it is linguistic experience, experience of objects named . Consequently in our case signs are not directly and immediately connected with the motor system, as they are in the case of even the higher species of animals. Perceptual objects are for us signs For the performance of an action when we encounter them in actual perception; even then, however, they are not necessarily Signs For immediate action, as they are for animals. The reason is that, thanks to our linguistic ability, our perceptual experience of objects is not direct nor exclusively related to our needs and desires. We experience objects only through the intermediary of  a conception of the object as signified by its name, and this mode of perceiving is distinctive and  characteristic of the way in which properly  linguistic signs function.

To begin dealing with this important subject, let me remark that thanks to our linguistic ability we can meet or experience objects in two ways: (1) in a symbolic way through words, when we think and talk about them, regardless of their actual  presence or absence and of their instrumentality for the satisfaction of our needs, and (2) in a perceptual way when we encounter them perceptually, which is always in their presence and mostly in terms of their instrumentality. In other words, we not only can deal with the objects when we perceive them in their presence, as animals can, but, in addition, we can deal with them conceptually, when we talk about them and think about them, whether or not they are actually  present to us. Animals, though,  cannot do this,  because having no genuine linguistic capability they can only directly and perceptually and interestedly or instrumentally with their object of experience.

This is not to say that no animal understands names, but that they do not understand names the way we do. Dogs understand their own name when they are called, and the name of their master when that name is mentioned. Their behavior in these two cases manifests that they ‘understand’ names, for they react to them in characteristic ways. But in their case these names function exclusively as signs-For immediately performing an action: the action of moving toward the caller, and the action of responding to their master when the latter’s name is mentioned. Now,  for the dog its master’s name functions in the same way as its master’s characteristic way of ringing the door’s bell, or as it’s master’s characteristic smell. The perceived smell prompts the same conduct as the perceived sound of the name, because both signify for the dog the imminent presence of its master and, thus, are a sign-FOR reacting to the master. . But that is not the way in which  the objects we perceive always function for us, because our experience is  linguistic through and through. In the case of dogs, a certain sound of the door’s bell signals the perceived master and prompts an immediate response to the master as object of desire for the dog.. For a dog, which is not a linguistic creature, the perception of the sound of a certain ring of the door-bell or of the characteristic smell of its master function exactly in the same way as the sound of the name of its master., to wit:  as a sign FOR responding to the imminent presence of its desired master, so the dog infallibly runs to the door, wagging its tail, to meet its master.

The sound of its master’s name functions for the dog in a similar way as the sound of the bell functioned in the case of Pavlov’s dog. The sound of the bell functioned for Pavlov’s dog as a sign For salivating, because that sound  was associated by the dog with the presence of food.  Which is attractive to dogs as a satisfactor of a natural need of theirs. In t his regard, we humans have much in common with dogs. Perceptual objects come to function as signs-For the performance of a certain action by virtue of their conjunction with the natural needs of animals or of people to whose satisfaction they happen to be relevant. It is from that relationship, established and confirmed  through association, of the thing denoted by a the sound of a  name to the needs of people or other living creatures that names become meaningful for those particular animals and for people, and, thus, begin to function as natural signs FOR. To repeat. it is through association with the object denoted , which happens to be relevant to the satisfaction of needs, that names become proxy for their perceived object as signs For action. In the case of animals, that is all. But for us people, names which are also connotationally meaningful, that is, may also connote the concept of the object named, independently of the presence of the object named  and of its instrumentality for the satisfaction of our desires.

Thus, for me,  who am not an animal, a certain sound of the door-bell may sometimes function as a sign of my wife’s arrival, in which case it works as a sign For my immediately moving to the door to greet her. However, that is not typical of the way in which names are meaningful or significant for us humans. Indeed, t he mention of my wife’s name is seldom forme a sifn-For an immediate action in response to her imminent presence; most often it is sign for thinking of her, for conceiving her.

That is what Helen Keller discovered when she discovered language. The word ‘w-a-t-e-r’ telegraphed to her by her teacher by certain movements of her hand, was not, for Hellen, a sign FOR reponding to the imminent presence of water in relation to her present dispositions, that is, her desires. For that kind of action language wa not required. She already knew, for instance, that the sensation of her hat being pur on her head was a sign FOR bracing herself for going out for awalk. But that is not what took place in the case I am referring to. In this case she did not brace hertself to interact immediately with water. Rather the word or name was a sign of water as something  to be conceived or thought about. In short, the name-word, in this case, was not a proxy for the perceptual thing, but for a concept of the perceptual thing. Thus, unlike dogs, from then on Helen Keller was capable of responding at the sound of the word by asking, “What about water?” (as an object of thought). Thus she had conceived water not in immediate  relation to her present dispositions or interests –what the actually perceived water could do to her or for her in the current circumstances–  but what water as such, as itself  an agent capable of producing effects, was capable of doing in general – the collection or variety of effects it invariably or systematically  produced (and, therefore, was capable of producing) on the objects exposed to its action, independently of their dispositions and projects.

That in this latter case, for Keller, the name “water’ functioned as an object of thought, as concept of a perceptual object to be enriched through observation or through interaction with the object symbolized by the name rather than an as a particular existent actually encountered shows in the abstraction from distances of exposure as revealed by the indexicality of perception.  The very indexicality or directionality of water to the perceiving subject had disappeared. No distances of any kind unfolded from water as conceived by Keller to Keller herself, because water was – at least provisionally, until actually perceived with distances of exposure– a mere concept-water, an object of thought symbolized rather than signified  by its name, not a thing actually situated in a particular place within the world inhabited by Helen Keller.

Now, this water-concept, this abstract subject of enunciative predications would eventually be enriched by Keller’s own experiences and observations of water, as well as by the observations and  experiences of others linguistically communicated to Keller, and would eventually form Keller’s notion of water, as well as the notion of water registered in the dictionaries of Helen’s language. All that, or a portion of it, would be signified by the word “water,” when actually used by Keller and the other English speakers to refer to the concept-water, rather than to the concrete object to be interacted with. It would be signified by the word but merely as symbolized or connoted by the word, that is, as merely conceived, not as denoted in presence or imminent presence as a concrete object for interaction with it.

If so, however, when used connotationally rather than denotationally, names function for us as conceptual tokens of the object named, not as signs-FOR. Because the animal’s perception is not linguistic, that is, not mediated by a concept that symbolizes the object, animals cannot deal in a conceptual and non-immediately interested way with the objects they encounter in perception, as we are able to do thanks to our ability to attach genuinely linguistic names to the objects we perceive.

Let me elaborate. Words signify only when actually used used by someone to mean something. But that is not how words function in the dictionary. The dictionary defines or exemplifies the conventional meaning of words as words, that is, as the conventional signs words are. Out of any context, the uttered sound ‘w-a-t-e-r’, or the written word in the dictionary, may evoke in me, who am familiar with the English language and have a history of experiencing the object named, certain images and predicates related to the perceptual object we English speakers call water. However, the word as it is in the dictionary is not a linguistic sign of those images, for it is not actually used or uttered to signify any of those images in particular. When a name is properly used, it is used in a sentence and uttered in a particular perceptual situation. The tenor of the sentence with its temporal and spatial qualifiers, as well as the situation in which the sentence is uttered, usually indicate to the hearer (or reader) whether the word is used connotationally , that is, to refer to the notion or concept of the object named, or denotationally, that is, to refer directly to the concrete object named as immediately or imminently present and to be interacted with immediately.

As already said, when  used connotationally, names function they function as symbolic proxies for,  or tokens of, the concept of their respective objects. However, it is important to realize that they still refer to the objects named, though only indirectly and via the concept, for the names remain names of the objects they name and  and not of others. That this indirect reference –be it imaginational and concrete or strictly conceptual and abstract– remains attached to the name is manifested by the fact that the only legitimate or valid way to enrich the concept of a perceptual object with new predicates is either actual observation of the effects they produce when they act, or actual experience of its sensed effects through interaction with them. Were it not for this indirect reference, the return to observational experience of the object either to control the validity of the  predicates attached to the concept of the object, in the scientific practice, or to interactive experience with the objet named –that is, the actual application of the name in ordinary percepetion, would make no sense. ^(F8)

Now, at least one  important aspect of our ability to use NAMES connotationally is that   it makes possible for us the practice that we call “science,” with all its huge benefits . Plato speaks of the silent language through which the thinker communes with himself  when he thinks. He is referring to the connotational use of language, which allows us to refer indirectly to perceptual objects through their concepts, and thus to enrich our notions of the objects in our surrounding world independently of our immediate desires to whose satisfaction they may be useful. Animals cannot do that. This is the sense in which “knowledge” is disinterested, as strongly underscored by Plato and Aristotle, for we do name and think of objects independently of their capacity to satisfy our needs. However, Plato and Aristotle failed to realize that, when the objects thought about are perceptual, concrete objects, there is still is, through their concepts, an indirect reference to them as they are for us in perceptual observation and in interactive experience with them. Not only can their concepts be enriched only through perceptual observation or interactive experience of them, and their phenomenal or explanatory predicates obtained through personal experience can be communicated linguistically to others and thus enrich their concept of those objects, but, under the appropriate conditions of actual perception and of desire that render those objects instrumental , the return to experience is applicative . In other words, the return is of the order of interested action to satisfy our needs and desires, though that return may be preceded by a long period of provisionally theoretical, that is, connotational or conceptual  use of the name, be it to enrich the concept, or to understand  form an explanatory theory of the named object’s action, or to test a theory, or to verify a ‘law’ concerning his constant behavior, etc. Think of physics as provisionally theoretical or explanatory grasp of the action of physical objects, a grasp which makes certain projects possible and which is ultimately  based in perceptual observation, and think  of engineering as interested instrumental application either through solving problems on paper (planning action) or in reality (actual instrumental action). Think, too, of the practical origin of geometry and of the instrumental value of the linguistic theorems because of their indirect reference to the order of experience.

Back to Polanyi’s views on language

This long detour concerning points which are only implicit in Polanyi’s presentation of his view was a necessary preface to grasp fully Polanyi’s ideas on language and on the relationship in which knowledge stands to language. To those ideas we return now.  

Polanyi expounds repeatedly the difference between the perception of a sign as a sign and and the  perceptual observation of an object’s characteristics by remarking that they are not only different but incompatible with each other. For instance, he observes,  if  when using a musical partition to play a piece in the piano ^(F9) I try to observe and become aware of the individual characteristics of the notes written on the partition, my playing gets disturbed and ultimately becomes impossible. The reason is that the written notes, as all things perceived, are signs FOR, not real things to be known themselves, as perception is traditionally imagined to function. The correct perception or grasp of signs as signs is manifested not in the awareness or in the verbal enunciation of their individual characteristics of the object named, as we are used to assume, but  in the production, at the appropriate time, of the action they signal, for signs are signs-For performing an action,. Because the musical notes are written on the partition, they can be mistaken for object of observation or observed objects. But this distracts the attention from their musical meaning and interferes with the performance of the action signaled because the sign ceases to be perceived as a sign-For, as it should. In the case of oral linguistic exchanges the words uttered cannot be observed; at most they can be remembered once they have been uttered and  heard. This would  interfere even more with the performance of the action meant by the sign. I cannot comprehend or be referred to the signified if my attention is directed to something other than the signified, t thereby making the words to cease functioning as signifiers.. This is why the two operations –hearing signfiers as signfiers by being referred to the signified as a signified, and attending to the signifiers as objects, that is, attending to them as non-signifiers, are not only incompatible but mutually exclusive.

  

To appreciate the difference between sign OF and sign FOR, implicit in Polanyi’s ideas on TK by contrast to perception or what ordinarily goes by the name of knowledge, let us take a concrete example . I stated above that a red traffic light signals the action of stopping. Such formulation, however, is very imprecise, for what a red traffic light signifies is not the action of stopping as something to become aware OF –an awareness OF that can be enunciated or said and made understood to others, but as an action to be performed. Since this performance is what is actually signified, the red light actually functions as a sign only if the person actually stops, that is, if the action signaled is performed, but not if the person who reads the sign simply becomes aware that the action of stopping is meant by the red light and even manifests this awareness by enunciating that the action of stopping is signified. In such case there is talk about signs, not exposure to them. [Searle, who is obsessed with enunciation, confuses the enunciation of the rule with following the rule] Moreover, not only is not the enunciation of the action signified not equivalent to the performance of the action, but the enunciation is not even required. . A child may learn to stop at red on a traffic light long before being linguistically capable of enunciating that the red light is a sign for stopping. And so may a well-trained dog which, even more obviously than a child, is radically incapable of making any linguistic enunciation..

This may help us to grasp the radical difference between Searle’s and Polanyi’s respective conceptions of language. Both of them think that a language functions as a system of signs. But of signs OF object of knowledge, or of signs FOR action? Their respective answers to this all-important question differ.

Let us consider Searle’s answer first. ThatSearle thinks that language is primarily a system of signs OF sosething to be aware of and capableof being enunciated is reflected in his entire view of language . Indeed, that is  the reason why he attributes to the illocutionary effect, which consists in knowledge OF object, absolute primacy over the perlocutionary effects, which consist in action. As he himself explains, the uniqueness of language, and its superiority over all other signs, is not that language means, for that is common to linguistic signs with all other signs, but that language says, that is, enunciates, what it means. Remember his analysis of the difference between language and games: a certain move in the game of chess means checkmate in the sense of signaling that the checkmated person is out. However, unlike the linguistic enunciation, the move in the game does not say or enunciate or express in words what it means. S aying, then, in Searle’s view, is making known through words that express or enunciate or are signs-OF something  to be known. Indeed, according to him, the primary function of language is to inform or make known. Consequently, not only does Searle privilege the enunciative linguistic form because it is the only purely informative linguistic form, but, as already explained, he subordinates the speech acts that have perlocutionary force to the illocutionary ones –in that in order to have my hearer do what I want him to do I have first to inform him of what I want him to do by enunciating what I want him to do. In so doing Searle implicitly assimilates the speech acts that have  perlocutionary force to illocutionary ones, for he hears them as enunciative OF something to be known through understanding and as something to be merely understood. This is why Searle thinks that making known the perlocutionary effects one wants to obtain is a necessary condition for obtaining those effects.

As a striking example of Searle’s subordinating of all the other speech acts to the enunciative ones let me mention his denial that the American soldier performed a linguistic action even if he obtained the perlocutionary effect he intended  by uttering the sounds “ Ich komme von dem Land wo die Zitronen blühen. ,” The reason Searle does not consider linguistic the Italians’ action is that the soldier did not say or enunciate “I am an American soldier,” which is what he intended to communicate and which needed to be understood by the Italians in order to treat the soldier as a German soldier. ^(F10) In this case, therefore, the action intended by the American speaker obtained in a non-linguistic way, not as a result fo what was said by the speaker.

But the most clear indication of Searle’s privileging of the enunciative linguistic form shows in  what he calls “the principle of expressibility,” to wit: that anything that can be meant can (and, ideally, should) be expressed  in words that actually say or enunciate what one wants to make known, that is, have replicated in the mind of the hearer. ^(F11) This demand of expressing in words what one wants to make known makes sense only on the assumption that language is primarily a system of signs OF something to be known, rather than a system of signs For performing certain actions. Needless to say, the principle of expressibility is the opposite of the conception of language and knowledge as tacit. According to Searle, the enunciation of what the speaker is aware of and wants to communicate is necessary because language is a system of signs-OF. But if    it is a system of signs-For, as Polanyi conceives it, then not only the objectification through enunciation is not required but it interferes with the correct functioning of language.  

It is important, however, to remark that Searle treats the enunciative linguistic form, to which he subordinates all other speech acts, as if it itself were a speech act. But it is not. Speech acts function as such only when meant a such by a particular speaker to a particular hearer or hearers in particular circumstances. But enunciating in general or saying in general, which is what Searle takes it to be the primary function of language, does not function as a speech act. Of course nothing can be said without saying something in particular. But what Searle calls “saying” is nothing else than the customary meaning of words by themselves –the very customary meaning he castigates Grice for confusing with what is meant in actual speech acts. ^(F12) Now, as Searle himself observes, words mean only when used in concrete speech acts. In short, Searle’s entire conception of the primary function of language as a system of signs that say or enunciate what they mean is grounded in the confusion of the enunciative linguistic form with the speech act of saying something to somebody in particular circumstances!

For Polanyi, by contrast, all speech acts, enunciative speech acts included, are meant to function, not as  signs OF the characteristics of an object to be known, but as signs FOR performing an action, that is, as instruments that guide the agent in the performance of the action meant. The difference between enunciative speech acts and the other speech acts is not that the enunciative speech acts are signs OF something to be known rather than signs FOR an action to be performed as is the case with all speech acts, but that the enunciative speech acts do not need to specify the action meant and expected from the hearer because its performance depends on the desires the hearer and on the extent to which the circumstances  make instrumentally possible the realization of the hearer desires, much as it happens to be the case with  perception, when the perceived object functions as a sign-instrument for the performance of an action.

*As to why this difference, the reason is that linguistic agents, as such, are capable, because they are linguistic agents, of applying circumstantially (not automatically, as animals do), since the actual meaning of genuinely linguistic signs depends at least in part on the  circumstances. Their actions are linguistic, and words do not cause actions but are signs for actions conceived and meant by the subject who says or hears the words as signs depending on his present desires and on the circumstances.

This is obvious in the case of utterances that have what Searle calls “perlocutionary force.” For an order is to be obeyed and a request is to be granted by actually  performing the appropriate time the action commanded or requested, not by conceiving or enunciating or knowing the action to commanded or requested. Moreover,  the same is the case with enunciations and statements. For, contrary to what Searle thinks, saying is not a sign OF what is said, and so it is not meant as a sign to conceive or imagine what is meant by what is said (as a copy of)  what is said, but a sign FOR linguistic action in response to what is said. As Polanyi’s analysis of tacit knowledge progresses, his emphasis shifts from the tacit character or lack of expression of the characteristics of the object known to what he calls “INTERIORIZATION, ” which relates knowledge to action and manifests the instrumental function of what is known.

“The identification of tacit knowing with indwelling involves a shift of  emphasis in our conception of tacit knowing. Since we were not attending to the particulars in themselves, we could not identify them; but if we now regard the integration of particulars as an interiorization , it takes on a more positive character. “I nhabiting” or “dwelling in” are terms repeatedly used by Polanyi by contrast to “observing” or “perceiving or “knowing,” which refer to an object of knowledge as traditionally conceived.   Polanyi’s notion interiorization would have gained much in precision if he had made explicit that “inhabiting” and “indwelling”  refer to instruments for action used by an agent, not to objects of knowledge as knowledge is traditionally understood. Thus, to take one of Polanyi’s examples, the stick in the hands of the blind person in which the blind person “indwells” to find his way is not what the person observes and knows but the instrument he uses to guide his steps to the place he wants to reach. Similarly, the eyeglasses worn by a person are not what the person knows or sees or  but the instrument he uses to guide his action to achieve the purposes he desires.

What, precisely, is interiorized here?  The stick and the eyeglasses. The stick, in the sense that rather than being perceived itself as an object in the environment,  it is assimilated to the agent’s body to the point that it ceases to count or function as an external object and functions as an extension of the agent’s body. Similarly,  when worn by an agent, the eyeglasses are not seen or perceived themselves, but are assimilated to the agent’s own body and function as an extension of the eyes of the agent’s body to guide him in his action. When a pair of glasses rest on a table and I look at them, they do not function as eyeglasses but as object known. I see the eyeglasses . When I wear the eyeglasses, however, I do not see the eyeglasses, I use them as instruments to find my way as an agent in the realization of my projects. The eyeglasses now function as an extension of my eyes, which I do not see but which I use as instruments for my action. My eyes only function as objet known when I look at them in the mirror, but ordinarily they function as the instruments I use to act efficiently in the realization of my projects. Polanyi calls interiorization this shift of an object  from object known in the environment of the agent to its functioning as an extension of the agent’s own body when actually used as an instrument, to indicate that the object is, so to say, assimilated to the agent’s body. ^(F13)

Interiorizing something , Polanyi explains –and that which is interiorized is in each and all of Polanyi’s examples an instrument for action-- is to identify ourselves with it by making it function as the immediate term of TK in the way we use our own body when we are actually engaged in action. Because of this assimilation of the instrument interiorized to our own body Polanyi speaks of “interiorization.” Perhaps the metaphorical value of the term will be better appreciated if we compare the way in which a blind person uses a stick to guide his action with the way we use our own body when we move in total darkness in a place with which we are not very familiar. In that case our situation is comparable to that of a blind person. Instinctively,  in order to find our way, we extends our arms in front of our body, so as to touch the objects that are on our way before hitting them, thereby being able to avoid them. But this we achieve not by seeing those objects, but  by using the sensations we experience when he touches those objects as instruments to guide our steps to avoiding them and to move towards the place we want to reach.

As already indicated, in connection with internalization Polanyi uses such terms as “inhabiting” or “indwelling” always by contrast to “observing” or “paying attention to” something  whose characteristics we want to know, that is, to bring to our full awareness, so as to be able to enunciate them . Inhabiting or indwelling, not observing,  is what  we do normally when we perceive. We perceive as signs the objects we encounter perceptually. When we come across a sign we do not observe or pay attention to the sign as such; we glide through it, so to say, toward  the performance of the action signified or called for by the sign. Instead of paying attention to the sign itself, we “inhabit” or “dwell in” it toward the action signaled, which is the appropriate way to perceive signs. We interiorize the sign in that we use it as an instrument that guides us in the performance of the action signaled.

When playing the piano, the pianist is using it as an instrument to produce the desired music. In that case, the consciousness he has of the keys he touches is instrumental. If the word knowledge is to be employed in this context, it has to be described as “tacit,” for involves no awareness and internal or external enunciation of the object’s characteristics. This is totally different from the consciousness of object with which the ordinary notion of knowledge (and of  saying or enunciating) are identified. So much so that, as just explained by Polanyi,  if, in the process of playing, that is, of having tacit knowledge of his fingers and of the keys they touch, the pianist turns his attention to his fingers or to the piano’s keys and observes them, thereby turning them into objects of knowledge, automatically  his activity of playing becomes impaired. Similarly, if, while reading a text, we shift our attention from the meaning we are getting through reading to the letters that compose the text in order to know them as such, our reading becomes impaired. While reading we only have a tacit awareness of the written signs, for we are not attending to them as such but using them as instruments to get their meaning. If we turn our attention to the signs, we gain awareness of their characteristics, but they cease to function as instruments that signify the performance of an action. These two forms of consciousness are not only different from each other but incompatible with each other, which is the reason why we cannot get the meaning if we try to know the letter themselves.  

To summarize, Polanyi calls “interiorization ” the special, non-objective, quasi oblique consciousness we have of what we  actually use as an instrument to achieve a purpose. The reason for the name is that we assimilate this instrument to our own body, since the consciousness we have of our own body when we act is itself instrumental and the ultimate model of tacit knowledge:

“ But our awareness of our body for attending to things outside it suggests a wider generalization of the feeling we have of our body . Whenever we use certain things for attending from them to other things,  in the way in which we always use our own body, these things change their appearance. They appear to us now in terms of the entities to which we are attending from them, just as we feel our body in terms of the things outside to which we are attending from our body. In this sense we can say that when we make a thing function as the proximal term of tacit knowing, we incorporate it in our body or extend our body to include it –so that we come to dwell in it.” ^(F15)

The generalization of the awareness each of us has of his or her own body at the time of action as the basic model of the interiorization or assimilation to our own body involved in TK gives Polanyi the key to understanding the mechanism of agency. He finds that mechanism exemplified in a large number of particular cases. In the first place, in the case of the acquired bodily skills, such as playing the piano or typing, or even those more complex skills such as driving a car.  These skills are fundamentally similar to the way a blind person ‘drives’ his own body with the  help of a stick. They are acquired as a result of interiorization through practice; thanks to repetition, the agent transforms the stick, the typewriter, the piano, the car into extensions of his own body by using them as he uses his own body. At the end of that long practice of interiorization the use of those objects becomes as natural to the agent as the use of his own body in, say, the action of walking. When a child learns to walk, he is guided by the sensations he experiences on the plants of his feet every time he makes a step, but the does not focus his attention on them, he does not observe his  sensations, they are only “that from which ” he makes the subsequent steps toward the place he wants to reach.

The typewriter, the piano, the car are examples of material instruments invented by human agents to multiply the efficiency of their agency and extend its reach in order to satisfy first their inherited needs and then the desires that emerge as the transformation of the surrounding world renders possible the emergence of such desires. However, in addition to the invention of material instruments people also invent symbolic (that is, significant)  instruments , among which the most important are spoken and written language, because they make possible the invention of all other symbolic instruments . such as mathematical symbols, scientific theories, etc.

Along with the example of acquired bodily skills as examples of TK, Polanyi refers us to the use of  maps by an agent who, with their help, attempts to find his way in an unfamiliar territory. Before analyzing that case, though,  let us remark that the reading and actual use of maps are far more complex operations than they seem to be to those who have already mastered them. Actually, a child, even a child who has already acquired the skill of reading written language, is not thereby automatically capable of using a map to find his way in unfamiliar territory, much less of drawing a map to be used by others. The reason for this difficulty is that a map is not a picture of a territory, any more than a name is a picture of the object named. A map is a symbolic sign of a territory, but not of a territory to become aware of, but of a territory to be covered. In other words, not only is a map to be deciphered or ‘read,’ as all symbols are, but to be deciphered in relation to the action of moving through the territory to which the map refers, in order to reach a particular place within that territory. This amounts to saying that a map is a symbolic instrument to be used by an agent as an instrument to achieve his goal of moving through the mapped territory toward a place he wants to reach. That is why, if somebody claimed to understand a map but proved himself incapable of using it in practice, he wouldn’t know what he is saying. Indeed, since the map is an instrument for action, understanding it amounts to being able to manifest that understanding in one’s action. The mistake of the person claiming to understand a map but being incapable of actually using it to find his way would be comparable to the one Searle makes when he claims that a person who is given an order understands it if only he grasps objectively the meaning of the words, without realizing that an order is to be obeyed, not to be understood objectively. In short, the genuine understanding of a map, as in the case with any other instument, is to be manifested in the conduct that the map is meant to render possible, that is, in the conduct of reaching the desired place within the territory to which the map refers, much as the genuine understanding of a verbal joke is to be manifested in the affection of being actually amused by it.

Polanyi’s view of reading  maps  goes as follows. The agent who reads the map must “inhabit” the map as he inhabits his body when he acts to realize a project, for instance, when he is nailing a nail to hang a picture. In other words, he must make the figure on the map function not as an object to be looked at and observed in itself, but as “that from which” he must act in order to arrive at the desired place. In short,  TK is always practical, for it involves the use of an instrument by an agent to achieve an intended goal, not a fctual representation of object.

A more striking example of a symbolic instrument mentioned by Polanyi is the case of scientific theories, on which I will focus in the next chapter. Used, as we are, to think of knowledge as a visual or intellectual representation of the mater theorized about, we will be surprised by the idea that scientific theories, like maps for action, function instrumentally rather than representationally. In other words, we tend to consider the statement in which, for instance, a physical law is expressed as a symbolic representation of the intelligible ‘nature’ – as Aristotle called it–  of the particular phenomenon to which the law refers. For his part, Polanyi argues that the statements of scientific laws are meant to be used by the scientific agent as instruments to guide his conduct in the solution of concrete problems related to the portion of the world to which the law refers, much as a map is meant to function as an instrument for the person who uses it to find his way within the mapped territory. In the following text,  Polanyi explains how theories function in the actual practice of science, that is, when the theoretical instrument is actually used rather than talked about:

“To rely on a theory for understanding nature is to interiorize it. For we are attending from the theory to things seen in its light, and we are aware  of the theory, while thus using it, in terms of the spectacle that it serves to explain. This is why mathematical theory can be learned only by practicing its application: its true knowledge lies in our ability to use it.” ^(F16)

This text, quoted  in isolation from its context, may be too dense and too cryptic to make us understand how, in Polanyi’s view, scientific theories function. What is clear is that Polanyi’s view is at odds with the prevailing conception of science as a an exact, or at least an increasingly approximative, representational  view of reality. Thomas Kuhn, who on this fundamental point  repeatedly indicates his agreement with Polanyi, expressed it forcefully:

“A scientific theory is usually felt to be better than its predecessors not only in the sense that it is a better instrument for discovering and solving  puzzles but also because it is somehow a better representation of what nature is really like. One often hears that successive theories grow ever closer to, or approximate more and more closely to, the truth. Apparently generalizations like that refer not to the puzzle solutions and concrete predictions derived from a theory but rather to its ontology, to the match, that is, between the entities with which the theory populates nature and what is ‘really there.’ Perhaps there is some other way of salvaging the notion of ‘truth’ for application to whole theories, but this one will not do.... the notion of a match between the ontology of a theory and its ‘real’ counter part in nature now seems to me illusive in principle” ^(F17)

What Kuhn is pointing out here is that the statements in which scientific theories are expressed are not meant to function as representations of reality but as symbolic instruments to be used by agents to solve  problems concerning the surrounding world. In this regard, though, scientific statements constitute no exception. Indeed, if Polanyi is right, no statement –whether scientific or not– is  meant to function as a representation of its referent; rather, all statement are meant to function as  instruments for action.

Searle and Polanyi on language

We are now well equipped to determine which of the two authors whose views on language we have studied is right. According to Searle, by replicating in his own mind what the speaker intends to communicate, thanks to his comprehension of what is said, the hearer gains the knowledge of what the speaker means or wants to communicate. Thus, according to Searle,  at least in the case of linguistic communication, knowledge consists in the correspondence between what is meant and said by the speaker and what is understood by the hearer. This, needless to say, coincides with the ordinary notion  of knowledge, which the Scholastics, following Aristotle, defined as adaequatio mentis ad rem : the conformation of the knower’s mind to the object. The only difference is that here Searle extends that conception from the order of extramental reality to which it originally was applied by Plato and Aristotle, to the order of linguistic communication. Now, precisely regarding  this conception of knowledge as correspondence  Polanyi parts ways with Searle.  

For Polanyi knowing is not a matter of becoming aware of the object to be known by acquiring a sort of reproduction of it that corresponds to the object, and which is true in the measure in which it corresponds to the object. For Polanyi, knowledge is tacit because it does not aim at reproducing –nor does it reproduce– the object in the mind by enunciating its characteristics, in which case the knowledge of the object would be explicit rather than tacit. Knowledge consists, rather,  in the practical  disposition to ACT in the way appropriate to achieve the knower’s goal, and in actually enacting that disposition at the appropriate time. Thus, in the case of the shock-producing syllables, the subject has no awareness of the individual characteristics of the syllables; for that very reason the subject cannot say or express in words which are those characteristics of which he has no visual awareness. What the subject acquires, thanks to his past painful experience of being shocked, is not a faithful  image of the syllables. Rather,  the subject has a natural disposition to avoid pain and, hence, to perceive what produces pain not as object of ‘knowledge’ in its particular characteristics, but as to be avoided through action. This  practical disposition to avoid the shock manifests itself the subject’s in his bracing himself for the shock when the shock-producing syllables appear again on the screen, in order to protect himself as much as possible from the shock.

Language as representational or language as instrumental?

Regarding those acts in which a perlocutionary effect is intended by the speaker, I already indicated in the last chapter how unpersuasive I find Searle’s view that in every act of communication the speaker intends to produce the knowledge of his communicative intentions –that is, the illocutionary effect–  and that this knowledge suffices to insure the success of the communicative act. For this means, concretely, as Searle maintains, that when I give you an order, what I intend primarily is that you understand and thereby be informed or acquire factual knowledge that I give you an order, not that you obey my order.

Why do I think that Searle is wrong on this point? Because this aspect of Searle’s characterization is at odds with the practice of the competent linguistic agents. It is true that I cannot disobey an order without understanding it and thereby knowing it. But what is in contention is what constitutes the appropriate knowledge or understanding of an order and, in general, of all those linguistic acts that possess perlocutionary force, such as threats, warnings, promises, demands, etc. According to Searle, the primary intention of the speaker in all of the them is to inform the hearer of what he means, and only secondarily and only accidentally to achieve the perlocutionary effect typical of the particular perlocutionary act, such as obeying it in the case of an order, expecting the fulfillment of the promise in the case of promising, etc. Hence, the necessary and sufficient condition for the success of the speech acts that possess perlocutionary force is that the hearer be informed of what the speaker says, whereas the actual execution of the perlocutionary effect by the hearer is not essentially required.   

However, Polanyi would claim that the linguistic facts contradict Searle’s opinion. If  Searle were right, there would be no difference in actual use between the grammatical imperative, employed by alll competent speakers when they give an order, and the grammatical indicative, properly used only when one wants to inform of a fact. Furthermore, the imperative mood in the English language  would be totally superfluous,  for imperatives should be understood as  indicatives. Indeed,  if Searle were right, giving an order (in the imperative) would be exactly equivalent to saying or enunciating (in the indicative) that one gives an order. But such is not the case. As I have already argued, when I give an order, I do not say – in the sense of enunciating – something to be merely understood, to wit: that I give an order; rather, I say the order I give in the sense of giving an order to be obeyed. When I say something in the sense of enunciating it, I refer my hearer to what I enunciate as something to be understood, that is, I intend to produce in my interlocutor the knowledge in which, according to Searle, the illocutionary effect consists. Therefore, if in giving an order I were enunciating that I give an order and the order I give, I should be satisfied if the addressee responded  “I understand your order.” But that is not the case. When I give an order –and this is why I use the imperative, not the indicative–  I expect and intend my order to be obeyed, which is the appropriate ‘understanding’ of an order by a competent linguistic agent. The order I give is meant to be used by the addressee as an instrument to guide his action, not a a picture of theat of which the speaker is aware.   

Moreover, Searle’s opinion is also at odds with the practice of linguistic agents not only concerning what he calls ”perlocutionary effects,” but also in the case of statements, in which only an illocutionary effect seems to be intended.. For, as I have already argued, contrary to what Searle thinks, in a real-life situations, by contrast to the artificial classroom situation in which people are learning a second language, linguistic agents do not consider their linguistic action successful if a purely theoretical  understanding of what is said obtains; success, for linguistic agents, is identical with linguistic action, that is, with action that results by virtue of what is said.            

It is about time we turn our attention to the speech acts that express a purely illocutionary intention. Let us therefore ask if, at least in their case, Searle is right in seeing their function as purely representational of what is t he case. As for Polanyi, he claims that they are meant to function as instruments to guide action, not as signs OF anything, certainly not as signs of what is the case.

Imagine, then, that the telephone rings. I take the phone and, turning myself toward my friend who is also in the room, I tell him, “Your sister is so the phone.” This is a very ordinary statement in the English language. Now, if Searle is right, I am attempting to communicate to my friend my awareness of what is the case, and he will gain the knowledge of the fact of which I am aware by “comprehending” what I say to be the case. Now, if Searle were right, a perfect ly adequate conduct on my friend’s part would be to tell me, “I understand that my sister is on the phone,” and to do nothing in relation to what I told him. However, I, who on this point disagree with Searle, as a competent speaker of English would be very disconcerted by my friend’s conduct, because I expect as the appropriate response to my statement to be for my friend to take the telephone and talk to his sister, or at least to explain why he would not do that. To put the same otherwise, I expect my statement to function as an instrument by which my friend guides his

action, as “that from which” he acts, as a sign FOR, not a s a sign OF.  

Similarly if I tell my friend who is looking for the hammer, “The hammer is in the next room,” I expect it to go to the next room and take the hammer, for my statement was not meant to communicate to him an objective vision of what I deem to be the case, with no intrinsic connection to action. And the same is the case with any statement referring to a perceptual state of the world by which speaker and hearer are surrounded and in which they find themselves.

As a last example of the instrumental rather than representational way in which statements actually function I will refer to the imaginary case cited by Searle himself, who does not realize its implications because he considers it from a different angle. I am thinking of the curious example of the American soldier in Italy. In order to make the Italians believe that he is German and, therefore, their ally rather than their enemy, the soldier uses the only German sentence he knows, borrowed from a poem: “Ich komme von dem Land wo die Zitronen blüen .” The sentence means

“I come from the land where the lemon trees flourish.” Searle’s point is that, even if the American  had succeeded in tricking the Italians into believing that he was German and they had set him free, the Italians’ action wouldn’t have been genuinely linguistic because the American did not tell them in so many words “Set me free because I am German.” What Searle fails to consider is that the American soldier, unlike Searle himself, had the correct idea of the way in which statements actually function, for his trick consisted precisely in having the Italians hear his statement instrumentally , (as statements are meant to be heard), and thus he expected     His statement to result in an action, their action of setting him free, rather than their hearing it representationally of his being  German and result in their purely factual awareness with no connection to action.

To bring this chapter to an end, let me condense its central point, which concerns the relationship between knowledge and its linguistic expression.. Searle uncritically accepts the traditional notion of knowledge as awareness of what is the case. Such knowledge finds its natural expression in enunciative speech whose content, if genuine knowledge, corresponds to what is actually the case in reality. Polanyi, for his part, substitutes this contemplative epistemic subject of the Platonic-Aristotelian tradition by the agent situated within the world he perceives and exposed to its action, as reflected in the way language actually functions. Because the way language functions is at odds with Searle’s views, they are unpersuasive when carefully examined. I have tried to demonstrate that in the two   previous chapters, availing myself of Polanyi’s views as a point of contrast. As fort the fertility of the Polanyian substitution of the contemplative knower for the agent, we shall test it in the coming chapter by applying it to the field of the scientific practice.
