                       THE QUESTION OF GOD IN THE PLAGUE

- Why don't I lose my faith in God when an innocent child suffers and  dies,
or when a young man has both his eyes destroyed? Because I am not  a  theist
but a Christian. Bertrand Russell's argument applies to the theist, for  the
god  of  theism--the  god  of  philosophers--may  be,  for  all   we   know,
indifferent or cruel. But the  God  of  Christians  is  Claudel's  God,  who
doesn't explain or suppress suffering, but who came to suffer and  die  with
us.

- Faith is primarily trust in God. Trust like that of the child who goes  to
the hospital to be operated and suffer without understanding why, but  whose
mother's hand who takes him to the hospital reassures him  that  it  is  for
his or her own good.

- On the proofs for God's existence. The question of God is not a matter  of
proofs.  One   cannot   demonstratively   justify   atheism   anymore   than
Christianity or any other form of belief in  God.  Like  the  issue  of  the
Other's  existence,  the  issue  of  God  is  not  a  matter  of  logic,  of
demonstration. Even Camus acknowledges this, since he doesn't  anymore  dare
to say that Christians are wrong; only that he has been unable  to  believe.
If atheism were logically justified... But it is not. No proof  or  argument
justifies atheism. In that sense, atheism is also a leap of faith. The  fact
is that, when one is confronted with the problem of  evil,  one  is  already
either a believer or an atheist. In other words,  one  never  confronts  the
absurdity of life 'objectively,' that is, from  a  neutral  point  of  view.
Now, from the standpoint  of  the  Christian,  suffering  and  death  appear
mysterious (for there is no  explanation)  but  not  incompatible  with  the
existence of God, given that the God in whom the Christian  puts  his  faith
came to suffer and die with us. By contrast,  from  the  standpoint  of  the
atheist, suffering and death appear incompatible with God's existence.  Now,
there is no neutral  standpoint  from  which  suffering  and  death  can  be
considered.

- The  question  of  God  is  an  existential  question,  like  the  Other's
existence.
