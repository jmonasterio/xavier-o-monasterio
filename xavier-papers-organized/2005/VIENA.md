\wp\chantill\Viena                           July 22, 1992
                       Les droits de l'homme
      There is tension between the universal and the particular.
Human rights express the universal. They refer to a an abstract individual
human being who is neither here nor there, neither now nor then, neither
male nor female, neither poor nor rich, neither Mexican nor American,
neither sick nor healthy, neither religious nor a-religious, whose identity
is defined in terms of needs and  aspirations which are supposedly common
to all human beings as such and which are considered to be equally
fundamental and of equal importance.  Obviously, no such individual exists
or can possibly exist. Yet this view of the human being presupposes that it
is representative of, and realized in, each actually existing individual,
that all the particularities of the kind mentioned above are merely
accidental to human individuality and can therefore be safely ignored, and
that either there is no possible tension between this universal human
identity and the particularities mentioned, or at least that if such
tension arises it is always to be resolved in favor of the essential
universal rather than of the accidental particulars. More concretely:
nationality, sex, race, family ties, religious commitment, etc., actually
are--and are to be treated as--irrelevant in ethical matters.
      One thought that occurs to me in this connection is how close this
conception of the individual is to Kant's picture of the autonomous,
rationally self-motivating and self-sufficient individual in the Kingdom of
Ends. The connection, I think, is far from accidental. Indeed, Kant is both
a child of the same Enlightenment--rationally self-motivating and self-
sufficient--that gave birth to "les droits de l'homme" and, at the same
time, the most forceful and influential of its foundational theoreticians.
      A second thought in this connection, not unrelated to the first.
Rationality is, in this view, divorced from and opposed to affectivity.
Acting out of love of fear is below the dignity of the Kantian subject. The
only legitimate motivating force is the force of reason--the same reason
that creates the picture of the universal, self-motivating and self-
sufficient individual which excludes the alterity of individuals and
ignores the difference in circumstances.
      Finally, a third thought. History, which is all made of changing,
interrelated particulars is totally out of this picture of the human being.
      Were this picture of the human being a purely theoretical matter
without practical consequences I would not bother myself with it. But it is
not. It is a picture to which we are culturally sold and which,
correspondingly, we take as normative, as categorically or unconditionally
imperative.
      One contemporary example. The resurgence of nationalities and
nationalism in Europe. It is the triumph of the repressed particular. We
felt authorized to ignore nationalities and to repress national tendencies
in the name of the universal, as a step toward the universal Kingdom of
Ends of which we all are potential members and which represents "the end of
history," the total pacification of humankind. Naturally, we see the
resurgence of nationalism as an awesome aberration, as a breakdown in
rationality, as a tragic flare up of blind, untamed affectivity
to be opposed and somehow overcome. And, indeed, if our Kantian picture is
correct, we are right in seeing the resurgence of nationalism in this way
and in opposing it. But that is the question, the whole question, indeed:
Is the picture to which we are culturally sold, and on the basis of which
all our international organisms are chartered and attempt to function,
correct?
      To deal comprehensively with such a large question is beyond my scope
in this paper. I will restrain myself to one central aspect of the question
which has to do with tension and conflict between opposites.

      It is characteristic of the view of the "universal individual" a
certain treatment of tension and conflict. Tension and conflict imply
diversity of elements and a certain opposition between them. The universal
in the individual are needs and aspirations that are common to all human
beings and equally fundamental. All the rest--sex, nationality, race,
religion, etc.--is particular. This particular individual is male, that one
is female; this one is Mexican, that one is French... All particulars,
therefore, have been abstracted from the picture as inessential. What is
particular does not count, is irrelevant. But what is particular is not
only what distinguishes this individual from that one; it is also what
potentially opposes them. In other words, the systematic exclusion of the
particular from the universal picture entails the suppression of tension
and conflict. And if there happens to be conflict between the universal and
the particular within an individual, or between the particular and the
particular among individuals, the conflicts are always to be solved in
favor of the universal, when applicable, and the conflicts between
particularities are to be treated as either illegitimate or at least as
irrelevant.
      It is worth remarking concerning the universal individual stripped of
all particularities that no  individual can exist that is neither male nor
female, neither of this nor of that historical moment, etc. Thus, the
universal individual is a mere abstraction. Actually, however, the
universal individual is not only abstract (as opposed to concrete) but is
not even individual at all, for it stands for the universal or common in
each individual rather than for the individual in each individual, which is
the particular. This is to say that the human being which is defined by
certain needs and aspirations and which is the subject of rights in "les
droits de l'homme" is a mere intellectual concept which, as such, has
neither needs and aspirations nor any rights whatsoever.
      Yet, this intellectual concept is deemed to be representative of, and
realized as a "nature" in, each concrete, existing human being. Needless to
say, this way of conceiving things is typically Greek, Platonic in
particular, strongly reminiscent of "the Forms." And the idea of Forms has
its origin in geometry. This amounts to saying that the geometric object is
the implicit model that structures the whole view of "l'homme" and of his
"droits" in "les droits de l'homme." But can the geometric object be an apt
model for the human being and for human rights? In other words and more
crudely, can a concept of the human being modelled after the triangle of
which geometrists talk sustain such predicates as needs, aspirations and
rights?
      First of all, the geometric object is an object of contemplation, not
of interaction. Secondly, it is considered by itself, in isolation from all
other objects and abstracting from time and real space which involves
relation to other objects. Thirdly, it is considered as object to be
understood, as merely intelligible, not as to be manipulated or otherwise
dealt with. Fourthly, insofar as its universality is concerned, it is taken
as representative of a kind, not as individual or particular. Fifthly, the
characteristics of the object which are taken into consideration define the
object to the exclusion of any other characteristics that a concrete object
known by the same name in ordinary language may have, such as color, size,
time, place, etc. Sixtly, the understanding of the object so defined yields
the essence or purely intelligible characteristics of the object so
considered.
      The geometric object is, therefore, a mere intellectual concept, not
a real object. By this I mean that the actual triangle drawn by the
geometrist is not itself the geometric object on which the geometrist works
and of which he talks; the object is the triangle as conceived and defined
by the geometrist, the concept that corresponds to the definition, a tri-
angular figure--nothing else, nothing more, nothing less than this.
Consequently, what is understood by the practitioner of geometry are the
purely intelligible properties of the concept as conceived and defined.
Change the concept and the intelligible properties will change, as they
actually do if you move from the intelligible properties of the triangle to
those of the circle.
      Nonetheless, geometry has applications; thus, the surface of a
particular triangular patch of land can be calculated when the particular
measurements of the patch are taken into account. Because of this we say
that the concept of triangle is representative of all triangular surfaces
and is 'realized' in each of them. What do we mean by this? Do we mean that
the defined concept mirrors conceptually something intelligible that is
actually contained in each triangular surface? This seems to be the way in
which the Greeks, and Aristotle in particular, understood this matter. Do I
agree with them?
      Geometry grew up out of practical interests, not out of a
disinterested desire to know. Before being formalized for didactic purposes
under the form of definitions, axioms and demonstrations it existed under
the form of practical recipes or prescriptions for the realization of
concrete projects. To sell my quadrangle of land I had to calculate its
surface. The project of selling this piece of land created the practical or
calculative conceptual space for the discovery of the means. It is as
vendendum that the patch of land opens the calculative space for geometry.
      Given the desire of the end, the point is to find out the means. This
is done through the conceptualization of the quadrangle as composed of two
triangles. The concept does not include measurements, nor do the
intelligible relations understood, anymore than Galileo's law of falling
bodies on inclined planes includes measurements. In other words, size (as
color or shape) is not included in the concept as defined. Nonetheless, the
concept is abstracted from a particular triangle. In this sense, the
concept may be said to mirror or stand for the particular triangle, and the
particular triangle is indeed a triangular figure as expressed by the
definition of the concept. Otherwise the definition would apply to any
other figure. And this particular triangle's intelligibility is really
grasped in grasping the intelligibility of the concept. This, exactly, is
Aris's understanding of the matter, who is therefore rightly said to have
brought the Platonic forms to within the world. But, again, do I agree with
him?
      It is clear that the objectification of the triangle for purposes of
understanding is made possible by the desire of the end. It is clear,
furthermore, that this objectification is fruitful and necessary. What is
intriguing is that it allows the detachment of vendendum from object, so
that object can be understood by itself independently of the project. Now,
vendendum is, so to say, a 'desirous predicate,' that is, a quality that
belongs in the practical order of exposure, not in the speculative one.
Qualities of this type--motivating qualities--cannot be objectified. They
make calculation possible, but they themselves do not enter into the
calculation, for they are temporo-spatial and relative to a singular
subject through and through.  Thus, what occurs in objectification is a
shift from the order of exposure to the order of contemplation with which
we tend to identify the really objective, the really real.(The reason for
this identification is that we all are bodies and bodies as such interact
in a uniform way, the same for Chinese that for Mexican bodies). What we
conceptualize when we constitute the concept-object within the context of a
project is  the aspects of the object that are relevant for the realization
of the project. But we consider the object by itself, in its own
intelligibility, in the way in which we are purely passive to it but not as
exposed to it. The aspects that we abstract to constitute the concept-
object are real, if by real we understand that they are revealed to human
beings who are capable of owning and transferring ownership of pieces of
land and of conceiving projects. The relationships correctly understood are
real, if by real we understand that they delimit the field of conceivable
projects (not mere dreams), of what is to be expected, of what can and what
cannot be achieved. But they are not real as concepts, nor do they give us
a "picture" or "image" of "what is out there."  What they give us is a
general delineation of the practical field within which our activity and
passivity obtain, where "general" means "for-bodied-linguistic-beings-like-
us" and, therefore, potentially intelligible for all "of us."
      Activity and passivity are correlates, relative to each other,
mutually defining each other.
      What does this imply for the question of "truth"? Ortho-doxia or
ortho-praxis?
      Truth concerns primarily judgment (with assent) and statement, not
decision and action--in other words, orthodoxia rather than orthopraxis. If
my analysis is correct, orthodoxia is subordinated to orthopraxis. But it
is not subordinated in a linear but in a dialectical way. The primacy of
orthopraxis comes from the fact that the purpose of orthodoxia is
'successful' action-passion, not the other way around. But the
subordination is not linear because orthopraxis conditions and fosters
orthodoxia, and orthodoxia enhances and enlarges orthopraxis.
The Greeks error, partly corrected or at least deeply sensed by Aris, was
the primacy of orthodoxia, its separation from orthopraxis, and its
substantification as image of reality, which results in purely mechanical
rather than decisive action, in the mind-body deualism. and in all
coneptual (undialectical) dualisms in general.
      More detail is needed here. (1) Geometry is 'true' not because we
judge (understand with assent) but because it makes possible the successful
carrying out of certain projects. (I reform the judgments I made in the
past when they do not correspond to reality, that is, when they do not
allow for the successful realization of projects. By contrast, I never
reform my successful projects because I see otherwise. What happens after a
while in inverted vision is a case in point). Hence the primacy of
orthopraxis and the ultimate subordination of orthodoxy to it.  (2) This
does not imply that we do not 'see,' that we do not judge. But our seeing
is not like taking pictures (except in the aesthetic mood), for the seen is
always in the gerundive mode, transcended toward the future and defined by
a goal. Only in the reflective mode do we stop and objectify by taking
ourselves out of the field of exposure and becoming ideal contemplators.
      What did Aris discover when he realized that ethics could not be
treated as geometry and that ethical study could not result in universal
and necessary knowledge?
      In fact, he did not quite discover anything, since his written ethics
yields understanding at best, not correct action.
And because it  yields  understanding,  that  is,  knowledge,  although  not
universal  and  necessary,  amd  because  the   agent's   activity   icludes
"calculative thinking," he himself confused phronesis with an inferior  kind
of  knowledge.  Nonetheless,  he  sensed  something  important.  First   and
clearly, that practical "knowledge,"  the  agents  knowledge,  was  desirous
knowledge,  though  he  did  not  quite  realize  that  desirous,  gerundive
knowledge is not knowledge anymore, that calling it  by  the  same  name  we
call geometric knowledge "knowledge" is a bad equivocation. For it is not  a
matter of two species of the same genus. In  fact,  species  and  genus  are
categories of the abstractive intellect and apply only to concepts,  not  to
objects  of  encounter,  for  objects  of  encounter  are  not   explanatory
concepts. Second, that in order  to  reach  correct  ethical  knowledge  one
needs to be a good agent--something which Aquinas apparently denied.
      What, then, should Aris have discovered when he realized  that  ethics
could not be a strict science? That he had  shifted  regimes,  that  he  had
moved  from  the  regime  of   contemplated   spectacle   where   dichotomic
abstractive intellect can properly operate to the regime of  exposure  where
abstractive intellect cannot properly  operate.  Also,  that  under  Plato's
influence, he had attributed primacy to the contemplative  regime  and  made
it into the paradigm of knowledge, when in fact it is the other way  around.
But this is a meaningless  answer  as  long  as  it  is  not  explained  and
persuasively monstrated.
