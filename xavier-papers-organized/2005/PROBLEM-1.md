- THE QUESTION IS: HOW WAS "REASON" UNDERSTOOD?

- The answer to this question depends on how were "revelation" and "faith" understood, AND where the people who so understood reason and faith-revelation were situated in relation to them [THIS MAY BE WHAT SEARLE'S "ILLOCUTIONARY" AS KNOWING-UNDERSTANDING IGNORES]. as a protestant author has observed, the understanding of reason and of revelation-faith remained the same at least until the nineteenth century, shared by both Christians and atheists. What he does not explicitly observe and was for the most part missed by those who shared that understanding, is that the understanding is only "nominally," not "really," the same, for it is a practical understanding, a disposition to act , an understanding that consists in a particular organization of the world as a practical field.

The shift forced people to understand themselves and the world in one way in their practice and in another incompatible way theoretically, without realizing what was going on, but experiencing the effects of the dichotomy. Moreover, the shift forced people to acknowledge the supremacy of the scientialis understanding and, therefore, to judge their faith by the scientific standard to which it could not possibly live up

- WHAT IS THE RELATIONSHIP OF THIS TO BACON'S COMPLAINT?

The particular understanding of reason to which I am here referring has an historical origin : it was gradually elaborated from the eleventh to the thirteenth century, when it came to be taken for granted and institutionally supported and reinforced, to the point of appearing as natural and, therefore, not only not disputed, but indisputable because it was the very framework of disputes. My purpose is to bring this understanding as such to the fore, to show its historical formation, and to dispute its validity.

What Bacon decries--and Bonaventure fears--is the way in which this new, different, practical understanding has become institutionalized in the respective roles of the Sententiarius and the Bacularius. He senses that "liber Historiarum  est magis proprius theologiae" [quam modus quaestionum]. It is as a Christian that he decries the shift from one to the other, for he is situated in the world as a Christian. In this he was more perceptive than Aquinas who saw the shift as a progress (cf. above his Perihermen.). Bacon should have decried the shift as a human and as a humanist as well, for it radically trivialized the humanities, wich are situated and non-foundationalist . The transformation of theology, the appearance of philosophy as a different discipline side by side with theology, the tensions between them, were only symptoms of "Aristotelianism," as is the scientism of our days . For once the conviction that that there was such a thing as Aristotelian "reason" (of which two species: reason guided by faith and reason left to its own devices, that is, reason tout court, reason guided by itself), the dice were cast: a fundamental transformation of the practical field and, therefore, of the appropriate way of moving within it had taken place , both faith and the humanities were doomed. Not for nothing did Plato banish the poets from the city as the modern world banishes the believers, "oi pistontes" as well as the humanities, which deal with the imaginary and the emotional.

- WHAT, THEN, IS "ARISTOTELIANISM," the different "spirit" that transformed the cultural landscape?

I characterized it above as "the spirit of the independence of reason." It is time to spell this out, for only in this way can the reservations of the anti-Aristotelians be understood and appreciated.

A fundamental point: the independence of reason amounts to the supremacy of reason-evidentialism . If evidence is the criterion for knowledge of reality, for justified assent, then it is radically incompatible with faith, for knowledge excludes authority . Faith could perhaps make room for reason, for revelation is only of what it is necessary or useful to know for salvation. But reason cannot make room for faith, or even accept its scope to be limited by faith, for it is ultimate and reclaims as its field the whole of the intelligible and identifies the intelligible with the real. Hence, the substance of faith represented by the articuli being unamenable to evidence, faith is irrational, and reason guided or limited by faith is reason guided or limited by the irrational. Since reason is taken as 'objective' (not the reason of theexposed agent but of an invulnerable contemplator), the faithful feel entitled to appeal to Reason-evidentialism as to a neutral, impartial tribunal to settle their differences among themselves and with the non-believers in matters of non-revealed truth . Since vision is foundational of practice, once the truth is established independently of practice, the right practice will become possible--even necessary for Plato--, whereas the right practice is possible only by mere chance without knowledge of reality . In addition, the truths of faith (which being irrational cannot really be truths) can go beyond the truths of reason, but cannot contradict the truths of reason, for that would imply that there are two orders of of truth-reality. This cannot be the case, for revelation deals with reality in its two dimensions: divine and human. For the comparison between the truths of faith and the truths of reason to obtain, faith has to consist of, or be convertible into, truths to be assented to; hence the reduction of the scriptural "modi" to the "modus" affirmativus (statements), of the scriptural genres to the argumentative genre, and of the scientialis reading of the Scripture as the basis of the religious reading, NOT THE OTHER WAY AROUND .

Why did the shift occur so 'naturally'? Because the articuli fidei take the grammatical form of statements , and when attended to focally by a pure contemplator, not by an agent,seem to function as objective, that is, as statements of object of contemplation (Aquinas, Periherm). (Notice that the articles of faith, not Scripture, is Aquinas' direct referent, and they function as Aristotelian "principia"). Here is the misunderstanding . For, in fact, the articuli fidei are sedimented speech acts (as much as the commandments, among many other speech acts characteristic of the Christian idiolect and of the Chrstian faith ), meant by a competent Christian speaker-agent to competent Christian hearers-agents, abstracted from the Christian practice, and not primarily--in fact only rarely--meant as expressing and intending assent as their perlocutionary effect. By contrast, the Aristotelian scientific idiolect is exclusively constituted by speech acts (if at all) which express and exclusively intend assent as their perlocutionary effect. The two points are important for my present purpose: constituted exclusively by, and intending exclusively. - But there is another fundamental point of Aristotelianism, namely, the primacy and basicality of contemplation over action--in linguistic terms, of stating over all other speech acts . In other words, all other speech acts, which express and intend..., are parasitic on statements, which express judgments, beliefs-that (never object for response?) , whereas statements are selbststandig.

As for the scientialis reading of the Scripture being the (intelligible) basis of religious (writing and) reading , this creates the possibility and, to an extent, imposes the necessity of the project of converting theology into a science, at least into a quasi-science grounded on the vision possesed by God and the saints . It creates the possibility, because truth is convertible with intelligibility, and revelation is true; thus it has to possess the intelligibility of a system. It creates the necessity, too. For, though scientialis knowledge is not necessary for salvation (vulgaris knowledge suffices for that), those who have the opportunity of feebly achieving in this life the vision that will beatify us all should aim at that. Perhaps, too, they will be better able to direct the action of the second-class citizens of the Church.

If in the case of speech acts the mistake was to privilege  statements disregarding all other speech acts and to interpet them as speech acts of an invulnerable, objective contemplator, here it was to privilege the doctrinal and to reduce the storical to the 'objective' doctrinal .

It is important to analyze the difficulties encountered by the very perpetrators of the shift . They were used to a religious, Catholic reading of the Bible that enhanced their life of faith and for the purpose of enhancing their life of faith , that is, of loving God and their fellow-human beings. Thus they had an implicit comprehension of the world--which for them was the biblical world--, and of themselves in that world, in that history. It is true that they also were acquainted and practiced the linguistic and comparative reading of texts of the Bible, but the ultimate purpose of these ancillary disciplines, including the study of logic and the few philosophical texts they had available, was to enhance their religious-responsive reading which projected them into a sacred world-history in which they themselves lived. Also from their practice of their faith they had an implicit comprehension of their faith--as obscure (inevident) but certain, as meritful, as affectful, etc. Now, the scientialis reading of the Bible created problems for them in that it did not square with their comprehension of the text that projected them to the world, nor with the world in which they lived as exposed to it, nor with the faith they lived. This was the source of their difficulties and of their spontaneous reluctance to adopt the very different perspective and the very different 'world' into which Aristotelianism projected them. Hence their objections: (a) Fides is dark, authoritative, not evident; evidence suppresses the merit of faith; fides is not mere intellectual assent; fides is inseparable from love and action (practica); fides is not a matter of speculative intelligence. (b) The biblical tex t is not only enunciativus or assertivus in its speech acts, therefore it produces multiple perlocutionary effects; it is not fundamentally assertivus in its genre, but  historicalis, narrativus, therefore it projects the reader into a world of exposed agents, not into a system of intelligible objects;  the biblical text is normative and cannot be submitted to and judged by another norm, be it reason. (c) Hence the biblical world into which the reader is projected and meant to be projected (by the narrative force of the text and by the revelatory force of the narrated text) is a temporal, dramatic world, not an intelligible system.
