Nussbaum: Summary

Aristotle

250: Posterior Analytics: Scientific method. Scholastic interpretation.

255: Aris's 'refutation" of the thing in itself

256-7: Aris's realism: the truth is one for all thinking, language-using beings.

258: The return to experience is difficult: which of the experiences are to be regulative; whether we prefer the systematic hierarchical order of episteme, or a more elusive form of perception.

259: Intellectual "wonder". Deisre to make the world comprehensible to reason. 290: Henry James: "minds for which the vision of life is an obsession."

262: Philosophy that captivates with clarity. QUOTATION

275: Intellect does not move without orexis. Boulesis is a type of orexis.

276: Animals need selectively, go for what they see and imagine according to need. Response not to the world simpliciter but to the world as seen according to need.

    The De Motu superior to the De Anima. Phantasia and aiszesis play the same role as {PRACTICAL) intellect in an exploratory schema , in that they are concerned with drawing distinctions, while boulesis, thumos and epizumia are forms of orexis. Thus two movers of the animal, unlike in De Anioma.

277-8: Orexis doesw nothing alone without perception or thought.

The good and the possible have to come together for movement to obtain. But, in the absence of impediment, movement obtains.

279: Dog sees something as meat because needs meat, and actually desires meat because sees meat. But the cognitive faculties have to come up with a possible and available route for movement to obtain.

280: MY OBJECTION: These are explanations for an inquirer, not principles of operation of the agent. SEE 283: "The desires and beliefs of an agent are not directed at that action in such a way as to explain it."

284ff.: Deliberation on ends. 296: We do not deliberate only about means.

285: The bekousion: account of certain ethical attitudes and practices that are central in the development of an animal creature towards deliberate choice.

286: The desiderative (oretikon) in the child partakes of reason as indicated by the practice of advise, reproof and exhortation.

From bekousion to prohairesis, as explained in the Politics.

287: No access to things in themselves, to any truth beyond the deepest and most pervasive appearances. An account that succeeds in correctly articulating these appearances will have the strongest claim to the truth. Internal order, and fit with other things we believe and say.

289: Summary of Aris on action.

290: "That practical wisdom is not scientific understanding (episteme) is obvious" (EN).

291: Aris refuses to make ethics (good judgments of value) into a techne. An epistemology of value  and an account of vulnerability go hand in hand.

Aris denies the Platonic "God's eye" standpoint. Internal truth, truth in appearances, is all we can get.

292: But ethics is anthropocentric in a stronger sense: An account of time and motion which are not human time and motion. With the good, things are otherwise. There is no trans-species account of the good, though Plato attempted that single episteme of the good . He criticizes that in EN, and contrasts it with the theoretical study of nature.

MY OBJECTION: "bECAUSE HIS INTEREST IS NOT THEORETICAL  but practical." But what he wants is to understand, not to practice.

294: An anthropocentric ethics could still be scientific .

Perception of particular cases takes precedence over general accounts in ethical matters.

297: Intrinsic value: what would lack in a valuable life is of value for its own sake=choiceworthy.

297: Defining each value. Definitions are universal. Has Aris reintroduced the scientific project? Aris sees ther person whose choices are paradigms for us as using a rule. But practical wisdom is not an episteme concerned with universals but with ultimate particulars grasped through experience.

299: For Arist universal principles are rules of thumb, summaries of good judgments. For Plato they are ultimate authorities to assess the correctness of particular decisions. He scrutinizes the particular to see the universal features it exemplifies. The unexpected cannot make us revise the rule.

300: Aiszesis  is a judgment concerned with concrete particular S. qUOTATION.

305: Aris believes there is a single best human life (no relativism).

Something closer to perception than to episteme: QUOTATION.

306: Practical wisdom is not rootless perception that rejects guidance from ongoing commitments and values. Character presupposes a certain continuity in commitments.

307: The intuitive perception that constitutes practical wisdom is not an ability of the detached intellect . Prohairesis is on the borderline between the intellectual and the passional: deliberative desire..

308: Practical wisdom. Desire singles out discriminatively, informs the creature of its needs by responding to wehat will satisfy them. Proper choice combines correct selection with correct practical response.

309: Perception of the death of a loved one. Without passionate response it would be unknowable. FIRE.

310: Practical wisdom. Like archers more likely to hit the target.

Aris: from a Platonic conception of truth to prephilosophical ideas. Plato: from tradition to rational self-sufficiency.

311: If the choices of the wise are definitive of value, vicious circle?

312: The nature of aiszesis.

313: Aris seems to support correct narrative to show the truth

313-4: Hecuba: example of deliberation and practical perception.

315: Her choice arises from an antecedent conception , but not inflexible application. No first identification and then action. Her discriminatory perception is constituted by her response.

The ethical appropriateness of her response is inseparable from its concreteness. Also illumination about the gods' pity on mortals.

318: Pure luck "strikes a false note."

327: Tragic reversal that deprives of the good life.

342: For Aris, unlike Plato, from God's standpoint many goods are unperceivable.

343: The contemplative activities are the most stable and sufficient. Recognizes and rejects the tradition of the solitary, self-sufficient life.

374: Places contemplative above practical wisdom. NO CONTRADICTION .

375: mAKES ANOTHER STEP TOWARD pLATONISM: DEFENDS EUDAIMONIA AS IDENTICAL WITH THE ACTIVITY OF THE BEST PART, THE THEORETICAL INTELLECT. The temptation of an intellectual

378-9: Tragedy cannot simply represent character types; must show characters in action.

380: The above is connected with vulnerability.

384: Pity is connected with the bkelief that you yourself are vulnerable. Fear is connected with the expectation (belief) of impending harm due to our exposure.

Platonism

238: Deviates us from the possible in the name of a holy grail. Criticism valid for scholasticism.

272: Plato's intellectualistic (speculative?) account of action.

275: Plato fails to recognize orexis, reaching out for

284: Plato admits only bruthish necessity or the purely unaffected self-moving causality of reason.

289: Without being affected, there will be cleverness and even contemplative wisdom , but not  other praiseworthy elements without which life would not be a good life.

290: Platonic attempts to make the notion of good a single subject matter for a single (not a species-relative) episteme.

293: Context-relative values disqualified by Plato as of intrinsic value ( see ch. 5 ).

297: The Platonic scientist would try for a system of rules that prepares us for the demands of any situation by trying to see each situation in terms of this system. IS THIS WHAT AQUINAS SPEAKS OF WHEN HE SPEAKS OF THEOLOGY AS A PRACTICAL SCIENCE?

342: For Plato, the truly valuable (apart from the species-relative valuable) is what a perfect,. non-limited being would have reason to pursue.

384: The Platonist (or Kantian) has nothing to fear: the really valuable is under his control.

388-91: Katharsis: not intellectual clarification but practical recognition of values and of ourselves

391: Why did not Aristotle write tragedies? Is there a neutral philosophical style? Reflective and explanatory style. Aris is committed toexplanation. Respects the autonomous cognitive value of tragic poetry . The ethical works are works of interpretation, ordering of the appearances found in ordinary life and in poetry.

But they do not replace tragedies. Only tragedies giv e us illumination through and in pity and fear. Bad philosophy requires good philosophy in its stead. WHY? IS THIS UNDERSTANDING THEORETICAL OR PRACTICAL? WHAT IS THE STATUS OF ITS TRUTH COMPARED TO THE TRUTH OF TRAGEDIES?
