The Enlightenment

Bibliography

(1) The Church in the Age of Absolutism and Enlightenment , by Wolfgang Müller and others, translated from the German by Gunther J. Holst (Crossroad, N. Y. 1981. It is volume VI of the History of the Church , Edited by Hubert Jedin and John Dolan.

(2) "The Age of the Enlightenment," by Robert Niklaus, in The Age of the Enlightnement: Studies Presented to Theodore Besterman , edited by W. H. Barber and others (Oliver and Boyd, Edingurgh-London, 1967), pp. 395-412).

(1)

The name : Enlightenment, Lumières, Akufklärung ("applied since the middle of the 18th century to the intellectual progress taking place."). ("Man's self-illumination in the light of autonomous reason.")

Historical Roots : "The rationalism of the Enl. is historically inconceivable without the philosophy of Scholalsticism." "The deepest roots of the Enl. reach back to the Middle Ages" (F. Valjavec). From Christianity, a sense of the individual (freedom and responsibility) and "a secular ereinterpretaion of Christian ekschatology." According to Croce, the Enl not only emerged from the 'darkness' of the middgle agres, but alsop from the 'dawn' of

Humanism and the Rebnaissance into the full light of reason. Humanism had retrieved history and had been critical of the Church, but contrary to Voltaire it left the Historia Divina unscathed. "The changed relationship with the xtian faith is the most essential element of the connection as well as of the difference between humanism and the Enl."

Main traits (not necessarily coherent and consistent)

- Rationalism: celebration of the autonomy and supremacy of reason. Radicalization of Western rationality. But reason could also criticize itself and result in moral anomie and skepticism (Hume attacked Locke's deism and the rational foundations of morality.

- Scientific empiricism: extended to all domains.

- Naturalism, materialism.

- "Detheologization of natural law in a line from Grotius to Pufendorf and Thomasius".

- Vague deism to avoid moral chaos, indifferentism, religious tolerance

- Anti-institutionalism: initially against Church, then against absolutist state, finally democratic and revolutionary.

- Orientation toward democracy ("the general will," later the law of the majority), anti-authoritarianism.

- Secularization of universities (different in France and in Germany, where the sovereign State remained the proitector and employer of the Church ministers; in France, at the beginning of the 19th century, "The Ecoles Centrales, the Ecole Normale Supérieure, the Ecole Polytechnique and the Insktitut were established, and the UYniversities, aks indeed all other grades of education, were reorganised on modern lines" [Niklaus, p. 406]) and independence of university teaching.

The Encyclopédie

d'Alembert and especially Diderot were responsible for the change of scope of the publication, from 5 volumes and 120 plates foreseen in 1745, to 17 volumes, plus 11 volumes of plates finally brought out. 150 contributors and 4,000 subscribers. Big and very productive business for the investors. - "The editors were in touch with writers, scientists, industrialists, technologiskts, artisands and artists, men of action; a bourkgeois élite as attached to soical order as to reform, preparing unwittingly the revolution to come, deeply involved in the industrial rather than the political transformation."

Main contributions

"The main contributions of French 18th century thought to the history of ideas remains of course unchallenged." The "philosophes":  "Their main theories weere constantly reiterated: acceptance of nature, as opposed to asceticism; of reason, opposed to naive faith in the supernatural; and of tolerance opposed to religious persecution. Their vindication of the rights of man and the need to establish a better world on earth heralds the beginning of modern times."

'Historically, in spite of their obvious moderation, it is Diderot and his fellow materialists who attacked both the existing monarchy and the Church, whilst Voltaire and the deists wished to preserve the monarchy, and Rousseau wished to preserve religion."

Their politics: "They did not ask for political freedom... They did not wish to see all forms of censorship abolished, but rather the appointment of censors favorable to their cause. They unfailingly attacked inequalities in the social system, and the idea of a social contract as the basis of society gained ground... Rousseau's insistence on the need for popular consent provided the rational basis for a revolution that sprang from multiple causes. The philosophes, especially Rousseau, presented an idyllic picture of primitive man,, born innocent..., that was not acceptable to all... the idea of natural man had the advantage of placing the responsibility for man's misery and crime on society, thereby justifying change... the doctrine of the original goodness of man had the advantage of directly challenging a cardinal tenet of the Roman Catholic Church: original sin... in their attitude to natural law (to which Rousseau is hostile and which Diderot favors) the philosophes held divergent views.

"In the quest for happiness, sentiment and reason combine to reconcile anarchical man and social man... the majority of the philosophes clung to the bourgeois dream of reconciling happiness and virtue... they recognised man as an authentic individual, as well as a social and political namial, conditioned by education, economic, social and political factors. It is the power of reason in individual man... that is the condition of his very real if limited freedom. And in spite of the determinist belief of many of the philosophes it is the constant awareness of this personal freedom so manifest in practice that enabled them to lay the foundations of much progressive thought in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries."
