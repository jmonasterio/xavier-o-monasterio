What to exist means and what inwardness signifies

Here it is: to discover where the misunderstanding lies between speculative philosophy and Christianity. This therefore became my resolve... But chiefly I sought by reflection to trace the misunderstanding to its roots.... It must, in short, doubtless be rooted in the fact that on account of our vastly increased knowledge, men had forgotten what it means to EXIST, and what INWARDNESS signifies ( Concluding Unscientific Potscript , Princeton, p. 217).

I will hazard as a first approximation that "the misunderstanding between speculation and Christianity" (though K never pinpointed it in exactly this way) consists in mistaking the Christian faith for speculative, disinterested, merely objective knowledge... that results in assent and involves no other desire than the speculative desire to know, [rather than identifying it as] practical knowledge that results in action and is 'desirous knowledge' (XOM, \wp\97\97-aug-1, p. 16).

M. de Rollebon,... who believed in nothing, bet the Curé de Moulins that he would need less than two hours to bring the sick man back to Christian sentiments. The Curé took the bet and lost. Rollebon began at three in the morning, the sick man confessed at five and died at seven. 'Are you so forceful in argument?,' asked the Curé. 'You outdo even us.' 'I did not argue,' answered M. de Rollebon. 'I made him fear Hell' (from Ségur as quoted by Sartre in Nausea , p 26).

Your beliefs will be the light by which you see, but they will not be what you see and they will not be a substitute for seeing (Flannery O'Connor, Mystery and Manners , New York  1957, P. 91).  

Perhaps here is the place to take note of the common insistence that the biblical writings contain no theories, and that in this respect they differ from

the writings of theologians. I think that the point usually being made by those who say this is that the biblical writings are not disinterested . They are addressed to practical, religious problems; whereas the writings of theologians are generally disinterested, concerned just with the truth of the matter. As a rough-and-ready generalization this is probably true. But of course theories may be entertained either in interested or disinterested discourse. Thus I see no harm in saying that in St. Paul one finds theological theories (unless one defines a 'theory' as something held tentatively, which I do not) (Nicholas Wolterstorff, Reason within the Limits of Religion , William B. Eerdmans Publishing

Company, Grand Rapids, Michigan 1976,, p. 114, note 44).

"When the earth quakes at the outburst of a volcano, or when the pestilence is abroad in the land, how swiftly then and how thoroughly does even the dullest scholar... comprehend the uncertainty of everything! But then, when this is over, he is no longer capable of putting things together . And yet it was then that he ought to be employing hinmself about it, for when nature puts things together for him, when the fury of the elements preaches to him with more than Sunday eloquence--why then understanding comes pretty much of itself, so easily indeed that the task is to prevent despair by having understood earlier the same thing" (Kierkegaard, Postscript , p. 476).

"For Augustine, our hearts are restless until they rest in God; for Aquinas, not our hearts, but first and most our minds are restless until they rest in seeing him" (B. Lonergan, Verbum , University of Notre Dame Press 1967, p. 90).

A first thing to remark is that K proposes to trace the misunderstanding through reflection . This is a philosophical, rational task. Yet this reflection takes place in the context of the unscientific Postscript and refers to the Christian experience. In other words, it is a reflection on the non-objective, not disinterested but passionately self-interested response of faith to the already encountered God and still to be further encountered of Christians. Although K is himself a Christian, he reflects on the experience of Christians, bracketing his own case. In that this understanding of faith goes completely against the grain of the accepted philosophical understanding of knowledge as speculative, the faith described by Kierkegaard has to appear not merely as rationally unacceptable but as wrongheaded and nonsensical to those who think along the lines of traditional philosophy. Yet non-Christians are human agents, and Kierkegaard's Postscript is an indirect argument ad hominem to them as existing persons who face the future by way of decision and action, although they do not move in the realm of faith. In support of the legitimacy of Kierkegaard's understanding and of his reflective undertaking--and alluded to by Kierkegaard himself at least once--there is Aristotle's account of practical, desirous knowledge that terminates in action, and of rhetorical persuasion through intentional affection as an alternative.

My underlying intuitions

Insofar as knowledge may be said to include vision, something viewed, it is not image of object but applicative vision to be transcended in action, something like a habitual expectation that manifests itself in an emotional and corporal--that is, attitudinal--bracing for action. Therefore, to understand knowledge--or at least this practical knowledge--as image and to try to cash its truth-value in terms of contemplative image mirroring its object is totally wrongheaded. Statements are not pictures whose resemblance to reality can appropriately be discussed, but practical linguistic tools that inform the action of situated agents. Their truth-value can only be cashed practically, in terms of actual appropriate response, not theoretically, in terms of resemblance between the mind and reality. [The diference between knowledge and tool and knowledge as image is comparable to that between speaking, in the sense of stating something performatively and hearing in the sense of responding to something performatively stated, on the one hand, and, on the other hand, seeing a statement written and reading it understandingly, but to which the reader doesn't have to respond because the reader is not addressed, and about which all the reader can do is to contest and test its mirroring truth. I can tell something to someone, but I cannot say something to someone. A spoken statement has an adressee; a written statement (like a philosophical paper or a doctrine) has no addressee].

Aristotle and Aquinas on Ethics and on Rhetoric

Following Aristotle's Peri hermeneias , Aquinas makes a radical distinction between enunciativae orationes, namely, statements, and other speech acts. Other speech acts (deprecative, imperative, interrogative and vocative) are, we would say today, performative, in that they aim at a performance--by others?--as their result (secundum... conceptum alia dirigere et ordinare). These speech acts may be appropriate or inappropriate, successful or unsuccessful, but they are neither true nor false for they neither affirm nor deny anything. Statements, by contrast, according to Aquinas, are either true or false because they express what the mind conceives and judges to be the case. [Notice that it is the written (doctrinal?) statements that can be said to express merely what the writer conceives and judges, for they have no addressee and are not communicated in situation of exposure. Indeed, they are not properly communicative and are not meant performatively, asuming that they are meant at all. By contrast, in the case of the exposed agent who makes a concrete particular judgment the judgment doesn't express what the agent conceives and judges (for it functions as tool). The idea that judgments are silent statements to oneself, that is, that they express (internally or mentally) what the agent conceives and judges arises from the consideration of the content of the judgment. However, in the agent's case, the non-stated judgment functions not as an image but as a sort of tool "that one is" (to borrow Sartre's expression), for it informs action towards a desired goal. Thus there is a fundamental misunderstanding when the expressed judgment is considered in itself and mistaken for an image and the mind for a speculum.] Judging, Aquinas thinks, consists in intellectual assent to the "it is thus," (not, as I think, in a disposition to act in response to the stated). Hence a judgment expresses and conveys mere assent. [For Aquinas, the paradigmatic case of affirming or denying, that is, of judging, is strict predication, which is purely objective (S is P): saying something of something with the verb "is" or "is not." In this case, the point seems to be an ontological--be it descriptive or explanatory, temporal or essential--elucidation of the subject of the predication. Here the judging and the stating appeal exclusively to the judging ability of the heraer as to the truth or falsity of the predication.] The judging mind is led to assent, that is, to judge, by the exclusive consideration of the characteristics of its object as it is in itself ontologically, objectively, rather than in relation to the subject or anybody's subjective desires or interests. Ideally, in case of disagreement, the judge should be able to gain the assent of other minds or intellects by presenting the same evidence that--Aquinas assumes --led the judge to assent in the first place. [I say Aquinas assumes, for 'evidence' belongs in the sphere of statements, as in demonstrative geometry, where every stated theorem is supported, rather than in the sphere of action of exposed subjects, where the object is encountered as fearful or desirable]. Judgments terminate in the intellectual act of assent to what is judged and, when expressed in statements, express inmplicitly the speaker's assent by stating the object as judged. This is their truth-claim, which can be assented to or dissented with by the addressee or the hearer, whereas in the case of other speech acts, which are meant to terminate in performance, there is no room for assent or dissent. Indeed, by contrast to statements, the other speech acts are guided by and express the subject's desires. [Aquinas forgets the category of objects of encounter, which are desirable or repellent objects for response, that is, objects to be desired or feared, not mere objects which motivate no action. These 'objects' are not communicated by the speaker as merely subjective feelings of desire or aversion. If Aquinas doesn't quite forget these objects, ar least he doesn't include them as objects of scientific discourse. Furthermore, he implicitly suggests that they are not legitimate objects of knowledge inasmuch as desire and aversion, which militate against objectivity and truth, intervene].

Aquinas connects these characteristics of statements with scientific discourse. He calls it demonstrative science, "in qua animus hominis per rationem inducitur ad consentiendum vero ex his quae sunt propria rei, et ideo demonstrator non utitur ad suum finem nisi enunciativis orationibus significantibus res secundum quod earum veritas est in anima." [Again, Aquinas forgets the category of objects of encounter, which are not communicated ad consentiendum vero per rationem, but to be responded to]. What characterizes demonstrative science or scientific discourse is that it necessitates the unconditional assent of intelligent minds, because of the purely intelligible (universal and necessary) character of the evidence adduced.

Aristotle and Aquinas on poetry and rhetoric

In the process of characterizing demonstrative science and its use of statements, Aquinas contrasts this science with the way in which the poet and the orator lead their audience to assent: "Sed rhetor et poeta inducunt ad assentiendum ei quod intendunt, non solum per ea quae propria sunt rei, sed etiam per dispositionem audientis. Unde rhetores et poetae plerumque movere auditores nituntur provocando eos ad aliquas passiones..."

There is something odd here. It seems that Aquinas sees poetry and rhetoric as forms of communicative discourse composed of statements (as he understands statements). Indeed, in his view, poetry and rhetoric, like demonstrative science, that is, scientific discourse, are true or false, and they aim at conveying knowledge, which by its very nature consists in intellectual assent. For this very reason, however, in Aquinas' view (as in Plato's, with his grudge against myths and feelings), poets and orators try to elicit assent in an illegitimate way. Indeed, instead of being merely objective, of being guided and guiding their audience exclusively by the consideration of the object, instead of attempting to elicit assent in a purely intellectual way by means of objective evidence, the orator and the poet refer the object to the desires and interests of their audience by presenting the object as desirable or repellent (appealing to the agent's imagination rather than to a pure contemplator's intellect). Thus they lead their audience to assent, which should be a purely intellectual act, by exciting their wants and fears. We may ask, however, whether Aquinas is right in seeing poetry as a discourse that aims at conveying truth and eliciting assent, as well as in seeing (forensic) oratory as a discourse that aims at intellectual assent that "sees" the truth rather than at action. I will first consider the case of poetry and literature, leaving rhetoric for later.

Aquinas himself grants that there is a difference between demonstrative science and poetry. Though both aim at assent, demonstrative science goes by objective evidence, whereas poetry appeals to the desires and fears of the audience. Because of this, Aquinas sees poetic discourse (for he sees poetry as discourse that conveys knowledge and aims at assent) either as illegitimate as a form of knowledge (with Plato), or at least as a lesser species of knowledge (with Aristotle, because it deals with inexact objects, though possible rather than existent ones). But let us see whether Aquinas is right in considering poetic discourse as knowledge (where the paradigmatic case of knowledge is scientific discourse, from which it follows that poetry can only be a lesser, or even a totally illegitimate, form of knowledge).

The intention of scientific statements and demonstrative discourse is exclusively predicative, that is, it belongs in the order of predication, which is the order of (ontological) statements rather than of exposure and action. [A complicating factor in the area of what above I called strict predication (by contrast to performative presentation of object) is the predication of existence, which of course is not a property (neither essential, nor constant, nor accidental). I will deal with this below, when I come to historical narratives.] Indeed, scientific statements aim at assent to what is predicated of the object without any relationship to the speaker or the hearer, and for that very reason they offer purely objective arguments that convey 'evidence'in support of what is predicated. In this way, they appeal exclusively to the hearer's or reader's intellect, which alone is capable of grasping the truth or falsity of the predication through its own judgment. If assent is obtained, success is accomplished, for there is no further point to scientific statements than expressing and conveying truth.

Now, leaving aside lyric poetry, which tends to be purely expressive, a poetic narrative--a play or a novel--is not of itself predicative. It doesn't express the intention attributed to it by Aquinas of conveying a predication as knowledge to be assented to. Unlike a statement or a demonstrative discourse, a poetic narrative doesn't express the intention of affirming something as true, as being the case, much less does it support with evidence, since nothing is affirmed as being the case and since asent is not intended. Therefore to deny that a poetic narrative is true (or 'real'?) would be wrongheaded, for "the poet affirmeth nothing." In other words, in response to a poetic narrative assent or dissent are out of place. In short, whereas demonstrative discourse may well be for the purpose of conveying knowlege by necessitating assent, the purpose of poetic narrative is not to convey knowledge and lead to assent.

Thus, at least in this regard Aquinas is wrong. And so is Aristotle. For it is not the case, as Aristotle suggests, that poetic narrative conveys a less exact form of knowledge (though at least it doesn't deal with existence but with imaginary possibility!). In fact, poetic narrative does not constitute a denatured form of knowledge (as Aquinas, too, would have to conclude, because it appeals to the audience's feelings rather tahn to their intellect). It neither is nor intends to pass for a form of knowledge, if knowledge is identified with intellectual assent. Rather, it is Aquinas and Aristotle who are wrong in attributing to poetic narratives the intention of conveying knowledge--that is, predicative knowledge of the kind conveyed by scientific, demonstrative discourse.

A poarenthesis on the truth of historical narratives

It is necessary, however, to clear a complication. Is not a historical narrative true or false? For so far we have only spoken of poetic, not historical, narratives.

First of all, Aquinas would claim that a statement is either true or false by virtue of its very nature, since by virtue of its very nature it affirms something as true, that is, something to be assented to or dissented from. And since poetic narratives are composed of statements and are a form of discourse that conveys knowledge and aims at assent, they too are true or false by virtue of their nature. Now, I have argued that the narrative genus is neither true nor false, much less true or false by virtue of its nature. Yet we distinguish between poetic or imaginary narrative and historical narrative. In other words, the hearer or reader of a narrative can appropriately ask the question whether it is true or false, that is, whether or not the events narrated actually took place, and whether the author of the narration meant those events as real or as imaginary. [Here we are entering the complicated region of existential predication, if such be an appropriate name. Indeed, if existence is not a property of the object, then the affirmation of existence cannot be a case of predication.] As a mater of fact, in the case of historical narratives, we assume on the author's part the intention of affirming the reality of the events and of conveying knowledge of them as real. Thus at least in this case agreement and disagreement seem possible and appropriate.

As we shall see below when we come to practical knowledge, certain narratives appeal to feeling, perhaps on purpose, or at least provoke an emotive reaction. St Augustine narrates that he used to cry for Dido's death when reading The Aeneid . I myself have been deply moved by narrations of Thomas More's last days. Certain people cry reading certain novels or watching certain plays. (THIS WILL BE PARTICULARLY IMPORTANT FOR US WHEN WE CONSIDER THE CASE OF RHETORIC). Perhaps it may be said that poetic narratives, in general, tend to provoke emotional reactions exclusively, that is, imaginary attitudes, so to say, since they will not inform actual action. At the other end of the narrative spectrum, though, we have what might be called descriptive or merely assertive history, in which the events are coldly and objectively described rather than hotly narrated, as they are in the most important portions of the Gospels. This is the case when the historian's main purpose is to bring the readers to assent to the reality (or unreality) of events. In this case the description or objective narration is embedded in a context of argument, which would ideally be demonstrative or scientific to the point of necessitating assent, though the nature of the historical object which involves appeal to the readers' experience of the world allows only for probability and for intellectual persuasion rather than demonstration. This kind of non-narrative history is common in biblical studies, particularly concerning the historicity of the Gospels or portions thereof. Thus, between the two extremes--poetic narratives and non-narrative history--we have narrative history and--a very special genre--the Gospels. St John writes "ut credatis," but his credere (not being about a closed past, as ordinary historical discourse is) does not consist in intellectual assent but in practical response.

Hhaving just skimmed the complex issue of the truth of historical (by opposition to poetic) narratives and discourse, I pass to practical knowledge and forensic rhetoric.

Aristotle, practical knowledge, and forensic rhetoric

It seems to me that dialectics should deal with the probable that is object of speculative knowledge, where there is exclusive appeal to the intellect and where intellectual assent is the aimed at outcome, and that (forensic?) rhetoric covers the probable that is object of practical knowledge toward the future, where the appeal is not to the intellect but to the involved agent gifted with imagination, and where the aimed at outcome is action. This distinction between the probable of the dialectics and the probable of the rhetoric is often ignored, with the expectable confusing results. The confusion is often compounded by the lack of awareness that, contrary to Aristotle [cf. Suzanne Mansion] , the probable (of the dialectics) can be object of certain, that is, akribos , knowledge, whereas the probable of practical knowledge and of rhetoric not only cannot be akribos , but is not even knowledge in a univocal sense, since practical and speculative knowledge are not species of the common genus knowledge.         

In addition, we should remember from Aristotle that not all matters are susceptible of demonstration. Only the universal and necessary can be demonstrated. Furthermore, if scientific discourse is the same as demonstrative discourse, it follows that what cannot be demonstrated, what is not universal and necessary, what is temporal or corruptible or irregular or particular or contingent desirable, cannot be objet of scientific discourse. [But perhaps what characterizes scientific discourse is that it aims at assent and attempts to convey knowledge, in which case demonstrative discourse and dialectical discourse are both species of scientific discourse.] Indeed, what characterizes scientific discourse is not only that it aims at conveying truth and eliciting assent, but that it is akribos , exact, absolutely certain rather than merely probable. Historical discourse, then--for Aristotle, who often confuses the probable of the dialectic and of the practical region, and the undemonstrable with the merely probable--cannot be scientific, because it deals with the particular, and the particular cannot be demonstrated with necessity. Poetic discourse, likewise, cannot be scientific, since it too deals with the particular. However, Aristotle thinks, it deals with the particular as possible, as merely exemplary, without affirming its existence. Hence it is less inexact than historical discourse, for it conveys quasi-universal though not scientific or demonstrable truths about the human condition. What is regular but not essentially necessary, purely intelligible, is only probable.

In all the cases mentioned we have kinds of discourses (and of knowledge?) that aim at conveying truth and leading to mere intellectual assent. Neither desire, nor deliberation, nor choice, nor action enter into the picture. The knowing subject is supposed to objectivy, to place himself totally out of the picture, to cut all personal ties to the object, to be prompted to assent exclusively by the object as it is in itself from no particular viewpoint or situational standpoint, and to guide others to assent through arguments that arise from the exclusive consideration of the object--arguments, that is, that appeal exclusively to the judging, intellectual, totally disinterested  capacity of those addressed. Let us call this speculative knowledge , which is a pure contemplator's knowledge, that is, the knowledge of a subject who is not himself within the picture.

Now, if knowledge can only be of this sort, then Aquinas is right if indeed he dismisses as illegitimate, that is, as not knowledge, the orator's attempt to bring his audience as exposed participants into the picture and to appeal to their emotions. In doing this he follows Plato, for whom feeling interfers with and disturbs the knowing process and for whom poetic images and myths are lies. [But see Julius A. E;lias, Plato's Defence of Poetry, State U of NY Press, Albany 1984]. Yet Aristotle in the Ethics (and more implicitly in the Rhetoric ) grants that there is another kind of knowledge, namely practical knowledge. This is a knowledge that doesn't aim at and and doesn't terminate in assent but in action, and that includes deliberation. It is inexact, not scientific, because one can deliberate only about what is not necessary, and also because action is always particular and about a particular. Since it involves deliberation and action, this knowledge also involves desire. Therefore, it cannot be the knowledge of a disinterested  subject, but of a participant affected by the outcome of his action, and whose action affects other participants, and who is himself affected by the actions of other participants. Moreover, this desirous knowledge by participants as well as the kind of discourse among participants that corresponds to this knowledge are not considered in the Analytics or in the Logic , not even in the Dialectics (which deals with the probable but purely objective and disinterested) but in the Ethics and the Rhetoric .
