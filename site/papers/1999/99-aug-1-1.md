CHRISTIANITY IS NOT A DOCTRINE

“What modern philosophy understands by ‘faith’ is what properly is called an ’opinion,’ or what is loosely called in everyday speech ‘believing.’ Christianity is made into a doctrine, and the next state is to comprehend this doctrine, and that is what philosophy does. On the whole, this is quite right, in case Christianity were a doctrine; but since it is not that, it is a crazy proceeding” (Soren Kierkegaard, The Offence) .

Faith would be an opinion, a belief, the intentional correlate of believing or assenting without sufficient evidence, IF Christianity were a doctrine. But it is not. A doctrine is a statement or collection of statements to be assented to. One can assent to a doctrine with or without sufficient evidence, or one can refuse to assent. That is all one can do. But assenting is not what people of  faith do. Even the demons, as characterized by James in his epistle, who have no ‘faith,’ do better or otherwise than having a belief, since they are afraid of God. One is not afraid of a doctrine or a statement. A doctrine cannot be feared since it cannot do any harm and since the distances of the subject to a doctrine are not distances of exposure, existential distances. (Monasterio)

Existential distances

Without specifying it explicitly, Ricoeur is working his way through the maze of the differences between statements and narratives. His interest arises from the fact that the Bible is listened to and read in the Church as being primarily a live narrative, but is often confused with a collection of (necessarily true, scientific) statements about the past, or at best with a dramatic   but imaginary narrative.

In his attempt to create a scientific theology after the Aristotelian model, Aquinas read the Bible as a collection of scientific, objective, a-temporal, written, necessarily true statements. Such had to be the case because only statements can be true or false, and God’s word is necessarily true. Aquinas’ theory of reference in the case of statements is that the assent is given to the reality stated (be it intelligible as a theorem or factual as a historical fact), not to the  locution or statement itself. Needless to say, concerning particular historical facts, Aquinas inherited from the Greeks the classical difficulties as to their truth, given their temporality and contingency.

As to the failure of Aquinas’ attempt to create a scientific theology, it is attested by the very early lamentation “Sicut liber historiarum solebat legi!” But now the storical theologian has been for the nineteenth century by Kierkegaard and his desperate effort to clarify the confusion that led the the loss of the faith of the son of the man described in the Unscientific Postscript . This shows the persistence of the confusion.  I think that the confusion hasn’t been fully dispelled yet by the end of the twentieth century, as shown by the supposedly scientific departments of theology in Catholic universities.

Wherein lies the confusion? An enlightening indication comes from Orson Well’s braoadcast of The War of the Planets. The confusion here is between live and not live narrative.

More concretely, what was meant by the narrator as a not live narrative, was heard as a live narrative. What does this mean? And what name are we to give to this confusion?

Merleau-Ponty wrote that depth is the most existential of the three dimensions. Why?

Because depth is the dimension of the object that involves an implicit reference to the perceiving subject. Actually, the three dimensions involve such a reference. What I wanted to say is that depth is the dimension that involves an implicit reference to the subject as exposed to the object and of the object as exposed to the subject, which reveals the perceiving subject as agent-patient.

Hume realized that the perception of an object involved expectations on the perceiver’s part of the hidden powers of the object, that is, of its conduct, its actions and, consequently, the effects it would produce. Now, these effects may be effects produced by the object on other objects of perception, in which case objective observation would have its appropriate place. But sometimes the effects expected are effects on the perceiving subject. In this case objective observation is out of place, since the effects expected are favorable or unfavorable. Sheer curiosity–what Aristotle called “the disinterested desire to know,” may be the appropriate attitude on the part of an invulnerable knower (as Aristotle implicitly assumes the human subject to be in the Metaphysics and in the Analytica Posteriora), or in the particular case in which only inanimate objects are involved. But sheer curiosity–the attitude of scientific objectivity–is, or at least should be, displaced by the attitude of  ”concern” when either the perceiving subject or other animate objects of perception worthy of concern are going to be affected by the powers of the perceived object in question. ( This, I think, clarifies the obscure distinction between speculative and practical reason ).

To the expected action of the object in the particular case corresponds, in the attitude of concern, a disposition to act on the perceiver’s part. This disposition to act may lead to automatic action in the case of inherited instinct, or to quasi automatic action in the case of past successful experience in coping with the object, or, given time and capacity, to elaborately projected action.

The purpose of these considerations is to explain what I mean by live narrative. We humans are exposed to the surrounding world. Our perception is perspectival . We grasp the world as surrounding us, as enveloping us. Any object we perceive is either above or below us, in

front or behind us, to our right or to our left, near or far away from us. In other words, the objects we perceive indicate us as being in the midst of the world as the center of it, since all distances unfold from us and toward us. I am the center of the world. “Here” is where I am, and from my here all “there” and “over there” is determined. The same applies to temporal distances.

“Now” is my present, and by reference to my present all “past” and “future” is determined.

However, I am not in the midst of the world as a thing but as an agent-patient, exposed to the world and having the world exposed to me. Hence my space is, as Sartre says, furrowed by the ways of my projects, and my time is consequently dramatic. Indeed, my future is underpinned by desires and fears, and my past has the inchoate structure of a history because it is marked by successes and fears. This is the dramatic structure of experience preserved by the narrative genre  as well as by theatrical plays.

By its very nature, a narrative by the subject is always about the past and in the past tense.
