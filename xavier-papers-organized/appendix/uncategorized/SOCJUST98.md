     THE CATHOLIC POINT OF VIEW ON ORGAN DONATION

Our parish is engaged in a campaign to promote organ donation in order to meet a very urgent need.. A very important and legitimate question you may have is whether organ and tissue donation are acceptable from the Catholic point of view.  The fact that our parish is involved in this campaign may already have answered this question implicitly.  However, we Catholics should be aware that  Pope John Paul II has explicitly stated that “The Catholic Church would promote the fact that there is a need for organ donors and that Christians should accept this as a ‘challenge to their generosity and fraternal love’ so long as ethical principles are followed.”

Christianity views the body as an essential and good part of the self.  We Christians believe in the resurrection of Christ and have hope in our own resurrection in the flesh.  The dignity of the person extends to the body, which deserves to be treated with respect.  Although a cadaver is no longer a person, Christians treat the cadaver with respect, care, and reverence for the sake of the person who has died.   As indicated by the Pope, the donation of organs not only does not constitute a desecration of the body, but is a challenge to our Christian generosity.

Body and soul are God’s creation.  Foreseeing the time when the unity of soul and body  will be disrupted by our death, we Christians have the opportunity of a final act of fraternal love and generosity, in remembrance of our Master who loved us to his death,  by donating our organs and tissues to someone who is in urgent need of them to continue living.

IS THERE REALLY A  NEED FOR ORGAN AND TISSUE DONATION?

Pope John Paul II has invited the Church to attend to the need  for organ donations.  Is

there really a great need out there? Here are some data.

-- Every eighteen minutes a new patient is added to the national waiting list for organ transplants.

-- Every day seven people on the waiting list die because of the lack of available organs.  --- The national waiting list has increased 100% since 1987, and by June 1995 it reached a record high of 40,000 patients.  

The improved success rate of transplants has led to this substantial increase of patients awaiting transplantation. This demand is not expected to abate.

Now, despite estimates of between 10,000 and 14,000 people who could

be donors each year, organ donation has not increased significantly and has remained well below the level of the need, as evidenced by the following data.  

–  1990: 4,512 organ donors

–  1991: 4,530 organ donors

– 1992: 4,497 organ donors

– 1993: 4,895 organ donors

– 1994: 4,891 organ donors

While organ transplantation needs are the most critical because of their life-saving nature,

bone and other tissues are also needed.  Bone reconstruction improves the outcome for patients with bone cancer and spinal ailments.  Skin grafts change the lives of people who have been gravely burnt.  On any given day 5,000 people are waiting to have their sight restored through corneal transplants.

     FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS CONCERNING ORGAN DONATION

In connection with our parish campaign to contribute to meet the urgent need for organs and tissue donations, here are the answers to some questions that are often asked.

– Does organ donation prevent the family from having a wake and an inhumation or cremation? No.  The organs and tissues are taken in the hospital immediately after the death of the donor.  The corpse is treated in the hospital with all due respect and is returned to the family for the usual funeral procedures.

– Aren’t there abuses to be feared? Whatever may or may not happen in other countries, organ and tissue donation is strictly regulated in the United States.  On October 19, 1984, the National Organ Transplant Act was signed into law by President Reagan. It contained four major provisions: 1) it outlawed the buying and selling of organs; 2) it called for the creation of a task force on organ procurement and transplantation; 3) it mandated the creation of a national Organ Procurement and Transplantation Network, and 4) it ordered the formation of a scientific registry to be operated in the private sector.  The medical team in charge of taking the organs and tissues from the corpse has no vested interest in the use of the organs, since they don’t know who will be the recipient or recipients, and  they have no power to designate the recipient or recipients.

– Does it suffice to have volunteered to be a donor at the time of getting one’s driving licence?   NO. When the donor dies, the next of kin has the power to determine whether there will or will not be donation of organs.  Therefore, THE PERSON WHO VOLUNTEERS TO BE A DONOR SHOULD SHARE WITH HIS OR HER CLOSE FAMILY HIS OR HER DECISION TO BE A DONOR, AND SHOULD ASK THEM TO RESPECT HIS OR HER WILL IN THIS MATTER  IF THEY HAPPEN TO BE IN A SITUATION IN WHICH THEIR

CONSENT IS REQUIRED. BE A DONOR.  MAYBE YOU WILL BE A RECIPIENT.
