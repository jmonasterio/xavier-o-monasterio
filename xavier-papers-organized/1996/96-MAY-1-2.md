SPEECH ACTS AGAIN

- "Draw me a map."

- "I can't. I've never been there."

Indeed, only someone who has been there, who has personal experience of having been in a certain place, can trace the map to that point. A map is not a picture but a linguistic instrument made by somone "who has been there"  without the help of a map, on the basis of his or her having been there.

 

Do statements "purport to state... how things are"? (Searle, The Construction of Social Reality, The Free Press, N.Y, 1995, p. 210) (See also pp. 214 and 216 and 217). Or are they like all other speech acts, to be understood practically, that is, to dispose to respond?  

When I command I give an order to someone, I don't state that I give an order or state that you should do this or that. I do not say that you should do this or that; by giving you this order I am making myself ordering and making you ordered to do this or that. Why, then, when I make a statement would I be stating that I am stating, rather than making you disposed to confront and respond to things as so and so ?

To begin again. When I give an order I speak as an agent to an agent in order to achieve linguistically an effect in or through the addressee. The communication-action is direct, addresser to addressee, I-Thou, so to say. 'Understanding,' in the case of a command, is understanding the speaker's intention to have the addressee commanded to do this or that. However, since the addresser's intention is not to convey an understanding-that but to have something done linguistically in and sometimes by the addressee, the speech act is successful not by the speaker's intention being understood objectively or theoretically, but by the intended effect being achieved . *

[ * Searle is wrong in thinking that the point of language is to inform of the speaker's intentions; it is, rather, to achieve effects linguistically. Speech acts are acts .].

In other words, the illocutionary effect is having you, not informed of my command, but practically disposed to carry out my command, and the perlocutionary effect is your actual carrying out of my command as an act of obedience to me .

When I beg you for money , the same analysis applies. And this is the case, in general, for all other speech acts. HOWEVER, WHEN IT COMES TO STATEMENTS THE CASE IS SUPPOSED TO BE TOTALLY DIFFERENT. IS SUCH CHANGE WARRANTED?

In the case of a statement --for example, "Your house is burning"--everything changes. Supposedly the speaker has no practical perlocutionary intentions or even practical expectations concerning the adressee's conduct. The addresser merely states how things are in themselves, objectively, without any existential relationship to the addresseee. Language constitutes a closed circuit of its own represented by the theoretical illocutionary effect, with only an accidental relation, if any, to the causal order through the perlocutionary effects. Supposedly the speaker is a mere curious contemplator addressing another mere curious contemplator, not an exposed participant in the order of causality. As a curious contemplator of the world the speaker says, that is, paints a linguistic pinture, that things are so and so . The addressee becomes informed of the speaker's intention to communicate that (that is, in a merely factual way to be merely registered by the addressee) things are so and so, and with this the speech act supposedly succeeds, FOR STATEMENTS HAVE NO PERLOCUTIONARY EFFECT STATED AND, THEREFORE, INTENDED. Sometimes--this is the best scenario, but not necessary for success--the addressee is not only informed of what the speaker intends to communicate, but assents to it, makes the speaker's picture his own.

[ Perlocutionary effects, therefore, in Searle's view,  are somewhat accidental to language, purely behavioral rather than actions incarnating a linguistic intention . Indeed, speech acts succeed by informing of the speaker's intention, not by achieving linguistically the effect intended in or through the addressee. Thus they really are not speech- acts ].

When the whole theory of language is based on statements as the paradigmatic case, and on this particular understanding of statements as objectively informative of how things stand, the rule will be drawn, at least by Searle, that the illocutionary effect of all speech acts is mere understanding of the communicative intention of something to be understood--that the ability to paint understandable pictures and to understand them when painted by another is what defines the competent user of a language. As a result, the perlocutionary effects will be seen as merely behavioral rather than intrinsically linguistic-intentional .

What is my alternative concerning statements?

( KEEP IN MIND THE DISTINCTION BETWEEN PERSUADING TO ASSENT AND PERSUADING TO DO . In both cases I give reasons, but in the former I appeal only to the intellect, in the latter I give motivating reasons and I appeal to the agent. As in a practical syllogism the conclusion is a disposition  to act which, if not forcefully prevented, will terminate incarnated in an action , so in the case of a statement the 'understanding' consists in a disposition to act . ALSO, KEEP IN MIND THAT ONLY THE UNPERSUADED NEEDS TO BE PERSUADED, THAT THE INTENTION OF PERSUADING IS POSSIBLE ONLY IN RELATION TO THE UNPERSUADED. )

My first point is that the speaker speaks as an agent to an agent--not as a contemplator to a contemplator--in order to produce an effect in the agent or through the agent. Therefore the speaker has practical intentions. What is my intention when I say "Your house is burning"? Certainly not to have you assent intellectually to my statement . If you assented and did nothing, I would be baffled. And if you disbelieved my statement and asked for reasons that led to assenting to it rather than to taking it into consideration in your action, you would misunderstand my intention, for my intention is not to have you assent to that. If I tell you, "There is a hole in front of you," and you continue walking and fall into the hole, you are an incompetent user of language, even if you understood theoretically that which I said --the 'object' stated--and even gave intellectual assent to my statement. This is the understanding of a contemplator, not of a participant; of an overhearer of language, not of a linguistic agent. AND THIS IS SEARLE'S UNDERSTANDING OF STATEMENTS : they inform me of yout intention to impart true information, as begging informs me of your intention to beg.

The very fact that I would be baffled if you just assented and did nothing about your house burning shows that I have at least practical expectations about you, if not intentions. This is important because it shows that I address you as an exposed agent talking to an agent. Furthermore, I confront you--because both you and I are exposed agents and speak as such--with a motivating dramatic situation of which you are the structural-practical center and within which 'the object'--your house burning--is axial. Thus, I do, in fact, have a practical intention, namely, to have you, the one truly concerned because exposed, respond according to your reasonable choice by becoming practically disposed to respond when the opportunity comes .

The reasons for leaving you the choice may be multiple: I have no authority over you, the motivating object may be more of your concern than of mine, you are better placed than myself to respond to it, etc.

Suppose, however, that you disbelieve me in the sense of not assenting to the fact that your house is burning (for you could also 'disbelieve' in the sense of frustrating my expectations by not seeing your house burning as something to be prevented, as motivating you to prevent its burning, because, for instance, you prefer to cash the insurance). For commonsensical people who do not look for infallibility, my having seen the scene would suffice as a reason for believing-being-motivated . Not so for philosophers, because they do not see the intrinsic connection knowledge-action, because they see knowledge as a picture and are interested in the correctness of the picture, in finding a reason that guarantees its resemblance rather than in responding appropriately as exposed agents . After all, are the senses infallible, or at least reliable within limits? Thus philosophers need a justificatory epistemology (something which not only involves a trust in our ability to know but  which always fails as a satisfactory justification).

THE POINT HERE IS THAT DISBELIEF OF A STATEMENT (OR SUSPENSION OF BELIEF THAT A STATEMENT IS TRUE, THAT IS, RESEMBLANT) IS THE BASIS FOR THE MISINTERPRETATION OF STATEMENTS, OF SPEECH ACTS IN GENERAL, AND OF KNOWLEDGE. INDEED, KNOWLEDGE AND ACTION BECOME SEPARATED. kNIOWLEDGE BECOMES A COMPLETE UNIT BY ITSELF, A PICTURE THAT MAY OR MAY NOT BE RESEMBLANT AND THAT IS, THEREFORE, IN NEED OF JHUDITIFYING EVIDENCE. [Notice that, by its very methodical theoretical skepticism, the philosophical tradition begins with suspension of belief. Hence, until and unless grounded and previously legitimized through episteme, all action becomes merely "as if." After all, shouldn't Descartes--and even Aquinas (?)--, in all consistency, have renounced their practical certitudes, (that is, their potential practical knowledge) instead of leaving them untouched while questioning?] INDEED, DISBELIEF OR LACK OF BELIEF INTERRUPTS THE CONNECTION PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE-LANGUAGE-ACTION, AND TRANSFORMS THE POTENTIAL KNOWN-SPOKEN INTO OBJECT OF ASSENT TO BE JUSTIFIED BY REASONS OR 'EVIDENCE' FOR ASSENTING [RATHER THAN SEEING IT AS TO BE TRANSCENDED IN  PRACTICAL UNDERSTANDING, THAT IS, IN CONSTITUTING IN THE ADDRESSEE THE PRACTICAL DISPOSITION TO RESPOND TO THE THING AS SO AND SO FROM HIS OR HER OWN STANDPOINT AS CENTER OF THE SITUATION. THE THEORETICAL OBJECT WHOSE RESEMBLANCE IS TO BE JUSTIFIED BECOMES THE CENTRAL THING, INSTEAD OF THE  OBJECT TO BE RESPONDED TO]. Indeed, when the statement is disbelieved it ceases to be understood practically and, therefore, to dispose the addressee to respond to the object as so and so, and it becomes objectified, object of consideration, proposition whose truth-resemblance is in suspense . It is not anymore a matter of "Your house is burning" as linguistic instrument, but of " It is true-resemblant that your house is burning," ( THIS IS THE WAY IN WHICH SEARLE, FOLLOWING THE PHILOSOPHICAL TRADITION, INTERPRETS STATEMENTS AND MAKES THEM INTO THE MODEL OF ALL OTHER SPEECH ACTS) , and I will have to give you reasons not to persuade you to do (if you need them) but to persuade you to assent to the resemblance value of the picture. * Hence the idea that statements are pictures, and that truth is a matter of resemblance.

* That Aristotle, and most certainly Plato before him, are looking for speculative reasons is clear from the fact that they want universal and necessarily true statements, and that this makes it very difficult for them to consider as real knowledge ( episteme ) the necessarily particular and contingent statements based on sense perception.

One way of escaping the resemblance trap is Kant's denial of reality as thing in itself. Another is Searle's notion of "fact" as something not merely linguistic. But neither of them bridges the chasm, for both of them keep the notion of reality as something to be seen rather than responded to by an agent exposed to it. What is needed is a practical notion of knowledge in which the 'seeing' is practical seeing and, therefore, only metaphorical seeing.

The knower is the involved, exposed, vulnerable agent in interaction. Each action is a trans-action between the agent and something else.

Think of a piece of knowledge as a map. A map may look like a picture but is not a picture for it does not function as a  picture. Furthermore, a map functions as a map when it is understood as a map by disposing the agent practically to make it function as a map when the opportunity comes, and when it is actually applied. In the very success of the transaction made possible by the map and the practical understanding of it as a map

the 'truth' of the map, that is, its instrumental validity, is at least provisionally vindicated . (I can reach a place without a map by sheer chance, but when I use a map I do not reach the place by chance). Potential or habitual knowledge, therefore, is part of the agent's equipment, an acquisition that comes to constitute this particular agent's ability as agent. As far as the 'object' is concerned, since actual knowledge is an action that intends an effect, it is always particular, and it is knowledge only insofar as it succeeds. (What distinguishes knowledge of things from knowledge of other agents and, in particular, of people, is that success is less assured, for other agents have their own goals; they, too, are knowers-agents).

What distinguishes linguistic transactions is that they are necessarily cooperative between speaker and spoken to, so that their success is far from assured, too.

An important point: Language, unlike any other instrument, observes Searle, achieves by saying what it means and informing the addressee of the speaker's intention to say that. "Ich komme von dem Land wo die Zitronen blühen" does not mean "I am a German soldier," for it does not say that but something else. *

* Searle identifies, in the ideal case, saying with meaning: "Whatever can be meant can be said." I contend that, at least in the case of statements, what is meant is achieved through what is said, but is not itself said. One always states in concrete given circumstances in order to achieve a purpose which is intrinsically connected with what is said but which is not itself said. "Ich komme..." is not intrinsically connected with my being responded to (or seen, if Searle prefers) as a German soldier, whereas "Ich bin ein deutscher Soldat" is. Intrinsically connected means "possessing by virtue of what is said a motivating force for the addressee's decision." Searle wants to get what is meant out of attending objectively to what is said, as if the same words in different circumstances, where different purposes by different speakers addressing different addressees, are intended, meant the same.  

"Your house is burning," then, is not a picture. The question remains, however, whether its motivating force accrues to it from the fact that it gives information, so to say, concerning the object, or whether it just prompts a response, like a loud sound provokes fear. ( The alternative between these extremes may be the identification that takes place when one appetizes food. Appetition as practical disposition to eat is the appropriate identification of food. Appetition is neither 'un-intentiional' like the automatic response to a loud noise, nor objective information (to be understood and assented) that I am a German soldier ).   When the sight of food within reach provokes salivation, the salivation is an apt practical disposition, but neither the sight of food by itself nor the salivation convey mere information concerning the object. Is the case the same when I say "Your house is burning"?  

(1) Pace Searle, this example--"Ich komme..."--shows that, at least in this case, a perlocutionary effect is intended in stating, namely, my being treated as a German soldier.

(2) By the very nature of statements , the perlocutionary effect, if they have one, is never said, but only meant, for it is to be practically understood by the addressee becoming practically disposed to meet the thing as so and so and actually meeting it that way unless forcibly prevented. This is what misleads Searle into thinking that statements have no perlocutionary effect.

(3) What Searle realizes is that the thing so and so is not the same in both statements: "Ich komme..." and "Ich bin a deutscher Soldat." It is like giving different maps. Although the maps are not pictures, different maps make practical differences, as they are indeed supposed to. Speech acts function as such by saying, that is, by being linguistic. In Searle's example, "Ich komme..." does not function as language, according to what it says. But from this it does not follow that saying is painting a picture or referring to what is the case as something to be seen, which necessarily implies that what is stated is something to be assented to because it is "true." (Truth is a quality of statements, not of reality) .

(4) Particular existential or perceptual statements in the present or in the future intend to dispose the addressee to respond to things as so and so; but they do not do that by painting things as so and so, for things to be responded to are not to be seen but to be responded to . Such statements in the past state how things were for agents exposed to them and how they were responed to by those agents.

(5) But whence, then, the powerful illusion, to which not only Searle falls victim, that statements paint things as so and so--say how things objectively or factually are?

When our immediate concerns are taken care of, things cease --provisorily--to be responded to. They become--provisorily--merely visible, that is observable by a (methodically) unexposed agent. This visibility is like a token of their reality as well as a cipher of their behavior and their powers, but both their reality and their powers are neutralized. The distances of concern disappear--and with them 'existential depth' disappears too. All that remains is, so to say, 'a spectacle,' 'a phenomenon.' If we take this for 'the given' in 'the natural attitude' to the exposed, vulnerable agent, then we will be condemned to struggle in vain to justify the existence of the phenomenon. That, however, is by no means the given in the natural attitude, in which the agent is involved in practical transactions. Since when we philosophize or 'speak about ' we are not in the natural attitude but in the neutralizing mood, we fall into the trap of thinking of reality as something to be seen rather than encountered, and of knowledge as a picture rather than as an instrument whose value is to be cashed in its use.
