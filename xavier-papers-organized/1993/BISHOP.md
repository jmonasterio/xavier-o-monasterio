FROM: Xavier O. Monasterio

TO: ACPAS Ad Hoc Committee members

RE: Proposed Ordinances for Catholic Colleges and Universities in the USA.

The ACPAS of this University which has made in the past and continues to make in the present a deliberate effort to affirm its Catholic identity and to maintain the appropriate relationship as an institution of learning with the Catholic Church cannot but see with sympathy the concern of the American Bishops for the theological teaching in Catholic American universities.

While sympathizing with this concern, the ACPAS nonetheless feels that at the present time and in the concrete cultural climate of the USA the measures proposed in the projected Ordinances not only will fail to achieve the salutary results aimed at by the Bishops, but will actually create a polarization within Catholic colleges and universities that would threaten their survival as Church affiliated institutions. For this very important prudential reason the ACPAS respectfully recommends that, for the time being , the Ordinances be applied exclusively to departments of theology of pontifical universities, pending the day when the complex issues involved concerning the relationship of theology as an academic discipline to faith receive further clarification. Indeed, even the Apostolic Constitution on Catholic Universities, Ex Corde Ecclesiae , continues in our opinion to reflect an unresolved conflict between the " impartial search for truth" it encourages (# 5 and 7) and the " adherence to the teaching authority of the Church in matters of faith and morals" it simultaneously demands (# 27).

Two facts seem to us to support the prudential delay we recommend in the implementation of the proposed ordinances beyond Pontifical universities. The first is the reluctance of the majority of the presidents of American Catholic universities to their implementation in non-Pontifical universities at this time. There is every reason to consider the said presidents both as intensely committed members of the Church and as  highly experienced people who have a sense of what is likely to produce beneficial results in Catholic universities in the present cultural climate. The fact that the overwhelming majority of them fear the results that the implementation of the proposed ordinances would produce gives us pause. The second fact in support of the prudential delay we recommend in the application of the proposed ordinances is that in the recent past a significant movement has been taking shape and strength in the American Catholic universities to reaffirm their Catholic identity and to think through more deeply the distinctive character of education in Catholic universities today. There is reason to expect that in time such a movement will both contribute to a modification of the present cultural climate and result in the clarification of issues that in our turbulent moment appear intractable and whose resolution by decree, however well-intentioned, is likely to be tragically divisive.
