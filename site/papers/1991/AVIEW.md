A view of my project

As a way of clarifying to myself one latent but important purpose of my project, I will use as a point of contrast Maurice's Wiles article "Scriptural Authority and Theological Construction: The Limitations of Narrative Interpretation," in Scriptural Authority and Narrative Interpretation , Edited by Garret Green (Fortress Press, Philadelphia, 1987), pp. 42-58.

Characterizing one of the effects of the position he takes, he writes on p. 55: "The approach I have adopted, it may be argued, does nothing to rescue Scripture from the tyranny of the scholar and to restore it to its rightful place in the ordinary life of the Church. Instead it increases the scholar's power by ascribing to him or her not onlyu special skills in relation to the interpretation but an innovative function in the form of theological construction also. Certainly the contribution of the scholar remains important. One could hardly expect otherwise, for what the scholar does is only a more concentrated form of what is involved in any reflective reading of scripture--and it cannot be the church's goal to exclude that. But the language of 'tyranny' is out of place. For in the revised account that I am offering, neither scripture nor theology is being afforded any prescriptive role to which the word 'tyranny' might be suited. Scripture and theology are both important contributors to the never-ceasing process of discovering the most appropriate forms of Christian belief and practice. But that is a process in which neither has an exclusive role or any right of veto. For it is a process in which all Christians, out of their varied experience of the life of faith, have a contribution to make."

For starters, it is clearly one of the purposes of my project to rescue Scipture from the tyranny of the scholar." Or rather, to rescue the faithful and the Church with its hierarchical powers of interpretation, from the tyranny of the scholar with his-her supposed 'scientific' authority.

Wiles accepts the advantages of canonical scriptures: "The particular canon we have received is a matter of contingent fact. But the recgnition that its precise contours do not correspond to any set of determinable criteria should not lead us to suggest the abandonment or modification of the canon as such.. The existence of a universally acknowledged set of writings is of proven value, a value that would be freeed of its attendant disadvantages if the writings were freed of the claim to binding authority. Two illustrations of that value will suffice. In the first place, thje existence of an agreed canon helps to provide a common sensibility for the Christian community as a whole; the canon is compact enough to serve that role and also varied enough to relate as a basic resource of faith to the very wide rqange of human needs. Second, the canon's distinctive status enables it to stand apart in its historic singularity from the changing patterns of Crhistian belief, and so to serve as a potential source of prophetic correction over against the ever-present danger of Christians' being carried along uncritically by the beguiling streams of contemporary thought. (Cf. his essay "The uses of 'Holy Scripture,'"--see his note 31).

One sees Wiles's bete noire: the binding claim, the binding character of the canon. But how can a canon be a canon without being binding? And how can it correct the ever-present danger presented by the beguiling streams of contemporary thought without being binding? And how did it acquire its binding force?

Was it a matter of arbitrary decision, or because the canonical writings were recognized as expressing the faith of the Church?

He writes that "the quasi-divine authorship is a part of the description of what we decide to do, not a reason for the decision  (p. 46). No. It is, rather that the recognition by the Church of the quasi-divine authorship together with the Church's acceptance of it makes the canon binding, much as the recognition of an 'out' by the empire makes the 'out' binding for the participants, precisely because they recognize that the decision is not arbitrary. In the name of what do they make that decision? In the name of their faith--the faith that they share. Wiles is against the "probative" use of Scripture, which he identifies with its binding character or its authoritative character. In a sense, he is right against the probative use, if by this he means what the Scholastics did. But the Catholic Scripture is a product and a possession of the Catholic Church to whose authority it owes its authority as much as the Church is authorized by it. What Wiles wants is to reduce to scientific authority what is a matter of religious authority and, to an extent, to reduce the ecclesial authority to the authority of the supposedly critically minded individuals or group of scholars who reject the religious ecclesial authority.

According to Wiles, "the theological task itself, the determination of what can properly be believed, is something that has continually to be discovered in the light of changing experience" (p. 55). A good half-truth. But does the theological task compete to the critically-minded theologian on his own, or as part of the religious ministry of the Church? And does this mean that the theologian, or even the Church, can, for instance determine that from now on it can be properly believed that Christ is only symbolically present in the Eucharist, or that the scriptural canon is to be droped? Hwo does Wiles know--or, to use his own expression, by what set of 'objective criteria can [he] justify' (p. 53) such changes in what is to be properly believed, or determine that such changes are due to the beguiling streams of contemporary thought? In short, the objection he himself raises against his views may have more validity than he thinks: "Finally, it may be objected that the church will be left with no clear message to rpocalim or to live by. Doctrine, it may be said, has been turned into so elusive a phenomenon that it would serve better as the basis for an academic seminar than for a life of faith" (p. 55). And the fact with which he answers the objection, namely, "the difficulty... of the link between the doctrinal content of belief and its effective integration into the life of faith" does not change anything. What this fact demands, which is common to the old approach and to his approach, is a more sensible treatment of the distinction between doctrinal and practical.

All the above by way of preface. How does my project limit the tyranny of the scholar? By recognizing the social 'construction of reality," and the religious (rather than scientific or secular) character of the Catholic community.

But, properly speaking, since my book is philosophical rather than theological, it deals only with the liberation of human experience from the tyranny of a misinterpreatation of the structure of human experience that became authoritative at a time and continues to be operative
