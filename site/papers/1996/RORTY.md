- 21: "As long as we think that 'the world'  names  something  we  ought  to
respect as well as cope  with,  something  person-like  in  that  it  has  a
preferred description of itself,  we  hall  insist  that  any  philosophical
account [is wrong?] save the 'intuition' that 'truth is out there.'
- 68: "The idea of truth as correspondence to  reality  might  gradually  be
replaced by the idea of truth as what comes to be believed in the course  of
free and open encounters."
- 73: Definition  of  the  ironist:  (a)  Doubts  her  own  vocabulary,  (b)
realizes  that  argument  based  on  her  present  vocabulary  can   neither
underwrite nor dissolve these doubts, and (c) she does not  think  that  her
vocabulary is closer to realiity than others, that it is  in  touch  with  a
power not herself. They see the choice between vocabularies as made  neither
within a neutral and universal metavocabulary nor by  an  attempt  to  fight
one's way past appearances to the real,  but  simply  playing  the  new  off
against the old.
- 84: The idea  of  truth:  "Habermas,  and  other  metaphysicians  who  are
suspicious of a  merely  literary  conception  of  philosophyu,  think  that
liberal political freedoms require some consensus about what is  universally
human. DEFINITIONS RATHER THAN MOTIVATING FACTS?. We ironists who  are  also
liberals think that such freedoms require no consensus  on  any  topic  more
basic than their own desirability. From our  angle,  all  that  matters  for
liberal politics is the widely shared conviction that we shall  call  'true'
or 'good' whatever is the outcome of free discussion--that if we  take  care
of political freedom, truth and goodness will take care of themselves."
86: Metaphysics: "The idea that liberal  societies  are  bound  together  by
philosophical beliefs seems to me ludicrous. What bionds societies  together
are common vocabularies and common hopes. The vocabularies  are,  typically,
parasytic on the hopes--in the sense that  the  principal  function  of  the
vocabularies is to tell stories about future outcomes which  compensate  for
present sacrifices." "If social hope has become harder lately, this  is  not
because the clerks have been committing treason but because, since  the  end
of World War II, the  course  of  events  has  made  it  harder  to  tell  a
convincing story of this sort. The cynical and  impregnable  Soviet  Empire,
the4 continuing shorksightekdness and greed of the surving democracies,  and
the exploding, starving populations of  the  Southern  Hemisphere  make  the
problems our parents  faced  in  the  1930--Fascism  and  unemployment--look
almost manageable."
87: "But even if I am right that a liberal culture whose public rhetoric  is
nominalist and historicist is both possible and desirable, I  cannot  go  on
