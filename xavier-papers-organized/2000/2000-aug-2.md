2000-aug-2 September 11, 2000

ON THE SPECULATIVE-PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE DISTINCTION

I make judgments-statements such as “The hammer is in the other room,” not out of the blue but only when they are relevant for action, thus I make the judgments-statements inspired by desire : either I or the other addressee need a hammer and is looking for it. The perlocutionary force-effect of the judgment-statement is for the addressee to go to the other room in search of the hammer. Only when there is disagreement or disbelief on the addressee’s part (which of course cannot happen in the case of a judgment, only of a statement to somebody else) can the illocutionary force-effect of language appear as different from the perlocutionary and as a condition for it. Curiously, it is this invented illocutionary force-effect that becomes primary and renders possible and persuasive the representational view of knowledge. In both cases the invention is the deed of a contemplator of knowledge or of language, not of an agent involved in knowing or speaking. Indeed, the agent is involved in looking for the hammer, not in studying knowledge or language (Monasterio, Nov.3.99, added paragraph on p. 7).

And we have seen also that a true knowledge of a theory can be established only after it has been interiorized and extensively used to interpret experience [MY NOTE: cf. Galileo’s formulation of the law of falling bodies on inclined planes.] Therefore: a mathematical theory can be constructed only by relying on prior tacit knowing and can function as a theory only within an act of tacit knowing (Polanyi, The tacit Dimension, p. 21).

The distinction between a diagram and a (practical) picture may be useful in the context of this paper. A diagram is like a doctrine made up of statements; it speaks to the intellect. A picture moves to respond (Monasterio).

For if all propositions whether positive or negative are either true or false, then any given predicate must either belong to the subject or not, so that if one man affirms that an event of a given character will take place and another denies it, it is plain that the statement of the one will CORRESPOND WITH REALITY and the other will not (Aristotle, On Interpretation, ch. 9, Edition McKeon p. 46).

It is true that if the object of knowledge does not exist there can be no knowledge: for there will no longer be anything to know. Yet it is equally true that , if the knowledge of a certain object does not exist, the object may quite well exist... This is likewise the case with regard to perception: for the object of perception is, it appears, prior to the act of perception (Aristotle, Categories, ch. 7, Edition McKeon, p. 21).

Things are said to be (1) in an accidental sense, (2) by their own nature... (1) Thus when one thing is said in an accidental sense to be another, this is either because both belong to the same thing, and this is , or because that to which the attribute belongs is , or because the subject which has as an attribute that of which it is itself predicated, itself is . - (2) The kinds of essential being are precisely those that are indicated by the figures of predication [i.e. the categories]; for the senses of being are just as many as these figures... (3) Again, ‘being’ and ‘is’ mean that a statement is true, ‘not being’ that it is not true but false–and this alike in the case of affirmation and of negation.... (4) Again, ‘being’ and ‘that which is’ mean that some of the things we have mentioned ‘are ‘ potentially, others in complete reality. For we say both of that which sees potentially and of that which sees actually, that it is ’seeing’... (Aristotle, Metaphysics , ch. 7; Edition McKeon pp. 760-761).

The invention of the illocutionary

Exactly as speculative knowledge was distinguished from practical knowledge and was conceived as representational and was even given the primacy over the latter and made into a condition for the latter, so the illocutionary force of language was distinguished from its perlocutionary force-effects and even became a condition for the perlocutionary. Both speculative knowledge and illocutionary force-effect are inventions, and both inventions are the deed of a contemplator philosopher interested in understanding language, not of an agent-user of language. I want to explore this phenomenon in the field of language, in the hope of reaching a better understanding of the wrongheaded distinction between speculative and practical knowledge and of its disastrous effects.

Polanyi and Sartre

I sense that Polanyi’s “tacit knowledge,” which applies as much to scientific discoveries as to ordinary people’s knowledge and to skills, will be of help here.

In the representational view of language, judgments-statements --as the only linguistic pieces susceptible of truth-correspondence-- occupy the central, paradigmatic place for the understanding of language. [Is this not the case with Aristotle, too, in his philosophical use of the categories?]. This representational view seems to be a result of assuming that using language or acting linguistically is saying something objective to be understood (and, then, accidentally , to be or not to be acted upon, for not all speech acts produce or intend to produce perlocutionary effects ). Actually, however –such  is my opinion, anyway– the appropriate ‘understanding’ of all speech acts, even of  statements,  is itself perlocutionary, for it is practical understanding that consists in performing the expected-intended conduct in linguistic, free response to the speaker’s intention. In other words, in the actual use of language the addressee (anymore than the speaker)  doesn’t understand objectively or speculatively what is said (the content), as Searle and, in general, the Platonists imagine. Rather, the addressee dwells linguistically in the intended, or at least expected,  by the speaker to perform the expected conduct in response. Thus, pace Searle,  if this linguistic conduct doesn’t obtain, linguistic communication fails, for what was expected-intended, that is, meant by the speaker to obtain,  was a conduct-in-response, not a speculative understanding of something said that supposedly reproduces objectively what is the case as the speaker sees it.

How does the distinction illocutionary-perlocutionary originate? When the communicative act fails in the case of statements to elicit the expected response from the addressee, the participants’ attention turns to the content of the statement instead of dwelling in it practically; then the relationship of the addressee to the content shows as disbelief and the relationship between the speaker and the addressee appears as purely intellectual disagreement.. [MY NOTE: This is the case with the doctrinal papers presented at philosophical conventions. Because philosophers already work on the assumption of the objective meaning of statements, they aim at speculative assent, unlike religious sermons or the religious reading the Bible as a ‘storical’ revelatory book, in relation to whose stories the reader situates himself.] Thus the “content” is isolated from the speaker’s practical intention and becomes speculative, something said about the object referred to, something to be considered speculatively, to be understood and assented to or disagreed with. In this way –not in the moment of using language but in the moment of studying  or observing it– the representational view of knowledge and the illocutionary-perlocutionary view of language are born.

To come now to Polanyi , he speaks of “tacit knowledge” because the agent ’knows’ more than what he can say, that is, more than what he can encapsulate in the content of a statement. For instance, in the case of electric shocks, the person doesn’t know which syllables to avoid in order not to get a shock nor that he is avoiding them, yet he avoids them; so, concludes Polanyi, he ‘knows’ them, that is, knows them tacitly or unknowingly, since he responds to them ( The Tacit Dimension , pp. 7-9).  [MY NOTE: To translate this into my language, what Polanyi seems to be saying is that the person   knows the syllables practically , since he acts appropriately in response to the syllables to avoid the shock, but doesn’t know them theoretically at all , since he cannot state which syllables are accompanied by shocks. But doesn’t this interpretation amount to the Aristotelian mistake of calling two different things by the same  name of “knowledge” and assuming that speculative knowledge not only is the princeps analogatum but is a necessary condition for practical knowledge ? That the two things, namely, speculative and practical knowledge, are different is obvious in this example, since the person has no speculative knowledge and has practical knowledge. ] [SECOND NOTE: The translation I just made into my idiolect may be more problematic than it sounds. One possibility is that practical knowledge of any kind can never be explicitly expressed in a statement –and, consequently, that what is expressed can never correspond to what is the case-- precisely because it is never a matter of saying what is the case. In other words, practical knowledge is not knowledge, as the Platonists (Aristotle included) understand “knowledge,” because there is no correspondence between what is said and the world. Another possibility, closer to the concrete case of the syllables, is that the agent cannot say which syllables he is avoiding or even that he is avoiding syllables because he doesn’t know them even practically, that is, doesn’t know them even as vitanda . In other words, it is a totally unconscious automatism acquired through past experience of pain associated with the syllables.]  

Like Aristotle, Polanyi insists in using the word knowledge --“tacit knowledge” is his expression-- to name the unconscious or unknowing knowledge a person (or animal?) possesses. But is this not a contradiction in terms? After all, what else is this unconscious or tacit knowledge but a case of knowing unknowingly? And what can possibly be meant by knowing unknowingly other than responding appropriately on the basis of past experience? And is this not what Aristotle calls practical knowledge in the Metaphysics, the knowledge that animals share with people? And is this not incompatible with his own representational epistemology?

Sartre ‘platonizes’ in a similar way. Indeed, in the case of the Freudian unconscious “censor,” Sartre solves the problem of unknowing knowledge by saying that the person knows very well what is censored, only the person knows it non-tethically or non-positionally, that is, without stating it explicitly to himself. [MY NOTE:  Sartre, however, seems to assume that the person voluntarily avoids stating it explicitly –that is why the person is in bad fait-- and therefore could state it if he wanted to (which doesn’t seem to be what happens in the case of the syllables, at least not until reflection by an observer occurs). Thus, unlike Polanyi who is more ambiguous, Sartre would definitely reduce practical knowledge to theoretical knowledge . That is, he would say that if the person states explicitly, which he or she can do since she or he  knows implicitly or non-positionally , then the person will be automatically  liberated of his (apparently involuntary) defense and will act authentically. Which is like saying that speculative knowledge has the primacy, for it is the necessary and even sufficient condition for acting appropriately. This is pure platonism, unlike Aristotle’s case, who constitutes a half-way house between platonism and something else, namely, practical knowledge as Humean expectation which in linguistic agents can be reflected upon and systemaztized and instrumentalized as Galileo did ].

Polanyi’s analysis, though ultimately Platonic, for it doesn’t explicitly do away with speculative knowledge, is more sophisticated. And I think that what Sartre calls knowing non-positionally (that is, non-verbally, ‘non-judgingly’ or ‘non-statingly’) could be profitably translated into what Polanyi calls knowing by indwelling or “attending from,” which involves a certain awareness or consciousness of what is dwelled-in and can therefore originate the confusion of speculative with practical knowledge. [MY NOTE:   I myself call practical knowledge the interiorization or indwelling or practical disposition to act in a certain way –which in animals is totally non-verbal, and even in people in certain cases like that of the syllables is non-verbal too--under the appropriate circumstances, which actually results in action under the appropriate circumstances. However, I don’t conceive it as a variety of knowledge as representational of what is the case, for I don’t think that representational knowledge exists at all ]. So, unlike myself,  both --Sartre certainly and Polanyi probably-- think that it is a matter of knowledge-representation, though of a special variety, much as Aris thinks that practical knowledge is knowledge but of a special variety not fully identifiable with Plato’s knowledge, that is, contemplative knowledge. But are they right? Polanyi himself makes me hesitate to agree with him on this, for he says:

Since we were not attending to the particulars in themselves, we could not identify them, [MY NOTE: And even if we identified them objectively or thetically, that wouldn’t function as practical knowledge (as Polanyi himself acknowledges), for the two are different. But, then, since speculative knowledge is not related to action and is not resemblant to what is the case, it is not really knowledge at all], but if we now regard the integration of particulars as an interiorization, it takes on a more positive character. It now becomes a means of making certain things function as the proximal terms of tacit knowing [MY NOTE: something like a map or a blind man’s stick], so that instead of observing them in themselves, we may be aware of them in their bearing on the comprehensive entity which they constitute. It brings home to us that it is not by looking at things, but by indwelling in them, that we understand their joint meaning . - We can see know how an unbridled lucidity can destroy our understanding of complex matters... By concentrating attention on his fingers, a pianist can temporarily paralyze his movement (p. 18).  

We possess a practical knowledge of our own body , but the physiologist’s theoretical knowledge of it is far more revealing (p. 20). [MY NOTE: Like Aristotle who compares speculative and practical knowledge as to their precision, thereby assuming that they have a common genus, Polanyi makes a similar mistake here. Actually my (knowledge of)  my body for me is totally different of the physiologist’s knowledge of the body, as Polanyi himself will acknowledge a few lines below. Furthermore, the physiologist’s knowledge of the body is practical too (though not of the same kind as my knowledge of my body for me), not speculative or representational. The observed physiological regularities of the human body are the basis of the medical science, which constitutes a practical diagram and is, therefore, applicative.] The formal rules of prosody may deepen our understanding of so delicate a thing as a poem. - But my examples show clearly that, in general, an explicit integration cannot replace its tacit counterpart. The skill of a driver cannot be replaced by a thorough schooling in the theory of the motorcar, the knowledge I have of my own body differs altogether from the knowledge of physiology ,* and the rules of rhyming and prosody do not tell me what a poem told me, without any knowledge of its rules (p. 20) - And we have seen also that a true knowledge of a theory can be established only after it has been interiorized and extensively used to interpret experience (p. 21).

[* MY NOTE: As indicated in the previous note, the physiologist’s knowledge is itself applicative, like a map. The interest of the physiologist’s observation of regularities is that it can be applied. If there were no applications, nobody would have ever made, or made much case of,  the observations of physiologists. Concerning Aristotle on this point, he thinks  that in the moment of practice or application there is no difference between speculative and practical knowledge, because by calling both ‘knowledge’ he reduces practical to speculative knowledge. In other words, he assumes that the speculator and the practical knower know objectively and know the same; only the practical knower goes further since he applies what he knows. Yet Aristotle explicitly grants  that the knowledge of the practitioner is superior to that of the purely speculative knower, which indicates that, after all, speculative knowledge is not the same as practical knowledge. ]

My point against Polanyi (and Sartre and Aristotle) is that in “attending from” –be it in physiology or in the application of a formula to a particular problem to solve it in theory or to transform the  environment in practice– we do not know speculatively at all, for the formula or the verbal  formalization are themselves meant to function as maps; they do not say objectively or representatively “the [intelligible] nature” or “the essence” of the particulars . In fact, as Polanyi says, we do not know the particulars, we ‘know’ from them, and that which we ‘know’ from them is not an ‘ Objekt,’ as Kant calls the term or noema of the act of knowing. This is why the knowledge of the particulars from which we ‘know’ is not needed and may even disturb the conduct. In the case of typing, for instance, I begin by pushing a letter and see that that letter appears on the paper. The next time, guided by my desire tow rite, I do that on purpose. However, the full indwelling obtains only when I do that not as an end but, so to say, ‘indwellingly,’ when I attend to the letter to be written from the letter pushed –pushed, not known--, when I apply the stated recipe for typing. Seeing the letter typed on the paper refers to me the agent who have an interest in typing, not to me as a contemplator. I can know theoretically how to type, or the way in which typewriters function, but this knowledge doesn’t make me a typist, anymore than the speculative knowledge about driving cars makes me a driver or is even required for driving. The reason is not only that speculative knowledge is not necessary in order for the agent to perform correctly, but that there is no such a thing as speculative knowledge . For the so-called speculative knowledge does not reproduce speculatively or objectively what is the case (namely, that a typed letter appears on the paper when the letter is pushed), but is to be dwelled in by the interested agent , not to be seen or understood by a spectator. The disinterested spectator who grasps that the letter appears on the paper every time the letter is pushed knows nothing, literally, for this is not to be grasped as a fact, but to be dwelled in by the interested agent as a map or a recipe mediated by desire of a goal .

But is there such a thing as practical knowledge? If by knowledge we understand the factual or objective reproduction or representation of what is the case, the answer is no. If by knowledge we understand an agent’s ability to interiorize a regularity and attend from it in order to achieve a purpose --and in the case of the human agent to interiorize reflectively-- the answer is yes. But then, rather than equivocating by speaking of knowledge, whose paradigmatic cases --perception, and judgment statement-- involve reproduction as well as truth conceived as certitude of correct reproduction or representation, we should speak of different kinds of agents and agency: from purely mechanical effect of the environment on non-sensitive agents without needs and desires, to deliberate transformation of the environment (work) and deliberate self-creation by the agent.

We can now understand where Polanyi and Sartre went wrong : (a) they still believed that there was such a thing as speculative, objective knowledge; (b) although dwelling in and attending from and non-positional do not involve reproduction, they insisted, like Aris, in calling them “knowledge,” which is totally equivocal.

There remain two important issues. (1) Doesn’t Searle have a point when he says that language, unlike other instruments, involves understanding and, therefore, is cognitive? (2) If all knowledge is practical or functions as a map or recipe, what is the truth-value of my own reflections?

When I study geometry I internalize speculatively that the sum of the three angles of a triangle equals two straight angles. But this doesn’t guarantee that I’ll know how to apply the rule to solve a problem. It is by actually applying the rule that I internalize it practically, as the Egyptians originally did . [MY NOTE: A word means when it is used appropriately. By stating exactly what a word means Searle thinks that he will know what the word means, as if meaning were equivalent to speculative knowledge of what a word  means. This reminds me of  Plato’s Socrates in the case of piety (cf. the Euthyphro) , who seemed to believe that in order to be a pious agent one needed to possess the exact, scientific or essential definition-knowledge of piety.]

To say that a speaker S meant something by X is to say that S intended the utterance of X to produce some effect in a hearer H by means of the recognition of his intention (p. 43) GRICE in effect defines meaning in terms of intending to perform a perlocutionary act , BUT saying something and meaning it is a matter of intending to perform an illocutionary act, not necessarily a perlocutionary act... What we can mean is at least sometimes a function of what we are saying. Meaning is more than a matter of intention, it is also at least sometimes a matter of convention (p. 45) [Against Grice], “I wish to argue that saying something and meaning it is a matter of intending to perform an illocutionary act [ because, he argues,  many acts like “Hello” have no perlocutionary effect ]. “When I say Hello and mean it, I do not necessarily intend to produce or elicit any state or action in my hearer other than the knowledge that he is being greeted. But that knowledge is simply his understanding what I said ” (p. 46)... “ The characteristic intended effect of meaning is understanding , but understanding is not the kind of effect that is included in Grice’s examples of effects” (p. 47). “Understanding a sentence is knowing its meaning” (p. 48).

In the above text together with “my notes” are expressed my differences with Searle.

His view, against Grice’s, is that all speech acts say something to be understood speculatively, with no direct connection with action ; in addition, some speech acts produce a perlocutionary effect, that is, an effect that is different from understanding, but this is not essential for the success of the speech act as such.

I think that the problem with Searle is that he doesn’t get at all the practical understanding involved in speech acts among interested agents, but reduces it to a speculative understanding of a pure meaning among uninterested angels , as if when I give an order I intended primarily the addressee to understand that I am ordering rather than that my order be carried out by him. Actually, I intend my order to be understood practically, not as a pure meaning, as something meant, and that practical understanding amounts to, and is shown in, my order being freely carried out in response to me. Thus Searle is wrong in saying that understanding is not the kind of effect included in Grice’s examples of effects, for Grice explicitly says that the speaker intends to produce an effect in the addressee by the latter’s recognition of his intention, which is precisely what practical linguistic understanding amounts to . [MY NOTE: But Searle is right in thinking that understanding as he, Searle, understands it, as speculative understanding , is not included in Grice’s examples of effects.] In short, the recognition of which Grice speaks is practical understanding which, unless prevented by the circumstances or by the speaker’s decision, consists in actually obeying the S’s order . The “understanding from” characteristic of language is practical, for it trans-forms the agent by disposing him – as a free subject, for in its paradigmatic form linguistic communcation  is communication among human adults -- to act in a certain way in recognition of S’s intention. In the case of “Hello” there is no ‘object,’ that is, no reference to something in the environment. Here the communication is from subject to subject; therefore the appropriate response is to respond to the saluter by saluting him, as in the subject to subject communication “I love you” the appropriate response is to the subject too: “I love you too.” Thus Searle is wrong: in every speech act there is an intended perlocutionary effect.

In different ways, the same reduction of the practical to the speculative seems to have been the problem with many important figures of the Western philosophical tradition . Plato began as a contemplator of knowledge obsessed with necessity and universality. Therefore he reduced practical to speculative knowledge without even noticing the difference between them. Aristotle distinguished the two, but didn’t escape sufficiently from Plato’s influence. Thus, by calling both the practical and the speculative ”knowledge,” and by borrowing his notion of knowledge from Plato, he ultimately confused practical with speculative knowledge and, under the name of episteme or scientific knowledge as the highest kind, he attributed to speculative knowledge the primacy over imprecise (non-akribos) practical knowledge. Hume is the one who came closer to the acknowledgment of interiorized experience as knowledge from, as a practical map for action and decision. However, he didn’t quite escape the tradition, for “as a philosopher” he still attributed the primacy to speculative knowledge, while recognizing the impossibility of moving from presentation to action, from ‘is’ to ‘ought.’ Sartre and Polanyi recognized the essentially practical character of interiorized experience, yet they still thought of it as a sort of knowledge, that is, speculative reproductive knowledge. Finally, in the area of language, because he confused the language he overheard as a philosopher of language with the language he spoke and heard as a user , Searle returned to Plato before Aristotle and totally ignored knowledge from, that is, interiorized practical experience that functions for the agent as a map or a recipe for his or her future actions in relation to the surrounding world.

(2) On Kuhn and on the value of these reflections

“The paradigm as shared example is the central element... Exemplars will therefore require more attention than the other sort of components of the disciplinary matrix. Philosophers of science have not ordinarily discussed the problems encountered by a student in laboratories or in science texts, for these are thought to supply only in practice the application of what the student already knows. He cannot, it is said, solve problems at all unless he has first learned the theory and some rules for applying it. Scientific knowledge is embedded in theory and rules; problems are supplied to gain facility in their application. I have tried to argue, however, that this localization of the cognitive content of science is wrong . After the student has done many problems, he may gain only added facility by solving more. But at the start and for some time after, doing problems is learning consequential things about nature. In the absence of such exemplars, the laws and theories he has previously learned would have little empirical content ” [MY NOTE: Notice that the view Kuhn is attacking here is equivalent to Searle’s view of language as essentially illocutionary and only accidentally perlocutionary.] (Kuhn, The Structure of SR , Postscript, pp. 187-8. See what follows as an example on p. 188). - That sort of learning is not acquired by exclusively verbal means. Rather it comes as one is given words together with concrete examples of how they function in use; nature and use are learned together . To borrow once more Michael Polanyi’s useful phrase, what results from this process is ‘tacit knowledge’ which is learned by doing science rather than by acquiring rules for doing it ” ( Ibid. , p. 191). - “It is just because so very few ways of seeing will do that the ones that have withstood the tests of group use are worth transmitting from generation to generation. Equally, it is because they have been selected for their success over historic time that we must speak of the experience and knowledge of nature embedded in the stimulus-to-sensation route.   Perhaps ‘knowledge ‘ is the wrong word, but there are reasons for employing it. .. Those are characteristics of knowledge, and they explain why I use the term. But it is strange usage, for one other characteristic is missing. We have no direct access to what it is we know, no rules or generalizations with which to express this knowledge. Rules which could supply that access would refer to stimuli not sensations, and stimuli we can know only through elaborate theory. In its absence, the knowledge embedded in the stimulus-to-sensation route remains tacit ... Seeing water droplets or a needle against a numerical scale is a primitive perceptual experience for a man unacquainted with cloud chambers and ammeters. It thus requires contemplation, analysis, and interpretation (or else the intervention of external authority) before conclusions can be reached about electrons or currents. But the position of the man who has learned about these instruments and had much exemplary experience with them is very different, and there are corresponding differences in the way he processes the stimuli that reach him from them. Regarding the vapor in his breath on a cold winter afternoon, his sensation may be the same as that of the layman, but viewing a cloud chamber he sees (here literally) not droplets but the tracks of electrons, alpha particles, and so on. Those tracks are, if you will, criteria that he interprets as indices of the presence of corresponding particles, but that route is both shorter and different  from that taken by the man who interprets droplets ... In the metaphorical no less than in the literal use of ‘seeing’ interpretation begins where perception ends . The two processes are not the same, and what perception leaves for interpretation to complete depends drastically on the nature and amount of prior experience and training” ( Ibid. , pp. 196-198). - “The proponents of different theories are like the members of different language-culture communities. Recognizing the parallelism suggests that in some sense both groups may be right. Applied to culture and its development that position is relativistic. But applied to science it may not be...” (Ibid., p. 205). - “Compared with the notion of progress most prevalent among both philosophers of science and laymen, however, this position lacks an essential element. A scientific theory is usually felt to be better than its predecessors not only in the sense that it is a better instrument for discovering and solving puzzles but also because it is somehow a better REPRESENTATION of what nature is really like. One often hears that successive theories grow ever closer to, or approximate more closely, to the truth . Apparently generalizations like that refer not to the puzzle-solutions and the concrete predictions derived from a theory but rather to its ontology, to the match, that is, between the entities with which the theory populates nature and what is ‘really there’... Perhaps there is one other way of salvaging the notion of ‘truth’ for application to whole theories, but this one will not do. There is, I think, no theory independent way to reconstruct phases like ‘really there’; the notion of a match between the ontology of a theory and its real counterpart in nature now seems to me illusive in principle . Besides, as a historian, I am impressed with the implausibility of the view. I do not doubt, for example, that Newton’s mechanics improves on Aristotle’s and that Einstein’s improves on Newton’s AS INSTRUMENTS FOR PUZZLE-SOLVING. The preceding pages present a viewpoint or theory about the nature of science, and like other philosophies of science, the theory has consequences for the way in which scientists should behave if their enterprise is to succeed. [MY NOTE: This applies to Searle’s linguistic characterizations and mine.] Though it need not be right, any more than any other theory, it provides a legitimate basis for reiterated ‘oughts’ and ‘shoulds.’ Conversely one set of reasons for taking the theory seriously is that scientists, whose methods have been developed and selected for their success, do in fact behave as the theory says they should. On p. 3 above I raised two questions. One concerned Searle, and I took care of that in the preceding section. Now I want to deal with the second, whose point is to determine the value of my reflections in this paper, given that all knowledge is practical and functions as a map or a recipe. After all, my present reflections seem to be valid only if what is the case corresponds to what I say, which involves the very notion of knowledge and truth that I have attacked. In short,  I deny that what Searle says about language is valid because language is not an object but an instrument. Yet I seem to be doing the same as Searle, for I myself  am using speculative language that doesn’t seem to function as an instrument. But if that is the case,  then what is the use of the theory? [MY NOTE: We theorize because we make mistakes. Our theories should be a prescriptive formulation of what we actually do when we succeed. We do not need them in order to act successfully, since they are grounded in the successful practice rather than the other way around. But not all theories or characterizations are correct formalizations. And since they are formalizations of a practice, when they are incorrect they lead to malpractice, or at least to a wrong self-understanding by the agent. Hence they need to be opposed with the correct theory.]

. A theory of the kind we are considering here is like  the linguistic characterizations of which Searle speaks. (And like the philosophy of science Kuhn champions that finds its application and verification in the concrete particular natural sciences, particularly physics). A linguistic characterization supposedly is a formalization of what we do when we use language. Now  I have been saying  that Searle’s own characterization of language is wrong, that we do not use language the way he says we do, that Searle himself doesn’t use language according to his characterization but according to mine. In short, I have been saying that his characterization is wrong because it doesn’t correspond to what we do when we use language when we communicate, and that my characterization  is right because it corresponds. But “corresponds” here doesn’t mean “represents as a picture” but “corresponds as a diagram or as a map, practically, in that, as Kuhn says, it generates ‘shoulds’ and ‘oughts.’ It is not a matter of true or false, but of persuasive or not persuasive to the very linguistic agent whose linguistic action is characterized.

A HINT: As Polanyi says above (and is confirmed by Kuhn), the formalization of a theory follows a long practice: “And we have seen that a true knowledge of a theory can be established only after it has been interiorized and extensively used to interpret experience” ( Tacit Knowledge , p. 21). The important point, for my purposes, is that a theory is a formalization of a practice for the purpose of further, more refined practice. Thus, at least initially, it doesn’t precede but follows the practice. Moreover, the theory doesn’t determine the practice; it only makes explicit what we actually do in practice without an explicit statement of the theory, only with the tacit knowledge or non-verbal, practical internalization that cannot be substituted by any verbalization. In short, the theory is an explicit formulation of the rules of the game we play, but we play it without explicitly knowing the rules; we know the rules only in and by applying them. And we do not need to know speculatively the rules because the rules are practical, part and parcel of the practice. What counts is the practical internalization involved in the practice and, in the case of acquired habits, made possible by the frequent practice.

An example: Typing. [MY NOTE: This example is poorly chosen, for it is complicated by the communicative meaning of what one types. A mere skill, like car-driving, in which no meaning is involved, would be better]. The apparently speculative judgment-statement “A letter pushed shows on the paper” is not speculative for it is a formalization of the practice by an agent or for an agent interested in typewriting and is therefore meant to other interested agents as applicative, or directly herd-understood by the interested agent applicatively, as map for action under the appropriate circumstances. However, even this applicative interiorization that has the form of a rule (gerundive) remains theoretical until it actually informs action. As a person to whom an order is addressed understands an order by and in obeying it, for obedience to it is the practical understanding meant by the speaker, so it is in the actual pushing of letters by an interested agent to fulfill the communicative desire that the apparently speculative formulation reaches fruition. So the supposedly speculative formulation is an illusion of a contemplator, and understanding it as speculative, disconnected from action and unmediated by desire ,  is simply a mistake. Moreover, identifying the understanding therein involved as speculative is highly misleading as to our self understanding as people. for it makes us see ourselves primarily as disinterested contemplators in search of whatever can be known, as Aristotle imagined. [MY NOTE: Searle’s characterization of language as meaning something to be understood or known theoretically is of this kind and in no way corresponds to what the linguistic agent does or to the way in which language actually functions. Fortunately, not even the theorists, the ‘philosophers of language’ who  assent theoretically to that formulation assent to it practically, for if they used language in that way they wouldn’t be speaking and hearing language, anymore than the skeptics would be alive if they exited from rooms through the windows rather than through the doors, or anymore than the students of language making sentences in the classroom are speaking-hearing language, for they ‘say’ (utter sounds) without ‘meaning’ it. ]

Thus even the applicative theoretical interiorization is by itself useless, for it is meant applicatively, and thus, as just said,  it is useful only when applied by agents. In fact, however, this theoretical applicative interiorization doesn’t precede the practice; it follows the practice –and only for purposes of transmitting the practice to interested agents who will understand the theory only then they internalize it through practice. Indeed, only in the case of those who learn from the experts does the applicative theoretical ‘interiorization’ obtain, in the sense that it is stated by the teacher. And not quite, really, for it doesn’t obtain as theoretical but as practical, in actual application, for it is not genuinely internalized unless it is actually practiced and through practice. The original expert himself or herself didn’t need the theoretical statement. Geometry started as a practice of interested users in Egypt. Euclid formulated and systematized it, but not as a purely speculative science. The teacher knows that the disciple has understood, not when the disciple recites the theorem or even when the disciple demonstrates the theorem, but when the disciple has interiorized the model theorem as a ‘rule’ so that it can solve easily different problems by applying the rule, much as the teacher of a foreign language gives ‘problems’ to the disciples that lead them to apply the rule to different cases, so that the application of the rule becomes a facile habit until, ultimately, the disciple goes native in what previously was a foreign language. The teacher cannot teach the disciple to see a particular situation as one in which a particular formula is to be applied. The disciple has to find out by himself or herself the situation in which the  particular formula applies.
