2002-aug-1-Kuhn’s contribution                                August 16, 02

      A REINTERPRETATION OF KUHN’S CONCEPTION OF SCIENCE

(Cf. my <2001-April-1-, revised on March 2002, particularly pages 7-9>, of
which the present paper is a further development).

      The notion of “paradigm,” the paradigmatic character of normal
science, and the development of science through paradigmatic revolutions
seem to be the most important contributions credited to Kuhn by those who
attribute any validity to his conception of science. Oddly enough, the
majority of his admirers are philosophers, cultural anthropologists,
sociologists, linguists, but very seldom practitioners of the hard sciences
to which Kuhn was primarily referring. The reason for this oddity seems to
be, at least in part, that the practitioners of the hard sciences, because
of the nature of their disciplines, are less likely than the practitioners
of “soft sciences”’to be familiar with the philosophical notions involved
in Kuhn’s conception of science. That in the first place. In the second
place, when they take the time to study Kuhn’s works, they see him as
demoting the hard sciences from heir traditional pedestal by advocating a
notion of science that they find irremediably relativistic or even simply
skeptical – totally at odds with their own views concerning the certainty
of science. The “soft scientists,” by contrast, are less likely to resent a
conception of science that, on the one hand, implicitly denies the
superiority of the hard sciences over the soft ones, and, on the other
hand, does away with a notion of absolute truth which they themselves never
found applicable in their own area of expertise and never believed
applicable even in the area of the hard sciences.

      For my part, I agree with Kuhn’s admirers that the mentioned
contributions they credit to him are deserved. I think, however, that
Kuhn’s most important contribution consisted in a radically different
conception of science as a problem-solving activity by contrast to the
commonly accepted image, to which Kuhn refers as ‘the textbook view of
science’ as a knowledge acquiring activity I think, moreover, that,
educated himself in the traditional view of science perpetuated by the
textbooks, and  baffled by the novelty of the different view of science he
was championing, Kuhn himself failed to bring to clear focus the
alternative he was proposing to the old view. I will try to pinpoint the
novelty of Kuhn’s own idea of science. As for the objections of skepticism
and relativism leveled against Kuhn’s position, I am confident that when
his alternative view of science is fully explained, it automatically will
take care of such objections.

The textbook view of science

      In the very first page of the introduction to The Structure of
Scientific Revolutions Kuhn attacks “the image of science by which we are
now possessed,” and specifies his own purpose:

      “that image has previously been drawn... mainly from the study of
      finished scientific achievements as these are recorded in the classics
      and, more recently, in the textbooks from which each new scientific
      generation learns to practice its trade. Inevitably, however, the aim
      of such books is persuasive and pedagogic; a concept of science drawn
      from them is no more likely to fit the enterprise that produced them
      than an image of a national culture drawn from a tourist brochure or a
      language text. This essay attempts to show that we have been misled by
      them in fundamental ways. Its aim is a sketch of the quite different
      concept of science that can emerge from the historical record of the
      research activity itself”[1]

      The view of science presented by the textbooks that teach beginners
their scientific trade  focuses on the chain of the most salient, already
acquired pieces of knowledge contributed by the genuinely innovative
scientists, together with a description of the equipment, observations,
measurements and experiments through which those pieces  were
scientifically established. In addition, each section of the textbooks is
accompanied by concrete problems through whose solution the student is
supposed to obtain experimental confirmation of the validity of the law at
stake, as well as gaining an increasing facility in applying the knowledge
constituted by the laws at stake.

      Subjacent to this textbook view is the notion that science grows by
cumulative accretion of pieces of knowledge, much as a pyramid develops by
accretion of single bricks and of successive layers of bricks.  To spell
out a little more the textbook image, each genuinely innovative scientist
contributes a brick or two to the scientific pyramid, and each generation
of innovative scientists contributes another layer of observed facts and
laws and theories.

      As is too well known to need much elaboration, Kuhn’s idea of the
development of science is radically different from that which is implied by
the textbook view. “Normal science,” according to Kuhn,. appears after a
pre-paradigmatic period in which there is no fundamental agreement among
the practitioners. Each scientist moves on his or her own, according to his
or her own best lights. Such continues to be the case until a “paradigm”
emerges that gains general acceptance. Normal science, by contrast,  is
paradigmatic.  More precisely, it is an activity characterized by a shared
view of the nature of the entities that constitute the particular field
and, correspondingly, by a shared manner of solving the problems typical of
the field. The shared view of the nature of the entities that constitute
the field, though, confronts irregularities that threaten the shared view.
However, rather than renouncing the shared view defined by the paradigm
because of the irregularities incompatible with the paradigm, the
scientists who share the view defined by the paradigm live with those
irregularities and endeavor to accommodate them within the paradigm.  Some
of those irregularities prove, in the long run, to be merely apparent, when
a solution to accommodate them within the paradigm is found. However, some
other of the  irregularities that threaten the paradigm because of their
incompatibility with it may in the long run provoke a crisis. The crisis
may lead to the abandonment of the current paradigm when an alternative
view of the nature of the entities that constitute the field and,
correspondingly, a new manner of solving the problems typical of the field
emerges and rallies new adepts among the practicing scientists. Such an
event, that is, the shift of paradigms, constitutes what Kuhn calls a
“revolution.”

      Herein lies the explosiveness of Kuhn’s position. According to the
classical view of science, represented by the textbook image, science
genuinely grows. or develops, in that the truths discovered  by each
generation of innovative scientists are preserved after the revolution, and
the next generation adds to the previously known scientific truths while
preserving therm. As a result, there is genuine scientific growth or
increase in scientific truth; and science is asymptotically moving toward
total possession of the truth. Not so, in Kuhn’s opinion. Within a given
paradigm, there is  growth, indeed, in that the problem-solving activity
made possible by the paradigm progresses further and further.[2] With the
shift of paradigms, however, there is no preservation of the previous
scientific knowledge and, consequently, no genuine addition to it. There
simply is a “revolution,” a new scientific beginning – a new science,
literally, defined by a new paradigm. What was previously taken for genuine
knowledge ceases to be accepted and is superseded. Similarly, what is
presently taken for genuine knowledge under the currently prevailing
paradigm will in its turn cease to be accepted when the present paradigm is
eventually substituted by a new one. That, in Kuhn’s opinion, is what
happened, for example, in the field of physics when the Copernican paradigm
was substituted by the Newtonian, and again when the Newtonian paradigm was
substituted by the Einsteinian. In other words,  Copernican, Newtonian, and
Einsteinian physics are three entirely different physics in which the
putative ‘truths’ of the previous physics are replaced by new and
different, but no less putative, ‘truths.’ Hence the optimistic idea that
science is asymptotically moving toward the total truth is dead. In Kuhn’s
softer, perhaps less offensive words to the same effect,

      “We may... have to relinquish the notion, implicit or explicit, that
      changes of paradigm carry scientists and those who learn from them
      closer and closer to truth.”[3]

      Of course, nothing more is needed to understand the furious attacks
of which Kuhn’s view of science has been the object, as well as the
numerous accusations of relativism and even skepticism leveled against him.
Before pronouncing ourselves one way or another on this important matter,
however, we should pause to reconsider the entire case.

Science as a problem-solving activity

      As already indicated, Kuhn projected to substitute his own conception
of science for the textbook view, which he found defective. Now, as just
explained, the Kuhnian conception involves the paradigmatic character of
normal science, by contrast to pre-paradigmatic or primitive science, as
well as the periodic shifts of paradigms, with the threatening implication
concerning the development of science through revolutionary changes. But
these characteristics of the Kuhnian view of science are only consequences
that flow from his real contribution that consists in the shift from the
traditional conception of science as a knowledge-acquiring activity to a
view of science as a problem-solving activity. Kuhn himself, however, never
spoke clearly and categorically[4]  of this aspect of his outlook as its
essential feature, all to his own detriment and ours. Indeed, as I hope to
show it, everything becomes considerably clearer when the matter is seen in
this light.

      Let us begin with the contrast between the two notions or images of
science as sketched by Kuhn himself. In the following paragraph he explains
that the commonly accepted view of science presupposes that knowledge
amounts to an accurate representation of reality; Consequently, he
identifies the traditional view of science – the view implicit in the
scientific textbooks–  as a knowledge-acquiring activity, that is, as an
activity that results in progress in  the accurate representation of
reality. For his part, Kuhn denies that the scientific activity yields
knowledge in the sense of an accurate representation of the world by the
laws generated by the prevailing paradigm. What the scientific activity
terminates in, when successful, is not knowledge as accurate representation
of reality but in problem-solving ability. In the following paragraph Kuhn
definitely rejects the representational conception of knowledge embedded
the textbook view..

      “A scientific theory is usually felt better than its predecessors...
      because it is somehow representation of what nature is really like.
      One often hears that successive theories grow closer to, or
      approximate more closely to the truth. Apparently generalizations like
      that refer not to the puzzle-solutions and concrete predictions
      derived from a theory but rather to its ontology, to the match, that
      is, between the entities with which the theory populates nature and
      what is ’really’ there. Perhaps there is some other way if saving the
      notion of ‘truth’ for application to whole theories, but this one will
      not do. There is, I think, no theory-independent way to reconstruct
      phrases like ‘really there:’; the notion of a match between the
      ontology of a theory and its ‘real’ counterpart in nature now seems to
      me illusive in principle. Besides, as a historian, I am impressed by
      the implausibility of the view. I do not doubt, for example, that
      Newton’s mechanics improves on Aristotle’s and that Einstein’s
      improves on Newton’s as instruments for puzzle-solving. But I can see
      in their succession no coherent direction of ontological development.
      On the contrary, in some important respects, though by no means in
      all, Einstein’s general theory of  relativity is closer to Aristotle’s
      than to Newton’s. Though the temptation to describe that position as
      relativistic is understandable, the description seems to me wrong.”[5]

      A scientific theory, then, is not, in Kuhn’s eyes, a description of
what is the case in reality, as assumed by the textbook view. In other
words,  it is not a description that is somehow a representation of what
nature is really like.  In that assumption, scientific knowledge would be
localized in understanding what the theory expresses or describes. This is
t he way in which the textbook view of science localizes scientific
knowledge. Hence, as.Kuhn complains, However, Kuhn finds wrong such
localization of scientific knowledge. If it were correct, scientific
knowledge would be gained by understanding what the particular theory or
law says or describes. But that is not the case.  Rather than terminating
in understanding what is supposedly described  in the law scientific
knowledge comes to fruition in knowing how to deal with the world in terms
of solving problems, and in using the law at stake as an instrument to
solve problems.

      BUT I AM SAYING MORE THAN THIS, TO WIT: THAT THE EXPECTATION IS NOT
PRIMARILY OF VISUALLY SIMILAR EFFECTS TO BE OBSERVED, BUT OF EFFECTS TO BE
UNDERGONE OR SUFFERED. Hence the ‘mysterious’ or unformalizable character
of the ‘knowledge’ possessed by the agent, which can be acquired only
through practice until it becomes habitual. In other words, the knowledge
is indexical,: personal appropriation manifested in the habitual
disposition to respond to, or treat, something in the appropriate way to
obtain the desired effects. Chess playing is a good example. The expert
chess player knows the ‘enemy,’ that is, knows how to respond to the other
pieces in any given situation, and this is a ‘knowledge’ that can be gained
only through practice, but the chess player cannot express what he knows
otherwise than by actually responding   or by saying how the enemy is to be
responded to in a given situation. This is problem-solving.

      Another apt example is the way in which children learn their first
language and become ‘native’ in it. They learn it by imitative doing that
is rewarded with the desired effects. They come to know the grammar
practically, but not theoretically. They know grammar only in that they are
capable of producing instrumentally correct sentences to obtain the desired
effects. They posses their knowledge only habitually; they possess it as an
instrumental habit. They come to  know the grammar as one comes  to possess
one’s own body. Not only do they not know objectively the rules of the
grammar of their native language. Moreover,  they don’t need to know the
grammar in that way, for knowledge is practical-instrumental, after the
model of the habitual knowledge of one’s own body. Imagine a person who
knew perfectly the grammar of his or her native language by having learned
it, but who was incapable of generating any appropriate sentence to achieve
his or her linguistic aims. This person would not know his or her language
instrumentally, which is the only appropriate way of knowing a language –as
a problem-solving device that one masters as one masters one’s own body.

      HOWEVER –and this, indeed, is a BIG however– here enters the
intuition involved in the notion of practical knowledge that prevents the
free-fall into sheer relativism and unbounded skepticism, for, as
acknowledged by Kuhn, so very few ‘ways of seeing’ are successful.
Practical knowledge is not knowledge in the sense of contemplative
representation of the real out there. Yet it is practical in the sense of
being an apt guide for action, as all SIGNALS are meant to be. And signals
–even linguistic signals– are not representations of what is signaled, for
what is signaled is not an object to be perceived or understood, but an
action to be performed. We should therefore treat it as such rather than
insisting in exploring, and futilely attempting to justify, a
representational value that our judgments and statements about reality do
not possess at all because they are not pictures.*

* Notice, however, that there may be such a thing as intra-paradigmatic
truth. Once a ‘way of seeing’ has proven successful, the predicative
statements generated according to the grammar of the way of seeing will be
deemed true by the members of the paradigm. \\

      This all-important point about the practical nature of knowledge can
best be explained by reference to Kuhn’s notion of science as a problem-
solving activity, by contrast to the traditional, “textbook’ view of
science as a truth-gaining or representational knowledge-gaining activity.

      When I deny that there is such a thing as theoretical knowledge, I am
not denying by implication that science is valuable in any way; I am just
denying, as Kuhn does, that it is valuable in terms of giving us the
correct –or even just a better, more accurate– picture of the world. But,
then, what does science give us? As in the case of inverting lenses when
the inversion is reversed, it gives the agents, depending on their
situation at the moment of application, the optimal view for moving around
within the perceived space, depending on their particular project. But the
view is practical, hodological, indexical. And it is the result of acting,
not of seeing. Actually, more than a particular view of a particular
object, it is an assimilated, habitual ability to act in order to achieve
our goals, similar to the motor ability that a recently blinded person
loses and that in the long run partially reacquires as he or she moves
around, first in a familiar room and later in unfamiliar space, aided by
subliminal auditive or other clues. This is the ability that a person
wearing inverting lenses loses and, after a certain time of practice of
moving around, suddenly reacquires.

      What do I mean by saying that it is not a particular view but a
habitual ability? Think of a person who is quite familiar with a section of
a city. At any time, that person ‘knows’ how to go
from any point to any other point within that section. Moreover, if asked,
he or she usually can draw a map, or at least give verbal directions to
somebody else. We tend to imagine that such a person has a map of that
section in his or her head. But that is not the case. A primitive person
may not be able to draw a map of a region with which he or she is quite
familiar, nonetheless is capable of reaching unerringly any place. He or
she may not be able to give verbal directions, but is capable of guiding
others unerringly from any place to the place they want to reach. And to
reach that place he or she doesn’t have to give himself or herself
directions, as he or she would give to other people. This person ’just
knows,’ He or she possesses a practical spatial motor scheme of the region
constantly modified according to his or her present position ,(which does
not consist in any particular view of the region), assimilated in his or
her body thanks to the experience of moving in the region. We can say that
his or her body knows. This motor scheme is what I described above as a
habitual ability to move around in a certain region to achieve our goals.

      The acquisition of this motor scheme may be initially facilitated by
the use of maps drawn by other experts in the region, but it is not
equivalent to using a map. Only the expert in a region can draw a map of
the region, but the first expert did not gain his or her expertise by means
of a map. Most certainly, the first expert preceded and drew the first map,
not the other way around. In other words, maps are the products of
experience, but experience is not –at least not necessarily– the result of
maps.

      Keeping in mind these suggestive comparisons, let us move now to the
area of the experimental sciences, and of physics in particular, which is
the area of which Kuhn is talking. A great discoverer is the creator of a
paradigm, or the one who extends it to the solution of other problems
previously not dealt with within the prevailing paradigm. A paradigm is
comparable to the habitual motor scheme just mentioned. Kuhn speaks of it
as a shared way of seeing, but he realizes that the ‘seeing’ is
metaphorical. It is a practical construction or organization of the
scientific field –unspecifiable in words because it is not objective
knowledge–  that renders possible the scientific doing, the actual problem-
solving.[6] As all habits, it is acquired or internalized through practice
until its appropriate exercise becomes automatic. The sketch or formulation
 of the law is comparable to a map. The problem-solving is comparable to
the use of the map by actually going to desired places. Very appropriately,
Kuhn compares the acquisition of a paradigm to the acquisition of a second
language in which one ends up becoming ‘native’ thanks to much practice.

      Now there is a great difference between the geniuses who inaugurate
or significantly extend a paradigm and those who appropriate the paradigm
by being taught by those who already know. The difference is comparable to
that between a person who knows a geographical  region through practical
familiarity with it, so that he or she can reach any particular point from
any particular point without the help of a map (which is comparable to
solving problems in science), and a person who reaches one particular point
thanks to the directions received from someone who knows the region. The
latter, following the directions, can reach only one particular place and
from one particular place, whereas the former can reach any place, even
places where he or she has never actually been. The latter, by contrast,
has not learned ‘how to solve problems’ yet, because he or she has not
appropriated the paradigm, yet.

      The process through which we people come to know a region so as to be
able to go from any place to any other place and to give directions to
others is to an extent mysterious, at least in
that the actual procedure cannot be verbally specified. All we can say is
that it is a matter of practice, or of experience through the repeated
practice of moving around in the region. In a comparable way, Kuhn says
that the student learns to do science through the practice of solving the
problems typical of the particular scientific field until one becomes
‘native’ in the field, as one becomes ‘native’ in a second language.

      But how does this view of science as a problem-solving activity
differ from the view of science as a knowledge-gaining activity? And from
where does the latter view arise?

      One thing that Kuhn suggests is that the textbook view of science,
which he deems wrongheaded, focuses on already acquired scientific results
rather than on the process through which they are gained. Thus, for
instance, the discovery of oxygen will be attributed to a particular
scientist, or the discovery of the law of falling bodies on inclines will
be attributed to Galileo. Moreover, what is discovered is considered as a
piece of knowledge, the accumulation of which pieces gives us an ever more
complete and accurate view of the universe. Thus knowledge is understood as
the view that reflects or expresses accurately what is the case in reality.
(See p. 4 above, quotation that corresponds to note 4).

      For his part, Kuhn rejects the idea that science yields an accurate,
or at least an ever more accurate and complete view of the world. The
reason is that he rejects the notion of knowledge as correspondence to what
is the case, and, consequently, the idea that science yields knowledge in
the sense of a correct, or increasingly more accurate, image of the world.

      To grasp the difference between these two conceptions of science one
only needs to realize the very different function played by the problem-
solving activity in each of them.[7] According to the textbook view, what
is gained from solving problems is, in Kuhn’s words,

      “only practice in the application of what the student already knows.
      He cannot, it is said, solve problems unless he has first learned the
      theory and some rule for applying it. Scientific knowledge is embedded
      in theory and rules; problems are supplied to gain facility in their
      application. I have tried to argue, however, that this localization of
      the cognitive content of science is wrong. After the student has done
      many problems, he may gain only added facility by solving more. But at
      the start and for some time after, doing problems is learning
      consequential tings about nature. In the absence of such exemplars,
      the laws and theories he has previously learned would have little
      empirical content... A phenomenon familiar to both students of science
      and historians of science provides a clue. The former regularly report
      that they have read through a chapter of a text and understood it
      perfectly, but nonetheless had difficulty solving a number of the
      problems at the chapter’s end. Ordinarily also those difficulties
      dissolve in the same way. The student discovers... a way to see this
      problem as like a problem he has already encountered. Having seen the
      resemblance, grasped the analogy between two or more distinct
      problems, he can interrelate symbols and attach them to nature in the
      ways proved effective before. The law sketch say f=ma, has functioned
      as a tool, informing the student what similarities to look for,
      signaling the gestalt in which the situation is to be seen. The
      resultant ability too see a variety of situations as like each other,
      as subjects for f=ma or some other symbolic generalization, is, I
      think the main thing a student acquires by doing problems, whether
      with a pencil of in a well designed laboratory. After he has completed
      a certain number..., he views the situations that confront him as a
      scientist in the same gestalt as other members of his specialists’s
      group. For him they are no longer the same situations he had
      encountered when his training began. He has meanwhile assimilated a
      time-tested and group-licensed way of seeing” (pp. 187-190).

      According to the textbook view, the formulated law is what is to be
learned by the student, for it supposedly constitutes the piece of
scientific knowledge contributed by the genius who discovered it. The
textbook, therefore, will focus on the law or the theorem. It will state
and explain the law, and describe the experiment or experiments that
demonstrated that truth to the genius. Of course, since that truth is about
the world, it will find application in the world. At the time of
application, however, what the student will gain by solving problems is not
knowledge, for,  supposedly,  he or she already possesses it in knowing and
understanding the law. Rather the student will gain mere facility in
applying the already acquired knowledge to concrete problems. According to
Kuhn, however, “this localization of the cognitive content of science is
wrong.” What exactly is wrong?

      In the textbook view, the focus is on the law or the theorem because
it is a piece of knowledge, that is, it corresponds to, or represents
faithfully, what is the case in reality. Well, Kuhn argues, if this
localization  were right, the student who reads the pertinent chapter of
the textbook and understands the law –and even is able to prove it
experimentally in the laboratory– already possesses the essential, which is
the piece of knowledge discovered and formulated by the genius. In this
view, all that this student lacks is facility in applying the law, which he
will gain by solving concrete problems. Suppose, however, Kuhn argues ,that
a student who understands the law is incapable of  applying it , that is,
is incapable of solving any concrete problem to which the law applies. This
student will be flunked as a scientist, of course. But why, if indeed he
possesses the piece of knowledge expressed by the law, and is even able to
demonstrate it experimentally?

      Imagine a parallel case. A student understands a particular theorem
of Euclidean geometry and shows his understanding by demonstrating the
theorem, but is quite incapable of showing his understanding by solving
concrete problems in which the theorem applies. Again, this student will be
flunked as a scientist, and rightly so. But why? Because, Kuhn answers, the
localization of the cognitive content of science in the textbook view of
science is wrong. If this localization were correct, this student would be
a good geometer, for by understanding the theorem he has appropriated the
piece of knowledge, and by demonstrating the theorem on paper he has shown
that he actually understands what the theorem expresses. But then, why is
this student to be flunked, if the law or the theorem can be understood
independently of the application in problem-solving, which supposedly adds
to the  knowledge only facility in applying it–a facility which is not
itself constitutive of the knowledge?

      How, then, does Kuhn localize the cognitive content of science?  The
law sketch, that is the statement in which the law or the theorem is
expressed, answers Kuhn, does not function as a truth that mirrors or
reproduces what is the case in reality –which is the notion of knowledge
implicit in the textbook view of science. Rather, the statement of the law
“functions as a tool. signaling the gestalt in which the situation is to be
seen.”  Unlike a picture or an image or a representation, a tool does not
function as a tool by being looked at, for a tool is not a reproduction of
anything in the world; a tool functions by being actually used in the
execution of the task at hand. It is, then,  by actually applying the law
or the theorem to the task, that is, by actually solving  problems that
arise in the type of situation in which the law applies, that the student,
in the long run, acquires scientific mastery. This mastery consists, not in
contemplating the formulation or statement of the law, but in the habitual
ability to apply the law or, in Kuhn’s expression, to ‘see’ the situation
in the gestalt in which it is to be ’seen’ in order to solve the type of
practical problems in which the law finds application. And that is the
gestalt in which such situation is ‘seen’ by the other members of the group
which inhabit the same scientific paradigm.

      Of course, the ‘seeing’ here involved is not to be taken literally
but metaphorically. It is not a contemplative seeing, but a seeing
connected with action, a practical seeing, a seeing that renders possible
the appropriate action in response to what is seen. To use a comparison, a
good car driver is the person who, thanks to much exercise or experience in
driving, has acquired the habit of ‘seeing’ everything, when driving, in
the appropriate gestalt, that is, in the way appropriate to respond as a
driver to what is seen, depending on the .place hr wants to reach. Thus,
when this experienced or expert driver sees a curve ahead, he or she
instinctively knows the appropriate speed to take it. Similarly, when he or
 she sees an object barring the road, she instinctively knows when to start
braking, and how hard. And so on. This habitual facility to see things,
when driving, in the appropriate way to respond to them is acquired only
through much practice of driving.  The point of the comparison is that,
according to Kuhn, in each scientific field,  too, there is a  gestalt in
which the situation is to be seen by the scientist, that this habitual way
of seeing which results from the problem-solving practice  is what
constitutes a particular paradigm, and that it is acquired or internalized
by the student through the practice of solving problems.

      To explore a little further the difference between the textbook view
of science and Kuhn’s own we may refer ourselves to the not totally
unrelated area of ordinary language, in particular to the case of
perceptual statements.[8] Here, too, two views of the way in which
statements function are possible. According to one view –and this is the
one analogous to the textbook view of science– a statement reproduces or
mirrors or pictures the real state of the world to which it refers. Thus,
for instance, if I tell you, “Your sister is on the phone,” I am supposedly
 transmitting to you verbally a picture of a real state of the world, and
my statement  is meant to be understood by you, the addressee, as a picture
to be contemplated, for that is how pictures are to be responded to . Thus,
to continue with my example, if you ‘get the picture’ that your sister is
on the phone on the other side of the line, you understood what I meant,
for that is the representation of reality that I meant to transmit to you
by making the statement. And, in general, if in the case of any perceptual
statement in English you ‘get the picture’, you are a competent speaker of
English, for you respond to statements as verbal representations of what is
the case. According, however, to a different view of statements –and this
is the one analogous to Kuhn’s view of science–  statements are not verbal
pictures or representations of states of the world. Consequently,
statements are not meant to function as pictures, and if they are
understood as pictures they are simply misunderstood. To return to our
example, suppose that in response to my statement, “Your sister is on the
phone,”  you tell me, “All right,” but continue doing whatever you are
doing, instead of taking the phone or explaining to me why you do not take
the phone or what I am  supposed to tell your sister. If that is, indeed,
your reaction, my suspicion is that you misheard or misunderstood my
statement. So I repeat it. If  your impatient response is, “Yes, yes, I get
it, my sister is on the phone,” but you still continue doing whatever you
are doing, I realize that you do not understand how statements are meant to
function and, consequently, that you did not understand my statement.

      Has the person just described really understood my statement as it is
meant to be understood? Well, if statements are pictures of real states of
the world, and if my interlocutor ‘got’ the picture that her sister is
actually at the other end of the line, then she understood my statement and
 is a competent English speaker. Yet, I and, surely, every other competent
speaker of English will say that if this person doesn’t do anything in
response to what I stated, but simply continues doing what he was doing,
this person doesn’t understand statements, even if he gets in his mind a
very clear picture of her sister at the other end of the line. The reason
is that statements of the kind I am referring to, whether in English or in
any other language, are not meant to function as pictures of reality to be
contemplated, or even as  judgments to be assented to. Rather, they are
meant to function –to employ Kuhn’s expression in reference to the
statements of scientific laws– “as tools” to be used as a guide for action
in response to what is stated. Indeed, the person who in the situation just
described guides his action by what I stated and responds to my statement
about his sister by taking the phone to talk to her is the person who
understands my statement and who knows how statements are meant to
function. And he shows that he understands my statement and how statements
are meant to function, not by saying that he understands and by getting in
her mind the picture of his sister at the other end of the line, or even by
deeming that picture true, but by actually acting appropriately in response
to my statement. In short, understanding statements does not consist in
‘getting’ a picture, but in  acting appropriately in response to what is
stated. And this, too, is the way in which scientific statements are meant
to f unction, according to Kuhn.

      Kuhn maintains that the textbook view of science errs in locating
scientific knowledge in contemplative understanding of the formulation of
the law or theory.. As in the case of  perceptual statements in ordinary,
everyday language, understanding the formulations of scientific laws
consists in acting appropriately in response to what the formulated law
states. In other words, it consists in guiding one’s action by what is
stated in the law or the theorem, not in any contemplative grasp of what
the law states. This is why, contrary to the textbook view of science,
actual application of the laws in problem-solving is not, according to
Kuhn, something in addition to scientific knowledge;; rather it is the way
of acquiring what scientific knowledge consists in, to wit: the habitual
disposition to ‘see’ the region of the world to which the law refers in the
appropriate gestalt to deal with it..

      In rejecting the notion of knowledge as correspondence with the real
Kuhn is fighting against a long and deeply ingrained epistemic tradition in
which we all have been steeped. Indeed, the commonsensical notion, based on
a superficial reflection on of visual perception, that knowledge consists
in the match or exact correspondence between what the knower sees or  knows
and what is the case in reality, received its philosophical consecration
from Plato. But how can one be sure that what one sees actually corresponds
to what is the case? After all, everyone has had the experience of having
been wrong after being convinced of being right. In other words, the notion
of knowledge as correspondence to what is the case, coupled with the
obvious fact that putative knowledge proves some times to be false, that
is, not to be genuine knowledge, created the problem of discerning between
merely apparent and genuine knowledge. To distinguish the two, at least in
name, Plato called the former “opinion” and the latter “knowledge,” and he
tackled the problem of discerning the two in the following way. Only those
realities can be object of “knowledge” which are absolutely unchangeable or
permanent because they exist necessarily and are necessarily as they are.
For if the knower grasps such necessity, then the knower grasps that what
he or she knows necessarily corresponds to what is the case. Now, no
sensible or perceivable object is of that kind, for all of them are
contingent rather than necessary, that is, they are subject to change in
the flux of time. Thus my opinions about them, even assuming that my
perceptions actually corresponded at a certain instant to what is the case,
ceases to be true as they change. Thus Plato felt constrained to relegate
all the particular objects of sensible perception to the realm of opinion
or merely putative knowledge. Furthermore,  he determined that only the
intellect (as opposed to the senses), which was capable of grasping what is
necessarily the case, could yield knowledge (as opposed to opinion), and
only concerning the intelligible (as opposed to the sensible) realities,
which being purely intelligible were not subject to temporal change. The
notion of “scientific knowledge,” that is, of a knowledge whose
unshakeable, objective certainty is superior to the merely subjective
feeling of what is ordinarily taken for knowledge, has its origin in Plato.

      As to the avatars of the notion of knowledge as correspondence of
what is in the knower’s  mind with what is the case in reality, and the
notion of scientific knowledge with what is necessarily true, and the
intense scrutiny and criticism to which such notions haves been subjected
for centuries, the story has been told a hundred times. For my purpose, it
may suffice to say that Kuhn is not alone, by far, in finding such notion
defective. It is fair to say that Kant, who gave up on the notion of
knowledge as correspondence with the real on the basis that, since we have
no independent means of access to the real other than knowledge itself, we
cannot possibly find out whether or not what we take for knowledge actually
corresponds to “the thing itself,” is, in this particular regard, Kuhn’s
direct predecessor. Whether, however, Kant proposed a viable alternative to
the notion of knowledge as correspondence, or simply left us in the abyss
of skepticism, is not my question. Whatever the case may be with Kant,  my
question concerns  Kuhn: Does he, or doesn’t  he furnish us with a viable
alternative to the traditional notion of knowledge as correspondence?

Practical knowledge is not representational: Ortho-doxy and ortho-praxis

      Do animals know? To Plato, for whom genuine knowledge was a deed of
the intellect, and who had an acute sense of the radical difference between
animals and people, such question would have sounded trivial and
uninteresting, if not plain wrongheaded. Not so to Aristotle, who in time
came to distinguish practical knowledge from the theoretical knowledge
which Plato had explored, as well as to see some kind of continuity between
animals and people. The interest of Aristotle’s exploration of the issue
whether animals know is that it seems to open the way to an  alternative to
the traditional notion of knowledge as faithful representation of the real
–the sort of alternative Kuhn was obscurely groping for in order to give
full shape to his understanding of science.

      In my <2002-April-3-Aris-Devereux: Practical Knowledge in Aristotle>
I argued that Aristotle failed to distinguish cleanly practical from
theoretical knowledge. The reason for that failure is that, under Plato’s
influence,  he still could not help looking in practical knowledge for an
element of universality characteristic of Plato’s conception of theoretical
knowledge, though practical knowledge is particular through and through. At
the same time, however, in Aristotle’s view, the distinctive trait of
practical knowledge is not that it involves a universal component but its
connection with action. Let us reflect on this important point in more
detail.

      Unlike Plato, who had confused the two, Aristotle distinguishes
practical from theoretical knowledge. The connection with action is not
found in theoretical knowledge, whose intrinsic purpose is knowledge for
its own sake, the mere grasp of the truth. Thus, animals are incapable of
theoretical knowledge; they pursue, and are capable of attaining, only the
knowledge that is useful to the preservation and well-being of the
individual or of the species to which the animal belongs, while people are
capable of both theoretical and practical knowledge. Now, practical
knowledge is connected with action, but this does not detract from the fact
that it is genuine knowledge. And if it is knowledge, it is
representational grasp of what is the case, for that is what knowledge
consists in. In other words, whether what is known is grasped for the sake
of its utility or for the sake of its truth, what is known is the same. In
which case the difference between theoretical and practical knowledge is
purely extrinsic.

      To take a particular instance, on the basis of their respective past
experience, both lions and people may come to grasp the useful ‘universal’
fact that, say, eating  a plant of such a kind has beneficial purgative
effects. However, there is a difference between animals and people: lions
only know that fact practically, that is, they will eat that plant in
preference to others under the appropriate circumstances, whereas people
know it both practically and also theoretically –that is, predicatively,
objectively, as a truth concerning that type of plant, and can move from
there to the reason propter quid, that is, why such a plant to the
difference of others produces purgative effects.


      Such seems to be Aristotle’s conception of practical and theoretical
knowledge in the particular version to which we are presently referring.
Indeed, he emphasizes that practical knowledge is knowledge of the
universal, that is, of the general fact that a plant of such a kind
possesses purgative virtues. In other words, both people and animals (in
this case, lions) reach  what the scholastics called ‘knowledge quia, or
knowledge of the plant as cause of purgative effects. The difference
between them is that, unlike the animal,  the human is capable of reaching
knowledge propter quid, that is explanatory knowledge, knowledge of the
nature of the plant thanks to which the plant produces such purgative
effect.

      There is, however, another possible version of practical knowledge.
Think, in the first place, of the lion’s case. The lion can attain the
knowledge of the purgative effects of the herb only through experience of
undergoing itself those beneficial effects of eating the plant. This is no
purely notional knowledge, such as the knowledge a human who has no
previous experience of purgation may acquire by being told that the plant
produces purgative effects. This learning is experiential in that it is
gained through experience. It is a matter not only of knowing about
effects, but of effects felt by the agent as beneficial as a result of the
agent’s involvement in action. Because the purgative effects are
experienced as favorable by the agent who experiences them, the action of
eating the plant will automatically tend to be repeated under the
appropriate circumstances in the expectation of similar effects. Actions
followed by rewarding effects tend t1o be repeated; actions followed by
unpleasant effects tend to be avoided. And from objects that look similar
similar effects are expected. These are inherited mechanisms of living,
sensitive, desirous bodies. And these mechanisms are at the origin of all
further purposive action initiated by such bodies, as well as of all future
learning about the world as our abode.

      Suppose that the animal eats the plant and nothing happens to the
animal, that is, no beneficial or ill effects are undergone and felt by the
animal.     In that case, there will be no connection between the plant and
the animal’s future action. (The case is similar to that of lepers who, in
order not to get burnt, have to observe the cigarette they are holding in
their hands, because their hands are insensitive). Hence no practical
knowledge will be acquired, that is, no adaptive change in response to a
kind of objects, no eating of that kind of plant in preference to other
plants.

      It should be noticed that the effects can be experienced as
beneficial or malefic by the animal only if they are undergone by the
animal itself. (People, thanks to language, may fear something whose
effects they have never experienced, by analogy to the ill-effects produced
by other objects of which they have experience).  Again, it is a matter of
experiential knowledge, of knowledge gained from first-hand experience, so
to say. But what, precisely, is the nature of this knowledge? Does the
animal possess a kind of mental representation of the plant’s nature? No.
The animal doesn’t predicatively or objectively know-that all plants of
that kind are purgative. The animal just has learned to desire and look for
a similar plant, and eat it, when purgation happens to be needed.  There is
a sensory-motor memory of the plant as desirable to be eaten for purgation.
Whenever purgation is needed in the future, the animal will automatically
look for that kind of plant. This is purely practical recognition of the
plant for purposes of action, guided by acquired desire or inclination as a
result of the experience of beneficial effects. The animal has no
theoretical or predicative idea-that this is a kind of plant that uniformly
produces purgative effects in such a kind of animals. (The idea-that
presupposes language and is the result of reflection; so it is exclusively
found in people. Furthermore, even in the case of  people, it is not
specular or speculative but practical; a map for action, depending on the
agent’s project).

      Now, though the animal’s recognition of the plant as purgative is
purely practical and certainly not verbalized by the animal, Aristotle saw
it as somewhat akin to the theoretical or contemplative grasp of the
‘nature’ of things to which Plato had attributed representational value. In
other words, the gravitational pull of Aristotle’s Platonic heritage
weighed heavily on his view of practical knowledge. He doesn’t seem to ever
have suspected the viability of the inverse alternative, to wit: that
practical knowledge, which is not representational or predicative but
directly results in action, not even in inchoative theoretical predication,
because it is experience of object for response in terms of its effects –be
they undergone or observed--, is in fact the PRINCEPS ANALOGATUM in
epistemic matters.

      Ultimately, this reversal has the startling consequence not only that
theoretical knowledge has no primacy over practical knowledge, as the
Greeks assumed, but that there is not such a thing as purely theoretical,
representational knowledge –not even in the area of science. Or, to be more
precise, that what sounds as objective predication is in fact the result of
action and meant to function in the agent applicatively rather than
predicatively.
      Aristotle, then, ‘platonized’ badly concerning practical knowledge.
Yet his own observation that those who do not know (because they do not
possess any theoretical knowledge of the universal) but who possess
experience of those meats that produce wholesome effects, (meats which
because of that they call  light, that is, meats that produce wholesome
effects, by contrast to other meats) are capable of curing, whereas those
who know (because they posses theoretical knowledge of t he universal) but
who possess no experience of light meats that produce wholesome effects are
incapable of curing should have alerted Aristotle to the fact that the
theoretical component of practical knowledge (to wit, that this plant cures
through purgation, or that light meats are wholesome) is superfluous
because it has no truth-value or representational value at all,  in that
nothing in reality corresponds to the universal the theoretician has in
mind. Indeed, ‘light meats’ is a name that has no other referent than the
objects from whom the experts expect wholesome effects.  Such realization,
however, would have been impossible to someone like Aristotle who had been
steeped in, and wholeheartedly embraced as self-evident and indisputable,
the conception of knowledge as adaequation to reality, as representational
of reality. Of that epistemic myth Aristotle never quite managed to get
rid. And it is precisely of that conception of knowledge that Kuhn is
struggling to liberate both himself and us in the area of the experimental
sciences.

      I said that nothing corresponds to the universal which the
theoretician has in mind. In other words, the judgment, expressed in the
statement, that all light meats are wholesome, neither possesses nor is
meant to possess any truth value in the sense of resemblance or pictorial
homophormism to what is the case. Such judgment is not objective, in that
it grasps no object; no reality-fact that corresponds to it. In other
words, ‘light meats’ are those meats which the ‘experts’ or experienced
people recognize as light meats in terms of their expectation and desire of
wholesome, that is, healthy, beneficial effects. And this recognition
cannot be formalized in a theorem. It is not only that the Platonic eternal
Ideas required to save the objectivity of universal necessary judgments do
not exist, as Aristotle rightly argued, but neither do exist the necessary
unchangeable “natures” that Aristotle inferred from the perceptual
observation of certain constant natural phenomena,  with which he replaced
the Platonic Ideas and attempted to preserve the Platonic notion of
scientific knowledge, of episteme, of necessarily true knowledge, that has
been perpetuated in the modern notion of the superior certainty of the
sciences guaranteed by experiment. In short, I am saying that the Humean
criticism of the validity of scientific induction is legitimate, that the
constancy of nature in certain areas is merely contingent, that our
inductions about the world rest on the automatic, non-rational expectation
(because not grounded in the grasp of any necessity) that the ‘same’
‘effects’ will be produced in the future by the ’same’ ‘causes.’
