\wp\monas\kantkurt                                 April 24, 95

      "Most philosophers--particularly those  with  idealistic  tendencies--
      don't talk much about being struck  over  the  head  with  a  baseball
      bat... For it is this kind of abrupt  interaction  with  the  external
      world  that  perhaps  best  brings  out  the  motivation  for  various
      doctrines of the 'given'... (K. Mosser, "Stoff and Nonsense in  Kant's
      First Critique," History of Philosophy Quarterly, vol. 10, n. 1,  Jan,
      1993, p. 21).

      "Nature also teaches me by these feelings  of  pain,  hunger,  thirst,
      etc., that I am not in my body, like a pilot in his ship, but besides,
      that I am joined to it very  closely  and  indeed  so  compounded  and
      intermingled with my body, that I form, as it  were,  a  single  whole
      with it. For, if this were not so, when my body is hurt, I  would  not
      on that account feel pain, I who am  only  a  thinking  thing,  but  I
      should perceive the wound by my understanding alone, just as  a  pilot
      sees with his eyes if any damage occurs to his ship, and when my  body
      needs to drink or eat, I would know this simply without  being  warned
      of it by the confused feelings of hunger and thirst. For in truth  all
      these feelings of hunger, thirst, pain, etc., are  nothing  other  tha
      certain confused ways of thinking, which arise from and depend on  the
      union and, as it  were,  the  mingling  of  the  mind  and  the  body"
      (Descartes, Sixth Meditation, translation F. E. Sutcliffe, p. 159).


      "The intentionality of  feeling  is  in  one  of  its  expressions  an
      intentionality of self-reference, functioning as a lumen naturale with
      respect to the experiencer. The intentional  vectors  of  anxiety  and
      hope reveal the sense of anxious and  hopeful  situations  in  such  a
      manner that the experiencer becomes an  issue  and  possible  thematic
      topic.  The  intentionality  of   feeling   discloses   the   sentient
      experiencer... with the situation and within the horizon-forms of time
      and space. ... The intentional vectors of feeling radiate into a world
      and  concomitantly  reveal  an   embodied,   speaking   and   communal
      experiencer who lives in  an  oriented  time  and  space"  (Calvin  O.
      Schrag, Experience  and  Being.  Prolegomena  to  a  Future  Ontology,
      Evanston: Northwestern University Press, 1969, pp. 98-99).

      "...feelings are the sensors for the match  or  lack  thereof  between
      nature and circumstance. And by nature  I  mean  both  the  nature  we
      inherited as a pack of genetically  engineered  adaptations,  and  the
      nature  we  have   acquired   in   individual   development,   through
      interactions with our social environment, mindfully and willfully,  as
      well as not. Feelings, along with emotions they come from, are  not  a
      luxury.. They serve as internal guides, and they help  us  communicate
      to others signals that can also guide them... Contrary to  traditional
      scientific opinion, feelings are just as cognitive as other  percepts.
      They are the result of a most curious physiological  arrangement  that
      has turned the brain into the body's captive audience... The body,  as
      represented in the brain, may constitute the  indispensable  frame  of
      reference for the neural processes that we experience as the mind; our
      most refined thoughts and best actions, our greatest joys and  deepest
      sorrows, use the body as a yardstick" (Antonio R. Damasio,  Descartes'
      Error.  Emotion,   Reason,   and   the   Humn   Brain,   New   Yorkk,:
      Grodsset/Putnam,  1994,  p.  15.  Cited  by  Andrew  Tallon,   "Triune
      Consciousness  and  Some  Recent  Studies  of  Affectivity,"  in   ACP
      Quarterly, vol. LXX, Spring 1996, Issue # 2, p. 252).

      "Without these feelings our knowing and desiring would be paper  thin"
      (B. Lonergan,  Method in Theology (New York, Herder 1972, pp.  30-31).



Underlying questions

      Does Kant think that my "given" in being hit over the head is  of  the
same  kind  as  (that  is,  functionally  equivalent  to,  as  far  as   the
constitution of knowledge of object is  concerned)  the  perceptual  "given"
someone else has who sees me being struck?
      Does Kant think that I who am hit have,  or  could  conceivably  have,
two "givens": the perceptual "given" corresponding to  the  bat  beating  my
body (which is of the same kind as that of an observer  who  sees  me  being
struck over the head), and the internal or immediate experience of pain?  If
so, do I constitute two objects: (1) Perceptual experience of  bat  touching
a skull placed where I am situated, and (2) pain, from  which  I  constitute
the knowledge that the skull struck is mine?
       Is  this  latter  experience  informative  about  object,  perceptual
experience? [It does not seem so. For this experience is not  only  mine  in
the sense that I am the subject who knows, who is informed by the  knowledge
acquired, who acquires knowledge but which is not the object known; here  my
pain is not known but felt, and it is felt as mine in the sense of I am  it,
I am in pain.] Is "being struck over the head  with  a  baseball  bat,"  for
Kant,  what  ordinarily  we  would  call  "a  perception,"   "a   perceptual
experience"? If not, what kind of experience is it, for Kant, and  how  does
it differ from a perceptual experience?
Perceptual and affective experience in Kant's Critiques
      I ask these questions because I think that there is really no room  in
the First Critique for the kind of "given" involved  in  the  experience  of
being struck over the head (if one may  speak  of  a  "given"  that  is  not
involved in the constitution of object but of  subject),  for  the  sort  of
special passivity involved in my being struck over the head with a  baseball
bat, which in my opinion is not a perceptual experience like seeing  someone
being struck over the head with a baseball bat.
      At least in the first Critique, though arguably also in the second  at
least by  default,  there  is  room  only  for  perceptual  experience.  But
perceptual  experience  is  experience  of  object,  that   is,   experience
informative about object, experience yielding knowledge of object. A  person
seeing me beaten over the head would have the  kind  of  "given"  that  fits
with the first Critique, a given that when categorized results in  knowledge
of object: the  knowledge  by  an  observer  that  Monasterio-object-in-the-
causal-order is being struck over the head with a bat. What  interests  Kant
is the knowing or epistemic experience, which involves  its  own  passivity,
its own kind of "given," the perceptual given. But that is not the  kind  of
passivity involved in being struck with a bat over  the  head,  nor  is  the
"given" of the same type.
       Let  me  elaborate.  The  first  Critique  is  about  the   objective
constitution of reality on the basis of  a  "given"  of  which  the  subject
(that is, a knower, a subject exclusively capable of  knowing--for  that  is
the way Kant conceives of  the  subject  in  the  first  Critique--  is  the
recipient.* [* Actually, it is not even this, for the  Critique  deals  with
object of possible knwoledge,  with  the  objective  knowledge  of  the  non
exposed, 'possible,' merely ideal, transcendental physicist].  Therefore,  I
claim, in the first Critique there  is  no  room  for  the  exposed  agent's
experience (of) bodied-exposed-agent-self, no room for  my-body-for-me,  for
the-body-subject exposed to the world and to the Other, for the body-I-am.
A contrast with Descartes
      What do I mean by the-body-I-am, and how is this  connected  with  the
special passivity involved in my being struck with a bat?
      I can begin to explain myself concerning the-body-I-am by means  of  a
contrast with Descartes. For Descartes, I am a mind that has a body, a  mind
to which a body is attached. Granted, Descartes claims  that  this  body  is
substantially attached to, or connected with, the  mind.  This  is  what  he
means by saying that I am not in my body as the pilot is in  his  ship.  But
the connection, according to Descartes, is of  the  order  of  knowledge:  I
KNOW this body which is mine in a way in which the pilot does not  KNOW  his
ship, in  a way I do not KNOW any other body, and in a  way  no  other  mind
KNOWS this body. For to  come  to  know  other  bodies  I  need--in  Kantian
language--perceptual "givens" (as the pilot needs to see or touch  his  ship
in order to come to know that there is a whole in its  underbelly),  whereas
I do not need to see or touch my head in order to come to know--that is,  be
informed--that I have been struck over the head. Thus,  for  Descartes,  the
difference between my body and another body is the way  in  which  they  are
known by me, the way in which they are accessible to the knowing mind I  am:
the  former  through  perceptual  experience  alone,  the   latter   through
perceptual experience as well as through internal feeling [of being it?).*
* The question remains, however, whether Descarts thinks that  my  sensation
of being hit by a bat informs me about  my  body  being  hit,  much  as  the
perception of my body being hit informs another about my body being hit.  If
such is  the  case--and  that  is  my  contention  concerning  Kant's  first
Critique--Descartes has no room for my body-for-me as affective body.

      Well, pace Descartes, whether  or  not  I  am  a  mind  at  all,  most
certainly I am not a mind that has a body, that  is,  a  mind  that  through
internal feeling is informed of having a body substantially attached to  it.
Just as Descartes would not say "I am a mind that has a  mind,"  but  rather
"I am a mind," for I  am  aware  of  being  the  one  who  has  "ideas,"  so
Descartes should say "I am a body, this body," because I  am  this  body  in
the sense Descartes says I am a mind, this mind.  Indeeed,  it  is  not  the
case, as Descartes suggests, that of this body which I have, apart from  the
perceptual 'external' experience others and I have, I  in  addition  have  a
sort of internal or immediate experience that informs me  that  my  body  is
touched or hurt even when I do not perceive what touches or hurts me.  I  am
the body touched or hurt as I am the mind that perceives.
      For external or perceptual  knowledge  of  my  body  as  conceived  by
Descartes there is indeed room, at least conceivably though  not  explicitly
(for it deals with transcendental conditions of possibility), in  the  first
Critique. Thus, if my pain is taken as a peculiar perceptual  "given,"  then
there is room for it in the First Critique. In this  case  my  pain  on  the
skull would be a private "given" to which only I  have  access  and  on  the
basis of which I can come to know that I have a body hit by a bat  over  the
skull. In other words, in this interpretation, my pain, which I am the  only
one to feel, is an informative "given" or datum from which I can  constitute
as object my body struck on  the  skull.  In  this  way,  through  different
"givens," though ultimately "givens" of the same kind as  far  as  knowledge
is concerned,  my body as part of the world, that is, as part of  the  order
of causality studied by physics, can be constituted both by  an  (objective,
purely curious, unexposed) perceiver and by myself thanks to  an  'internal'
or 'immediate' "given."  THIS PRESUPPOSES, HOWEVER, THAT I KNOW MY  BODY  AS
IT ISKNOWN BY AN OBSERVER, AS AN OBJECT FOR A KNOWER WHO I  AM  NOT  BUT  IN
WHOSE SHOES I PLACE MYSELF. [NOTICE THAT THIS IS, ACCORDING TO  SARTRE,  THE
WAY IN WHICH LANGUAGE TEACHES ME TO SPEAK OF MY BODY. ACTUALLY, HOWEVER,  MY
BODY FOR ME IS NOT, AND CANNOT BE REDUCED TO, THE BODY  SPOKEN  OF;  IT  IS,
RATHER, AT LEAST PRIMARILY THOUGH  NOT  EXCLUSIVELY,  NOT  THE  BODY  SPOKEN
ABOUT BUT THE BODY WHO SPEAKE AND ACTS ON THE BASIS OF HIS HAVING BEEN  HIT,
OR OF HIS FEAR OF BEING HIT BY A BAT].  IN  OTHER  WORDS,  THIS  PRESUPPOSES
THAT I STAND TO MY BODY AS TO AN  OBSERVED,  THOUGH  I  HAVE  AN  ADDITIONAL
SPECIAL GIVEN CONCERNING MY  BODY  THAN  IN  THE  CASE  OF  OTHER  BODIES  I
OBSERVE.  BUT THIS NOT THE BODY I AM, FOR THE BODY I AM IS  EXPOSED  TO  THE
SURROUNDING  WORLD  AND  IS  SPONTANEOUSLY  'CAREFUL'  OR  'CIRCUMSPECT'  IN
HEIDEGGER'S SENSE. [THE PROBLEM OF UNITING MY BODY  FOR  ME,  THAT  IS,  THE
PARTICIPANT'S BODY THAT I AM, TO MY BODY FOR THE OTHER, MY BODY AS  PART  OF
THE WORLD AND INVOLVED IN THE ORDER OF CAUSALITY, IS THE SAME PROBLEM  DEALT
WITH BY RICOEUR CONCENRNING COSMOLOGICAL AND PERSONAL TIME AND  TEMPORALITY.
BUT THE PROBLEM ARISES ONLY WHEN WE OBJECTIFY MY BODY TEMPORAL AND  MAKE  IT
MERE SUBJECT OF PREDICATION, WHEREAS MY BODY TEMPORAL IS NOT A  BODY  I  USE
BUT THE BODY I AM].
      The world contemplated or understood (not  the  surrounding  world  to
which I am exposed and which is exposed to my action, the existential world-
-not the world talked about--which is the correlate of my body for  me)  and
the order of causality are the only world and the only order with which  the
first Critique deals and is fatally designed to deal, for feelings are  left
out as non-cognitive-ergo-non-intentional. There is no  room  in  the  first
Critique for any other kind of reality and any other kind  of  order.  There
is, however, the body-subject-object and the world inhabited by it. But  for
the world inhabited by this body-subject-object as well  as  for  the  body-
subject-object there is no room in the first  Critique.  For  the  body  who
invents its own projects (on the  original  basis  of  psycho-reflexes)  and
sometimes succeeds in realizing them, there is no room. AND THIS BODY  WOULD
NEVER INVENT PROJECTS AND COULD NEVER REALIZE THEM IF IT  AND  THE  ONES  IT
LOVES OR HATES WERE NOT EXPOSED AS OBJECTS-SUBJECTS TO OTHER BODIES,  SO  AS
TO BE ABLE TO HAVE AVERSIONS AND DESIRES WHICH ARE THE BASIS  FOR  PROJECTS,
AND IF THE SURROUNDING WORLD WERE NOT EXPOSED TO THEM.
      Neither is there room for  this  body  in  the  second  Critique,  for
Kant's free subject is not a body at all (for bodies can  be  inserted  only
in the causal order), much less--what Kant would  find  no  less  scandalous
than Descartes--a body-subject (if  anything,  the  subject  in  the  second
Critique is rather a special sort of Cartesian  mind),  since  there  is  no
possible connection  between  the  order  of  causality  and  the  order  of
freedom, and since the only body Kant acknowledges or makes  room  for,  the
body-thing-object-of-knowledge cannot act intentionally.
Back to Kant and the original hypothesis
       But let me return to the beginning, for perhaps I  have  not  as  yet
positively shown that the passivity of the "given" for which there  is  room
in Kant is not the perceptual, objective passivity. Indeed the former  is  a
"given" to be decoded, as it were, a passivity  taken  as  a  indication  of
object, as  potentially  informative  of  object  in  conjunction  with  the
constituting subject-knower. In this perceptual passivity, that is,  in  the
given that corresponds to this passivity, what is significant and  important
is not the subject (which is itself not known, for it  is  not  object)  but
the object indicated in or by the "given." Even my  pain,  in  being  struck
with a baseball bat, can count for Kant only as  a  basis  for  constituting
the knowledge of a body struck with a baseball bat (a body that  happens  to
be mine or that I constitute as mine in the same way as I  constitute  other
bodies as not mine.    It happens, however, that my experience in being  hit
on the skull with a bat is of a different kind. It  is  not  experience  of,
but experiencer-experiencing, as  it  were,  distanceless  experiencer  from
whom all distances will unfold. My "given," if  it  can  be  so  called,  is
pain, that is, pain-felt for there is no other, distanceless pain (as  shown
by the case  of  the  phantom  limb).  The  perceiver's  "given,"  what  the
perceiver passively receives, is not pain and is  unlike  my  pain.  For  my
pain is not--at least not  primarily,  though  perhaps  secondarily  through
shared language--an indicator or code of my skull being struck  with  a  bat
as the perceptual given of my skull being struck is  supposedly  a  sign  or
code of my skull being struck with a bat. Pain--like  all  affection--is  on
the subject's side, like my being touched--when felt, not  when  perceived--
not on the object's, as the perceptual "given" is.  Pain--my painful  bodied
self--may or may not be a brute effect  of  being  struck,  but  it  obtains
whether it is something to be decoded or not as struck by a bat, whether  or
not I decode it causally and on the basis of past experience. And I  do  not
know my pain (that is, my  pain  is  no  object  known,  about  which  I  am
informed). I know  your  pain  through  your  (expressive,  not  indicative)
gesture of pain, but I do  not  feel  your  pain.  As  far  as  my  pain  is
concerned, I am it, I am in pain. The  object  of  which  Kant  speaks,  the
object known, is for the subject, but it is other than the subject  and,  so
to say, on the object's side, on the objective pole. Not so my  pain.  I  am
in pain, and I am not the known, I am the one  who  knows  objects,  objects
which do not become myself by being known. The fact that I am pain does  not
imply that there is no constitution  of  object  and  even  constitution  of
grammatical subject (which obtains, for instance, in my  possible  statement
that I am in pain). But the actually constituted object is my body seen,  my
body known to be in pain,  not  the  constituting  subject  who  experiences
itself as subject exposed and who in his/her  fear  and  "care"  constitutes
objects to be  averted  and  avoided  (therefore,  non-objects  of  pratical
knowledge). For this object and for this surrounding world there is no  room
in Kant.   The  constituting  subject  is  primarily  subject  of  exposure,
vulnerable subject, subject who cannot but be agent-patient who projects  to
avoid pain, who attempts to 'transcend' itself  practically  toward  a  non-
suffering state by 'transcending' the object of exposure and bringing  about
a different state of the world, if possible, or, if impossible, at least  by
choosing its own attitude in response.
      I speak of myself as of somebody else: "I have a headache," "He has  a
headache." But the body  I  am  is  the  speaking  body.  If  someone  says,
speaking of me, "He has a headache," he confronts me with a "he"  object  of
assent, not with the one I am.
