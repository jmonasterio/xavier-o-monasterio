On MacIntyre, Rationality, and Dramatic Space
by Xavier 0. Monasterio

      The central issue in this paper is rationality, that is, the set of
implicit or
explicit rules that are taken as normative concerning our knowledge of, and
dealings with, the world around us. Whether we realize it or not, however,
any such set implies as its correlate a particular conception of the
relation-
ship in which the subject stands to the world. Though what I want to do
here can hardly make sense if it is presented in isolation from the
analysis by
which it will be supported, it can be put abstractly as an attempt to
substitute the conception of our relationship to the world implied by the
currently prevailing notion of rationality for another conception that
seems
to me to be the appropriate one.

      To enlighten the reader a bit more concerning the scope of this
paper, I
will mention two distinguished books in the current philosophical
literature.
Both of them have a bearing on the central issue, and with both of them,
though in significantly different ways, I find myself in deep sympathy. The
first is Rorty's devastating~attack on the sort of philosophy that results
from
taking for granted that the knower is an ethereal mind whose function con-
sists in mirroring the world. s While I find Rorty's own stance very
confusing
and of little value, I am in total agreement with him as to what he
rejects.
The second book is MacIntyre's After virtue.2 For reasons that this paper
will attempt to make clear and persuasive, MacIntyre's work seems to me to
constitute an important, positive step in the right direction.

Phenomenal presence and existential presence

      The notion of presence is absolutely central in Husserl's works. His
motto
of going "back to the things themselves" expresses his decision to return
to
what is actually present, to the things in their own evidential givenness.
The
reason why, as a philosopher, Husserl despises the natural attitude is
because it goes beyond what is actually present. In that attitude we are
com-
mitted to the existence of what is present, naturally, that is,
spontaneously,
but blindly, that is, without presential support. Hence the need for the
epoche', which neutralizes this blind commitment while leaving intact what
is given in presence.

      Whoever is familiar with the Husserl of Ideen remembers his
fastidious
and ferociously tedious analyses of what is actually present in the
perceptual
mode. Husserl holds a sheet of paper in his hands. But does he really per-
ceive a sheet of paper or, rather, just a white surface? And does he really
perceive a quadrangular surface or, rather, a surface of a different shape?
It
is worthy of attention that in all these analyses Husserl is infinitely
attentive
to what is present to him but has not one word to say of his own presence
to
what is present to him. In this, it seems to me, he follows a long phil-
osophical tradition, inaugurated by Plato and footnoted, in more recent
timevlby Descartes, Hume, and Kant-to name only a few of its most dis-
tingulshed members. In any case, as far as Husserl is concerned, I want to
claim that he works within a philosophical paradigm characterized by a dis-
torted notion of presence-a unidirectional presence that has nothing to do
with the presence we live in the perceptual encounter.

      Let us start with Husserl's notion of presence. That which is
intentionally
present to a subject is, in Husserl's Kantian parlance, a phenomenon. Now,
this phenomenal presence is, by definition, unidirectional, exclusively
oriented toward the subject. It is presence of object to subject, of what
ap-
pears to whom it appears. There is no presence of subject to object, only
the
other way around, so much so that "subject" and "object" or "phe-
nomenon" are correlates reciprocally defined by this unidirectional re-
lationship. Such, according to Husserl, is the actual mode of presence in-
volved in our lived experience. But is he right?

      In ordinary language, the words "spectator," "witness," and
"participant"
are not synonyms. What distinguishes them is that they name three different
modalities of presence. The difference among them, however, cannot be
analyzed in terms of the Husserlian notion of presence-mere presence of
object to subject-for part at least of what distinguishes the spectator,
the

witness, and the participant lies, not in what is present to them, but in
their
own presence to what is present to them.

      Consider, first, the spectator's case. I am watching a performance of
[famZet. The prince is telling the sweet Ophelia to get herself into a
nunnery.
1, the spectator, feel like telling Hamlet that he is abusing the poor
child,
that Ophelia deserves better than his brutal tirade on the bestiality of

if I shouted them, as children do sometimes in the movies to forewarn their
hero that the bad guy is about to attack him from the back. If I shouted,
the
actor would hear me, but Hamlet would not. For what is happening before
my eyes does not happen in the geographical location of the stage, but in
an
imaginary space to which I have access only as a spectator. The world in
which Hamlet and Ophelia live is, in relation to me, transcendent. I cannot
enter it, I do not belong within the space in which the action takes place.
Nothing can happen to me, the spectator, within the space of the spectacle,
but neither can I interfere with what happens within that space. Hamlet can
insult Ophelia, but he cannot insult me. He can be murdered by his uncle,
but not by me. This is to say that the spectator is defined by his own mode
of presence to the spectacle. And, his mode of presence is, by comparison
to
the forms of presence which we will now analyze, a sort of absence, in that
he is out of the reach of what happens, and what happens is equally out of
his reach.

      Obviously, our ordinary relationship to what we perceive is not the
spec-
tator's. Our world is not just in front of us, like the spectacle, but
around
us. Neither is what we perceive transcendent in respect of us, nor are we
transcendent in respect of it. What happens within the perceptual space in
whose midst we find ourselves may-and sometimes does-happen to us,
depending on whether we are functioning as witnesses or as participants.
Sometimes, indeed, we function merely as witnesses. While strolling in the
street, for instance, I may witness an accident. A cyclist is hit by a car.
I my-
self am not involved in the event in any way. What happens in this case
within my perceptual space does not happen to me. I am neither the cyclist
nor the driver of the car. What happens happens to them. I am only a
witness of what happens.

      At first sight, the relationship between the witness and what he
witnesses
seems to be the same as that between the spectator and the spectacle. In
fact, it is not. For the distinction between the world in which the
spectator
lives and the world in which Hamlet lives is radical and the distance
between
them unbridgeable. The spectator who would jump on the stage to stop
Hamlet in the middle of his tirade to Ophelia would not only make a fool of
himself, he would be a fool, for there is no Hamlet within his reach. The
witness, on the other hand, is himself within the space in which what he

for what it is, namely, a move by which the subject~X}a philosopher

himself thereby to an utterly transcendental no man's land. Unsurprisingly,

modification of the world itself. Instead of finding himself in the active
in-
terchange in which we live with what surrounds us and within whose reach
we are in the perceptual encounter, the philosopher finds himself, as
philosopher, confronted with an array of objects whose only relationship to
him is that of a purely intentional presence. What Husserl calls "the
phenomenal" and explores tenaciously in his writings is nothing else than
the intentional correlate of a subject who is beyond the reach of what is
present to him, that is, of a transcendental subject to whom nothing can
happen and who, like Odysseus before the Syrens, cannot hear the call to
shape what happens. Needless to say, the total absence of the Husserlian
philosopher from the world to be explored differs abysmally from our exis-
tential presence in the world in which we act.

Existen f ial presence and dramatic space

      Perceiving, then, is not, as Husserl assumes, being confronted by
phenomena. It is experiencing that which is within our reach and within
whose reach we ourselves are. It has the doublesidedness of an encounter,
not the safe onesidedness of a pure observation by a transcendental
subject.
Now, the fact that the perceiving subject belongs himself within the space
of
the perceived, within its reach, as exposed to the perceived as the
perceived
is to him, makes our perceptual space into an essentially dramatic one. I
turn now to the exploration of this fundamental characteristic of the lived
world.

      Once the world is phenomenalized through the epoche, there is no
place
left for the qualitative differencXs of experience which we express through
the names "spectator," "witness," and "participant." But, furthermore, as
the subject's existential relationship to the object is neutralized, a
conse-
quent transformation of his perceptual field takes place, by which the ob-
jects themselves lose their existential significance. An apple falling from
a
tree, Hamlet talking to Ophelia on the stage, a piece of paper, someone
pointing his gun at me-all these become, or should become, qualitatively
similar experiences, since in all these cases the object is supposedly
characterized by the same mode of presence to the subject. I wish to God
that the man pointing his gun at me had exactly the same mode of presence
as Hamlet talking to Ophelia before my eyes and were as unthreatening as a
piece of paper on my desk! That may seem so to an epocheic observer, who
by the same token must find my different conducts in the theater, in my of-
fice, and in the street utterly unintelligible. Something is amiss in a
bracketed world. What is it? The dramatic charge by which the objects we
encounter acquire their proper existential significance, and without which
they become as dead and unintelligible as certain relics of an ancient
culture
in a museum for the layman. For the space in which we live, precisely be-
cause we live within it, is dramatic through and through. As the Euclidean
space is such that only a certain type of geometric figures can find a
place in
it, so the only figures that can be inscribed in the space of the world in
which
we live are dramatic figures. I find this idea particularly difficult to
convey,
but perhaps it can be brought gradually into focus, starting by allusion
and
trusting the reader's own familiarity with our world.

      Let me then, begin with a concrete, though imaginary, instance.
Suppose
that Husserl is conducting his phenomenological investigations in his own
garden. The world before him is in parentheses. All of a sudden he realizes
that his house has taken fire, where his wife (whether phenomenal or not,
the only one he has) is asleep. If Husserl insists on keeping the world in
parentheses instead of giving in to the furious but despicable clamor of
the
natural attitude, he might as well continue his exploration of the lived
world
based on the case at hand. After all, what else is there to do about a
phenomenon but to explore it phenomenologically? Now, it would be un-
fair to Husserl to say that he would do that, for he never claimed that any-

one, himself included, could remain constantly in the unnatural at-
titude-still less than anyone should, for there is, and there can be, no
place
for "shoulds" and "should nots" in the Husserlian paradigm. All I want to
say is that, under these circumstances, Husserl's experience would undergo
a total transformation. From being a-dramatic it would become dramatic.
The objects around Husserl would gain the existential significance
characteristic of that which affects us and demands not only our curious at-

tention but also our practical response. In other words, Husserl would have

been recalled from his transcendental limbo and would find himself back,
willy-nilly, in the world in which we never cease living, whose space is
characteristically dramatic.

      We might get a better grip on this elusive notion by means of a
contrast
between two literary forms, namely, description and narration. For it seems
to me that the structural difference between them is precisely a difference
between a dramatic and an a-dramatic space.

      One cannot reduce the difference between a description and a
narration
to a difference in the author's intentions. If that were the case, one
would be
able to describe or narrate at will. Well, I challenge the reader to
narrate a
door. On the other hand, he won't have any trouble describing a door. Ob-
viously, then, the respective structures of description and narration are
not
totally independent of the identity of their respective objects.

      One may, then, feel tempted to say that the difference is that the
object of
description is something static, while that of narration is something dy-
namic. This comes closer to the point, for the object of a narration is
always
dynamic indeed, namely, an event. However, this distinction will be insuf-
ficient unless we specify further the particular sort of events which are
nar-
rable, for not all events are. Take, for instance, the process of division
of a
cell, or the eruption of Mount St. Helen. Those are events. Yet, they can
be
described. The reason is-and here we arrive at the core in our search-that
the object of narration has to be a dramatic event, while the object of de-
scription, be it static (as a table) or dynamic (as the eruption of a
volcano) is
a-dramatic.

      But what makes an event dramatic? Let us see. Indisputably, the novel
belongs in the narrative genre. Why is a novel narrative rather than just
descriptive? To answer this question it will be useful to realize that one
can-
not possibly write a novel in which the characters are things. If you don't
believe this, try, for instance, to write a novel about typewriters, book-
shelves, and desks-assuming, of course, that you do not 'personalize'
them. It is not just that it would be a very dull novel, whatever the
number
of events you may introduce. It simply won't be a novel at all. It will be
a
mere series of descriptions and a terribly dull book. And it will be
terribly
dull and not a novel at all because it lacks something essential to the
novel,
namely, a dramatic space.

      How is the dramatic space of the novel opened? As I observed above,
the
eruption of Mount St. Helen can be described. The reason is that, by
itself,
that event is a-dramatic. However it becomes dramatic, and unavoidably
so, as soon as the eruption happens to someone. Indeed, you have only to
introduce into your description of the eruption someone who finds himself
threatened by the event, and, whether you like it or not, your description
ceases being a description. It becomes dramatic and, by the same token, it

becomes a narration. Thus, what distinguishes a narration from a descrip-
tion is that the former is dramatic, while the latter is a-dramatic. And a
nar-
ration is dramatic in virtue of the identity of its object, which is not
just an
event or a series of events, but an event or a series of events lived by
some-
one who is affected by them. Since things are not affected by the events of
which they are part, since what happens does not happen to them in the
sense in which what happens happens to us-participants who are affected
by what happens-it is impossible to write a novel about things. To do that,
one would have to personalize those things, that is, to make them capable
of
living dramatically-and then they would not be things anymore. Thus the
novelist does not need to decide to open a dramatic space, which is the
space
characteristic of the novel. The space of the novel is irremediably
dramatic
because it is populated by beings who are affected by the events in which
they participate and who grasp the significance of those events. And if
novels as well as plays convey automatically, in spite of their imaginary
character, a flavor of reality to the reader or the spectator, it is
because they
preserve the dramatic structure of the space in which the reader or the
spec-
tator actually live. The depth of our human space is not the neutral,
aseptic
depth of Newtonian space. It is the depth of a drama-which does not mean
that the drama is necessarily or constantly a tragedy. The day Robinson
Crusoe saw the trace of a human foot on the shores of his island his whole
life was joyfully redefined because his dramatic space was restructured by
an event in which he himself was involved and, as a linguistic agent, he
grasped its significance. For an epocheic observer Robinson's change after
that experience has to remain unintelligible. We, however, the poor people
who live in the only attitude that is natural to the dramatic agent, have
no
trouble relating to it.

Rationality and the contemplative perspective

      It is time to return to our starting point. We began with Descartes
and
Hume. In their radical separation of the thinker from the agent and their
unhesitating self-identification as philosophers that is, as rational
beings,
with the former we sensed something awry. In order to detect precisely what
was wrong with them we moved to Husserl. The advantage of Husserl is
that thanks to his explicit and systematic use of the epochd as the
essential
philosophical tool he renders easily accessible to analysis the sort of
rela-
tionship between subject and world tacitly presupposed by Descartes and
Hume.

      The purpose of my analysis of the participant was to show, by means
of a
contrast with the subject of the Cartesian tradition, to which Husserl be-
longs, how little justice the unidirectional model of presence which
defines

that tradition does to the richness of the experiential encounter. The par-
ticipant-and such happens to be, whether we like it or not, our situa-
tion-finds himself in a dramatic space of which, for obvious reasons, we
find no inkling in Descartes, in Hume, or in Husserl.

      It is important to realize, however, that far from constituting the
excep-
tion in the history of philosophy, Descartes, Hume, and Husserl are them-
selves typical representatives of a tradition that goes back to Plato. For
it
was Plato, indeed, who gave shape to the idea of knowledge as accurate
contemplation of reality and made, therefore, normative for the rational
subject to attempt to transcend the world by cutting his ties to it.
Securely

cav-

its vividness, he as a pure contemplator had every reason to identify as
mere
appearances. Disgusted with shadows, Plato turned his eyes away from the
cavern toward the light in search of genuine realities, that is, of objects
capable of living up to the "rational" demands of a pure contemplator.

      That Plato's approach, based as it is on a distortion of the
fundamental
subject-world relationship, may have become the normative model of ra-
tionality and come to give authoritative shape to our notions of
experience,
of evidence, of values, of objectivity, of knowledge, and of truth, is
almost
beyond comprehension. For the fact is that Platonism is as alive today as
it
has ever been, and that philosophers continue to be the official carriers
of
that tradition. It is true that Plato is seldom followed in his search for
eter-
nal Ideas. But that is not what characterizes Platonism. The theory of the
Ideas is only Plato's personal contribution to the series of bad solutions
to
the impossible problem with which all those are unavoidably faced who
conceive the subject-object relationship the way Plato did. What charac-
terizes Platonism is the neutralization by the thinker of his own exposure
to
the surrounding world. It is in this sense that most of what goes on today
under the name of philosophy continues to be a series of footnotes to
Plato,
a futile search for an impossible accurate representation of reality, as
amply
demonstrated by Rorty's shattering criticism of the prevailing
philosophical
model.

      But one does not need to be familiar with Philosophy and the Mirror
of
Nature to realize that such is the case. What do most philosophers deal
with
today? Pictures, though they call them by the more respectable name of
"descriptions." For some, the issue is still justifying the picture they
happen
to prefer-most often the latest description of the world that they can
glean
from "the hard sciences"-though they themselves know that such a justifi-
cation would have to be based on a neutrally descriptive language that is
im-
possible. As for others, tired of finding only shadows-shadows of bodies,
shadows of values, and the shadow of the very self who finds those

shadows-devote themselves joyfully to the deconstruction of the whole

MacIntyre: a return to dramatic space

      It may be objected that if my criticism of traditional philosophy
applies
to those who reduce philosophy to epistemology, it ignores completely the
ethical vein that runs through Plato's works and through the whole history
of the philosophical tradition to which he gave birth, manifested still in
our
day by the large number of contemporary philosophers who devote them-
selves to ethical issues.

      This brings me to Maclntyre's book. For, although he does not put it
in
these terms, what he offers us in After Virtue is a reconstructed history
of
the failure to which the thinkers belonging to the tradition inaugurated by
Plato were condemned in their attempt to furnish a rational foundation for
ethical discourse and virtuous practice. The venture ends up, by its very
failure, in a sort of justification by default of what MacIntyre calls "emo-

tivism." "Emotivism is the doctrine that all evaluative judgments and more
specifically all moral judgments are nothing but expressions of prefer-
ences . . ., of attitude or feeling, . . . Factual judgments are true or
false.
But moral judgments are neither."6 It is not difficult to see this doctrine
as
the logical outcome of a tradition that separates the thinker from the par-
ticipating agent and makes rationally normative the judgments of that pure
contemplator. For if such a contemplator can still entertain the hope,
however vain, of making true statements concerning "factual states of af-
fairs" on the basis of the presence of the object to him, he cannot but
find
ultimately meaningless all those expressions that presuppose the subject's
exposure to the world by which he is surrounded.

      As for Maclntyre's own positive contribution, it is not my intention
to ex-
plain it in detail, but simply to indicate why I consider it particularly
im-
portant and promising.

      To those who approach After Virtue with the traditional ideas of what
qualifies as philosophy and of philosophical rigor, MacIntyre's book has to
be profoundly disconcerting. He seems to ramble all over the place, mixing
in the process such disparate things as Homer's poems, Aristotle's anthro

162 1984 Proceedings of the ACPA

pology, the Greek tragedies, Weber's sociology, Jane Austin's novels, and
some of Marx's views, to name only a few. He has a visceral aversion to
ethical subjectivism, liberal individualism, and Stalinist totalitarianism.
Out
of it all he constructs an extremely long and intricate argument which, of
course, ends up in a sort of justification of his own stances. Does this
amount to anything more than a motley collection of personal opinions on
the individual, on society, on morality? Where are his "hard facts"? Which
are his epistemological criteria for reaching them? What is the
philosophical
justification for his strange method?

      Maclntyre's work is indeed bound to be disconcerting and ultimately
unintelligible to anyone who works, so to say, within the traditional
philosophical paradigm. Such a paradigm is characterized by the assump-
tion of the uni-directional presence of object to subject. Hence the con-
stitutive rules of the rationality game. The player is implicitly defined
as one
who suspends belief in the reality of what is present to him until it is
other-
wise established. Ultimately, then, the task of the rational subject is two-

fold. In its negative aspect, it consists in debunking the natural
attitude,
that is, in criticizing those who take what appears for genuine reality, so
as to
convert them from irrationality to rationality. In its positive aspect, his
task
consists in devising the appropriate method to discriminate between mere
appearances and genuine realities, between phenomena and noumena. And
devising the appropriate method amounts to specifying and justifying to the
satisfaction of a transcendent contemplator the criteria by which such all-
important a discrimination is achieved or to be achieved. Obviously,
though, Maclntyre's procedure in After Virtue does not conform to these
strictures and, consequently, it can hardly amount but to a motley collec-
tion of arbitrary opinions.

      Now, it is perfectly true that MacIntyre does not play that game.
And, if
that game is the rationality game, then MacIntyre's procedure is
irrational.
But what is at stake is the paradigm itself by which the game is implicitly
de-
fined.

      If our relationship to the world were that of subjects to whom
objects ap-
pear, then the rational subject would be the traditional philosopher. How-
ever, it is not for us to decide of the relationship in which we stand to
the
world. Now, the form under which the agent unavoidably lives and tells his
life is a dramatic narrative precisely because his relationship to what sur-

rounds him is that of the participant, not of the transcendent contemplator
contrived by philosophers. In MacIntyre's own words, "human life has a
determinate form, the form of a certain kind of story."6

      As I remarked above, the novelist does not have to decide whether
what
he will write will be dramatic or not; it will, necessarily, because his
subject
matter has a structure of its own. Maclntyre makes a similar observation:

Practical Reasoning 163

It is not just that poems and sagas narrate what happens to men and
women, but that in their narrative form poems and sagas capture a
form that was already present in the lives they relate.'

      Does this imply that human life is a novel or a poem or a saga? Of
course
not. And the reason is that, as I just quoted, "human life has . . . the
form
of a certain kind of a story," a unique kind. MacIntyre calls it a history.

What I have called a history is an enacted dramatic narrative in which
the characters are also the authors . . . The difference between
imaginary characters and real ones is not in the narrative form of what
they do; it is in the degree of their authorship of that form and of their
own deeds. Of course just as they do not begin where they please, they
cannot go exactly as they please either.S

      In short, the central piece and the focus of intelligibility of
MacIntyre's
book is the narrative structure of life.9 That is the way in which he
replaces
himself, and coaxes his reader to replace himself as a thinker, within our
native dramatic space. This is why I consider After Virtue a particularly
im-
portant and promising work. Whether or not the reader comes to agree with
each and every point of MacIntyre's analysis, the philosophical argument
has nonetheless been brought from the chemically pure and sterile sphere of
Platonic rationality back to where it belongs-to the world in which the
business of living takes place and where the thinker is called to act
ration-
ally.
