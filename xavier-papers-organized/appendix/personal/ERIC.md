August 11, 1993

Dear Eric:

On the occasion of this celebration it has dawned on me that you have been around at UD for twenty-five years. Twenty-five years! I hardly can believe it. For some reason, my encounters with you have kept a freshness and vividness that do not square with the what the calendar says.

It was many years ago, I know , but it feels to me as if it had been yesterday. Out of curiosity more than anything else, I attended at UD a series in theological dialogue among a Protestant, a Catholic, and a Jew. The Protestant was Bill      Anderson, Jim Heft the Catholic and, of course, you were the Jew. There I knew of your existence and saw you for the first time. Why do I remember this so vividly? Because I was terribly impressed by you--not only by what you knew  but by your way of knowing it, not only by what you said but by your way of saying it. Theology, in your case, was not just a field of study; it was a form of life. You knew not only with the head but also with the heart, and you had a quiet, assured, humorous way of saying what you had to say that spoke not only to the head but also to the heart. There and then I knew that I wanted to become your friend. There and then I decided that one day I would take one of your courses in order to learn from your very Jewish way of being related to The Most High.

My second encounter with you was less dramatic. Ed Hatch and Bob Conard invited me to participate in a small-group discussion on the proofs for God's existence. Knowing that I am a Catholic and, if not a philosopher, at least a teacher of philosophy, they imagined that the subject would interest me particularly. In fact, there are few subjects I find less attractive (or should I say more repellent?) than the proofs for God's existence. I was, therefore, about to decline their invitation as politely as possible, when  I discovered that they had managed to trap you and Jim Heft for that discussion. Needless to say, I accepted the invitation for the sole purpose of coming to know you a little better. The discussion took place at Ed Hatch's home and was as unmemorable as discussions on this subject always are. We ended

up, you and I, talking with each other on our differences concerning original sin... But the encounter fulfilled its purpose and confirmed my intuition that you and I were born to become friends.

Then I had a sabbatical term, which furnished me the long awaited opportunity  to take one of your courses. As you well know, I loved being introduced by you into the rabbinic culture--a world of which till then I had only a very vague idea. Also, I was full of admiration for the marvelously personal rapport you managed to establish with the students and for the subtle way in which, little by little, you got them interested in a subject so alien to their ordinary preoccupations. Above all, it was the beginning of our friendship.   

Since then we have had numerous encounters. In time you met Geneviève, my wife. We have become close friends. We have gossiped together, the three of us. We have laughed together. We have drunk and eaten together. We have prayed together in English, in French, in Hebrew, in Latin, in Spanish. We have exchanged views on most everything. We have shared our joys, our preoccupations, our sorrows. Through it all, Geneviève and I have indeed learned much from your way of believing in The Most High. And it all continues to feel so fresh that we hardly can believe that we have known you for so long, that you have been around at UD for twenty-five years.

Xavier O. Monasterio
