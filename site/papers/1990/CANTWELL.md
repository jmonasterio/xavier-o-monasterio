Cantwell Smith on Faith in Aquinas

Taken from: Wilfred Cantwell Smith, Faith and Belief , Princeton

University Press, Princeton, N. J., 1979, chapter 5).

79: For Augustine, Credo is an act of self-commitment with predominance of the will. For Aquinas, in the act of faith the mind plays a predominant role. But this does not mean that Aquinas equates faith with belief. 80: He did more than anyone else to intellectualize faith, but he does not reduce it to belief.

80: The relation between faith and belief in his conceptual scheme has been little elucidated . It is not easy, because (a) he was an intellectual, (b) his system of ideas constituted a language that was not his faith, but in which he said what he had to say about faith , and (c) the relation between faith and belief has been obscured by translating his "credo" by the verb "believe".

81: (1) The question of his faith as an intellectual :

An intellectual is someone who thinks that the intellect apprehends reality. He is a lover of truth not yet apprehended. For Aquinas the object of truth is ultima veritas; but for him, as a religious intellectual, truth and goodness are ultimately identical. Faith for him is a dedication to truth with applause for it (assensio) and involves acting in terms of it . It is lifeless if the will involved in the pursuit of truth is not also a will to good. Fides informis is not for him a virtue .

83: (2) His explicating of his position within his conceptual scheme :

He did not identify his system of ideas with faith, and admitted he could be wrong (but nof faithless). However, he felt some of his views not has 'his views" but as expositions of manifest truth , for he was neither a religious comparativist nor a comparativist of world-views. The only form of faith he recognized was deeply implicated with the only set of ideas and propositions he would regard not as beliefs but as universal truths . He therefore interpreted one in terms of the other, but did not equate one with the other .- He was aware that various ideas and presuppositions are presupposed in the Church

s analytic interpretation of what faith is; accordingly, he calls these " faith preambles "; though we would have to qualify this by characterizing these as faith's preambles at a certain historical period . In any case, he did not reduce faith to beliefs.

84: His use of Latin terms referring to faith :

Translating his "Credo" by "believe" in today's predominant sense is a distortion of his views. Faith for him involves a relation of a person, and specifically a person's mind, with truth, which for him is God. But what is the relation of faith to belief?

The Latin words for what we today understand for belief are opinio and opinari. Those who understood Abelard's aestimatio of faith as opinio were shocked and condemned him. For Aquinas faith is more than believing in this sense.; it is a commitment of oneself, through one's mind, loyally to truth. It involves an awarenes of a truth that transcends one's present grasp.

85: He explicitly distinguishes between Aristotle's pistis (conviction, very strong belief) from faith (n. 67), and distinguishes faith from fides communiter accepta, that is, belief (n. 68)

85: If faith is not belief, how are these two related?

Concerning the cognitive dimension, he saw faith as close to knowing, intermediary between belief and knowledge (scientia); nonetheless, it is cognitio, notitia, knowing without full comprehension. But it is not mere information but something more personal, and is more sure than scientia . Propositional belief such as Deum esse (recognition of God's existence) is not faith . for all rational men recognize intellectually God's existence, though they have no faith (n. 86). Faith is a quality of personal life, the capacity of the intellect to recognize the genuiness of the transcendent (n. 90). - 87: Nevertheless, there is propositional truth in the man God relationship that is faith , but this is on the human side of the relationship; on the other side is simply God: faith is not a proposition, but veritas prima, ultimat truth, God (n. 91) . In faith we apprehend God, not as He is in himself, not yet in visio beatifica, but we apprehend Him. Propositions are means to an end, human means to represent the Simple to us; but they are not faith if it is not to God that we are directed (nn. 95-7). - This is clarified by his notion of heresy. Error is not heresy ; heresy is the pertinax (perverse and stubborn) denial of an article of faith (n. 100). Even if a heretic believes al the other articles, he has no faith (n. 102) but only belief of those articles. For him, then, faith and belief are not the same, but they are intertwined , as shown in his position on the question of diversity of beliefs among persons of faith. - His position on this issue can be drawn only by inference and bold analogy. He was not aware (as we may be) that Jews and Moslems have faith; for him faith is only fides catholica (N. 106).For this reason, it is impossible to know whether he was speaking of faith in general, or of Christian faith . Perhaps (against our modern tendency) what he had was a Christian view of faith . (Its correspondent would be a 20th century Xtian view of faith, n. 107). He himself saw no difference between the generic (faith) and the specific (Xtian faith): faith for him is generic, for there are no species (n. 119). To the question whether for him men differing in belief may nonetheless have an underlying unity of faith, two answers are possible: (1) He never considred the question; (2) but insofar as he considered it, his answer is yes: faith is one despite the diversity of its many human forms (n. 111). For he recognized that beliefs differ while emphasizing the unity of faith. And he recognized a problem in the articulation of faith in articles and symbols, though he defends them rationally (n. 113). He solves the problem by implicit and more explicit faith (nn. 114-5). "He comes very close to saying that the implicit substance of faith is God himself, while the explicit articulation is mundane. (NO. THAT IS WHY HE INSISTS ON BELIEFS). He does not go so far as to say that all creeds contain the truth (that is, implicitly, God); but he says some explosive things. For instance, Israel believed that Xt nasciturus est, while we believe that Xt natus est. " Yet the principle is the same as ours: the expressing of fatih in formulae that are time-bound and culture-specific' . NOT AT ALL.

The nasciturus est (n. 117 shows that belief is human and fallible, but the substance is true .

    91: Conclusion : "The starting point and foundation of Thomas's view of faith is that its object is primal truth and ultimate reality is God; immediately so of its formal object, finally also of its material object. Meanwhile various other material objects that come into play and are not themselves God do not for him constitute objects of faith except insofar as they are oriented to God, and through them persons are oriented to God (n. 119).   A modern wording might be that in Thomas's vision faith is man's relation to transcendent reality, and the mundane objects through which faith is expressed, whatever they be, including, he says, ritual and ceremony, the sacraments, scripture (n. 120), including propositions, belief, and other intellectual constructs, have to do with faith insofar, and only insofar, as thery serve as activating symbols or effective channels of that reality."
