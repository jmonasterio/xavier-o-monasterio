All texts are indexical

“Thinking of religion as a cultural product thus directs attention to the process of its mediation; in other words, to how the content of religion is produced, transmitted, and received. Since the transmission of religion or any other symbolic form involves the packaging of meanings, the mediation process is a thoroughly interpretive activity... Interpretive activity, as we know from scholars of the mass media, is an active and creative process, one that is socially, historically, and locally contextualized... In this sense, as Dorothy Smith (1990b; 197) observes), ’ALL TEXTS ARE INDEXICAL’ SINCE ‘THEIR MEANING IS NOT FULLY CONTAINED IN THEM BUT COMPLETED IN THE SETTING OF THEIR READING.’  In making sense of the meanings packaged by a producer, the interpreter creates new meanings. Accordingly, the ‘reception’ or interpretation of meaning is itself part of the meaning process... In practice, therefore, a sharp dividing line cannot be drawn between the production and reception of symbolic content, and this includes religious doctrine ” (Michelle Dillon, Catholic Identity: Balancing Reason, Faith, and Power, Cambridge U Press, 1999, p. 26).

“In the agent, the judgment is constituted by his or her actual disposition to respond to what is judged (object of exposure -- Gegenstand rather than Objekt ) in the way determined by deliberation and decision, not a theoretical conviction that S is P or that the object ‘exists’ theoretically or is ‘real’ theoretically” (Monasterio, see below, p. 6).

“Scientific knowledge, like language , is intrinsically the common property of a group or else nothing at all. To understand it we shall need to know the special characteristics of the groups that create and use it” (Thomas S. Kuhn, The Structure of Scientific Revolutions, U. of Chicago, Second Edition, Appendix, p. 210).

The indexicality of perception

To determine what interests me about the indexicality of spoken texts or spoken language, I will  begin with the concrete case of the indexicality of perception, which tends to be ignored in the common understanding of perception on the basis of  its ‘content’ or ‘object.’

Perception is perception of object, but it is structured around the subject who perceives as its center. Indexicality is this tacit pointing of any particular perception to the perceiver. Husserl called this the perspectival structure of perception. Sartre related perception to action and completed Husserl by showing that the subject toward whom perception is oriented is not a contemplator but an exposed agent, an agent within the perceived field.

From seeing a picture one can determine where the camera was and, therefore, where the eye of the photographer was. Actually the picture places us imaginarily or virtually where the photographer was by making us see what the photographer saw, for the spectacle that was oriented toward the photographer is now oriented, thanks to the picture seen, toward the contemplator of the picture. One difference, however, between the photographer and myself who see his picture is that the photographer was the center of perspective of the picture as an agent placed within the field of action of the picture, whereas I am not the center toward which his picture is oriented as an agent but just as a contemplator. The photographer could do something to the objects he saw, but I cannot, for I am not perceiving what he photographed, I am perceiving a photograph of those objects.

Now, the important point for me concerning the indexicality of perception (which can be extended to all intentional acts, that is, to all conscious moments, is that, as Sartre puts it, all consciousness of something is consciousness (of) self. In other words, all awareness of anything involves on the subject’s part a non-tethical awareness of the subject as the center toward which the object is oriented. In the case of perception,  this means that whenever I perceive, what I perceive points at me as center, though I am non-focally or non-positionally aware of myself as subject in relation to what I perceive. Concretely, I do not only perceive the table, but I perceive the table near (me-subject) or far away from (me-subject).

What Sartre grasped is that  perception doesn’t point at me as an invulnerable contemplative consciousness, as Husserl thought, but as an exposed agent. The table I perceive  near or far (from me subject) comes qualified with an index of positive or negative instrumentality depending on my project. In other words,  “I can” or “I can not” (I can or I cannot reach the table) is the way in which what I perceive points at me, whenever I have a project.

One of the defects of Sartre’s analysis of perception is that, though it corrects Husserl’s analysis to an extent,  it still deals with the agent as an ethereal consciousness rather than with the agent as a situated body. Supposedly, Sartre replaces the agent in the world, but he replaces the agent as a consciousness, not really as an exposed body, for Sartre’s agent cannot really be acted upon by the objects by which he or she is surrounded in his or her perceptual field. Having started with consciousness, like Descartes and Husserl, Sartre could not escape from their body-mind dualism, and therefore their subjectivist idealism. Such was Sartre’s philosophical situation at least until “Materialism and Revolution” (1946-1947). And even afterwards he couldn’t quite get rid of his individualism and quite overcome the dualism between his subjectivism and his materialism. It seems to me that he would have had a better chance of putting things together in the right way if he had started his analysis from the standpoint of language and culture, in the stile of what for other purposes Simone de Beauvoir conducted his analysis of being woman in Le deuxième sexe.

Language and culture

De Beauvoir shows that being woman in the Western world is not a biological destiny, and therefore, inescapable, but a Western cultural destiny. “Woman” has been socially or culturally  predestined to be object – different in this from “man” who is subject. But how does this predestination actually take place? Through language. Our native language colonizes us.

Language is not an abstract, preexisting, available system of signs that one learns to use as an instruments to say what one wants to say and to understand what others say. That may be the way in which one acquires a second language, but it is not the case with one’s native language.  A native language is always a particular language. It is always acquired and used within a particular community of speakers-hearers. Furthermore, and there is a close relationship between verbality and conduct. To put it otherwise, there is no way one can genuinely come to understand a language, that is, to be a genuine participant in a given linguistic community, without being an agent in it, that is, without acting within it and being acted upon. This acting and being acted upon is linguistic, in-formed by language. It consists, concretely, in playing roles. Or, in Wittgenstein’s idiolect, in playing linguistic games.

A very important characteristic of language is that, like Sartre’s perceptual intentionality, language is indexical, in that the speaker is always indicated to himself or herself by the roles he or she plays, precisely because a role is necessarily linguistic, not merely a behavior. It occurs, not as a solitary activity, but as a game played with others within a linguistic community without which it would be impossible. I couldn’t possibly learn the social game of obeying without an other giving me orders that I didn’t issue and may or may not like, and that I follow in acting accordingly, that is, in the act of obeying, initially because I feel constrained and then obligated to obey. If Plato’s Gyges had been born with his famous ring on, he wouldn’t have become a member of a linguistic community and therefore wouldn’t have become a genuinely linguistic agent, for he wouldn’t have been able to play the passive linguistic roles, which constitute as essential a half of  being a linguistic agent as the active ones. For instance, he wouldn’t have been able to be held responsible effectively for his conduct by being punished for his bad actions, and therefore he wouldn’t have been able to learn to hold himself responsible for his actions. Actually, he wouldn’t even have learned that certain actions are bad and certain actions are good, for this is learned in the first place by being object of the linguistic response of others to our actions, before being subject who responds positively or negatively to other people’s actions because we have interiorized the standards of the community.  

Consider the sorts of roles that language renders possible. As a member of a linguistic community I can act as an addresser or as an addressee. Theoretically at least, I can give and receive orders. A dog cannot do that, not only because dogs cannot articulate verbally an order, but because they cannot learn to participate in a linguistic community. In ‘obeying’ an order of his master a dog is not obeying as a competent linguistic agent obeys. In the dog’s case it is pure conditioning, as it is in a young child. In an adult’s case, obeying an order is a linguistic response. What does this mean?

Think of  Helen Keller’s accession to her linguistic community. According to Susanne Langer ( Philosophi in a New Key, 1948, pp. 62 ff),  Helen accessed to language by learning the concept ‘water’ as a concept, not as a sign of water. For a dog, its master’s name is a sign of the imminent presence of his master. A human can hear his mother’s name and ask “What about her?” A human can conceptualize (connote), and then refer (denote)  to his or her mother in different ways through the concept and thanks to the concept.

Though partly right, Langer’s analysis is seriously misleading. She has a primitive idea of language. She thinks primarily of a system of symbols and totally ignores linguistic roles determined by the games one can play with others. Helen accessed to language not by learning that words signify concepts (this, at best, is the case only of names), but by learning to communicate with others, to play linguistic games with others in which she played definite roles.

If Helen had made statements to others, such as “The hammer is on the table,” and others hadn’t played the stating game by acting according to what Helen had stated, Helen wouldn’t have learned to state nor to act according to what others stated. If nothing had happened when Helen asked for water, Helen wouldn’t have learned to play the begging linguistic game. And so on. In other words, the illocutionary effect of speech-acts in which Searle sees the essence of language in Speech Acts is only an abstraction from the perlocutionary one, and speech-acts are themselves abstractions from the roles one plays.

In the linguistic games one plays definite roles. Not only the purely linguistic roles of

speech acts, but the real linguistic games in which others participate in the world of social action as the necessary feedback for the linguistic agent to go on. As a speech-agent, I am perfectly capable of giving orders. As a social agent, though, I cannot give an order to the President of the United States. As a poor man, I cannot play the game of buying a house.  As Sartre suggests,  I truly am –to myself and to others– the roles I play. But, of course, the roles I play are roles I can play. For Sartre, however, I can change my roles at will. So there really are no roles I cannot play if I choose to. In other words, my identity depends on me. I am totally free to choose myself. In order to create for myself a new identity, all I need to do is to nihilate through reflection the roles I have played in the past and to project the roles I want to play in the future. Go and tell that to women in Pakistan! Sartre doesn’t get the difference between projecting and dreaming. A project that cannot be realized is not a project but a dream. And since the roles I can play are roles that I play in games with others, if others do not play with me certain games I cannot play certain roles, however much I would want to.

I remember my little friend Joe’s answer to my childish question, “Why do I have always to choose the games we will play, and you never do?” “Because you are the master’s son.” As a young child, Joe had already interiorized ‘his station,’ that is, who he was, given the games and the roles in them he was socially predestined to play, as de Beauvoir says Western woman is predestined to play certain roles. This is what I meant by indexicality. At any given time, the “I” indicated by the field of linguistic action is the I who can play certain roles and who cannot play certain other roles. Theoretically , language as an instrument of communication is equally available to all those who know how to speak the language, that is, to the theoretical speech-acts agents, to all the members of a linguistic community. Practically , however, not everybody can play at will any and all the theoretically available linguistic roles. Even if I am a minimally competent linguistic agent,  as my little friend Joe was in his very young age, I know the roles I can play and the roles I cannot play in ther linguistic games theoretically available. The games and roles available or possible to me constitute my repertoire as an agent. This repertoire of games and roles defines my identity, my persona, and is inscribed in my ‘expert’ body as agent-patient. I AM INDICATED TO MYSELF AND TO OTHERS –HENCE INDEXICALITY–by the successive situations in which I find myself as an agent. As my expert physical body tells me whether I can or cannot jump over the hurdle that blocks my way, so my expert linguistic body tells me whether I can or cannot address this person, whether I can or cannot give her an order. And this linguistic body as distinct from my purely physical body (assuming that there is such a thing as my pure physical body) is a social creation, like de Beauvoir’s  woman.

Indexicality, therefore, is as personal as individuality. Each person has his or her own indexicality which is developed through interiorization of past experience as agent-patient within a linguistic community. This indexicality is relative to the agent’s social and physical environment by which one is indicated to oneself and to others. Thus, at the same time that it involves lived, bodily comprehension of onself as agent, it also involves a correlative bodily comprehension  of the agents –both persons and things– with which one interacts, as well as of their specific coefficient of favorableness and adversity in any given situation. And this interiorization is not only gained through first person’s experience as agent but also through observation of the roles played by other people in the linguistic games.

Of course, this bodily comprehension or habitual disposition to respond to the world is fluid rather than static, for it is constantly changing and depends on a multitude of factors. Moreover, the agent himself or herself is not totally at the mercy of the cultural grammar as to the roles he or she can play, as what I have so far said may have suggested. But this is a theme for another day.  

Compare this notion of language to that of Susanne Langer’s. What Helen Keller discovered  the day the linguistic miracle happened was, according to Langer, that she (Helen) could play linguistic games because she had played the linguistic game of naming things by having had her teacher name water to her. That this game involved conceptualization and that animals are incapable of that, I don’t dispute. But what Helen really learned was not that a name connotes a concept and that denotation presupposes connotation, or even that she could connote and denote by naming things, but that she could converse with others (and later on with herself in what Plato called silent language). And then, little by little, she learned with which others she could and  could not converse, and about what, and in what circumstances. Conversation is a linguistic game, and one develops this skill through the practice of participating in conversations with definite others in which one plays different roles. Or compare this notion of language to that of speech-acts theory, which focuses on abstract speech acts, not on actual linguistic games and roles in concrete geographical and social environments.

Science and Christianity as linguistic games

How does all this relate to Kuhn’s view of science, from whom I have received so much light concerning the practical, applicative knowledge involved in science? He sees science as a particular cultural game into which the students of sciences are socialized. As they develop as scientists, they come to occupy different roles within the scientific community that plays the game. What Kuhn fails to see is that the game, which as all social games is regulated by its members by the very fact of sticking to their respective roles and protecting their place in the pecking order against their inferiors while themselves aspiring to a higher place, is not –to speak with Maslow-- a merely expressive game  whose point is the enjoyment of the game itself, but a purposive one. Its purpose is to gain control over our natural environment to tailor it to our needs. Kuhn fails to see this because he is still a prisoner of the representational view of knowledge. Though he realizes that this view is wrong, he sees no viable alternative to it. The alternative is the practical ‘knowledge’ of agents who participate in socio-linguistic games.

What is more important, how does this relate to Christianity? Science is the game we have developed to multiply our chances of being in control of the natural environment. However, science is a reflective, second order game, based on abstract, interested observation of regular natural phenomena ex iis quae sunt propria rei . Religion, by contrast, is a first order game based on the experience of exposed, vulnerable agents concerned about their dependence on powers beyond their control, which they personalize, mythologize in sacred histories, and placate in ritual communicative actions. The young members of the religious culture are socialized into it as our children are socialized into believing in Santa. Religion ‘takes’ easily and is fairly universal because it satisfies our profound needs for security, for ultimate sense, for ultimate dignity as people who ‘count’ in the scheme of things, and for ultimate happiness. In this sense, we tend to be the gods’s creators rather than being created by them. “Religion” is a human invention, not a result of divine initiative.

Although Christianity satisfies the same needs that religions attempt to satisfy –for otherwise it would have no possible ‘take’ on us--, Christianity is not a religion. Indeed, it begins at God’s initiative, not ours. Hence, it doesn’t personalize natural phenomena, and  its central stories are not mythical but historical. But precisely because Christianity satisfies the same ‘religious’ needs that religions satisfy, and that the ‘progress’ of modernity has  killed religion and unmasked the gods by showing that they are the creatures of our wishful thinking, nonetheless, for all its differences with religions, seen from the outside Christianity has to remain irremediably suspect.

Now, linguistic cultures, characterized by their games, structure reality for their members, because the games they play are linguistic games that involve a non-thetic, practical awareness on the agent’s part. Though we usually don’t think of languages as true or false, at least a few linguistic games are susceptible of truth and falsity. Actually, among the games mentioned above, religion and science seem to be of this kind. How does this come about, and what does this imply as to the issue of truth in cultures?

The issue of truth in Christianity

Aristotle, in his Peri hermeneias , remarked that of all the speech-acts only statements, which express judgments, are susceptible of truth and falsity. Now, because of his speculative conception of knowledge as correspondence with what is the case, Aristotle understood judgments-statements as claiming correspondence. However, if we realize that perceptual judgments are practical [see my 2001-April-1-Kuhn: “Kuhn and the Issue of Truth,” p. 9, on the difference between judgments and statements], then the interpretation changes. In the agent, the judgment is not a theoretical conviction that S is P, or that the object ‘exists’ theoretically or is ‘real’ theoretically. The judgments consists in the agent’s actual disposition to respond to what is judged (object of exposure, Gegenstand rather than Objekt ) in the way determined by deliberation and decision, given my concrete project.*

*As I have explained elsewhere, this, precisely, is what  the agent’s perceptual statement, which is always particular and in the present tense, means or intends to communicate to another exposed agent to dispose him or her for action according to his or her indexicality.* Thus,  when the stating speech-act succeeds, that is, if the addressee disposes himself or herself for action in response to the stated object of exposure, the flow of action continues uninterrupted. If, on the contrary, the addressee doesn’t believe the statement (and if there is time), dialogue may begin and the game of persuasion may take place. This is rhetoric. If, however, the interruption of the flow of action is taken, as philosophers are wont to do, for the occasion to separate knowledge from action and to subordinate the latter to the former under the pretext of moving from ‘opinion’ (δσξα ) to ‘knowledge’ (επιστημη) as a condition for action, then we are in the philosophical conundrum in which what Kuhn calls “the textbook idea of science,” underpinned by the notion of truth as correspondence with reality, has its origin.

The idea of correspondence with reality arises when the judgment, which is practical in the sense of disposition to respond, is confused with the stated, and what is stated is interpreted as a picture of what is the case. This illusion is unavoidable once the flow of action is interrupted and discussion begins, for in this reflective moment the agent confuses his or her disposition to act with a theoretical commitment to the case being as judged, which is what seems to be contested by the one who doesn’t believe the statement, who expresses it as “It is not true that...” However,  there is no question of truth as correspondence even in their religious and scientific  linguistic games, except when the flow of action has been interrupted and the religious or scientific statements are considered by themselves and reflected upon, instead of being internalized and acted upon.

However, even if not precisely of theoretical correspondence, there are disputes of truth among the participants in the scientific and the religious games. What then is in dispute here?

One participates in a linguistic culture characterized by a group of linguistic games by playing them, that is, by saying and doing certain things, by telling others and being told by others certain things, according to the role oneself and the others play in the game. Now, to focus on religious language, the grammar of religious linguistic cultures --that is, the significant tacit ‘rules’ for saying and doing from which the players gain their respective identities as agents– is derived from their canonical stories in which God is the main character in the complex of events. The saying and the doings of all those who play the game have ideally to be in harmony with the sovereign role of the main character as defined by the historical events narrated in the canonical stories. Hence any saying about God or about the community of those who play the game that seems inconsistent with the sovereign role of God or with the traditional sayings of the community will tend to be excluded as false, and any doing anything that seems inconsistent with the spirit of the community as reflected in the traditional doings of the community will tend to be excluded as wrong. Thus truth is not primarily a matter of theoretical correspondence between what is said and what is the case, but of harmony with the roles of the participants in the game as defined by the content and the grammar of the canonical stories and the subsequent history of the community. The more seriously a saying or a doing disrupts the ideal harmony that favors the continuation of the game, the more it will tend to be rejected by the community.  

Christianity’s claim of universality

There are, however, extra-systematic disputes that concern the respective claims of universality or uniqueness or absolute supremacy of a particular religious culture. (Notice that these claims are always made by the members of a certain culture in favor of their culture, and are always rejected by the members of other cultures who play other games according to a different grammar. Cultures, as already said, structure reality for their members. Reality, however, is not something to be seen or understood objectively, independently of the practices of the culture, but is constituted by the game itself and its participants, who are themselves part and parcel of the game and who, as players, have at any moment a pre-comprehension of the game that allows them to play their roles. Reality is constituted by the cultural games in which one participates and of which one has the practical pre-comprehension that makes possible for the player to play the game with other players. As there is the intra-reality of the game of chess, so there is the intra-reality of the Catholic game. However, chess is just a game, that is, an expressive activity whose whole point is the enjoyment of the activity, like dancing. In this sense, Christianity is not a game; it is a purposeful linguistic activity whose purpose is to live according to the will of the Utmost Important. Now this category of the Utmost Important, of the Ultimately Important, is by its very nature universalistic, not in the sense of a game-transcending objective reality, but of being capable of absorbing all other religious games, of appealing to the players of all other games to reach their full potential as religious players. Now, it is true that each language constitutes a self-enclosed universe of discourse and action. Thus languages are as incomparable among themselves as basketball and football. Yet games satisfy profound human needs. That is why we find games, physical and otherwise, in all cultures. The same with religious games, which are meant to satisfy the needs mentioned above. Thanks to these shared needs, religious people can appreciate the respective merits of their religion compared to other religions or their lack thereof. Hence religious conversions are possible and do occur from time to time.

The universalistic claims of Christianity boil down  to this: although its own truths are no less intra-systematic than those of religions, the way in which Christianity is apt to satisfy the human religious needs is superior and, therefore, capable of appealing to people in a superior, unique way.

A comparison with science may be enlightening. Both science and magic are human cultural creations meant to satisfy the same human need of being as much as possible  in control of the natural environment. Yet, because of its superior ability to satisfy this need, science is capable of appealing to people of all cultures in a superior, unique way. This is what Husserl meant in The Crisis by saying that there is a sense in which no member of the Western world would want to indianize himself or herself, whereas as a matter of fact many Indians have wanted to ’westernize’ themselves as far as science is concerned. The reason is that magic claims, but fails, to be able to satisfy the need that science does actually satisfy. Well, Christianity’s claim of universality is similar, mutatis mutandis. The Greek and Roman gods are all dead, but the God of Christianity is alive.

Of course, the truth of this claim of universality cannot be demonstrated, for demonstration can at best be intra-paradigmatic, intra-systematic. Other religions aside from Christianity make the same claim, and their members are as convinced as we Christians are of the validity of their claim. But the point is not whether the claim is demonstrable, but whether the claim an make sense in spite of the admitted incomparability of the religious games.

By the way, as, concerning human needs, science and magic have a common root, so do religion and magic. The common root, in the latter case. is the need to be in control as much as possible, Moreover, as science has tended to eliminate religion because it satisfies the common need in a ‘truer’ or more efficient way, so, and for the same reason, it has tended to eliminate religion, insofar as religion is magic. And, insofar as Christianity is confused with religion, the progress of science has tended to eliminate Christianity together with all religions. And in the measure in which we Christians ourselves confuse Christianity with religion and practice it as a form of magic we have to feel  threatened by the progress of science.
