      NUSSBAUM ON PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE IN ARISTOTLE

          

“It is the great merit of Apelt to have firmly grasped the pple that, whatever the applications to which Aris put the scheme of the Categories, it is primarily connected with the use of linguistic thought to make assertions about reality, and hence with the proposition, the judgment as expressed in language .” (p. 1).

“The ancient commentators on the Categories distinguished three relations as implied in logical judgments: (1) A relation asserted between things; (2) a relation between the terms as verbal signs; (3) a relation between ideas in the mind of him who forms the judgment... The third is recognised by Aris as a ’combination of thought’, to be treated merely as a ’psychological presupposition’ of the logical proposition or judgment as expressed in words, WHICH IS THE SUBJECT OF LOGICAL ANALYSI S [Note 10: The phrase is Meinong’s. Meinong’s Gegenstandtheorie is useful in its recurrence to the thoroughly objective and realistic attitude towards knowledge which distinguishes ancient from most modern thought ]. So in Plato THOUGHT IS DESCRIBED AS INNER SPEECH ( Sophist 263a). Moreover, as the relation in thought tends to be merged in the relation in language, the latter tends to have incorporated in it objective relations between things .” (C.M. Gillespie, “The Aristotelian Categories,” In Articles on Aristotle: 3: Metaphysics , 1960-1976, Ohio State U. Press), p. 4).

The doctrine of causes, as it is summarized in Phys. II, 3, gives the impression that we are dealing with the results of an investigation which is not communicated to us... But the truth is that the doctrine of four causes does not consist on a recondite theory of fundamental metaphysical principles.... Here again as in the case of the structural concepts discussed in the last chapter [what we presuppose when we speak of things], we are in fact confronted with the results of an analysis of linguistic usage. Cause has several meanings in ordinary usage. Strictly speaking, therefore, we are dealing here not with four causes but with the four senses in which we speak of causes. Here too, the formal unity of these distinct meanings is established through a functional element, namely through the question ’Why?’ ( dia ti ). Aris says this explicitly (198a14ff)...   Here, too, then, we are dealing  only with topoi , with the classification of the points of view which we adopt in answering the question ‘Why? ’” (W. Wieland, “The Problem of Teleology,” ( Articles on Aris. Vol. I, Science, p. 147).

Nussbaum’s philosophical undertaking is closely related to her interpretation of Aristotle’s method as defined by him in the expression to which the title of this article refers. The idea of  “saving the appearances” as the defining moment of Aristotle’s method was  suggested to her by an article on Aristotle’s  method, entitled  “Tizenai ta phainomena,” by  her philosophical mentor, G. E. Owen. Not only would Nussbaum realize the central importance of that point for the interpretation of Aristotle’s philosophical approach, but she herself would adopt it as her own in her philosophical  work.

Nussbaum’s article of 1982 reacts to an article of Owen’s, entitled “Tizeani ta  phainomena,” originally published in 1961. ^(F1) Let me begin there.

Owen (“Tizenai ta phainomena)

A discrepancy between the methods of scientific reasoning recommended in the Analytics and those of the Physics.

But the discrepancy is not as usually characterized: that in the Physics Aris is not deducing from definitions but constructing the definitions. For that is not true either of the Analytics or of the Physics.

The real diferences: (1) In the Physics , by contrast to the Posterior Analytics , Aris does not try to distinguish and keep apart first the process of finding the principles and then of applying them. This is one difference.  (2) But the really important difference concerns the means by which the principles are reached. In the Prior Analytics Aris tells us that “it falls to experience to provide the pples of ANY subject. .. In astronomy, for instance, it was only when the phainomena  were adequately grasped that the proofs in astronomy were discovered. And the same is true of any science whatever.” Elsewhere he draws the same Baconian picture : the phainomena must be collected as a prelude to finding the theory which explains them. The method is expressly associated with natural science. It seems clear that the phainomena in question are empirical observations . In the natural sciences (astronomy, biology, meteorology) the phainomena are reached thorough empirical observation ; in the Physics the phainomena are reached by conceptual analysis guided, but not founded upon, empirical data. Here it is not a matter of “observed facts,” for Aris is not attempting to set the observed facts; it is A MATTER OF MENTAL ENTITIES, the endoxa (common conceptions ), or a mater of LEGOMENA (MATTERS OF LINGUJISTIC USAGE OR, IF YOUR PREFER, OF THE CONCEPTUAL STRUCTURE REVEALED BY LANGUAGE) . - Same in NE in the case of incontinence, where Aris describes his method as first considering the difficulties and then, as  in (all) other cases, vindicating if possible all the common conceptions, or at least most of them and the most important.   Here Ross’s translation – observed facts – is a mistranslation. It is a matter of endoxa and legomena. The Socratic  view that the incontinent act against their own conviction out of ignorance ????????? plainly contradicts the phainomena: not the observed facts but the endoxa, the legomena, “what would commonly be said” [by agents who have the experience of akrasia] . Aris, unlike Socrates,  wants to preserve a sense of doing what is wrong with some knowledge that it is wrong, but not in the full knowledge in the given circumstances. “It is in the same sense that all dialectical argument can be said to start from the phainomena. - Similar ambiguity with the epagoge, which establishes the pples of science by starting from the data of perception (AP. II, 19. 100b3-5; I, 18, 81a38-b9); yet it is said to be one of the met hods of dialectic ( Top. I, 12, 105a10-19) , and as such must begin with the endoxa , what is accepted by most men or by the wise. Similar ambiguity with the aporiai : when the phainomena are empirical data (in biology, meteorology), the aporiai associated with them will tend to be questions of empirical fact or of explanations of such facts, or of squaring a recalcitrant fact with an empirical hypothesis. But in the case of incontinence, the aporiai are things that men are inclined to say. We wouldn’t say that the man of practical wisdom is incontinent (not false, but absurd). Only in the latter sense can the Physics be said to begin with a survey of the phainomena, which in this regard ranks itself not with our physics but with philosophy. Its materials are for the most part not materials of natural history but of dialectic, and the aporiai are not questions of empirical fact but conceptual puzzles.. This is reinforced by the act that in the Physics Aris does not start with h is own observations but with a celebrated set of logical paradoxes that appeared in the Parmenides ., which supplies Aris with most of his central problems and also with the terminology and the methods of analyses he uses to resolve them. - Even with the second sense of phainomena –endoxa, legomena– some distinctions lie concealed . Legomena can be an appeal to common belief about matters of fact, OR to established forms of language , OR to a philosophical thesis claiming the factual virtues of the first and the analytic certainty of the second . And the broader ambiguity between the two senses could be exposed by Aris. For when he wants to restrict  phainomenon  to its first sense he calls it ”a perceptual phenomenon” and distinguishes it from endoxon. Hence Owen thinks that it is a mistake to ask, in the hope of quite a general answer, for the function Aris assigns to phainomena, or aporiai, or epagoge , for the function varies with the context and style of inquiry.

Yet the uses of phainomena have much in common. F. e., it is not a peculiarity of phainomena in the second sense that they may fail to stand up for examination, for the same happens with the phainomena of perception which are the touchstone for the correctness of physical pples. Indeed Aris came to realize that even the observations of astronomers were inadequate and mere hearsay, legomena to be treated with caution, in need to be properly established to qualify as firm data. Nor, if Aris associates the phainomena with experience ( empireia ), as he does in the text from the Analytics , must it be supposed that his words are meant to apply only to phainomena in the first sense. Endoxa also rest on experience, even if they misrepresent it. This is why Aris could include in his epistemology that if an endoxon is shared by all men is ipso facto beyond challenge (EN X 2, 1172b36-1173a1; cf. VII 13, 1135b27-28; EE I 6, 1216b26-35).

It is not surprising that writing in the tradition of Parmenides and Protagoras Aris tended to assimilate the different senses of phainomena .  For Parmenides, the ‘mortal opinions” include not only the supposed evidence of the senses but the common assumptions and the common uses of language which form men’s picture of the physical world . As for Protagoras, both Plato and Aris represent his theory as applying indifferently to perceptual phainomena and endoxa , and use phainestai in describing both these applications. Same in the formula from the Prior Analytics.  And though in the De Caelo , Aris says that the phenomena of perception are the ones by which the adequacy of our pples in physics are to be tested, he is speaking of physics as a whole, a body of science, not of his own Physics, where he does not start with inspections of the world but with the familiar data of dialectic (for he observes at the end that he has been speaking about the way in which the premisses of deductive argument are to be chosen, and refers to the more detailed treatment of the same matter in the ‘treatise on dialectic,” i.e., the Topics , where the premises of scientific argument are allowed to be established  by methods that start with the endoxa.

In the second Part, Owen turns to the Parmenides and  the part played by the arguments

in which Aris is the interlocutor, in shaping the Physics . Perhaps it is by misreading the Physics that critics like Ross have been led to make excessive claims of its originality.

Saving the appearances

Nussbaum’s philosophical career and her concern with Aristotle did not begin in 1982, with her reaction to Owen. Long before, in the work to which I will presently refer, she had already begun to make up her own mind about Aristotle in a way that would make foreseeable her reaction to Owen’s thesis.       

Aristotle’s De Motu Animalium

Such was the title of the study devoted in 1978 by Nussbaum to the  work of Aristotle whose title was  translated in that way int Latin by the Medievals. ^(F2) She herself translated afresh this Aristotelian work, issued a critical edition, and supplemented it with a series of substantial and extremely rich interpretive essays.  What is to be pointed out as relevant to my purposes is the connection of that work with the Aristotelian theme of practical reason by  contrast to theoretical reason.

The title of Aristotle’ work suggests that it has something to do with animals. And  it actually does. But not exclusively. For the “animalia” of the title refers to all animated, that is,  living creatures. Indeed, Aristotle proposes to study of the movement, that is, the action, of all living creatures, from plants through animals to people.   Now, in the case of animals and people, Aristotle sees a connection of their action with their knowledge . However, as is evident from a number of Aristotelian works, though Aristotle is convinced that both people and animals are capable of  knowing, he nonetheless saw in this regard a  a radical difference between animals  and people, to wit: that people are capable of both theoretical and practical  knowledge, whereas animals are only capable of practical knowledge. Insofar, then, as in the de De Motu Animalium he is speaking of the epistemic abilities of both people and animals, Aristotle must be speaking of practical knowledge, not of theoretical knowledge. That the case is actually such is clear in that the knowledge of which  Aristotle speaks in this work referring to both animals and people is the knowledge related to their action –their motus – and the distinguishing characteristic of practical knowledge is precisely its relationship to action, which is absent in theoretical knowledge. Now I point this out for two extremely important reasons: (1) The model of knowledge with which Nussbaum will work throughout her career and on which she will base her interpretation of Aristotle emerges from her reflection on the De motu animalium. Therefore, her epistemic model will  will be what Aristotle calls practical knowledge by contrast to theoretical knowledge, which she will tend to ignore without denying it but without  quite realizing that as a model of knowledge it is incompatible with practical knowledge. (2) Insofar as Nussbaum, contrary to Owen, will claim that Aristotle has only one notion of  “experience,” she will implicitly be claiming that Aristotle thought that the “experience” involved in practical knowledge is the same as the “experience” involved in  theoretical knowledge, a knowledge which is not connected with action. I believe that Nussbaum is wrong in

claiming that Aris’s notion of experience is one and the same; consequently, I believe that she is also wrong in her implicit belief that the “phainomenon” is the same in the case of theoretical and of practical knowledge. But that will come later. Let us go for the moment to the relevant points of Nussbaum’s interpretation of the De motu animalium.

At a crucial moment of Essay 4, Nussbaum quotes the Aristotelian question formulated in the text: “How does it happen that thinking is sometimes followed by action and sometimes not?” ^(F3)

Nussbaum comments:

“With this very general question Aristotle introduces the account of action in which the practical syllogism will furnish the explanatory model . Having enumerated the various faculties that figure in the explanation of animal motion –species of cognition and of desire– Aristotle must now go on to say how their activities must be combined if action is to follow. The initial question, ‘Why does cognition sometimes leads to action and sometimes not?” should be understood to demand the answer that the syllogistic examples will provide: because sometimes there are present both a desire for the apprehended goal and a cognitive specification of what must be done if the goal is to be reached, and sometimes not.” ^(F4)

Several remarks should be made in connection with the above paragraph. First, the Aristotelian practical syllogism is mentioned – a subject which will occupy an important place in Nussbaum’s interpretation of Aristotle notion of experience in both The Fragility of Goodness and in Love’s Knowledge. Second, the practical syllogism emerges in Aristotle’s work by contrast to the theoretical syllogism: the latter terminates in theoretical knowledge, the former terminates in action. There is therefore a parallelism –which is not the same as identity– between theoretical an practical knowledge.  As Nussbaum herself puts it in the same section of the De Motu,

“ Aristotle now clearly distinguishes the conclusion of a piece of theoretical REASONING from its practical counterpart : But in that case the end is a SPECULATIVE PROPOSITION (for when one thinks the two premises, one thinks and puts together the conclusion), whereas here the conclusion that results from the two premises is an action ” ^(F5)

Third, the presence and the importance of desire in practical knowledge . Not only does the presence of desire characterize practical knowledge, but it distinguishes practical from theoretical knowledge essentially . Indeed, as Nussbaum will take pains to point it out in her two later mentioned works, the Platonic view of knowledge (from which, in my opinion, Aristotle derives his own view of theoretical knowledge, similar to the Platonic without being identical to it), positively excludes emotion and desire as corrupting influences on  knowledge because of their connection with the body . In other words, the Platonic notion of knowledge, from which Aristotle derives his own view of theoretical knowledge, is such that it positively excludes emotion and desire; not only do desire and emotion happen to be absent from knowledge, but they have to be absent if knowledge is to be knowledge . Fourth, the very question posed by Aristotle as to why knowledge is sometimes followed by action and sometimes not, implies that, contrary to what Hume would think many centuries later, Aristotle thought that there was a species of knowledge in which “is” implied “ought,” namely, “practical knowledge.” ^(F6)

AFTER POINTING OUT THE PARALLELISM BETWEEN PRACTICAL AND THEORETICAL KNOWLEDGE, ON WHICH PARALLELISM THE PRACTICAL SYLLOGISM RESTS, Nussbaum proceeds to explain the oddity of the Aristotelian notion of the practical syllogism because of its non-verbal or non-propositional character:

“ As von Wright observes: ‘It is of the essence of propositions that they are expressed by sentences.... Wants, states of knowing or believing, and acts have no analogous connection with language.’ At 701a33-34 Aristotle speaks figuratively of the faculties ‘ saying ’ the premises, indicating that literal verbalization is not a central feature of this scheme (how could it be, if it is a model to help to EXPLAIN the activities of all animals) . - All this is a very odd way of talking about the conclusion of a syllogism. But the practical syllogism is, after all, only a piece of formal apparatus that we invoke to EXPLAIN what is supposed to be going on psychologically, and what faculties we must mention in accounting for the animal’s behavior. If the analogy to the syllogism [AS WELL AS THE PARALLELISM BETWEEN THEORETICAL AND PRACTICAL] shows signs of strain, it, rather than the complexities of the phainomena of action, should be sacrificed . ^(F7)

As for the ‘faculties’ involved in practical knowledge, Nussbaum has already explained that, in Aristotle’s view,  the are twofold: “a desire of the apprehended goal, and a cognitive specification of what is to be done if the goal is to be reached. “ ”To have action we must have an end characterized as desirable and a specification, provided by a cognitive faculty, of how it can be realized .” ^(F8)

“This is not, however, the full extent of the problem. For there really seem to be two logically distinct cognitive faculties that Aristotle somewhat subsumes under the general rubric of the ‘minor premise,’ or premise ‘pertaining to the possible.’ First. the belief about what must be done. Second, the perception that one can do that act now . [BOTH THIS BELIEF AND THIS PERCEPTION ARE TACIT, INSTRUMENTAL, NOT OBJECTIVE. AND IN THE CASE OF THE ANIMAL PURELY DETERMINED BY INSTINCT OR ACQUIRED HABIT. IN THE CASE OF PEOPLE, THANKS TO LANGUAGE, ANOTHER AVENUE BECOMES POSSIBLE]. A calculation of this sort (‘If I am to get a cloak, there must be this’) must be completed before the time when the action is. ‘If I am to get that job. I must go to the interview tomorrow at 11 A.M.’ The conclusion is still action, but one acts only when the appropriate time arrives, and only if one’s desire and belief are still fully active when that time comes. To take account of this, Aristotle might either add an extra minor premise (‘Now it is 11") or he might just say that the conclusion is the action of going to the interview at 11 A.M. the next day. In the  latter case, the perception of ‘Now is the time to act,’  either would not figure in the syllogism at all or would be conflated with the conclusion: the agent acts, when (he thinks that) the appropriate time has arrived” ^(F9)

After these complicated considerations to rescue Aristotle’s idea of the practical syllogism, Nussbaum observes soberly concerning animals:

“These complexities are of very little concern to the analysis of animal motion, where there is rarely, if ever (in Aristotle’s opinion), a distinction to be made between resolve and application . Animals simply follow the [PERCEPTUAL] appearance of the moment [FOR THEY HAVE NO CULTURAL ENDOXA OR LEGOMENA] (EN 1147b3-5, Metaph. 980b25-7). For this reason, and, furthermore, because it is the desire and belief at the time of acting , and not at a prior time, that are really needed to explain the action , Aristotle typically considers only cases when the time to act is now.” ^(F10) [BECAUSE ANIMALS LACK LANGUAGE]

In MA, Nussbaum observes, Aris concerns himself with the explanation of action , and that is the purpose of his treatment of the practical syllogism, whereas in EN the practical syllogism enters only in connection with [THE EXPLANATORY DEFINITION OF] akrasia.

How the method works

“ First, nothing universally believed is entirely discarded. ‘For that which seems to everyone, this we say is’ ( EN 1172a36)... ^(F15) “Second, nothing that we have to be using in order to argue or inquire can get thrown out.” ^(F16)  “Beyond this, we must, Aristotle believes, ask ourselves whether, in the inquiry at hand, we share some conception of the good judge, of the person or persons we will trust to arbitrate our disputes. ... Often our idea of the competent judge is more broadly shared among us, and less subject to disagreement than is our view of the subject-matter... In ethics, for example, we agree more readily about the characteristics of intellect, temper, imagination and experience that a competent judge must have than we do about the particular practical judgments that we expect him to make. The same is true in other areas as well. Engberg-Pedersen, “More on Aristotelian epagoge”, Phronesis, 24, 1979, 301=319) argues convincingly that episteme is understanding rather than knowledge.

The Analytics tells us how through experience we can gain insight into the fundamental status of the first principles. But only in Metaph IV does Aris defend his first pples against the sceptic who challenges the principle of contradiction. The sceptics demand a demonstration of the pple out of apaideuxia , the education by precept and practice that initiates the young Greek into ther ways of his community : “acculturation,” “intellectual habituation,” the sensitive awareness produced by education and experience, or the role this pple plays in all our PRACTICES and DISCOURSE . Aris attacks this sceptic by showing that he is in fact USING the pple he attacks , for in saying something definite he is ruling out at least the contradictory of what he asserts . If the person does not speak, he ceases to be one of us; if he speaks, he contradicts himself. “ So in a way Aris does not answer the opponents’ challenge. He does not offer him the exterior, Platonic certainty he wants [SCIENTIFIC DEMONSTRATION OF EXISTENCE], “ or the cataleptic impression” or perception that certifies its own accuracy (286). “A similar position is implied in the passage we examined earlier, where Aris rejected the Eleatic One on the grounds that not even a lunatic believes in it, if we judge from his TELEOLOGICAL ACTIONS. All Aris says is that no human being who undertakes to act in the human world, no human being who does not stand so far outside as not to be acting among us all, can be seriously holding this view. Action, even bizarre and abnormal action, commits itself to the existence of movement and plurality . In Metaph IV, 4, 1008b14-19, extends his point to the case in which the opponent, though silent to the argument’s verbal demands , REVEALS THIS COMMITMENT TO THE PRINCIPLE THROUGH HIS PRACTICES : “For why does he go to Megara and not stay put, when he thinks he should go? Why doesn’t he go straight out early in the morning and throw himself into a well.., but instead obviously avoids this, as though he does not actually hold that it is not good and good to fall in? It’s clear then that he believes one thing better and the other thing not better.” The opponent can defeat us only by ceasing to ACT humanly in our world, as well as by ceasing to SPEAK. As soon as he acts in some fashion, he is being responsive to the definite features of the world as it strikes a human being, namely himself. He is accepting certain appearances, both perceptions and common human beliefs – .. About the badness of early death, about the danger of being killed if  falling into a precipice.... He is allowing the humanity he shares with us to govern his choice. But this reply comes from within human practices. The pple is a priori in the sense of unrevisable, but not outside the lives and practices of human beings . For Plato each science must start from a pple or pples which are ‘unhypotehtical’ or known to hold themselves by themselves, independently of all conceptualisation or thought. For Aris the status of the basic truths on which science si based is a status of necessity for discourse .

290: So Aris “says goodbye to the Platonic forms. They are teretismata (meaningless sounds) that have nothing to do with our speech.  When the Platonist speaks of The Good and The White, he is not referring to anything or communicating anything to us. But to say goodbye to the forms is not to say that they do not EXIST entirely outside of our experience and thought. That we couldn’t say either. Even THE CONTRAST BETWEEN THE WORLD AS IT IS FOR US AND THE WORLD AS IT IS BEHIND OR APART FROM OUR THOUGHT may not be a contrast that the defender of A HUMAN INTERNAL REALISM  should allow himself to make using human language. Here we might say that Aris usually maintains his internality more consistently than Kant, refusing, most of the time, even to try to articulate what it is that we cannot say. Aristotelian reason is committed to language and thought, and the limits of these. Appearances and truth are not opposed, as Plato believed. We can have TRUTH only inside the circle of appearances, because only there can we communicate, even refer, at all.

291: This is, then, a kind of realism [CORRESPONDING REALITIES], neither idealism of any sort nor scepticism. It has no tendency to confine us to internal representations.. It is fully hospitable to truth... It is not relativism, since it insists that truth is one FOR ALL THINKING, SPEAKING BEINGS. It is a realism, however, that articulates very carefully the limits within which any realism must live. Talk of the eternal or immortal has its place in such realism, but only because such talk is important to our world... The belief in the divinity and eternity of heavenly bodies has weight in philosophy, because it has survived so many changes of social and political belief of a more superficial nature (Metaph. 1074a39). Even the EXISTENCE of an unmoved Mover is established as one of the conclusions of physical science, none of whose pples has a deeper status than the pple of non-contradiction, and many of which are obviously less firmly grounded.               

A few reflections on Nussbaum

- She fails to detect Aris’s ambiguity on the matter of essence and existence . Essence (as well as substance and natures) is connected with explanatory principle (which is always universal and necessary) and with cause (which is real in the sense of realistic existing,  i.e., contingent object-fact corresponding to what is in the mind).

- She confuses logical consistency or non-contradiction between statements with the agent’s practical consistency of the Camusian-Humean kind. Thus Aris doesn’t demonstrate the validity of the pple of non-contradiction, but shows that out practice supports it.

- Her job of showing the compatibility between the Aris of the Metaphysics and the Analytics with the Aris of [the De motu animalium and] the Ethics is superficial. The latter works has to do with action, not with knowledge of an entity (assent AND CORRESPONDENCE) and explanation and definition . And action understood as Nussbaum understands it – as a constitutive of “situational Perception,” in which action  always takes place in the agent’s present as a response to what is perceived which leads to the conception of a goal to be pursued and to a realization of the means available. It is not a matter of action talked about or known or observed or explained, but of the action actually to be carried out by the exposed, vulnerable, desirous AGENT, NOT KNOWER, and from his or her own LINGUISTIC OR NON-LINGUISTIC standpoint. The former works, by contrast, have to do with objectified knowledge, with knowledge as judged and stated . Thus, in her eagerness to canonize Aristotle, she fails to go beyond Aristotle on the basis of the good points found in Aristotle.

- She has no hint that the two Aristotelian notions of knowledge –theoretical (something stated or statable and assented to either necessarily because of demonstration or factually, and implying correspondence, on the one hand, and, on the other hand,  practical as appropriate action response – are incompatible, and that the former has to be let go. Actually, in Essay 5 she explicitly identifies theoretical knowledge with practical knowledge:

“ As in science generally the philosopher must begin with and return to the phainomena (and not so some ‘pure’ or ‘raw’ data), so, in the EXPLANATION of action [WHICH IS NOT WHAT THE AGENT IS ABOUT] , he must begin not with received, unprocessed sensory material, but with the way the world looks to the particular creature, what it sees things as [WHAT IT SEES THINGS AS NOT WHEN THE CREATURE IS  EXPLAINING OR REFLECTING AND HAS NO PRACTICAL GOAL OR DESIRE MOTIVATED BY WHAT IT PERCEIVES, BUT WHEN IT IS INVOLVED INACTION AS AN AGENT. It is this interest in appearing, and not a conviction that IMAGES play a central explanatory role, that underlies Aris’s development of a theory of phantasia and his use of it to explain such apparently diverse happenings as imagination, dreaming, delusion, and motivation . This UNIFIED THEORY OF AWARENESS supplements his account of aisthesis in an important way. Imaging could, and does, find its place within the theory, but Aris is right not to make it theoretically central ; because he does not, his sub theory of imaging is less objectionable than the blanket theory that is sometimes ascribed to him. AND HE IS ALSO RIGHT TO MAKE IT THE JOB OF THE SAME THEORY TO EXPLAIN BOTH PRACTICAL AND THEORETICAL CASES, FOR THESE DIFFER NOT COGNITIVELY, BUT ONLY WITH REFERENCE TO THE ROLE PLAYED BY DESIRE. It is perhaps THE MISTAKEN NOTION THAT EPISTEMOLOGY AND THE EXPLANATION OF ACTION ARE, FOR ARIS, TWO DIFFERENT BRANCHES OF INQUIRY, THAT HAS LED MANY CRITICS TO MARK OFF SOME PHANTASIA CONTEXTS FROM OTHERS AND TO FEEL NO QUALMS ABOUT OFFERING AN INTERPRETATION FOR ONE THAT COULD NOT FIT THE OTHER . TO MOVE A CREATURE TO ACTION , AN OBJECT MUST APPEAR TO HIM: HE MUST SELECT  IT, MARK IT OFF, ORGANIZE IT, INTERPRET IT. BUT THIS IS, DESIRE ASIDE, WHAT IT IS TO SEE IT. LEARNING TO PERCEIVE, AS ARIS TELLS US, IS LEARNING TO MAKE DISTINCTIONS. THE PASSIVE INFANT MAY RECEIVE SOME STIMULI, BUT PERCEPTION, THOUGHT, AND ACTION ALL PRESUPPOSE THE EXERCISE OF PHANTASIA. ” ^(F19)

Same identification of theoretical and practical knowledge:

“ Phantasia , then, is the animal’s awareness of some OBJECT OR STATE OF AFFAIRS, which MAY well prove to be an object of desire . It can serve both to present the object of desire initially and, later , to specify the object at hand as what is desired. This need not involve two steps, but often does. I am thirsty, I have (in the absence of the object) a phantasia of drink [ as object of general desire, i.e. instrumentally related to action, much as “health” implicitly functions in the practical science of medicine] . I then see a water fountain and direct my attention to it as drink. It is singled out from the cognition conglomerate of my perceptions. It means something to me [in relation to my general goal of drinking represented by my desire to drink, by my being thirsty] . I go and take a drink. P hantasia can also be used to account for delusion in practical cases : “Poor dog, he saw that as water (or as drink) when it was really ammonia solution.’ The role of phantasia here is, of course, THE SAME AS IN THE NON-PRACTICAL CASE (e. g, ‘He saw the sun as a foot wide’ ) [NO. HERE THE SUN IS NOT OBJECT OF RESPONSE,  AS THE DRINK IS] ; THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN THE PRACTICAL AND THE NON-PRACTICAL LIES SOLELY IN THE ROLE PLAYED BY DESIRE.] And in both cases the exercise of an opposing judgment can prevent the agent from acting on the basis of phantasia. In the case of non-linguistic creatures we will, as a rule, speak of phantasia only in practical cases ; we infer the content a perception has for them from the appropriateness or inappropriateness of the response... If we can guess the creature’s probable desires, we can infer from the behavior to the phantasia .” ^(F20)

Nussbaum is right in rejecting what she calls the empiricist or Baconian picture or notion of knowledge. But in her eagerness to absolve Aristotle completely of any contamination with the empiricist picture (only the strictly Baconian would be anachronic), she fails to take seriously the texts that led Devereux and so many others to distinguish theoretical from practical knowledge, and to see in theoretical knowledge (when it is a mixture of knowledge of universal and of particular) a precondition for practical knowledge (in order to obey an order I need first to have theoretical knowledge of the order, as Searle claims).  

- I have a feeling that she does not fully realize the TACIT  indexicality of perception, by which the exposed, situated agent is tacitly indicated ,  though at a certain point she speaks of its two aspects and of situational perception.

- She does not exploit, concerning the difference between animals and people, the fact of language, with its double-sided denotation-connotation, which allows for our ability to abstract from presence or absence and to speak in the indicative-EVEN IN THE CONCEPTUAL, NON-COMMUNICATIVE INDICATIVE . For animals the “appearances” are directly connected with action; not so for people, because of their linguistic ‘perception.’ Moreover, she doesn’t distinguish between the animal’s ‘beliefs’ and the linguistic beliefs of people.  

           - I suspect that Nussbaum’s distinction between two components of practical knowledge

–belief and desire– is invalid, particularly concerning animals AND THEIR ‘BELIEFS’, because of their lack of language. They are two moments constitutive of action. This will show in a moment when I deal with her ambiguity about “belief.”

- For lack of consideration of the abstraction that enters into theoretical objectified knowledge of object-substance, she feels entitled to identify the knowledge in the sciences with the agent’s knowledge. “When Aristotle sits on the shore of Lesbos taking notes on shellfish, he will be doing something that is not, if we look at it from his point of view, so far removed from his activity when he records what we say about akrasia . He will be describing the world as it appears to, as it is experienced by, observers who are members of our kind” (p. 274). Owen has more of a point than she thinks concerning the issue of two (or more) methods of setting the appearances, however wrong he may be in other respects. For the appearances in relation to the exposed agent are not the appearances to those who reflect in order to know theoretically. Doesn’t this show in Aris’s ambiguity about “images” and “phantasia” related to the object of desire or fear?

Owen and Nussbaum

Owen : Aris has  different methods of setting the experienced phenomena in the field of the natural sciences and in other areas of exploration, such as the ethical field. In both cases Aris begins by setting the phainomena, the experienced phainomena. In the natural sciences (astronomy, biology, meteorology) the phainomena that are to be determined before finding the theory that explains them are “empirical observations.” But not in the Physics , where Owen reflects that,” as Mansion observed, in general everything comes down to more or less detailed conceptual analyses –analyses often guided and illustrated by, rather than founded upon, empirical data.’ “In this sense,” Owen comments, “it would be grossly misleading for Aristotle to claim that he is establishing the principles of his physics upon a survey of the phainomena.” (Owen ‘Tizenai,’ p.114). “But in other contexts similarly concerned with methods of inquiry ‘ phainomena ’ has another sense [than empirical observations]” (ibid.). In his discussion of incontinence [ akrasia ], Aris tells us:

“Here as in other cases we must set down the phainomena and begin by considering the difficulties, and so go on to vindicate if possible all the common conceptions about these states of mind , or at any rate most of them and the most import ant” (EN). ^(F21)     

Ross’s translation of phainomena in this case as “observed facts” misses the point. It is not a matter of the observed facts but of endoxa, the common conceptions, the ‘legomena,”

“matters of linguistic usage or, if you prefer, the conceptual structure revealed by language” (Owen, “Tizenai,”  p. 114).

Nussbaum: protests. Aris has only one method in all areas. Moreover, in no case is it a matter of “empirical observations.” This Baconian picture of the Aristotelian method is anachronic. When in Lesbos Aristotle takes notes on shellfish he is setting the phainomena, and the phainomena are of the same kind as when he deals with akrasia . In all cases the initial phainomena are endoxa or legomena, and Aris’s purpose is to preserve the majority of the legomena, or at least the most important of them. Moreover, in no case does Aris try to transcend the phainomena toward something hidden [SUCH AS NATURES?]. In this regard there is no difference in Aris’s procedure in the Physics, the Ethics, and the Metaphysics.

Monasterio :  Nussbaum’s interpretation of Aristotle reaches its maturity in The Fragility of Goodnesss (1986) and in Love’s Knowledge (1990) , after her initial excursion into Aristotle’s notion of teleological animal action in her study of the De Motu Animalium (1978). According to Nussbaum, phainomena are initially what phainetai or appears, the appearances. Now phainetai , in Aristotle’s idiolect, is related to phantasia, a word that is usually translated as “imagination,” but whose meaning is very different from that of our modern English  word. Phantasia , in the Aristotelian vocabulary, is the name for the perceptual faculty that finds its actualization in what Nussbaum calls “perception.” What appears, then, in the Aristotelian meaning, is what pahinetai to a particular concrete subject in a particular situation, what is perceived by a particular subject who has phantasia, the capacity to perceive in the Aristotelian sense of perceiving, not in the modern sense of perceiving.  Herein, precisely, lies the difference between Nussbaum’s and Owen’s interpretation of Aristotle’s. Owen thinks that when Aristotle speaks of experiencing (in the case of perceptual experience) he understands perceiving in the modern philosophical sense, that is, as gaining empirical awareness of something , whereas in fact, according to Nussbaum, Aristotle understands it in his sown sense, as gaining the kind of awareness of something that is typical of a subject who has Aristotelian phantasia. This is very different from empirical awareness of something. Empirical awareness of something is awareness of the sensible qualities of an object, for (in the modern view) only the sensible qualities of an object are empirical, that is, capable of being  experienced. But what a creature gifted with phantasia experiences of an object when he or she perceives is totally different from the sensible qualities that appear to a curious observer gifted with senses – for that is what the perceiving subject in the modern sense amounts to.

In Essay 5 of De Motu animalium Nussbaum analyzed in much detail the experience of the subject gifted with phantasia , which constitutes  “the appearances” as these are understood by Aristotle. Suffice it, for the moment, to say that the experiences of a subject who has phantasia , as Aristotle assumes the human being to have, involves emotions, beliefs and desires, and is intrinsically connected with the (tacitly situated) subject’s responsive action. All of these characteristics are absent in the empirical awareness of object, which is the one of Aristotle at Lesbos when he is collecting observed data as a scientific observer. In other words, emotion, beliefs, and desires are not involved as constituents in the modern notion of experience which Owen attributes to Aristotle in his scientific works , However, they are involved in what Nussbaum, in “Love’s knowledge” calls  Aristotelian “perception.” But such perception is not found in Aristotle’s scientific works.

Though Nussbaum attacks the imagism attributed to Aristotle in connection with phantasia , she acknowledges that the notion of the resemblant image is present incertain contexts, along with other views related to action:

“It will be argued... that the most fruitful approach ... is always to remain aware of the connection with the verb phainestai and to find images when there is concrete evidence of their presence.... There will be some passages where Aris does refer to picture-like ‘internal objects;’ some where he merely invokes episodes of envisaging; some where his interest in the faculty of phantasia is still the more general one of how it is that one sees X as F, or ascribes some content to the object of one’s actual perception... After surveying the variety of questions phantasia is invoked to answer, we will be in a better position to appreciate its role in the account of action in MA 6ff. and DA III.9-11.” ^(F22)

According to Nussbaum, what is perceived by the subject is not something “empirically observed”; it involves the subject’s endoxa, of which (in my opinion) the subject is not objectively or predicatively aware. The endoxa are subjective in that they involve (practical, in my opinion) beliefs* and desires of the subject and are intrinsically connected with the subject’s responsive action. This sort of “experience” is what Nussbaum calls “Perception.”

“ For what phainetai F to someone is, after all, what is seen by him as F . His phainomenon agathon is his view of the good. The phainomena are things in the world as seen (and reported ) by human observers. This broad interest in how things appear to sentient beings seems to form the basis for Aris’s more specialized discussions of envisaging and of the sort of awareness that leads to action.” ^(F23)

Nussbaum attributes beliefs not only to people but to animals. However, on this particular point Nussbaum equivocates . One thing are the situated   agent’s practical beliefs that involve emotion and desire and terminate in action. The ‘beliefs’ involved in what Nussbaum calls ‘‘perception” are of this sort. Quite another thing is the curious philosopher’s or scientific observer’s’s theoretical beliefs that terminate in universal or particular statements about objects. Nussbaum, however, does not take this significant difference into a account. This is why she thinks that when Aris is observing shellfish on the shore or Lesbos... Since animals are non-linguistic creatures, their beliefs can only be of the former kind. However,  what we normally call beliefs are theoretical beliefs, beliefs-that, beliefs with a specified or at least specifiable “propositional content” which are not directly connected with action. Nussbaum fails to grasp explicitly, not to say anything of keeping separate and distinguishing adequately, these two senses of “belief.” The “beliefs” of animals are exclusively manifested in the animal’s actions, whereas the beliefs of humans can be manifested both in their actions and expressed in their own linguistic statements. But herein lies the ambiguity which is at the origin of Nussbaum’s equivocation, for the expressed or stated beliefs may be theoretical or practical (and are always theoretical when stated by somebody else than the agent, or even when expressed by the agent out

side of the situation within which the statements refer). The practical beliefs of the situated agent are tacit, as much as their desires are, and both are constitutive moments or elements of action. This tacit component is  not found in the statements of those who observe the agent. The same has to be said of the beliefs attributed to people: they are constitutive of the subject’s action, as shown by Aristotle’s own analysis of even the lunatic’s action. This connection of belief with action shows, too, in the case of akrasia, where we induce from the agent’s action that the agent doesn’t really believe what he says he believes and knows about the action’s goodness. Even Nussbaum in one of her analyses of the practical syllogism, insists that the belief here involved cannot be identified with its verbal expression. In other words, the verbal expression of a belief is not constitutive of the human belief in the same sense as the action is constitutive of the belief. If the action is missing, as in the case of akrasia, the belief does not exist, for action is a constitutive moment of the belief, but the akratic person can express linguistically his belief in the goodness of the action without lying, for he believes theoretically but not practically. However, the belief that is constitutive is the practical belief, the belief that necessarily (with conceptual necessity) results in action. Why, then, as Nussbaum says, animals cannot be akratic? Not because they can have no purely theoretical beliefs, (which is actually the case), but because they cannot refer themselves to the object connotationally or ‘conceptually” (in a non-Aristotelian sense), but only through desire or fear, as object to be pursued through action or turned away from. That is why the dog reacts to the name of his master by wagging its tail in expectation of its imminent presence, whereas even the child can ask “What about mom?” in response to his or her mother’s name.

My difference with Nussbaum : She has not given up on the very project Aristotle carries out contrary to his Nussbaumian conception of the appearances, the project of reaching objective natures beyond the appearances, which Nussbaum inconsistently allows for. Owen is right in detecting this project in Aristotle, however inconsistent it may be with the Aristotelian appearances to the exposed agent. And Nussbaum is wrong in failing to detect this inconsistency in Aristotle, and in falling victim to this inconsistency herself in her acceptance of the impossibility of denying existences that transcend the appearances.
