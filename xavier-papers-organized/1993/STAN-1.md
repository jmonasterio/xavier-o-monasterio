March 6, 1993

Dear Stan:

Yesterday I was notified that I have been awarded the Summer Stipend of the NEH for which I applied. Since you wrote in support of my project and I have no doubt that your support weighed very heavily in the appraisal of my project, the first thing I want to do is to thank you warmly.

You seem to be concerned that my views on Aquinas, which you strongly suspect of being wrongheaded (as in fact they may well be), might vitiate my project. For your peace of mind, I remind you that the portion of my project which is funded deals only with Aquinas immediate predecessors, who are the ones who for all their eagerness to jump on the Aristotelian wagon found the alliance between Greece and Jerusalem highly problematic. What interests me is the reasons why they found this problematic. Whether or not Aquinas successfully overcame those problems is another matter.

I have, however, faithfully followed your advice and read

Gene Rogers' dissertation, which he kindly sent me a week and a half ago. The enclosed letter I wrote him to share with him my first impressions on his work might give you some idea of where I stand for the moment concerning this complex matter. Oddly enough, if MacIntyre is right, Aquinas was a full-fledged Aristotelian who corrected and completed Aristotle. If Rogers is right, Aquinas' Aristotelianism, at least in the Summa Theologica , amounts practically to a misnomer, for all Rogers' rhetoric of "the more Aristotelian, the more Christian." I love Rogers' Aquinas, but such Aquinas (if he ever existed) had nothing to do with any synthesis of faith and (Greek) reason. Anyway, my concern is not historical. I am not trying to rediscover "the real Aquinas." I sense in the thirteenth century an epistemological rupture with the past in the way in which Christians reflected on their faith, which was bound to result, among other things, in such projects as Kant's Religion within the Limits of Reason and in the consecration of a silly notion of philosophy radically incompatible with Christianity. This is what interests me because of the tremendous impact it has had and continues to have.

Fare well, Stan. And thanks again. Peace and love.
