1998: STATE OF MY PROJECT

The intellectual tradition of the Catholic Church, as in general the intellectual tradition of the West, has been tainted with an intellectualism-rationalism that has done gret damage to the self-understanding of Christians. With post-modernism that tradition has come to a screeching halt, but no more promising alternative has so far emerged. The result has been mere chaos,  a a sterile relativism and skepticism which has deeply affected Christian intellectuals too.

In the Church's magisterium, beginning with Vatican II, there was some movement away from rationalism. Delhougne's work on Barth gives clear indication of this shift in the region of faith and revelation. However, the very Christian intellectuals who have contributed to that shift are as much at a loss as their non-Christian counterparts for lack of a kclear alternative. Actually, they are even more at a loss, for the Catholic intellectual tradition is very deeply markekd by the intellectualism-rationalism that has harmed the Church, to the point that the Christian faith seems to stand or fall with that intellectualism-rationalism. Indeed, the whole notion of truth, and consequently of 'the truth of faith' appears to be endangered. My project aims at clarifying and contributing to overcome that critical situation. But h ow can such project be made comprehensible to Catholic intellectuals, when it involves a demystification anmd debunking of such untouchable an idea as that the truth is one and, what is worse, of the very notions of knowledge and truth on which our fundamaental sense of orientation has until now depended? Indeed, it unavoidably looks as a a project bound to promote relativism and skepticism, much as the post-modern project that terrifies Christian Catholics. However, the reason it looks like is that, instead of shifting paradigms, we continue to understand that project within the intellectualistic-rationalist paradigm that is to be left behind.

Yet, the question has to be met: What is left after we give up the notions of truth and knowledge that have sustained us for so long? The answer is: What is left is the Christian faith by which we are saved and without which we are lost. Salvation is not a luxury, it is a necessity. We are saved by God, not by ourselves. Contrary to our vacuous humanistic prejudices, to be fully human we need God, and without being rescued by God we cannot be fully what we are meant to be. Far from its being truth that the death of God makes possible the birth of man, without God we cannot be human for being human is a way of being related to God. However, this can be realized only within faith, after being rescued. And this entails two difficult things.

One , that we cannot prove to those who do not share our faith that we have more and better than they have. We have to compete with them on equal terms, as it were. The only way we have to tempt them to become one of us is to be genuinely and glaringly what we are meant to be as Christians, to witness to God's grace with our lives.   

Two , to realize and accept ourselves that we literally live by God's grace, that we ourselves have no other guarantee of being in the right path than Christ crucified and risen from the dead. In other words, even to ourselves we cannot prove that we are in the right path. In short, we are totally dependent--totally dependent on our gracious God and on the faith we have gratuitously received. Now, this is not only difficult by itself, but it is even more difficult because we have been educated in the pride of reason, because we have been trained to think that our faith is rational, that is, if not quite guaranteed by reason at least in harmony with the realities discovered by reason, and to loath fideism.
