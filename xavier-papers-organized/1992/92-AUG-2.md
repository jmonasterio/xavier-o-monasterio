THE CATHOLIC INTELLECTUAL TRADITION

Faith and reason: A cultural revolution

The irruption of Aristoteles' major works into Europe and their rapid translation into Latin between the second half of the twelfth century and the first half of the thirteenth century created a totally unprecedented situation. The thinkers of the time, whose world-view had until then been fashioned fundamentally by the biblical stories, found themselves suddenly confronted with what for all practical purposes amounted to a different world-view which at least in certain respects appeared to be vastly superior to, and more sophisticated than, their own. What they found extremely compelling in the latter was not only its daring scope, which encompassed or at least attempted to encompass the whole of reality, but the systematic way in which it was constructed and presented, and above all the fact that it appealed to their intelligence rather than to their credulity by virtue of the demonstrative character of each of its pieces. True, its author had lacked the benefit of divine revelation and his world-view was therefore at least incomplete, in spite of its all-encompassing ambition. On the other hand, however, his knowledge in the domain naturally accessible to human intelligence was staggering and opened vistas until then unsuspected. The Aristotelian corpus  fascinated the medievals because it furnished them a definite standard for the evaluation of anything that passed or pretended to pass for knowledge as well as a practical program to be followed for the acquisition of new knowledge.

One of the most immediate results of the irruption of Aristotles' works was a sharp awareness of the difference between faith and reason, soon to be visibly reflected in the scholarly curriculum by the emergence of philosophy as a separate discipline. As long as there had been no rival world-view to contrast with their own it was practically impossible for the medievals to distinguish those elements in their Christian world-view that proceeded from divine revelation from those that did not, or that at least were conceivably accessible to human knowledge independently of revelation. Until then, the Bible had given shape to their world-view, which was historical in structure, in that they situated themsevles within that history that was moving toward the furutre. The study of the text of the Bible had undisputedly occupied the central and supreme place in the curriculum, and all other studies in the formation of the clerics--the study of logic included--were undertaken in view of their preparation for the study of the sacred text. The situation, however, changed radically when the medievals became acquainted with the Aristotelian corpus which confronted them with a world-view that not only owed nothing to divine revelation but that explicitly appealed to rational evidence as its formative criterion and discarded what could not measure up to that criterion. Almost overnight, philosophy as a rational endeavor independent from revelation and concretely identified with the study and discussion of Aristotle’s views acquired right of citizenship in the curriculum. ^(F1)

The emergence of reason side by side with faith in the consciousness of the intellectuals of the time, which progressively filtered down to the entire population, amounted to nothing less than a cultural revolution, for its effects shattered the very order of things which until then had appeared as natural and indisputable. Frei’s study of narrative traces this revolution.  Everything was dislocated. For instance, could theology understood as the study of God's word retain its until then undisputed supremacy now that it was faced with and challenged by a comparable corpus of knowledge that owed nothing to divine revelation and  that claimed to cover the whole realm of reality? Was it a matter of two domains of truth--the truths divinely revealed and the truths acquired through the use of reason--that were independent from each other, or complementary to each other, or perhaps contradictory to each other? Could the latter be the case? And, should a contradiction arise between a truth of faith and a truth of reason, what choice to make, and how?

These were not merely theoretical problems for the people involved, nor were the collisions among intellectuals and the social and religious dislocations that followed mere academic games, for they attained the very foundations of the medieval order. To get a sense of the depth and significance of this cultural revolution it suffices to take a very cursory glance at the literature of the time, which is charged with the electricity of the powerful tensions--religious, intellectual, political--that agitated first the universities and then entire towns from the first quarter of the thirteenth century on, as a result of the Aristotelian avalanche. Students of philosophy met with the passionate resistance of theologians, who tried to check their free use of the Aristotelian approach. "When these students move to theology," complains a theologian, "they are incapable of setting aside their scientific ideal, as is evident in the case of those who cannot sever themselves from Aristotle in their theological studies and end up substituting the spurious metal of their philosophical questions and opinions for [our] gold." ^(F2) The  theologians of the old tradition clashed violently with those of their colleagues who tried to introduce Aristotelian pieces of knowledge and Aristotelian methods into their discipline. The following exchange between two Dominicans is typical and gives an idea of the temper that prevailed in the schools:

"There are some," accuses a conservative theologian without mincing his words,

"who learned well the spiritual language of theology and nonethelessspeak this language as barbarians, corrupting it with philosophy. Because they studied metaphysics they want

to proceed metaphysically in sacred biblical matters...

They dress the king in dirty and thorn clothes; they darken the light with dust..."

To which an Aristotelian theologian, Albert the Great, furiously  replies:

"There are ignoramuses--particularly in the Dominican Order, where nobody checks them--who speaking the blasphemous language of dumb animals oppose the use of philosophy by

every conceivable means." ^(F3)

Theologians accustomed to the previously undisputed supremacy of the study of the biblical text itself bitterly resented the favor enjoyed by the practitioners of the new theological approach by " quaestiones " (theological problems only indirectly related to the scriptural text), by " sententiae " (professorial settlements of the problems treated), and " summae theologicae " (theological textbooks different from and not directly related to the biblical text). What follows is the lamentation of a theologian of the old school:

"Not only in Paris but everywhere the reader of Holy Scripture gives way to the reader of sententiae . The latter gets all the honor and the benefits. Indeed, in

Paris, the principal hour for teaching is assigned to the reader of sententiae , who also has an adjunct and is roomed in a convent. By contrast, the reader of Scripture

enjoys neither of these benefits and has to beg for his time for teaching, which depends on the will of the reader of

sententiae . In other places the reader of sententiae

has the right to conduct public discussions and is seen as a real professor, whereas the reader of Scripture is not entitled to conduct public discussions. Such has been the case this very year in Bologna and in many other places." ^(F4)  

The spirit of free inquiry progressively gained strength in the schools, particularly among the students of philosophy, a few of whom boldly proclaimed the absolute autonomy of their discipline and their right to carry their rational undertaking to whatever conclusions it may lead. By the middle of the century the authority of reason was being explicitly opposed by some to the authority of the Church. Unheard of doctrines, tersely condensed by M.-D. Chenu in the following quotation, were publicly defended and propagated:

"Theology is made up of fables, philosophers are the sages of this world, Christianity is an obstacle to knowledge, there is happiness only in this world, there is nothing after death, it is silly to care for Christian burial, confession is just a rite, praying is an idle exercise, fornication is no sin, virginity is unnatural, etc." ^(F5)

If these are not to be taken for the symptoms of a genuine cultural revolution, which in the particular case had its origins in the irruption of the Aristotelian corpus, then there has never been such a thing as a cultural revolution. Naturally, the Church's hierarchy could not possibly remain aloof, under the circumstances. In fact, from early in the second quarter of the century and increasingly so as the century advanced, the ecclesiastical authorities, worried about the survival of the Christian faith and morals in their purity, repeatedly intervened in the fray with exhortations, admonitions, interdictions, condemnations and fulminations. Aquinas himself, whose deep Christian faith and whose loyalty to the Church are beyond doubt, did not escape unscathed. Indeed, three years after his death, in 1277, the theologians of the University of Paris, headed by the bishop of the city, condemned 219 propositions. "Thomas Aquinas was involved in that condemnation which more or less explicitly concerned some twenty points characteristic of his Aristotelianism, for Aristotelianism was deemed to be the roots... A few days later, at the University of Oxford, a similar measure, more restrained but no less precise, proceeding this time from the Archbishop of Cantorbery..., mirrored the Parisian condemnation." ^(F6)

Faith and Reason in theology

It is not my intention here to pursue the details of this cultural revolution but, rather, to concentrate my attention on a particular aspect of it, namely, the controversy among the theologians of the time about the feasibility of placing their discipline within the Aristotelian map of knowledge without detriment to the Christian faith which they sincerely professed and by which they felt bound. Now, in order to grasp the problem as they confronted it it is especially important to focus on the fascination exerted on them by a particular piece of the Aristotelian corpus, the Posterior Analytics ^(F7), where Aristotle pointedly expounds his model of genuine knowledge--what he calls episteme , a term translated by the medievals as scientia . ^(F8)  

Very roughly speaking, for Aristoteles a science is a corpus of statements about a class of objects, expressing universal and necessary properties belonging to that class of objects by virtue of their essential nature, and resulting either from immediate self-evidence in the case of the "principles" of the science, or from strictly deductive evidence in all other cases. ^(F9)

Explain more

Whether such knowledge is attainable at all or within which limits, whether Aristoteles' 'scientific' or even 'metaphysical' works correspond to his own precepts, whether Aristoteles in the Posterior Analytics is talking about an already constituted scientific corpus rather than about its acquisition, whether Aristoteles changed his mind about the possibility of such knowledge or proposed other more realistic alternatives which reflect better his own practice are very legitimate questions which have received much attention in recent times by such specialists as Owens and Nussbaum among many others. ^(F10) However, these issues lie beyond the scope of the present study in that for the most part they did not concern directly the medieval theologians involved in the particular controversy with which this study deals. It may be safely said that, as far as this controversy is concerned, the medieval theologians who will occupy our attention found the Aristotelian scientific model of the Posterior Analytics eminently persuasive. What was a problem for them was not the validity of the model but the feasibility and wisdom of its application in the theological realm. More concretely, the questions they asked themselves and which constituted the core of the debate that interests us here were whether the theological endeavor in which they were engaged could, or even should, fit the demands of scientific knowledge as conceived by Aristotle, and whether consequently the knowledge resulting from this endeavor could constitute a genuinely scientific corpus of knowledge.  

Why would these deeply convinced Christians be especially fascinated by the Aristotelian model of science of the Posterior Analytics is not difficult to understand. It constituted a model for understanding their own Christian faith. Indeed, precisely because they were deeply convinced Christians, their faith gave them the certainty that the knowledge they possessed through divine revelation was true and that no knowledge could be higher in kind that the knowledge they possessed. If, therefore, the Aristotelian ideal of knowledge was correct, as they deemed it to be, and if it determined the characteristics that a corpus of knowledge had to posses in order to be necessarily true, then the necessarily true corpus of knowledge they owed to divine revelation necessarily had to square with the Aristotelian demands and even square with them in a more perfect way than any piece or corpus of knowledge acquired through merely human endeavors.
