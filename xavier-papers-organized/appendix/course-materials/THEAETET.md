                             ORAL EXAMS; THEAETETUS

      A slave  reads  to  Euclides  and  Therpsion  the  compte-rendu  of  a
conversation (dialogue) between Socrates and Theaetetus and Theodorus,  many
years before. Theaetetus is now dying, but at the time of  the  conversation
he was a youth full of promise, admired by Sopcrates.

      Socrates asks Theodorus whether there are any rising geometricians  or
philosophers among the youth in Athens. Theaetetus  is  the  one  named  and
joins them in conversation. Since he has been praised by Theodorus as  wise,
Socrates wants to examine him.

- Is wisdom the same as knowledge? And what is knowledge?

- First answer: Geometry,  and music, and the art of the cobbler  and  other
craftsmen are knowledge.
      BUT Socrates did not ask about subjects of knowledge;  he  "wanted  to
know the nature of knowledge in the abstract."  For  one  cannot  understand
the name of anything if one does not know what the thing is.
      (Socrates describes himself as a midwife  who  helps  others  who  are
pregnant with conceptions to give birth but who is himself barren, for  "the
god  compels me to be a midwife, but has not allowed me to bring forth.  And
many youth have profited from his midwifery, as Theaetetus might).

- Second answer: Knowledge is simply perception (as Protagoras thought  when
he said that "man is the measure of all things,  of  the  existence  of  the
things that are, and of the non-existence of the things that are not.").
      BUT dreams and illusions are false perceptions;  so  far  from  saying
that everything is what appears to every man,  we  should  rather  say  that
nothing is which appears. Furthermore, how can we determine whether  now  we
are dreaming or awake? So the reality of sense is  easily  doubted.  And  if
everything is what appears to each man, why should Protagoras be  considered
wiser than us ignoramuses? Finally, if seeing is  knowing,  and  the  person
who remembers knows what he or she remembers without  seeing,  then  knowing
is not seeing, that is,  perceiving,  which  as  an  impossible  consequence
disqualifies the thesis.
       However,  Socrates  admits  that  Protagoras  could  say  that   each
perception is true to the perceiver and nonetheless that  there  are  better
and worse perceivers capable of educating others  into  better  (not  truer)
perceptions. So he wants now to discuss with Theodorus rather than  with  an
inexperienced youth like Theaetetus. The fact that everybody thinks  himself
wiser than others in some things and their  inferior  in  others,  and  that
people look for masters in certain things shows that  ignorance  and  wisdom
exist, at least in their opinion.
            One consequence of Protagoras' position is that he  acknowledges
that his own opinions are false, for he admits that the opinions of all  men
are true, and many would believe that Protagoras'  opinions  are  false.  So
his opinion that man is the measure of  all  things  is  false,  because  it
appears false to molst p[eoplke. And Protagoras even acknowledges the  truth
of the opinio who is believed false by the one who  holds  it.  Further,more
Protagoras has to allow that neither a dog  nor  any  ordinary  man  is  the
measure of anything which he has not learned.
      DIGRESSION about the clever slave-lawyer who works against  the  clock
in court and whose speeches are utilitarian and  the  free-phiolosopher  who
is guided only byy the search for the truth and despises the temporal.
      Application of Protagoras' views to the just and good: the  ordinances
which a state commands and thinks just are just to the state  which  imposes
them, while they are in force. But orinances refer to the  future.  And  the
future cannot be known with certainty, but the  previsions  of  experts  are
more reliable than the  previsions  of  those  who  have  no  expertise.  So
everyone is not the best judge of what will happen and seem to  him  in  the
future. So there are wiser people than others, and the  wiser's  opinion  is
the measure, byut Socrates' opinion is  the  measure  of  nothing.  However,
Socrates acknowledges that contingent matters are matters of mere  opilnion,
not of knowledge, and therefore every opinion may be unassailable.
      But let us give a ring to Heracleitus'  view  of  the  universal  flux
which is makking rapid strides in Ionia and which is as old as  Homer.  "The
nature of motion appears to be the quesstion with which to  begin.  What  do
they mean when they say that all things are in motion?  Is  there  only  one
kind of motion?
      Local motion is one kind. Growing old (generation and  extinction,  or
"change") another.
      Those who say that all is motion would say that everything moves  both
ways., for otherwise things would be both in motion and at rest.  But  since
white is changing into another color, can the name  of  any  color  be  used
rightly? Or that we see something rather than nmothing?  But  if  everything
changes, then every answer upon whatever subject is equally  right.  In  the
hypothesis of perpetual flux knowledge is not perceptioin.
      Now let us discuss Parmenides'  doctrine  that  "all  is  one  and  at
rest," for whom Socrates has reverence. This will furnish an opportunity  to
reexamine Theaetetus' view that knowledge is perception.
      The senses are the subject's senses, who perceives through  them.  But
there is qlso the mind that by  herself  grasps  or  judges  that  color  is
unlike sound--sameness and difference, good and evil, being  and  non-being.
All this the mind perceives by comparing in herself things past and  present
with the future. Through the skenses I perceive hard, but not their  being--
t5hat they are. So "knowledge does not consist in impressions of sense,  but
in reasoning about them; in that only, and not  in  mere  impression,  truth
and being can be attained." "Perception has no part  in  the  attainment  of
truth, since  it  does  not  attain  to  being."  So  perception  cannot  be
knowledge; only thinking or opining--mind's operations- can.  Howeever,  not
all opinion: only true opinion
      BUT we say that some opinions are false  and  others  true,  and  that
some things are known and others unknown. Now, he who has  a  false  opinion
has to know that to which his opinion refers;  so  he  knows  and  does  not
know. And if all thing are either known or unknown, how can he form a  false
opinion?
      False opinion is heterodoxy: false belief (or predication), saying  to
oneself that one thing is another.
      BUT no one who thinks two objects will say  taht  one  is  the  other.
Thus false opinion isnot heterodoxy.
      False opinion can be heterodoxy in just two cases:  when  I  idkentify
something I keep in memory whith something I see from a distance, or when  I
see something clearly but misidentify with something I remember vaguely.
      So there is true opinion and false opinion.
      BUT false opinion cannot after all be  explained  as  a  confusion  of
thought (memory) and sense, for in that case we would  never  been  mistaken
about thoughts; yet there are people who make the mistake in  adding  six  +
seven = 12, and 6 ad 7   are  thoughts.  Thus,  either  false  o-inion  does
notexist, or a person does not know what she knows.
      Another try: "To know" means to possess knowledge. We  take  "possess'
in the sense of having available but not necessarily actually using, as  one
has different birds in an aviary. The birds are kinds of knowledge, and  the
aviary was empty at  birth.  A  man  cannot  not  possess  a  bird  that  he
possesses, but he may get a false opinion about it, for wishing  to  capture
a certain sort of knowledge he may take the wrong bird by mistake, but  when
he catches the on e he wants then he has true opinion..
      BUT how can a man who knows be ignorant of what he knows?
      Another try: Right opinion with rational definition or explanation  is
the most perfect form  of  knowledge;  without  reason,  that  is,  rational
definition or explanation, an opinion is not  knowledge,  or  at  least  not
perfect knowledge.And things of which there is no rational account  are  not
knowable. And explanation or reason is nlot the enumeration of the  elements
(a car is wheels and an axe...), but the ability to tell the  mark  or  sign
of difference which distinguishes the thing in question from all  others.And
he who has right opinion about somethingk and can  find  what  distinguishes
it from other things comes to know that of  which  before  he  only  had  an
opinion.
      BUT if right opinion is not grasp  of  what  distinguishes  the  thing
then it is not opinion about that about which it is opinion.

But at least you know that you do not know. These are the limits of my  art.
