DIRECT AND DELIBERATIVE PRAXIS

My set of problems here derives from "\wp\monas\Practica," particularly pp 7-22. My central question is the

puzzling nature of Aris's Ethics , which is a work composed of sentences and arguments which demand assent and purports nonetheless to be a practical work that is meant to result in action.

From the pages alluded to above I have come to grasp that the solution to the puzzle may lie in distinguishing for the agent's case between deliberative and not deliberative action, and for the ethicist's case between the agent engaged in action and the ethicist who thinks "about" general human desideranda "from" his/her experience as agent . It is important to remark that because of the human intelligence involved in human action and because of the sedimentation of past experience that becomes constitutive of what the agent is, the first of these distinctions may not be as tight as it appears conceptually, for deliberation can be sedimented and may not be necessary at the time.

(May the Ethics be a case of legislative deliberation (with its correspondent 'rhetoric')?

On p. 19 of "Practica" I formulated my target as explaining to myself " the difference between the agent's knowledge and the reflective knowledge involved in Aris's Ethics ." Whether the latter knowledge is reflective or not, or in what sense, remains to be determined. What seems clear is that it is different from the agent's knowledge. This is the point. How? And why?

Deliberation and pressure

Deliberation is an intelligent weighing of factors that demands time. The British captain who abandoned his ship lacked it; his judges had it.

Deliberation, in the sense I understand it here, is not a merely calculative, cold, objective process to determine the efficient means. As Aris says (see Beiner, p. 92: "no one deliberates about things that cannot be other than they are"), no one deliberates about what is fully knowable; the agent's deliberation is about the future which is never fully known. Deliberation involves orexic or desiderative knowledge, knowledgeable desire. The British captain deliberated (fast and under pressure), informed by the conflictive desire to save his ship and to save his men's lives and his own.

Deliberation may take place under pressure of urgency because of lack of time and of the importance of the stakes, or without either of those pressures, or under one of them only.

The British captain had both pressures; his critics only the second.

Related to the preceding entry, deliberation may be on the part of an involved agent who is directly affected by his/her decision, or by a someone who is not so affected by the effects of his/her decision. The British captain was existentially engaged in his decision; his critics were not.

Related to the preceding entry, deliberation may be prompted or even forced by a situation by which one is already and unavoidably faced, or be relatively spontaneous and free.

Deliberation and action

Aris says that "no one deliberates about things that cannot be other than they are" and that "deliberation is about action."

But is all action susceptible of deliberation?

No. Because some actions cannot be other than they are; this is the case with reflexes. It seems that only those actions that

involve "orexis" are susceptible of deliberation. However, the actions of animals are orexic, yet animals do not deliberate.

So are the actions of young children. And so are some of the actions of adult people, unless one assumes with Sartre that we are free even under the pincers of the torturer, which Aris rightly seems to reject. In short, one deliberates only about those actions that are under one's control, that can be determined by the agent's decision.

Decision (from de-cidere ) presupposes, I think, alternative courses of action available to the agent. This is what Sartre claims is available always to the agent, for the agent always can choose death rather than... In other words, the agent not only always can put the world in abeyance, but put himself as desirous in abeyance; he chooses to go along or not to go along with his "passion." On the other hand, Sartre acknowledges a certain "facticity" both in the world and even in the agent, and a certain  factual "coefficient of adversity" in the world which makes certain projects impossible because the means are not available. Under the pincers of the torturer I cannot deliberate whether or not to eat chocolate. Thus even Sartre has to admit that certain actions are not susceptible of deliberation for lack of available alternatives. No available alternatives, no deliberation-decision.

The availability of means which make alternatives available is relative to the agent. Connors can deliberate whether to attempt to participate in the Olympics or not; I cannot.

Deliberation and knowledge

Aris distinguishes theoretical from practical knowledge.

It seems that the fundamental difference is that theoretical knowledge is meant to terminate in contemplative assent whereas practical knowledge is meant to terminate in action. In the former case knowing amounts to assenting to what is the case; in the latter, to acting appropriately.

However, the difference between theoretical and practical knowledge is not merely dependent on the subject's intentions (on whether his purpose is to know or to act) but also object-dependent; certain objects, Aris suggests, don't lend themselves to theoretical knowledge. (Or perhaps they do not lend themselves to scientific knowledge for lack of regularity, but lend themselves to theoretical knowledge--unless Aris identifies scientific knowledge with theoretical knowledge, a point on which I believe he is ambiguous).

At this point Aris gets confusing. I believe he confuses universal and necessary knowledge (epistemé) with theoretical knowledge: since knowledge of particulars is not universal and necessary, particulars do not lend themselves to theoretical knowledge; and since particulars are always contingent, the knowledge of particulars is always contingent and cannot, therefore, be theoretical.

For Aris all universal and necessary knowledge is theoretical. Geometry is a case in point, for him. Furthermore, all practical knowledge is of the particular, here and now or then and there. Since episteme owes its absolute certitude to the fact that it results from essential demonstrations, and only episteme deserves fully the qualification of "knowledge," only theoretical knowledge can be knowledge; the rest is a matter of "opinion," real but less exact knowledge. Consequently, all practical knowledge is a matter of "opinion." Yet, Aris is not Plato. As Suzanne Mansion argues, opinion for Aris is not epistéme, yet it is not, as it is for Plato, non-knowledge because it is not knowledge of the Forms. Let us call episteme Knowledge with a capital K, and the non-universal-necessary knowledge knowledge with a small k. If practical knowledge is to be knowledge in any sense, it has to be "knowledge," for it is--or involves--knowledge of particulars. The question is whether all "knowledge" is ipso facto practical rather than theoretical, and whether all "Knowledge" is ipso facto theoretical. The first of the two questions is the one that interests me. Is all "knowledge" ipso facto practical, by Aristotle's book?

It seems to me that there is a real divergence between Owen and Nussbaum, and that it is related to this point. Owen holds that scientific knowledge (in our sense of scientific, not in Aris's), because it is scientific, differs from prudential knowledge; Nussbaum holds that scientific knowledge because it is revisable is prudential knowledge.

The introduction of "prudential knowledge" opens a more precise avenue. Scientific knowledge of the kind Owen speaks about may be "opinion" but is on the side of what Aris calls theoretical knowledge because of its strict universality grounded on its explanatory character; the knowledge of which Nussbaum speaks may be "opinion" but is on the side of what Aris calls practical knowledge because of its lack of strict universality and of its connection with action. And here a further distinction is in order: knowledge like the one involved in the case of De Motu Animalium is not epistemic, but is on the side of what Owen would call scientific knowledge for it is not practical in the way in which prudential knowledge is. By contrast, prudential knowledge is practical all the way. Another way of putting it: scientific knowledge is knowledge about; practical knowledge is knowledge about and from in view of action.

This is still pervaded by Aris's confusions. More clarity and precision are needed. Let us start with one Aristotelian confusion studied by S Mansion (pp. 119 ff).

Aris distinguishes science (epistéme) from opinion (doxa).

He grants that opinion is knowledge; he denies that it is scientific knowledge (epistéme). It is knowledge because it grasps being, reality; it is not epistéme because it does not grasp the essence that renders the relationship between subject and predicate necessary. "Si l'opinion nous montrait  la liasion essentielle entre homme et animal, elle serait déjà science" (mansion, p. 12).  Now, among what is contingent what "happens often" (hos epi to pollu) is to be classed. It forms an important part of "opinion." Yet Aris classes 'habitual, regular but not necessary facts' among the objects of epistéme. "How is it possible for the hos epi to pollu to be at the same time object of demonstration (of science) and of opinion, given that to episteton and to doxaston are irreducible to each other?"

(Mansion, p. 119).

Whether or not Mansion's or Régis's solutions to this aporia are valid, Aris himself did not propose those solutions, perhaps he did not even realize the problem. In short, Aris's views on scientific knowledge as different from opinion are equivocal on this point. Cannot equivocation be found in those of Aris's views that relate more directly to what concerns me?

Aris does not consider the contingent as possible object of episteme. Now, all prudential knowledge is knowledge of contingents. Hence, Aris, consistent with himself on this point, excludes prudential knowledge from scientific knowledge.

Biological regularities, I suppose, belong among hos to epi pollu, among the for the most part. Yet their knowledge consists in mere assent. This seems to me to be Owen's point. For her part, because their regularity, being only for the most part, cannot be essential, Nussbaum attempts to reduce them, or at least the method for setting the phenomena, to orexic knowledge.   I think she is wrong. In De Motu Animalium Aris' knowledge is about orexic knowledge, but is itself not orexic knowledge; here the setting of the phaenomenon is "scientific" in Owen's sense. By contrast,m practical knowledge, as in the Ethics, is itself orexic, and the setting of the phenomenon is different. Owen's point is that Aris, in his scientific treatises, does not follow the essential method. Nussbaum's point is that, because Aris does not follow the essential method in matters of orexic knowledge, there is no significant difference in the method for setting the phenomena between Aris's scientific treatises and his De Motu Animalium or his Ethics or even the engaged agent's knowledge.

LET US REDEFINE TERMS: (A) PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE

(A) Practical knowledge is knowledge informed by and at least partly dependent on desire; it is, therefore, orexic

- Prudential knowledge is deliberative knowledge. It is orexic, not merely objective. Animals do not and cannot deliberate because they do not choose their goals and their means; their goals and means are determined for them by instinct and present environment. Prudential knowledge always concerns the future. It consists in an invented project (not a mere dream, but not a pre-determined project either), and its point is to bring about the future chosen under the circumstances. As such, it can only be the deed of engaged agents in particular, concrete circumstances.

- Orexic knowledge is the non-deliberative knowledge of animals in which end-means are ore-determined by instinct, accumulated experience and present circumstances. It occurs in humans too, in overwhelming circumstances, when there is no possibility of putting the world and oneself in abeyance.

- Ethical knowledge , à la Aris, seems to be deliberative. Yet it does not result in a concrete decision to act, which is typical of prudential knowledge. The reason seems to be that in this case the deliberation is about general human desideranda given what people and the world are in relation to each other, from which particular goals in concrete circumstances are to be carved out. It presupposes a general pre-understanding of what is possible and impossible, probable and improbable (terms which can appear only to desirous agents) , given the way the world and humans are, as well as of the classes of goods humans generally desire, based on one's experience of desiring them and of interacting with others who desire them. The classes of goods generally desired are then contrasted to the classes of goods that ought to be desired in view of the individual and social nature of people and of the nature of the world in order to maximize the chances that people reach their full development as people compatible with the nature of the world, as well as the kind of transformed world that ought to be desired for the same purpose. - Is this knowledge practical? Only in a mediate rather than immediate way, and only if such reflection succeeds in according the desires of those who deliberate with the general human desideranda through persuasive argument , so that people actually begin making their concrete decisions according to the changed, more rational 'desires'. The difficulty here is that the subject who deliberates is the same subject who desires and has to put himself or herself in abeyance as desirous of certain definite goods, which is very difficult. This is why Aris demands good people or at least well trained people for this deliberation, which seems to create an unbreakable vicious circle. Indeed, if the education of the youth is already good it is because the republic or society is already informed by the general human desideranda to be reached through ethical deliberation; thus, the deliberation is not needed, or needed only as a confirmation, not as a re-education. If, on the other hand, the participants in the deliberation are not good, the human desideranda will not persuade them practically, only intellectually at best and they will be akratic as Aris suggest in his Ethics. (The solution, of course, is slow change favored by circumstances and ethical deliberation, as suggested in the last chapter of To Be Human) .

N. B. On Prudentia : The above shows that prudentia is an intellectual virtue heavily qualified by the general desires of the deliberating agent. What is considered as prudent, and who is considered as prudent, in a society like this one that maximizes the acquisition of material goods and the pursuit of pleasure, and shapes the desires of people accordingly, is very different from what would be considered prudent, and who would be deemed prudent, in a society informed by Christianity.

(B) THEORETICAL KNOWLEDGE

(B) Theoretical knowledge may be inspired or motivated by desire, but it is not in-formed by desire because it is at least partly independent from desire. In this sense, it is not orexic knowledge. What I mean can only be explained through examples and by contrast to orexic knowledge.

When knowledge is in-formed by desire it is codeterminant of object. The good-object is correlative of desire in such a way that if there is no desire there is no good-object. This is what I mean by determination of object. I speak of codetermination, because the determination is reciprocal, that is, is determination of subject by object, too. The good-object is desirable, makes itself desired, for without this there is no desire and, therefore, no good-object.

However, there can be "object" (I do not say good-object) without desire. And this brings us closer to theoretical knowledge. What I call "object" is what appears to a subject independent of his/her concrete desires because it is at least in a sense or partly independent from desire. And it seems that "object" can be either concrete or abstract, either an individual or a class, either identifying of explanatory.

At least in a sense or partly independent from desire. What does this mean? Suppose I am involved in deliberation for purposes of deciding, of choosing my course of action. Certainly the British captain had the general desire of saving his ship and the lives of his people. His problem "Can I save the ship?" arose from that general desire. Thus, the problem may not be considered

totally independent from desire. It is, however, independent from desire in a significant sense: whether or not there are means available to save the ship does not depend straightforwardly on desire. If it depended, the passionate desire of the captain would have created the means. Furthermore, some of the critics of the captain may have been pure naval technicians, with no desire one way or another. Yet they determined (that is, judged, not decided) that the ship could have been saved, and the captain did not disagree; he thought (at the time) that his critics were right. ( Whether the critics' knowledge was deliberative or not is important and to be considered later).

Now, this is a matter of 'objective knowledge,' of knowledge related to what I call "object." In this case, it is knowledge of a concrete particular, since the circumstances are unique and unrepeatable as such in all their complexity. This ship was salvageable under the circumstances because there were means to save it available. The captain could not possibly make saving the ship into his actual goal in spite of his general desire to save it, because he falsely judged that there were no available means and, therefore, that it was impossible at the time to save this ship. If he had judged otherwise, things would have been very different. A more intense desire may have prompted him to look more attentively and persistently at the matter, but the fact that the means were available did not depend on desire , his or anybody else's, as he implicitly acknowledged it by not disputing his critics' knowledge. This is what Sartre would call "facticity," the coefficient of favorableness or adversity which renders certain goals possible or impossible.

"The means are not available to save the ship." Is this a prudential judgment? Abstractly, that is, without considering the pressures of the situation, the captain was imprudent as a captain. Thus, in his case, the judgment was prudential, because it was in the context of a decision to be taken. But in the case of his critics it is not connected with deciding to save or to abandon the ship. They have no decision to make in this respect, no goal to pursue in action. Since prudence is intrinsically related to decision and action, the critics' judgment is not prudential. Yet it is the same judgment, so much so that if the captain's judgement had been the same as his critics' judgement the ship would have been saved by the captain. As Nussbaum would say, it is the same judgment minus desire (or will to act according to the judgment).

It is to be remarked that the same as for means obtains for goal. For if the goal is unattainable it cannot be chosen as a goal. On the other hand, a goal that is not chosen as a goal, although it is attainable, is not a goal.

"Objective" means at least not directly dependent on desire. But it means more; it means dependent on the way things are and things work, which may or may not be in agreement with the subject's desires. As said just above, both means (as available) and goals (as attainable) are objective in this sense. Yet one can know that a goal, that is, a certain state of affairs is concretely attainable or feasible without making it one's goal and intending to pursue it, and equally one can know that the means for attaining or realizing a certain state of affairs are available in a concrete circumstance without using them. Knowing that a nail can be sunken in such and such a wall with such a hammer available does not imply that one will sink the nail, and knowing that such a frame can be hung in that wall does not entail that one will want to hang such frame. But, on the other hand, one cannot determine as a goal to hang such frame if one does not know or believe that it is possible, and one cannot know or believe that it is concretely possible without knowing or believing that such are the means and that the means are available. Thus there seems to be a relative independence of the "objective," and a relative dependence of the pursuance of goals and the use of means on the "objective."

Why the independence of the objective is only relative? Because I believe that desire and object are co-determinant, reciprocally dependent. In other words, I believe in the primacy of action over contemplation. Contemplation is possible because there is need. If there were no need there would be no "objects."

We start with objects for response; from there we move to "objects." The world and need are correlates. Our carving objects for response out of our surroundings obtains thanks to need and in order to satisfy need. We are tied to the world primarily by need; curiosity is secondary. However, we humans are linguistic creatures. Not only is our carving of objects for response linguistically directed; language also allows us, when the demands of actual inter-action are taken care of and therefore neutralized, to speak "about."

We start by being spoken to: being called, saluted, scolded, ordered, etc. But there is also the language of statements, of being talked about and talking about present objects through names that designate (not "call") them.  The names work like hangers to hang predicates of the object on. Hellen Keller had the experience of water as object for response. What he acquired with the name "water" was a hanger-about which allowed her to speak about the object and to learn about the object through others' experience, whether or not the object was present. Names are proper and common, depending on whether they are used to designate a particular or a class. Thus we learn to designate and make the difference between what is common to many and what is distinctive of one. Initially the names are concrete, like "mom", "dog"; later they can be abstract, like "motherhood."

Animals can come to 'know' (in the sense of carving out) only objects of fear or desire, that is, insofar as they are related to them as helpful or harmful to them, and only in their presence or in their imminent presence revealed through signs. Their generalization of experience is automatic and Humean: from objects that look and smell and sound similar they expect--that is, react to--similar effects. If dogs could speak, they would be able to speak about objects only as present or imminent (for a dog the name of its master is sign of its imminent presence to which it responds by wagging its tail), and only as desirable and harmful to them as a species. The language of statements makes it possible for us to speak about not only in presence or imminent presence but in absence and without reference to their presence or absence, because it makes it possible for us to speak about not only in terms of our exposure to them as desirable or harmful for us as individuals or as members of the human species but 'in themselves, in terms of their own activity and passivity. This is the totally objective language of curiosity .

A distinction has been implicitly suggested between objects of curiosity and objects of concern, between objects in themselves and objects for response, between objects as helpful or harmful to us and objects independently of our needs, between the order of exposure and the order of contemplation. Animals are confined within the latter and unlinguistically, that is, without the ability to objectify their objects of concern to learn from the experience of other members of their species except by their immediate reactions in presence or imminent presence, much less to understand 'why' their objects for response are harmful or helpful and, therefore, to control their harmfulness or helpfulness. Thanks to our linguistic ability with all this includes, we people are not confined within the order of exposure.

How are the order of exposure and the order of contemplation related?

Within the order of exposure, that is, considering objects in their relation to us as harmful or helpful, our linguistic objectifying ability allows us to abstract from presence or absence and therefore to focus on the object without having to respond to it. The objects that constitute this order are the 'natural objects' of our 'cultural world': telephones, trees, mountains, cars, people, mother, books. All these objects are concrete, that is, capable of being encountered in interaction when we are exposed to them and they are exposed to our action, though we may be considering them abstractly--not as this object in this situation of encounter, but as a class, in terms of their species-activity and their possible effects on us in our species-passivity. In other words, all these objects are objects of our possible experience as interactive agents-patients.

However, we may also consider these objects "in themselves," not in terms of their harmfulness or helpfulness to us. We have shifted to the order of contemplation. We are guided by curiosity, by the pure desire to know them, to learn about them. Nonetheless, if these objects are objects for which there are names in our natural-cultural language and they are taken in our culture as objects of our possible interactive experience (for instance, in our culture the snowman is not such an object but a merely imaginary one like Hamlet), these objects are the same as those we considered in the order of exposure. The predicates of the object may not be identical in the two orders, but the subject of the predicates is the same, that is, is the same object of possible interactive experience, although not considered in terms of this but rather 'in itself.'

----------------------------------------------------------------- November 13, 1991

The question is: are these objects really the same?

Compare De Motu Animalium to the case of the captains critics. In De motu Aris wants to understand and explain, which for him amounts ideally to the knowledge of essential connections. He is dealing with an object as a class of objects and for explanatory purposes. The captains critics dealt with a particular in a particular situation, which is unrepeatable and, therefore, not reducible to a class. They did systematically and in detail, and taking time, what the experienced captain had to compute in an instant. But they did the same. Yet they considered it in terms of its pure intelligibility, not of its desirability. Is this a significant difference? In any case, there seems to be a difference. When the agent considers possible means, he weighs them in terms of their desirability. A general may decide to attack a city if he has to sacrifice half of his men, but not if he will have to lose 90% of them. In the latter case, taking the city is unattainable for this general, whereas in purely technical terms and for someone not concerned about the price to be paid, it would be attainable and the means available. This is why the critics judgment was not a prudential judgment, since they did not have to decide and bear responsibility for their choice and its consequences. There is therefore a difference between technical judgment and prudential judgment. But is the difference significant for my purposes?

It would be extremely significant if the difference implied that the object for response and for curiosity is not the same.

However, we cannot simply say that, because in that case the captain could not have agreed with his critics' judgement and acknowledged that his own judgment about the same object was wrong, nor would a different judgment-decision informing the captain's action have made a difference. Yet, as we just concluded, there is a difference between technical judgment and prudential judgment. Can we sharpen the difference through analysis?

Take the case of the man hanging on a branch and of the bag of potatoes. The question whether the branch will brake is the same and can be answered equally concerning the man and the bag, and can be answered more precisely and accurately by an engineer who desires nothing than by the man hanging on the branch. However, for the engineer's case, this presupposes certain human desires that carve out from the environment certain objects and create the demand for engineers. It also presupposes a certain general professional goal of acting as an engineer under the appropriate circumstances. The same with physicians and biologists. The biologist will identify the virus. The physician will respond to the virus given his pre-existent goal of curing the patient if the virus is identified and the means to do away with it are available. They all are dealing with objects for response, but not all of them in situation of encounter where all the concrete circumstances are to be taken into account and where prudential knowledge enters exclusively. For the biologist the virus is object for curiosity; for the physician, object for response under concrete circumstances. Is the virus the same for both?  

For the biologist the virus is relatively abstract--a supposedly representative case under laboratory conditions; for the physician it is a concrete object of response under present conditions--killing the virus is the necessary means to achieve the goal of restituting the patient's health. The biologist's analysis does not dispense the physician from deliberative judgment about how to respond, whereas the biologist's judgment is clearly not deliberative for it is not about action but, at best, about the conditions for action given a certain goal. Yet the physician's judgement, in the case at stake, does not seem to be genuinely deliberative either; though it is reached in a more intuitive way than the biologist's judgment and involves a lot more factors than the biologist's about the general state of this patient (for example, a certain drug that works cannot be used because the patient has diabetis), it may not be deliberative but purely technical, as the biologist's judgment. A computer is conceivable that would factor in all the factors factored in subconsciously by the physician and would do an even better job, and the physician would automatically apply the results to the case. In other words, concerning the means and their availability as well as the objective possibility of the goal there is a sort of judgment in which desire is irrelevant, in which the subjective and the objective aspects are separable. In this regard, Nussbaum would be right: a goal is knowledge + desire; technical and deliberative knowledge are of the same kind, and Aris's explanation is the same for both.

   Yet, I am not convinced that that is the case. As Aris says, we do not deliberate about what is certain, that is, known with certitude. The efficiency of the means, at least in the abstract , is not object of deliberation. In this case, the present determines the future, at least hypothetically, if the means are used. And, as Aris explains concerning the practical syllogism, the means are necessarily used if the goal is set. However, Aris also says that deliberation is about action and nothing else. Since action for the purpose of bringing about a desired state of affairs is always future, action is never certain. Now, what we are saying implicitly is that, given a certain goal and technical knowledge of the world, action becomes automatic, so that the point is to know the world in order to have scientific action. Of course, Aris would grant that there are degrees of certitude. If my goal is to build a house, the calculation of the triangular surface on which it is to be built is automatically to be according to Euclidean procedures. And so on in other more complex cases like whether the branch is going to break, like whether the virus is of one kind or another and to what it responds favorable or unfavorably. The certitude would not be the same in every case, but the application in action would be equally automatic.

I start again: Yet I am not conviced.

Aris says that we do not deliberate about what is certain, and that the [only?] thing we deliberate about is action.

If so, action has to be uncertain. What is the uncertainty about?

It seems that the point of deliberating is making persuasively certain to oneself and to others what is initially uncertain. This is why, I believe, we do not deliberate about what is certain. For instance, we do not deliberate whether a person is going to be harmed if machinegunned. Now, the biologist in the identification of a virus and the physician in the diagnosis of an illness are often initially uncertain and move to the certainty possible in their fields. Is this deliberation?

An analogy. Any action presupposes a certain state of affairs known. If there is no fire in the ship, deliberation about extinguishing the fire or abandonning the ship because of the fire is impossible. If I hear a sound in my home at night as 'just one of those sounds' I go on sleepng; if I hear it as "someone is in the house" I proceed ccordingly, to avoid getting mugged or deprived of my property. I try to as-certain the most efficient means, calculating the other's possible moves. Since, like a general, I am not dealing with the purely physical order that can be dealt with technically, but only strategically, like in a chess game, the calculation cannot be mathematical, algorythmic. In short, as Aris concedes, the degree of certainty varies with fields and cases. The degree of certainty of what? Of the present state of affairs? Of the efficiency of the means?

The captain was certain that there was fire. This did not need to be ascertained. The physician needs to ascertain the illness. Now, as Sartre says, the state of affairs by itself is incapable of motivating the action, however certain it may be.

On the other hand, there can be no action but in reference to a given state of affairs. It seems that one of the questions I am asking is whether the state of affairs for the agent and for the

objective contemplator is the same. Another question is whether the ascertaining of the state of affairs is deliberative in the relevant sense. Is the relevant sense to be equted with prudential judgment? Are judgments about past performnce prudential?

(1) Is the object the same for the agent and for the objective contemplator?

When I ask whether the object is the same I am speaking of the object that 'causes' the response, not of the future state of affairs to be brought about (the goal).

Returning to Sartre, no (present) state of affairs is in and of itself capable of 'causing' action. Why not? Because a state of affairs as such does not reveal its goodness or badness but to someone who confronts it in situation of interaction, whether in actuality or in imagination, that is, to someone whose needs have not been neutralized. What is revealed, however, is the goodness or badness of the state of affairs , or its coefficient of adversity or favorableness if a goal has already been chosen. It seems, therefore, to be the same state of affairs. What the state of affairs is conditions on the object's side the possibility of the action through which the goal would be attainable, and what the state of affairs is does not depend on the subject's desire.

Now, what a state of affairs is is what a state of affairs is "known" to be. What a possible state of affairs is (concerning the goal) is what a possible state of affairs is known to be. And what a possible means is in relation to a goal is what a possible means is known to be. In other words, it presupposes judgment, though not prudential but technical. In the case of the agent, though, the two are inseparable or, rather, the judgment is prudential all the way. This is so not only because the agent has to factor in all the concrete circumstances into the equation, but because he is involved in the equation as part of the equation. His fears, his desires, his strength are active in the equation as constitutives of the equation.

Is the above correct? Is the agent's judgment prudential all the way? Not sure. It is probably true that if we were not exposed we would never identify objects. But, judging by their response, animals identify objects selectively and according to the standards of the species to which they belong--only objects for response, depending on the species. We humans, by contrast, can talk about objects, that is, identify them independently from our desires. "A man in the house" is not the result of a prudential judgment, it is independent from whether I or anybody desires it. True, it is object for response in situation of exposure and interaction. But it can be such only because someone is actually exposed to it under actual conditions.

Carthago can become delenda only for someone who has the needs and desires of the Romans, and servanda only for someone who has the needs and desires of the Carthaginians. But Carthago is objectively delenda only if the Romans can destroy it, for only in that case can the Romans choose to destroy it. And this does not depend on the Romans' desire--at least not exclusively--but on the power of the Carthaginians and on the relative power of the Romans. That USA could subdue Vietnam and that Vietnam could not subdue USA was true in the sense of being a proposition that demanded assent rather than dissent. This had nothing to do with desire; it depended on an objective consideration of the relative strengths of USA and Vietnam and on experience of the way things work. But this is an abstract proposition. Vietnam will become concretely and objectively delenda only if the USA mke it their goal to subdue Vietnam. Can-propositions are always abstract; will-propositions are concrete. Can-propositions take into account only the objective aspects in an abstract, congealed present; will-propositions combine the objective and the subjective aspects in concrete presence. Will- propositions depend on can-propositions as their condition of possibility; can-propositions do not depend on will-propositions.

The last sentence above brings a delicate point concerning the primacy of the dimension of exposure . It is true that we humans can objectify, that is, liberate ourselves from the immediate pulsion of presence and need, to consider the object 'in itself.' But this is an abstraction effected on the basis of exposure. In order to reach can-propositions we have to have encountered the object by reference to which the can will be predicated. Thus identification of object is an abstraction from practical identification of object for response. All the proper and common concrete names (not abstract, like patriotism, slavery) in a dictionary are sedimented identifiers of real or imaginary objects for response. They are available to the members of the culture that generated the dictionary as objects of consideration and subjects of can-predication only because they have been identified in situation of encounter as objects for actual or future potential response. Thus if we were not exposed and had no needs there would be no objects of consideration and subjects of can-predication.

What is the importance of this? Let us take "this ship afire." Only because fire is threatening, fearful, can fire become object for curiosity and consideration: what it does, how it works, to what it responds. Same with "virus." Thus can-propositions depend on is-propositions, which are also abstract.

But is-propositions depend on desire. They depend not as to their actual content but as to their emergence as objects for consideration. And their content is delineated by implicit reference to human needs and desires. If so, this is important because it shows that all knowledge is, ultimately, interested and practical.

Is-propositions concerning singular, concrete matters of fact state what an exposed agent in a given situation would have to respond to or to take into account as relevant for his action, independently from his particular goals and as a condition of possbility for his particular goals.

Is-propositions concerning universal (abstract) 'natures'

render intelligible constant ways of acting of objects of encounter independently from circumstances . Thus they ground possibilities of controlling that action on the object's side.

In short, they potentially ground can-propositions on the object's side. Potentially, because the means may not be available in general (for the moment we cannot cure aids), or not available to the agent under the circumstances.

Let us consider this "on the object's side." The general background is anthropocentric and interested, that is, the exposed, needful human agent. Exposure indicates object of exposure. Unlike animals, we can think, that is, invent our action. Culture, at least in one of its aspects, is sedimented past invented action and its conditions. But we can think both in present exposure and in absent exposure. Let us concentrate on thinking in absent exposure , which involves objectification. Objectification consists in abstracting an element or a segment of the whole from the rest to consider it by itself. Objectifying thinking my be functional (about the world encountered) or reflective (about the encounterer). Let us concentrate on functional thinking . Since it abstracts from the rest, from the circumstantial, it renders visible the common and regular--the always or the for the most part. Since it concentrates on the encountered rather than on the encounterer, it renders visible the not subjective (sensing, feeling, needing, desiring, thinkig...) and invisible the subjective. Since it is in absent exposure, it renders visible the by itself and invisible the

desired. This yields what I called "on the object's side."

However, the encounterer may also be object of thought , of consideration. Materially (not under its proper formality but under the formality of encountered) it falls under functional thinking. Formally (under the formality of encounterer, of subject of encounter) it falls under reflective thinking, which we are now considering .  By encounterer formally or as such I mean the human being as agent-patient, as exposed. At this point, however, we have to be prticularly careful to avoid common confusions.

Functional thinking is inherently controlling. We objectify and think functionally, not out of idle curiosity but in order

to be in control as much as possible by foreseeing the activity of the encountered and by manipulating it in view of our purposes if possible. Now, human beings are among the objects encountered by the human beings who think functionally. This is why human beings  fall materially under the formal object of functional thinking.

The first temptation here is to divide the human being into two halves: the objective half, identified with the body, which is observable and whose regularities are physical and physiological, and the subjective half (sensations, desires, feelings, thoughts...) identified with the soul, the mind, or the spirit. Once this is done, the second temptation is to attempt to understand and treat the subjective half as the physical and physiological objects are treated, as determined by 'psychological' laws. (This is what introspectionism did). Finally, when this attempt fails, as it is bound to fail, the third temptation is to do away with the subjective aspects, to reduce human beings to their behavior, and to understand and treat their behavior as determined by behavioral laws. (That is what behaviorism does).

Clearly, "the first temptation" is the fundamental one, for it leads to the other two. What is wrong with it?

The proper subject matter of reflective thinking is the human being as encounterer , not as encountered in presence, much less as considered in absence (as object of thought). What characterizes the encounterer as such is actual exposure as center of exposure, as subject of exposure with some initiative. However, we have so far been considering unsituated thinking in absent exposure. By its very nature this thinking is objectifying, that is, constitutes what is thought into abstract object, into grammatical object of objective general predicates. Now, when the object thought is not subject of exposure, the objective predicates of the abstract object, if true, are particular predicates of the concrete object, though they do not exhaust its concrete identity for an encounterer. When, however, the object of thought is (and functions as) subject of exposure, no objective thinking can capture that; in other words, no objective thinking can capture the encounterer as such. And if it attempts to, it results in silly psychologism of the kind practiced by Descartes.

Our query is what is wrong with the notion of "the two halves." Sartre might help us here . His positive insight is that the encounterer is revealed to himself in the experience of inter-action. His negative insight is expressed as follows:

"The problem of the body and its relations with consciousness is often obscured by the fact that while the body is from the start posited as a certain thing having its own laws and capable of being defined from outside, consciousness is then reached by the type of inner intuition which is peculiar to it. Actually if after grasping 'my' consciousness in its absolute interiority and by a series of reflective acts, I then seek to unite it with a certain living object composed of a nervous system, a brain, glnds, digestive, respiratory, and circulatory organs whose very matter is capable of being analyzed chemically, etc., then I am going to encounter insurmountable difficulties. But these difficulties all stem from the fact that  try to unite my consciousness not with my body but with the body of others . In fact the body which I have just described is not my body as it is for me . I have never seen and never shall see my brain nor my endocrine glands" (BN p. 401).

What Sartre criticizes here is the idea of "the two halves." Their mistake, according to him, is to glue the reflected experience of the subject to the body observed, which is not his own body but the Other's body. Sartre is confused here in important ways, but his insight seems to me valid concerning "the two halves." What introspection supposedly grasps is not the encounterer at all, but a pseudo-object. As if this were not enoughas a mistake, this pseudo-object, for explanatory purposes, is then joined to the body 'observed' (that is, understood by the physiologist for explanatory purposes) and constituted into a picture of the human being. When this fails, as it has to--particularly if the standards are those of empiricism--the result is behaviorism.

I return to Sartre's positive insight in order to see whether he furnishes the alternative to objectifyig thinking I am looking for.

The encounterer as such is revealed to himself in the experience of interaction. Two precisions: in the passive experience of interaction with the Other , what is revealed to me is my objectivity for the Other which (under certain conditions of agreement in interpretation on my part and of power on me on the Other's part) I have to assume as mine; that is, I am exposed as object to the Other subject. In other words, what is revealed to me is together my objectivity for the Other's subjectivity (and my passive subjectivity). Secondly, in the active experience of interaction with the Other , what is revealed to me is the Other exposed to me subject. In other words, what is revealed to me is my subjectivity and the Other's objectivity for my subjectivity. (Notice, however, two things: 1) I experience, in the sense of suffering, my own passive subjectivity, but not the Other's passive subjectivity. 2) Since the Other has a certain hold on my subjectivity through my objectivity--by torturing me with his pincers the torturer can extract from me the confession I want to deny--, objectivity and subjectivity reveal themselves as mere abstractions; concretely they are one and the same, two sides of the same coin.

Let us not get lost. Sartre is reflecting. His subject is abstract and objectified. It amounts to an objectification of the sedimented understanding of the subject as having objects exposed to him as subject and as being exposed to other subjects as object.

Furthermore, Sartre is reflecting, objectifying, and abstracting because his purpose is explanatory; he wants to know, not to act. He wants "a proof" of the Other's existence. In this sense, he remains Cartesian. He is using the agent's sedimented understanding of himself related to the world and of the world related to himself not as the agent 'uses' it by being it , but as a thinker who wants to know.

Still in addition, Sartre divorces totally my body from the body I present to the physiologist or the physician, as if the latter were not my body at all.

In short, Sartre may be of help in that he retrieves the agent's sedimented understanding, but we cannot follow him in his further explanatory undertaking which is as a-temporal and 'essential' as all functional thinking is.

Can we do better than Sartre?

There is a sense in which Sartre, unlike the Cartesians, retrieves the dimension of exposure, which is the agent's dimension and the one within the figures of good and bad, of avertendum and amplectendum, can be inscribed. This is a gain. How does Sartre escape the Cartesian two halves"? By not objectifying and not abstracting but, rather, considering the subject in situation of encounter.

However, the subject is in situation of encounter only when he encounters and under the conditions under which he encounters, not when he is seen or considered and understood as encountering.

The subject--the agent-patient--encounters from his past, in his present, and toward his future. Sartre's reflective thinking on the encountering subject a-temporalizes the encounterer and the encountered, as all objectifying thought does. The encounterer grasped by Sartre in reflection is not encountering encounterer, but encounterer seen by Sartre. To this seen encounterer (who is not encountering encounterer) the encountered as encountered cannot be united for the same reasons that the seen body cannot be united to the introspected psyche, much less to the encountering encounterer.

We might see more clearly these points if we take our clue from the notion of behavior.

In a way, the notion of behavior is not Cartesian. Descartes is a 'substantialist': two totally different substances that support totally different attributes--body, and mind; res extensa and res inextensa; unthinking and thinking substance. Behavior is not to be identified with the Cartesian body. If it is observable, it is observable as a temporal process, not as a substance.

Now let us consider Skinner's case (which coincides with Watson). The origin of "Behaviorism" is evidently Cartesian. It is a reaction against "Introspectionism," which is evidently dualistic in a Cartesian way. The study of the human being insofar as humans have bodies belongs to anatomy and physiology; the study of the mind, the "res non-extensa" belongs to psychology. This res non-extensa manifests itself through consciousness, that is, through phaenomena of which the subject is conscious, though they are accessible only to introspection, not to extrospection or plain observation as res extensae are.

Dueto his scientism and its accompanying radical empiricism Skinner rejects the mind. He, therefore, is left only with the res extensa. In this sense, Skinner clearly is a descendant of

Descartes. However, what Skinner calls behavior cannot be simply identified with the body, for anatomy and physiology also are concerned with the body and yet they are not concerned withwhat Skinner calls 'behavior.' What, then, is behavior?

We may roughly say that both physiology and behaviorism study what Descartes called the body, the human res extensa. But while physiology studies the functioning of the body from the skin in, behaviorism studies the functioning of the body from the skin out, not the dynamic interactions of its parts (as physiology does), but the dynamic relations between the observable body as a whole and its environment. This behavioral body, so to say, to distinguish it from the physiological body, behaves--that is, functions--as it does in part because of hereditary reactions to certain stimulants from the environment, and in part because of acquired conditionings fixated by the consequences that follow the behaviors.

The distinction between the physiological body and the behavioral body is highly unstable. Take, for instance, the case of the basic physiological reflexes, such as crying. The same phenomenon, namely, crying, is supposedly explained in its totality by both the physiologist and the behaviorist.  

It is important to remark that both the physiological body and the behavioral body are abstractions. Supposedly both are about the same body. Yet the body about which they predicate is formally different: the body's functions considered in relation to its parts, and the body's functions considered in relation to its surroundings. Moreover, both are explanatory in purpose, inspired by the desire to know why, to know about the body.

As being a body, I can apply to my own body that knowledge insofar as I find it persuasive; but that knowledge is not from the point of view of the body I am, but from the point of view of somebody else observing and understanding my body. We assume that these pieces of knowledge, since all of them are predicated of the body, are to be added up an will result in knowing the totality of the body, that is, the body as a totality, as a whole composed of a physiology and a behavior. In other words, we assume that our abstractions, that is, our objectifications for explanatory purposes, are as such real, and that the sum total of our objectifications constitute the real object. If to this objectified body we add the mind, the resulting 'reality' is 'a ghost in a machine'. Notice that all these explanatory objectifications are a-temporal.

One reason to doubt that the case can be as we assume it to be is that the three types of explanation sometimes are presented as if each were an exhaustive explanation and therefore exclusive of the others as not being what is the real case. For example, crying will be explained physiologically as the result of certain physiological changes in the organism; given those changes the phenomenon of crying is exhaustively explained and renders superfluous any other explanation. In its turn, the behavioral explanation will be that the body cried because the environment made it cry. Finally, the ghost will claim that the body cried because the ghost felt sad. Insofar as, because of its empiricist prejudices, behaviorism excludes the mind as an explanatory entity, the behavioral explanation excludes the consciential one.

And insofar as the consciential explanation portends to be the adequate explanation, it excludes the behavioral one. Thus, at least in certain cases, the explanations are incompatible and mutually exclusive; the reality, therefore, that they intend to portray cannot be at the same time as each of the explainers portrays it.

What is the mistake here? Sartre claims that we have joined the obectified behavioral body with my consciousness (mind) by accepting the predicates of the objectified behavioral body as if they applied to the body I am conscious (of), to the body I live and am. There seems to be something to Sartre's claim, but it is not quite satisfactory; for though he says that he does not want to deny the body of phisiology, he offers no possible way of uniting 'my-body-for-me' and 'my-body-for-the Other' with the physiological body, much less with the Skinnerian behavioral body of which Sartre's explanation is definitely exclusive. Again, can we do better than Sartre in detecting the flaws of the view of the body and of reality we are presently considering?

Deliberation and reflection

Is it true that deliberation involves detachment?

Deliberation is guided by desire. He who desires nothing has nothing to deliberate about. And it is not about prima facie desire but about willful desire, that is, knowledgeable desire.

Let us unpack this.
