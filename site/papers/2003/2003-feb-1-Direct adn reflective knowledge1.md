DIRECT AND REFLECTIVE KNOWLEDGE

The ‘judgment’ of which Aristotle speaks –the intellectual act to which alone he attributes validity as knowledge–is a reflective act. It presupposes abstraction from the sensible phantasm, a concept or definition, and the sentence passed on the validity of the definition as conforming on the basis of sufficient reason for affirming or denying the conformity of the defined to reality, in which judgment properly called consists. For Aristotle, who as a philosopher the certainty of scientific knowledge (see II Posteri0r Analytics , lectio 19) is of fundamental importance in order to distinguish knowledge from opinion, the reflective character of judgment is essential.

In strictly scientific judgment, though, the case of the truth of the judgment is straightforward and simple, at least for Aristotle, because the object of such judgment is a ‘nature,’ that is, a reality that is universal and necessary –a genuine epistaton or object of epsiteme. However, the case of a sensible judgment, that is, of a judgment whose object is not intelligible but sensible and, therefore, not scientifically knowable is thornier. Aristotle solves it

by accepting the probable material truth of the pereceptual phantasm and making this the object of the reflective judgment as to the conditions under which the phantasm was obtained. It he conditioins were adequate, the affirmative judgment will follow and its content will be accepted as ture or conform to reality.
