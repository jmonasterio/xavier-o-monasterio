Stories November 6, 2006

“What modern philosophy understands by faith is what properly is called an opinion, or what is loosely called in everyday speech believing [a belief one has in one’s mind]. Christianity is made into a [stated] doctrine, and the next step is to comprehend this doctrine, and that is what philosophy does. On the whole, this is quite right, in case Christianity were a doctrine; but since it is not that, it is a crazy proceeding” (S. Kierkegaard, The Offence).

HISTORY AND STORIES

The literary genre of the Gospels is narrative because it tells dramatic events. That is, events told from the standpoint of those who participated in them, those whose actions were constitutive of the events told and who were affected by the events in which they were involved as participants.

Think of the eruption of a volcano when it happens to people and when there are no people involved. In the former case, if the scene is expressed in words, it will necessarily be the object of a narration, however dull from the literary point of view; in the latter case, the eruption will be object of a mere description, however vivid.

Events are told by narrating them, not by stating them, because events are dramatic. I.e. the participants act in view of an outcome that matters to them.

The literary genre of a textbook of geometry is different from that of the Gospels, because, among other things, a textbook of geometry is not narrative and is non-dramatic. and it is not narrative and not dramatic because it doesn’t narrate events that happen to people, events in which people participate. The same is the case with “scientific” treatises, be they historical, or philosophical, or theological, whose purpose is to attain knowledge.

The Gospels narrate not mere facts, but what happened to people when Jesus happened to them, that is, when people actually encountered Jesus and were involved in the event.

The members of the “Jesus Seminar” mistake the Gospels’s dramatic narratives for statements of facts to be understood and believed, that is, assented to. Now, assent may be the appropriate response to statements, as Aristotle thinks, but it is not the appropriate response to narrated events. The people involved in the events narrated in the Gospels were not confronted with facts to be understood, like  Kantian “phenomena.” The appropriate response to what they perceived is action, as what is perceived  is in our everyday life. If I see a car coming in my direction, the appropriate response is not to assent to that fact but the action of avoiding the car. In short, the objects we perceive are signs-For acting, –signals– depending on the needs and desires of the recipient of the sign.

A geometry textbook is composed of sentences that have the grammatical form of statements. The gospels are narratives or stories, not statements. These Gospel’s stories are composed of sentences that have the grammatical form of statements, but the stories are stories told or narrated, not statements. From the fact that the stories contain grammatical statements originates the mistake of the members of the Jesus Seminar (JS). Like Aristotle in De Interpretatione , the members of the JS think that statements (which express judgments) are the only form of linguistic expression that can be true or false. Since they hear or read the Gospels narratives as composed of statements, they find it appropriate to suspend judgment as to the truth of the Gospel’s statements, in order to determine which are true and deserving of assent. But what if the sentences that have the grammatical form of statements found in the narrative portions of the Gospels are not genuine statements and are, consequently, neither true nor false?  In that case, denying or assenting to their truth is inappropriate in the sense of incoherent. For if nothing is affirmed, its truth cannot be coherently denied. Now, a narrative narrates or tells events, but it doesn’t affirm events as to be understood and assented to.  

  

If a novel begins with the following sentence, “It was a beautiful morning, so John decided to go out,” the denial of the truth of either portion of the sentence or of the two portions would be incoherent, absurd, because the author of a novel states and affirms nothing. The author is narrating events, not affirming something to be understood and assented to. And such is the case with any narrative , the Gospels’ narratives included.

For example, in chapter 4 of his Gospel Mark narrates an episode in which the apostles and Jesus were involved. There was a tempest. Jesus was asleep in the boat in which he was crossing the lake together with his apostles. The boat began to be inundated. Terrified, the apostles awakened Jesus and asked him whether he didn’t care about what was happening to them. Jesus ordered the sea to calm down, and the wind ceased. The apostles asked each other in awe who is this man to whom the elements obey.  

Many interpreters –perhaps some of the members of the Jesus Seminar among them– refuse to assent to the truth of the statement by Mark that Jesus commanded the wind to stop, and that the wind ceased as a result of Jesus’ command. They therefore deny the truth of Mark’s statements. But my point is that the Gospel’s author makes no such statements and that, therefore, it is incoherent to deny the truth of an affirmation that has not been made. Rather than making a series of statements the author is narrating a single event in which both Jesus and the apostles participated, and in which  Jesus is the protagonist or central character. The author is not affirming that .. Jesus did this, or said that. Narrating is not affirming . One can narrate an event, and one can affirm a fact, but one cannot narrate a fact. What we call a fact is the “content” of a statement that is affirmed as true.

In my summary of the Markan episode there appear to be a number of statements that parallel Mark’s narrative, that is, a number of stated facts to be understood and assented to. But this is an illusion created by the grammatical form I used in my summary. It is an illusion, for in Mark’s narrative there are not several stated or affirmed facts: that Jesus was asleep, t hat the boat began to catch water, that the apostles awakened Jesus... In Mark’s Gospel all these apparently different statements combine into a single narrated episode to which the reader of the Gospel is imaginarily made present as if the event narrated were taking place before his eyes. In other words, the narrative speaks to the imagination of the person who reads or hears the narrative. That is why the reader or hearer is made present to the event narrated only as if he or she were actually present to the event narrated. For his presence to the event is only imaginary, as our presence to the events that take place in a movie is only imaginary. This imaginary witness is present only as if because he cannot take part in the action or be affected by what happens. Unlike the participants in the event narrated, the reader is only a witness –and an imaginary witness, at that.

Here we may begin to sense the difference between the grammatical statements contained in a narrative and ordinary statements which are not part of a narrative. If I state, “My apartment has three rooms,” I am stating, but not narrating. And not only because narratives are narratives of events, and the statement about my apartment refers to no action taking place, but also because it doesn’t speak to your imagination. Imagination is always concrete, like perception. A perceived three-bedroom apartment is either big or small, its walls are of a definite color, etc. But my statement that my apartment has three bedrooms refers you to no apartment in particular, but to any three bedroom apartment. That is, any three bedroom apartment can verify my statement. The reason is, as Aristotle surmises, that ordinary statements or enunciative speech acts are meant to affirm their own truth, the truth of their stated content. That is why “It is not true that your apartment has three rooms” would be a possible appropriate reply. For, supposedly,

or so at least Aristotle holds, statements are implicit affirmations of the

truth of their content. BUT –and this is my point– such is not the case with the grammatical statements contained in a narrative. For the author’s primary intention is not to affirm the truth of those statements, so as to communicate that truth to others. Indeed, contrary to Aristotle’s assumption, the author, unlike the philosopher or the historian, is not writing a scientific treatise that aims at certain truth; the author is narrating events that speak not to the reason or intelligence but to the imagination of the readers or hearers. For the purpose of the Gospel’s narratives is not to bring the readers to assent but to Christian practice in response to the protagonist of the narratives who is Jesus. (Concerning imagination, cf. Nussbaum).

The divinity of Christ, as Kierkegaard would say, is not a doctrine, for doctrines are Aristotelian statements of intelligible “facts” to be believed or assented to intellectually. Rather, the affirmation of the divinity of Christ refers implicitly to the Gospels’ stories in which Christ is the divine protagonist who commands adoration.

  Notice however that my summary is a summary of Mark’s narrative, but is not itself a narrative. What is the difference?

Ths Gospel’s narratives tell events that happened in the past with respect to us, but not with respect to those who actually participated in the events narrated. One is always contemporary with the events in which one participates.

Apparently, both the Gospels and a textbook of geometry are composed of statements. This may be grammatically correct but is semantically incorrect, for the grammatical statements of geometry do not refer to events, much less to dramatic events in which people are involved, whereas the Gospels do. In this sense, the literary genre of a geometry textbook and the literary genre of the Gospels are different.
