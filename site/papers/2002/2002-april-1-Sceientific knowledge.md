            SCIENTIFIC KNOWLEDGE AND PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE
                 (This paper continues 2001-dec-1-Kuhn and faith wpd)

      The Aristotelian tradition places scientific knowledge, as
necessarily true, among the examples of theoretical knowledge. Without
fully realizing it, Kuhn shows that the experimental sciences are examples
of practical knowledge, breaking with the Aristotelian tradition of
necessary truth.

      Under Hume’s pressure, Kant realized that geometry, Plato’s
paradigmatic example of scientific knowledge, was not made up of analytical
but of synthetic a priori judgments. That, combined with his Copernican
revolution, was an advance, for it did away with the notion that something
in nature, in the thing itself, was necessary.* However, Kant still
believed in demonstration or at least monstration which brought synthetic a
priori judgments to intuition as typical of scientific knowledge as
somewhat necessarily true. Kuhn does away with demonstration and
experimental proof. Experiments do not prove. They are a case of intra-
paradigmatic problem-solving through which the habitual group-licensed
practical vision of he members of the particular science will be acquired.
* Notice the ambiguity of  “necessary” in the philosophical tradition. It
may apply to the object as non-contingent or to the judgment as necessarily
true. Plato confused the two: a judgment could be necessarily true, that
is, correspond necessarily to the real, only if the object itself was
necessary. By the way, this clearly shows that Plato was thinking of
knowledge in terms of correspondence with the real. - Aristotle sensed that
a judgment could be factually, though not necessarily, true (perception
being the main example). At the same time, however, he preserved the notion
of scientific or necessarily true knowledge, and even created the ideal of
a totally necessarily true science in the Analytica Posteriora (which,
tough modified, would persist in the modern notion of science proved by
experiment). To complicate things still more, by contrast to theoretical
knowledge which terminated in vision of the truth, Aristotle introduced the
notion of practical knowledge in matters of deliberation and decision,
which terminated in action. In spite of its lack of necessity, he seems to
have classified sensible knowledge as theoretical (for he disconnected
vision from action), though it had the uncertainty (lack of necessary
correspondence) of practical knowledge. By the same token, the notion of
truth as correspondence in the realm of action becomes highly problematic.

      In short, as far as Kuhn is concerned, the point is to show his break
with science as a vision that corresponds to the real –hence the abolition
of necessarily true knowledge proven by experiment– and his implicit
alternative of practical, tacit knowledge. Knowing is for the sake of
acting, and the person who responds correctly knows –but not in the sense
of seeing objectively what is the case or having a view that re-presents or
corresponds to what is the case. What the person has is an acquired
disposition to respond, prompted, in the appropriate circumstances, by the
desired state of affairs and the present state of affairs.
