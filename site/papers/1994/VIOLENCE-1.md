            Albert Camus on the Legitimacy of Violence


      When the Nobel Prize in literature was  awarded  to  Albert  Camus  in
1957, it was intended, as the citation read, to celebrate  an  author  whose
works "focus upon and enlighten the problems confronting the  conscience  of
contemporary man." To none  of  them  did  Camus  devote  a  more  obstinate
attention than to the always contemporary and urgent problem  of  the  moral
legitimacy of the use of violence. And  whether  one  ends  up  agreeing  or
disagreeing with Camus' final stand, his intense, probing reflection on  the
issues of violence and non-violence as well as  his  personal  evolution  in
this regard are enlightening in at least a negative  way,  as  I  hope  will
become clear from what follows.
1936-1941: From absurdism to moral commitment
      If violence as a purely literary theme were  my  concern,  this  study
should analyze in depth such of Camus' early works as Le mythe  de  Sisyphe,
L'Etranger and Caligula. But violence, in the sense that interests  us  here
and came to obsess Camus, is not a literary theme; it  is  a  moral  problem
which, as such, did not fall within the range of  Camus'  reflection   until
he had disentangled himself from his  youthful  philosophy  of  absurdity--a
philosophy which, as Camus himself declared a few years later, he  had  just
picked up in the streets of his times.  Why did not the moral  dimension  of
violence emerge in Camus' early writings? The explanation is simple:  in  an
absurd  world  there  are  no  moral  values  and,  consequently,  no  moral
problems. Where, as Caligula puts it unflinchingly, "All  [actions]  are  on
equal footing"[i] --justice  and  injustice  lose  all  meaning  and,  thus,
neither  violence  nor  non-violence  can  be  justifiably  approved  of  or
condemned.
      It comes as a relief, however, to know  that  even  in  his  absurdist
initial period Camus was better than his written words.  At  the  very  same
time that Meursault,  the  protagonist  of  L'Etranger,  who  like  Caligula
exemplifies Camus' absurdism and  its  ethical  consequences,  remorselessly
kills an Arab fellow-human being just because he feels like  it,  Camus,  in
real life, is losing his job as a reporter for an Algerian paper for  having
written a courageous series of articles  on  the  discriminatory  conditions
under which the Arabs lived and were kept  in  by  the  people  of  European
origin. This passionate concern for justice on Camus' part, totally at  odds
with the a-moral message of the writings of his absurdist  period,  supports
the judgment of one commentator of The Myth  of  Sisyphus:  "...  Camus  had
explored the absurd only in order to be  in  a  better  position  to  commit
himself to our reasons for living".[ii]
      If (as I believe in Morvan Lebesque's company) it is really  the  case
that in his absurdist period Camus was actually searching  for  our  reasons
for living, he soon found them in real life  and--ironically--in  the  midst
of violence.
1941-1945: The legitimacy of violence
      As is well known, Camus joined the underground Resistance against  the
Nazis and  risked  his  life  by  collaborating  in  the  publication  of  a
clandestine anti-Nazi newspaper, "Combat." His decision was prompted by  the
execution of another resistant, Gabriel Péri, on  December  19,  1941.  With
this we reach the point at which Camus' entire view of life shifted.  As  he
himself would later  comment,  he  had  begun  the  war  as  an  indifferent
spectator and finished it as a committed resistant.
       Camus'  anti-Nazi  commitment   was   not   initially   prompted   by
philosophical reflection but, rather, by a sort of elementary  instinct  for
justice, as made clear by the following text:
      "At the beginning or your article," wrote Camus in 1948, "you  ask  me
      why I chose the Resistance. Such a question has no meaning for certain
      people, and I belong in that number. I could not imagine myself on the
      other side, it is as simple as that. I thought at the time and I still
      think that one cannot be on the side of the labor camps. I  understood
      at that moment that I hated the institutions  of  violence  even  more
      than violence itself".[iii]

      Camus may well have chosen the Resistance just  because,  all  to  his
honor, he could not see himself on the side of the labor  camps.   The  fact
remains, however, that his choice presupposed the moral  legitimacy  of  the
use of violence  at  least  against  the  Nazis.  Now,  not  only  was  this
assumption in need of justification,  but  the  very  notion  that  not  all
actions are on equal footing--that the resistants  were  morally  right  and
the Nazis morally wrong--put him at odds with the  absurdist  philosophy  he
had until then made his and  fervently  preached.  Camus'  problem  was  the
following: in Le mythe de Sisyphe he had forcefully asserted  that,  because
life is absurd, morality is just a  matter  of  arbitrary  preferences.  If,
therefore, he was to remain consistent  with  his  philosophical  views,  he
would have had to  admit  that  neither  was  there  any  injustice  in  the
absolute will-to-power of Naziism, nor  a  justice  in  the  name  of  which
Naziism ought to be  condemned  and  opposed.  However,  his  own  sense  of
justice had led him to oppose Nazi violence with  violence  and  to  condemn
Naziism.  Camus,  therefore,  found  himself  constrained  to   revise   his
absurdist philosophy.
      The war period was not, of course,  the  most  propitious  moment  for
calm philosophical reflection. Nevertheless we find the first seeds of  that
reflection in Lettres à un ami Allemand, a series of  articles  of  the  war
period in which Camus presented the resistance against the Nazis  not  as  a
matter of personal or national preference but of moral obligation.
      Clearly, the German  friend  addressed  by  Camus  is  no  other  than
Nietzsche, under whose powerful philosophical influence Camus  had  produced
his early works and whom he now viewed-- whether rightly or wrongly  matters
little for our present purposes--as an indirect  precursor  of  Naziism.  To
his German friend's ethical nihilism--"You supposed that in the  absence  of
any human or divine code the only values were those  of  the  animal  world:
violence and cunning"[iv]--Camus opposes his own new stand: "I  continue  to
believe that this world has no ultimate meaning. But I know  that  something
in it has meaning, and that
is man, because he is the only creature to insist on having one. This  world
has at least the truth of  man".[v]  In  other  words,  although  the  human
destiny is ultimately absurd because  life  ends  in  the  senselessness  of
death, human life is not itself absurd; it  has  a  potential  meaning  that
ought to be respected by "giving a chance to the justice that man alone  can
conceive".[vi]
      In striking contrast to his pre-war  absurdist  years,  Camus  emerged
from the war as a new man, a sort of prophet of justice. The  great  success
of L'Etranger together with his high reputation as a resistant placed  Camus
in an influential position of which he decided to take full  advantage.  His
articles in "Combat," widely and avidly read, spoke of a new era of  justice
for all in France, and their tone was frankly revolutionary. It seems  that,
having accepted the legitimacy of the use of  violence  against  the  Nazis,
Camus felt seriously inclined to fight socio-economic oppression at home  by
the same method, as a last but justifiable resort.  In  fact,  in  an  essay
entitled Remarque sur la révolte, he  bluntly  offered  a  kind  of  general
justification of the use of violence against injustice.[vii]
      Lest that essay be dismissed as less indicative than it  actually  was
of Camus' very real and very practical conviction that the use  of  violence
was morally justified in certain cases, it will suffice to allude to  Camus'
famous controversy with François Mauriac at the beginning of 1945. In  brief
summary,  Camus  publicly   demanded   the   death   penalty   for   certain
collaborators with the Nazis. Mauriac, whose reputation  both  as  a  writer
and as a resistant was at least comparable to Camus' own, rejoined  that  it
was better to let bygones be bygones. Camus replied indignantly:
      "Mauriac has just published 'on the disregard of charity'  an  article
      that I find neither just nor charitable... Every time that... I  speak
      of justice Mauriac speaks of charity... As  if  our  choice  were,  in
      these worldly matters, between loving  Christ  and  hating  people.  I
      reject that. ... Charity has no place here... What is needed today and
      in this case is justice... I dislike hatred... But in this case pardon
      would be worse and would amount to injury...  I  shall  wholeheartedly
      pardon together with Mauriac only when Velin's parents, when Leynaud's
      wife will have authorized me to pardon."[viii]

      Mauriac retorted that his real  point  was  that  a  justice  that  is
fallible, as all human justice is, has  no  business  condemning  people  to
die, since death is irreparable. Camus,  however,  had  the  last  word  and
carried the day: he got the heads he had asked for. To his  horror,  though,
he not only got those heads, but also many others he had not asked  for  and
even some heads of people who were possibly innocent, because  "l'épuration"
for which he had pleaded actually  went  far  beyond  his  expectations  and
clearly beyond the limits of justice. A shattering experience for Camus.  So
shattering, indeed, that it was to precipitate  a  deep  reconsideration  of
his position concerning the legitimacy of  violence  and  constrain  him  to
change it, as indicated three years later by this public confession: "...  I
have never ceased meditating on what he [Mauriac] said. As a result of  that
reflection, I have reached the conclusion, which here I  make  public,  that
on the substance and on  the  precise  point  of  our  controversy  François
Mauriac was right and I was wrong."[ix]
      Thus, ironically, the very violent  treatment  of  the  collaborators,
for which, in the name of justice, Camus had so  self-righteously  clamored,
actually came to constitute  a  turning  point  in  his  reflection  on  the
morality of violence.
1946-1948: Violence is unjustifiable
      La peste, published in 1947 and for which Camus was awarded the  "Prix
des Critiques," witnesses to Camus' significant change of mind. It  is  true
that, on its surface, this novel appears to be one  more  of  the  works  in
which the use  of  violence  is  considered  legitimate.  As  Camus  himself
declared, " If The Plague  is, in certain regards, more than a chronicle  of
the Resistance, it certainly is no less than that."[x]   Thus,  the  disease
that isolates Oran from the rest of the world symbolizes the Nazi  invasion,
and the main characters of the novel  who  jointly  marshall  their  efforts
against the plague stand for the resistants. Jospeh Grand speaks for all  of
them when he says: "Plague is here and we've got to  make  a  stand,  that's
obvious. Ah, I only wish everything were  as  simple."[xi]  It  would  seem,
therefore, that this novel is just one more among  the  works  that  reflect
Camus' conviction of the legitimacy of the use of violence. For by his  very
choice of an impersonal disease as the symbol  for  the  Nazis  (who,  after
all, were human beings, not microbes), Camus  appears  to  be  automatically
justifying the use of violence against them and even to have suppressed  the
possibility of moral conflict, at least in that particular  case.  Yet,  the
signals sent to the reader of The Plague concerning the moral legitimacy  of
violence, even against the Nazis, are  actually  mixed.  To  understand  the
ambiguity of this novel in this  regard  we  have  to  remember  that  Camus
worked on it for five long years and produced a  number  of  very  different
drafts.  Now,  if  an  early  period  in  the  elaboration  of  The   Plague
corresponds to the time in which Camus was engaged  in  the  Resistance  and
had  no  doubts  whatsoever  about  the  moral  legitimacy  of  his  violent
undertaking,  the  final  version  was  completed  when  the   excesses   of
l'épuration  against  the   collaborators   had   already   shattered   that
conviction. Thus--however inconsistent this may be, from the literary  point
of view, with the conception of The Plague as a chronicle of the Resistance-
-, from the  point  of  view  of  his  philosophical  evolution  it  is  not
surprising to find Camus using one of his characters, Tarrou, to convey  his
new stand on the use of violence.
      Tarrou's father was a prosecutor. As a  young  man,  at  his  father's
invitation, Tarrou attended a day in court in which his  father  asked  for,
and obtained, the head of a human being. Tarrou was horrified by "that  foul
procedure whereby dirty mouths stinking of plague told a fettered  man  that
he was going to die, and scientifically arranged things so  that  he  should
die, after nights and nights  of  mental  torture  while  he  waited  to  be
murdered in cold blood."[xii] The real plague, for Tarrou, was the virus  of
violence  with  which  his  entire  society  and   his   own   father   were
contaminated. He left his home with the obsession of finding a  way  to  put
an end to the violence used by human beings against other human beings,  and
he joined a group of agitators that promised to bring about a world  without
violence. However, as Tarrou soon discovered to his dismay,  his  own  group
also sometimes resorted to violence as the necessary means  to  bring  about
the promised world. Though Tarrou  balked  at  that,  for  a  while  he  let
himself be half-persuaded by the arguments  of necessity and  force  majeure
given him by his comrades, until the day...
      "Until the day when I was present at an execution...
      "Have you ever seen a man shot by a firing-squad? No, of  course  not;
      the spectators are hand-picked and it is like  a  private  party,  you
      need an invitation. The result is that you've gleaned your ideas about
      it from books and pictures. A post, a blindfolded man,  some  soldiers
      in the offing. But the real thing isn't a bit like that. Do  you  know
      that the firing-squad stands only a yard and a half from the condemned
      man? Do you know that if the victim took two steps forward  his  chest
      would touch the rifles? Do you know that, at  this  short  range,  the
      soldiers concentrate their fire on the region of the heart  and  their
      bullets make a hole into which you could thrust  your  fist?  No,  you
      didn't know all that; those are things that are never spoken  of.  For
      the plague-stricken their peace of mind is more important than a human
      life. Decent folks must be allowed to sleep easy  o'  nights,  mustn't
      they? Really it would be  shockingly  bad  taste  to  linger  on  such
      details, that's common knowledge. But personally I've never been  able
      to sleep well since then. The bad taste remained in my mouth and  I've
      kept lingering on the details, brooding over them."[xiii]

      The extraordinary  pathos  of  this  passage  reveals  Camus'  intense
feelings at the thought of an execution. Was he remembering the  people  for
whose heads, like Tarrou's father, he had asked and obtained? Could it  even
be the case that, in his journalist's capacity, Camus had  been  invited  as
an official witness to an execution like to "a private party," and had  seen
with his own eyes and in all its gory details  what  he  had  so  vehemently
asked for? Be it as it may, after witnessing an  execution  Tarrou  "learned
that I had had an indirect hand in the deaths of thousands of  people;  that
I'd even brought about their deaths by  approving  of  acts  and  principles
which could only end that way,"[xiv]  and  he "resolved  to  have  no  truck
with anything which, directly or indirectly, for good reasons  or  for  bad,
brings death to anyone or justifies others' putting him to  death."[xv]   As
for the impressive arguments given him by his comrades to justify their  use
of violence, Tarrou found them all wanting when weighed against the life  of
a single human being: "In any case, my concern was not with arguments...  My
concern was with that hole  in  a  man's  chest.  And  I  told  myself  that
meanwhile, so far anyhow as I was concerned,  nothing  in  the  world  would
induce me to accept any argument that justified such butcheries."[xvi]
      Tarrou's position is unequivocal: violence is unjustifiable no  matter
what the reasons and the circumstances. But have we any  basis  to  identify
Camus with Tarrou? Yes, at least to a significant  extent.  I  have  already
mentioned Camus' avowal  that  Mauriac  had  been  right  in  rejecting  the
legitimacy of violence against the collaborators. But, in addition, we  also
have an explicit  declaration  by  Camus  in  this  period  of  his  life--a
declaration whose very wording is reminiscent of Tarrou's statements  in  La
peste:
      "Having said that, after the last two years' experience, I  could  not
      anymore accept any view whatsoever that  would  impose  on  me  either
      directly or indirectly the obligation to  condemn  a  human  being  to
      death, certain people whom I respected remarked that  I  was  utopian,
      for there  is  no  political  view  that  could  avoid  imposing  such
      obligation at one time or another...
      "They presented this argument forcefully. However, I think  that  much
      of the argument's force was due to the fact that those who  voiced  it
      had no imagination when other people's death was concerned. It is  one
      of the handicaps of our century. As nowadays we love only through  the
      telephone and as we do not work anymore with our hands, so we kill and
      are killed by proxy.
      "It makes things more hygienic, no doubt, but also it  makes  us  less
      perceptive."[xvii]
      It, therefore, does not seem unreasonable to see in Tarrou's
passionate rejection of the legitimacy of violence Camus'  own  position  in
the years 1946-1948.  Yet,  there  are  also  reasons  to  see  in  Tarrou's
absolute condemnation of the use of violence a mere transitional  moment  in
Camus' tortuous way toward the position he will finally  adopt  from  around
1948 till his death in  1960.  Indeed,  Tarrou  himself  realizes  that  his
absolute pacifism, in a  world  in  which  others  remorselessly  resort  to
violence to achieve their goals, actually amounts to allowing them to  shape
the future: "... I know I have no place in the  world  of  today;  once  I'd
definitely refused to kill, I doomed myself to an exile that can never  end,
I leave it to others to make history."[xviii] But does one  have  the  right
to absent oneself from history in the name of  morality  and  to  allow  the
violent to oppress the non-violent and control the world? Camus'  hesitation
is reflected in the cautious, provisional way in which Tarrou expresses  his
own stand: "And I told myself  that  meanwhile,  so  far  anyhow  as  I  was
concerned, nothing in the world would induce  me  to  accept   any  argument
that justified such butcheries.  Yes,  I  chose  to  be  blindly  obstinate,
pending the day when I could see my way more clearly."[xix]
      That day would come for Camus, though not for  Tarrou  in  the  novel,
and it would be reflected in the final stand Camus took in L'homme révolté.
1948-1960: Violence is both necessary and unjustifiable
      Since the time of his participation in the Resistance  Camus  realized
that he had definitively left behind his absurdist period. This  development
forced him to articulate to himself his new philosophy. Such  was  the  task
that he attempted to accomplish in The Rebel--a long essay  which  was  five
years in the making and in which Camus squarely confronted what  had  become
for him the fundamental issue: "Is it possible to find  a  rule  of  conduct
outside the  realm  of  religion  and  its  absolute  values?  That  is  the
question..."[xx]
      As for the importance of the place that the issue  of  the  legitimacy
or illegitimacy of violence is to occupy  in  The  Rebel,  Camus  leaves  no
doubt, since in the Introduction he states that "The purpose of  this  essay
is... to face the reality of the present, which is  logical  crime,  and  to
examine meticulously the arguments by  which  it  is  justified,"[xxi]   and
that the question for  whose  answer  he  is  looking  is  none  other  than
"whether we have the right to kill our fellow men, or the right to let  them
be killed."[xxii]
      The very way in which the problem is now posed shows the  progress  in
Camus reflection, for now, for the first time, his question encompasses  the
problem  in  all  its  complexity  and,  consequently,  prevents  simplistic
answers. Indeed, if only the first  half  of  the  question  is  considered,
namely whether we have the right to kill our fellow men, which is  the  only
side of the problem that Tarrou had taken into account, then the  temptation
to answer with an unqualified "No" is irresistible.  It  is  only  when  one
realizes that an absolute  rejection  of  the  legitimacy  of  violence  may
result, under certain circumstances, in our permitting  innocent  people  to
be killed by those who resort to violence, that  we  can  see  the  possible
shortcomings of Tarrou's absolute pacifism. What would a  Tarrou  have  done
confronted with a Hitler, when violence was the  only  possible  resort?  He
would have been constrained to allow Hitler to  exterminate  the  Jews.  But
would he have had the right to do that? Camus definitely does not think  so,
in The Rebel, because he realizes, as Tarrou had not,  that  "Absolute  non-
violence is the negative basis of slavery and its acts of  violence."[xxiii]
 Will he, therefore, return to his early position that the use  of  violence
is at least sometimes morally legitimate? No, again.  In  Camus'  eyes,  the
easiness with which we have come to justify the use of violence has made  of
our century, by far, the most violent epoch in history: "We  are  living  in
the era of premeditation and the perfect  crime.  Our  criminals...  have  a
perfect alibi: philosophy, which can be  used  for  any  purposes--even  for
transforming murderers into judges."[xxiv] Indeed, since we  prejustify  our
use of violence  in  the  name  of  justice,  or  of  freedom,  or  even  of
fraternity, we feel entitled to kill and still consider ourselves  innocent.
And, of course, since these prejustifications give us a  perfect  alibi,  we
are always eager to use more and more violence. In short, if  in  The  Rebel
Camus categorically discards  Tarrou's  absolute  refusal  of  violence,  he
nonetheless identifies himself with Tarrou  as  to  the  invalidity  of  any
argument that attempts to legitimize the use  of  violence:  "My  reply  was
that if you gave in once, there was no reason for  not  continuing  to  give
in. It seems to me that history has borne me out; today there's  a  sort  of
competition who will kill the most. They're all mad  over  murder  and  they
couldn't stop killing men even if they wanted to."[xxv]
      Now, if on the one hand Camus rejects Tarrou's  absolute  non-violence
because it is the negative basis of slavery and its acts  of  violence,  and
if  on  the  other  hand  he  rejects  with  Tarrou  the  validity  of   any
justification of violence because it  fosters  violence,  what  then?  Camus
rallies himself to the position he had explored  in  his  play  Les  justes,
which in L'homme révolté  he  defines  as  follows:  "...  they  too,  while
recognizing  the  inevitability  of  violence,  nevertheless   admitted   to
themselves that it is unjustifiable. Necessary and inexcusable--that is  how
murder appeared to them."[xxvi]
      To them, and to Camus, too, till the end of his life. To use  his  own
formulation in one of the articles later collected in  Actuelles  under  the
title "Ni victimes ni bourreaux," "In  short,  people  like  myself  do  not
aspire to a world in which we cease  killing  each  other  (we  are  not  as
utopian as that!), but merely a world in which  killing  be  not  considered
legitimate."[xxvii]
A critical appraisal
      We have followed  Camus  through  the  meanderings  of  his  agonizing
evolution concerning the legitimacy of violence after his initial period  of
absurdity: from an ad hoc and fundamentally impulsive rather  than  rational
justification of the use of violence, through an  absolute  condemnation  of
its use, to a final, reluctant acceptance of the necessity  of  the  use  of
violence conjoined with a  rejection  of  its  legitimacy.  One  cannot  but
admire Camus  for  his  deep  moral  concern  and  for  his  extraordinarily
careful, responsible reflection on such  a  tragic  human  problem.  As  the
problem, however, continues to plague us as much as  it  plagued  Camus,  it
seems imperative to pass judgment on the validity of  his  final  stand.  In
other words, the issue I want to consider is whether the  view  explored  by
Camus in his play Les Justes and finally adopted by him in L'homme révolté--
namely, that the use of violence is sometimes necessary but never justified-
-constitutes an adequate answer to  the  question  to  which  that  work  is
devoted, namely, "whether we have the right to kill our fellow  men,  or  to
let them be killed."
      Alas, my personal, definite opinion is that  Camus'  final  answer  is
radically  inadequate.  Saying  that  violence   is   both   necessary   and
unjustifiable amounts to imposing on  the  agent  the  moral  obligation  to
resort to the use of violence when it is the only efficient  means  left  to
prevent the exploitation and massacre of the innocent, and at the same  time
imposing on the same agent the moral obligation to abstain from the  use  of
violence because such use is never justified. In other words,
according to Camus' prescription, when violence is the  only  recourse  left
to save the innocent, morally concerned agents ought to resort to  violence,
for otherwise they would become "indirect accomplices" with the  oppressors.
Yet, since  even  in  such  a  case  violence  is  unjustifiable,  that  is,
ethically illegitimate, morally concerned agents  ought  not  to  resort  to
violence.
      Camus himself, at the end of  L'homme  révolté,  came  to  a  sort  of
melancholy acknowledgment of the impossible quandary  which,  in  his  view,
constitutes the destiny of "the rebel," that is, of  the  morally  concerned
agent:

      "If rebellion exists, it is because falsehood, injustice, and violence
      are part of the rebel's condition. He  cannot,  therefore,  absolutely
      claim not to kill..., without renouncing his rebellion and  accepting,
      once and for all, evil and  murder.  But  no  more  can  he  agree  to
      kill..., since the mere  reasoning  which  would  justify  murder  and
      violence would also destroy the reasons for his insurrection. Thus the
      rebel can never find peace."[xxviii]
      What Camus, however, does not seem to have fully realized is that,  in
merely resigning himself to  this  contradictory  destiny,  he  was  leaving
himself and his readers in the  very  ignorance  that  it  was  the  central
purpose of L'homme révolté to dispel. Do we  have  the  right  to  kill  our
fellow men, or to permit  them  to  be  killed?  Yes,  because  violence  is
necessary;  no,  because  violence  is  unjustifiable.  Hardly  a  practical
conclusion on the part of someone who had undertaken  "to  find  a  rule  of
conduct outside the realm of religion and its  absolute  values."  Hardly  a
practical conclusion, too, for anybody concerned with  the  agonizing  issue
of the moral legitimacy of the use of violence.
       Tragically,  not  long  after  the  publication  of  The  Rebel,  the
historical circumstances of his time were to force Camus to put to the  test
the position he had adopted, and the price he paid for  its  inadequacy  was
high. In the early  50s,  during  the  Algerian  war  of  independence  from
France, he was called by his countrymen to help by  writing  in  defense  of
the legitimacy of their violent revolt as the only resort left  to  them  to
conquer their country's autonomy. Trapped,  however,  in  his  view  of  the
necessity and the unjustifiability of the use of violence, Camus could  only
produce a series of self-neutralizing articles whose essence  was  that  the
Algerian war of  independence  was  both  just  and  unjust:  just,  because
violence had become necessary; unjust, because  violence  is  unjustifiable.
Needless to say, such  articles,  which  at  the  same  time  justified  and
condemned both the Algerians who were fighting for  their  independence  and
the French who were fighting for their interests, met with general  derision
on both sides of the conflict.
      As is well known, the last years of Camus' life, though he was in  the
full vigor of his forties, were a  period  of  self-doubt  and  despondency.
There is every reason to think that the futile,  even  ridiculous,  role  he
had played in the independence of his country brought about his own  "fall,"
of which the title, the content and the tone of his last  novel,  The  Fall,
are a sad, thinly veiled, symbolic reflection. Reduced to political  silence
and inaction by virtue of his self-contradicting position on the  legitimacy
of violence, and profoundly embittered as a result, in the last eight  years
of his life--in  striking  contrast  to  the  preceding,  highly  productive
decennium--Camus only wrote that one  work  worthy  of  mention.  Clamance's
fall from his pedestal of merely imagined concern for  others,  revealed  to
him on the occasion of the young girl's fall into the river and of  his  own
refusal to help her, is cynically excused  in  the  novel,  and  even  oddly
justified, by the expected confession on the readers' part that we  all  are
like him, that our supposed moral concern is only a façade,  and  that  even
our repentance would be phony for, anyway, "the water is too  cold"  for  us
to have any serious intention of plunging to save a  girl  that  might  fall
into the river.
      A bitterly disappointing ending, I must say, for a thinker who out  of
genuine moral concern had so honestly and tenaciously tried to find his  way
through the labyrinth of the problem of the legitimacy of violence.
A viable alternative?
      It seems, however, that a merely  negative  criticism  of  the  stance
finally adopted by Camus will not do in this matter, precisely  because  the
ethical problem for which he attempted to find a solution has  unfortunately
lost none of its tragic urgency since his death, and we might be called  any
time to take our own stand regarding it. In addition, thanks to  Camus'  own
patient, protracted exploration and to the hindsight afforded us by his  own
failure, we might be in a position to do better than him. Do  we,  then,  or
don't we have the right to kill our fellow human beings, or to  permit  them
to be killed? I will speak for myself, anyway, if for nobody else, and  with
the trepidation that the gravity and intricacy of this matter induces.
      The problem of the ethical legitimacy of the use of violence   may  be
understood  as  a  particular  instance,  though  doubtless  the   thorniest
possible one  because  of  its  irretrievable   consequences,  of  the  more
general problem of the legitimacy of the use of the morally wrong  means  in
order to achieve the morally right  end.  After  all,  any  sensitive  moral
agent will concede that the use of violence is prima  facie  wrong,  without
which there would be no reason to agonize as to whether its use can ever  be
justified. Now, though Camus himself never quite formulated his  problem  in
terms of the possible justification of the means by the end, he  came  close
to see it from that angle in L'homme  révolté  in  a  passage  in  which  he
refers to the characters and events he portrayed in Les Justes:
      "... these fastidious assassins lived out the  rebel  destiny  in  its
      most contradictory form. It is possible  to  believe  that  they  too,
      while recognizing the inevitability of violence, nevertheless admitted
      to themselves that it is unjustifiable.  Necessary  and  inexcusable--
      that is how murder appeared to them.
      Mediocre minds, confronted with this terrible problem, can take refuge
      by ignoring one of the terms of the dilemma. They are content, in  the
      name of formal principles, to find all direct violence inexcusable and
      then to sanction that diffuse form of violence which  takes  place  on
      the scale of world history. Or they will console  themselves,  in  the
      name of history, with the thought that violence is necessary, and will
      add murder to murder, to the point of making of history nothing but  a
      continuous violation of  everything  in  man  which  protests  against
      injustice. This defines the two aspects of contemporary nihilism,  the
      bourgeois and the revolutionary.
      "But the extremists with whom we are concerned  forgot  nothing.  From
      their earliest days  they  were  incapable  of  justifying  what  they
      nevertheless found necessary,  and  conceived  the  idea  of  offering
      themselves as a justification and of replying by personal sacrifice to
      the question they asked themselves. For them, as for all rebels before
      them, murder is identified with suicide. A life is paid for by another
      life, and from these two sacrifices springs the promise  of  a  value.
      Kaliayev, Voinarovsky, and the others believe in the  equal  value  of
      human lives. Therefore they do not value any idea  above  human  life,
      though they kill for the sake of ideas... Other men to come,  consumed
      with the same devouring  faith  as  these,  will  find  their  methods
      sentimental and refuse to admit that any one life is the equivalent of
      any other. They will then put an abstract idea above human life,  even
      if they call it history." [xxix]
      What  emerges  immediately  from  this  dense  passage  is  that  "the
extremists" Camus is talking about are the very people he portrayed  in  Les
Justes, for they are "Kaliayev,  Voinarovsky,  and  the  others,"  and  that
Camus finds their stance admirable. But what is their stance? Precisely  the
one which Camus will end up making his own,  namely,  that  they  considered
violence both "necessary" and "unjustifiable." It  is  in  this  sense  that
they are "extremists," for  they  hold  fast  to  the  two  extremes,  while
"mediocre minds" let one of the extremes go. Indeed, one  type  of  mediocre
mind has eyes only for the  unjustifiability  of  violence,  forgetting  its
necessity, whereas the other type pays attention only to its  necessity  and
forgets its unjustifiability. Now, we only  have  to  think   of  these  two
types of minds in terms of the problem of the justification of the means  by
the end to realize that  they  represent  two  opposite  solutions  to  that
problem. Does the end justify the means? One type of mediocre  mind--and  in
this connection we may think of Tarrou as  a  perfect  example--answers  the
question by an absolute negative. Since, according to him, the end does  not
justify the means, Tarrou will abstain from using  violence  no  matter  how
necessary its use may be. In striking  contrast--and  here,  without  naming
him, Camus has in mind Lenin, of whom he will speak a few  pages  later[xxx]
and of whose morality of expediency he traced the  portrait  in  Les  Justes
under the features of Stepan--the other type of mediocre  mind  answers  the
question whether the end  justifies  the  means  by  a  rotund  affirmative;
Stepan-Lenin will therefore freely use violence whenever  necessary  in  his
relentless  pursuit  of  a  perfectly  just  world,  feeling   automatically
justified by the moral quality of the end he pursues. As for Camus  himself,
he finds  fault  with  both  types  of  mediocre  minds.  Tarrou's  conduct,
inspired as it is by the purely abstract principle of  the  unjustifiability
of violence, leads him to allow the innocent to be abused and  massacred  in
the concrete world in which we live, when the only means of  defending  them
would be the use of violence. Lenin's conduct, by contrast, inspired  as  it
is by "history," that is, by the just world  to  be  brought  about  in  the
distant future, leads him in the meantime to  pile  up  murder  upon  murder
without limits.
      Does the end justify the means? Camus  has  just  discarded  both  the
negative answer of a Tarrou and the affirmative answer of a  Lenin.  Indeed,
he sees incarnated in them  "the two forms of contemporary  nihilism":  "the
bourgeois,"   grounded   in   abstract   absolute   principles,   and   "the
revolutionary," with its appeal to future  historical  effects.  But,  then,
what is Camus' own answer?  The  one  he  attributes  to  Kaliayev  and  his
companions, who forgot neither the necessity  nor  the  unjustifiability  of
violence.
      As to the adequacy of this position I have already
expressed my definite skepticism. Its inadequacy, though, becomes even  more
evident when the position finally espoused by Camus is couched in  terms  of
end and means, for it shows itself as just a variety  of  Tarrou's  position
that the end does not justify the means, but with  the  utterly  incongruous
additions (a) that the wrong means are nonetheless to be used when they  are
necessary to achieve the good end, and (b) that those who use  them  do  the
wrong thing and therefore should pay for it with their own lives:  "...  for
all rebels..., murder is identified with suicide. A  life  is  paid  for  by
another life." This is why, in Les Justes, Kaliayev absolutely wants  to  be
executed after having killed the Grand Duke, for  he  has  to  pay  for  the
wrong done, even though killing the Grand Duke was necessary to achieve  the
end that Kaliayev saw at the time, and continues to see, as morally right.
      Yet, though defective in  its  ultimate  outcome,  Camus'  penetrating
exploration of the alternatives leaves us, I think, better equipped.  Guided
as he was by a high moral sensitivity, in rejecting the affirmative and  the
negative answers to the question whether the end justifies the means in  the
particular case of violence Camus seems to have  been instinctively  groping
for a dialectical middle term that combined the fastidiousness of  a  Tarrou
about the morality of the means used with the efficiency of a Lenin  in  the
pursuit of the morally worthy  end.  But  what  is  that  middle  term?  The
unqualified No of Tarrou means that  the  end  never  justifies  the  means,
while the  unqualified  Yes  of  Stepan-Lenin  means  that  the  end  always
justifies the means. It seems, then, that the middle term  for  which  Camus
was looking is sometimes. That is, contrary to Tarrou's conviction, the  end
sometimes justifies the means, and, contrary to  Stepan-Lenin's  conviction,
sometimes it does not. The Sometimes Yes limits the Never of a  Tarrou,  who
would therefore be entitled to use violence against the Nazis, when  violent
opposition was the only means left to prevent the abuse and massacre of  the
innocent. Thus, I would claim, Camus was right  in  rejecting  the  mediocre
minds of this kind. Equally, the  Sometimes  Not  limits  the  Always  of  a
Stepan-Lenin, who would therefore be prevented from  freely  using  violence
for a good  end  without  even  having  to  consider  whether  the  concrete
circumstances and the consequences  authorize  its  use  in  the  particular
case. Thus, I would again claim, Camus was  right,  too,  in  rejecting  the
mediocre minds of this kind.
      Of course, there is an obvious objection to the view I  am  proposing,
that the end sometimes justifies the means and sometimes does  not,  namely,
that it does not specify when it does and when it  does  not;  consequently,
as feared by Tarrou and by Camus himself, this view opens the  door  to  all
sorts of abuses, depending on who makes the  judgment.  My  answer  is  that
general solutions to ethical problems are always  vague  in  this  way,  for
they are not meant to function as recipes to be  automatically  applied  but
as principles of action for agents who have to judge for themselves  in  the
particular situation and have to assume responsibility  for  their  actions.
In any case, only morally concerned people have genuine moral problems,  and
general solutions to those problems are only for morally  concerned  people.
The morally unconcerned ones will  never  worry  whether  they  are  morally
entitled to do anything, and will always do whatever is more convenient  for
them.
      I will finish with a thought that persuades me  that,  given  time  to
reflect, Camus might  have  come  to  find  acceptable  the  solution  I  am
proposing. The thought is that this was the very  view  that  Camus  himself
intuitively followed at his most exemplary moment. Did he  not,  after  all,
oppose the Nazi violence with violence when there was no other  means  left?
And is there any reason to think that he ever felt that he  had  done  wrong
in following that course of action, much less that he had  to  pay  for  it?
But this amounts to saying that Camus deemed that, in the  particular  case,
the use of violence was justified by the end of preventing his fellow  human
beings to be killed, and that abandoning them to the Nazis in  the  name  of
an abstract principle would have been as morally shameful  as  the  omission
that brought about Clamance's fall.
