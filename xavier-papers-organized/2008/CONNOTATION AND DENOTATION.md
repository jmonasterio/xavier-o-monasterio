2008-March-Connot-Denot Reviewed April 7, 2008

CONNOTATION AND DENOTATION

The primary meaning of words are concepts, objects of thought, objects conceived by the mind, not real things. (Since animals are incapable of conceiving-thinking, they are radically non-linguistic creatures). To put it otherwise, words are primarily signals for conceiving what is said. If I say Napoleon to you, you don’t salute Napoleon or bow before him; you ask what about Napoleon of whom you have conceived a concept, prompted by what I said. By contrast, if a dog’s master is named Napoleon and I pronounce His name before the dog, the dog wags its tail, because the sound Napoleon is for the dog not a sign to conceive or think of Napoleon but a signal of his imminent presence, much as the smell of Napoleon is.

The primary meaning of a word is the concept connoted by it; not –as Aris thought in Peri hermeneias – the object whose presence is denoted by it. The perceived smell of Napoleon denotes or indicates or points to his presence to the dog. Perception signifies or denotes or refers to the object perceived. A pronounced name signifies or connotes the concept of the object named, it is a signal to think of the object named.

For a name to connote the concept of its object, the presence of the object is not required, for the object can be conceived as well in the presence as in the absence of the object connoted. For a name to denote its object, the presence or imminent presence of its object is required, for denotation—as well as perception –

is a signal for acting, not for conceiving or thinking.

What is the difference between conceiving and perceiving? That the object conceived or thought of is an abstract  a-temporal concept, not a concrete object. The triangle of which geometers speak is a concept, a thought, not a particular perceived triangle. It has no color or extension. It is neither here nor there. We can think of it and talk about it whether not is actually present before our eyes. For it is an object of thought, a concept.

Searle says that words differ from all other signs in that they say  what they mean. But there are two ways of meaning or signifying something, to wit: denoting a real object and connoting a concept. Searle does not distinguish explicitly these two ways of signifying linguistically. Implicitly, however, he reduces linguistic meaning to connoting a concept, for he claims that “the characteristic effect of meaning is understanding,” that is, deciphering the meaning of what is said by conceiving it. Now, this form of meaning applies to the case of linguistic meaning, but not to the case of perceiving. For what is perceived is a signal for acting, not a sign of a meaning to be deciphered and verbally enunciated.

Linguistic meaning terminates in interpretive understanding of what is said, which yields a concept of what is understood.. That is what the translator of a linguistic text does and is meant to do, and what we all do internally when we hear a sentence in a language known to us. But that is not what a perceiver of a perceptual signal does or is meant to do. A certain noise I hear in my house in the middle of the night is not a sign of a linguistic meaning to be interpreted; it is a signal for an action to be performed – be it calling the police or attacking the uninvited guest. But of this non-linguistic way of meaning, which is characteristic of what is perceived, Searle has no notion, since he confuses it with linguistic meaning which terminates in interpretive understanding. This is the confusion Wittgenstein warns us against.

And his warning is mighty important for two reasons. (1) Because we fall so easily into it, as shown by the fact that the confusion is ubiquitous in our cultural history;* and (2) because of its dire cultural consequences, among others the difficulty this creates for the faith of Christian intellectuals . This is the problem against which, under a different guise or formulation, Kierkegaard struggled  all his life.

Wittgenstein protested against this kind of confusion when he said that “there is a way of grasping a rule which is not an interpretation, but which is exhibited in what we call obeying a rule and going against it in actual cases.”  By that he meant that rules are not meant to be interpreted but to be obeyed, and, as far as our case is concerned, that perceptual signs are meant to function as rules for action not as linguistic texts.

* Searle is far from being alone in having fallen into this confusion. ARIS did, too, when he spoke of the practical syllogism, and so did NEWTON when he conceived  of nature as a book in which his laws were written as to be read and interpreted or understood rather than followed.. Actually, KUHN saw this confusion involved in the misinterpretation of science in what he called “the scientific textbooks ”, and he endeavored to correct it. According to him, the authors of scientific textbooks conceive of scientific laws as linguistic texts to be deciphered and representing what is the case rather than –as they actually are– as rules to be followed at the time and place they and the involved perceiver who obeys them determine.
