"Anticipatory looking." Foresight = anticipation, Retrospection = Reflection.

"To make use of an analogy drawn from natural understanding, we might say that the movement of faith belongs to the order of foresight, whereas the objective recognition of the intellectual content belongs to retrospection. In the former, the mode of knowing is direct and concrete, 'one's whole life is projected forward by concentrating on it.' In retrospection, analysis discovers and itemizes under an abstract form, without of course ever exhausting it, the treasure of truth that the simple act of foreseing poresupposes and puts into effect. To speak once again using Maurice Blondel's words, we might say that the movement of faith is its penumatic element, whereas the notions that it implies and the inventory made of them constitute its noetic element (Cf. Maurice Blondel, "Le point de

départ de la connaissance philosophique" ( Annales de philosophie chrétienne [1906], vols. 151 and 152, etc. Each of these calls for the other. Even if retrospection remains rudimentary, its object is always present just the same, under a more or less implicit form. The unity appears even stronger here than it did when we recognized it between faith and beliefs. It has been expressed by Romano Guardini in a definitive phrase: ' Faith is its content .' Faith is determined by what it believes. It is the living progress toward One in whom one believes. It is the living response to the call of One who annouces himself in revelation and who draws man by grace. - If faith, then, is a movement, will this movement end with earthly existence? Is it destined to cease or to continue? - The passage in the first Epistle to the Corinthians ... has been interpreted in contradictory ways... Without denying the textual difficulties, one might consider that on this point exegesis has at times allowed itself to te ruled by a theological prejudice, i.e. by an idea of faith which is incomplete. For faith, which is directed toward our Master, will always remain firm.' And Origen writes: 'Today I believe in an imperfect manner, but one day perfect faith will come, and that which is imperfect will disappear. "True or false is said to be in the enunciation as in a sign of true or false thought; but true or false is in the mind as in a subject (as said in VI Metaph E, 4, 1027b 17 - 1d028a 5) and in the thing as a cause (as said in the book Praedicamendotum Categ. 5, 4a 35 - 4b 9), for it is from the facts of the case, i.e., from a thing's being so or not being so, that speech is true or false... Truth or falsity is not present in all speech however... In the case of imperfect speech it is clear that it does not signify true or false, since it does not make complete sense to the mind of the hearer and therefore does not completely express a judgment of reason in which the true or false consists ...There are five species of perfect speech... enunciative, deprecative, imperative, interrogative, and vocative... Of these... the enunciative is the only one in which there is truth or falsity, for it alone signifies the conception of the intellect absolutely, and it is in this that there is truth or falsity . But the intellect or reason does not just conceive the truth of a thing. It also belongs to its office to direct and order others in accordance to what it conceives . Therefore, beside enunciative speech, which signifies the concept of the mind, there had to be other kinds of speech to signify the order of reason by which others are directed. Now, one man is directed by the reason of another in regard to three things: first, to attend with his mind, and vocative speech relates to this; second, to respond with his voice, and interrogative speech relates to this; third to exectue a work, and in relation to this, imperative speech is used with regard to inferiors, deprecative with regard to superiors. Optative speech is reduced to the latter, for a man does not have the power to move a superior except by the expression of desire. These four species of speech do not signnify the conception of the intellect in which there is truth or falsity, but a certain order following upon this . Consequently truth or falsity is not found in any of them, but only in enuciative speech... Enunciative speech belongs to the present consideration and for the follwing reason: this book is ordered directly to demonstrative science, in which the mind of man is led by an act of reasoning to assent to the truth from those things that are proper to the thing; to this end the demonstrator uses only enunciative speeech, which signifies things according as truth about them is in the mind. The rhetorician and the poet, on the other hand inducunt ad assentiendum ei quod intendunt non solum per ea quae sunt propria rei [is proper to the thing] but also through the dispositions of the hearer . Hence, rhetoricians and poets for the most part strive to move their auditors by arousing certain passsions in them." (Aquinas's and Cajetan's commentary on Aristotle's on Peri Hermeneias , translated by Jean T. Oesterle, Marquette U. Press, Milwaukee, Wisconsin, 1962, pp. 61-62. See my <\wp\chant\excor-3>).

What we discuss when we discuss is, often, the truth of something stated . Thus the conclusion we reach takes the form "it is true that" or "it is false that." That couldn't be said without reference to a previous statement or proposition for consideration as to its truth, that is, its resemblance to what is the case. This truth or falsity of what is judged or stated abstracts the stated from the commitment of the stater. The very fact of discussing its truth makes the commitment irrelevant or inoperative, at least methodically, although the one who holds or teaches the stated as true may keep his or her commitment while discussing it.

In this case the focus is on what is stated (what is usually called "the content" of the statement, the "factual" element), and the point is to show through demonstrative or probable arguments which appeal to the intellect's prospective assent that what is stated corresponds to what is the case. This assent ammounts to intellcetual commitment--immediately to the truth of thestatement and mediately to what is the case independently of the statement/

However, as we shall see below, there is commitment and commitment. Here the commitment is purely intellectual and takes the form of assent to something conceived as being objectively the case. If persuaded by the arguments given, at the end of the discussion I am committed to the truth of the statement in the sense of assenting to what is stated: what is stated is true, "it is true that..."  My commitment takes the form of firm assent, and the matter ends there.  Being purely intellectual, that commitment is cold--as cold as the objective, disinterested reasons that led to it.  This is the view of truth that leads to, or rests on, the conviction that truth is a property of judgments and, subordinately, of statements that express judgments.  Now, since truth and knowledge are synonyms, knowing has to consist in judging.  The knower, therefore, appears to be someone who has a true judgment in his or her mind--a judgment that is adequate to its object, for otherwise there would be no truth, since truth is correspondence or adequation and is formally in the knower.  

However, as I said above, there is commitment and commitment. So far we have spoken of purely intellectual commitment to the truth of something stated. However, at least in the case of existential encounters (on purpose do I say existential encounters rather than existential judgments) the case seems to be different.   In the middle of the night I hear somebody downstairs. "There is somebody downstairs."  Here the "knowledge," assuming it can be called by the same name as in the previous case, is not cold but hot. Indeed--to take in the first place the "first person" case , that is, the participant's rather than the witness's--the "knowledge" is not merely objective but subjective in that it is self-referential, referential to myself as being the vulnerable subject exposed to object to be responded to. The object, therefore, is motivating object .  By the same token, "knowledge" in this case--that is, encounter with particular motivating object--is, by its very nature, a moment of action in that the grasp of object for response is existential rather than purely intellectual and is meant to terminate in action, not in assent.  

Correlatively, the very notion of truth--at least of truth-adequation as pictorial or merely visual-objective representation or correspondence, becomes totally inadequate . First of all, because there is no merely objective, predicative judgment-statement here. Actually, there is no judgment at all, for judgments are reflective intellectual acts that assent to and affirm the adequation of a concept-content to what is the case; however, in the case we are considering there is intentional-emotion- attitude rather than judgment.  The emotion in response to object becomes constitutive of prospective or anticipated object for response (at least before deliberation as to the accuracy of the constitution of object for response, or of the appropriateness of aiming at the implied future state of affairs as a goal).  The man downstairs is-dangerous-man and to be responded to accordingly by the exposed agent who, unlike animals, can choose his own response. Running away, if posssible at all, so as to find oneself not exposed anymore to the dangerous man, is only one possibility to be considered in the deliberation that will finally choose one state of affairs as the (satisfactory) end and, accordingly, also the appropriate means.

Thus in this case there is no objective-predicative judgment claiming resemblance or correspondence to which the subject assents and aiming at mere intellectual assent.  Since there is no predicative-objective judgment involved, much less are there cold, merely objective reasons given for assenting, for nobody's assent is aimed at.   By emotionally constituting anticipatorily the object as dangerous-man-downstairs I am correlatively anticipatorily co-constituted, as it were, by the encountered object as (appropriately) being afraid of it or him and being braced to respond to it as dangerous, not as knowing it or him objectively and without any existential or exposural relation.*

* Notice that this--namely, through objective-predicative judgments--is the way in which "the critical turn in theology" vainly attempts to establish the truth about Jesus-- that Jesus was this or that, that Jesus did this or that, that Jesus said this or that, that Jesus existed--even in his merely historical reality as the Jesus of history, not to speak of the Christ of faith. As if the "factual" judgment were independent from response and a necessary condition for action, and as if there were a possible passage from "objective is" to "subjective-existential ought ."

To come now to statements. If, in this situation of shared exposure, I address to you--coprewent and equally exposed--the statement "There is a man downstairs," cold assent on your part would be a sign of utter misunderstanding, for you are an exposed vulnerable subject too. You too, as I am part and parcel , are part and parcel (that is participant in, in volved in) of the practical problem or bind, distant from the encountered motivating  object with existential distances of exposure.  This implies that existential statements convey practical "knowledge" and are to be understood anticipatokrily-applicatively by the addresse.  In other words, the addressee is meant to be constituted as disposed to respond to object.

I turn now to the "knowledge"involved in the witness's case , (on the assumption that I cannot interfere with what is going on either because there is nothing I can do in spite of being intentionally present without myself being exposed, or because I am absent because the event is already past, literally or imaginatively).  In this case there is emotion without action, because I grasp you as vulnerable exposed subject.
