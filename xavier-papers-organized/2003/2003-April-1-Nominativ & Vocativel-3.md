NOMINATIVE AND VOCATIVE

Nominative and Vocative here stand as a symbol for language as a representational cognitive system, to which philosophers tend to attribute primacy, and language as communicative. In the former case, names in the nominative as well as statements occupy a central place; in the latter case, names in the vocative take precedence, and statements have no privileged place among the speech acts. Susanne K. Langer, Searle, and Aristotle in the Peri hermeneias  are typical representatives of the former view of language. The communicative view is implicit in Aristotle’s Rhetoric and in Kuhn’s implicit understanding of scientific statements as applicative. The problem with the communicative view is that it can be made explicit only by being talked about, and therefore objectified as object of representational knowledge, which gives the appearance of justifying the representational view of language that results from reflecting on language rather than from using language.

As an epistemologist, that is, as a philosopher looking for absolute certainty, Aristotle subscribes to the representational view of language. Indeed, his epistemology is grounded in a conception of language in which statements, as verbal expressions of judgments, are privileged as representational of reality to which all other linguistic forms are subordinated. However, his

notion of practical knowledge, which is an explosive mixture of representationalism and non-representationalism, stands as an inassimilable oddity in his epistemological system.

Searle’s case is even more curious. As a subscriber of the speech-acts theory, which is based on the notion of linguistic effects , and therefore on the behavior of linguistic agents, one would expect him to overcome representationalism and privilege ‘communicativism.’  However, his view of language as a saying or stating system is inspired by his philosophical background, and thus he falls back on representationalism. Indeed, he makes of understanding what is said the essential illocutionary effect of language to which all other effects are subordinated. In this way Searle renders explicit the representational view of language implicit in Aristotle’s ideal science.

The Catholic Church has a venerable and, in a way, unique tradition of the centrality of doctrine, which is to be preserved. Unfortunately, the Western Church’s alliance with Athens, particularly with Athens as understood or misunderstood by the philosophically-minded medievals who were fascinated by Aristotle’s notion of science, has led the Church to consider the language of doctrine as representational of reality rather than as a practical or applicative, communicative ensemble of speech acts whose essential locutionary effect is not understanding, or even understanding with assent, but disposition to act in response. The disputes on transubstantiation are a typical example of the representational view of doctrine. (I suspect that the whole notion of analogy in the language about God, as a middle term between univocal or exact representation and equivocal or simple misrepresentation, as well as the notion that God’s essence is unknowable to us, indicate the underlying representational view of language). The medieval controversy on whether theology can be a scientific discipline –in the Aristotelian sense of being composed of necessarily true doctrines to be assented to by a correlative “act of faith”– a controversy that was finally settled by the common adoption of Aquinas’s affirmative answer, contributed enormously to consecrate the representational view of doctrine sustained by an implicit, undisputed, taken for granted  Searlean view of language.*

* For interesting points on the Aristotelian idea of science, on transubstantiation, on analogy, and on essence, see C. Dumont, “La reflexion sur la methode theologique,” in Nouvelle Revue Theologique , December 1962, January 1962.
