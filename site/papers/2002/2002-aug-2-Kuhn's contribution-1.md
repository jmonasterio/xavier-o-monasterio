It concerns the difference between judgment and its verbal expression in a statement. They are neither the same nor equivalent. Judgment (in the case of the originator of the judgment –who may be compared to the originator of a map meant to guide others– , not in the case of those to whom the judgment is communicated in a statement) precedes the verbal statement in which it is expressed (either as an aide-memoire for oneself or for purposes of communication to other people, or simply because there is no thought  without verbalization), in the same way as knowing how to reach a place (a skill), in the case of a person who draws a map, precedes the actually drawn map. My reference to a map is not accidental, for judgment (whether universal or particular-factual) is practical in that it is not a static representation of what is the case but inclines or disposes the judge to act in a certain way in respect of what is judged. Moreover, though most likely we cannot judge without verbalizing, the judge’s internal wording does not function in the same way as the external words in which the judgment is stated function for the addressee. In the judge the words flow from and express the judgement, which itself consists in a practical disposition to respond to the judged, to the object as seen practically. The judge doesn’t need the statement to guide him in action, anymore than a person who knows a territory  and draws a map for others to reach it needs the map in order to reach the place. By contrast, the person taught through a statement relies on the statement of the particular ‘fact’ or of the universal ‘law’ until he or she, through  applicative practice, develops the practical disposition or habitual ability in which judgment consists. Thus, even for the person who receives the statement from the judge, the statement is meant to function as the judgement functions in the judge, that is, as an instrument, as (so to say) an internalized map, not as a representation of reality, much less as reality itself. Preface to the study of Kuhn’s views

MOVE TO WHEREVER NOTE 4 IS

In the very first paragraph of the The Structure of Scientific Revolutions Kuhn attacks “the image of science by which we are now possessed,” and states his own purpose:

“that image has previously been drawn... mainly from the study of finished scientific achievements as those are recorded in the classics  and, more recently, in the textbooks from which each new scientific generation learns to practice its trade. Inevitably, however, the aim of such books is persuasive and pedagogic; a concept of science drawn from them is no more likely to fit the enterprise that produced them than an image of a national culture drawn from a tourist brochure or a language text. This essay attempts to show that we have been misled by them in fundamental ways. Its aim is a sketch of the quite different concept of science that can emerge from the historical record of the research activity itself” ^(F1)  

Part and parcel of the textbook view of science, as Kuhn points out, is the notion that science grows by cumulative accretion of facts and laws and theories, much as a building develops by accretion of single bricks and of successive superposed layers of bricks. To put it more concretely, each innovative scientist contributes a brick or two to the edifice, and each generation of innovative scientists contributes another layer of observed facts and laws and theories, and so on. From this process results the progress of science in the knowledge of the universe.

Kuhn’s idea of the development of science is radically different. “Nor

mal science” appears after a pre-paradigmatic period in which there is no fundamental agreement among the practitioners; each scientist moves on his or her own, according to his or her own best lights. Such continues to be the case until a “paradigm” emerges that gains general acceptance and inaugurates a period of “normal science.”. Normal science is a problem-solving activity characterized by a shared view of the nature of the entities that constitute the particular field and, correlatively, by a shared manner of solving the problems typical of the field. Some of the entities that constitute the particular field show irregularities that threaten the shared view. However, rather than renouncing the shared view because of the irregularities, scientists live with those irregularities in the hope of being able to assimilate them to the shared view. This happens to be the case with many of the irregularities, which prove, in the long run, to have been  merely apparent, when a solution to accommodate them within the prevailing  paradigm is found. However, some other of the  irregularities that threaten the paradigm because of their

incompatibility with it  may in the long run provoke a crisis. The crisis, in its turn, may lead to the abandonment of the current paradigm, but this occurs only when an alternative view of the nature of the entities that constitute the field and, correspondingly, a new manner of solving the problems typical of the field emerges and rallies the universal allegiance of the practicing scientists. Such an event, that is, the shift of paradigms, constitutes what Kuhn calls a  “revolution.”

Herein, for the most part, lies the explosiveness of Kuhn’s position. According to the common view of science, represented by the textbook image, science genuinely grows . The truths discovered  by each generation of innovative scientists are preserved, and the next generation adds to the previously known scientific truths constituted by facts and laws and theories. Consequently, there is genuine growth in scientific truth, because each generation of scientists preserves the truths established by the members of the previous generation and adds to them. In this way, science is asymptotically moving toward the possession of full knowledge of the universe. In Kuhn’s opinion, however, the case is very different. Within a given paradigm, there is  growth indeed, in that the problem-solving activity progresses further and further. ^(F2) With a shift of paradigms, however, there is no preservation of the previous scientific knowledge and, consequently, no genuine addition to it. There simply is a revolution, a new scientific beginning – a new science, literally, defined by a new paradigm . Indeed, what was previously taken for genuine knowledge ceases to be accepted as such. That, in Kuhn’s opinion, is what happened when the Copernican paradigm was substituted by the Newtonian one, and again when the Newtonian paradigm was substituted by the Einsteinian one. In other words, Copernican, Newtonian, and Einsteinian physics are three entirely different physics in which the putative ‘truths’ of the previous physics were replaced by new and different, but no less putative, ‘truths ,’ since in the short or long run they happened to be superseded by other so-called ‘truths,’ at least in the first two cases. And, if past history is to be taken seriously, there is no reason to think that this process reached its end with the advent of Einsteinian physics. Hence the optimistic idea that science is asymptotically moving toward the total truth is an illusion. In Kuhn’s own softer words to the same effect,

“We may... have to relinquish the notion, implicit or explicit, that changes of paradigm carry scientists and those who learn from them closer and closer to truth.” ^(F3)

Of course, nothing more is needed to understand why Kuhn has been accused of relativism and even skepticism, or the furious attacks of which his conception of science has been the object on the part of those who think that the very function that the experiment fulfills in the experimental sciences is to demonstrate the validity of the scientific facts, laws, and theories, or at the minimum, as Popper argues, to falsify and exclude those that are wrong.

Whether or not such accusations and attacks are justified is a question that I will leave for later. As for Kuhn’s views themselves on the paradigmatic character of science, on the paradigm-dependency of scientific, laws and theories, and on  paradigmatic revolutions, they are so widely known and have been explored and discussed to such an extent and for so long that a more detailed presentation of them would be superfluous. At any rate, whatever not yet pointed out may need to be said concerning them, I will deal with it as the opportunity requires. Rather, then, than repeating what has already been said many times concerning Kuhn’s views, I will try to refocus the treatment of Kuhn’s position..  

Science as a problem-solving activity

In the first page of his book Kuhn himself  told us that his purpose is to find and  propose an alternative to the conception of science  perpetuated by the textbooks.  I have already indicated that the Kuhnian image involves the paradigmatic character of normal science, the paradigm-dependency of scientific facts, laws and theories, as well as periodic shifts of paradigms, with the threatening implication concerning the development of science through revolutionary changes. However, these characteristics of the Kuhnian view of science, on which most of the attention has been concentrated, are only consequences that flow from what he himself, after much reflection, took for his most important contribution:

“The paradigm as shared example is the central element of what I now take to be the most novel and least understood aspect of this book. Exemplars will therefore require more attention than the other sort of components of the disciplinary matrix. Philosophers of science have not ordinarily discussed the problems encountered by a student in laboratories or in science texts, for these are thought to supply only practice in the application of what the student already knows. He cannot, it is said, solve problems at all unless he has first learned the theory and some rules for applying it. Scientific knowledge is embedded in theory and rules; problems are supplied to gain facility in their application. I have tried to argue, however, that this localization of the cognitive content of science is wrong. ^(F4)

to wit:   the shift from the traditional image of science as a knowledge-acquiring activity to an INSTRUMENTAL problem-solving activity , together with the abandonment that this entails of the traditional conception of knowledge and truth presupposed by the commonly accepted textbook image .

We may gain a first insight into the difference between the textbook image of science as a knowledge-acquiring activity and Kuhn’s own view of science as a problem-solving activity if we pay close attention to the contrast between the two views as sketched by Kuhn. In the following paragraph he identifies what is traditionally understood by knowledge as the accurate representation of reality . Furthermore, he identifies the traditional view of science, reflected in the scientific textbooks, as a knowledge-acquiring activity, that is, as an activity in which there is continuous progress in  the accurate representation of reality. For his part,   Kuhn denies that the scientific activity yields knowledge in the sense of an accurate, and even  in the sense of an at least increasingly accurate, representation of the world. In Kuhn’s view, the result of the scientific activity, when successful, is not an increase in  knowledge in the just defined sense but an increase in  problem-solving ability. In other words, he moves from the traditional notion of science, represented by the textbook view, to a conception of science as instrument for problem-solving . “ A scientific theory is usually felt better than its predecessors... because it is somehow representation of what nature is really like . One often hears that successive theories grow closer to, or approximate more closely to the truth. Apparently generalizations like that refer not to the puzzle-solutions and concrete predictions derived from a theory but rather to its ontology, to the match, that is, between the entities with which the theory populates nature and what is ’really’ there. Perhaps there is some other way of saving the notion of ‘truth’ for application to whole theories , but this one will not do. There is, I think, no theory-independent way to reconstruct phrases like ‘really there’; the notion of a match between the ontology of a theory and its ‘real’ counterpart in nature now seems to me illusive in principle. Besides, as a historian, I am impressed by the implausibility of the view. I do not doubt, for example, that Newton’s mechanics improves on Aristotle’s and that Einstein’s improves on Newton’s as instruments for puzzle-solving. But I can see in their succession no coherent direction of ontological development. On the contrary, in some important respects, though by no means in all, Einstein’s general theory of  relativity is closer to Aristotle’s than to Newton’s. Though the temptation to describe that position as relativistic is understandable, the description seems to me wrong.” ^(F5)

Let us be as precise as possible. KUHN LIMITS HIS EXPLORATION TO THE FIELD OF THE EXPERIMENTAL SCIENCES. One of his main conclusions is that, in this particular field, there is no such thing as knowledge as accurate representation of reality. Whether or not the conception of knowledge as accurate representation of what is the case finds application in other fields is not his business at all. He is not doing what might be called general epistemology or theory of knowledge. He is not attacking the epistemological notion or knowledge as accurate representation of reality in general. His point is simply that the notion of knowledge as accurate representation of reality finds no application in the domain of the experimental sciences, and of physics in particular. Now, though Kuhn is not doing epistemology or general theory of knowledge, nonetheless his position has great epistemological significance for at least two reasons. First,  the physical experimental sciences constitute the supreme model of scientific knowledge. Consequently, if the traditional notion of knowledge finds no application in what is supposed to be its supreme instantiation, there is little chance of its functioning in other fields. Two, the traditional notion of knowledge expelled by Kuhn from the  field of the experimental sciences has, as a matter of fact, been under attack, and has already been banned by many, from other fields, among which Kuhn himself mentions “parts of philosophy, psychology, linguistics, and even art history,” ^(F6) in which one would expect the traditional  notion of knowledge to be functional and  find application. But if that is not the case, what could possibly  be the point of clinging to a notion of knowledge that apparently finds no application in any important field? *   

* In rejecting the notion of knowledge as correspondence with the real even if he limits himself to the field of the experimental sciences, Kuhn is fighting against a long and deeply ingrained epistemic tradition in which he himself and we all have been steeped. Indeed this idea of knowledge , which appears to be commonsensical and which is in fact  based on a superficial reflection on visual perception and on the language of statements, that knowledge consists in the match or exact correspondence between what the knower sees or states and what is the case in reality , received its philosophical consecration from Plato. But how can one be sure that what one sees actually corresponds to what is the case? After all, everyone has had the experience of having sometimes been wrong concerning perceptual facts after being convinced of being right. In other words, the notion of knowledge as correspondence to what is the case, coupled with the obvious fact that putative knowledge proves some times to be false, that is, not to be genuine knowledge, created the problem of discerning between merely apparent and genuine knowledge. To distinguish the two, at least in name, Plato called the former “opinion” and the latter “knowledge,” and he tackled the problem of discerning the two in the following way. Only those realities can be object of “knowledge” which are absolutely unchangeable or permanent because they exist necessarily and are necessarily as they are. For if the knower grasps such necessity, then the knower grasps that what he or she knows necessarily corresponds to what is the case. Unfortunately, no sensible or perceivable object is of that kind, for all of them are subject to change in the flux of time. In other words, all sensible objects are contingent rather than necessary. Thus my knowledge of them, even assuming that my perceptions actually corresponded at a certain instant to what is the case, ceases to be true as the perceived objects change. Consequently Plato felt constrained to relegate all the particular objects of sensible perception to the realm of opinion or merely putative knowledge. Furthermore,  he determined that only the intellect (as opposed to the senses), which was capable of grasping what is necessarily the case, could yield knowledge (as opposed to opinion), and only in the case of the intelligible (as opposed to sensible) realities, for by the very fact of being purely intelligible rather than material these intelligible objects are not subject to temporal change. The notion of “scientific knowledge,” that is, of a knowledge whose unshakeable, objective certainty is of a different kind than the merely subjective feeling of certainty that accompanies  what is ordinarily taken for knowledge, has its origin in Plato.

As to the avatars of the notion of knowledge as correspondence with what is the case in reality, and the intense scrutiny and criticism to which it has been subjected for centuries, the story has been told a hundred times. For my purpose, it may suffice to say that Kuhn is not alone, by far, in finding such notion defective. It is fair to say that Kant, who gave up on the notion of knowledge as correspondence with the real on the basis that, since we have no independent means of access to the real other than knowledge, we cannot possibly find out whether or not what we take for knowledge actually corresponds to “the thing itself,” may be seen, in this particular regard, as Kuhn’s direct predecessor. But wrongly. For unlike Kant, who dogmatically continued to maintain the validity of the synthetic a priori judgments in the domain of the physical sciences on the basis of the intuition  that confirms their validity, an intuition which is absent in all other domains, Kuhn gives up completely on  the traditional notion of knowledge of which Kant never managed to get rid of. Indeed, while unable to justify the ontological correspondence to the thing in itself, that is, to reality, of the synthetic a priori judgments in the domain of the physical sciences, and of the practical judgments in the domain of faith in God and in freedom, Kant continued to postulate their truth without modifying the traditional notion of knowledge as representational of what is the case. That indeed is the reason why, while denying the possibility of truth as correspondence with the thing in itself, Kant had to preserve the notion of the thing in itself without which the epistemic validity of any judgment whatsoever was simply inconceivable. However, to understand this radical difference between Kuhn and Kant as well as Kuhn’s own alternative position, we have to backtrack.

We just heard Kuhn declare “illusive,” that is, illusional, “the notion of a match between the ontology of a theory and its ‘real’ counterpart in nature.” In other words Kuhn is simply giving up, in the domain of the physical sciences, on the traditional notion of knowledge as accurate representation of the real. Yet, in the very same paragraph, while finding understandable the inference that his position is relativistic, he considers the inference “wrong.” How can this be?

In The Structure of Scientific Revolutions, Kuhn  tells us several times how difficult he finds it to escape from the representational conception of knowledge, a powerful epistemological tradition that he refers back to Descartes and that he finds reflected in the textbook image of science. According to this “textbook” image, nothing like what Kuhn calls shifts of paradigms ever occurs. They amount simply to different interpretations by the scientists of the same brute data:

“Many readers will surely want to say that what changes with a paradigm is only the scientist’s interpretation of observations that themselves are fixed once and for all by the nature of the environment and of the perceptual apparatus. On this view, Priestley and Lavoisier both saw oxygen, but they interpreted their observations differently; Aristotle and Galileo both saw pendulums, but they differed in their interpretations of what they both had seen.

Let me say at once that this very usual view of what occurs when scientists change their minds about fundamental matters can be neither all wrong nor a mere mistake. Rather it is an essential part of a philosophical paradigm initiated by Descartes and developed at the same time as Newtonian dynamics. That paradigm has served both science and philosophy well. Its explanation, like that of dynamics itself, has been fruitful of a fundamental understanding that perhaps could not have been achieved in no other way. But as the example of Newtonian dynamics also indicates, even the most striking past success provides no guarantee that crisis can be indefinitely postponed. Today research in parts of philosophy, psychology, linguistics, and even art history, all converge to suggest that the traditional paradigm is somehow askew. That failure to fit is also made increasingly apparent by the historical study of science to which most of our attention is necessarily directed here. ^(F7)

Notice that Kuhn refers to the understanding initiated by Descartes and developed in Newton’s dynamics, which he sees reflected in the textbook image of science, as “a philosophical paradigm.” Moreover, he sees it as  a paradigm in a crisis that cannot be indefinitely postponed .*

* CONFUSION BY KUHN. The philosophical ‘paradigm’ he is attacking is the representational view of knowledge and language, whose main representatives are Plato, Aristotle and Searle, not the scientific Cartesian paradigm of “particles,” that he characterizes on p.    . Now, whether a philosophical paradigm that enunciates what is the case concerning knowledge and language is really a paradigm, given that paradigms are characterized by their being “Tacit,” shows the complication of Kuhn’s confusion. WHAT HE IS FIGHTING IN HIS BOOK IS THE SO-CALLED PHILOSOPHICAL PARADIGM (See pp. 8-9). HE FIGHTS IT WITH HISTORY, BY SHOWING THAT THE CHARACTERIZATION OF SCIENCE BY THE TEXTBOOKS MAKES IT INTO A REPRESENTATION OF REALITY, WHICH IT IS NOT, AND BY SHOWING THAT IT ACTUALLY IS A MATTER OF TACIT KNOWLEDGE, THAT IS “KNOWLEDGE THAT IS ACQUIRED THROUGH PRACTICE AND THAT CANNOT BE ARTICULATED EXPLICITLY” (P. 44, NOTE), THAT IS, CANNOT BE ARTICULATED IN A STATEMENT OR STATEMENTS THAT ENUNCIATE WHAT IS THE CASE OR THE RULES TO BE FOLLOWED, BUT ONLY IN A NON-REPRESENTATIONAL ENUNCIATIVE SPEECH ACT TO BE LEARNED BY PRACTICE AND TO RESULT IN PRACTICE. It is clear, then, that in looking for an alternative to the textbook view of science which is itself paradigm-dependent, he is attempting to bring about a shift of paradigms not in physics itself, that is in the way physics is practiced, but in the philosophy of physics, that is, in the way in which the practice and the results of physics are interpreted when reflected upon.

To put it otherwise, what needs to be changed is not the way in which science is actually practiced, nor the way in which  it is practically (not predicatively and explicitly) conceived by its practitioners when they practice it, as revealed by their actions , but the way in which science is conceived and talked about or characterized by those who reflect on the scientific activity, and thereby objectify it, be they scientists or not. Their predicative or formulated or explicit conception is at odds with the practice of the scientific agents it is supposed to characterize, because, as Kuhn says, their ‘image [of science] has... been drawn mainly from the study of finished scientific achievements” (p. 1). I previously argued that Searle’s linguistic characterizations (that is, his formulated, explicit, PREDICATIVE-OBJECTIVE conception) of the way language functions is at odds with the actual practice of the linguistic agents IN THE SAME WAY AS THE PREDICATIVE CONCEPTION OF THE SKEPTICS WHOM HUME CRITICIZES, WHO USE DOORS RATHER THAN WINDOWS, IS AT ODDS WITH THEIR FORMULATED SKEPTICISM, WITH THEIR EXPLICIT SKEPTICAL BELIEFS. I also argued that the reason for that disparity is that Searle confused the practical interiorization of the competent linguistic agents, which is gained through practice and is constituted by, and manifested in, their actual competent linguistic performance , AND WHICH CAN NOT BE EXPRESSED EQUIVALENTLY IN WORDS THAT CORRESPOND TO WHAT IS THE CASE, with the theoretical PREDICATIVE interiorization made by the theoretician who contemplates language as an object of study, and which is manifested not in his or her linguistic performance but in his or her statements of the rules that linguistic agents supposedly follow, when in fact they have no (objective) knowledge of those rules, that is, any consciousness of those rules as object of knowledge, any predicative or positional awareness of those rules . (A similar confusion concerning language is reflected in at least one possible understanding of  Goethe’s famous dictum, “ Wer keine fremede Sprachen weisst, kennst nicht von ihrem eigenem.”

As if in order to be a competent linguistic agent in a particular language required the theoretical or predicative knowledge of its grammar. Of course, this is belied by the fact that children are perfectly capable of understanding and expressing themselves in complex sentences without any theoretical knowledge of the language’s grammar). The reason for this is that the linguistic  agent’s consciousness is not consciousness of object, but Tacit knowledge, that is, practical, instrumental knowledge, knowing how that is not expressed in rules for action but in a practical disposition to act in a certain way manifested in the actual action, not in a statement.. In other words, the tacitly known (constituted by THE NON-PREDICATIVE PRACTICAL BELIEFS OF THE AGENT, MANIFESTED BY HIS OR HER ACTIONS) does not function as object of knowledge but as that “from which,” as the instrument with  which the  agent guides his or her linguistic action, much as the pianist’s playing is instrumentally guided by the signs traced on the partition which are not known in themselves by the pianist but merely ‘inhabited” by him, much as the spectacles are inhabited rather than known by the agent who uses them.* The signs on the partition are not a representation of the music, but the  “from where” the musician plays the music.Notice, however, that in the case of the pianist who plays a piece already written or formulated, the practical reading or ‘knowledge’ of the signs in a certain way precedes the action it informs; but the inverse is the case with the musician who creates or invents the music, for he or she doesn’t read the music in order to play it for the first time; rather he plays it, and the played music will be the basis for the writing of the music in order to communicate it to other future players. THIS IS THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN SPOKEN AND WRITTEN LANGUAGE.

* BUT WHAT IF THE CORRECT MODEL FOR LINGUISTIC COMMUNICATION HAPPENS TO BE THE CASE OF THE MUSIC TO BE PLAYED, RATHER THAN, AS SEARLE THINKS, THE ALREADY FORMULATED TEXT THAT SAYS-ENUNCIATES WHAT IT MEANS?

There is a difference, though, between the signs on a musical partition and  linguistic signs, be they written or spoken. Such, at any rate, is Searle’s conviction, who expresses the difference  as follows: words are the only signs that not only mean, that is, refer to something, which is the distinctive characteristic of all signs, but the only signs that mean by saying or enuntiating what they mean. [THAT IS, BY MAKING KNOWN WHAT THEY MEAN]. Linguistic communication, therefore, amounts, on the speaker’s side, to saying what he or she means, and, on the interlocutor’s side, to gaining the knowledge of what the speaker means by comprehending it. True, by saying something the speaker may want to produce or obtain a number of other effects aside from making known what he or she means, as is the case with all the speech acts that intend perlocutionary effects on the different speech acts, but the only effect the speaker cannot fail to want to produce when he or she says something is making known what he or she means, which is Searle’s “illocutionary effect.” Not only is this effect essential in the sense that this is what communication is primarily about, to wit, making known what one means to communicate, but also in the sense that the illocutionary effect is the necessary condition for the realization of  all the other effects the speaker may intend to obtain. Indeed, if I make a request, my request cannot be granted by the addressee unless he or she knows what I say, the request that I make. Having understood my request, the addressee may or may not grant it, but certainly he or she cannot grant it if he or she does not know my request. Saying, therefore, is intending to make known what one means. And this implies that what is said is always something to be known. Now, since the distinctive characteristic of language is expressing what it means, it follows that saying is always an enunciative speech act that expresses what the speaker intends to make known . Sometimes, though not always, but only in the case in which perlocutionary effects are also intended, the enunciation also comprehends other effects intended by the speaker which are not merely to be known by the addressee but to be executed or suffered by the addressee, such as granting what is requested, or being amused in the case of a joke told.

My point is that Searle makes of all speech acts enunciative acts, for what he primarily  understands by saying is “enunciating” what one means in order to make it known. Thus, requesting is enunciating a request, threatening is enunciating a threat, promising is enunciating a promise... to make them known to the addressee. Hence what Searle means by saying that the distinctive feature of linguistic signs is that they say what the speaker means is that they enunciate what the speaker means.

Does it follow from this that Searle reduces all the speech act to statements? No, because ‘saying’ and ‘enunciating’ are the same thing, but not ‘saying’ and ‘stating.’ What distinguishes the enunciative sentences of which Aristotle speaks in his Peri hermeneias is that not only do they enunciate or say or put in words what the speaker wants to communicate by making it known, but that, in addition ,  they convey the speaker’s claim that what is said is true . Now for that claim to be possible the sentence has to refer to something as being or not being the case. This is what Aristotle called, on the speaker’s part as a knower, a “judgment.” Obviously, however, as manifested by multitude of perlocutionary speech acts, such is not the case with the majority of the meaningful sentences through which a speaker intends to make known to the addressee what he or she wants to communicate; only statements fulfill this condition. In short, all meaningful speech acts express or enunciate what the speaker intends to communicate or make known, but only statements express what is the case in reality as judged by the speaker, as well as the speaker’s commitment to the truth of what he affirms or denies.

Let us contrast this –that is enunciations and statements– with the signs on a musical partition to distinguish the latter from linguistic signs. In Aristotle’s and Searle’s view, what is stated corresponds to what is the case in the sense of saying it so as to make it known, for that is what t he truth of judgments consists in. Certainly musical signs, unlike linguistic signs, don’t say what they mean, for they do not enunciate the music. Actually, they don’t say anything, for they don’t enunciate anything. YET THEY MEAN SOMETHING, for they are signs. Now we tend to think that the pianist understands and interprets the signs by actually producing the music meant by the musician. If so, what the signs on the partition mean is the music played by the musician. The musician understands the signs when he ‘reads’ them. Yet the signs don’t mean in the sense of saying the music, of enunciating something to be known. Rather “reading, “ in this case, consists in the production by the reader, that is, the pianist, of the action that will create the music meant, for such is the intention of the writer of the partition. . The same in the case of “reading” a map that was drawn to give me directions from where I am to where I want to go. The same in the case of “reading” a traffic light: stopping when the light is red is the correct reading or understanding, for such was the intention of the person who set the sign. In all these cases, then the reading is applicative, NOT REPRESENTATIONAL OF WHAT IS THE CASE, AND NOT EVEN IMPERATIVE AS THE LANGUAGE OF A RECIPE THAT ENUNCIATES IN A RULE WHAT IS TO BE DONE. By this I mean that the ‘understanding’ consists in an action, not in a knowing or an understanding thanks to which something becomes objectively known. And such is the case with all the speech acts that aim at producing  a perlocutionay effect. From the speaker’s point of view, the proper understanding of a request is, not the understanding or knowing that a request was made , but the granting of what is requested; the proper understanding of a command is, not the knowledge that a command was made, but the execution of what was commanded.     

Now, Searle makes the understanding by which something becomes known the distinctive effect of ALL linguistic acts, whereas he makes the understanding that consists in an action (or an affection) the effect of certain particular speech acts called perlocutionary. Moreover, he claims that this latter effect is parasitic on the former, whereas the illocutionary effect is independent from the perlocutionary one. However, in chapter II I showed that knowledge of what the speaker means is not the effect intended by competent speakers and ‘understood’ by competent hearers even in the case of statements, which because of their enunciative appearance are the best candidates to represent Searle’s contention that all saying expresses the intention of making something known. In showing that, my purpose was to point out that the illocutionary effect, the mere intention of making known what the speaker has in mind to communicate, is an illusion. In other words, the proper understanding or reading of all speech acts, statements included,  is applicative and is fulfilled either by the actual action intended by the speaker or, in case of delayed application for lack of the appropriate opportunity, by the habitual disposition to act in that manner at the appropriate time.

For his part, with his notion of Tacit Knowledge and the analysis of its application in numerous cases, Polanyi prepared us to tackle the particular case of oral statements, and by the same token the meaning of laws and theories in the physical experimental sciences, which is the area explored by Kuhn. For it prepared us to approach this subject with the realization that there is no such a thing as objective knowledge, that all direct understanding is applicative rather thn representational, and that no verbal formulation of an understanding means in the sense of saying and meaning to communicate what is objectively the case. As music cannot be said but only played in order to be music, and as musical language does not say the peace of music and does not even enuntiate the rules to be followed to play the music, so scientific statements do not say what is the case in reality and do not even enuntiate the rules to be followed to deal with it. To speak of tacit knowledge amounts to saying that there is no way of expressing in enunciations that say in words what is tacitly or practically or instrumentally ‘known,’ precisely because tacit knowledge is not objective or representational knowledge and not equivalent to it, As the only way only way to ‘understand’ the music meant by the musical signs is producing it, so the only way to understand a law is by applyint it appropriately.. Objective knowledge is an illusion that results from objectifying reflection, similar to the illusion that identifies oral with written communication and thereby reduces the former to the latter.  

This long preface to the study of Kuhn’s views will prove or disprove its usefulness in the measure that it throws light or fails to throw light on that  subject.

This may be illustrated by an example . According to Searle’s conception or notion of the

linguistic activity , as we saw it in the first chapter, linguistic communication between speaker and addressee consists in the acquisition by the addressee of knowledge of  what the speaker intends to communicate when he says something. By “knowledge” Searle means accurate view gained by the addressee of what the speaker intends to communicate. Such is the idea of linguistic communication  reflected in Searle’s general “characterization” of the linguistic activity as well as in his particular characterizations of what is going on,  in the case of the diverse speech acts, when a speaker says something to an addressee. Polanyi, for his part, has a very different conception of the linguistic activity, for he does not think that the speaker’s intention in saying is to communicate the objective knowledge of what he intends to say when he says something. The speaker’s intention is, rather, either to affect in a particular way the addressee, or to induce the addressee to act in a certain way. Now, it might seem that the difference in the way a practice –in this case, the linguistic communicative practice–  is conceived by the people who engage in the practice has no importance at all in that it does not affect the actual practice. Indeed, though their respective conceptions of the linguistic practice differ, nevertheless as linguistic agents both Searle and Polanyi seem to act linguistically in the same way. But such is not the case. It is true that, to take a particular instance, both Searle and Polanyi expect to be obeyed when they give an order to their young children, though Searle thinks that what he intends is to communicate the knowledge of the order he is giving, not directly his interlocutor’s obedience. The addressee’s obedience is conditioned by the knowledge of what the speaker means, and  whether or not the obedient action  obtains is irrelevant to the success of the linguistic act as such, for it aims exclusively at communicating the knowledge of what the speaker means.

However, it is not true that the fact that Searle’s idea of the linguistic practice differs from Polanyi’s makes no difference. If Searle were consistent with his conception of the linguistic activity, he should expect his child to understand that in giving an order to his child Searle intended to say or state an order to be understood and known, not directly an order to be obeyed.. Accordingly, Searle should consider his child’s obedience as irrelevant, as long as the child acknowledged the order received. In fact, my argument against Searle in Chapter I was that, given his conception of the linguistic practice, Searle should be perfectly satisfied as a linguistic agent if, instead of obeying his order,  his child just told him that he understood the order he had received, for Searle intended to obtain nothing else and nothing more by his linguistic command. Of course, my claim as a competent linguistic agent is that  neither Searle nor any other linguistic agent in the described situation would be satisfied with that response.

That is the way children learn to play baseball, and to speak their native language, and to read –by practicing it. But how, concretely, do physicists do physics? The whole of Kuhn’s argument shows that

they, at any rate, don’t do it in the way in which physics is characterized in the textbooks, anymore than geometricians do geometry in a purely demonstrative way, but applicatively, by solving problems. Their collection of “facts” through observation and measurement, as well as their facts themselves, are paradigm-dependent. Their theories are not automatically abandoned when they are at odds with certain observations; rather, they live with those ‘irregularities,’ in the hope of assimilating them to the paradigm in the future. This happens sometimes, but in other cases the irregularities prove to be un-assimilable and force a change of paradigms. In short, contrary to the textbook view of science, neither are the perceived ‘facts’ the result of neutral observation, that is, pure of all interpretation, nor does the experiment play the role of automatic verification of the scientific hypotheses, nor are the hypotheses automatically dismissed when unsupported by experiment. Rather, collection of data, facts, hypotheses, and experiments are paradigm-driven. Now all these unfulfilled demands of the textbook view which  Kuhn rightly equates with the demands of the Cartesian philosophical paradigm flow from the assumption that for knowledge to be genuine knowledge it has to be “objective knowledge,” that is, it has to be dictated by strict observation and measurement of what is the case, and its hypothetical component has to correspond to what is the case.

Thus in its negative aspect Kuhn’s argument can be fairly summarized as a dismissal of the notion of objective knowledge in the area of the experimental physical sciences, which after all is the area in which that notion of knowledge seemed bound to find its field of application par excellence.

But what is the positive aspect of Kuhn’s view of science? In other words, what is the view with which he proposes to substitute the textbook view? Here Kuhn’s answer is less definite. True, he maintains that he is neither a skeptic nor a relativist, but without giving any fully explicit explanation for his denial. In all fairness to him, however, we may acknowledge that implicit explanation may be gathered from the text of The Structure... He is not a skeptic, though he denies that science progresses towards the truth, that is towards ontological correspondence with what is the case, because he is nonetheless convinced that the succession of interpretive paradigms is accompanied by a progress in our problem-solving ability. He is not a relativist, either, because, although he grants that our views of the physical universe change radically with the successive shifts of paradigms, he is convinced nonetheless that not any view will do. As he puts it,

“To say that the members of different groups may have different perceptions when confronted with the same stimuli is not to imply that they may just have any perceptions at all. In many environments a group that could not tell wolves from dogs could not endure. Nor would a group of nuclear physicists today survive as scientists if unable to recognize the tracks of alpha particles and electrons. It is just because very few ways of seeing will do that the ones that have withstood the tests of group use are worth transmitting form generation to generation.” ^(F8)

Yet all this is too little to satisfy us that Kuhn has a definite view of science, much less that he has provided us with a definite substitute for the textbook view of science. To realize Kuhn’s predicament and our own in this regard, we have only to remember that Kuhn himself has reminded us that no paradigm is ever abandoned unless a better, more promising one is available, which can barely be the case with an ill-defined paradigm. Moreover, our dissatisfaction with Kuhn’s own substitute for the textbook view, if indeed he has one, is bound to grow  if we pay close attention to his own hesitations in very fundamental matters. Kuhn’s entire argument in The Structure consists in showing that the way in which physics is actually practiced is at odds with the way the practice of physics is conceived by the authors of the scientific textbooks. The reason those authors misconceive physics is that they unwittingly work under the constraints of the paradigm of classical epistemology, which determines what Kuhn calls  the textbook view of science. According to this view , the purpose of each science is to gain knowledge, and knowledge is concretely constituted by judgments-statements that represent correctly what is the case in reality. Now, as Plato became acutely aware of it, what appears to be genuine knowledge, which he called episteme, may turn out to be merely apparent. The latter he called doxa , commonly translated as “belief” or “opinion.” What distinguishes scientific knowledge from other species of knowledge is the quality of its certainty, of the certainty, that is, of its correspondence to what is the case. In the particular case of  the experimental sciences the correspondence between the judgments-statements of the scientists to what is actually the case is insured, as much as that is possible, in the first place by the rigor of the observation of the facts on the basis of which the explanatory hypotheses will be reached, a rigor which is constantly being enhanced by the increasing quality of the instruments employed for the observation and measurement of the phenomena; in the second place, the correspondence of the scientific hypotheses to what is the case is insured  by the repeated experimental confirmation of those hypotheses, which as a result are taken for  laws of reality.

Now, “reality,” in this view, is conceived as that which is self-subsistent

and, more generally of any practice–  may be incorrect, and that, misguided by it , the linguistic agent who believes an incorrect conception to be correct may sincerely continue to cling theoretically to that second-order conception based on reflective or objective  rather than practical interiorization. That is, to affirm and assent to the objective formulations dictated by that  conception,  while continuing, as a linguistic agent to practice or act linguistically in a way that is at odds with his theoretical conception . By contrast,   Hume’s argument, which is similar to Kuhn’s in the field of the experimental sciences, against the skeptics who don’t get out of a room through the window but through the door focuses on a particular case of this inconsistency and underscores the primacy of action over vision, that is of practice over theory. A primacy we all take for granted in our everyday life, for we all agree that “What you do speaks so loud that I cannot hear what you say .”

Now, what does my example about Searle and Polanyi’s different conceptions illustrate concerning both Kuhn’s conception of science and the conception of science of those who buy the very different notion represented by textbook image of science? Several points.

(1) That there is, or at least ought to be, a coherence between the conception and the practice of those who deem the conception correct.

(1) Notice that we are speaking not of any sort of conception or idea, which would apply to the case of “ objekts, ” but exclusively of conceptions of a practice , that is, of a way of acting in which living organisms engage (initially by hereditary reflex or by acquired reflex, later by imitation, still later by project, and still  later by inventive project) .

(3) That reflective conceptions are not irrelevant, since they result or ought to result in a particular practice on the part of those agents who are consistent with their conceptions.

(2) That significantly different conceptions dictate different and incompatible practices, and that different practices imply different conceptions on the agent’s part.

(3) Conceptions dictate, or ought to result in, particular actions characteristic of a practice. They are dictated primarily to the agent who engages or participates in a particular practice. If the practice is social rather than purely individual, as most (or perhaps all) practices are, they are reflected in the habitual actions of the community and expressed in the “symbola fidei” of the community. These symbola cohere with the foundational (or at least exemplary) narratives of the community because they result from reflection on those narratives. The practices dictated by different conceptions are incompatible because each conception results in a different practical self-understanding of the practitioner agent in relation to his or her world. Now, because the self-understanding is practical, it is to be incarnated in action rather than in objective statements-about or in blind recipes for action,. Hence conceptions are necessarily “tacit. ” Thus, for instance, the self-understanding of the agent Galileo in relation to the world, that underpinned  his law of falling bodies on inclineds, was tacit. It could not be expressed as self-understanding otherwise than by his applicative practice in particular cases, and application can only be learned through practice. In Galileo’s case, the action informed by the practical judgment as disposition to respond came first and it generated the explicit vision of object for response, that is, the law for action. In the case of Galileo’s  followers, the quasi-blind action comes first, and in time it generates the practical vision or judgment, that is, the habit to see the objects as objects for response. Pascal’s advise in the order of faith may be an illustration of this: If you want to believe, start practicing by imitation of those who believe, reciting their words and reproducing their gestures blindly, until the ‘vision’ or ‘understanding’ comes. What else does the child who is learning to make additions but to replicate blindly the words and applicative gestures of his teacher until the tacit understanding comes which is manifested in the child’s ability to make additions when appropriate? The same with learning how to speak, and later how to read.

(4) AND VERY IMPORTANT: That conceptions may be implicit or “tacit” (as “paradigms” actually are); that even when they are explicit or stated they are to be ‘understood’ not theoretically, as resemblant reproductions, but practically, as guiding, dictating or at least authorizing an action. Hence, genuine understanding shows in applicative action, not in mere words that supposedly resemble what is the case.

(4) Most of what needs to be said in this respect has already been said under (3). I will only add, for the sake of being more clear, that instead of “conceptions,” a word that suggests purely static images of what is the case or ideas about what is the case, we should speak of “convictions,” for this word suggests or connotes  a connection with an action in which they are to be incarnated or realized. In this vein, Camus wrote: “For a man who does not cheat, what he believes to be true must determine his action.”  ( The Myth , “An Absurd Reasoning,” p. 5). However, thinking of the always possible inconsistencies and of the primacy of action over vision or thought, that is, of praxis over theory or doxa , Camus cunningly added: “It is a common place to compare philosophical theories and the behavior of those who profess them... Schopenhauer is often cited as a fit subject for laughter, because he praised suicide while seated at a well-set table... In the face of such contradictions and obscurities must we conclude that there is no relationship between the opinion one has about life and the act one commits to leave it? Let us not exaggerate in this direction. In a man’s attachment to life there is something stronger than all the ills in the world. The body’s judgment is as good as the mind’s and the body shrinks from annihilation. We get into the habit of living before acquiring the habit of thinking . In that race which daily hastens us toward death, the body maintains its irreparable lead ” (Ibid. pp. 6-7). - What I like about this formulation is that it places the instinctive and purely reflex bodily act or unthinking response, and then the purely customary unthinking behavior guided by desire, at the origin of the ‘cognitive’ process, which is in fact an extension

Kuhn’s own crisis as well as his despondency about the philosophical paradigm in which he finds himself trapped is no less clearly expressed a couple of pages later. Referring again to the misconception by the traditionalists of what actually is the result of a new paradigm for a mere different interpretation of the same brute facts, he replies:

“But is sensory experience fixed and neutral? Are theories simply man-made interpretations of given data? The epistemological viewpoint that has most often guided Western philosophy for three centuries dictates an immediate and unequivocal, Yes! In the absence of a developed alternative, I find it impossible to relinquish entirely that viewpoint. Yet it no longer functions effectively, and the attempt to do so though the introduction of a neutral language of observations now seem to me hopeless.” ^(F10)

At least we are more clear now than we initially were about the nature of the historical situation in which both Kuhn and ourselves as members of his own historical  generation are, and of the outcome searched by Kuhn, to wit: a developed alternative to what he identifies as the Cartesian-Newtonian philosophical paradigm that weighs on us into viewing science according to the textbook image. On the basis of this understanding, we may now ask whether, or to what extent, was Kuhn himself successful in developing that alternative.

We get an important clue to answer this question in Kuhn’s own constant comparison, sometimes explicit and more often merely implicit but clear nonetheless, of the practice of science to the practice of a language. For us, who in the two previous chapters explored the way in which language functions, this approach to the functioning of science may prove to be particularly  fruitful. I will go even farther along this line and say that we shall get an enormous amount of light if we realize that the textbook view of science rests on the same assumption on which Searle’s conception of language rests, to wit: that knowledge is to be equated with “objective knowledge.” . In a parallel way, I say that the alternative view of science that Kuhn struggles to articulate rests on the understanding of knowledge as “ tacit, that is, instrumental knowledge ” on which Polanyi’s implicit conception of of language as I developed it in chapter II rests. Moreover, as Searle’s view of language appears initially persuasive to us because it is determined by the Cartesian-Newtonian philosophical  paradigm under whose influence bot h Searle and we live, and yet we realize that it does not reflect the way in which we as linguistic agents actually act, so does Kuhn find it difficult to get rid of the textbook view of science because it is determined by the Cartesian-Newtonian philosophical paradigm under which he lives, while at the same time realizing that this view is at odds with the way in which he and all other scientific agents actually act in the practice of their scientific trade. Finally, as the notion of “tacit knowledge” appears initially improbable if not outright unintelligible to us, because it is at odds with the philosophical paradigm under which we live, and yet, as we apply the idea of instrumental knowledge to the analysis of the actual functioning of language, this notion seems to fit the facts of the case in a way in which the objective knowledge of the Serlean analysis does not, and hence we begin to feel at home within it, so I predict that in the long run both ourselves, and Kuhn too, if he were alive, would find ourselves at home within this instrumental conception of knowledge and of scientific knowledge..

Practical animal knowledge is not representational: Ortho-doxy and ortho-praxis

Do animals know? To Plato, for whom genuine knowledge by contrast to mere belief or opinion ( doxa ) couldn’t be but a deed of the intellect, and who, consequently had an acute sense of the unbridgeable difference between animals and people, such question would have sounded not only trivial and uninteresting but simply wrongheaded. Not so to Aristotle, who, unlike Plato, in time came to distinguish practical knowledge from the purely theoretical knowledge which Plato had exclusively explored, and who therefore could not exclude the possibility of some kind of continuity between animals and people. My interest in Aristotle’s exploration of the issue whether animals know is that it seems to open the way to an  alternative to the traditional notion of knowledge as representation of the real –the sort of alternative Kuhn was obscurely groping for in order to give full shape to his understanding of science.

In my <2002-April-3-Aris-Devereux: Practical Knowledge in Aristotle> I argued that, at least at the stage of his philosophical evolution to which the texts studied by Devereux belong, Aristotle failed to distinguish cleanly practical from theoretical knowledge. The reason for that failure is, I surmise,  that, still very much under Plato’s influence, Aristotle could not yet  help looking in practical knowledge for a universal component characteristic of Plato’s conception of  knowledge, though practical knowledge is unavoidably particular. However, in an alternative view, already less dependent on Plato’s, the distinctive trait of practical knowledge, according to Aristotle,  is its direct and immediate connection with action, its actual ‘terminating’ in action without the mediation of any previous theoretical vision. Let us reflect on this important point in more detail.

The connection with action, in Aristotle’s view,  is not found in theoretical knowledge, whose intrinsic purpose is knowledge for its own sake, the mere grasp of the truth, of what is the case. That is the radical epistemic difference between people and  animals. Animals are capable of attaining only the knowledge that is useful to the preservation and well-being of the individual or of the species to which the animal belongs, while people are capable of both theoretical and practical knowledge. Now, according to this view, the fact that practical knowledge is connected with action does not detract from the fact that it is knowledge, that is,  grasp or ‘vision”of what is the case, for that is what knowledge consists in. To give an example, on the basis of their respective past experience, both lions and people may come to grasp the useful ‘universal’ fact that, say, eating such a plant has beneficial purgative effects, though lions only know that fact practically, that is, for the purpose of applying that knowledge to obtain the benefits of the practice, whereas people know it practically and also theoretically or predicatively, as a stated or statable truth independent of its practical utility.

         

Such  is Aristotle’s conception of practical and theoretical knowledge in the particular -an, I suspect, primitive– version to which we are presently referring. In this version, the difference between practical and theoretical knowledge is purely extrinsic. Indeed, the fact known by both the lion and the human is the same, though the lion knows it only because it is actually useful and only for the purpose of applying the knowledge, whereas the human knows the ‘universal’ fact also theoretically or predicatively. There is, however, another version of practical knowledge. In the one we have so far considered, the connection between knowledge and action is extrinsic, in that the knowledge of the same fact may be applied or not, and it is knowledge whether or not it is applied. But think, in the first place, of the lion’s case. The lion can attain the knowledge of the purgative effects of the herb only through personal experience of those effects, that is by experiencing them by being affected by them,  while the human may come to know the purgative effects of the herb without any personal experience of them, by being told of them by somebody else. To be exact, the lion’s experience is not only experience of the purgative effects, but also experience of eating the plant. In other words, it is experience of both the purgative effects and of the action that is followed by the purgative effects. Thus the lion learns not only the objective side, so to say, namely, the purgative effects the plant produces, but learns also the subjective side, namely, what to do to obtain the effects. Eating the plant becomes the means to obtain the effects.

This learning is experiential in that it is gained through experience . It is a matter not only of effects, but of effects felt (though not necessarily attended to explicitly) by the affected agent and learned because felt . The purgative effects of the action of eating this plant are undergone and experienced, in the sense of being felt; they are not observed or perceived, by the agent who eats the plant. Because the purgative effects are experienced as favorable or pleasant or satisfactory by the agent who experiences the effects, the action of eating the plant will automatically tend to be repeated under the appropriate circumstances in the expectation of similar effects. Actions followed by rewarding effects tend to be repeated; actions followed by unpleasant effects tend to be avoided. And from objects that look similar similar effects are expected. (The expectation is incarnated and manifested to us in the animal’s conduct, not in statements, of which animals of course are incapable. The same applies to people, though they are capable of making statements. . Indeed, I can make a statement of expectation without really expecting, thas is, when my expectation is not incarnated in the conduct of expecting. Example: I can say,  “Expecto resurrectionem mortuorum,” when my conduct throughout life and especially just before death belies my statement. Once again, the primacy of praxis over doxa ).   These are inherited mechanisms of living, sensitive bodies, particularly and more clearly of superior mammals. And these mechanisms are at the origin of all further purposive action initiated by living sensitive bodies and of all future learning about the world as our abode, as our concrete dramatic space.

As realized by Hume, these are practical  mechanisms directly connected with action without the mediation of any theoretical knowledge. In other words, the animal doesn’t know-that all plants of that kind are purgative. The animal just has learned to desire and look for that kind of plant when purgation is desirable.   There is memory of the perceivable plant as desirable for purgation. Whenever in the future purgation is desirable for the animal’s survival or wel-being, the animal will actively look for that kind of plant. This is purely practical recognition of the plant for purposes of action, for the animal has no theoretical idea-that there is a kind of plant that uniformly produces purgative effects in such a kind of animals.

Though the recognition is practical and certainly not verbalized by the animal, at least at a certain stage of his philosophical evolution, Aristotle saw this practical recognition as akin to the cognitive, objectifying  universalization involved in reflective human judgments, to which universalization Socrates and Plato had given epistemic supremacy in their quest for absolute certainty, and of which Plato thought geometry was the supreme model . It seems that Aristotle changed his mind, at least partially, concerning practical knowledge as terminating in action. This is reflected in his De Motu Animalium and in the sections of his Ethics that refer to the practical syllogism. Martha Nussbaum identifies Aristotle’s notion of practical knowledge with this second view, exclusively, ignoring the texts that reflect the first view. By contrast, in his article on “Practical Knowledge in Aristotle,” Devereux considers exclusively the first view as reflecting Aristotle’s convictions in this matter.
