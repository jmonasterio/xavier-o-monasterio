Knowledge and linguistic enunciation according to Searle

      In The Tacit Dimension Michael Polanyi  introduces the notion of
“tacit knowledge,” by which he became known, to refer to those cases in
which a speaker “knows more than he can verbalize.” He exemplified this
knowledge by referring to the experiment conducted by psychologists Lazarus
and McCleary in 1949.

      “These authors presented a person with a large number of nonsense
      syllables, and after showing certain of the syllables, they
      administered an electric shock. Presently the person showed symptoms
      of anticipating the shock at the sight of ‘shock syllables’; yet, on
      questioning, he could not identify them. He had come to know when to
      expect a shock, but he could not tell what made him expect it. He had
      acquired a knowledge similar to that which we have when we know a
      person by SIGNS we cannot tell.”[2]

      For his part, like most of us,  Searle would find nonsensical, at
least at first sight, Polanyi’s notion of TK –that is, of a knowledge that
cannot be enunciated, since it seems to amount to an unconscious knowledge.
Indeed,  the subject who supposedly knows is not aware of what he knows,
since he cannot say or enunciate– even to himself internally, much less  to
others externally–  what he knows.

      In this view, then, knowing consists in being aware of what one know;
furthemore, knowing and being capable of enunciating what one is aware of
are equivalent. Hence a knowledge that is “tacit,” that is, a knowledge in
which the knowing subject cannot enunciate even to himself what he knows,
is an oxymoron.

      Now, an unconscious knowledge is an oxymoron. Why? Because the very
notion of knowledge, or at least the ordinary notion of knowledge that
common sense seems to impose on us and which Searle, like most of us,
accepts uncritically, implies on the subject’s part the awareness of what
he knows. We know what we know thanks to the awareness we have of what we
know, so we would not know what we know without that awareness.

      From the above it follows that, according to Searle,  the main
function of language on which all the other linguistic functions depend is
expressing in words or enunciating what is known by the speaker in order to
make it known to himself and, when needed, to a hearer or hearers. This
coheres, indeed,  with three of Searle’s most characteristic views on
language. (1) That language is a unique system of signs, in that, by
contrast to all other signs, linguistic signs mean by saying or enunciating
what is meant; (2) that, consequently, what is said in linguistic
communication should ideally express or enunciate exactly what the speaker
intends to communicate, which is the point of Searle’s “principle of
expressibility”; (3) that in every case of linguistic communication what
the speaker intends to communicate is essentially and necessarily the
knowledge of what he intends to communicate, so that if only this
illocutionary effect is achieved the communicative action is successful.


      For his Part, Polanyi rejects these three views. (1) According to
him, language does not mean by saying what is meant, but by tacitly or non-
verbally signaling an
action to be performed. Precisely because language is a system of signs, it
cannot possibly  mean by saying or making explicit what is meant by the
sign, for signs by their very nature refer to something other than
themselves, to something that is not presently experienced by the
interpreter of the sign (2) Since the intrinsic effect of language is not
to produce knowledge or awareness of object but to elicit an action, the
speaker doesn’t have to enunciate –much less to enunciate exactly–what the
hearer is to become aware of, for the hearer is not to become aware of
anything thanks to what is said, but to act in a certain way. (3)
Consequently, the essential effect of linguistic communication on which the
success of the communication exclusively depends is the actual performance
of the action signaled. If that action was performed, the communication
succeeded; if not, the communication failed.

Knowledge and linguistic enunciation according to Searle

      To render fully explicit the way in which Searle conceives the
relationship between knowledge and linguistic enunciation, consider, in the
first place, what he has to say about the illocutionary effect. In every
instance of linguistic communication, he explains, what the speaker means
or intends to communicate to the hearer is the knowledge of what he means.
This is necessarily so, with conceptual necessity, for that is what
communication consists in, essentially. Now, the knowledge of what a
speaker intends to communicate is what Searle calls the “illocutionary
effect” of the communicative act, the only effect that cannot fail to be
intended by the speaker, for otherwise nothing wold be communicated. Now
–this is my observation, not Searle’s– in order to intend to communicate
that knowledge to the hearer, the speaker himself has to know or be aware
of what he intends to communicate, since he intends to communicate that.
Thus, Searle is implicitly telling us that in every instance of linguistic
communication the speaker informs or makes known to the hearer a piece of
knowledge by enunciating or saying what he himself knows. Hence, according
to Searle, the  awareness or knowledge of what one knows is the condition
for the possibility of enunciation.

      If the communicative action succeeds –Searle continues to explain–the
hearer will thereby acquire the piece of knowledge communicated to him by
the speaker. However –and, again, this is my observation, not Searle’s– in
order to avoid an infinite  regression, we have to assume that either the
speaker or someone else originally acquired in a non linguistic way,
through direct personal experience, the information that the speaker
transmits linguistically. Searle does not deal with this epistemic
precondition of linguistic communication for he is exclusively concerned
with linguistic action, that is, with action that results as an effect of
enunciation. But whether or not he concerns himself with that, my claim,
along Polanyi’s lines, is that linguistic action through which knowledge
obtains of that of which the speaker is aware and intends to communicate
presupposes a knowledge that is not itself linguistic–a knowledge that was
not acquired under the form of something said or enunciated but through
direct experience.. This is to say that one can know ‘TACITLY’or
wordlessly, which is what we do everyday in our perceptual dealings with
the world by which we are surrounded .

      However, “TACITLY,” in Polanyi’s sense, does not only mean not
actually enunciated but un-enunciable. What is known cannot be enunciated
by the one who knows it, as it happens in the case of the shock-producing
syllables. To grasp the difference between  Polanyi’s and Searle’s
respective conceptions of language  we may ask what in Searle’s view of
linguistic communication would be lost if there is no enunciation of what
is to be known. Everything would be lost in the communicative act, Searle
would answer. And that, indeed, is the case, IF one presupposes, as Searle
does, that enunciating is a sort of describing exactly what one knows so as
to make it known to the hearer. For without the exact enunciation by the
hearer of what he knows, the hearer cannot know what the speaker knows and
intends tocommunicate. That is why Searle would  claim that even if the
American soldier succeeded in being liberated by the Italians, this effect
was not genuinely linguistic. For his being set free by the Italians  was
not effected, strictly speaking, thanks to  what the American soldier said.


      To continue making fully  explicit the way in which Searle conceives
the relationship between knowledge and linguistic expression, consider,
secondly,   what Searle has to say about what he calls “the principle of
expressibility” which he formulates as follows: “that whatever can be meant
can be said.”[3] What is “saying”? It is enunciating  “exactly”[4] what one
is aware of and wants to communicate. Searle, however, makes some
clarifications: (1) That

      “Often we mean more than we actually say. If you ask me ’Are you going
      to the movies” I may respond by saying ‘Yes’ but, as is clear from the
      context, I mean ’Yes I am going to the movies”... In such cases, even
      though I do not say exactly what I mean, it is always possible for me
      to do so– if there is any possibility that the hearer might not
      understand me, I may do so.”[5]

      That (2) if the speaker’s language lacks the expressive resources to
enunciate what the speaker intends to say, the speaker can always, at least
in principle,  “enrich” the language to enunciate exactly what he wants to
say.

       That (3) the  exactness of the enunciation of what the speaker wants
to say is to be measured or judged by the speaker who has the communicative
intention, not by the hearer, for a language may lack the resources to make
known to others what one is aware of. Indeed,  Searle explicitly preserves,
without categorically affirming it, the logical possibility of a private
language, “a language that it was logically impossible for anyone but the
speaker to understand.”[6]

      Leaving aside  this logical possibility of a private language, a
possibility that  Searle himself does not affirm categorically, it is clear
that for Searle language, constituted concretely by speech acts,  is
normally communicative. That is,  language at the service of a competent
speaker is capable of making known to others what one wants to make known,
which is the essential linguistic effect, the “illocutionary effect,” the
effect that the speaker cannot fail to intend to produce in the hearer by
saying something, the effect and on whose success the success of the speech
act as such exclusively depends.

      In short, what is said should ideally  express  exactly the knowledge
that the speaker possesses and wants to communicate. Such is the main point
of the principle of expressibility. As to why Searle takes so seriously the
requirement of the exactness of the linguistic enunciation to make known to
the hearer what the speaker himself knows and wants to make known to the
hearer is made evident in this farfetched but so telling example of the
American soldier in Italy during World War II. (SA, p. 4).

      From this example  and Searle’s own elaboration on the principle of
expressibility it follows that, even if the American had obtained the
effect of being set free by the Italians which he wanted to obtain by
saying “Ich kome..,” this effect was not genuinely linguistic but rather a
purely accidental fact, since the German expression he used does not mean
anything even remotely related to being set free. It means “I come from the
land where the lemon trees bloom.” Thus what the American said did not
express the knowledge he wanted to communicate to the Italians.

      The above shows that the ordinary notion of knowledge  to which
Searle subscribes not only involves the assumption that knowledge consists
in awareness of object, but it also involves the further assumption that
the awareness of object in which knowledge consists  amounts to, or at
least requires, the verbal enunciation of what is known. To explain this I
will  backtrack  for a moment.

      Why does Polanyi call “tacit” the knowledge he is talking about?
Because the subject cannot say or enunciate what he knows, what he is aware
of.  But precisely because he cannot say what he knows Searle would argue
that the subject doesn’t know, for Searle takes it for granted that being
aware of and enunciating internally to oneself what one is aware of or
knows are convertible because one knows by enunciating or giving linguistic
form to what one knows.. If one knows, one can say what one knows; and if
one cannot enunciate what one knows, one does not know, for one is not
aware of what one knows.. Thus, Searle would argue that  knowledge can be
silent or only internally expressed, but that it cannot be “tacit,” in
Polanyi’s sense, because being  aware of something and enunciating
internally to oneself that of which one is aware are one and the same.
This is why Searle thinks that the essential and most important function of
language is the enunciative function, for it is  constitutive of the
awareness of object in which knowledge consists.

      Moreover, this essential function is different from, and independent
of, action (and of affection).


Knowledge and linguistic enunciation according to Polanyi

      To move now to Polanyi’s views on the relationship between knowledge
and linguistic expression of what is known , Polanyi implicitly replies to
Searle’s objection against the nonsensicality of a knowledge that is tacit
or unenunciable by referring us to the undeniable facts of the experiment
with the shock-producing syllables (which happens to be an instance of sign
signification). The subject of the experiment cannot say which are the
shock-producing syllables because he does not know them in the ordinary
sense of the word, that is, is not aware of them as object of knowledge.
Yet the subject’s behavior in reaction to those syllables shows that he
knows them in a certain way, for he braces himself for a shock when they
are presented to him on the screen. Now, this ‘certain way of knowing,’
namely TK, that is, a knowledge that does not involve verbalization of what
is known, and it does not consist in  awareness of the object known.

      Thus Polanyi’s notion of TK, if valid, avoids Searle’s   assumption
concerning the nature of knowledge as awareness of the objet known, which
is the ordinary conception of knowledge uncritically accepted by Searle.
Secondly, since TK does not consist in awareness of the object known, there
is no room for linguistic enunciation, for the subject has nothing to
enunciate. Thus, with his notion of TK Polanyi also avoids Searle’s
assumption  concerning the relationship of knowledge to enunciative
language, to wit: that what is known can, by definition, be expressed in a
linguistic enunciation that describes –and in this way corresponds to– what
is known. Finally, since TK entails action as a necessary component, for,
as we shall see, TK is meant to terminate in action RATHER THAN IN
ENUNCIATION OF WHAT IS KNOWN, it avoids Searle’;s assumption  concerning
the accidental relationship of action to knowledge , for acording to
Searle, action only sometimes and thereefore, only acidentally,. follows
knowledge.

      The three preceding assumptions define Searle’s position by contrast
to Polanyi’s. Now, if Polanyi’s notion of TK avoids indeed those
assumptions, their very absence will help us to grasp the originality and
the significance of Polanyi’s conception of knowledge, who credits himself
with having discovered “a novel idea of human knowledge” as a result of his
research.[7]

      According  to Polanyi, what occurs in the subject of the experiment
at the sight of the shock-producing syllables is not an awareness of the
object’s characteristics, for he does not know the shock-producing
syllables. Rather it is an automatic reaction of bracing himself for a
shock when certain syllables appear on the screen. Now bracing himself is
not awareness of object, not even a silent or non verbalized one, for the
subject doesn’t know the  syllables that  produce shocks; rather it is a
conduct or behavior. This behavior is observed by those who conduct the
experiment. Actually, the subject’s behavior is precisely the basis on
which Polanyi grounds his claim that the subject ‘knows’ in one way or
another, for without that behavioral manifestation of knowledge at the
sight of the shock-producing syllables, and not at the sight of the other
syllables, Polanyi would have no basis for his claim of tacit knowledge on
the subject’s part.

      This close connection of TK with action, in the absence of
(descriptive) verbalization or enunciation, is an important clue. Indeed,
it is a first indication that, in Polanyi’s view,  knowledge may consist in
an action of a certain kind independently of verbalization or enunciation,
rather than in consciousness of the object’s characteristics expressed or
at least expressible in verbalization. To find out whether Polanyi’s
alternative to the ordinary notion of knowledge as descriptive or quasi
descriptive awareness of the characteristics of the object known, which  in
 turn results in the descriptive enunciation of those characteristics makes
sense, let us study in detail Polanyi’s views by contrast to Searle’s.

      Searle, as we already know, distinguishes two kinds of linguistic
effects: the illocutionary effect, which consists in knowledge, and the
perlocutionary effects, which for the most part consist in actions.
However, in separating the illocutionary from the perlocutionary effects,
not only does Searle imply that knowledge is independent of action, but he
ipso facto subordinates action to knowledge, because the knowledge of what
the speaker means appears to Searle as a  necessary condition for the
success of the perlocutionary intentions of a speaker, whereas the success
of the illocutionary intentions of a speaker, that is, the hearer’s
knowledge of what the speaker means, does not depend on the success of any
of the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker. To put it concretely, a
speaker cannot say anything without intending to convey to the hearer the
knowledge of what he means, but he can say something without obtaining or
even intending to obtain any other effect than the hearer’s knowledge of
what he means. So Searle sees knowledge is independent of action, in that
knowledge can obtain without any action obtaining.. Correlatively, the
hearer cannot fulfill the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker without
knowing those intentions, that  is, without the illocutionary effect
obtaining; but the hearer can know the perlocutionary intentions of the
speaker, thereby insuring the success of the communicative act as such,
without fulfilling any of the speaker’s perlocutionary intentions. For
instance, if I do not know what you mean when you ask me to lend you  my
pen, I cannot lend you my pen; however,  I can know that you ask me for my
pen and not lend it to you. Moreover, you can produce in me the
illocutionary effect without intending any perlocutionary effect. For
example,  at least in Searle’s interpretation of saluting,  when you salute
me, all you intend to produce in me is the knowledge that you mean to
salute me, and the communicative exchange succeeds if only I gain that
knowledge.[8] So, in Searle’s view, knowledge is independent of action, but
action is dependent on knowledge, in that linguistic action cannot obtain
if knowledge does not obtain. Now, because the perlocutionary effects
depend on the realization of the  illocutionary effect, but not the other
way around, and because the speaker may say something without intending any
perlocutionary effect but cannot say anything without intending to produce
the illocutionary effect, which consists in the hearer’s awareness of  what
the speaker means, Searle sees language primarily and essentially as a an
instrument to communicate knowledge as awarenes of object, and only
secondarily and accidentally and conditioned by the realization of the
illocutionary effect,  as an instrument to obtain action or to produce
other effects than knowledge. This is why I concluded in the previous
chapter that the way language functions, according to Searle, is the way in
which language functions in the bastard situation of a course of English
for foreigners, in which the understanding of what is said is separated
from, and independent of, the perlocutionary effects ordinarily meant and
intended by the speaker, but not the way in which language actually
functions in ordinary linguistic communication.

      Polanyi, for his part, based on the analysis of the way in which
language actually functions, construes the relationship between knowledge
and action in a totally different way. Indeed, knowledge  is, in his view,
essentially practical in that it is meant to terminate in action, not, as
the case is for Searle, in a theoretical awareness of a fact, an awareness
whose natural manifestation is not an action but the linguistic enunciation
of the object’s characteristics. For example, knowing an order received
does not consist, according to Polanyi, in becoming aware of the order as a
theoretical fact, but in the practical disposition to obey the order
received, and in actually obeying it at the appropriate time, unless
prevented by extrinsic causes. As we shall see in a moment, [Searle’s
requirement of exact explicit enunciation results, at least in part, from
his ignorance of the operator we call “desire,” for no sign signfies in the
absence of some desire on the subject’s part.] Similarly, according to
Polanyi, knowing a promise received does not consist in becoming
theoretically aware of that fact, as one becomes aware of a color when one
sees and observes it in the course of an experiment in a laboratory, but in
actually disposing oneself for the fulfillment of the received promise at
the appropriate time. If you promise to repay me the $100.00 you owe me,
when I come to pick them up at your office at 3.00 P.M., the appropriate
understanding of your promise does not consist in registering that fact and
then doing nothing, as Searle contends,  but in expecting the fulfillment
of your promise, which normally terminates in the action of actually coming
to your office at 3.00 P.M. to collect my money.

      Now, the idea that knowledge consists in a certain kind of  action
rather than in the consciousness of an object’s characteristics and in the
linguistic enunciation of what is known is neither outlandish nor even
new. Indeed,  when free from epistemological prejudices, most of us believe
that animals, or at least the members of the higher species of mammals,
say, dogs, possess the ability to know. At the same time, nonetheless, we
believe that dogs are non-linguistic agents, that is, that they do not
posses the linguistic ability we possess and, therefore, are incapable of
expressing or enunciating  in words what they know. But this shows that we
spontaneously assume that knowing is not identical with the consciousness
of an object’s characteristics nor with the consequent linguistic capacity
to enunciate what one knows or is aware of.

      Since dogs do not express in words what they know, on what basis,
then, do we attribute to dogs the ability to know? On the basis of their
actions. Certain of their actions are, we say,”intentional” or
“purposeful.” That is, they  reveal an agent’s purpose, in that the dog’s
agency is not uniform but adapted by the dog to different circumstances for
the sake of a desired goal that satisfies the dog’s desire. By contrast, a
marble’s fall down an inclined plane is a uniform action, not an adaptive
or ‘intentional’ action, because it is caused by the environmental
circumstances; moreover, the action is not intended or desired by the
marble as a means for the satisfaction of a desire and, therefore, is not
modified by the marble according to the circumstances. For that very
reason, we have no temptation of attributing knowledge to marbles: their
action is not intentional, not for the sake of a goal intended-desired  by
the marble, and the uniformity of the marble’s action shows no ability on
the marble’s part to adapt its action to the circumstances in view of the
realization of a goal. In a dogs’ actions, by contrast, we grasp the
intention of a goal and, with the dog’s increased experience, an increasing
ability on its part to adapt its actions to the circumstances and thereby
make its actions more efficient for the realization of their natural goals.
Hence we conclude that dogs know , but in a different sense than Polanyi
conceives of knowledge.

      Of course, this analysis may be refined, but as it is it suffices for
my present  purpose. For at this stage  my purpose is simply to show that
knowledge is not necessarily to be conceived, as Searle among so many
others conceives it, as an awareness of object that mirrors or reproduces
the object’s characteristics and that is totally unrelated to action.
Rather, my analysis of what – without any epistemological preconceptions –
we spontaneously recognize as knowledge indicates that what we identify as
knowledge, at least  in the case of animals, involves intentional actions
for the sake of desired goals, and that verbalization of what is known is
not a necessary component of knowledge, since dogs do not verbalize and yet
we grant that they know.

      To summarize: Searle conceives knowledge in the traditional way – as
awareness of its object’s characteristics, and/or as verbal enunciation  of
the characteristics of the object  known. For his part,  Polanyi conceives
knowledge as independent of linguistic enunciation and as intrinsically
related to action. To grasp more precisely what this means and the
difference it makes, let us reflect on the way signs function, for
according to Polanyi  knowledge  functions as signs do, when they are
actually experienced as signs, that is, when the person who experiences the
sign is actually referred to what is signified in the way in which it is
signified.

How do signs function when experienced as signs?

      This reflection is important for two reasons. (1) Because, as it will
become apparent, Polanyi thinks that  the objects experienced in ordinary
perception sometimes, under conditions to be specified, function  as signs.
(2) Because reflection on the way signs actually function is required to
correct a fundamental mistake in which Searle incurs. Indeed,  when
actually experienced as signs, signs function as signs FOR performing an
action,  not as signs OF something to be known or to become aware OF, as
mistakenly assumed by Searle. [The reason is that  Searle’s conception of
signs is modeled on a misinterpretation of linguistic saying as descriptive
of what is said.].

      How, then, does language mean or signal, according to Searle? By
saying or enunciating what it means. Hence he considers linguistic an
effect only when it results as an effect of understanding what is said and
meant by a speaker. This is why, in the case of “Ich kome...,”even if the
American soldier had been set free by the Italians,  Searle wouldn’t think
that  this conduct of the Italians was linguistically achieved, that is,
was a genuinely linguistic effect. For the soldier did not say “I am an
American soldier,” though that was what he wanted the Italians to believe.
This is why, too, Searle insisted so much in saying exactly what one means
when he presents his principle of expressibility. Ideally, one should say
“exactly “what one means, for the nature of language as a system of signs
is to say or enunciate what it signals in order to make known what it
signals. By contrast to linguistic signs, all other signs signal what they
mean but do not enunciate what they mean. Moreover, even in the case of
linguistic signs the connection between knowledge and action is not
intrinsic but contingent or accidental, for not all speech acts possess
perlocutionary force.

      Other signs, that is, non-linguistic signs, signify or mean without
enunciating what they mean. Thus it is possible for signs to signal or
refer to what they are supposed to refer without enunciating. A red traffic
light signals me to stop, but it does not say or enunciate “stop.” When
actually experienced as a sign, not when seen merely as a red light, the
red light functions as a sign immediately, that is, without the mediation
of an enunciation, and I show my understanding of the sign by stopping, not
by enunciating what the sign signals. - A more subtle example of knowing
through signs, in this case linguistic signs, is that of the American
soldier who intended to be set free by the Italians. For that purpose, he
recited a line from a German poem, which the Italians could not understand,
in order to make them believe that he was German and that he was telling
them that he was German. Now, because the American  did not tell the
Italians what he wanted to communicate to
them, Searle would have to deny that the American’s being set free was a
genuinely linguistic effect, for linguistic effects should correspond to
what is enunciated, since that is the very purpose of the enunciation.
Suppose, however, that the American had told  the Italians in Italian“Io
sono tedesco,” that is, “I am German.” In that case, given the point of
Searle’s example, he could conceivably have obtained the effect he
intended. But would that effect be genuinely linguistic? Not so, according
to Searle, for he did not say “I am German,” still less “I want you to set
me free because I am German.” Polanyi, by contrast, would claim that the
effect produced by the line of the German poem was genuinely linguistic,
because linguistic expressions mean as signs, not as verbal representations
of what they mean, and under the circumstances the words uttered functioned
–and were meant to function– as a sign for the Italians as a sign to
perform the action of liberating him. In other words, to obtain the conduct
desired, linguistic signs do not have to enunciate the conduct desired,
anymore than a red traffic light has to enunciate the conduct desired,
because signs –linguistic signs included, and contrary to what Searle
claims– do not signal by enunciating what is signaled. They are not signs
OF, but signs FOR.

       How, then, do signs signal or signify or mean? The operator, in the
case of signs, is inherited need or acquired desire , not enunciation of
what is meant. The German soldier of Polanyi’s example realized that if the
Italians knew that he was an ally, they would desire to set him free, and
would act accordingly. Thus the German words  “Ich kome...,” connected with
the Italians’ habitual desire to treat well their allies, were rightly
expected and meant by the American  to terminate in the Italians’ action of
freeing him. In the concrete circumstances, these German words functioned
as a sign for the Italians to set him free.
N.B.- Searle ignores the operator DESIRE for at least two reasons. (1)
Because he doesn’t realize that signs are signs FOR action  – and only in
connection with action does desire need to be taken into account as the
operator that connects ths sign with action. (2) And he ignores this
because his model of signs is language misunderstood by him as enunciating
what is meant, that is, making explicit through enunciation what the sign
is sign OF.  This, however, is an absurdity. A sign is always a sign of
something other than itself, for it can announce or signify only what is
not actually present to the interpreter of the sign. Moreover, a sign is
not to be observed or known itself but only to signal, that is, to direct
attention to the signaled, which is always something other than the sign
itself. In other words, to function as signs, that is to direct attention
to something other than themselves, signs cannot signal if they are
explicit in the way Searle imagines, for in that case they would be signs
of themselves.

      Because Searle is concerned with linguistic action, exclusively, and
because he sees language as a system of signs that mean by saying or
enunciating what they mean in order to communicate it, he requires the
explicit and as exact enunciation as possible of what is meant or known. By
contrast, because Polanyi is concerned with action in general, and because
he doesn’t think that linguistic signs mean by enunciating what they mean
but rather, as all  other signs, by signaling –in conjunction with need or
desire– an action to be performed, he doesn’t think that the main function
of language is to enunciate explicitly or exactly what is to be
communicated, but to signal the action to be performed in the particular
case. And for this no enunciation of what is meant is required when the
appropriate desire occurs. Moreover, ordinary experience of a sign as a
sign rather than enunciation performs the signaling function.

      If we equate initially knowledge with the ability to experience signs
as signs, rather than with the ability to enunciate or to understand
enunciations, we will have no trouble in attributing the capacity to know
to at least the superior species of mammals. However, there are degrees of
knowing –all of them related to the satisfaction of a need or desire
through appropriate action.

      Consider hunger. Hunger is a sensation related to the need for food.
For it to become desire of food, the sensation has to be connected with the
object food. This may obtain in two ways: either the connection is
inherited or instinctive, so that in the presence of what is food for the
particular organism, the desire  for it automatically obtains, or it is
acquired through experience. The sensation of hunger functions as a sign to
perform the action of eating at the appropriate time, that is, when food is
present.

      On analysis, it becomes clear that Searle’s theory of language and of
meaning in general –that is of signs– is modeled on reading a written text,
which terminates in understanding, not on speaking and hearing, that is on
live, face to face linguistic oral exchanges. In the case of reading a
text, what is perceived functions as sing OF something to be understood.
Reading is meant t o terminate in understanding Perceiving, then, is
construed as a sort of reading the text of  nature. What is the difference
between reading texts and oral face to face exchanges?  That oral exchanges
(discounting modern technology, such as the telephone) take place in the
presence of the parties to each other and in a situation commonly shared by
the communicating parties. Such is not the case with reading a text. A
written text has meaning, but a meaning to be understood, a meaning that
becomes known by being understood and that can be expressed in words.  In
th is case the act of reading is successful if it terminates in
understanding what is meant, for here the meaning is something to be
understood . This is the model after which Searle conceives meaning and, in
particular, the meaning of signs. Reader and writer are not present to each
other and do not share a common perceptual situation to which they can
refer each other and in which they can act and act on each other, as is the
case with  oral exchanges.  For this very reason, reading is meant to
terminate, and can only terminate, in understanding by contrast to action.
In an oral exchange, by contrast, because we, the parties in the exchange,
are present to each other, I am exposed to your physical and your
linguistic action and you are equally exposed to mine, and through your
action you can modify the commonly shared perceptual situation, and so can
I. Being present to each other and in the same environment, my speech acts
may aim at terminating or  resulting in other effects than mere
understanding to become aware of what I mean. For instance, I can ask you
to lend me the pen I see in your pocket.

Two questions, now: (1) Why did Searle choose the model of meaning –the one
based on reading, rather than this perceptual one? (2) What difference
would this other choice have made?

      In anwswer to (1): Because he REFLECTED on the way language functions
WHEN ONE THINKS OF IT, rather than when one is actually involved in a
linguistic exchange. Consequently, he attended to what was said, rather
than attending from the sign to the performance of the action signaled, as
we do in live linguistic face to face exchanges. No wonder that  he deemed
the originality of language to reside in its enunciating what is meant so
as to make that known.(Thus, in Searle’s view, speech acts mean to say
something to be understood, which something enunciated may be true when the
referent corresponds to what is said.). Now, Searle grants that non-
linguistic signs mean, that is, have meaning, though they do not say what
they mean. So he could at least conceive that linguistic
signs, too, mean without enunciating. However, having already conceived
meaning as something to be understood and to become aware of, and having
conceived that something as different from and independent of action, after
the model of reading a text rather than after the model of interactive
communication in a commonly shared perceptual situation, in which case
meaning may terminate in action rather than in mere theoretical
understanding, he developed a tunnel vision that prevented him from
assimilating and even from considering the experiential alternative.
Needless to say, his blindness to the experiential alternative was
enormously influenced by the Aristotelian epistemic tradition that had
separated knowledge from action and subordinated action to knowledge as its
necessary condition. Had he started with Aristotle’s theory of the action
of animate creatures...

      In answer to question (2): The paradigmatic example of meaning, for
Searle, was the case of enunciating what is meant, in which case what is
meant becomes what is known. Thus knowing and enunciating are, for him,
convertible.

      To grasp this fundamental point, let us begin with our daily
experience of traffic lights.

      We think of the red and green lights as signs. And we are not wrong,
for they are signs, that is, they mean something. Where we go wrong –not
when we experience directly the traffic lights, but when we reflect on our
experience of them–  is in assuming that signs are primarily signs OF
something (ENUNCIABLE) to be known by the subject who perceives the signs,
that what is meant is something OF which the subject who perceives the
signs is to become aware, and which – therefore, or by the same token–
will be susceptible of being made explicit when verbally enunciated by the
subject. This equivalence between knowing something, being aware of
something, and the something of which one is aware being enunciable, that
is, capable of being meant or linguistically signaled as something  to
known,  deserves attention.

      Whether or not it is a mistake to think that signs are primarily
signs OF something to be known, such at any rate is the way in which Searle
thinks of language as a system of signs. The difference he sees between
linguistic signs and all other signs is that linguistic signs, which
consist concretely of speech acts, say or enunciate what they mean, whereas
all other signs mean but do not say what they mean. Saying is expressing in
words that enunciate or describe what one means in order to make it known
to the hearer, who in his turn becomes aware or acquires the knowledge of
what is meant by the speaker by understanding what is said. Thus what is
meant or signified linguistically is always and primarily something to be
known by understanding what is said..

      In other words, in Searle’s view, speech acts are always signs OF
something to  be known. In addition, however,  certain speech acts –but
only those that possess perlocutionary force– are meant to terminate in
something other than knowledge, for the most part in an action. A
commandment, for instance, is meant to terminate in the action of  obeying
it. But even in those cases, the action cannot obtain if the hearer does
not know which action is meant to obtain. Knowledge, therefore, is the
precondition for action. On the other hand,  the hearer may know which
action is intended by the speaker and refuse to perform it, that is,
without the perlocutionary effect obtaining, and this fact will not
detract from the success of the speech act, for the action intended is only
accidental to the success of the speech act.

      In fact, however, that is not how signs work. All signs without
exception – whether natural or conventional – linguistic signs as used in
ordinary, everyday communication included–are meant to function as signs
FOR action, not as signs OF something of which one is to become  aware and
which can be expressed and made known to others through linguistic
enunciation.[9] For example,  red in the case of a traffic light, is a sign
FOR performing the action of stopping at the sight of the red light.
Similarly, a green light is a sign FOR performing the action of moving
ahead at the sight of the light. Again, Similarly, an arrow pointing in a
certain direction on a road is a sign not OF an object to become aware and
then enunciate it, it is a sign FOR performing the action of moving in the
direction pointed at. It follows from this that the correct comprehension
of signs is manifested not in awareness of what is signified nor in
linguistic enunciation of  the awareness of what is signified, but in the
performance of a certain action, for  what is signified or signaled is in
every case an action to be performed. Consequently,  the correct grasp or
comprehension of the sign consists in the actual performance of the action
signaled.

      Traffic lights are examples of artificial, non-linguistic signs that
become signs by convention. An arrow is an arbitrary or purely conventional
 sign FOR the action of following a certain direction; a green traffic
light is a conventional sign FOR the action of proceeding  ahead, etc. What
now about natural by contrast to artificial signs? For instance, smoke is a
natural sign of fire when fire is not present to the interpreter, a dark
sky is a sign of rain, when rain is no present. Well, Polanyi thinks that
some the objects we experience in ordinary perception function naturally
rather than  by convention,  and under certain conditions, as signs, that
is, as signs For performing a certain action AT THE APPROPRIATE TIME,
without any knowledge or awareness or enunciation –be it internal or
external– OF the action signaled. IN THE CASE OF ANIMALS THIS IS EVIDENT,
for being non-linguistic agents they are incapable of expressing verbally
to themselves, let alone to others, anything so as to become aware OF that.
But the same is the case with us people, though we are linguistic
creatures. If, for instance, when I am about to cross a street, I look on
my left and see a car moving in my direction at a certain speed, that is a
sign For stopping, not a sign OF anything that can be the content of an
enunciation, that is, OF anything to be known . In other words, the action
of stopping, not an enunciation, constitutes the correct comprehension of
the sign, for signs are signs For performing an action.

      We are not aware of the sign’s characteristics nor of a meaning to be
known when we perceive a sign as a sign, the knowledge of the sign is
tacit, for we cannot express in words that of which we are not aware. And
exactly the same applies to language, that is, to the  linguistic signs or
speech acts in which language concretely consists. Thus, contrary to what
Searle thinks, for whom the originality of language resides in that it says
 what it means as something to be understood, Polanyi thinks that what is
said is not something to be understood or known  or to be informed OF, but
rather –as in the case of all other signs– heard as a sign FOR action by
being practically referred to what is meant.  In other words,  language is
a system of artificial signs, and signs –be they natural or artificial–do
not function in everyday communication as signs OF anything to be known,
but as signs FOR action. Thus,  when in the appropriate circumstances I
tell you, “The car is in the garage,” I mean it as a sign for you to go to
the garage and get the car you desire or are looking for. Which is exactly
the way you, the addresee, understand what I say, as manifested by your
actions.

       It is to be remarked, though —and this is something that Polanyi
does not make explicit– that for a perceived object to actually function as
a sign and lead to the performance of the action signaled, a desire on the
subject’s part is required, for whose fulfillment the action signaled is
relevant as a MEANS OR INSTRUMENT. For instance, the presence of water
functions as a sign For drinking it only for an agent who is thirsty (and
who has learned through experience that water satisfies thirst). And the
agent’s action of drinking, that is, the action signaled is the instrument
through which the satisfaction of the agent’s desire is achieved.
Similarly, if I am hungry, the sight of a piece of ham within my reach
functions as a sign FOR eating it. Consequently, under ordinary
circumstances, the objects perceived function not representationally but
instrumentally– as the signs that guide me in the performance of the action
appropriate to satisfy my desire of eating.  The perceived ham guides me in
the satisfaction of my hunger. If I did not perceive the ham I wouldn’t
move toward the place where the ham is. But what I perceive is not an
object with certain characteristics. Rather it functions as a sign FOR
acting in a certain  way which is appropriate way to satisfy my desire.

       It is precisely in this sense that signs, when desire is present,
signify or function as instruments FOR the performance of an action. When I
see a car moving in my direction at a certain distance and speed, my
stopping is an intentional purposeful action, a means to achieve my goal of
reaching alive the other side of the road. Thus, I perceive the car not
objectively, that is, independently of my dispositions as an exposed agent;
I perceive the car as to be avoided, and the action signified is the
instrument for the achievement of my goal..[10] That is what Aristotle had
in mind when, as Nussbaum remarks,  he observed that only when desire is
present does action follow perception. Only when the role of desire and its
connection with action are taken into account does the reason why certain
objects of our ordinary perception function as signs FOR performing certain
actions becomes intelligible.

      To summarize:  perceptual objects, when presently experienced or
encountered, and if they are relevant for the satisfaction of a desire of
the encountering subject, function as signs. Moreover, signs  mean
performatively or instrumentally, that is, as signs For action, not
enunciatively or descriptively, as signs OF something known or to be known.
In other words, the signified or signaled is not of the order of awareness
OF, an awareness whose content or object is or can be expressed
linguistically in an enunciation of the characteristics of the sign or of
the signified. For neither the sign nor the signified is an object to be
aware of, as an enunciation suggests, but an action to be performed.

      An important consequence of this is that the perception of what we
perceive, is totally different from the visual observation of an object ,
with which we tend to confuse perception. Through perceptual observation ot
that about which one is curious one becomes aware of the characteristics of
the object observed. But that is not what takes place in ordinary
perception.  Precisely because a perceived object is a sign For action
rather than an object to be itself known or a sign of an object to be known
thanks to the sign, the sign refers practically, not theoretically. In
other words, its perception is meant to terminate in action, not in
awareness of object or in an enunciation of that awareness. The visual
observation of an object is inspired by curiosity concerning the observable
characteristics of a thing. But the perception or experience of  object  as
a sign does not consist in observing the sign. This is what Polanyi was
underscoring in his example of the shock-producing syllables. Because signs
are neither things to be known themselves nor signs OF something to be
known, the subject for whom the syllables function as a sign does not
thereby gain the awareness of the individual characteristics of the
syllables. Consequently, the subject cannot say which are those
characteristics, for he experienced them not as object of observation but
as a sign FOR.

      So far I have been presenting my own interpretation of  Polanyi’s
views. I have to avow, however, that Polanyi himself does not consistently
use the language of signs I have used to characterize his views;  still
less does he make fully explicit that signs are always signs FOR action,
and that they can function in this way only when a subject’s acquired
desire or an inherited need is present. I think, however, that my
explanation of  Polanyi’s views in terms of signs For action, far from
distorting them, illuminates them and shows their relevance. Let us now
refer ourselves to Polanyi’s own presentation.

Polanyi on knowledge and its relationship to enunciative language and to

      Here, for once,  Polanyi is unmistakably explaining his views in
terms of signs, and he assumes that signs function as I have been saying
that they function. Signs signify or mean something. When we experience a
sign as a sign, we do not pay attention to the observable characteristics
of the sign, but to what is signified. We, so to say, glide through the
sign to its meaning, we attend from the sign to its meaning. This gliding
through the sign toward its meaning rather than attending to the sign
itself  Polanyi calls “indwelling” or “inhabiting”  And because the subject
of the experiment only indwells in the sign to get its meaning rather than
attending to the sign or to what is meant, the subject cannot enunciate the
characteristics of the sign or of the object meant, for he is not aware of
them.

      It is clear that what  Polanyi  calls “inhabiting” or “ indwelling”
is precisely what I have claimed that we  we do in the case of signs,
except for my iniostence on action. When we interpret a sign we do not
observe or pay attention to the observable characteristics of the sign;
rather, we glide through it, so to say, toward what is signaled. which is
the appropriate way to experience signs, for signs function as such insofar
as they signal or refer.

      Thus the  “inhabiting” or “dwelling in” that take place in our
ordinary perception are meant by Polanyi by contrast to “observing,” which
is what we do when we want to become aware of the observable
characteristics of an object.

      However, the intrinsic connection of signs with action on which I
have insisted so much starts appearing only when Polanyi speaks of
“interiorization. Polanyi himself senses an important conceptual shift in
his thinking when he moves from “indwelling,” which may still suggest
merely a particular way of being aware, to “interiorization,” in which the
connection of the sign with action becomes prominent.

      “The identification of tacit knowing with indwelling involves a shift
      of  emphasis in our conception of tacit knowing. We had envisaged
      tacit knowing in the first place as a way to know more than we can
      tell... Since we were not attending to the particulars in themselves,
      we could not identify them; but if we now regard the integration of
      particulars as an interiorization, it takes on a more positive
      character. It now becomes a means of making certain things function as

      The instrumental character of perception for action is at least
implicitly definitely  involved in what Polanyi calls “interiorization.”
Indeed,  Polanyi uses this particular word to indicate the shift that takes
place in the perceptual experience  from the object seen as something
external to the subject, something in the subject’s environment and
supposedly observed by the subject, to the agent’s body – to the sensations
experienced by the subject in his hand when pounding a nail or, when using
a stick to find his way, etc. This shift from the object perceived to the
agent’s body, in which interiorization consists, may be illustrated by the
difference between the moment at which a pair of eyeglasses functions as
object of perception and the moment at which it functions as an instrument
for action. When the eyeglasses rest on a table and I look at them, they do
not function as eyeglasses but as object seen or observed;  I see the
eyeglasses over there. The interiorization obtains when, instead of
observing the eyeglasses, I put them on my nose. For when I wear the
eyeglasses I do not see the eyeglasses. I “interiorize’ them, that is, they
become, a it were, part of my acting body, for I use them as I use my own
body when I act, as instruments to guide myself as an agent in the
realization of my projects. The eyeglasses on my nose function as an
extension of my eyes, which themselves are part and parcel of my agent’s
body, the body which I do not observe but which I constantly use as an
instruments for my action. As in the case of the eyeglasses, I see  my eyes
only  when I look at them in the mirror, when they function as object
observed;  ordinarily, however, they function as the instrument I use to
act efficiently in the realization of my projects. Polanyi calls
interiorization this shift of an object  from object known over there,  in
the environment of the agent and external to him, to its functioning as an
extension of the agent’s own body when actually used as an instrument for
action. The term “interiorization” is used by Polanyi  to indicate that the
object instead of being part of an observing subject’s environment  is, so
to say, assimilated to the agent’s own body and beings functioning as a
sign for action.

      A dentist, for example, uses a probe to explore a cave in a tooth.
The dentist, by profession, has a general desire to heal teeth, when
needed. The purpose of his probing is to find out whether his healing
action is needed. When the result of his exploration is that the tooth
needs curing, this is for him a sign FOR action, not for becoming
theoretically aware or merely informed of anything. So the dentist’s
probing terminates in the appropriate action. The same is the case when a
blind person uses a stick to fin his way. The awareness of the impact of
the stick on whatever is on his way, joined with his desire to reach a
certain point,  is for him a sign FOR moving in a certain direction. And
this is the case whenever we use a tool for a certain purpose . Now,
although practically all the examples furnished by Polanyi  –the use of a
probe, of a stick, of any instrument– terminate in action, because of the
desire or need present in the subject– Polanyi fails to render fully
explicit this point that is so important for his views, to wit that signs
are signs FOR performing an action. Only when I myself realized that such
is the case ,and that this remains only implicit in Polanyi’s explanation
of his views, was I able to make full sense of what Polanyi’ calls TK and
to grasp the  FECUNDITY of his position.

      When playing the piano (another example of action),  the pianist is
using it as an instrument to produce the desired music. In that case, the
consciousness he has of the keys he touches is instrumental to produce the
desired music. If the word knowledge is to be employed in this context, it
has to be described as “tacit,” for it involves no awareness of the keys of
the kind we have when we perceive an object and are, consequently,  capable
of enunciating the characteristics of the object, of which we are
explicitly aware. The consciousness we have of an object we use as an
instrument to achieve and end  is entirely different from the consciousness
of object with which the ordinary notion of knowledge is identified. So
much so that, as just explained by Polanyi,  if, in the process of playing
the piano, that is, of having tacit knowledge of his fingers and of the
keys they touch, the pianist turns his attention to his fingers or to the
piano’s keys and observes them, thereby turning them into objects of
knowledge, automatically  his activity of playing becomes impaired. If we
turn our attention to the signs, we gain awareness of their characteristics
as such, but then they cease to function as instruments that signal the
performance of an action. These two forms of consciousness are not only
different from each other but incompatible with each other, which is the
reason why we cannot get the meaning if we try to observe the letter
themselves.

      After the case of acquired bodily skills as examples of TK, Polanyi

remark that the reading and actual use of maps are far more complex
operations than they seem to be for those who have already mastered them.
Actually, a child, even a child who has already acquired the skill of
reading written language, is not thereby automatically capable of using a
map to find his way in unfamiliar territory, much less of drawing a map to
be used by others. The reason for this difficulty is that a map is not a
picture of a territory, any more than a name is a picture of the object
named. A map is a symbolic sign of a territory, but not of a territory to
be contemplated or observed but of a territory to be walked through. In
other words, not only is a map to be deciphered or ‘read,’ as all symbols
are, but to be deciphered in relation to the action of moving through the
territory to which the map refers, in order to satisfy the desire to reach
a particular place within that territory. In other words, a map is a
symbolic instrument to be used by an agent as an instrument to achieve his
goal. That is why, if somebody claimed to understand a map but proved
himself incapable of using it in practice, he wouldn’t know what he is
saying. Indeed, since the map is an instrument for action, understanding it
amounts to being able to manifest that understanding in one’s action. The
mistake of the person claiming to understand a map but being incapable of
actually finding it to find his way would be comparable to the one Searle
makes when he claims that a person who is given an order understands it if
only he grasps objectively the meaning of the words, without realizing that
an order is to be obeyed, not to be understood objectively. In short, the
genuine understanding of a map, as in the case of any other instrument, is
to be manifested in the conduct that the map is meant to render possible,
that is, in the conduct of reaching the desired place within the territory
to which the map refers, much as the genuine understanding of a verbal joke
is to be manifested in the affection of being actually amused by it.

is nailing a nail to hang a picture. In other words, he must make the
figure on the map function not as an object to be looked at and observed in
itself, but as “that from which” he must act in order to arrive at the
desired place. In short,  TK is always practical, for it involves the use
of an instrument by an agent to achieve an intended goal, not a
representation of object.



       A more striking example of a symbolic instrument mentioned by
Polanyi is the case of scientific theories. Used, as we are, to think of
knowledge as a visual or intellectual representation of the mater theorized
about, we will be surprised by the idea that scientific theories, like maps
for action, function instrumentally rather than representationally. In
other words, we tend to consider the statement in which, for instance, a
physical law is expressed as a symbolic representation of the intelligible
‘nature’ of the particular phenomenon to which the law refers. For his
part, Polanyi argues that the statements of scientific laws are meant to be
used by the scientific agent as instruments to guide his conduct in the
solution of concrete problems related to the portion of the world to which
the law refers, much as a map is meant to function as an instrument for the
person who uses it to find his way within the mapped territory.  In the
following text,  Polanyi explains how theories function in the actual
practice of science, that is, when the theoretical instrument is actually
used rather than talked about:

      “To rely on a theory for understanding nature is to interiorize it.
      For we are attending from the theory to things seen in its light, and
      we are aware  of the theory, while thus using it, in terms of the
      spectacle that it serves to explain. This is why mathematical theory
      can be learned only by practicing its application: its true knowledge
      lies in our ability to use it.”[22]

      This text, quoted  in isolation from its context, may be too dense
and too cryptic to make us understand how, in Polanyi’s view, scientific
theories function. What is clear is that Polanyi’s view contradicts the
prevailing conception of science as a an exact, or at least an increasingly
approximative, representational  view of reality. Thomas Kuhn, who on this
fundamental point  repeatedly indicates his agreement with Polanyi,
expressed it forcefully:

      “A scientific theory is usually felt to be better than its
      predecessors not only in the sense that it is a better instrument for
      discovering and solving  puzzles but also because it is somehow a
      better representation of what nature is really like. One often hears
      that successive theories grow ever closer to, or approximate more and
      more closely to, the truth. Apparently generalizations like that refer
      not to the puzzle solutions and concrete predictions derived from a
      theory but rather to its ontology, to the match, that is, between the
      entities with which the theory populates nature and what is ‘really
      there.’ Perhaps there is some other way of salvaging the notion of
      ‘truth’ for application to whole theories, but this one will not
      do.... the notion of a match between the ontology of a theory and its
      ‘real’ counter part in nature now seems to me illusive in principle”
      [23]

      What Kuhn is pointing out here is that the statements in which
scientific theories are expressed are not meant to function as
representations of reality but as symbolic instruments to be used by agents
to solve  problems that the surrounding world raises. In this regard,
though, scientific statements constitute no exception. Indeed, if Polanyi
is right, no statement –whether scientific or not– is  meant to function as
a representation of its referent; rather, all statement are meant to
function as  instruments for action, not as representations.

Searle and Polanyi on language

      We are now well equipped to determine which of the two authors whose
views on language we have studied is right. According to Searle, by
replicating in his own mind what the speaker intends to communicate, thanks
to his comprehension of what is said, the hearer gains the knowledge of
what the speaker means or wants to communicate. Thus, according to Searle,
at least in the case of linguistic communication, knowledge consists in the
correspondence between what is meant and said by the speaker and what is
understood by the hearer. This, needless to say, coincides with the
ordinary notion  of knowledge, which the Scholastics, following Aristotle,
defined as adaequatio mentis ad rem: the conformation of the knower’s mind
to the object. The only difference is that here Searle extends the same
conception from the order of extramental reality to which it originally was
applied to the order of linguistic communication. Now it is precisely
regarding  this conception of knowledge that Polanyi parts ways with
Searle.

      For Polanyi knowing is not a matter of becoming aware of the object
to be known by acquiring a sort of reproduction of it that corresponds to
the object, and which is true in the measure in which it corresponds to the
object. Rather, for Polanyi, knowledge is tacit because it does not aim at
reproducing –nor does it reproduce– the object in the mind by enunciating
its characteristics, in which case the knowledge of the object would be
explicit rather than tacit. Knowledge consists, rather,  in the practical
disposition to ACT in the way appropriate to achieve the knower’s goal, and
in actualizing that disposition at the appropriate time. Thus, in the case
of the shock-producing syllables, the subject has no awareness of the
individual characteristics of the syllables. For that very reason the
subject cannot say or express in words what are those characteristics of
which he has no awareness. What the subject acquires, thanks to his past
painful experience of being shocked, is not a faithful  image of the
syllables. Rather, given his natural needs, the subject has a natural
disposition to avoid pain and, hence, to perceive what produces pain not as
object of ‘knowledge,’ in its particular characteristics, but as to be
avoided through action. This  practical disposition to avoid the shock
manifests itself in his bracing himself for the shock when the shock-
producing syllables appear again on the screen, in order to protect himself
as much as possible from the shock.



      Whose conception of the relationship of knowledge to action is right:
Searle’s or Polanayi’s?

      Regarding those acts in which a perlocutionary effect is intended by
the speaker, I have already shown how unpersuasive I find Searle’s view
that in every act of communication the speaker intends to produce the
knowledge of his communicative intentions –that is, the illocutionary
effect–  and that this knowledge suffices to insure the success of the
communicative act. For this means, concretely, that when I give you an
order, what I intend primarily is that you understand and thereby be
informed or acquire factual knowledge that I give you an order, not that
you obey my order. Searle is so convinced that in the case of an order what
the speaker intends is that his order be understood rather than obeyed that
he considers the communicative exchange successful in the case in which the
order is not obeyed, as long as the order is understood by the hearer.

      Why do I think that Searle is wrong on this point? Because this
aspect of Searle’s characterization of linguistic exchanges is  at odds
with the practice of the competent linguistic agents. It is true that I
cannot disobey an order without understanding it and thereby knowing it.
But what is in contention is what constitutes the appropriate knowledge or
understanding of an order and, in general, of all those linguistic acts
that possess perlocutionary force, such as threats, warnings, promises,
demands, etc. According to Searle, the primary intention of the speaker in
all of the them is to inform the hearer of what he means, and only
secondarily and accidentally to achieve the perlocutionary effect typical
of the particular perlocutionary act, such as obeying it in the case of an
order, expecting the fulfillment of the promise in the case of promising,
etc.

      But the linguistic facts contradict Searle’s opinion. If  Searle were
right, there would be no difference in actual use between the grammatical
imperative, used in the case of orders, and the grammatical indicative,
properly used only when one wants to inform of a fact. Furthermore, the
imperative mood  would be totally superfluous,  for imperatives should be
understood as  indicatives. Indeed,  if Searle were right, giving an order
(in the imperative) would be exactly equivalent to saying or enunciating
(in the indicative) that one gives an order. But such is not the case. As I
have already argued, when I give an order, I do not say – in the sense of
enunciating– something to be merely understood, to wit: that I give an
order; rather, I say the order I give in the sense of giving an order to be
obeyed. When I say something in the sense of enunciating it, I refer my
hearer to what I enunciate as something to be understood, that is, I intend
to produce in my interlocutor the knowledge in which, according to Searle,
the illocutionary effect consists. Therefore, if in giving an order I were
enunciating that I give an order and the order I give, I should be
satisfied if the addressee responded  “I understand your order.” But that
is not the case. When I give an order –and this is why I use the
imperative, not the indicative–  I expect and intend my order to be obeyed,
which is the appropriate ‘understanding’ of an order by a competent
linguistic agent.

      Moreover, Searle’s opinion is also at odds with the practice of
linguistic agents not only when what he calls ”perlocutionary effects,” but
also in the case of statements, in which only an illocutionary effect seems
to be intended.. For, as I have already argued, contrary to what Searle
thinks, in a real-life situations, by contrast to the artificial classroom
situation in which people are learning a second language, linguistic agents
do not consider their linguistic action successful if a purely theoretical
understanding of what is said obtains; success, for linguistic agents, is
identical with linguistic action, that is, with action that results by
virtu of what is said. [Cf. GEOMETRY AS APPLICATIVE}.

      Why does Searle identify saying with enunciating?  Because his
linguistic characterizations and, hence, his entire conception of language,
 is based not on his own experience as a linguistic agent who is actually
participating in communicative interactions, but in his reflective
experience as a curious philosopher who reads or overhears the words said
by those who participate in communicative interactions, or as a curious
philosopher who reflects after the fact on the words said by himself and
others when he and they participated in communicative interactions. I
underline “the words said,” to indicate that Searle’s characterization of
language, in spite of his disclaimers, focuses on the words said,
independently of the conduct and the intentions or dispositions of those
who say or hear them as actual participants in linguistic exchanges. This
amounts to identifying linguistic meaning with saying in the sense of
enunciating something, and, consequently, it amounts to considering the
conduct of the linguistic agents as accidental to linguistic meaning,
rather than integral to, or co-constitutive of, linguistic meaning.
Clearly, however, this is not the kind of interiorization through agency
through which children learn to play baseball, without being able to
enunciate the rules they supposedly follow.

      Thus Searle excludes from strictly linguistic meaning anything that
in a linguistic exchange is not  words in which something is said or
enunciated. Such is the case with  the actions and affections intended by
the speaker in the communicative exchange, which consequentlyare seen as
pure;y acidental and are excluded by Searle from being constitutive
elements of linguistic meaning. That, indeed, was the reason why, in his
example of the American soldier captured by the Italians, Searle explicitly
denied that the action by which the American tricked the Italians into
believing that he was a German soldier by uttering the sounds “Ich komme
von dem Land wo die Zitronen  blühen” was a linguistic action, since the
words expressed did not enunciate the action intended.

      The exclusion from linguistic meaning of whatever is not explicitly
said in words is reflected in the separation Searle postulates between the
illocutionary and the perlocutionary effects, as well as in the secondary
and merely accidental role he attributes to the latter. Searle himself , in
explaining his principle of expressibility that  “whatever can be meant can
be said,” implicitly claims that whatever is not said in words cannot be
meant, for he implicitly  excludes from meaning the perlocutionary
effects.[24]

      In short, because he identifies linguistic meaning with what is
expressed in words that supposedly say or enunciate something to be
understood or known, Searle automatically excludes from linguistic meaning
those no less linguistic effects that do not consist in words that
supposedly enunciate something to be understood, namely, the perlocutionary
effects. No wonder, then, that  the way in which language functions in the
artificial context of a course of a language for foreigners, in which only
understanding  what is said counts as linguistic, to the systematic
exclusion of any of the affections or actions intended by the speaker, ends
up appearing as the model linguistic situation for Searle. Indeed it
corresponds to Searle’s defective philosophy of language.

      Now, to understand the differences between Searle and Polanyi, we
have to realize that Searle’s linguistic philosophy amounts to an
application to the field of language of the Platonic-Aristotelian
epistemology inherited by the Western philosophical tradition. In that
tradition,  knowledge consists, on the subject’s part,  in the
representational conformity of his mind to reality, and, on the object’s
part, in the ontological correspondence or conformity to what is in the
subject’s mind. By contrast,  Polanyi’s conception of tacit knowledge
constitutes an alternative to that tradition, for tacit knowledge is to be
used instrumentally by the agent to achieve a desired goal, not a
representation of what is the case.

      As we shall now see, Polanyi does not contend that the Searlean
perlocutionary effects as Searle conceives them, that is, as separated from
the illocutionary effect, are a constitutive element of linguistic meaning,
for the Serlean conception of the illocutionary element as different from
the perlocutionary presupposes the legitimacy of his conceptual distinction
between them. Polanyi would contend, rather, that this Searlean distinction
reflects the separation between the knowing subject and the agent that is
inherent to the epistemology inherited from Aristotle and Plato, which
begins by conceiving knowledge as different from and separable form action.
The Searlean distinction between illocutionary and perlocutionary effects
is possible only on the wrong assumption, common to Aristotle and Plato,
that knowledge and action stand to each other in the relationship
presupposed by their own epistemology.

      According to Searle, the primary and essential function of language

      Having distinguished the common or traditional conception of
knowledge, shared by Searle, from Polanyi’s own, I should now make clear
that the two conceptions are not only different but are mutually exclusive.
In other words, the two cannot be correct. Why not? If for no other reason,
because of the essential role desire plays in tacit knowledge, a role which
is positively excluded in the traditional conception of knowledge, of which
Searle’s views are only a particular version.  Indeed, not only does desire
play no role at all in the traditional conception of knowledge, but it is
positively excluded, for it is deemed to militate against the ‘objectivity’
of the cognition. The reason is that the consciousness of  object, in which
knowledge supposedly consists, as well as its linguistic expression in
enunciative speech, have to represent factually or ‘objectively’ the
object, that is, without addition or subtraction by the subject. Not for
nothing, in his close commentary on Aristotle’s treatise on linguistic
interpretation, did Aquinas contrast what Aristotle sees as the genuinely
enunciative speech of the scientist, which is dictated exclusively by the
object and results in the knowledge of what is the case, with the non-
enunciative speech of the rhetorician that appeals to the desires of the
hearer and, therefore, distorts the object to be known.

       Aquinas’s passage is such a succinct formulation of the distorting
function of desire in matters of cognition that it deserves to be quoted in
full:

      “Then Aristotle says, Let us therefore consider enunciative speech,
      etc. Here he points out that only enunciative speech is to be treated;
      the other four species [to wit, deprecative, imperative,
      interrogative, and vocative] must be omitted as far as the present
      intention is concerned, because their investigation belongs rather to
      the sciences of rhetoric or poetics. Enunciative speech belongs to the
      present consideration and for the following reason: this book is
      ordered directly to demonstrative science, in which the mind of man is
      led by an act of reasoning to assent to truth from those things that
      are proper to the thing; to this end the demonstrator uses only
      enunciative speech, which signifies things according as truth about
      them is in the mind. The rhetorician and the poet, on the other hand,
      induce assent to what they intend not only through what is proper to
      the thing but also through the dispositions of the hearer. Hence,
      rhetoricians and poets for the most part strive to move their auditors
      by arousing certain passions in them, as the Philosopher says in t he
      Rhetorica.[29]

      Notice the contrast between the mind being led to knowledge by the
object –exclusively “from those things that are proper to the thing”–
which Aristotle thinks is the case with genuinely enunciative speech, on
the one hand, and, on the other hand, being led by the desires of the
knower – “through the dispositions of the hearer.” As long as knowledge is
conceived as a factual replication of the object by the consciousness of it
the subject has and/or by the linguistic enunciation by the subject himself
 of what is known to him, then desire can only be seen as a distorting
element in the cognitive act. When, however, as is the case with TK, the
point of cognition is not to represent or mentally reproduce the object but
to function as an instrument for the agent’s conduct to achieve a desired
end, then the desire of the end to be realized through action becomes an
essential constitutive of cognition.[30]

      Let us return to the example Polanyi employs in The Tacit Dimension
to introduce the notion of tacit knowledge. The subject in the experiment
with the syllables knows which syllables produce shocks. However, that
knowledge does not consist in, nor does it manifest itself in, internal or
external  words that express his awareness of those syllables, for the

knowledge manifests itself  in the subject’s conduct of bracing himself for

are the shock-producing syllables, the subject of the experiment does not
know. Yet he knows the syllables in the sense of acting in response to
those syllables by bracing himself for the shock to come. Action,
therefore, rather than linguistic enunciation, is the way in which
knowledge manifests itself in this case.

become aware of the fact that the action is signified. In this way Searle
thinks that he has shown that all signs are signs OF something to be known,

intermediary of the knowledge of what is signified, are meant to terminate

perform the action signified, he has first to enunciate to himself the
action that is to be performed. Polanyi denies the need for this knowledge
of the action signified as a condition for the performance of the action.
According to him, signs signify directly the performance of an action.

      I have already argued this point in favor of Polanyi on the basis of
the difference between the imperative and the indicative moods. Commands
are not enunciated, for the indicative is the mood that corresponds to
enunciation; commands are given, and given in the imperative, as to be
obeyed. The enunciation (in the indicative mood) of what a sign signifies
corresponds to the awareness of a mere fact. As Hume correctly argued,
there is no passage from IS to OUGHT, that is from the indicative to the
imperative, and anyway it doesn’t matter because commands are directly
given in the imperative mood. However, rather than repeating my argument,
let me explore the reasons for the disagreement between Searle and Polanyi
on this point.

      Searle accepts uncritically the traditional notion of knowledge as
awareness of object, thanks to which the object becomes present to the
subject by being verbally re-presented. This is reflected in his theory of
linguistic communication. His demand of saying or enunciating exactly what
one means is based on the assumption that what is said has to correspond to
what is meant in order to render it present to the interpreter. Here it is
a matter of becoming aware. But what if the point of saying is not to make
aware of the characteristics of an object but to elicit an action, which is
the way in which signs function, according to Polanyi?

      As long as knowledge is conceived as a mental word that corresponds
to its object Searle appears to be right. However, if knowledge is
conceived after the way in which signs actually function –that is, as signs
For performing an action– the demand of representational correspondence
between knowledge and object loses its ground, for the correspondence
required is not of the order of awareness but of the order of action. If
the action signaled is performed, the appropriate correspondence obtains.



      Searle is not  interested in action in general but specifically in
linguistic action,* that is, in the action of a hearer that obtains as a
result of what a speaker says and means. Polanyi, for his part, is
interested in action as related to direct perception, not as related to
linguistic communication about an object perceived by a speaker, that is,
about an object of which a hearer becomes aware through understanding what
a speaker says about it.
* He sees a theory of language as a theory of action. Not, however, of all
action but exclusively of linguistic action, that is, of effects that
obtain as a result of a hearer’s understanding of the meaning of what is

in perceiving. Now, a hearer’s action may or may not be among the effects
produced by the speech act of the speaker, for only in the case of speech
acts possessing both illocutionary and perlocutionary force does linguistic
action obtain by virtue of what is said. Thus, action is not an essential
but an accidental effect of speech acts. By contrast, knowledge of what the
speaker means is the essential effect of a speech act, that is, the
essential linguistic effect.
