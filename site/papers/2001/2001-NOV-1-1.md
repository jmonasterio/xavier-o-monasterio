THOMAS KUHN ON SCIENCE AND TEXTBOOK SCIENCE

If someone says that he understands promises because he understands the words in which promises are made, but expects nothing when promised something or intends to do nothing when promising something, he in fact doesn’t understand promises. For the attitude of expecting the fulfillment of the promise when one is promised something, or of intending to fulfill the promise when one promises is constitutive of the practical understanding involved in promising and being promised. And if he claims that being promised amounts to being informed that one is being promised something is what the one who makes the promise means to do by promising, as Searle would logically have to maintain, he is not a competent user of language. (Monasterio)

One of the reasons for the resistance encountered by Kuhn’s views is that he seems to

deny the validity of scientific knowledge even in the case of physics and chemistry, the hardest of the experimental sciences. Whether or to what extent that is indeed the case –something which remains to be determined– that would be at best a conclusion that follows from Kuhn’s analysis of the nature of science. Hence, to dispute that conclusion without considering the argument by which it is supported, as has often been the case with Kuhn’s opponents, just because the conclusion goes against their ingrained convictions, is wrongheaded. In an effort to do justice to Kuhn, I will attempt to reconstruct his argument as precisely as possible, and to pinpoint  the purport of the conclusion that follows from it. However, in order to render fully intelligible Kuhn’s non-traditional understanding of science as well as our attachment to the traditional understanding, I will try to place the whole issue of the nature of science in the context of the history of the two different conceptions of knowledge bequeathed to us by the Greeks, to wit: speculative knowledge and practical knowledge, to which our very notion of science is intimately related.

Kuhn’s argument seems to me to be the following. The traditional view of science in which we all have been steeped is the one presented in the textbooks of science. Such view, however –Kuhn calls it “textbook science”-- is at odds with the way in which science is actually practiced and actually functions. If that is indeed the case, Kuhn argues on the basis of abundant evidence, we have to give up the traditional view of science and draw the consequences that follow from the new view, whichever they may be and however much at odds they may be with our ingrained convictions.

The traditional view

Let us begin with the textbook view of science. What distinguishes scientific knowledge from what most of us ordinarily take for knowledge is its is its certainty, whereas non-scientific knowledge is at best probable, a mere opinion. Scientific knowledge is knowledge reached. by the systematic application of the scientific method, and its certainty  is due precisely to the scientific method. In our everyday knowledge we reach our convictions based on little or no observation at all, whereas scientists begin with intense and very systematic observation of the phenomenon studied.. Thus, for instance, Galileo, who was interested in discovering the law that determines the fall of bodies on inclined planes, actually rolled, one after another, hundreds of marbles of different weights down a plane at different angles of inclination, and carefully measured the time it took each marble to move from the beginning to the end of the plane. Based on this systematic observational stage, at a certain point Galileo understood what appeared to him to be the law that determined the fall of bodies on inclined planes, which in the particular case took the shape of a mathematical formula. One would think that at this second stage of the scientific process Galileo had moved from ignorance to knowledge of the law of falling bodies on inclined planes, much as on the level of ordinary perceptual knowledge one moves from the observational stage of listening carefully to a sound to knowledge that it is the sound of the steps of someone moving along the corridor. Galileo, however, considered as merely hypothetical the supposed knowledge expressed in his mathematical formula. In order to take his formula for legitimate, certain knowledge rather than a mere hypothesis, he needed to check experimentally its validity. If his formula was valid, Galileo reasoned, it should allow him to predict exactly the time it would take any particular marble at any particular angle of inclination to move down an inclined plane. Hence Galileo proceeded to predict, without having ever observed it, what would happen in one particular case after another. As his prediction proved to be exact in every case, at a certain point Galileo became satisfied that he actually knew what he had wanted to know, namely, the law of falling bodies on inclined planes. Now, according to the textbook view, it is this third stage of the scientific process –the experimental verification of the validity of the hypothesis, after the two stages of systematic observation and of  formulation of a hypothesis– that definitely distinguishes scientific knowledge and assures its certainty. If in the case of ordinary perceptual knowledge two of us listen attentively to a sound, and I am convinced that it is the sound of steps in the corridor and you are no less convinced that it is a vibration of the plumbery, our respective convictions are equally hypothetical, and we have no means of settling our disagreement. By contrast, the experimental verification of the validity of hypotheses on the basis of their predictive quality allows the scientists who are in disagreement to determine which of any hypotheses concerning which they may  disagree is the correct one. This is why in physics, concerning the law that determines the fall of bodies on inclined planes, there is no disagreement after Galileo among physicists of any nationality, whereas people continue to disagree endlessly on matters to which the way to apply the scientific method has not been found yet. Because of the crucial role played in them by experiment regarding the validity of their knowledge, the ‘hard sciences,’ such as physics, chemistry, astronomy are rightly called ‘experimental sciences,’ by contrast to such ‘soft sciences’ as history or economics, as well as by contrast to the ordinary, unscientific knowledge of ordinary people.

Some history of scientific knowledge (See my wpd2001-feb-1, pp. 5-13)

For the purpose of rendering fully intelligible Kuhn’s debunking of the textbook view of science just presented it will be useful to undertake an historical excursion regarding the notion of scientific knowledge. Though avowedly superficial, this excursion will not be, I hope, necessarily distorting.

As is well known, Socrates waged a war against the rhetoricians of his time who in their schools taught the youth of Athens how to conduct themselves in both their public and their private lives. Socrates’ grudge against them was that they were ‘sophists,’ that is, people who pretended to know, since they taught others, but who really did not know. What they taught was their opinions –we might say, in reference to the scientific method, their hypotheses-- which they mistook for knowledge. The Platonic dialogues, where we see Socrates in action, show him again and again debunking his adversaries’ claims and showing that they actually didn’t know what they claimed to know. Socrates, for his part, more humble than his adversaries, but also more perceptive, concluded that he didn’t know anything, for none of his opinions was certainly true, but at least he knew that he didn’t know. In short, probably for the first time in history, Socrates conducted a critical review of what ordinarily goes under the name of knowledge and found it wanting. The fact that in this process he exposed the ignorance of his vain and powerful enemies

cost him his life.

We need to be clear, however, as to the particular area in which Socrates found wanting what went under the name of knowledge. Indeed, far from disputing the validity of mathematical knowledge, Socrates seems to have taken it, implicitly, as the standard by which he judged the knowledge in all other areas. As Plato, Socrates’ disciple would later argue, what distinguishes mathematical knowledge is that it can be demonstrated to be necessarily true. In geometry, for instance, the validity of the Pythagorean theorem, or of any other Euclidean theorem for that matter, can be demonstrated with absolute certainty, so that one may be absolutely sure of its truth. Knowledge whose absolute validity could be demonstrated, as is the case with mathematics   – that  is what Socrates wanted.. However, personally, Socrates was not interested in mathematics; at all;  the areas that interested him were what today we would call ethics in personal and political matters. What he wanted to know with absolute certainty is what the sophists claimed to know and actually didn’t, to wit: how human beings ought to conduct themselves in their personal and in their political lives. In spite of his strenuous efforts, Socrates died without being able to overcome his ignorance. Unlike the sophists, however, he died knowing at least that he was ignorant.

Socrates’s most distinguished disciple, Plato, to whom we owe most of what we know of Socrates’ quest and personality, pursued his master’s undertaking after his death. He founded a school or academy. It is told that in its frontispiece the following inscription was engraved: NON NISI MATHEMATICI. Clearly, Plato identified mathematical knowledge as the concrete epistemic model of what his mater had dreamed of. More sanguine than Socrates, though, Plato came to believe, at least for a while, that the demonstrative, absolutely certain knowledge that obtained in mathematics was possible in the ethical areas that had interested Socrates. Plato’s famous theory of the eternal existence of the “Forms” or “Ideas” was the support he claimed for this conviction. Indeed, Plato thought that for any judgment to be necessarily true, as was the case within geometry, its object had to exist necessarily and, therefore, eternally; for otherwise the  judgment  concerning that object would either be altogether false, since it did not correspond to what was actually the case, or would be true only for a while, for it would cease being true as the object to which it referred ceased to exist as stated. What made each of the geometrical theorems true, Plato thought, was that an ideal, perfect triangle, square, circle, etc., existed eternally and necessarily in an ideal intelligible world, so that  each of the theorems of Eucledean geometry was necessarily and eternally true, since what it affirmed corresponded to the properties of the ideal intelligible figures.

It is not difficult to gather from the above that Plato understood by knowledge the absolutely necessary, and therefore absolutely certain, correspondence between what the knower thinks to be the case and the object known, for if the correspondence between the two is not necessary the judgment may conceivably be false. Such correspondence, Plato beleved, was possible only in the area of the purely intelligible, for all sensible objects are temporal and mutable; consequently, the judgments that refer to them can never be absolutely certain, since their correspondence to what is the case cannot be guaranteed. Plato saw in the demonstrations of of the necessary truth of geometrical theorems the proof of the eternal existence of the intelligible Forms that guaranteed their truth. He taught, by contrast, that all the judgments about the existence of sensible objects, given the irremediable temporality of the latter, were inherently and irremediably uncertain. The existence of the sensible world, as we perceive it, was, therefore, in Plato’s view, a matter of belief rather than of knowledge -- doxa rather than episteme.

What, then, about the knowledge in the area of ethics in private and public life, which alone had really interested Socrates? Was it a matter of genuine, demonstrably true knowledge, or of mere opinion? Plato seems to have thought, at least for a while, that in matters of conduct absolute certainty was possible. Indeed, aside and above from the other Forms or Ideas of the purely intelligible there existed the supreme Idea of the Good or the absolutely desirable, to whose intuitive contemplation the genuine “ philosophoi ” or lovers of wisdom could raise themselves, thereby gaining the absolutely certain knowledge of the way in which people ought to conduct themselves and organize the city. Once they possessed that knowledge, they were ready to govern themselves and others for they actually knew how people ought to act. In Plato’s view, the human world would be set aright the day these philosophers or genuinely wise people would be kings.

For our purposes it is not important to settle the much disputed questions of the nature of the supreme Form of the Good by contrast to that of the other Forms, nor the extent to which Plato actually thought that absolutely certain knowledge could be attained in matters of human conduct. The reason is that, as we shall see, both the existence of all the Forms, not only of the form of the Good, as well as the idea of the possibility of absolutely certain knowledge in ethical matters would be debunked by Aristotle and, for all practical purposes, lose currency after him. What we should keep in mind concerning Plato is that he consecrated the notion of scientific knowledge as necessarily true and therefore absolutely certain, and that he made no distinction whatever between theoretical and practical knowledge –an important distinction which we owe to Aristotle. With these remarks we can move to Aristotle.

After a period of full identification with the views of Plato, Aristotle, his disciple, would start taking his distances from him. And the most significant difference between the two of them is that Aristotle found unacceptable and dismissed the Platonic view of the eternal existence of the Forms.  As we know, for Plato, the eternal existence of the intelligible Forms that actually corresponded to the universal judgments of mathematics was the justification of the necessarily true character of those judgments. In other words, the existence of the intelligible world of the Forms was the necessary condition for scientific or necessarily true knowledge. Did Aristotle, therefore, deny the possibility of scientific knowledge by denying the existence of the Platonic forms? The answer is no. Aristotle discovered the abstractive ability of the human mind that allowed it to isolate the intelligible from the sensible component in those objects in which the two components coexisted, and thus to make absolutely certain, universal judgments concerning the intelligible component Thus, in the case of geometry, for instance, the mind was capable of abstracting the intelligible form “triangle” from the particular sensible triangle of such a color and size, and of making necessarily true, universal judgments concerning that form. The form, however,  didn’t exist independently of the sensible, material triangle in a heaven of eternal ideas, as Plato had claimed; rather, it was the form of this particular triangle. However, as the form itself was intelligible rather than sensible, it was the from of all possible triangles, and therefore its intelligible or essential properties had to be the same in all triangles, even in those triangles that had never been and would never be actually observed by anybody. And what happened in the area of mathematics, happened, mutatis mutandis, in the area of the natural phenomena, as those covered by physics, in which strict uniformity reigned..Thanks to that natural uniformity, universal judgments can therefore be made  in the area of physics as in the area of geometry. However, the physical phenomena do not possess the same kind of intelligibility as the geometrical objects, since the former cannot be demonstrated a priori but were based on previous observation. To put it concretely by means of an example, it is a uniform natural fact that all bodies fall. However, from the mere notion of “body” it cannot be deduced that a body will necessarily fall. In order to know that bodies fall experience is needed. By contrast , from the mere notion of “triangle” it can be demonstratively shown that its three angles necessarily are equal to two straight angles. Thus the ‘laws’ of geometry are strictly universal in that their necessity is intelligible, whereas the laws of physics are merely general, though no less certain.

For our purposes, however, the knowledge of these details of Aristotle’s views is not

important. What is important is to realize that, though Aristotle did away with the Platonic transcendent heaven of eternal ideas, he nevertheless preserved the Platonic notion of scientific knowledge as necessarily true, since the universal judgments in such areas as mathematics and physics had their corresponding objects in the immanent forms.
