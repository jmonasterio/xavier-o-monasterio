"THE HUMAN CONDITION" CLUSTER

Course: Existentialism (Phl. 360)

The very expression "The Human Condition" was if not coined at least rendered famous by a French existentialist, André Malraux.

This course, which focuses on Albert Camus' philosophical and literary works (with, in addition, a brief excursion into one of Sartre's works), follows in detail the former's philosophical evolution.

Camus begins with the 'existentialist' view of the human condition, which together with Sartre he contributed to popularize in the early 40's, that life is absurd and that, consequently, morals are a matter of arbitrary preference. Further reflection, however, prompted by his exposure to Nazism and his active involvement against it, led Camus tot the conclusion that, although life is indeed absurd, morals are not a matter of arbitrary preference. In both moments of this process Camus contrast his understanding of the human condition with the Christian understanding, which adds a further dimension to the theme. The close connection which Camus establishes between the understanding of the human condition to which one adheres and the way in which one lives shows the practical relevance of the theme of the human condition, which at first sight might appear as purely theoretical.

Xavier O. Monasterio
