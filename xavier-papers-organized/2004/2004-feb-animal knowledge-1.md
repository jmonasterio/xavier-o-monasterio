ANIMAL KNOWLEDGE AND NATURAL INFERENCE

Practical animal knowledge, Practical Human Knowledge, and Aristotelian “Scientific Knowledge”

Do animals know? To Plato, for whom genuine knowledge by contrast to mere belief or opinion ( doxa ) couldn’t be but a deed of the intellect, and who, consequently, had an acute sense of the unbridgeable difference between animals and people, such question would have sounded not only trivial and uninteresting but simply wrongheaded. Not so to Aristotle, who, unlike Plato, in time came to distinguish practical knowledge from the purely theoretical knowledge which Plato had exclusively cared for, who therefore could not exclude the possibility of some kind of continuity between animals and people. My interest in Aristotle’s exploration of the issue whether animals know is that it seems to open the way to an  alternative to the traditional notion of knowledge as perceptual or intellectual representation of the real –the sort of alternative Kuhn was obscurely groping for in order to give full shape to his understanding of science.

In my <2002-April-3-Aris-Devereux: Practical Knowledge in Aristotle> I argued that, at least at the stage of his philosophical evolution to which the texts studied by Devereux belong, , Aristotle, contrary to what Devereux thinks, failed to distinguish adequately practical from theoretical knowledge. ^(F1) The reason for that failure is, I surmise,  that, still very much under Plato’s influence, Aristotle could not yet  help looking in practical knowledge for a universal intelligible component which was an essential characteristic of Plato’s conception of  knowledge as a deed of the intellect, though practical knowledge is unavoidably particular. There is, however, in Aristotle's writings, still a third version of practical knowledge not considered by Devereux , in which not only is there a connection between knowledge and action, but the connection between knowledge and action is intrinsic, that is, knowledge is practical in that, thanks to desire, it is bound to terminate in action, and this without the mediation of any previous theoretical or purely factual  knowledge. ^(F2)

The connection of knowledge with action is not found in theoretical knowledge, whose purpose is knowledge for its own sake, the mere grasp of the truth, of what is the case. Therein lies, in Aristotle's opinion at the stage of his evolution studied by Devereux, the difference between theoretical and practical knowledge, as well as the consequent radical epistemic difference between people and  animals. Animals are capable of attaining only the knowledge that is useful to the preservation and well-being of the individual or of the species to which the animal belongs, while people are capable of both theoretical and practical knowledge. However, at this  stage of Aristotle's evolution, the connection of practical knowledge with action is extrinsic . In this view, the fact that practical knowledge is connected with action does not detract from the fact that it is knowledge, that is,  grasp or ‘vision' of what is the case that corresponds to what is the case, for that is what knowledge consists in.

In what way is the connection of practical knowledge with action, extrinsic? In that the same fact may be object of both practical and theoretical knowledge. To begin with a concrete example, on the basis of their respective past experience, both horses and people may come to grasp the useful ‘universal’ fact that, say, eating such a plant has  purgative effects. However, the difference between people and animals is that horses only know that fact practically, that is, for the purpose of applying that knowledge in their practice, whereas people know the same fact practically and also theoretically or predicatively, as a stated or statable truth about nature,.independently of its practical utility. Thus the connection between practical knowledge and action is extrinsic, in that what is known in the case of practical and theoretical knowledge is the same fact, to wit, that this plant produces purgative effects, but in the case of people the knowledge of this fact does not necessarily terminate in the action of eating the plant. What is known by both the animal  and the human is the same, even though the animal grasps the purgative power of the plant only from the point of view of its usefulness and only for the purpose of using what it knows, whereas people know it both from the point of view of its usefulness and also as an objective universal factual property of the plant. In other words,  the human knows the ‘universal’ fact also theoretically or predicatively, as universal and independently of its utility and its connection with action.*

* Aristotle deems this knowledge to be "experiential." What does this mean? Think, in the first place, of the horse’s case. The horse can attain the knowledge of the purgative effects of the herb only through 'personal' experience of those effects, that is by experiencing them, by being actually affected by them,  while humans may come to know the purgative effects of the herb without any personal experience of them, by being told of them by somebody else, or by observing the purgative effects of the plant in somebody else, without themselves actually experiencing them in the sense of suffering or undergoing them. To be precise, however, the horse’s experience is not only experience of the purgative effects, but also experience of suffering or undergoing those effects after eating the plant. In other words, it is experience of both the purgative effects and of the action that is followed by the purgative effect. Thus the horse learns not only the purgative effects a plant produces, but also learns what to do to obtain or avoid that effect, for the horse associates the action of eating the plant with the effects it produced. Thereafter, eating the plant becomes the means to obtain the purgative effects.

This learning is experiential in that it is not inherited or innate, but acquired through experience . Experience, however, is not a matter of observed effects, but of effects felt or suffered (not   observed ) by the animal and tacitly connected with eating such a plant. The purgative effects of the action of eating this plant are undergone and experienced, in the sense of being felt; they are not observed or perceived by the agent who eats the plant. If the purgative effects are experienced as favorable or pleasant or satisfactory by the agent who experiences the effects, the action of eating the plant will automatically tend to be repeated under the appropriate circumstances in the expectation of similar effects. In other words, in a sort of combination of Hume’s and Skinner views, actions followed by felt rewarding effects lead to the expectation of the same effects in the future and tend to be repeated; actions followed by unpleasant felt effects tend to be avoided, and from objects that look similar similar effects are expected. {Herein lies the natural inference, of which Newman speaks, but he does not notice that the inference is practical, for it consists in expectation, in bracing oneself for the effects at the appropriate time, not in enunciation of what is expected ) ..And this mechanism is  at the origin of all further purposive action initiated by both animals and people as living sensitive bodies, and of all future learning about the world as our abode, as our concrete dramatic space.

It is very important, though, to underscore that the expectation of certain effects to which I am referring, of purgative effects in the case of our example,  is ‘expressed’ or exhibited by the animal – and manifested to us– in the animal’s conduct of eating or abstaining from eating the plant, not in statements , of which animals are, of course, incapable. And the same applies to people, though they are capable of making statements. For expecting and stating that one expects are not the same; so the conduct of expecting, not the statement of expecting, is the genuine expression of expectation. Indeed, I can make a statement of expectation without really expecting, that is, when  my expectation is not incarnated or expressed in the attitude and the conduct of expecting. Example: As implied by Nietzsche, Christians can say,  “ Expecto resurrectionem mortuorum ,” when their conduct throughout life and especially just before death belies their statement. (Once again, the primacy of praxis over doxa ). The converse is also true: I can expect certain effects without stating what is expected by me as an objective fact.  

Now these acquired expectations are directly connected with action without the mediation of any theoretical  knowledge, that is, purely objective or factual knowledge independent from desire, for expectation is not stated expectation. In other words, the horse doesn’t know-that all plants of that descriptive kind are purgative. The animal just has learned to expect purgative effects from that kind of plant, and to look for that kind of plant when purgation is desired. There is memory of the perceivable plant but only as desirable for purgation when opportune . Whenever in the future purgation is desirable for the animal’s survival or well-being, the animal will actively look for that kind of plant and eat it. This is purely practical recognition of the plant for purposes of action, for the animal has no theoretical or purely facutal idea-that there is this kind of plant that uniformly produces purgative effects in such a kind of animals.

Thus the recognition involved in animal knowledge is purely practical in the form of virtual or actual expectation, depending on the circumstances, and it certainly  is not verbalized or stated by the animal. Unfortunately, at least the stage of the evolution of his thought concerning practical knowledge studied by Devereux, Aristotle saw this practical recognition as akin to the  universalization involved in reflective human judgments, instead of seeing the apparently objective, linguistic human ability involved in practical knowledge as akin to the animals' instrumental knowledge intrinsically related to action, as SIGNS are. In other words, Aristotle assimilated expectation to stated or objectively known expectation . However, Aristotle changed his mind, at least partially, when he realized that practical knowledge is mediated by desire and, thus, intrinsically connected with, or bound to terminate in, action. This is reflected in his De Motu Animalium and –though less clearly–  in the sections of his Nicomachean Ethics that refer to the practical syllogism .

Martha Nussbaum  identifies Aristotle’s notion of practical knowledge with this latter view, exclusively , ignoring the texts that reflect the probably former view studied by Devereux. For his part, in his article on “Practical Knowledge in Aristotle,” Devereux considers exclusively the first view as if it represented Aristotle’s only or at least definitive conviction on this matter. As for myself, I think that Devereux's position represents an earlier, and Nussbaum's position a later and last stage, in Aristotle's evolution concerning practical knowledge.

What precisely is the difference between the notion of practical knowledge that Devereux attributes to Aristotle (position "a") and the one that Nussbaum attributes to him (position "b")? Firstly, in the case of (a), the practical knowledge animals possess is akin to the theoretical (linguistic, predicative, objective) knowledge of which people are capable, in that a certain grasp of the universal by the animal is supposedly involved. ^(F3) Secondly, the connection of knowledge with action is extrinsic, in that action may or may not follow because practical knowledge is similar to theoretical knowledge, and may, but is not bound to, terminate in action. This amounts to saying that practical knowledge is subordinated to theoretical knowledge in that theoretical knowledge is a necessary condition for practical knowledge. ^(F4) By the way, this is how Searle construes the connection between the illocutionary and the perlocutionary effects: if the addressee does not know theoretically the illocutionary meaning of what the speaker says, the addressee cannot perform the perlocutionary action expected by the speaker. That is to say that in order to actually do what the speaker commands, the child needs first to know factually or objectively the order given. Moreover, the addressee may comprehend theoretically the perlocutionary force of what the speaker says, and yet not perform the action intended by the speaker, because the action is purely accidental  to the linguistic meaning. Iin other words, the connection  between the knowledge of the perlocutionary meaning and the action is extrinsic.

The case is different in (b), the version of practical knowledge that Nussbaum attributes to Aristotle. Here the connection of knowledge with action is intrinsic. Practical knowledge is practical in that, being informed by desire –which is the novelty of the De Motu Animalium by contrast to the Posterior Analytics an the Metaphysics –  it is bound to terminate in action when the  appropriate conditions of perceptual presence of object and of desire obtain.. Here no theoretical knowledge is prerequired for action because practical knowledge is genuinely practical, not theoretical knowledge only accidentally or extrinsically related to action.  In (b), if there is knowledge, there is action, when the appropriate circumstances obtain, unless the action be prevented by factors beyond the agent's control. To use Nussbaum's example, if I am in Smyrna, and  I want to see my friend John and I know that John is in Athens, I will take a boat to go to Athens if and when there is a boat going to Athens. Similarly, if a hungry dog knows that there is food available in a certain place, the dog will go to that place, because knowing is genuinely practical, that is,  intrinsically connected with action. If a child desires a toy and knows where the toy is, the child will go to that place, if at all possible. (By the way, this is how Polanyi conceives tacit knowledge: what is known functions instrumentally, as the "from where" or sign-for ACTION, , but the sign is not known theoretically or objectively or predicatively by the agent. Hence no theoretical knowledge is prerequired as a condition for practical knowledge).

However, Nussbaum rightly observes a difference between animals and people, concerning  practical knowledge. In the case of the animal, if there is conflict of desires, the knowledge connected with the strongest desire at the time will necessarily (as a matter of conceptual necessity), under the appropriate conditions, result in action; in the case of people, though, the action is not absolutely certain, because they are genuinely linguistic creatures and, therefore, can deliberate and choose their course of action. To this I want to add another observation that completes the one made by Nussbaum. Thanks to language and its connotational-conceptual character , people are capable of abstraction from their own exposure. This results in abstract knowledge in which the connection with action is temporarily relaxed. From this arises the illusion of disinterested knowledge, of knowledge for its own sake, with which Aristotle identified theoretical or objective knowledge, which he wrongly interpreted as knowledge of the object through the concept of the object.  However, theoretical knowledge, as conceived by Aristotle, is an illusion, in that the potential connection with action of what is situationally or ‘indexically’  known (rather than conceptually known) remains implicit, as shown by the return to dramatic space at the moment of application.

Language is, therefore, the clue for understanding both the so- called scientific knowledge, as well as the difference between the practical knowledge of animals and of people. But language is the clue only if it is understood as language actually functions, tacitly and instrumentally, that is, dramatically, in the case of exposed agents, not explicitly and objectively, as Aristotle thought when he invented the categories that made possible his metaphysics. ^(F5)

How, concretely, is language the clue? What precise difference does language make between people and animals?  By relaxing the immediate connection between knowledge and action, linguistic connotation  makes abstraction from the subject's immediate exposure possible. This opens the field for the space and time of the natural sciences, with its ‘objective’ observation of the interactions of things. The relaxation, however, is only provisional, for all action takes place in dramatic space where agents attempt to achieve their goals, and the return to that space takes place at the moment of application to reality. Application on paper is a sort of tacit map for action. Application to reality takes place at the dramatic or indexical time at which the map is activated by the agent, when the appropriate conjunction of desire and circumstances obtains.
