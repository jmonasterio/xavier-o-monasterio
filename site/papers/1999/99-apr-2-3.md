The Enlightenment’s Reason

“‘Not to philosophize is still to philosophize.” The philosophical discourse of the West claims the amplitude of an all-encompassing structure or of an ultimate comprehension. It compels every other discourse to justify itself before philosophy. - Rational theology accepts this vassalage. If, for the benefit of religion, it reserves a domain from the authority of philosophy, one will know that this domain will have been recognized to be philosophically unverifiable. - The dignity of being the ultimate and royal discourse belongs to Western philosophy because of the strict coinciding of thought, in which philosophy resides, and the idea of reality in which this thought thinks. For thought, this coinciding means not having to think beyond what belongs to ‘being’s move’ [‘geste d’etre’]... For the being of reality, this coinciding means: to illuminate thought and the conceived by showing itself. To show oneself, to be illuminated, is just what having meaning is... That a thought comes to know of it is intelligibility. Rationality has to be understood as the incessant emergence of thought from the energy of ‘being’s move’ or its manifestation, and reason has to be understood out of this rationality. Meaningful thought and thought about being would be pleonasms and equivalent pleonasms... - Philosophical discourse therefore should b e able to include God, of whom the Bible speaks–if this God does have a meaning. But as soon as he is conceived this God is situated within ‘being’s move.’ He is situated there as an entity par excellence. If the intellectual understanding of the biblical God, theology, does not reach to the level of philosophical thought, this is not because it thinks God as a being without first explicating ‘the being of being,’ but because in thematizing God it brings God into the course of being. But in the most unlikely way–that is, not analogous with an idea subject to criteria, or subject to the demand that it show itself to be true or false–the God of the Bible signifies the beyond being, transcendence. Rational theology, fundamentally ontological, strives to take account of transcendence in the domain of being by expressing it with adverbs of height applied to the verb being: God is said to exist eminently or par excellence... - One can also... claim that the God of the Bible does not have a meaning, that is, is not properly speaking thinkable. This would be the other term of the alternative. ‘ The concept of God is not a problematical concept; it is not a concept at all,’ writes Mme. Delhomme in a recent book, continuing a major tradition of philosophical rationalism which refuses to accept the transcendence of the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob among the concepts without which there would be no thought. What the Bible puts above all comprehension would have not yet reached the threshold of intelligibility! -  The problem which is thus posed, and which will be ours, is whether the meaning that is equivalent to the esse of being, that is, the meaning which is meaning in philosophy, is not already a restriction of meaning... Over and beyond being, does not a meaning whose priority, translated into ontological language, would have to be called antecedent to being, show itself? It is not certain that in going beyond the terms and beings one necessarily relapses into speaking of opinion or faith. In fact, in staying or wanting to be outside of reason, faith and opinion speak the language of being. Nothing is less opposed to ontology than opinion and faith. To ask, as we are trying to do here, if God can be expressed in a rational discourse which would be neither ontology nor faith is implicitly to doubt the formal opposition, established by Yehouda Haleve and taken up by Pascal, between the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, invoked in faith without philosophy, and the god of philosophers. It is to doubt that this opposition constitutes an alternative” (Levinas, “God and Philosophy,” in E. Levinas, Collected Philoso0phical Papers , Martinus Nijhoff, Dordrecht 1987, pp. 153-155).

Cette philosophie a pour fonction essentielle d’etablir un horizon d’intelligibilite sur lequel puissent apparaitre, d’une maniere admissible pour la raison, les verites de la revelation. Oeuvre qui se veut rationnelle, certes, mais qui a la particulalrite d’avoir ete mise au point par des theologiens. Que l’on s’inspire d’Aristote ou du neoplatonisme, ou encore de Heidegger et de Kant, il y a de Thomas d’Aquin a Karl Rahner une continuite remarquable. Dans cette perspective, Bouillard a parfaitement raison de reclamer de la part du theologien estime et ouverture a la valeur de verite de la philosophie, mais il faut ajouter de ce type de philosophie - Car depuis longtmeps, la situation n’est plus la meme en theologie protestante. Editions du Cerf. Attelier National. Reproduction des Theses, Universite Lille III), pp. 195-196.

“Barth condammne toute pensee qui s’erige en systeme complet et, par le fait meme, unique, de la verite. Or comme il n’appartient pas a une ecole theologique qui a canonise et domestique une philosophie determineee, Barth rencontre des philosophies qui, la plupart du temps, se presentent sous cette forme totalitaire: Kant, Hegel, et la majorite des grands noms de la philosophie moderne. Barth s’opposera a ces philosophies, dans la mesure, en tout cas, ou elles s’elevent au rang de systemes absolus. Cette opposition n’est dailleurs pas originale: elle est ou devrait etre le fait de tout theologien, pour qui c’est la revelation, et non la philosophie, qui prononce la parole decisive sur l’homme, sur l’histoire et sur le monde. Barth a eu le merite de le rappeler a la theologie protestante, a une epoque ou celle-ci etait dominee par la theologie liberale, ou le sens du Dieu de la Bible risquait de se diluer dans des considerations philosophiques relevant generalement de l’idealisme. D’abord sur le mode prophetique, puis de maniere systematique (dans sa Dogmatique ), Barth se fit le defenseur sourcilleux des droits imprescriptibles de la parole de Dieu, qui transcend tout produit de la reflexion humaine” (Ibid. , pp. 192-193).

“I know that you risk appearing like some kind of new age goofball if you say you believe in angels, but I do believe in angels, along with all Judaism up to those German rationalists Reform Jews who, starting in Frankfurt in 1823, introduced a lot of good things into Judaism and also killed a lot of good things that were already in Judaism. What they killed was everything that did not fit with the historicist and rationalist and idealist philosophies of nineteent-century Germany. The idea that God revealed the Torah was rejected, Jewish mysticism was rejected, the world to come was rejected, angels were rejected, indeed for a while having a Torah scroll in a Reform Temple was also rejected. Unless you think Hegel, Fichte, Schelling and Kant are the last word of human wisdom and that the Prussian state was the epitome of human culture, you are going to have to move on, as our movement itself has done, and reclaim the authentic Jewish beliefs our movement ignorantly rejected and in doing so left us naked to the worst predations of secular humanism, positivism, and the spiritually desiccating equation of Judaism with political activity” (Marc Gellman, “What Are You Looking For,” in First Things (                         ), p. 109).

I think comparable ravages to the ones described by Gell in Judaism occurred in Catholicism. However,  in the American Catholic scene the ravages occurred place later, only when Catholic universities adopted the Enlightenment’s standards of secular universities, mainly through Catholics who had graduated from secular universities and imported from them their norms of scientific rigor and their correlative idea of academic freedom. By the way, the Enlightenment effected similar ravages–and even more brutally–in protestant theology, with its extreme liberalism that reduced faith to scientific history.

 I am convinced that these ravages are essentially due to a conception of reason and knowledge which reached its mature shape with the Enlightenment. Typical constitutives of this conception of knowledge and reason are the following : the separation of knowledge from desire and, consequently, from action; a purely specular, purely objective, reproductive not ion of knowledge; the depreciation of the senses as furnishing only, and at best, the material for knowledge; the correlative appreciation of intellect or reason as furnishing the formal aspect of knowledge, which involves universality and necessity; the depreciation of sensible, material reality

as particular, contingent and, therefore, strictly unknowable; the mind-body separation; the body as siege of the unreliable senses and as source of the surd, non-cognitive feelings and of the distorting passions, and the mind as the truly cognitive faculty or portion of the human being; the

potential, consequently unavoidable distinction between the thing in itself and the thing for us, between phenomenon and noumenon;  the resulting, unending opposition between idealism and realism.   

With Kant this conception of reason as related to knowledge became enthroned. Because of lack of intuition, which is possible only in the case of the sensible, metaphysics with its pretention to universality and absolute necessity became impossible, and has remained so. That is an aspect of Kant’s views that has become accepted dogma, though it rests on the distinction between phenomenon and noumenon and, thus, on the specular view of knowledge . Together with it, the acceptance of the validity of the hard sciences–though the idea of the  hard sciences and the justifications of their validity have evolved much since Kant. Since the ideas of God and of freedom belong among the necessary postulates of practical reason, for there is no possible intuition that corresponds to them, their respective objects cannot be known . If they exist, they are things in themselves, but not things for us. Yet Kant makes the existence of the respective objects of those ideas, together with the immortality of the soul, necessary postulates of practical reason, for without them the whole field of moral, dutiful, action would be inconceivable.

It is almost superfluous to add that Kant has been much less followed in the views presented in the Second Critique, while his rejection of metaphysics and of God in the First Critique have been taken very seriously by many intellectuals and have contributed to the death of God in learned circles.

Kant’s system, however, is far from being unproblematic. One of the attendant problems concerns the knowledge of other subjects as subjects, given that there is no possible intuition of the other as subject. Perhaps a more serious problem is the radical distinctions between the noumenal and the phenomenal, between the order of knowledge and the order of action, between the order of causality to which we belong as objects and the order of freedom and morality to which we belong as agents, with the puzzling result that the known human being is not free as an object in the physical world and is nonetheless free as an agent, or that the known human being and the agent are not one and the same . A third problem is the ontological status–phenomenal or noumenal–of the knowing subject, and the relationship of this knowing subject–identity or difference?–to the agent. The same problem,. in a larger dimension, is the relationship between the order of knowledge of the First Critique and the order of action of the Second. Or, what amounts to the same, the very distinction between Pure and Practical Reason.

These problems suggest that taking Kant for the final, fully satisfactory word on knowledge and reason may not be warranted. But for my present purposes the important point is that, much as his predecessors had done, Kant borrowed from the philosophical tradition, without any substantive modification, the Greek view of speculative knowledge as objective, neutral, dispassionate reproduction of reality. Only, unlike his predecessors, he denied that the actual thing in itself (and therefore the correspondence between the known and the actually existent reality) could ever be known for lack of confirmatory sensible intuition. However, being post-Galilean and fully conscious of the success of the physical sciences, Kant was inclined to identify speculative knowledge with the knowledge of the physical world.

Kant borrowed from the philosophical tradition the distinction between speculative and practical knowledge, which had always been problematic and obscure, partly because of the distrust of feelings and emotions as non-cognitive or as positively disturbing knowledge. Feelings are absent from the First Critique as non- cognitive, non-intentional. (By the same token, motivation to act disappears, and the connection between knowledge and action becomes extrinsic). Only in the Second Critique do feelings, or interests, as motives for action appear, but even there they appear only as dis-tracting the agent from the dispassionate respect of practical REASON for duty.
