\WP\chantill\Anselm

                                  Anselm
              "More perfect than which nothing can be"
            Understanding - Believing that - Believing in.
Experiential notion of rain:
                 water pouring from the sky that wets the earth... that
makes crops possible... without which there is nothing to eat... so that
people die...
                 water pouring from the sky that creates rivers... that
makes fluvial navigation possible... without which  goods do not reach
distant towns... so that people...
                 water pouring from the sky... which makes public games
impossible... which... so that people...
      Believing in rain is projective (toward the future) and
dispositional. It is desiring or fearing or enjoying or detesting water
pouring from the sky under a particular set of experienced effects. These
effects are constitutive of rain. They are not effects, as we usually
understand effects, but traits of rain itself, since they are constitutive
of the experiential notion of rain. Rain is, inseparably, desirable pouring
of water from the sky-getting the earth wet-conditioning crops...
      Believing that water pouring from the sky wets the earth, swells the
rivers...--independently of benefits or damages received from it.
            If "believing in rain" by definition involves fearing or
desiring rain as constituted by a particular set of traits, then "believing
that rain..." is idiosyncratic. I believe that rain wets the earth, you
believe that rain swells the rivers, he believes that rain prevents public
games. In other words, a "belief that" is common only to those who desire
or fear rain under the same set of traits. In this sense "believing in"
implies "believing that," but not the other way around. For you "believe
that rain swells the rivers," whereas I "do not believe that rain swells
the rivers," but "that rain wets the earth." This commonality among
believers-in makes it possible for the members of a particular community to
talk among themselves about the rain in the nominative without
equivocating; obviously, however, they would equivocate if they talked
about the rain with the members of other communities, for their definition
of rain involves different sets of desirables or undesirables, and some of
those desirables and undesirables may be incompatible with each other.
      In the case of rain, though, even though the sets of desirable traits
constitutive of rain are different, the  observable phenomena are common to
the members of different communities of believers-in: water pouring from
the sky-wetting the earth-swelling rivers... Now, that rain wets the earth
is an essential trait for the members of one community, since it is
constitutive of the rain they desire, while that it wets the earth may be
an inessential property for the members of another community. However, from
another point of view, abstracting from their desires and fears and placing
themselves as pure observers, they can create a community of pure observers
of 'rain in itself' and talk about 'rain' in the nominative without
equivocation. On this level they all believe "that water pouring from the
sky wets the earth, swells the rivers, prevents public games..."
      How does this kind of universal "belief that" relate to "belief in"?
Let us call this new universal community the community of observers, and
the previous different communities the communities of practitioners. All
the people who have an experiential notion of rain are members of the
community of observers, but only some of them are members of each of the
community of practitioners. But can a member continue to be a believer-in,
a member of a particular community of believers-in, while being a member of
the large community of believers-that? After all, we previously said that
the beliefs-that of different communities are different, that I believe
that rain wets the earth and you do not believe that. It seems that what
happens here is that we are equivocating in a very radical way. Indeed, we
have shifted from the universe of desire-aversion to the universe of
neutral observation. We might think that we are speaking about the same
rain, for water pouring from the sky is an observable common to all the
communities of practitioners. But each of those communities is a community
of desire-aversion, not of mere observation, and in the order of desire-
aversion water pouring from the sky wetting the earth is constitutive of
rain as an indissoluble whole. Water pouring from the sky is not a mere
observable for the community of practitioners that has the experiential
notion of water wetting the earth and desires rain for that reason.
      More clarification of this point is needed. One important difference
between animals and people is that people are not just agents but
linguistic agents who, as such, do not only encounter but also think and
talk about what they encounter. For me, initially, rain is water pouring
from the sky wetting the earth. For you, it is water swelling the rivers.
When we act, are we responding to the same? If we were capable only of
encountering rain and only as wetting the earth and swelling the rivers, we
would not be able to speak to each other about rain without equivocation.
However, the fact that we are lingustic agents capable of naming what we
encounter  and thus, of talking about what we encounter, not only of
responding to it, allows us to enrich our experiential practical notions.
Thus rain becomes for us an open 'that' as object of open possible
responses and instrumental uses: that which looks the ways it looks and
wets the eart, swells rivers, prevents games, makes taking baths possible,
quenches thirst... Are we talking about the same when we talk about rain?
Yes, if two conditions obtain. If both of us are talking about rain as
object for response for people and if we understand, as linguistic agents,
each other's present practical stand in relation to the rain we are talking
about. If you are hoping for rain because of the crops and I understand you
to be referring in your talk to impending rain as preventor of games,
assuming that your project is to play or to attend the games, we are using
the name rain equivocally. This equivocation, though, will be easily
discovered and can be easily corrected. If, for instance, you tell me that
you cannot wait for the rain when I believe you are talking about the rain
as preventor of your game, I realize that you are not talking about the
rain under that set of practical expectations. Other equivocations,
however, are irremediable and incorrigible, for they reflect different
beliefs-in. For instance, you believe in abortion and I don’t. This means
that we have totally different dispositions toward it and in respect of it,
so that you would practice, and approve of, the use of abortion, and I
would not. The same with slavery. In all these cases, however, the
equivocation is less radical than in the following.
      The following is the case in which one of the interlocutors is
talking on the practical level of object for response and the other on the
purely theoretical level.
      Socrates was interested in definitions because he thought that in
order to say something true one had to know what one was talking about,
where knowing what one is talking about means knowing the essence of a
subject of predication expressible in its essential definition. For
instance, Socrates would think that I cannot speak of rain until I know
what rain is in itself, what makes rain be what it is and, therefore, what
makes it function as rain. Now, Socrates was not interested in natural
phaenomena but in human phaenomena, like piety. And he was not interested
so much in talking about piety but in being pious. But he thought that one
could not attempt to be pious without knowing what one was attempting to
be. Thus one needed to know what piety is, what makes the genuinely pious
person pious, in order to be pious.
      Now, suppose that I say that one cannot become a slave without
knowing what being a slave is, that is, without knowing what slavery--to
wit: what makes one a slave--is. In one way becoming pious and becoming a
slave are very different, for becoming pious is something that supposedly
one intentionally makes happen, whereas becoming a slave is not like that.
In another and more relevant way, however, they are similar for both are
human phenomena.
      One thing seems sure. I cannot meaningfully speak about someone being
a slave without knowing what slavery is, that is, what makes a slave be a
slave. But I can function as a slave before knowing what slavery is and
that I am a slave, as I can speak English before knowing that I speak
English. Why cannot I speak about that before and without knowing? Because
speaking about predicatively is identifying as, placing an individual in a
class. This is a purely theoretical operation in that it carries no
practical consequences. If correct, it yields nothing more than the correct
use of the word slave as an identifier, but the attribution of the
essential form to the individual does not make the individual into a slave
anymore than the identification of a certain pouring of water as rain makes
it to be rain.
      The above is very confuse. What Socrates wanted to know was not rain
as the rusticus knows it but the essence of rain, what explains rain, the
essential definition of rain. As we saw above, the rusticus knows rain
through experience of exposure that yields in a linguistic agent the
practico-experiential notion of rain. The rusticus enriches his notion of
rain through witnessing
others exposed to rain, who highlight in their exposure other observable
properties of rain. "Water pouring from the sky" is a shorthand for all the
properties of rain for people exposed to rain. It is a practical notion.
What Socrates wanted to know, that is, to understand, was the reason that
explains all the properties of rain in general, that is, all the properties
of the notion of rain as such. But the notion of rain as such, constituted
by insight into the typical image of rain, is not practical anymore. It has
been peeled off, abstracted from, all reference to desire-aversion which
are constitutive of the belief-in from which the notion originated. It is
meant to lead to a reason understood which, if found sufficient, will
terminate in a categorical judgment expressing assent or dissent to the
judged--nothing else.
      It seems to me that this passage from the practical to the
theoretical notion is at the roots of the radical equivocation I am after.
I will explore it further.
      Primarily, we do not desire to know, we desire to cope as
successfully as possible. Our accumulation of exposed and witnessing
experience, multiplied by language that allows us to enrich it with other
people's experience, is a residue of, and organized by, that fundamental
instinctive desire. This is what leads to our openended, practical notion
of rain, which allows us, with the required circumstantial adjustments, to
cope successfully. This notion, as such, has no referent. It is a shortcut
for our practical recognition of rain as object for response in any
circumstance. It is the indistinct accumulation of all our practical
expectations from rain, much as "pencil" is. A practical identifier of
particular objects-for-response and instrumental use. What Socrates did
through his desire to understand was to convert this general notion itself
into object of knowledge by asking about it the questions later identified
by Aristotle as an sit, quid sit, utrum X sit Y, and cur sit. In other
words, he objectified the notion (which being a notion is no object) by
peeling it from all reference to desire-aversion and making it into the
essential form of real objects which only differed among themselves
irrelevantly as particulars and which, being a mere abstract form or the
basis for it could not be concrete object for response.
      Concept of rain: water pouring from the sky which wets the earth...,
which swells rivers...
      This concept amounts to an abstract definition which supposedly will
lead to the explanation of all rain as such by grasping the intelligible
form of rain.
      If we apply the above to the case of God we might get some useful
enlightenment.
Aquinas's God
      Those who believed in aether as an explanatory principle of
phaenomena looked for aether and supposedly detected it. If a substance of
certain characteristics was the explanation of certain phaenomena, aether
had to exist. We may now find their quest and their supposed discoveries
ludicrous, because we think differently and, supposedly, in a more
enlightened way. However, one enters a particular field of sktudy--in
order to know--with a set of paradigmatic assumptions that predefine the
field and what is to be looked for. In Aquinas's case the paradgimatic
assumptions were Aristotelian.
      Aquinas asks what is the proper subject of theology--Christian
theology of course, for there is only one God: it is not Christ but God.
But of what God is Aquinas speaking?
      Since he is doing theology, not philosophy, he is taking his basis  on
revelation. The revealed is the place where  the  experience  of  God  takes
place, when accepted as revealed, much  as  the  experience  of  rain  takes
place in being exposed to it as someone who is thirsty,  who  has  to  cross
rivers and usex them as fluvial  ways,  who  needs  vegetables,  who  enjoys
public games. There is a disparity, though. The  revealed  of  which  Thomas
speaks is a text, not immediately an experienced in exposure;  at  best  the
Bible talks about  revelation  under  inspiration,  but  it  is  not  itself
revelation.
      In the case of rain, the experience of rain starts with an episode  of
encounter with rain, and the typical image of rain is just a  shorthand  for
the practical comprehension of possible  encounters  with  rain.  Similarly,
faith starts with the experience  of  exposure  to  God  in  an  episode  of
encounter that ends with one's falling on  one's  knees  and  acquiring  the
beginning of a comprehension that will  be  progressively  enriched  through
successive episodes and witnessing of episodes. As  one  'understands'  rain
by using it or struggling against it and witnessing others doing  the  same,
so one understands God in interacting with  Him  and  in  witnessing  others
interact with Him.
      (Clearly, rain is not revelation in the sense of  involving  faith  as
its correlate. It is not even revelation in the sense of involving  a  self-
revealing intention on the rain's part. While "Revelation" with a capital  R
is not a mere fact but God's self-revealing gesture, much as a kiss of  love
is a self-revealing gesture of loving to the beloved which, when  successful
as such, constitutes the beloved's experience of being loved.  Gestures  are
communicative deeds. As a kiss  gestures  by  doing  and  does  by  being  a
gesture, so God's self-Revelation.  And  thus  it  constitutes  an  on-going
history of reciprocation rather than a finished story).
      Now, Aquinas is an Aristotelian whose very  perception  is  shaped  by
Aristotle's epistemology  which  corresponds  to  the  latter's  scientific,
Platonizing  project.  For  the  Platonizing  Aristotle  from  whom  Aquinas
borrowed, all I have said  about  episodes  of  encounter  would  be  either
nonsensical or at least irrelevant. What he  wants,  because  otherwise  his
scientific project would be impossible or nonsensical,  is  what  will  lead
him to the essential or at least causal concept of God. He will,  therefore,
objectify "rain," he will "set the phaenomenon  rain"  through  an  abstract
typical image--abstracted  or  isolated  from  all  concrete  circumstances,
reduced to a mere descriptive definition that has no reference to  aversion-
desire. The God of Aquinas theological writings, the God  subject-matter  of
the theological science, will be of a similar kind.
      But from where will Aquinas abstract? From where,  that  is,  will  he
"set the phaenomenon God"? This brings  us  back  to  the  Bible,  perceived
again by Aquinas through his Aristotelian spectacles.
