RESEARCH PROPOSAL

Statement of Purpose

A. Purpose of the Present Investigation

The purpose of the present investigation is to show that the loss of the semantics of narrative, placed by Hans Frei in the seventeenth century, is only a late effect of a           philosophico-theological revolution that took place in the thirteenth century with the birth of Scholasticism.

B. Context of the Study

In his enormously influential book The Eclipse of Biblical Narrative , Hans Frei established that the biblical hermeneutics of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries had completely lost the means to understand the Bible as a narrative--a loss that entailed disastrous consequences within theology. He traced the roots of this loss to Spinoza and Cocceius, in the seventeenth century. My thesis is that this loss actually took place long before, in the protracted medieval controversy on the nature of theology,  and that the consequences of this loss have affected as much and as disastrously the way in which philosophy of religion is conceived and practiced today.

The medieval controversy to which I am referring raged during the twelfth and the thirteenth centuries, mostly in the Universities of Paris and Oxford. It concerned directly the scientific status of theology, but indirectly it involved the nature of faith and reason as well as the proper relationship between the two. For all practical purposes, the controversy was settled with the final adoption of Aquinas's position. It is my conviction that Aquinas's position entailed the loss of the semantics of narrative not only for biblical interpretation but also--and this is no less important--for the philosophical understanding of God. That Pascal could not recognize "the God of Abraham, Isaac and Jacob" in "the God of philosophers" is a consequence of the revolutionary shift that took place in the thirteenth century.

Review of Selected Relevant Research

As a study of the medieval controversy on the scientific status of theology, M. D. Chenu's work, La theologie comme science au XIIIe siecle (1957) remains unsurpassed. His use of Latin originals and his general knowledge of the historical development of theology make this work precious. Undoubtedly, Chenu has a bias in favor of Aquinas, but, unlike many others, he remains sensitive to the thorny problems involved in submitting the biblical narratives to the strictures of Aristotelian reason, which deals with logical demonstrations .

Erich Auerbach's classic Mimesis: The Representation of Reality in Western Literature (1953), a monumental study of the development of narrative from the Bible through the nineteenth century, brings to the fore the originality and the importance of the semantics of narrative.

Hans Frei's detailed analysis in The Eclispe of Biblical Narrative: A Study on Eighteenth and Nineteenth Century Hermeneutics (1974) furnishes me not only the guiding thread for my interpretation of what was at stake in the medieval controversy but also a concrete model of the methodology to be followed in my work.

Henri de Lubac's four volumes of Exégèse Médiévale (1959-1964) have contributed much to my understanding of the ways of theology before the medieval controversy and to my knowledge of the biblical hermeneutics from which Aquinas and his contemporaries took their distances by adopting the Aristotelian framework.

Bernard Lonergan's Verbum: Word and Idea in Aquinas (1967) explores in depth the fundamentally Aristotelian notions of judgment, knowledge, truth and science which Aquinas brought to his understanding of faith and reason.

Finally, Paul Ricoeur's three volumes work Temps et Récit

(1983-l985), and Alasdair MacIntyre's After Virtue (1981) have made more clear for me what might possibly have been lost in our philosophical understanding of God and religious belief with the loss of the semantics of narrative.  

Objectives of the Study

A. Research questions

Aquinas's actual views on revelation, faith, reason, and on the use of reason in philosophy and theology, which guided him in the medieval controversy, are well known from his writings, particularly from the Summa Theologica , from his commentaries In Sententias and In de Trinitate , and from the Summa Contra Gentiles . There is no point in replicating a research that has already been done (cf. Chenu and Lonergan). The question that has not been sufficiently considered is whether the Aristotelian framework adopted by Aquinas and many of his contemporaries is capable of accommodating  revelation and faith without radically distorting their identity. From this point of view, the interesting voices in the medieval controversy are the voices of those who, obscurely sensing the possible danger involved in the passage from the Christian meditation on the biblical narratives to the 'scientific' reflection of the summae theologicae expressed their misgivings about the change that was taking place under Aristotle's influence. Those voices have not been sufficiently heard. I intend to concentrate on them very particularly.

My research questions, then, are the following: (1) What reasons do these people offer for their opposition to the increasingly influential Aristotelian current? (2) Do their reasons, however poorly articulated, rejoin Frei's intuition about the loss of the semantics of narratives? (3) Concerning Aquinas, do his views on revelation, faith, reason, and their relationship confirm that his adoption of the Aristotelian framework actually involves a loss of the semantics of narratives? (4) If so, what consequences follow from such a loss within the field of philosophy of religion?

B. Anticipated Organization of the Manuscript

- Short summary of the central points of the medieval controversy and of the main steps toward the solution finally embraced.

- Connection of the medieval controversy with Frei's thesis on the importance of the semantics of narratives.

- Systematic study of the voices of the opposition in the controversy as they relate to the loss of the semantics of narratives.

- Study of Aquinas's position to find whether it indeed confirms the fears of the opposition.

- Sketch of the consequences this entails for the philosophical approach to religious issues.

Theoretical and/or Practical Significance

The research sketched in this proposal is a portion of a larger project that concerns the way in which religious issues are usually treated by professional philosophers and taught in courses of philosophy of religion. I think, with Pascal, that "the God of philosophers" has little or nothing to do with the God before whom people kneel down and pray, and that a different approach is needed to do justice to religious issues. The point of my research is to make that case stand.

I hope that, if this portion  of my research is successfully completed, I will be able to obtain support from outside agencies for the rest.

Name: X. O. Monasterio

Title: A Medieval Controversy and Its Outcome for Philosophy

Objectives: (1) To show that the loss of the semantics of narrative, dated by Hans Frei in the seventeenth century, actually took place in the thirteenth century. (2) To show that the results of that loss for philosophy were no less disastrous than the results that, according to Frei, it had for theology.

Methodology: A careful study of the pertinent passages of the original writings of the main participants in the medieval controversy, with special attention to those who opposed Aquinas's position. The reason for this is that, if my thesis is correct, Aquinas's position, which in time became universally accepted, involved the loss of the semantics of narrative, with disastrous consequences for the philosophical understanding of God, whereas those who opposed Aquinas had at least a glimpse of those consequences.

Significance: If my thesis is correct, we will be able to understand why people can hardly recognize in the God of whom philosophers speak--be it to affirm or to deny his existence--the God in whom they believe. Furthermore, having realized the fundamental mistake of the current philosophical approach to religious issues, we might be able to redress the situation.
