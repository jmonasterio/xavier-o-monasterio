THOMAS  KUHN’S REAL CONTRIBUTION

(Cf. my <2001-April-1-, revised on March 2002, particularly pages 7-9>, of which the present paper is a further development).

Thomas Kuhn was a physicist turned historian of science, not a linguist. He himself thought that his main contribution concerned physics and the natural sciences in the first place. Yet his views, with rare exceptions, have hardly influenced physicists and natural scientists; by contrast, they have proven influential among a number of linguists and philosophers. The reasons for this oddity are multiple, and some of them will emerge incidentally in the course of this article. But my subject here is whether Kuhn left a valuable legacy and, if so, what was it.

One of the reasons for the lack of popularity of Kuhn’s views among physicists and, in general, the ’hard scientists’ is that they perceive him as attacking the validity of scientific knowledge, at least by implication. Indeed, such central Kuhnian notions as the  paradigmatic character  of “normal science” and of the development of science through paradigmatic revolutions seem to entail the conclusion that there is no progress in scientific knowledge, just a succession of prevailing scientific views. In other words, Einsteinian physics, for  instance, doesn’t constitute a superior physics to Newtonian physics – superior, that is, in terms of actual knowledge of the universe– but only a different physics. A conclusion that Kuhn himself, for all his protestations that he is not a relativist, seems to endorse, for he writes : “We may ... have to relinquish the notion, implicit or explicit, that changes of paradigm carry scientists and those who learn from them closer and closer to truth” (p. 170) .  

What then? Are we to agree with Kuhn’s critics that his legacy

is merely one of relativism and skepticism concerning the  hard sciences and, therefore, that the earlier his views are forgotten the better?

For my part, I don’t believe so at all. I think, however, that some serious reflection is needed to  rescue his genuine and truly valuable contribution from the hands of both his critics and of Kuhn’s own hands. That is what I propose to do here.

The textbook view of science

In the very first page of the introduction to The Structure of Scientific Revolutions Kuhn attacks “the image of science by which we are now possessed,” and specifies his own purpose:

“that image has previously been drawn... mainly from the study of finished scientific achievements as these are recorded in the classics and, more recently, in the textbooks from which each new scientific generation learns to practice its trade. Inevitably, however, the aim of such books is persuasive and pedagogic; a concept of science drawn from them is no more likely to fit the enterprise that produced them than an image of a national culture drawn from a tourist brochure or a language text. This essay attempts to show that we have been misled by them in fundamental ways. Its aim is a sketch of the quite different concept of science that can emerge from the historical record of the research activity itself” ^(F1)  

The view of science presented by the textbooks that teach beginners their scientific trade  focuses on the chain of the most salient achievements of the scientific activity in terms of observations, measurements,  laws and theories, as well as experiments, together with the names of the individual scientists to whom each of those contributions is to be attributed. That is usually the content of the introductory chapter of scientific textbooks. In the successive chapters, the scientific method is illustrated by the experimental techniques used in gathering the data pertinent to the particular subject-mater of the textbook, together with the logical operations involved in relating those data to the theoretical generalizations found in the textbook. In addition, across the textbook, a series of concrete problems is furnished, whose solution by the student will serve as experimental confirmation of the validity of the laws involved, and will contribute to increase the student’s facility in applying the laws.

Subjacent to this textbook view is the notion that science grows by cumulative accretion of independent elements, much as a building grows by horizontal  accretion of different bricks and of successive vertical layers of bricks. To explain the image a bit more: each truly innovative scientist contributes a brick or two to the edifice of scientific knowledge, and each generation of innovative scientists contributes another layer of observed facts and laws and theories. And so on.

As is too well known to need elaboration, Kuhn’s idea of the development of science is radically different. “Normal science” appears after a pre-paradigmatic primitive period in which there is no fundamental agreement among the practitioners; each scientist moves on his or her own, according to his or her own best lights. Such continues to be the case until a “paradigm” emerges that gains general acceptance. Normal science is paradigmatic.  More precisely, it is a problem-solving activity characterized by a shared view of the nature of the entities that constitute the particular field and, correspondingly, by a shared manner of solving the problems typical of the field. The shared view of the nature of the entities that constitute the field, though, is open-ended and confronts irregularities that threaten the prevailing view. However, rather than renouncing the shared view because of the irregularities, the scientists who share the paradigm live with those irregularities and endeavor to accommodate them within the paradigm. Some of those irregularities prove, in the long run, to be merely apparent, when a solution to accommodate them within the prevailing paradigm is found. However, some other of the  irregularities that threaten the paradigm because of their incompatibility with it may in the long run provoke a crisis. The crisis may lead to the abandonment of the current paradigm when an alternative view of the nature of the entities that constitute the field and, correspondingly, a new manner of solving the problems typical of the field emerges and rallies new adepts among the practicing scientists. Such an event, that is, the shift of paradigms, constitutes what Kuhn calls a  “revolution.”

Herein lies the explosiveness of Kuhn’s position . According to the classical view of science, represented by the textbook image, science genuinely grows . Indeed, the truths discovered  by each generation of innovative scientists are preserved, and the next generation adds to the previously known scientific truths. As a result, there is genuine scientific growth in scientific truth; science is asymptotically moving toward total possession of the truth. Not so, in Kuhn’s opinion. Within a given paradigm, there is  growth, indeed, in that the problem-solving activity progresses further and further. ^(F2) When, however, the irregularities confronted within the paradigm become unbearable to the practitioners a crisis is reached which sooner or later results on is a ”revolution,” when  paradigm emerges. A revolution entails a new scientific beginning – a new science, literally , defined by the new paradigm. What was previously taken for genuine knowledge ceases to be accepted. Similarly, what is presently taken for genuine knowledge under the currently prevailing paradigm will in its turn cease to be accepted when the present paradigm is eventually substituted by a new one. That, in Kuhn’s opinion, is what happened in the field of physics when the Copernican paradigm was substituted by the Newtonian, and again when the Newtonian paradigm was substituted by the Einsteinian. In other words,  Copernican, Newtonian, and Einsteinian physics are three entirely different physics in which the putative ‘truths’ of the previous physics are replaced by new and different, but no less putative, ‘truths,’ since nothing guarantees that the currently prevailing paradigm will not be superseded in its turn  Hence the optimistic idea that science is asymptotically moving toward the total truth is dead. In Kuhn’s softer, perhaps less offensive, but no less devastating words to the same effect,

“We may ... have to relinquish the notion, implicit or explicit, that changes of paradigm carry scientists and those who learn from them closer and closer to truth” (p. 170).

Science as a problem-solving activity

I have already said that, in my estimation, Kuhn’s most important contribution is the image of science he proposes as an alternative to what he calls the textbook image “by which we are now possessed.” Now, the Kuhnian image of science involves the paradigmatic character of normal science, by contrast to pre-paradigmatic or primitive science, as well as the periodic shifts of paradigms, with the threatening implication concerning the development of science through revolutionary paradigmatic changes. But these characteristics of the Kuhnian view of science are only consequences that flow from his real contribution that, in my opinion, consists in the shift from the traditional image of science as a knowledge-accumulating activity to that of a  problem-solving activity .

Let us begin with the contrast between the two notions or images of science as sketched by Kuhn himself. In the following paragraph he explains that the traditional view of science conveyed by the textbooks conceives of  knowledge as accurate representation of reality; consequently, he identifies the traditional view of science reflected in the scientific textbooks as a knowledge-acquiring activity, that is, as an activity in which there is progress in  the accurate representation of reality. For his part, Kuhn denies that the scientific activity yields knowledge in the sense of an accurate representation of the world; what the scientific activity yields, when successful, is rather problem-solving ability within its proper field:  

“A scientific theory is usually felt better than its predecessors... because it is somehow REPRESENTATION OF WHAT NATURE IS REALLY LIKE. One often hears that successive theories grow closer to, or approximate more closely to the truth. Apparently generalizations like that refer not to the puzzle-solutions and concrete predictions derived from a theory but rather to its ontology, to the match, that is, between the entities with which the theory populates nature and what is ’really’ there. Perhaps there is some other way of saving the notion of ‘truth’ for application to whole theories, but this one will not do. There is, I think, no theory-independent way to reconstruct phrases like ‘really there’; the notion of a match between the ontology of a theory and its ‘real’ counterpart in nature now seems to me illusive in principle. Besides, as a historian, I am impressed by the implausibility of the view. I do not doubt, for example, that Newton mechanics improves on Aristotle’s and that Einstein’s improves on Newton’s as instruments for puzzle-solving. But I can see in their succession no coherent direction of ontological development. On the contrary, in some important respects, though by no means in all, Einstein’s general theory of  relativity is closer to Aristotle’s than to Newton’s. Though the temptation to describe that position as relativistic is understandable, the description seems to me wrong.” ^(F3)

More succinctly, in another place, Kuhn repeats:

“We may ... have to relinquish the notion, implicit or explicit, that changes of paradigm carry scientists and those who learn from them closer and closer to truth” (p. 170) .

In rejecting the notion of knowledge as correspondence of the mind with what is the case in reality Kuhn is fighting against a long and deeply ingrained epistemic tradition in which we all have been steeped. Indeed, the commonsensical notion, based on a superficial reflection on visual perception, that knowledge consists in the match or exact correspondence between what the knower sees or  knows and what is actually  the case in reality, received its philosophical consecration from Plato and has been around in different guises till our days. In fact, as we just saw, Kuhn finds that conception of knowledge still involved in the currently prevailing view of science perpetuated by the scientific textbooks.

Now, Kuhn is not, by any means, the first to have found defective the conception of knowledge as correspondence with what is actually the case in reality. Kant had already rejected that conception on the basis that since we have no other access to reality than the knowledge we have of it, we cannot possibly know whether or not what we know actually corresponds to “the thing in itself.” Kant struggled valiantly to offer an  alternative conception of knowledge. However, subsequent history shows that he failed, for his epistemic heritage, far from having furnished us a better way to understand knowledge has been rather one of  idealism and, ultimately, skepticism. Now, didn’t Kuhn fall into the same trap as Kant in his similar attempt? After all, the objections raised to Kuhn’s views are practically the same as those voiced by his critics.

Let us back track, As I have  already said, Kuhn’s alternative consists in the shift a view of science as a knowledge a-accumulating activity – where knowledge is understood precisely as correspondence of

what is known with what is really out there– to a view of science as a problem-solving activity. To render this intelligible I’ll use a concrete illustration taken from Euclidean geometry.

Colonized as we are by the prevailing view of knowledge,

we spontaneously tend to think that the statements of each of the theorems are supposed to function as correct representations of what is the case. For instance, the affirmation that the sum or addition of the angles of a triangle amounts to a square angle is usually mistaken for a piece of knowledge that expresses or represents correctly what is the case with the angles of triangles. Not so, Kuhn would argue. The stated theorem is not a representation of a reality, nor is it meant to function as a representation. It is, rather, a rule for action in the solution of certain problems in which the rule applies.

sees actually corresponds to what is the case? After all, everyone has had the experience of having been wrong after being convinced of being right. In other words, the notion of knowledge as correspondence to what is the case, coupled with the obvious fact that putative knowledge proves some times to be false, that is, not to be genuine knowledge, reated for Plato the problem of discerning between merely apparent and genuine knowledge. To distinguish the two, at least in name, Plato called the former “opinion” and the latter “knowledge,” and he tackled the problem of discerning the two in the following way. Only those realities can be object of “knowledge” which are absolutely unchangeable or permanent because they exist necessarily and are necessarily as they are. For if the knower grasps such necessity, then the knower grasps that what he or she knows,. necessarily corresponds to what is the case. Now, no sensible or perceivable object is of that kind, for all of them are contingent rather than necessary, that is, they are subject to change in the flux of time. Thus my opinions about them, even assuming that my perceptions actually corresponded at a certain instant to what is the case, ceases to be true as their object changes. Thus Plato felt constrained to relegate all the particular objects of sensible perception to the realm of opinion or merely putative knowledge. Furthermore, he determined that only the intellect (as opposed to the senses), which was capable of grasping what is necessarily the case, could yield knowledge (as opposed to opinion). Hence, Plato concluded, only concerning the intelligible realities, which being purely intelligible are universal rather than particular like the sensible realities and consequently are not subject to temporal change is genuine, rhar is, necessarily true knowledge possible.

As to the avatars of the notion of knowledge as correspondence  between the mind and what is actually the case in reality, as well as the intense scrutiny and criticism to which it has been subjected for centuries, the story has been told a hundred times. For my purposes, it may suffice to say that Kuhn is not alone, by far, in finding such notion defective. It is fair to say that Kant, who also found himself constrained to renounce to the notion of knowledge as correspondence with the real on the basis that, since we have no independent means of access to the real other than knowledge itself, we cannot possibly find out whether or not what we take for knowledge actually corresponds to “the thing itself,” is,  in this particular regard, Kuhn’s direct predecessor. Whether, however, Kant proposed a viable alternative to the notion of knowledge as correspondence, or simply left us in the abyss of idealistic skepticism, is not my question. Whatever the case may be with Kant,  my question concerns  Kuhn. Does he, or doesn’t  he furnish us with a viable alternative to the traditional notion of knowledge as a reliable representation of reality?

Practical knowledge is not representational: Ortho-doxy and ortho-praxis

Do animals know? To Plato, for whom genuine knowledge was a deed of the intellect, and who had an acute sense of the radical difference between animals and people, such question would have sounded trivial and uninteresting, if not plainly wrongheaded. Not so to Aristotle, who in time came to distinguish practical knowledge from the theoretical knowledge which Plato had explored. Aristotle came as well  to see some kind of continuity between animals and people. The interest of Aristotle’s exploration of the issue whether animals know is that, in the first place, it seems to open the way to an  alternative to the traditional conception of knowledge as faithful representation or copy of the real, a variety of which notion of knowledge Aristotle ended up adopting as his own, Secondly, in that  Kuhn, for his part, was  obscurely groping for an alternative conception of knowledge in order to give full shape to his understanding of science,  it is of interest to us to find out

whether either of them succeeded in their ambition.

As already said, unlike Plato, who had confused the two, Aristotle distinguishes practical from theoretical knowledge. In the latter the direct  connection with action that characterizes the former is not found, nor is found  the concern with the usefulness of the knowledge acquired or to be acquired. Indeed, theoretical knowledge is disinterested, for it has no other purpose than the grasp of the truth for its own sake. Now, to return the question whether animals know, Aristotle’s answer, is nuanced: animals are incapable of theoretical knowledge; however, they pursue, and are capable of attaining the kind of knowledge that is useful for the preservation and well-being of the individual or of the species to which the animal belongs. People, by contrast, are capable of both theoretical and practical knowledge.

Now, though the above analysis indicates that theoretical and practical knowledge are significantly different from each other,

Aristotle realized that they have in common one thing, to wit, that both of them are  knowledge. And what is knowledge? In Aristotle’s view –a view that was to exert a tremendous influence in the Western world for centuries to this very day– knowledge is the correspondence between what the mind knows and the extramental reality known. As to the precise species of correspondence involved, that complex issue would be endlessly discussed in the future.

To take a particular instance, on the basis of their respective past experience, both lions and people may come to grasp the useful ‘universal’ fact that, say, eating  a plant of such a kind has beneficial purgative effects. However, there is a difference between animals and people: lions only know that fact practically, that is, they will eat that plant in preference to other plants under the appropriate circumstances, whereas people know it both practically and also theoretically –that is, predicatively, objectively, as a truth concerning that type of plant, and can move from there to the reason propter quid, that is, why such a plant to the difference of others produces purgative effects.

        

Such seems to be Aristotle’s conception of practical and theoretical knowledge in the particular version to which we are presently referring. Indeed, he emphasizes that practical knowledge is knowledge of the universal, that is, of the general fact that a plant of such a kind possesses purgative virtues. In other words, both people and animals (in this case, lions) reach  what the scholastics called ‘knowledge quia , or knowledge of the plant as cause of purgative effects. The difference between them is that, unlike th animal,  the human is capable of reaching knowledge propter quid , that is explanatory knowledge, knowledge of the nature of the plant thanks to which the plant produces such purgative effect.

There is, however, another possible version of practical knowledge. Think, in the first place, of the lion’s case. The lion can attain the knowledge of the purgative effects of the herb only through its experience of undergoing itself those beneficial effects of eating the plant. This is no purely notional knowledge, such as the knowledge a human who has no previous experience of purgation may acquire by being told that the plant produces purgative effects. The learning to which I am now referring is experiential in that it is gained through experience . It is a matter not only of knowing about effects through observation, but of effects felt by the agent as beneficial as a result of the agent’s involvement in action. Because the purgative effects are experienced as favorable by the agent who experiences them, the action of eating the plant will automatically tend to be repeated under the appropriate circumstances in the expectation of similar effects. Actions followed by rewarding effects tend to be repeated; actions followed by unpleasant effects tend to be avoided. And from objects that look similar similar effects are expected. These are inherited mechanisms of living, sensitive, desirous bodies. And these mechanisms are at the origin of all further purposive action initiated by such bodies, as well as of all future learning about the world as our abode.  

Suppose that the lion eats the plant and nothing happens to the animal, that is, no beneficial or ill effects are undergone and felt by the animal. In that case, there will be no connection between the plant and the animal’s future action. (The case is similar to that of lepers who have to observe the cigarette they are holding in their hands, because their hands are insensitive). Hence no practical knowledge will be acquired, that is, no adaptive change in response to a kind of objects, no eating of that kind of plant in preference to other plants.

It should be noticed that the effects can be experienced as beneficial or malefic by the animal only if they are undergone and felt by the animal itself. (People, thanks to language, may fear something whose effects they have never experienced, by analogy to the ill-effects produced by other objects of which they have experience).  Again, it is a matter of experiential knowledge, of knowledge gained from first-hand experience, so to say. But what, precisely, is the nature of this knowledge? Does the animal possess a kind of mental representation of the plant’s nature? No. The animal doesn’t predicatively or objectively know-that all the plants of that kind are purgative. The animal just has learned to desire and look for a similar plant in certain circumstances, and eat it, when purgation happens to be needed.  There is in the animal a sensory-motor memory of the plant as desirable or to be eaten for purgation. Whenever purgation is needed in the future, the animal will automatically look for that kind of plant. This is purely practical recognition of the plant for purposes of action, guided by acquired desire or inclination as a result of the experience of beneficial effects. The animal has no theoretical or predicative idea-that this is a kind of plant that uniformly produces purgative effects in such a kind of animals. (The idea-that presupposes language and is the result of reflection; so it is exclusively found in people. Furthermore, even in the case of  people, it is not specular or speculative but practical; a map for action, depending on the agent’s project).

Now, though the recognition of the plant as purgative is purely practical and certainly not verbalized by the animal, Aristotle saw it as somewhat akin to the theoretical or contemplative knowledge of the ‘nature’ of things to which Plato had attributed representational value. In other words, the gravitational pull of Aristotle’s Platonic heritage weighed heavily on Aristotle’s view of practical knowledge. He doesn’t seem to ever have suspected the viability of the inverse alternative, to wit: that practical knowledge, which is not representational or predicative but directly results in action, not even in inchoative theoretical predication, because it is experience of object for response in terms of its effects –be they undergone or observed-- , is in fact the PRINCEPS ANALOGATUM in epistemic matters.  

Ultimately, this reversal has the startling consequence not only that theoretical knowledge has no primacy over practical knowledge, as the Greeks assumed, but that there is no such a thing as purely theoretical, representational knowledge –not even in the area of science. Or, to be more precise, that what sounds as objective predication is in fact the result of action and meant to function in the agent applicatively rather than predicatively.

Aristotle, then, ‘platonized’ badly concerning practical knowledge. Yet his own observation that those who do not know (because they do not possess any theoretical knowledge of the universal) but who possess experience of those meats that produce wholesome effects, (meats which because of that they call  light, that is, meats that produce wholesome effects, by contrast to other meats) are capable of curing, whereas those who know (because they posses theoretical knowledge of the universal) but who possess no experience of light meats that produce wholesome effects are incapable of curing should have alerted Aristotle to the fact that the theoretical component of practical knowledge (to wit, that this plant cures through purgation, or that light meats are wholesome) is superfluous because it has no truth-value or representational value at all,  in that nothing in reality corresponds to the universal the theoretician has in mind . Indeed, ‘light meats’ is a name that has no other referent than the objects from whom the experts expect wholesome effects.  Such realization, however, would have been impossible to someone like Aristotle who had been steeped in, and wholeheartedly embraced as self-evident and indisputable, the conception of knowledge as adequation to reality, as representational of reality. Of that epistemic myth Aristotle never quite managed to get rid. And it is precisely of that conception of knowledge that Kuhn is struggling to liberate both himself and us in the area of the experimental sciences.

I said that nothing corresponds to the universal which the theoretician has in mind. In other words, the judgment, expressed in the statement, that all light meats are wholesome neither possesses nor is meant to possess any truth value, any resemblance or pictorial homophormism to what is the case. Such judgment is not objective, in that it grasps no object; no reality-fact corresponds to it. In other words, ‘light meats’ are those meats which the ‘experts’ or experienced people recognize as light meats in terms of their expectation and desire of wholesome, that is, healthy, beneficial effects. And this recognition cannot be formalized in a theorem. It is not only that the Platonic eternal Ideas required to save the objectivity of universal necessary judgments do not exist, as Aristotle rightly argued, but neither do exist the necessary unchangeable “natures” that Aristotle inferred from the perceptual observation of certain constant natural phenomena,  with which he replaced the Platonic Ideas and attempted to preserve the Platonic notion of scientific knowledge, of episteme, of necessarily true knowledge, that has been perpetuated in the modern notion of the superior certainty of the sciences guaranteed by experiment. In short, I am saying that the Humean criticism of the validity of scientific induction is legitimate, that the constancy of nature in certain areas is merely contingent, that our inductions about the world rest on the automatic, non-rational expectation (because not grounded in the grasp of any necessity) that the ‘same’ ‘effects’ will be produced in the future by the ’same’ ‘causes.’

BUT I AM SAYING MORE THAN THIS, TO WIT: THAT THE EXPECTATION IS NOT PRIMARILY OF VISUALLY SIMILAR EFFECTS, FOR THE EFFECTS ARE NOT OBSERVED ; THEY ARE UNDERGONE AND FELT, BUT THEY ARE NEITHER OBSERVED NOR EVEN OBSERVABLE. ANYMORE THAN PLEASURE OR PAIN ARE OBSERVABLE OR OBSERVED EVEN BY THE PERSON WHO FEELS THEM, FOR THEY HAVE NO EXTENSION.

 Hence the ‘mysterious’ or unformalizable character of the ‘knowledge

possessed by the agent, which can be acquired only through practice until it becomes habitual. In other words, the knowledge is indexical: personal appropriation manifested in the habitual disposition to respond to, or treat, something in the appropriate way to obtain the desired effects. Chess playing is a good example. The expert chess player knows the ‘enemy,’ that is, knows how to respond to the other pieces in any given situation, and this is a ‘knowledge’ that can be gained only through practice, but the chess player cannot express what he knows otherwise than by actually responding   or by saying how the enemy is to be responded to in a given situation. This is problem-solving .

Another apt example is the way in which children learn their first language and become ‘native’ in it. They learn it by imitative doing of what other people say, a linguistic doing that is rewarded with the desired effects. They know the grammar practically, but not theoretically. They know grammar only in that they are capable of producing instrumentally correct sentences to obtain their aims . They know the grammar only habitually; they possess it as an instrumental habit, as one comes to possess one’s own body. Not only do they not know objectively the grammar, that is, the grammatical rules of their native language, but they don’t need to know them predicatively, for knowledge is practical-instrumental, after the model of the habitual knowledge of one’s own body. Imagine a person who knew perfectly the grammar of his or her native language by having learned it as a series of  rules for speaking or writing, but who is incapable of generating any appropriate sentence to achieve his or her linguistic aims. This person would not know his or her language instrumentally, which is the only appropriate way of knowing a language –as a problem-solving device that one masters as one masters one’s own body.

HOWEVER –and this, indeed, is a BIG however– here enters the intuition involved in the notion of practical knowledge that prevents the free-fall into sheer relativism and unbounded skepticism, for, as acknowledged by Kuhn, so very few ‘ways of seeing’ are successful. Practical knowledge is not knowledge in the sense of pictorial contemplative representation of the real out there. Yet it is practical in the sense of being an apt guide for action. We should therefore treat it as such rather than insisting in exploring, and futilely attempting to justify, a representational value that our judgments and statements about reality do not possess at all because they are not pictures.*

* Notice, however, that there may be such a thing as intra-paradigmatic truth. Once a ‘way of seeing’ has proven successful, the predicative statements generated according to the grammar of the way of seeing will be deemed true by the members of the paradigm. This all-important point about practical knowledge can  best be explained by reference to Kuhn’s notion of science as a problem-solving activity, by contrast to the traditional, “textbook’ view of science as a truth-gaining or representational knowledge-gaining activity.

When I deny that there is such a thing as theoretical knowledge, I am not denying by implication that science is valuable in any way; I am just denying –together with Kuhn– that science is valuable in terms of giving us the right –or even just a better, more accurate– pictorial representation of the world. For the fact is that science does not give us any picture or image.  But, then, what does science give us? As in the case of inverting lenses, it gives the agents, depending on their situation at the moment of application, the optimal view for moving around within the perceived space, depending on their particular project. But the view is practical, hodological, indexical. And it is the result of acting, not of seeing. Actually, more than a particular view of a particular object, it is  an assimilated, habitual ability to move around in order to achieve our goals, similar to the motor ability that a recently blinded person loses and that in the long run partially reacquires as he or she moves around, first in a familiar room and later in unfamiliar space, aided by subliminal auditive clues. This is the ability that a person wearing inverting lenses loses and, after a certain time of practice of moving around, suddenly reacquires.

What do I mean by saying that it is not a particular view but a habitual ability? Think of a person who is quite familiar with a section of a city. At any time, that person ‘knows’ how to go from any point to any other point within that city. Moreover, if asked, he or she usually can draw a map, or at least give verbal directions to somebody else. We tend to imagine that such a person has a map of that section in his or her head. But that is not the case. A primitive person may not be able to draw a map of a region with which he or she is quite familiar, but is capable of reaching unerringly any place. He or she may not be able to give verbal directions, but is capable of guiding others unerringly from any place to the place they want to reach. And to reach that place he or she doesn’t have to give himself or herself directions, as he or she would give to other people. This person ’just knows.’ He or she possesses a practical spatial motor scheme of the region, constantly modified according to his or her present position , (which does not consist in any particular view of the region), assimilated in his or her body thanks to the experience of moving in the region. We can say that his or her body knows. This motor scheme is what I described above as a habitual ability to move around in a certain region to achieve our goals.

The acquisition of this motor scheme or motor program may be initially facilitated by the use of maps drawn by other experts in the region, but it is not equivalent to using a map. Only the expert in a region can draw a map of the region, but the expert did not gain his or her expertise by means of a map. Most certainly, the first expert in a region preceded the first map, and after possessing practical knowledge of the region he drew the first map, not the other way around. In other words, maps are the result of appropriate action, but appropriate action is not –at least not necessarily– the result of maps.

Keeping in mind these suggestive comparisons, let us move now to the area of the experimental sciences, and of physics in particular, which is the area of which Kuhn is talking. A great discoverer is the creator of a paradigm, or the one who extends it to the solution of other problems previously not dealt with by those scientists who work within the paradigm. A paradigm is comparable to the habitual motor scheme just mentioned. Kuhn speaks of it as a shared way of seeing, but he realizes that the ‘seeing’ is metaphorical. It is a practical construction or organization of the scientific field –unspecifiable in words because it is not objective knowledge– that renders possible the scientific doing, the actual problem-solving. ^(F4) As all habits, it is acquired or internalized through practice until its appropriate exercise becomes automatic. The sketch of the law, to use Kuhn’s expression, is comparable to a map. The problem-solving is comparable to the use of the map by actually going to desired places. Very appropriately, Kuhn compares the acquisition of a paradigm to the acquisition of a second language in which one ends up becoming ‘native’ thanks to much practice.

Now there is a great difference between the geniuses who inaugurate or significantly extend a paradigm and those who appropriate the paradigm by being taught by those who already know. The difference is comparable to that between a person who knows a region through practical familiarity with it, so that he or she can reach any particular point from any particular point without the help of a map (which is comparable to original solving- problem in science), and a person who reaches one particular point thanks to the directions received from someone who knows the region. The latter, following the directions, can reach only one particular place and from one particular place, whereas the former can reach any place, even places where he or she has never actually been. The latter, by contrast, has not yet learned ‘how to solve problems,’ because he or she has not appropriated the paradigm, yet.

The process through which we people come to know a region so as to be able to go from any place to any other place and to give directions to others is to an extent mysterious, at least in that the actual procedure cannot be verbally specified. All we can say is that ”it is a matter of practice, or of experience” gained through the repeated practice of moving around in the region. In a comparable way, Kuhn says that the student learns to do science through the practice of solving the problems typical of the particular scientific field until one becomes ‘native’ in the field, as one becomes ‘native’ in a second language.

But how does this view of science as a problem-solving activity differ from the view of science as a knowledge-gaining activity? And from where does the latter view arise?

One thing that Kuhn suggests is that the textbook view of science, which he deems wrongheaded, focuses on already acquired scientific results rather than on the process through which they are gained. Thus, for instance, the discovery of oxygen will be attributed to a particular scientist, or the discovery of the law of falling bodies on inclines will be attributed to Galileo. Moreover, what is discovered is considered as a piece of knowledge, the accumulation of which pieces gives us an ever more complete and accurate view of the universe. Thus knowledge is understood as the view that reflects or expresses accurately what is t he case in reality. (See p. 4 above, quotation that corresponds to note 4).

For his part, Kuhn rejects the idea that science yields an accurate, or at least an ever more accurate and complete view of the world. The reason is that he rejects the notion of knowledge as copy-correspondence to what is the case, and, consequently, the idea that science yields knowledge in the sense of a correct, or increasingly more accurate, image of the world.

To grasp the difference between these two notions of science one only needs to realize the very different role attributed to the problem-solving activity in each of the views. ^(F5) According to the textbook view, what is gained from solving problems is

“only practice in the application of what the student already knows. He cannot, it is said, solve problems unless he has first learned the theory and some rule for applying it. Scientific knowledge is embedded in theory and rules; problems are supplied to gain facility in their application. I have tried to argue, however, that this localization of the cognitive content of science is wrong. After the student has done many problems, he may gain only added facility by solving more. But at the start and for some time after, doing problems is learning consequential things about nature. In the absence of such exemplars , the laws and theories he has previously learned would have little empirical content... A phenomenon familiar to both students of science and historians of science provides a clue. The former regularly report that they have read through a chapter of a text and understood it perfectly, but nonetheless had difficulty solving a number of the problems at the chapter’s end. Ordinarily also those difficulties dissolve in the same way. The student discovers... a way to see this problem as like a problem he has already encountered. Having seen the resemblance, grasped the analogy between two or more distinct problems, he can interrelate symbols and attach them to nature in the ways proved effective before. The law sketch say f=ma , has functioned as a tool, informing the student what similarities to look for, signaling the gestalt in which the situation is to be seen . The resultant ability too see a variety of situations as like each other, as subjects for f=ma or some other symbolic generalization, is, I think, the main thing a student acquires by doing problems, whether with a pencil or in a well designed laboratory. After he has completed a certain number..., he views the situations that confront him as a scientist in the same gestalt as other members of his specialists’s group. For him they are no longer the same situations he had encountered when his training began. He has meanwhile assimilated a time-tested and group-licensed way of seeing” (pp. 187-190).

According to the textbook view, the formulation of the law at stake is what is to be learned by the student, for it supposedly constitutes the piece of scientific knowledge contributed by the genius who discovered it. The textbook, therefore, will focus on the law or the theorem. It will state and explain the law, and describe the experiment or experiments that demonstrated that truth to the genius. Of course, since that truth is about the world, it will find application in the world. At the time of application, however, what the student will gain by solving problems is not knowledge, for supposedly he or she already possesses it in knowing and understanding the law, he will gain mere facility in applying the already acquired knowledge to concrete problems. According to Kuhn, however, “this localization of the cognitive content of science is wrong.” What exactly is wrong?

In the textbook view, the focus is on the law or the geometrical theorem because it is a piece of knowledge, that is, it corresponds to, or represents faithfully, what is the case in reality. Well, Kuhn argues, if this localization  were right, the student who reads the pertinent chapter of the textbook and understands the law –and even is able to prove it experimentally in the laboratory– already possesses the essential, which is the piece of knowledge, the result achieved or discovered by the genius. In this view, all that this student lacks is facility in applying it, which he will gain by solving concrete problems. Suppose, however, that a student who understands the law is incapable of  applying it, that is, is incapable of solving any concrete problem to which the law applies. This student will be flunked as a scientist, of course. But why, if indeed he possesses the piece of knowledge expressed by the law, and is even able to demonstrate it experimentally?

Imagine a parallel case. A student understands a particular theorem of Euclidean geometry and shows his understanding by demonstrating the theorem, but is quite incapable of showing his understanding by solving concrete problems in which the theorem applies. Again, this student will be flunked as a scientist, and rightly so. But why? Because, Kuhn answers, the localization of the cognitive content of science in the textbook view of science is wrong . If it were right, this student would be a good geometer, for by understanding the theorem he has appropriated the piece of knowledge, and by demonstrating the theorem on paper he has shown that he actually understands what the theorem expresses. But then, why is this student to be flunked, if the law or the theorem can be understood independently of the application in problem-solving, which supposedly adds to the  knowledge only facility in applying it–a facility which is not itself constitutive of the knowledge?

How, then, does Kuhn, by contrast to what the textbooks suggest, localize the cognitive content of science?  The law sketch, that is the statement in which the law or the theorem is expressed, answers Kuhn, does not function as a truth that reflects or reproduces what is the case in reality –which is the notion of knowledge presupposed by the textbook view of science. It, rather, “functions as a tool ... signaling the gestalt in which the situation is to be seen.”   Unlike a picture or an image or a representation, a tool does not function as a tool by being looked at, for a tool is not a reproduction of anything in the world; a tool functions by being actually used in the execution of the task at hand. It is, then,  by actually applying the law or the theorem to the task, that is, by actually solving  problems that arise in the type of situation in which the law applies, that the student, in the long run, acquires scientific mastery. And this mastery consists, not in contemplating the formulation or statement of the law, but in the habitual ability to apply the law or, in Kuhn’s expression, to  ‘see’ the situation in the gestalt in which it is to be ’seen’ in order to solve the type of practical problems in which the law finds application. And that is the gestalt in which such situation is ‘seen’ by the other members of the group which inhabit the same scientific paradigm.    

Of course, the ‘seeing’ here involved is not to be taken literally but metaphorically. It is not a contemplative seeing, but a seeing connected with action, a practical seeing, a seeing that renders possible the appropriate action in response to what is seen. To use a comparison, a good car driver is the person who, thanks to much exercise or experience in driving, has acquired the habit of ‘seeing’ everything, when driving, in the appropriate gestalt, that is, in the way appropriate to respond as a driver to what is seen. Thus, when this experienced or expert driver sees a curve ahead, she instinctively knows the appropriate speed to take it; when she sees an object barring the road, she instinctively knows when to start braking, and how hard. And so on. This habitual facility to see things when driving in the appropriate way to respond to them is acquired only through much practice in driving.  The point of the comparison is that, according to Kuhn, in each scientific field,  too, there is a  gestalt in which the situation is to be seen by the scientist, that this habitual way of seeing which shows in the problem-solving practice of the good scientists is what constitutes a particular paradigm, and that it is acquired or internalized by the student through the practice of solving problems.  

To explore a little further the difference between the textbook view of science and Kuhn’s own we may refer ourselves to the not totally disconnected area of ordinary language, in particular to the case of statements. ^(F6) Here, too, two views of the way in which statements function are possible. According to one view –and this is the one analogous to the textbook view of science– a statement reproduces or mirrors or pictures the real state of the world to which it refers. Thus, for instance, if I tell you, “Your sister is on the phone,” I am transmitting you verbally a picture of a real state of the world, and my statement  is meant to be understood by you, the addressee, as a picture to be contemplated, for that is how pictures are to be responded to. Thus, to peoceed with my example, if you ‘get’ the picture that your sister is on the phone on the other side of the line,  you understood what I meant, for that is the representation of reality that I meant to transmit to you by making the statement. And, in general, if in the case of any statement in English you ‘get’ the picture, you are a competent speaker of English, for you respond to statements as verbal representations of what is the case. According, however, to a different view of linguistic statements –and this is the one analogous to Kuhn’s view of science–  statements are not verbal pictures or representations of states of the world. Consequently, statements are not meant to function as pictures, and if they are understood as pictures they are simply misunderstood. For they are meant to function as rules or instruments for action, as an invitation to take the phone and talk to your sister.

To return to our example, suppose that in response to my statement, “Your sister is on the phone,”  you tell me, “All right,” but continue doing whatever you are doing instead of taking the phone or explaining to me why you do not take the phone or what I am  supposed to tell your sister. If that is, indeed, your reaction, my suspicion is that you misheard or misunderstood my statement. So I repeat it. If  your impatient response is, “Yes, yes, I get it, my sister is on the phone,” but you still continue doing whatever you are doing, I realize that you do not understand how statements are meant to function and, consequently, that you did not understand my statement.

Has the person I just described really understood my statement as it is meant to be understood? Well, if statements are pictures of real states of the world, and if my interlocutor ‘got’ the picture that her sister is actually at the other end of the line, then she understood my statement and  is a competent English speaker. Yet, I and, surely, every other competent English speaker, will say that if this person doesn’t do anything in response to what I stated, but simply continues doing what she was doing,  this person doesn’t understand statements, even if she gets in her mind a very clear picture of her sister at the other end of the line. The reason is that statements of the kind I am referring to, whether in English or in any other language, are not meant to function as pictures of reality to be contemplated, or even judgments to be assented to. Rather, they are meant to function –to employ Kuhn’s expression in reference to the statements of scientific laws– as tools to be used as a guide for action in response to what is stated. Indeed, the person who in the situation just described guides her action by what I stated and responds to my statement by taking the phone to talk to her sister is the person who understands my statement and who knows how statements are meant to function. And she shows that she understands my statement and how statements are meant to function, not by saying that she understands and by getting in her mind the picture of her sister at the other end of the line, or even by deeming that picture true, but by actually acting appropriately in response to my statement. In short, understanding statements does not consist in ‘getting’ a picture, but in  acting appropriately to satisfy one’s desire or goal in response to what is stated .

To return now to our subject, which is the difference between the textbook view of science, which Kuhn rejects, and Kuhn’s own alternative to the textbook view. Kuhn maintains that the textbook view of science errs in locating scientific knowledge in theoretical  understanding, in a purely theoretical view of the universe. . Much as in the case of  statements in ordinary, everyday language, such as “Your sister is on the phone,” understanding the formulations of scientific laws consists in acting appropriately in response to what the formulated law states. In other words, it consists in guiding one’s action by what is stated in the law or the theorem, not in any contemplative grasp of what the law states. This is why, contrary to the textbook view of science, actual application of the laws in problem-solving is not, according to Kuhn, something in addition to scientific knowledge; it is the way of acquiring what scientific knowledge consists in, to wit: the habitual disposition to ‘see’ in the appropriate gestalt.

But why call this ‘knowledge’ if it involves no truth as visual correspondence to what is the case? We will find less outlandish this usage of the word ‘knowledge’ if we reflect on the fact that it comes to us very naturally to attribute knowledge to animals exclusively on the basis of their acquired problem-solving ability reflected in their behavior (and based in the inherited needs of each species).  When an animal who previously was incapable of behaving in the appropriate way  acquires the ability to do that on a habitual basis, as shown by its behavior, we reckon that as knowledge

The primacy of statements

In his very attempt to distinguish doxa from episteme, merely hypothetical from certain, genuine knowledge, opinion or belief from science,  Plato had implicitly made an epistemic determination in favor of the purely intelligible (universal rather than particular, and necessarily existent rather than contingent) objects to the exclusion of the sensible objects, and, consequently, of the intellect to the exclusion of the senses as the epistemic organ. More pragmatic than Plato regarding the degree of certainty that is possible concerning different areas of reality, Aristotle would reintegrate the sensible world to philosophical exploration, but would keep the judging intellect to the exclusion of the senses as the epistemic organ. Now, judging is a reflective operation of the intellect. It is reflective in that the intellect examines an already reached hypothesis, weighs whether there is sufficient reason for assenting or rejecting it, and passes judgment on that basis. Though the judging operation is not identical with the verbal statement, the result of the judgment finds its natural expression in an affirmative or negative verbal statement, which is the only linguistic form that expresses a claim to truth –and truth is after all the business of philosophy. Hence the exclusive concentration on statements and arguments in support of statements, typical of the philosophical literary genre. Not for nothing did Aristotle develop a LOGIC that aimed at determining the truth-validity of statements from the point of view of their form, independently of their content, and in the Analytica Posteriora sketched an ideal science composed of statements all of which either possess the self-evidence of undeniable principles, or rest on strictly rational demonstration.

Hence a philosophical dispute is a dialogue in which one of the participants states his or her thesis and furnishes the evidence in which he or she finds sufficient reason for assenting to his thesis, in the hope of eliciting agreement with his judgment from the other participants.

PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE AND PRACTICAL SCIENCE

In my paper on Devereux’s interpretation of practical knowledge in Aristotle (2002-june-1-Devereux) I detected a harmful confusion by Aristotle between practical knowledge and practical science, as if they were identical. Actually, they are not. I have a hunch that something of the same confusion is involved in the understanding of science that underpins the textbook image of science. Practical knowledge, which corresponds to Kuhn’s image of science, is the knowledge by which the practitioner of a science involved in the situation guides his decisions-actions. Practical science, by contrast, which corresponds to the textbook image of science, is the objectification of the practical   judgements of the practitioner, which are mistaken for pictures of reality. Practical science, though, is neither knowledge nor practical.

The same confusion seems to have been committed by those who are surprised that Aristotle doesn’t practice in his own work, as Euclid actually did in his geometry, the deductive, strictly demonstrative method he presented as the model of science in the Posterior Analytics . His ethical treatises, for instance, are not more geometrico. Neither, as I argued in my paper on Devereux, are they really practical, for the agent doesn’t need to know the doctrine contained in Aristotle’s ethical treatises in order to make correct ethical decisions.

But what about the science of medicine? Doesn’t the medical practitioner need to know the theory contained in the medical treatises in order to be successful in curing?

In another context, Kuhn has an interesting observation that may be pertinent here. The student of science is taught not by demonstration or proof but by authority. Let us transfer his point from physics to geometry. The student of geometry doesn’t need to know the truth of a particular theorem by demonstration in order to solve problems by applying the theorem. Which means that the demonstration of the theorems is irrelevant to the practice. The student may take the stated theorem as true on Euclid’s or on his teacher’s authority. Taking it as true doesn’t  mean thinking that the statement re-presents something that exists in reality; indeed, all realities are particular, whereas the theorem (or the law) are abstract and ‘universal.’ Taking the theorem as true means using the theorem as a guide for solving geometrical problems either on paper or in reality, much as taking as true your statement that my sister is on the phone is responding to it appropriately by taking the phone rather than understanding and assenting to it as a doctrine that is true, that is, as a picture of reality to be contemplated. However, the scientific language is not a natural language; it is reflective and abstract; it works on the basis of the natural language with which one deals with the ‘natural world’ to which it ultimately refers. Like in the case of a natural language, one has to learn the scientific language through practice until it becomes one’s own –  as it is ‘owned’ by the teacher, not when the teacher teaches science but when the teacher practices science, when as a practitioner of science the teacher ‘sees’ applicatively in the appropriate habitual gestalt.

To return to medical science. Does the practitioner need to know the theoretical doctrine in order to be successful? Or is the knowledge of the doctrine superfluous, as is the knowledge of the doctrine contained in Aristotle’s ethical treatises?

There is a significant difference between ethics and medicine. The practice of ethics deals exclusively in particulars. The ethical generalizations of the written doctrine are mere rules of thumb, drawn from reflection on the actions of the prudent agents, which rules of thumb are to be superseded by the agent’s judgment in the particular case. In medicine, by contrast, there are observational or experiential regularities, such as light meats are wholesome for bilious people (at a primitive stage of medicine), and such drugs are efficient in curing cancer of the prostate because they are of the kind that inhibits the multiplication of unhealthy cells (at a higher level of development of medicine). In ethics, whether or not one is virtuous is all-important; not so in medicine as such. In other words, medicine is more object- or world-oriented as motivator of action, ethics more subject-oriented as source of responsible action.  In medicine the goal of curing and being cured is unanimously shared by the practitioners of medicine, whereas the case is not such in ethics-politics. Because of these differences, ethics is not a science (or even an Aristotelian ‘art,’) in any  way, whereas medicine is, though far less exact in its applications than physics. Indeed, the natural regularities in physics are simpler, more accessible and even more easily measurable than in medicine, which renders their applicative use simpler (because more regular, less subject to deliberation ) than in medicine, but doesn’t exclude the less regular but real regularities in medicine.

Now, these real regularities are, indeed,  to be known by the medical practitioner. But ‘known,’ here, means appropriated or internalized through initially supervised practice as guides for action under the appropriate circumstances, not as true or resembling pictures of faithful copies of the world, a world populated by such and such factual entities.

This may be the opportunity to tackle the issue of the ontological value of laws, theorems, etc., which so far I have failed to confront. To formulate the problem in an acute way, God is for me the central character in the narrative story of the world in which I too participate as a character. I know Him by responding to Him appropriately. Obviously, however, God is not because I respond to Him and only insofar as I respond to Him. Now, what I have been saying may sound as if that were the case.

NEW BEGINNING

Kuhn’s real contribution consists in the shift from the traditional conception of science as a knowledge acquiring activity to a problem-solving activity. Science is practical, applicative, as all genuine knowledge is.

I need to take a more direct approach to explaining Kuhn’s real contribution which consists in shifting from a conception of science as  a contemplative or theoretical knowledge -accumulating activity to a practical problem-solving activity.

The difficulty lies in the overwhelming weight of the Greek epistemological tradition with its seemingly commonsensical notions of truth and knowledge as exact representations of what is really the case.  Since scientific knowledge is uncritically taken for t he highest representative of knowledge, the mentioned notions of truth and  knowledge are automatically transferred to the scientific field. Hence the conception of science alluded to for which Kuhn attempts to propose an alternative.
