                             ASSENT AND CONSENT

                                             Scio cui credidi.



                             On assent we may be brief.It is an act  of  the
                 possible intellect. It  is,  accordingly,  contrasted  with
                 consent which is an act of the will. The good is in things,
                 but the true is in the mind; consent is  a  motion  of  the
                 will with respect to the thing, but assent is a  motion  of
                 the intellect with respect  to  a  conception.  Again,  the
                 object of an assent is either side of a  contradiction.  We
                 do not assent by defining; again, we do not assent when  we
                 doubt or merely opine. We assent to  first  principles,  to
                 demonstrable conclusions, to the affirmations  of  reliable
                 authority. Assent occurs when we judge a conception of  the
                 thing to be true. It must be motivated;  thus  intellectual
                 life moves us to  assent  to  first  principles  and  first
                 principles in  turn  move  us  to  assent  to  demonstrable
                 conclusions. In a word, assent appears to be identical with
                 judgment but to emphasize  its  subjective  and  reflective
                 aspects; it is the judgment as a personal  act,  committing
                 the person, and a responsibility of the person; it  is  the
                 judgment as based upon  an  apprehension  of  evidence,  as
                 including an awareness of its own validity, as a  truth  in
                 the subject rather than as a  truth  absolutely  and  as  a
                 medium in quo reality is apprehended."
                 (B. Lonergan, Verbum, p. 6l. (See notes therein).

      In their treatment of the Bible the medievals reduced the
apparently  stating  portions  of  biblical  narratives   to   objects   for
consideration in view of  assent,  and  they  treated  those  statements  as
answers to  implicit  Aristotelian  theoretical  questions;  they  therefore
rendered those answers questionable in  the  Aristotelian  theoretical  way,
that is, in terms of evidence.
      Quesstioning in the Aristotelian way inovlves suspending  intellectual
assent unless and until one  is  intellectually  convinced  by  Aristotelian
intellectual evidence or by  reasoned  proof.  This,  however,  is  not  the
agent's questioning. The agent  does  not  suspend  assent  but  consent  to
object for response; the agent suspends provisorily the "bracing himself  to
respond" as well as the response.
      Since  the  early  medievals  were  people  of  faith  who  explicitly
acknowledged that they could--and should-fall back on their  faith  in  case
of insufficient evidence or  even  evidence  to  the  contrary,  it  may  be
doubted that they questioned seriously even in  the  Aristotelian  vein.  In
any case,  they  certainly  did  not  question  seriously,  practically,  as
agents, for  they  did  not  suspend  their  consent.  When  Bonaventure  or
Aquinas, following Saint Anselm, questioned God's existence,  they  did  not
suspend their consent to God; they continued praying, celebrating mass  and,
generally, practicing  their  faith  as  intensely  and  sincerely  as  they
preveiously had.
      Faith is an agent's relation; it belongs in the order of exposure  and
interaction, not in the order of pure contemplation. The medievals  did  not
realize  the  difference  between  the  two  orders  an,  under  Aristotle's
influence, reduced the order of exposure  to  the  order  of  contemplation,
without the slightest realizationof the shift they had effected.  There  are
several clear indicators of  the  confusion  of  which  they  were  victims.
Firstly, they took faith for "intellectual assent",  which  is  equivale4nte
to the 'belief  that'  of  Aristotle  and  Plantinga.  Secondly,  they  took
revelation for God's "knowledge" of Himself and of the  world  (cf.  Chenu).
Thirdly, they rendered questionable in the Aristotelian way  each  of  their
own statements or "theses, "threby destroying the hi-storical unity  of  the
Bible.
      With Lonergan, and therefore with Aquinas, I maintain  the  difference
between "assent" and "consent. But while  I  may  grant  that  assent  is  a
purely intellectual act, I do not think that consent is an act of the will--
in the way Aquinas conceives  "the  will,"  for  Aquinas's  will  is  merely
appetitive, totally blind. Faith, both as an  attitudfe  or  disposition  or
habitus and as an act, is not of "the will" but of  the  whole  human  being
and is both appetitive and sighted. In other words,m it  is  "consent,"  but
consent of the whole person, not the  unsighted  consent  of  the  thomistic
will.
      In order to start clarifying what I  mean  by  "consent"  and  how  it
differs from the "assent" to which Aquinas reduces  faith  I  will  consider
Descartes' "doubt." Descartes suspended intellectual  assent  to  statements
or 'objects' because they were not the result of evidence. But  he  did  not
doubt as an agent, he did not suspend consent, he did not stop being  braced
for response in future encounters with  the  objects  to  which  he  refused
assent. He himself underscores this radical difference:
             "...in  order  that  I  might  not  remain  irresolute  in   my
actions during the time that my reason would oblige me             to be  so
in my judgments, and so that I would not cease           to live  from  that
time forward as happily as I  could,  I               formed  a  provisional
moral code..." (Discourse on            Method, chapter 3).
      Refusing assent, Descartes found himself irresolute in his  judgments.
But  he  did  not  find  himself  irresolute  in  his  actions  because   he
"consented" to the very objects to which he did not assent.This  is  similar
to what certain medievals did in the realm of  faith,  in  that  while  they
questioned truths  of  their  faith  without  having  yet  found  sufficient
Aristotelian  evidence  for  assenting  to  them,  that  is,  for   judging,
continued nonetheless to consent to them, that is, to practice their  faith,
to entertain with God a relationship of faith, to live  as  Christians.  The
difference between these medievals and Descartes is  that  the  former  were
not  obliged,  as  Descartes  was,  to  be  irresolute  in  their  religious
judgments while they lacked sufficient evidence  to  support  them,  because
they could fall back on God's authority. Whereas Descartes could not  assent
to any proposition--even to the propositions of faith--without  Aristotelian
evidence, the medievals could continue assenting to  those  propositions  on
the basis of "God's authority."
       It  is  interesting  to  realize  that  Descartes  had   no   trouble
discriminating between doubting as  an  agent  and  doubting  as  a  curious
contemplator, while this distinction  escaped  the  medievals,  who  reduced
consent to assent.
   It is not quite  exact,  however,  to  say  that  the  medievals  reduced
consent to assent, for at  least  Aquinas  explicitly  distinguishes  assent
which is an act of the intellect from consent which is an act of  the  will.
But the medievals did not mean by consent  what  I  do.  For  the  medievals
consent is an act of the will, of the purely appetitive, unsighted  faculty,
whereas assent is an act of the intellect, of the purely intellective,  that
is, sighted faculty. For me, however, as I will explain  below,  consent  is
an act of the living agent who has to deal practically with the  surrounding
world, and an act of the living agent who is  both  and  at  the  same  time
sighted and appetitive, who wills the object he knows and knows  the  object
he wills, who knows the object by tending toward it  and  tends  toward  the
object by knowing it. As for  what  the  medievals  and  Descartes  mean  by
"assent," I consider it as a pure abstraction that  results  from  mistaking
what philosophers do when they reflect as pure contemplators  of  the  world
for what living agents, that is, we people, do  when  engaged  in  practical
dealings with the world.
      Thus, Descartes could go on consenting to the very  objects  to  which
he refused assent. In other words, the agent can consent without  assenting.
Descartes may have doubted as a contemplator whether the piece of  bread  he
was going to eat really existed or was really  a  piece  of  bread,  but  he
surely consented to the piece of bread for he ate it  as  a  real  piece  of
bread. Otherwise put, while Descartes  was involved in his methodical doubt
he could not assent to the proposition "This is a piece of bread;" he  could
not "judge," to use Lonergan's language, "This is a piece of bread." But  he
ate it nonetheless.  And  this  is  what  I  call  "consent,"  to  wit:  the
practical disposition for the future and  the  present  conduct  of  dealing
with 'that' as a piece of bread by eating it. This is not  mere  "acting  as
if." An actor acts as if; but Descartes did not; he acted as an  agent,  not
as an actor.
      (To explore further the nature of  consent  and  its  relationship  to
assent in actual life, remember Sartre: "Are you so forceful  in  argument?"
asked the Curé. "You outdo even us." "I  did  not  argue,"  answered  M.  de
Rollebon, "I made him fear Hell." Cf. My Notes in Process, I, entry 40.  And
see entry 190--Newman on being ashamed before  God  as  a  'proof'  for  His
existence).      Aristotelian "assent," then, is different from what I  have
called "consent." But is what I call consent  different  from  what  Aquinas
calls consent, that famous act of the will? I think  so.  Aquinas's  consent
is vulnerable to the traditional unsolvable quandaries: How does one  bridge
the gap between "facts" and "values"? And, if the  will  is  unsighted,  how
can it desire, or be repelled by, that which it does not  know?  Etc.,  etc.
What I call consent is not vulnerable to these objections because it is  not
an act of the unsighted will but of the sighted living agent. The  agent  is
sighted-appetitive in one piece because he lives in the order  of  exposure.
I consent to Jesus as God in that I practically relate to Him as being  God.
But I would not consent to just anybody  who tells me  he  is  God.  What  I
mean is that my response to Jesus as being God  is  not  a  blind  response,
like a  reflex,  but  a  practical  recognition  (from  cognoscere)  of  his
divinity. There is, so to say, a sort of "dramatic or  exposural  evidence,"
evidence which is not of the purely intellectual  order  but  which  can  be
cashed as evidence in the agent's reflective  moment  and  which  reinforces
his dispositional bracing himself toward the future. This is what I do  when
I reflect on my allegiance to Jesus by  contrast  to  my  non-allegiance  to
fools, or when I discuss my faith in Jesus with someone  who  dissents  with
me,  though  I  cannot  furnish   him   any   Aristotelian,   intellectually
necessitating evidence of Jesus' divinity--a proof which by  the  way  would
be useless, since faith is not Aristotelian  assent.  What  Kierkegaard  was
desperatingly trying to do, I think, particularly in the Postscript, was  to
cure himself and his readers of the Aristotelian speculative  tradition  and
to bring the thinker back to the order of exposure.
      As I said above, not  only  did  the  medievals  fail  to  distinguish
between Aristotelian assent  and  what  I  have  called  consent,  but  they
reduced faith, which is consent, to a purely intellectual act, to an act  of
assent, to the kind of  personal,  reflective  judgment  of  which  Lonergan
speaks. Why did they, as so many other philosophers  after  them,  Plantinga
included, make this mistake? Because it is easy to  make,  particularly  for
those who take their cue from the Platonico-Aristotelian tradition.  Indeed,
all philosophizing is reflective, that is, it takes place after the  events,
at the reflective moment, when one is thinking, questioning,  not  when  one
is dealing practically with the surrounding world.  What  one  has  at  that
moment is objects for consideration, not realities to which one  is  exposed
and has  to  respond.  The  consideration  may  be  purely  theoretical,  or
practical.  But  philosophers,  obfuscated  with  the  idea  of   infallible
knowledge, know nothing of practical consideration for future response;  all
they care is whether there is sufficient evidence  to  assent,  to  'believe
that'. Thus  they  automatically  identify  object  for  consideration  with
object for theoretical consideration, forgetting that that  is  not  at  all
what we do in actual life as agents when we consider future  action  or  try
to identify the objects we encounter.
DIRECT AND REFLECTIVE CONSCIOUSNESS
      To clarify what I mean by consent it might be profitable to  think  of
the direct consciousness the agent has of the world  in  his  active  coping
with it, amd of the reflective consciousness we have when  we  think  either
of the world without having to rerspond to it or of our past  dealings  with
it.
      As Sartre points out, the common distinction  between  perception  and
action is an abstraction--and a harmful one at that. The  agent  engaged  in
the world perceives hodologically, that is, as an agent,  as  someone  whose
world is not a mere spectacle but a field of action, as  someone  surrounded
by objects that are harmful or desirable, threatening or attractive,  useful
or noxious. Perceiveing an apple over there on the table, when I am  hungry,
is not perceiving a mere thing, something neutral; it is  rather  perceiving
an apple as food, as desirable and desired. The agent's  space  is  not  the
neutral,  abstract  space  of  Newtonian  physics,  but  a  practical  space
furrowed by paths that lead from the agent to the object,  from  me  to  the
apple over the table. Perceiving the apple "over  there  on  the  table"  is
also perceiving practically as an agent  the  distance  that  separates  the
apple from me and the obstacles that separate the apple from me  and  around
which I have to move to reach the apple and eat it.       The  same  can  be
said of the agent's time. It is not the neutral  Newtonian  time;  it  is  a
hostile or favorable time, the long time of waiting  for  what  one  desires
intensely, the inexorable time that carries us to  the  encounter  we  fear,
the short time of our periods of happiness. This  agent's  consciousness  of
the surrounding world is what I call direct consciousness.
      Perhaps the most distinctive and important  characteristic  of  direct
consciousness  is  that  it  is  subject-centered.  Distances,   directions,
instrumentality and value "unfold from the subject," acquire  their  meaning
by relation to the subject. Far  or  near,  above  or  below,  in  front  or
behind, here or over there, difficult or easy, obstacle or  help,  desirable
or undesirable, attractive or repellent, now or yesterday  or  tomorrow--all
these are in function of the agent-subject. Your  "far"  is  not  my  "far,"
your difficult is not my difficult, your desirable may  be  my  undesirable.
Carthago est delenda Romanis, sed non Carthaginensibus.  My  friend  may  be
missed  by  me,  but  his  absence  may  be  a  relief  for  you.  A  second
characteristic of direct consciousness is that it  is  value-laden.  Indeed,
the surroundig world of which the agent is conscious is  a  world  in  which
"facts" and "values" are inextricably mixed and equally constitutive of  the
world  perceived;  in  which--to  use  Sartre's  language,  there   is   not
perception first and then action, but agent's  perception;  in  which  there
are not things first and then instruments,  but  instrumental-things--things
whose identity is inseparable from their positive (that  is,  favorable)  or
negastive (that is, unfavorable) coefficient of instrumentality.
      We may understand  better  the  subject-centeredness  and  the  value-
ladeness of direct consciousness if  we  realize  that  it  is  the  agent's
consciousness of the world to whose action the agent is exposed and  of  the
world exposed to  the  agent's  action.  My  "here"  is  the  "here"  of  my
exposure, the"here" of which I am the vulnerable center; your "here" is  the
"here" of your exposure,  of  which  you  are  the  vulnerable  center,  the
subject who is affected by the action of that by  which  he  is  surrounded,
and the subject whose actions modify that by which he  is  surrounded.  What
Merleau-Ponty has to say about depth  being  the  most  existential  of  all
dimensions is pertinent  here.  Indeed,  depth  is  nothing  else  than  the
indication of the distances that unfold from a  vulnerable  subject  to  the
objects by which he is surrounded:

      I would only add, to clarify Merleau's  thought,  that  depth  belongs
indeed to the perspective, but that the perspective itself of which  we  are
talking here is value-laden because it is the perspective  of  a  vulnerable
subject. Depth is primarily the distance from me of what is  threatening  or
attractive to me, of the apple I desire, of the animal I fear.
      We may recapitulate. Direct consciousness is the consciousness of  the
agent situated within the very  world  of  which  he  is  conscious.  It  is
essentially subject-centered and value-laden. Let us now turn to  reflective
consciousness.
      Fortunately for us, although our exposure  to  the  surrounding  world
never ceases and the surrounding world never ceases  being  exposed  to  our
action, we have periods of respite, moments in which the  surrounding  world
is sufficiently under control and its immediate threats  and  sollicitations
sufficiently neutralized for us to be able to cease immediately  caring.  At
those times we cease acting  or  planning  our  future  actions  and  become
contemplators. Our contemplation may turn to the world to enjoy it; that  is
aesthetic contemplation. But it  may  also  take  the  form,  sometimes,  of
"thinking" prompted by the  desire  to  know,  to  understand,  to  explain.
Though there is a number of varieties of thinking, the  one  that  interests
me here is  philosophical  thinking.I  believe  that  the  most  distinctive
characteristic of philosophical thinking is its totalitarianism, its  desire
to understand and explain the whole of reality, not just a  portion  of  it,
though of course this thinking often proceeds piece by piece.  Anyway,  what
is important here is that thinking is reflective and that the  consciousness
of  the  world  and  of  oneself  involved   in   thinking   is   reflective
consciousness. Reflective consciousness presupposes direct or  prereflective
consciousness because it is on direct or  prereflective  consciousness  that
it reflects. In other words,  the  object  of  reflective  consciousness  is
direct consciousness, the data of direct consciousness,  for  that  is  what
thinking tries to understand and explain. As  Lonergan  says,  assent  is  a
reflective judgment. This means that for it to take place something  of  the
epistemic order must have previously taken place  on  the  level  of  direct
consciousness. Thus, Descartes sees the table on which he has  been  writing
and which, as an agent, by the very fact of using it to write, he takes  for
a real table. Now he is going to reflect on the table on  whose  reality  he
already believes as an agent. Is his conviction  of  the  existence  of  the
table grounded on evidence? Can he take his seeing the table for  sufficient
grounds to judge that the table really exists?
      Reflective consciousness, then, differs from direct  consciousness  in
that it works on the data furnished  by  direct  consciousness.  This  is  a
first characteristic, but there are others. A second characteristic is  that
reflective consciousness takes its objects as data, not  as  realities  with
which the subject has to cope as an agent. Remember  that  reflection  takes
place at the times in which the urgencies of action  are  neutralized,  when
Descartes has taken care of all  his  practical  problems  so  that  he  can
devote himself  to  his  philosophical  inquires  before  the  fire  in  the
chimney. For Descartes the table he sees is not an  instrument  to  be  used
but a phenomenon to be considered in view of possible assent. In this  sense
the table is a datum for reflection, not a reality for response.
      This leads us to a third characteristic of  reflective  consciousness.
Since its objects are mere data for thinking, not  realities  for  response,
they  are  value-neutral,  not  value  laden  as  the  objects   of   direct
consciousness. The table Descartes sees is  neither  an  instrument  nor  an
obstacle, neither repellent nor attractive, neither useful nor  harmful.  It
just is a datum for thinking.
      Fourthly, reflective consciousness differs from  direct  consciousness
concerning its subject-centeredness. All object of consciousness is subject-
centered in that it is object of consciousness, object  of  someone  who  is
conscious. In this  sense,  the  objects  of  reflective  consciousness  are
subject-centered, too. But the subject to which they refer or on  whom  they
are centered differs from  the  subject  to  which  the  objects  of  direct
consciousness refer. The subject in the case of direct consciousness  is  an
exposed  subject,  whereas  the  subject   in   the   case   of   reflective
consciousness is a contemplating subject. Now, a cpnte,mplating subject  is,
so to say, interchangeable with any other contemplating  subjec,  since  his
only relationship to the object is as contemplated object,  and  the  object
as merely contemplated is  the  same  for  this  contemplator  as  for  that
contemplator. By contrast, the exposed object is related to  the  object  as
object to which he is exposed and to whom the object is exposed. Because  of
this the  subject-centeredness  of  direct  consciousness  is  personal  and
total. Personal, because it is not interchangeable but singular, because  it
refers to this singular subject. Total, because the subject is not  involved
as mere contemplator but as a whole. By contrast, the subject-  centeredness
of reflective consciousness involves the subject only as contemplator,  only
his mind or his eye, not his whole singular being. We  can,  therefore,  say
that   the   subject-centeredness    of    reflective    consciousness    is
interchangeable because it is impersonal.
      All I have said so far  concerning  reflective  consciousness  may  be
better understood in terms of its  "objectivity."  Reflective  consciousness
is objective in several senses. First, since the exposure of the subject  to
the object is neutralized and the subject functions as mere contemplator  of
the object without any personal tie to it, the  object  itself  becomes  the
central element in the case of reflective consciousness, whereas  in  direct
consciousness the object is only important in  its  particular  relationship
to the subject and cannot be understood but in terms of  this  relationship.
Second,  since  the  special  function  of   reflective   consciousness   is
cognitive,  not  instrumental,  and  since   cognition   is   disinterested,
reflective consciousness  releases,  so  to  say,  the  objectivity  of  the
object, makes it into mere object for consideration, into mere datum in  its
pure giveness to the curious subject.
      WHERE AM I HEADING TOWARD? In other words, what is the  point  of  the
above considerations? My  fundamental  hunch  is  that  in  our  attempt  to
understand "reality" and in particular "the human being," we have  taken  as
our implicit model for reality the objects of reflective  consciousnesy  and
the  specialized   and   restrained   intentionality   of   the   reflective
consciousness for the human being. This mistake can be  shown  only  on  the
basis of a comparison between direct and reflective  consciousness  and  can
be corrected only on the basis of taking direct consciousnness  rather  than
reflective consciousness as our model. Perhaps I  can  make  my  point  more
clear by applying what I just said to the particular case of the  medievals,
for they represent a clear-cut case of a shift from direct consciousness  to
reflective consciousness.
      THE MEDIEVALS: FROM DIRECT TO REFLECTIVE CONSCIOUSNESS
      Nothing can be more revealing in the respect that  interests  me  here
than the medievals' analysis of faith and their treatment in their  writings
of God in particular and, in general, of the so-called "object of faith."
      Faith, for Aquinas, is an intellectual act of  assent  to  a  revealed
statement, not because of  its  intrinsic  evidence  but  because  of  God's
authority. God knows infallibly; he never makes a mistake.  Furthermore,  He
is incapable of lying. Therefore, we should assent  to  what  He  tells  us,
regardless of whether or not we have evidence for or against what  he  tells
us. Well, I must say that I cringe, as a Christian, when I hear this, for  I
think it is a total distortion of my faith--that is, of the faith  lived  by
Catholics and of the God in whom we Catholics believe. Why? To  condense  my
answer in a few words: (1) Because God's  revelation  is  not  primarily  of
what He knows, but of Himself--exposure of Himself to  us,  making  Himeself
accessible to us. It is primarily a matter  of  interaction  among  persons,
not of statements. (2) Because faith is not primarily assent  to  statements
but of consent to God.
      One thing is to be a faithful, to live one's faith. Another  thing  is
to analyze and talk about faith--a case in which the "about" indicates  that
it is an  activity  of  reflective  consciousness  on  the  data  of  direct
consciousness. Now, Saint Thomas was a genuine faithful;  he  also  analyzed
and talked about faith. He was great in the  former  but,  or  so  I  think,
hopelessly wrong  in  the  latter.  His  fundamental  mistake  consisted  in
mistaking the "faith" and the "God" about which he reflected and talked  for
the faith he practiced as a living agent and for  the  God  before  whom  he
kneeled as a Christian.
      Praying is an act of faith, an expression of faith,  the  deed  of  an
agent who believes in God, who consents to God, who responds to God as  "the
to be adored," who encounters God as the adorandus one.  But  one  can  also
think about faith and about  God.  Clearly,  though,  praying  and  thinking
about the action of praying or about the God one prays are as  different  as
kissing is different  from  thinking  about  kissing  or  about  the  person
kissed. Well, my point is, at least in part, that Saint Thomas  mistook  the
results of his thinking about kissing for kissing. In what way?
      Saint Thomas was  not  stupid  and  I  seem  to  be  accusing  him  of
something absurdly stupid, namely, that he mistook  thinking  about  praying
for praying,  something as grooss as mistaking thinking  about  kissing  for
kissing, when in fact he was perfectly  aware  of,  and  repeated  it  often
enough, that all his knowledge about faith was not worth  the  simplest  act
of faith of the simplest peasant. "Theology" and "faith" were, according  to
him, different, not identical. Obviously, then, he did not make the  mistake
I am attributing to him. But the case is more complicated than this.
      Certainly, theology and faith are  very  different  for  Aquinas.  But
both are acts of the same kind, namely, intellectual acts, acts  of  assent,
judgments of the kind characterized  by  Lonergan  as  "personal"--commiting
the person--and "reflective"--involving the awareness of their own  validity
because of  their  apprehension  of  evidence.  Theology,  therefore,  is  a
reflection either on the reflective act of assent of the believer or on  the
object assented to by the believer, whereas faith  is  simply  a  reflective
act of assent whose object is the revealed  as  revealed.  Because  theology
(insofar as  it  might  be  confused  with  faith)  is  a  reflection  on  a
reflective act or  on  an  object  of  reflection,  while  faith  is  simple
reflection, not reflection on reflection, theology and faith  are  obviously
different. What is important for us, however, is that both are acts  of  the
same kind, namely, judgments, intellectual acts.
      Now, above I compared believing to kissing. Needless to say, I do  not
think that believing and kissing are the same. But I do think that  the  act
of faith is structurally more like the act of kissing than like the  act  of
judging as Saint Thomas understands judging; secondly, I think that,  if  we
think of faith as an "habitus" rather than as an act,  faith  is  more  like
loving than as  virtual  assent;  thirdly,  I  think  that  this  is  mighty
important because it pinpoints where exactly Saint Thomas went wrong.
      For Aquinas faith is  an  intellectual  act,  a  verbum  mentis  whose
result is knowledge, something becoming known to someone.  Admittedly  faith
is not knowledge in the  ordinary  sense  of  the  word,  because  knowledge
results from intrinsic evidence; it is faith, because  it  does  not  result
from intrinsic evidence but from God's authority, but it  nonetheless  is  a
sort of knowledge in that it has on  the  believer  the  same  results  that
knowledge has on the knower. The believer "knows in faith" as much and  with
much more certainty what a knower would know who  had  sufficient  intrinsic
evidence for what the believer  believes.  Functionally,  then,  though  not
formally,  faith  is   knowledge,   according   to   Aquinas.   Given   this
interpretation of faith, it is easy to see how Aquinas  could  confuse  what
he confused. We do not confuse kissing with thinking about  kissing  because
kissing and thinking about kissing are structurally  different:  kissing  is
an action, while thinking about kissing  is  a  thought.  But  suppose  that
mistake we fancied that kissing is a thought, a judgment. How,  then,  would
we distinguish between kissing  and  thinking  about  kissing,  between  the
judgment-kissing and the judgment on the judgment-kissing?
      I am getting lost because I  am  mixing  two  different  things:  that
faith is a judgment, and that faith--being a judgment--is a reflective  act.
Let us start with the direct act of  faith.  For  Aquinas  this  act  is  an
assent based on God's authority. For me it is not an assent but  a  consent.
Abraham did not believe that God had commanded him to  sacrifice  Isaac;  he
obeyed the commandment and his  faith  consisted  in  this  obedience.  Now,
obviously, obeying and thinking about having obeyed or about  somebody  else
obeying are not the same. I can assent to the fact that somebody is  obeying
an order without myself obeying. But Aquinas would say that  I  cannot  obey
without assenting to the fact that somebody commanded and this, for him,  is
the act of faith. This makes the act of  faith  into  a  pure  assent  to  a
truth, whereas for me the act of faith is a practical response to a  reality
with which one is confronted. I  claim,  therefore,  that  Aquinas  confused
assenting to (someone's) having obeyed with obeying,  not  in  that  Aquinas
equated obeying with assenting, but in that he confused what he did when  he
reflected on having obeyed with what he did when he obeyed,  which  is  very
much like confusing what one does when one reflects  on  kissing  with  what
one does when one kisses. And not only  did  he  confuse  the  two,  but  he
reduced kissing (that is, obeying) to assenting  to  having  kissed,  rather
than the other way around.
      But why would Aquinas make such a mistake? Because he  was  reflecting
on the Scripture, which is itself not direct experience  but  reflection  on
past experience. Abraham was  the  faithful  one;  the  Scripture  is  about
Abraham. When Abraham obeyed God he was not assenting to the  fact  that  he
was obeying God nor was God's revelation  to  him  about  his  obeying  God,
exactly as my reflecting now on what Aquinas did in the Summa is  not  doing
what Aquinas did in the Summa. However, for Aquinas, what he believed  about
Abraham was what Abraham believed when Abraham consented to God. Wrong.  For
Abraham was not assenting to the fact that God was commanding  him;  he  was
consenting (which in this case amounts to obeying)  to  God-commanding.  The
mistake of Aquinas consisted in taking the  Scripture  in  relation  to  its
reader as the primary and paradigmatic case of faith, when  in  fact  it  is
secondary and derivative. Scripture, now, as any writing, is  about;  it  is
not direct experience, it is not interaction.  From there to  conclude  that
it transmits knowledge there is but a short step.  And  since  Scripture  is
the revealed to which the reader is not exposed and  in  relation  to  which
the most the reader can do is assent, there is but a short step  from  there
to assuming that revelation consists in making known and faith  consists  in
the kind of assent by which something becomes known to  the  assenter.  This
is what Saint Thomas did. This represents the medieval's shift  from  direct
experience to reflective knowledge.
      Direct experience, however, is not  knowledge,  much  less  reflective
knowledge. But neither is it blind, since  it  is  experience-of,  not  mere
activity. Kissing is not merely going through the motions  of  kissing,  for
it is experience of kissed-(kissing). Nor is it merely experience of-kissed-
(kissing), for it is a transitive  activity.  But  it  is  not  "knowledge,"
either, in that the agent does not know or even perceive himself kissing  or
being kissed. Furthermore, the agent's and the witness's experience are  not
the same even when they have "the same object." The  experience  of  kissing
is not the same as the experience of seeing someone  kissing.   However,  at
the reflective moment, when cashed in terms of judgment-knowledge (that  is,
of assent), the two experiences, for all their differences, are  covered  by
"the same" judgment: "M kissed G." This is the "knowledge"  that  interested
the medievals and that, in general, interests philosophers.
      The medievals confused this  reflective  knowledge  with  faith.  They
seem to have identified, in the first place, the  perceptual  experience  of
the witness, for whom the agent is object, with the agent's  experience  who
is subject; then, they identified the object of  the  perceptual  experience
of the witness with the object of the  judging  experience  of  the  judger.
This is what Sartre means by saying that "we have privileged  the  point  of
view of exteriority." Narration, by contrast, reinstates the point  of  view
of interiority, that is, the agent-patient's experience as  subject,  though
of course the experience delivered by narration is experience  "reconstrued"
by the narrator.
      If I am right about the medievals' confusion,  we  can  analyze  their
mistake. First, the agent's and the witness' experience are  NOT  the  same.
Obviously, nobody means that they are numerically the same;  only  that  the
object experienced by both is the same. But is this correct? Suppose that  X
tells me "kiss me," and that you hear it as  much  as  I  do.  Are  we  here
talking about the same experience? Did you and I have the  same  experience?
The illusion that this is so comes from  the fact that  we  heard  the  same
words.But identifying that with experience amounts to making the
momentous presupposition that the  experience  of  those  words  by  a  pure
consciousness that is neither here nor there is what experience  really  is.
Now, most definitely, that is not the way in  which the two people  actually
involved experienced X's telling me  "Kiss  me."  I  experienced  it  as  an
invitation to me. He did not experience it as an invitation to him,  but  to
me.Because I experienced it in that way, I proceeded to kiss X.  Because  he
did not experience it in the same way I did, he did  not  kiss  X.      This
amounts to saying that experience is  not  just  experience  of  object  but
experience of object by reference to onself in relation to  the  experienced
object. If you are exposed to a furious dog and I see the furious  dog  from
my window without being exsposed to  the  dog,  we  do  not  live  the  same
experience at all. And the difference is not  only  a  matter  of  different
feelings in your case and in mine; rather, my fear of the dog (a  fear  that
you do not experience) is part and parcel of my lived experience  that  will
terminate in my fleeing away from the dog, or perhaps in my kicking the  dog
to make it flee away from me.  This  self-referentiality  of  experience  of
object to the subject is constitutive of experience as we actually live  it.
To put the same in other words, all experience is experience  of  object  by
reference to the experiencing subject. This is why we do not experience  all
the cars we see rolling around us at a given time as threatening,  but  only
the car rolling toward us. Therefore, to say that  the  person  who  is  not
exposed to the car rolling toward me lived the same experience I did,  is  a
bad mistake. He experienced as a witness what  another  subject  experienced
as an agent--which is a very different way  of  experiencing,  that  is,  of
living an experience. Thus, we  may  say  that  the  first  mistake  of  the
medievals'  was  to  equate  the  agent's  experience  with  the   witness's
experience and to privilege the witness's experience over the  agent's.  But
they went a step  further  and  made  a  second,  perhaps  graver,  mistake.
Indeed, they  identified  the  witness's  experience  with  the  reflector's
experience  and  reduced  the  former  to  the  latter.  Thus,  second,  the
witness's experience is not the same as the reflector's  experience  How  do
they differ?
      To begin, whay do I mean by "reflector"? A reflector is  a  "thinker,"
that is, a  person  for  whom  the  "object"  is  not  primarily  object  of
experience  but  object  of  thoought,  object  to  be  thought  about.  The
reflector, in other words, does not function either as  an  agent  or  as  a
witness, but as a thinker. Both the agent and the witness  are  involved  in
direct  experience,  though  in  the  different  ways  indicated  by   their
respective names. The reflector, however, has no other involvement with  the
object than thinking about it;  he  takes  the  experienced  exclusively  as
object of thought. In the case of the agent and the witness, the  object  is
experienced by reference to  their  respective  exposure  to  it--the  agent
being himself exposed to the furious dog, and the witness not being  himself
exposed to it. In both of the
se cases we are in the sphere of exposure, where distances unfold  from  the
experiencing subject toward the object, and the distances are  distances  of
exposure of the subject to the object and of  the  object  to  the  subject.
This relationship of exposure is  not  to  be  found  in  the  case  of  the
thinker. For the thinker the object is object only insofar as it  is  object
of thought, object to be thought about. Take, for  instance,  a  student  of
Euclidean geometry. For the student of geometry, the circle before his  eyes
is not object of experience, does not function as object of experience.  The
circle is neither far nor near the student, neither small nor big. In  fact,
a sufficiently concentrated student would be  able  to  do  his  work  on  a
purely imagined circle instead of tracing a circle  on  a  piece  of  paper,
because the circle here functions merely as object of thought.  Another  way
of putting the same: there is no reference to
the subject here, for the  suybject  is  neither  potentially  nor  actually
exposed  to  the  object;  here  the  object  does  not  function,  nor   is
experienced as, object of exposure; it is merely object  of  thought.  Right
now, I am functioning as a thinker. I am thinking about  a  dog  that  barks
furiously at a guy exposed to it, and about another guy who could have  been
exposed to the furious dog but happens to be out of its reach, watching  the
dog from the safety of a window. For me the dog is pure object  of  thought,
and I could as well do my thinkiing about a furious dog if I am  seeing  one
without being exposed to it and if I am  imagining  the  furious  dog.  What
distinguishes the witness from the reflector is that the  witness  functions
in the same world as the dog, but happens not to be  exposed  to  its  fury,
whereas the reflector is, so to say, in an ideal world in  which  there  are
no possible distances of exposure from himself to the dog,  in  a  world  in
which objects appear only as to be thought about, as objects of thought.  We
may see this more clearly by means of an example. A  chemist  is  performing
an experiment with sulphuric acid. Of course, he can be burnt by  the  acid,
and he will be careful not to touch the acid,  for  he  is  exposed  to  the
acid. In this sense, he is an  agent  and,  as  such,  he  manipulates  very
carefully the acid. But  for him, as  a  chemist,  the  acid  is  object  of
thought, much as the  circle  is  object  of  thought  for  the  student  of
geometry. If the chemist describes his experiment to his students,  he  will
not describe what went on by reference to himself--to  his  having  been  in
danger of being burnt by the acid and having escaped unharmed--but in  terms
of the acid as object of thought, as object  about  which  his  students  of
chemistry have something to learn.
      Now,  I  said  that  the  medieval  theologians  confused  the  object
experienced by the agent exposed to it with  the  object  about  which  they
thought when they theologized,  that  is,  when  they  were  functioning  as
thinkers not exposed to the object. But in order to understand this we  have
to make a detour.
      The fact that the student of Euclidean geometry works  with  a  circle
drawn on a piece of paper does not prevent him from finding  the  properties
of all circles and of being later  able  to  calculate,  for  instance,  the
surface of a real circular piece of land thanks  to  what  he  learned  from
considering the circle as object of thought.  The  same  is  valid  for  the
chemist who works with sulphuric acid, taking it as a mere  instance.  What,
therefore, could be wrong with the medievals working  with  the  concept  of
God, and then assigning to the real God the properties of their concept,  of
their object of thought? To answer this  question,  it  will  be  better  to
think of the case of the chemist, for the chemist who works  with  sulphuric
acid does not work with a pure concept but with real sulphuric acid.
      Since the chemist's cares are taken care of, the chemist's  engagement
as an agent is neutralized so that he becomes a  pure  observer-thinker  and
sulphuric acid becomes mere object of observation-thought for  him.In  other
words, the chemist does not have to be concerned about  how  he  or  anybody
else has to respond to the action of the acid. In this he differs  from  the
witness, who is himself not concerned as an agent, but who  understands  the
situation in which another agent is threatened by a furious dog in terms  of
that  agent's  concerns.  "Concerns,"  therefore,  structure  the  witness's
understanding, and rightly so, for the agent's activity takes place  in  the
realm of exposure. Indeed, we  may  say  that  distances  in  the  realm  of
exposure are distances of concern defined in terms of the relative power  of
the participants. The  chemist,  however,  by  virtue  of  his  position  as
observer-thinker, does not function in the realm of exposure as  far  as  he
himself is concerned, since he  is  not  exposed,  or  has  neutralized  his
exposure to, the acid. In this he is  like  the  witness.  But  there  is  a
significant difference. The sulphuric acid, unlike  the  man  threatened  by
the dog,  is  not  subject  of  exposure,  cannot  function  as  subject  of
exposure, cannot have any concerns. To attempt to  understand  the  activity
of  the  acid  within  parameters  of  concern  would  amount  to  a   basic
misunderstanding. No chemist does that. Clearly, however,  the  God  of  our
faith is unlike sulphuric acid. God is and functions as subject of  concern;
never as a thing.If we neutralize or simply ignore our exposure to  Him,  we
can make Him into object of thought, as we can make a character in  a  story
object  of  thought,  but  only  as  witnesses  of  His  real  or  imaginary
interaction with others, or as agents who project their  future  interaction
with Him. This means that God cannot possibly be understood but  within  the
realm of exposure with parameters of concern.  Unlike  anybody  or  anything
else, God has only a proper name.
      Against this background we  might  understand  better  the  medievals'
mistake. Under Aristotle's influence, the medievals made God into object  of
thought, because real knowledge is in  judgment,  not  in  perception.  This
presupposes that the agent's faith consists itself in a judgment,  that  is,
that the reflector's knowledge is the same as  the  faithful's  faith.  Now,
the reflector is not himself exposed to God, he is thinking about God.  More
concretely:  the  reflector  thinks  that  Abraham  assented   rather   than
consented to his being called by  God  to  sacrifice  Abraham.  Furthermore,
this move by  the  medievals  implicitly  privileges  as  knowledge  with  a
capital K the reflector's knowledge. This so-called knowledge terminates  in
pure assent to something understood and expressed in a  proposition;  it  is
neither understanding incarnated in an action in response, nor does it  have
any relationship to action in response. By the same token the  God  to  whom
Abraham responds has been converted into an object like the  sulphuric  acid
of the chemist, that is, into mere object of thought in view of assent.  God
has "properties" as sulphuric acid does; not traits of  character.  And  God
is as little subject of exposure and cares as little  for  us  as  sulphuric
acid cares for the chemist.
      Can we render still more precise the mistake of the medievals'?
Let us try. The medievals' text was the Bible, which itself  is  written  by
someone who functions as a witness, who therefore understands  in  terms  of
exposure. But the medievals read the  Bible  with  Aristotelian  spectacles:
not as the narrative of a witness  but  as  a  series  of  statements  of  a
thinker's. The medieval theologians took what they did  when  they  assented
to those statements on the basis of God's authority for  what  the  faithful
involved in the narrative did when they consented to God.  In  other  words,
the medieval theologians took the concept of God in view of assent  for  the
God to whom the faithful involved in the narrative consented.
      "Statements of a thinker." Let us clarify this expression  I  used  in
the above paragraph. To  follow  Lonergan,  confronted  with  an  object  of
thought, the thinker first "understands," that is, conceives  a  concept  of
what the object is or does; then "judges", that is, assents or  dissents  to
his as yet merely hypothetical  expressed in the concept, depending  on  his
grasping or failing to grasp sufficient  reason  for  assenting.   The  only
thing a thinker can do is assent or dissent, once he  conceives  a  concept,
for his only point is to understand correctly. And the only thing  a  thiner
can do before understanding is  to  try  to  understand,  because  his  only
relation to the object is that of a  mere  thinker  to  object  of  thought.
Because the thinker is confronted with an object of  thought,  much  as  the
geometer is confronted with a drawn circle or the painter with  a  landscape
to be painted, neither the thinker himself nor anybody else  is  exposed  to
the object. In other  words,  the  object  functions  simply  as  object  of
thought, and the thinker functions simply as a thinker, not as an  agent  or
even as a witness. Now, when the medievalsw read the Bible, they took   what
the Bible said for object of thought. Most certainly, however, when  Abraham
was called to sacrifice Isaac, he was not related to God as a thinker to  an
object of thought; he was exposed to God. This is why Abraham did  not  take
God's words for a statement; he heard them as an order  to  be  obeyed,  and
his faith consisted in obeying them. Abrahm did not assent to the  statement
that God had called him; he believed in God calling him. We might  put  this
in another way. The medievals believed that God  had  called  Abraham;  they
assented to that on the basis of God's  authority  under  whose  inspiration
the author of the  biblical  book  had  written.  But  surely  they  already
believed in God, in the sense of consenting to  God,  of  being  willing  to
live by God's authority. Otherwise, why would they assent to what  God  said
through the biblical author? Thus, their willingness to  to  live  by  God's
authority, that is, their consent to God, preceded and made  possible  their
assent to what the biblical author had stated. Well, Abraham's faith in  God
was a consent of the same kind the medievals'  was,  whereas  the  medievals
imagined that Abraham's faith was of the same kind as their  assent  to  the
statement that God had called Abraham. Hence, for the  medievals,  Abraham's
consent was just an act of the will preceded by and  grounded  upon  on  his
assent to what God had stated.

      But there is another important point to be  considered.  The  biblical
author of Genesis wrote a narrative, not a series of  statements  about  God
and about Abraham, whereas the medievals interpreted or understood  or  read
the narrative as a  series  of  statements.  I  turn  now  to  this  second,
monumental mistake of the medievals', the mistake so brilliantly  denounced,
but insufficiently tracked back to its medieval origins, by Hans W. Frei.
      The author of a novel does not lie when she writes "John was still  in
bed when he heard someone knocking at the door." The  reason  she  does  not
lie is not  that  the  whole  thing  is  imaginary  rather  than  real  and,
therefore, that it never happened; it is, rather, that the novelist  is  not
affirming anything. The author is  narrating.  Of  course,  a  narrator  can
narrate something imaginary or something real or  that  he  takes  for  real
rather than imaginary. But, by the very nature of the narrative genre,  when
a narrator narrates a narrator is not making statements.
