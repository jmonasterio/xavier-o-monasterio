NARRATIVE AND STATEMENT

I maintain that in the XIIth-XIIIth centuries the semantics of narratives got lost and was substituted by the semantics of statements, of which Aquinas's Summa is a prime example. I further maintain that, as a result , the God of whom philosophers speak has little to do with the God people believe in and to whom they pray. In other words, the God of philosophers is not a God who can be adored and prayed to. There may have been before--St Anselm might be an example--precursors of the new direction, but the direction itself became institutionalized and made canonical only in the above mentioned period with the birth of Scholastic theology by which the role of philosophy became defined.

Aquinas's Summa is constituted by a series of questions to each of which corresponds an answer. The answer takes the form of a statement suported by evidence, preceded by counter-evidence and followed by reasoned dismissal of the counter-evidence. Fundamentally, therefore, the Summa is constituted by a series of argued statements that express Aquinas's considered judgments and are meant to elicit intellectual assent on his reader's part.

Now, one of my claims is that Aquinas's theology differs from the theology of the Fathers, that a fundamental epistemological shift has occurred in between under the allure of Aristotle's idea of science. What do I mean thereby? Wherein lies the difference?

Aquinas's purpose is primarily theoretical. By this I mean that he addresses exclusively the intellect of his readers and is meant to terminate in assent. If Aquinas's reader assents to each of Aquinas's theses, Aquinas has been fully successful. If the reader of one of the articles of the Summa assents to the thesis defended by Aquinas in the article, nothing else is left for the reader to do but to move to the next article and repeat the process.

Aquinas himself, the author of the Summa, is a believer, that is, a member of the Christian community, one of the faithful. This precedes his writing of the Summa . In other words, Aquinas does not come to faith as a result of his arguments. As explicitly avowed by him, he does not believe because of the arguments but on God's authority. Furthermore, he does not bracket his faith while he is writing the Summa . He is a Catholic theologian looking for understanding of his faith and working on the basis of his faith. And he is addressing other Catholic theologians like himself, as evidenced by his constant use of the argument of authority--the authority of the Bible, of the symbola fidei and other ecclesiastical documents, and of the Fathers of the Church. It is true that on occasion--for instance, concerning God's existence as a praeambulum fidei--he argues exclusively from reason, not from faith, and that he often leans on the authority of the Philosopher. But it is no less clear that the context in which these arguments belong is theological, not philosophical.

It would seem, therefore, that, except for his being more systematic and all-embracing in his theological project, there is no fundamental difference between Aquinas and, say, Augustine, in the latter's theological works. Is that the case? If not, again, wherein lies the difference?

It is true that both Aquinas and Augustine theologize from their faith. Aquinas's theologizing, however, to the difference of Augustine's, is mediated and shaped by a systematic theoretical infrastructure that is fundamentally Aristotelian. Augustine's theologizing, by contrast, is not underpinned by any definite, systematic theoretical infrastructure but directly by his practical experience of the world as a Christian, as a member of the Catholic Church . The unity of Augustine's vision as a theologian, of his self-understanding and of his understanding of the world, comes directly from his faith and is expressed in The City of God wich presents the universal history within which Augustine finds himself and everything else situated. The unity of Aquinas's vision is theoretically mediated by Aristotle's epistemology, is expressed in Aquinas's Summa , and implicitly defines the way in which Aquinas as a thinker understands himself and everything else. To realize this it suffices to refer to Aquinas treatment of a few fundamental notions, all of which are conceived and defined by him in reference to his Aristotelian epistemological framework.

To begin, his notion of faith. Faith is defined as intellectual assent. Why? Because the truth of the Christian faith is a given for Aquinas as a Christian, and he has been persuaded by Aristotle that truth is an exclusive predicate of the intellectual act of judgment. Such Aristotelian persuasion

makes it unavoidable for Aquinas to conceive of faith as an assenting judgment. Again, for Aquinas as a Christian it is a given that faith does not rest on intellectual evidence but on God's authority. How, then, will he place the knoweldge of faith within the Aristotelian conception of knowledge, which seems to exclude whatever does not rest on intellectual evidence? By assimilating the judgment of faith to the scientific judgments with which Aristotle deals in terms of the certainty of both.

Since the truth of the judgment of faith rests ultimately on the intuitive evidence of God, and since the assent of the faithful rests on God's authority, the certainty of faith is of a superior quality to the scientific knowledge of which Aristotle speaks.

What about revelation? Well, since faith is an assenting judgment, and since the judgment of faith is not based on the  evidence of its object, that is, of the reality to which the judgment refers, it has to be assent to God's statements about that reality, which themselves express God's intuitive judgments. This prescribes what divine revelation has to be, namely divine statements that convey God's knowledge, which the faithful appropriate by assenting to them on God's authority. Accordingly, revelation is a doctrine (a series of theses) taught to us by God, and the divine statements to which the faithful assent extend and complete the Aristotelian map of knowledge which was bound to be incomplete by the fact that Aristotle did not enjoy the benefits of divine revelation.

By the same token, the task of the theologian is defined by analogy to the Aristotelian scientist. The latter's task consists in coming to know whatever is to be known on the basis of compelling evidence, in ordering what is so known in terms of self-evident principles and deductive conclusions to render it intelligible and certain as a whole, and in resting on the blissful contemplation of it. Now, because of the authoritative rather than evidential character of the knowledge of faith, the theologian cannot aspire in this life to evidential fruition of the object of faith; this is reserved for the after-life. The theologian's task, nonetheless, is analogous to the Aristotelian's scientist's task. It consists, at least ideally, in coming to assent explicitly to each and all of God statements, in ordering what is so known in terms of principles and deductive conclusions to render the revealed intelligible and certain as a whole, and in resting in the blissful contemplation of it while wating for the after-life in which the evidential contemplation of the whole will obtain. In addition, to achieve the maximum of intelligibility possible, the theologian, doubling this time as a philosopher who proceeds on the exclusive basis of natural reason, will demonstrate evidentially those truths which are preambula fidei, that is, those points on which revelaled knowledge and purely human knowledge touch each other and which are necessary both for the full intelligibility of the whole constituted by the revealed and the naturally known and for the demonstration of the perfect harmony and complementarity between the knowledge of faith and natural knowledge, such as the existence of God, the existence and spirituality of the soul, etc. Clearly, the completion of such monumental task is, so to say, a merely regulative ideal, given its enormous extension and complexity. In addition, the theologian is called to defend the revealed truths within the community against the attacks or doubts of those of its members who by ignorance or malice have strayed from the truth, and to defend the revealed truths

against the attacks of those who do not share the Christian faith.

How does all this differ from the traditional approach? Did not Augustine discuss with pagans in their own terms? Did he not theologize about his faith and on the basis of his faith? Would he not have granted that there is a significant difference between philosophy and theology by virtue of both their object (revealed and not revealed) and their subject (believer as believer and knower as knower)? The answer is "yes but." And the "but" refers to the different context in which Augustine worked both as a knower and as a believer. As a knower, Augustine worked from the standpoint of the exposed agent and on the basis of his Christian exposure to the world, whereas Aquinas as a knower worked from the point of view of Aristotelian speculative or contemplative, unsituated reason. Correlatively, as a believer, Aquinas worked as if faith were primarily intellectual assent, whereas Augustine worked on a very different comprehension of

faith related to his being an exposed agent . My purpose here is to unpack these differences.

The Aristotelian shift of paradigms

There is an important shift between Aris's metaphysical works and his ethical works. He himself remarks at least two differences between them. First, the latter, because of the nature of their object , do not allow for epistéme or essential knowledge, but only for knowledge for the most part based on observed but not unexceptionable regularities. Furthermore their object is particular and, therefore, contingent. Second, the knowledge with which the ethical works are concerned is practical in that it results in action rather than in judgment. This seems to be connected with the fact that practical knowledge is phronetic rather than epistemic, that is, is a matter of prudence, of practical intelligence, rather than of pure understanding. To these differences I shall return after mentioning a different one that seems to me significant.

In the metaphysical works their object is referred to in the third person, so to say. In other words, their object is something observed by the speaker who judges. Clearly, the essences or forms are not a matter of observation but of understanding after a process of abstraction. Nonetheless the essences or forms are essences or forms of something observed. Thus, the soul is the soul of an observed plant, or animal, or person. Without this relation to an observed (or observable) the soul would be the form of nothing. In addition, the word "observed" is here to be understood in a broad sense, for it includes not only plain observation but, in certain cases, introspection. For instance, intellectual acts. (As Sartre would say, here Arist is uniting his consciousness to the an observed body which is nobody's body).

By contrast, the ethical works are based on experience in the first person. This is not precise enough, though it seems correct. For, as I just said, Aris uses what we might call his internal experience (for instance, of intellectual acts) in his metaphysical works. The difference lies elsewhere. What he uses in his ethical works is his experience as an exposed agent confronted with a world to which he has to respond and in which he has to act as a historically situated particular agent . His Ethics is a persuasive deliberation among agents, given their self-understanding as exposed human agents, as to the kind of persons they ought to be. Furthermore, his purpose is not explanatory (to confer intelligibility to a manifold) but to lead to right action or at least to persuade the agent to become the right kind of agent insofar as it depends on him or her rather than on his or her circumstances. It is in this sense that there is a shift between the metaphysical (and perhaps the scientific) works and the ethical or practical works.

Yet, Aris does not seem to be aware of the significance of this shift , since he makes phronesis into an intellectual virtue, considers its acts as acts of practical knowledge , and compares the qualities of episteme to those of phronesis as if they belonged under the same genre, whereas I think that the categories apply only to the observed (observed, objectified being ) and only considered as an explanandum . By the way, I think that Aquinas suffers from this fundamental mistake and is as innocent of it as his philosophical master.

Why do I think that this difference between the metaphysical and the ethical works is, in a certan way, more important than the differences sensed by Aris? Because Aris mixes the objectivist and the existential paradigms in his very articulation of the differences he grasps, thereby subordinating the existential to the objectivist. For Aris, after all, the supreme form of knowledge is epistéme, knowledge of the universal and necessary, not practical knowledge or knowledge of particulars . This will show if we consider the way in which Aris distinguishes epistéme from pratical knowledge, particularly in book VI of the Nicomachean Ethics , and contemplative happiness and practical happiness at the end of his work. But first we need the data to be gathered from the Nicomachean Ethics. A basic reference book, in this connection, is: René Gauthier et Jean Yves Jolif, L'Etique a Nicomaque: Introduction, Traduction et Commentaire (Publications Universitaire, Louvain 1970, deux tomes).

Having gone through the Aristotelian data, I sense that my point is well taken. Aris does not fully realize that deliberative desire or desirous deliberation is not a species of "knowledge" or an inferior, degraded, less perfect "knowledge" but something totally different . Perception itself is desirous, because it is exposed agent's perception. Actually, the 'perception' of the exposed agent is always interested, related to success and failure, and future-oriented. Aris subordinates fronesis to sofia because (see 1141a20) he is a Platonist, a foundationalist who needs everything grounded on evident principles which philosophia (in the sense of metaphysics) furnishes. He does not see that deliberative desire is not "knowledge" (which comes from Plato) in any sense. This is why in the Ethics Aris rightly dismisses the "puzzles" of theoretician-philosophers which have no place in his inquiry. The Primus Motor of the Metaphysics (see Gauthier p. 494 and 496) cannot be in any way ultimus finis since it cannot be object of desire, only of the "knowledge" reserved to the philosopher. To say, therefore, as Gauthier does (p.               ), that metaphysics and ethics 'visent' the same object but from different perspectives is nonsensical. Either God enters as object of desire in the Ethics, as our practical end, or there is no place for God at all, only for the God of philosophers. Same confusion in Aquinas betwen God as the object of faith (that is, of knowledge, of assent) and God as Ultimus Finis.
