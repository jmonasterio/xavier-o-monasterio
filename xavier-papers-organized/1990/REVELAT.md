The notion of revelation

The discussion of the medievals was about the sacra pagina, the Bible. Their reflection on faith and revelation is, therefore, mediated by the Bible. As they understood it, the Bible says something, and what the Bible says is true because it is God who says. Revelation, in that case, is a matter of statements made by God, and faith cannot be but intellectual assent to what is stated by God because God says it, not because  it is evident.

Pieper remarks that, at some point, revelation has to rest on knowledge. By knowledge he understands vision, evidence that compels assent. Apparently, what he has in mind is another statement assented to because its truth is seen. While that may be the case of God, the revealer--and indeed, that is the way Aquinas took it to be, for what God reveals is his intuitive knowledge--and might be understood to be the case of the people to whom God 'revealed' what is written in the Bible, it is not the case of the people whom the Bible presents as the direct recipients of God's revelation. Abraham's faith could not be of the type considered by the Scholastics. And if we share the faith of the father of the faithful, our own faith cannot be of the type considered by the Scholastcs, either.

Abraham's faith was a consent, rather than an assent. It was not assent, for there was nothing to be assented to. First of all, because God was not, at least not most of the time, stating anything. When he ordered Abraham to sacrifice Isaac, he was not

stating anything; he was ordering. Neither did God expect Abraham to assent to the (not made) statement that God had ordered something to Abraham, nor did Abraham's faith consist in assenting to such a statement, but in obeying God's command. Had a spectator been present to the scene and heard God's command, he would have made with assent the statement that God had ordered to Abraham. But he would not have thereby shared Abraham's faith, for that is not what Abraham's faith consisted in, but in ob-auditing God's command. Commanding is commanding, not stating that one commands--although when used in the present "I command you" it may sound as a statement.- Furthermore, even the statements made by God to Abraham are no mere statements of fact, purely informative pieces; they are either directly a call for action, for response, or conditions of possibility for such a response. Much like "Your mother is on the phone, John. The phone is in the upstairs room." The first part is a call for the action of John talking to his mother on the phone. The second part is a condition that makes possible the action called for.

The case of the girl in Camus' The Fall might help us. The girl cried for help. Although the cry, as a linguistic action, had conditions of possibility (such as a bridge over the river, without which the cry would have been unintelligible), it was not a statement informing of those conditions; it was a cry for help that took place under those circumsances. Nor was it a statement by the girl that she was crying for help. It was a cry for help incidentally addressed to Clamance, who just happened to be there at the time. As a competent linguistic agent, Clamance heard it as a cry for help, not as a statement, as a piece of neutral, impractical information. Clamance refused to help while realizing that he ought to help. By contrast to Abraham, Clamance did not have faith, at least not the faith of the father of the faithful.

What then is faith? A consent. A consent is practical, not theoretical; its proper incarnation is an action-in-response, not a mere assent. It is not purely intellectual, as assent is. But it is nonetheless also intellectual, in that it involves an implicit understanding of the caller and of what one is called to. But it is not merely intellectual, for it involves the willingness to respond to the caller because of who the caller is, even when one does not understand the appropriateness of what one is called to do. In short, the identity and the motivating power of the caller are inseparable, mutually constitutive: the caller motivates because of what the caller is and does in the particular situation, and the caller is and does what he is and does because of his motivating power. More concretely, the crying falling girl has a motivating power because she is a crying falling girl, much as Children love Santa and wait for him because he is Santa--a kind guy who brings toys to children. He motivates because he is Santa, and if he did not motivate he would not be Santa--a kind guy who brings toys to children. Believing in Santa amounts to loving him and waiting for him.

What then was the Scholastics' mistake? To take as their primary model for faith, not Abraham's faith or their own faith as agents, but what they did when they read the Bible's text. They misunderstood that text as a collection of statements rather than as a narrative. To this they were induced by Aristotle (the logician-metaphysician), for Aristotle's epistemology was itself based on what he did when he thought about reality, not what he did when he interacted with the world. For Aristotle, apart from the fact that he took judgment as the purely intellectual act of a pure contemplator rather than an involved agent, the important issue was not direct but reflective judgment. It was not sufficient for him to know, but to know that he knew--and this necessarily.

What has the above to do with the narrative genre of the Bible? The Bible is not storical by accident, nor can it be transferred into non-storical language. The reason is that only narratives replicate the strucutre of life and can, therefore, give rise to the existential understanding, that is, the consent, in which faith consists.

Let us return to the model of faith taken by the Scholastics . They seem to have confused inspiration with revelation. The Bible was not revealed to the writers; therefore, the text of the Bible is not revealed. But the Scholastics did not realize this. They seem to have thought that God had undertaken to dictate the text of the Bible. What God said the writer wrote. And what the writer wrote they believed, that is, they assented to. Therefore, faith was assent. In other words, they understood the text of the Bible as a unified whole constituted by just one speech act: This is what God states, which expresses what God knows. Now, as I said above, Abraham's faith was not of this type. But nor does the Bible itself conform to this description, for the Bible is not a statement. As someone who gives an order is not stating, but giving an order, so someone who narrates is not stating, but narrating. And a narrative is not to be understood as a statement but as a narrative. A novelist is not making a statement but writing a novel. And the specific speech-effect of a novel is totally different from an assent. Furthermore, a narration is made in specific circumstances and destined to specific people for specific purposes. Thus, the intention of a novel is very different from that of a contemporary book of history. If the narrative is good, the intrinsic speech-effect of the narrative as narrative is likely to obtain; but the circumstantial effects intended by the writer depend on many other factors. Among this factors counts very heavily, when the issue is the historical truth of the narrative, whether or not the hearer is already familiar with the characters and the plot of the narrative and already accepts them as historical rather than storical, and whether or not he trusts the story-teller, as well as the existential implications of the historical truth of the story for the teller and the hearers. Particularly important, too, for our purposes, is whether the story is told in presence (as, for instance, much of the Gospels seems to have been before they were written), or whether it was originally written rather than told. (Think, for instance, of the Nican Mopowa). For, generally speaking, the logic of a written text is very different from that of a spoken text, where both speaker and hearers share the same circumstances. Finally, it is extremely important to realize the difference between lived history and told or written history, for it affects the distinction between lived faith and faith spoken or thought about. A told history is the deed of a direct or indirect witness; a lived history is the deed of participating agents. A told story is a speech act; a lived story is not. A story is told in the past and with knowledge of the outcome; a history is lived in the present and without knowledge of its outcome. In short, the structure of a told history and of a lived history is totally different. Abraham's faith--and ours--is of faith of participants in the making of history. And, mutatis mutandis, so is the faith of the writers of the Bible in general and of the writers of the Gospels in particular. As thinkers, we can neutralize our faith or unfaith (at least to an extent) and discuss it 'objectively.' But as agents we cannot, anymore than Descartes could neutralize his faith in reality as an agent.
