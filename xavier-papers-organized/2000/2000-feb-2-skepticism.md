KURT MOSSER ON NIETZSCHE’S SKEPTICISM

(cf.”Should the skeptic live his skepticism? Nietzsche and classical skepticism, Manuscrito, 1998, April, pp.57-84 ),

- P. 48: Pyrrhonic skepticism is practical, a way of life, whereas academic or Cartesian skepticism is theoretical or epistemological and deals with the existence of the external world. The latter is dogmatic; the avoidance of dogmatism of the former leads to suspension of belief (epoche) and ataraxia.

Pyrrhonic skepticism

– p. 56: Basic Pyrrhonic claim: “For any ASSERTION “ p ” an equally strong ARGUMENT can be made for “not p. ” Because appearance claims are equipollent, isostheneia yields suspension or epoche: No more this than that. In an indirect way epoche gives rise to tranquillity or ataraxia.

- p. 58: The skeptics were hoping to achieve ataraxia BY RESOLVING THE ANOMALY OF PHHENOMENA AND NOUMENA; being unable to do this, they suspended judgment. Then, as if by chance, the ataraxia followed the suspension as the shadow follows the body.

- p. 58-9: Neither pyrrhonic skepticism nor Nietzsche’s perspectivism entail relativism. The relativist says “x is f for me,” the pyrrhonic “”x appears f.” The pyrrhonist rejects the assertoric form. We recognize that it is day and that we are alive; but, unlike the dogmatists, we suspend judgment because these facts are not certain and we confine knowledge to our impressions.

[WHAT IS JUSTLY REJECTED IS THE KNOWLEDGE AND CERTAINTY OF CORRESPONDENCE. SO THE PYRRHONIST ( AND NIETZSCHE with his non-dogmatic perspectivism without ataraxia ?? ) SHARE WITH THE DOGMATISTS THE ASSUMPTION THAT KNOWLEDGE EQUALS CORRESPONDENCE AND CERTAINTY OF IT]. See last two quotes on p. 59.

- p. 60: So both reject dogmatism–whether the assertoric claims the pyrr.’s opponent makes, or the metaphysical assertions about the world as it really is put forth by Nietzsche’s opponents, “the subtle philosophers.”

- p. 60: Diogenes and Nietzsche: Possibility of different phantasiai (appearances) to animals and people. Animals and people react differently to same phenomena.

- p. 61: The pyrr. still presupposes that there is some fact of the matter that escapes humans, whereas N wants to abandon any conception of truth that might ground such a presupposition

and, in doing so, to transcend the fatigue of philosophical laboring in order to put in its place his own perspectivism .

-p. 62: N rejects the picture (of correspondence) holding us captive, and this allows him to philosophize with a hammer in both the constructive and the destructive sense. He agrees with much of the critical aspect of Pyrrhonism, but calls into question the value of tranquillity.

N’s critique of Phyrrhonism

- p. 63-4: While N prefers Phyrrhonians to subtle philosophers, his central conviction is that the epoche and the ataraxia are characterized by a weary acceptance of the way things are or appear to be (Budhism). “Laughing and staying silent–is that the whole of your philosophy?” The Pyrr fail to react boldly to what results from the rejection of dogmatism–still nihilistic and a form of Greek decadence, an ascetic extreme of slave morality, for they accepted the conventions of the society in which they lived.

- p. 65: Montaigne finds himself not flexible enough to accept all conventions, but is a fideist in matters of religion: “Since I am not capable of choosing, I accept other people’s choices and accept the position where God put me.”

- p. 66: Inconsistency of Pyrrh according to N: Unwillingness to remain comfortable with the received truths of the day, and then turn around an claim that one must live with the custom and conventions of one’s society!

- p. 67: The coming philosophers are friends of truth as all philosophers. But traditional philosophers claim their truth to be universal, whereas N: “My judgment is my judgment; no one is easily entitled to it.” Rejection of all convention: xtian slave morality as well as science:

“It is an illusion that something is known [IN THE SENSE OF CORRESPONDING TO] when we possess a mathematical formula for an event is only designated, described, nothing more.” [YES, SOMETHING MORE OR OTHER: TO BE APPROPRIATED PRACTICALLY].

- p. 68: The skeptic’s ataraxia prevents N’s bold transvaluation of all values, his fierce criticism of all accepted values.

- p. 69-70: ON TRUTH: Pyrrhonic and Cartesians sk. assume the correspondence theory of truth, and both say that truth is unreachable [but don’t deny the notion of truth]. - N denies that he is a skeptic. He endorses the skeptics’ doubts but, unlike Montaigne and Hume, goes beyond for he criticizes the very idea of truth. Even the Pyrr. who attacks the entire assertorial form succumbs to dogmatism, namely to custom and convention –“the herd.” Thus the skeptic praised by N is not the Pyrr–a passive nihilist Buddhist without beliefs–but “the skeptic of the future” who will dare to live without truth and certainty.

Can and should the skeptic live his skepticism?

- p. 71:Pyrrho couldn’t : He was frightened by a dog. He attributed this to human weakness.

This was Hume’s argument: You go out at the door, not at the window.

- p. 73: The Pyrrhonist doesn’t believe like the herd, but acts like the herd. For those who believe in truth, even divine revelations would do [this seems to be Montaigne’s case], as long as they guarantee correspondence with the real. But N rejects the notion of truth correspondence. The Pyrrhonists are not free: they still have faith in the truth; they suspend inquiry into such truths.

- pp. 74-75: N: The question is not whether the skeptic can, but whether he should live his skepticism. N shifts from a metaphysical-epistemological inquiry fo a normative one. The skepticism that conforms to custom and convention is as life-denying and Buddhist as the other versions of salve morality–the opposite of the will to power.

- p. 76: To passive nihilism N opposes frohliche Wissenschaft –such a pleasure of self-determination that the spirit will take leave of faith, dancing even near the abysses. Models: Napoleon, Goethe, Alexander, Borgia. The courage to give birth to new goals, new hypotheses, displaying the will to power.

Concluding word

-p. 78: There is a pervasive skeptic strand in N, but “he rejects the conception of truth [and knowledge] that makes Cartesian methodological skepticism, as well as Pyrrhonian, even possible .

- p. 79: Instead N seeks to ground genuine philosophical inquiry (?) in courage, manifested in the will to power and attested by the embrace of eternal recurrence (?) in the face of likely futility.

N.B. For questions to be asked see p.  69, p.74, note 11, and the underlined expressions on p. 69 of this summary.

Important points for me

N brings to a closure the Greek philosophical tradition that began with Socrates.

In his attempt to reform the Greek polis Socrates thought that knowledge as opposed to opinion, that is, scientific knowledge, necessarily true knowledge, was a necessary condition for acting rightly. Virtue is knowledge, and vice is ignorance. This amounted to declaring the absolute primacy of knowledge over action.

Plato seems to have obscurely initiated the distinction between speculative and practical knowledge, but he still believed in the eternal ideas and in the possibility guaranteed by them of

eternal, universal, necessarily true knowledge.

Aristotle definitely distinguished and separated speculative knowledge, which was knowledge of universal and necessary natures and  which could therefore be scientific, from practical knowledge, which was of particulars and which could only be imprecise and approximative. However, he called both branches “knowledge,” which assumes that practical knowledge, too, involves the attainment of truth, though perhaps not for the sake of knowing. Moreover, Aristotle never consummated his independence from Plato, not only because he still believed in necessarily true knowledge, but because till the end he remained at least indecisive as to the superiority of the vita contemplative over the vita activa. In addition, like Plato, Aristotle could never move beyond opinion concerning the knowledge of particulars, though he pragmatically took it for knowledge. To make matters worse, Aristotle confused the knowledge of particulars involved in perception with the knowledge of particulars invovled in matters of action.

As for the respective theories of knowledge of the three above named, one thing is sure: at least Plato and Aristotle distinguished between phenomenon and reality (or thing in itself), and at least inchoatively had a theory of knowledge that involved the correspondence or adaequatio between what is in the intellect or the mind of the knower and what is actually the case. Hence the correspondence theory of knowledge and the notion of truth as known and certain correspondence.

The Pyrrhonians of whom Mosser speaks were, like Socrates, primarily interested in practical philosophy: how to live, and living as people ought to live. It was only with Descartes that the absolute primacy of epistemology-metaphysics began. (Because it waws then and there that the primacy of the speculative over the practical and the definite shift from exposure to contemplation took place).

What is interesting in Mosser’s article is that, after the debacle of epistemology, the connection between knowing and action remains active and unquestioned.
