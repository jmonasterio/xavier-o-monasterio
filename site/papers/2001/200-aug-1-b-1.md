Barth-Kant

The whole interest of my reason, whether speculative or practical, is concentrated in the three following questions: 1. What can I know?  2. What should I do?

3. What may I hope? - The first question is purely speculative... The second question is purely practical... The third question... is at the same time practical and theoretical, the practical serving as a guidance to the answer of the theoretical and, in its highest form, speculative question” (Kant, CPR, pp. 515-516).

      

The only reason I entitle this paper “Barth-Kant” is because its main point originated in

the contrast I discovered between Barth and Kant in “Barth et la connaissance de Dieu” (99-nov-1).By main point I mean the lack of connection in Kant between knowledge and action.

The lesson I learned from Mosser’s “Should the Skeptic Live his Skepticism?” concerned the connection between knowledge –or the lack thereof--and action ( Manuscrito , xxi, 1998). “Pyrrho was frightened by a dog, thus abandoning his fundamental principle of indifference. He found that ‘it is not easy to strip oneself of human weakness’” (p. 71). And Hume in his Dialogues concerning Natural Religion has Cleantes say: “Whether your skepticism be as absolute and sincere as you pretend, we shall learn by and by, when the company breaks up; we shall then see whether you go out at the door or the window, and whether you really doubt if your body has gravity or can be injured by its fall... And this consideration... may, I think, fairly serve to abate our ill-will to this humorous sect of skeptics” (quoted by Mosser, p. 71).

Kant, I think, would find preposterous this sort of refutation of skepticism because in the Critique of Pure Reason he establishes –or, rather, assumes , given the way he conceives knowledge– that knowledge can be understood independently from action and, thus, that there is no connection whatsoever between knowledge and action. For him, knowledge is the deed of a

purely speculative, purely epistemic subject. Purely speculative because the only function of this subject is to attain the “ Objekt, ” the knowable, which by definition is independent from sentiment and desire. Kant would argue that what is dependent on sentiment and desire is not Objekt but, in Feuerbach’s parlance, Gegenstand , that is, invention of wishful thinking, object of desire. Objectivity , therefore, that is, the intention of object of the gnoseological or epistemic subject, excludes desire. By the same token, knowledge necessarily is a purely contemplative intuition of Objekt , for this non-exposed, invulnerable subject has nothing to do, literally. In other words, not only is the Kantian epistemic subject not an agent, but there can be no relationship between the epistemic subject and the subject who acts. Hence the First Critique (from now on FC) , whose subject-matter is the first of Kant’s famous questions –“What can I know ?”-- is complete in itself and independent from the Second Critique (from now on SC) , which deals with the second of Kant’s questions –“What should I do?”-- and the third –“What may I hope?”  

Yet, things do not seem  work quite that way. There is, after all, some relationship between knowledge and action, as Kant is constrained to acknowledge in the SC . For without the knowledge of his freedom, of the immortality of his soul, and of the existence of God as the warrant of the immortality of his soul the agent cannot act rationally as he ought to. Now the possibility of such knowledge had been explicitly excluded in the FC . Hence the appearance of these three items –God, freedom and immortality– in the S C , if not as strictly known (for that would put the SC in contradiction with the FC) at least as objects of rational faith , that is, as necessary postulates of the practical reason.

There is, therefore, a certain tension between the First and the Second Critiques. For if knowledge were totally independent from action, and if the epistemic subject were totally different from the subject who acts, as assumed in the FC, then the agent of the SC wouldn’t require to possess any knowledge at all and, thus, wouldn’t need to postulate as objects of rational faith the items whose knowledge had been denied him in the FC. However, the tension between the two Critiques cannot be resolved without changing radically the conceptions of knowledge and of the epistemic subject on which the FC rests, for if knowledge is what the FC assumes it to be, then there is no possible connection between what can be known or cannot be known and the agent’s action.

But can the Kantian notion of knowledge and of the knower be modified? What would such modification entail?

 

A contrast between Hume and Kant may prove enlightening.

Hume realizes that there is no necessary connection between the sensible qualities and the secret nature of an object. Yet we matter-of-factly make inferences from the former to the latter, so that from objects that look like those we have experienced in the past we expect similar effects and regulate our conduct by those expectations . Is such an inference rationally justified? No, answers Hume, since the connection between the sensible qualities of an object and its secret nature is not necessary; our expectation is simply a matter of habit. Rationally, therefore, I should suspend judgment as to whether the future will replicate the past concerning the action of objects, since I don’t know that such will be the case.

As is well known, this Humean skepticism concerning the validity of the principle of causality, required to guarantee the necessity of the connection, woke Kant up from his dogmatic slumber. Till then Kant had thought that he knew that objects that looked similar to those he had experienced in the past would necessarily act as the objects he had previously experienced had acted, when in fact his conviction was a mere belief that rested on an assumption prompted by repeated past experience and habit. It would take Kant the entire FC to establish the rational foundation for what had previously been a blind assumption, that is, to convert his blind assumption into knowledge.

For his part, Hume had been unable to overcome his philosophical skepticism concerning causality:

My Practice, you say, refutes my Doubts. But you mistake the Purport of my Question. As an agent , I am quite satisfy’d in the Point. But as a philosopher , who has some share of Curiosity, not to say Skepticism, I want to learn the Fundation of this Inference” (D. Hume, An Enquiry concerning Human Understadning and Other Essays , edited by Ernest E. Moisner (Washington Square Press, Inc., New York 1963, pp. 47-48). My emphasis.

In a moment I shall return to the distinction introduced by Hume between himself as a knower and himself as an agent. Before that, however, I want to underline that both Hume and Kant are in agreement that knowledge involves the grasp of rational necessity. In this they remain true to the old Platonic tradition, for Plato adamantly maintained the distinction between “opinion” or “belief,” however well grounded but not necessarily true, and “knowledge,” which is by definition necessarily true because its objects is what is necessarily so. Kant was grateful to Hume because Hume helped him to realize that what he had previously taken for necessarily true had been merely an opinion. And the progress of Kant over Hume was to establish that certain judgments –and that was the case of the judgments of causality– could be necessarily true though they were not a priori but arose from experience. In short, unlike Hume, Kant overcame his Skepticism as a philosopher.

Now, to return to the passage in which Hume introduces the distinction between himself as a philosopher and himself as an agent, he rejects the agent’s argument: “My Practice, you say, refutes my Doubts.” The reason is that the fact that the expectations of the agent are confirmed in practice, this fact involves no necessity that the case will continue to be such in the future. Hence, lacking necessity for his inference, the agent takes for knowledge what is a mere opinion. Thus, Hume is a skeptic as a philosopher . On the other hand, however, he is not a skeptic as an agent . On the contrary, as an agent he is quite satisfied in the point. But what, precisely, does this mean? To answer this question we have to go to the other passage of Hume’s writings, already cited,  in which, interestingly enough, he seems to make his the agent’s argument. Indeed, in the Dialogues concerning Natural Religion he has Cleantes say:

Whether your skepticism be as absolute and sincere as you pretend, we shall learn by and by, when the company breaks up; we shall then see whether you go out at the door or the window, and whether you really doubt if your body has gravity or can be injured by its fall... And this consideration... may, I think, fairly serve to abate our ill-will to this humorous sect of  skeptics” (Quoted y Mosser, p. 71).

Obviously, the humorous sect of skeptics is constituted by those who think as philosophers. And what is humorous about them is that they claim that they do not know whether the door is really a door rather than a window and whether they would fall to the street if they go out of the room through the window rather than through the door,  yet they are afraid of falling to the street if they go out through the window and consistently choose going out of the room through the door rather than through the window. So, they don’t know but they know , since they consistently act as if they know, and this discrepancy is what Hume finds humorous.

But is it true that these people know and don’t know? Or is it, rather, that two different conceptions of knowledge are involved here? Indeed, according to one conception, knowledge involves rational necessity (and universality),  excludes sentiment and desire,  focuses exclusively on the characteristics of the object to the exclusion of the way in which the subject is affected,   appeals exclusively to the intellect or reason, and makes knowledge independent from action (though not necessarily action independent from knowledge). The exact inverse is true , according to the other conception.  

Curiously, Kant seems to have entertained both conceptions of knowledge –the first in the FC and the second in the SC– which allowed him to develop in the FC a view of knowledge that had nothing to do with action, the second in the SC, where he found himself constrained to require the kind of ‘knowledge’ represented by the rational faith in his freedom, in God and in the immortality of his soul. Hence the tension between the two Critiques . For the FC establishes or assumes that knowledge is an interest of speculative reason, yet in the SC a knowledge that is not knowledge is required by practical reason in order to act as it ought to. In the FC knowledge excludes desire, whereas in the SC action is impossible without the ‘knowledge-expectation’ that the desire for happiness will be satisfied.  

As for Hume, insofar as he conceived knowledge as contemplation by an invulnerable subject, he agreed with Kant that there is no possible passage from knowledge to action, from the intuitive contemplation of what appears to the epistemic subject to “the secret powers” of the object, so that the disposition to respond to the object in one way rather than another was rationally unjustified, just a matter of automatic expectation based on past experience. Nonetheless, as shown by his refutation of skepticism, he found in the action of the agent in response to the grasped object a validation of the agent’s knowledge of the object.      Hume, too, entertained the two conceptions of knowledge, one as a philosopher and the other as an agent. But at least his Cleantes is fully “satisfyd,” not in the speculative certainty that the future will necessarily resemble the past, but in the practical expectation that allows him to make prudent decisions.

Why the distinction between speculative and practical knowledge?

This seems to be the fundamental question to be asked.

One important point: Knowledge, not when lived by the agent but when reflected upon for study, involves both object and subject. Now, “object” is supposedly what is, the real, independently from the subject and the subject’s personal interests or desires. Hence the speculative conception of knowledge with its corresponding notion of pure objectivity. To this should be added the separation between subject and object implicit in the gnoseological relationship, with the consequent requirement of necessity for the certainty and validity of knowledge. Notice that the necessity is twofold, as indicated by the words certainty and validity: certainty on the subject’s part, to guarantee that what appears in the mind is the case with logical or rational necessity; validity on the object’s part, to guarantee that what is is so by nature or essence, that is, necessarily, not contingently or by chance.    

In all the above, the needs and desires of the subject as well as the way in which the subject is affected by the object are not only absent but positively excluded as interfering with objectivity. Something similar, by the same token, applies to action and the agent, for which, in the absence of desire and need, there is no conceivable room within epistemology. In other words, the separation between the knower and the agent, from which the distinction between speculative and practical knowledge originates, is already involved in the very attempt of considering knowledge as object of study, which is what philosophers do. Indeed, philosophy began as foundational epistemology to determine with absolute certainty the truth or the falsity of what people believed to be the case.

The models of knowledge for the philosophers were mathematics and logic, precisely because they were interested in necessity. In both cases (at least before Kant, concerning mathematics), the knowledge is formal. Whatever the content, certain entailments are necessarily valid by virtue of their form. Now this doesn’t apply to the case of perception, where the particular content is the object and where, as Hume realized, even the regularities are purely factual or contingent, not intelligible.

Moreover, when knowledge is considered as object of study, for the one who studies it the object is merely contemplated object (though for the needful agent-perceiver in the midst of the world that is not the case). Moreover, for the philosopher interested in necessity, the form or “formal cause” is what counts, and this is purely intelligible and, thus, again, merely contemplated object. Indeed, neither is the perceiver  exposed to the action of the perceived object, nor is the perceived object within the practical reach of the needful, desirous subject. Thus, obsessed as they were with necessity, universality, intelligibility, and objectivity, on the one hand, and, on the other hand, blind as that made them to the contingent, particular, sensible, and attractive or fearful, the Greeks  failed to realize that their distinction and separation between knowledge and action doesn’t hold --at least not in the case of perception. For we are tied to the world by desires and fears and, under certain conditions (as shown by science and technology) we can act instrumentally, unlike the bees of Marx’s example.

This latter model of ‘knowledge’ is the one Aristotle follows for practical knowledge in the Metaphysics , when he attributes knowledge to animals by virtue of their prudent-adaptive action, and even more clearly in De motu animalium when he points out that what makes the difference between speculative and practical knowledge is desire. (Notice that Aris introduces desire as something in addition to knowledge, as if desire didn’t modfy the’object,’ as if the ‘object’ were he same whether desired or not desired However Aristotle is confused , for he continues to believe that both kinds of knowledge fall under the same genus, since he calls both by the same name, and not only doesn’t he discard the notion of speculative knowledge modeled after mathematics and logic, but continues to subordinate practical to speculative knowledge as its necessary condition. That he confuses the two is clear from, among other things,  the fact that he attributes to animals “material knowledge,” which is not practical but speculative and which is the condition of possibility for the prudent action of animals. Most unfortunately, we inherited from Aristotle his confusion. To this day, we have been unable to leave behind the idea of speculative knowledge with its notion of truth as certain correspondence. Consequently we have continued to view practical knowledge as a variety of knowledge, that is, of speculative knowledge, since speculative knowledge is the princeps analogatum.

But do people, in their dealings with the world in which they find themselves, actually possess “knowledge” as conceived by philosophers? And, if not, shouldn’t the princeps analogatum of knowledge be what Aristotle called “practical knowledge” -- knowlege because he still conceived it, most un-Platonically, as knowledge? I say most un-Platonically, because Plato, who remained true to speculative knowledge as the model, would have vehemently rejected the idea that practical knowledge was knowledge at all, and because Aristotle himself could classify it as knowledge only by introducing the bastard notion of material knowledge in animals.

Practical knowledge as the princeps analogatum (See my NOV3.99)

Practical knowledge is purposeful action guided or informed by desire. It is not automatic because it is not inherited but presupposes experience. Any action that happens to satisfy a desire will automatically tend to be repeated when the desire is present and predominant. This is the beginning of all purposeful action in both animals and people, though important differences between them appear later. Without experience, and memory connected with desire, no purposeful action of the ‘cognitive’ type that interests us is possible.
