Essay. 6                          Chantilly, May 24, 1993
                       Applying Frei to my issue
      Few people if any in the recent past have contributed more to create
a space for and render academically respectable the narrative, though not
necessarily religious, reading of the Bible than Hans Frei. Nor was it his
intention to reinstate the religious reading of the Bible in theological
schools. Indeed, while acknowledging that Barth's biblical exegesis is "a
model of the kind of narrative reading" he himself has in mind to retrieve
from its long eclipse, he equally acknowledges that in Barth's hands
"theology becomes an imperious and allegiance-demanding discipline, and he
might well have rejected out of hand the external treatment it receives in
the present essay" (p. viii).
      As a Christian, I am as unhappy as Barth would be both with the
prevailing absence of Christian allegiance in the theological discipline as
well as with Frei's systematic separation of narrative reading from
religious reading. And this for the very reasons that moved Frei to rescue
the loss of the narrative reading of the Bible. For I do think that
rescuing the narrative meaning of the Bible while neutralizing its
religious meaning is in practice equivalent, in the present post-
Enlgightenment climate, to ratifying the very notion of a rational, neutral
understanding which underpins and made inevitable in the first place the
loss of the narrative reading regretted by Frei.
      Be it as it may, pressured to do so by the conditions of the grant
that made this essay possible, I will follow here Frei's strategy of
separating the narrative from the religious reading of the Bible. The story
I will consider is not only a former episode of the story studied by Frei
but, in my opinion, the historical origin of the latter. It is, in other
words, my conviction, that what happened in the field of biblical
hermeneutics in the seventeenth and the eighteenth centuries to which Frei
devotes exclusive attention was just logical development of the
epistemological shift that took place in the eleventh and twelfth centuries
which I will study here.
      Perhaps it would be more precise to say that I will study the moment
at which the narrative reading  was suppressed together with the religious
reading of the Bible, so that the latter remained possible only as a
devotional and rationally unjustifiable oddity that had no place in
academia.
      The religious reading is not a species of a genus called narrative
reading, as Frei implicitly assumes. It is a different, original reading,
correlative to an original way of being in the world. The Aristotelianism
of the thirteenth century amounted to a theoretical redefinition of the
world itself and, thus, of our being in it, which brought about a
'scientialis' practice corresponding to the structure of the redefined
world. The modus scientialis and the modus Christianus are mutually
exclusive because they imply, and correspond to, two different ways of
being in the world and two different worlds.
      The above expresses my comprehension of the shift that took place in
the Middle Ages. But in the present work I will only be laying the
foundations for the explicitation amd justification of such a
comprehension. Thus my task is more modest. What I want to show in the
first place is that a shift took place on the level of the reading of the
Bible--from historialis to scientialis (by virtue of which theology and
philosophy became complementary). This is easily done and not very
original; Chenu did it many years ago. I will take my basis on his work. My
perspective, however, differs significantly from Chenu's. Enters Frei. For
Chenu, the change was a net progress, for it made possible the scientialis
(and superior) reading-understanding of the Bible, while leaving untouched
the vulgar, narrative reading proper to faith. For mei, by contrast--if my
extrapolation of Frei views to the Middle Ages holds water--the scientialis
reading of the Bible is incompatible with the narrative reading, because
they project the reader into different and mutually exclusive practical
fields of action.
