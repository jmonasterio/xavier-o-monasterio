For, then, what is said (independently from who, to whom, and under what circumstances says it) is taken, if true, for what is the case, for the real. Aquinas and rationalism in the Catholic intellectual tradition

There has been in the last forty years or so a rescuing operation to absolve Aquinas from the rationalism wrongheadedly projected onto him by a post-Cartesian pseudo-Thomism that had become an orthodoxy in Catholic intellectual circles. Salient names in this rescuing operation are de Broglie, Wolterstorf, Kenneth Konyndyk, Ralph McInnerny, Eugene Ferdinand Rogers and--more recently and less directly--John Inglis, among others. De Lubac, who himself was rather ambiguous in his evaluation of Aquinas' rationalism, was certainly unambiguous in his castigation of what passed for orthodox Thomism when he began his theological career (cf. his review of his writings).

No doubt, with his radical principle of "clear and distinct ideas" Descartes introduced an evidentialism and a foundationalism which were later codified by Locke. This would lead in time to the extreme rationalism of the Enlightenment, which was obviously incompatible with the Christian faith in that it subordinated the aceptability of divine revelation to the unchecked demands of human reason as ultimate arbiter.

Needless to say, such extreme rationalism can hardly be attributed to Aquinas, since he explicitly maintained that the core of divine revelation, represented concretely by the articles of faith, was inaccessible to human reason left to its own devices, and even after revelation surpassed human comprehension and remained mysterious, inevident. Evidentialism, therefore--that is, the view that only what is evident is worthy of unconditional assent--was most definitely not espoused by Aquinas.

What then is to be made of Aquinas' view of the ability of human reason to come to know God's existence without the aid of divine revelation, and of the fact that he himself offered his famous five proofs for God's existence? Even in this terrain the suspicion of rationalism is dispelled by Aquinas's own explicit statements that the unconditional character of the assent of faith doesn't rest on the arguments of reason, which are complex and byond the ken of most Christians, but on God's authority, and that in case of apparent contradiction between rational evidence and divine revelation Christians should give their unconditional assent to the latter, ignoring the former.

Given these facts, Wolterstorf could persuasively argue that Cartesianism constituted what might be conceived as a paradigmatic shift, so that the theistic arguments underwent a radical epistemological transformation in their migration from Aquinas' pre-Cartesian conception of natural theology to the post-Cartesian evidentialist apologetics inaugurated by Locke. In this way, Aquinas was neatly absolved from the rationalism of which post-Cartesian Thomists suffered while innocently believing that they were walking on Aquinas' steps.

My purpose here is to probe the validity of the Woterstorffian absolution of Aquinas, for I am far from convinced that it is valid. I do this with the intention of preparing the ground for a genuine purification of the Catholic Western tradition from the rationalism by which it is still afflicted and by which, in my opinion, it has been badly harmed.

Was Aquinas a rationalist?

As befits a post_Cartesian, Locke was an evidentialist. Rational assent, according to him, is assent prompted exclusively by evidence and proportionate to the evidence by which it is prompted and supported. From this it follows logically that in order for the assent of faith to be rational and fully and genuinely certain, what is divinely revelaed would have to be totally evident. Now, as already suggested, Aquinas would have rejected such radical evidentialism as incompatible with the Christian faith, for that would evacuate mystery from revelation and would turn the the assent of faith Christians give to divine revelation into mere assent of reason.  Now, Wolterstorff rightly argues that Aquinas was not a rationalist in this sense, for he was not an evidentialist. Surprisingly, however, neither was Locke himself an evidentialist in matters of faith, for he explicitly excluded the domain of faith from his rule of evidence. Wolterstorff admits this. Now, granted, in Locke's case, given his notion of rationality as proportionality between assent and evidence, and between evidence and certainty of assent, the admission of lack of evidence in the case of the object of faith would imply logically that a rational being ought to deny the object of faith, or at least be incapable of assenting unconditionally to it. Of course, the majority of Locke's successors--and this was fairly typical of the Enlightenment, l'âge des lumières , the epoch of evidence--were more logical than Locke, and rejected the Christian faith as irrational, as undeserving of assent by a rational being. One may, therefore say, that the latter were fullfledged rationalists, whereas Locke, who opened the way for them with his evidentialism, was not himself a rationalist, since he balked from applying his evidentialism to the domain of faith. But now the interesting question arises. If the post-Cartesian Locke could remain a Christian, though he was a rationalist at heart and should logically have given up his faith, cannot Aquinas's case be similar? In other words, cannot Aquinas have been a rationalist who inconsistently excluded the domain of faith from the very strictures of rationality to which he gave his allegiance?

There is an obvious difference between Aquinas and Locke. Whereas the latter has an explicit theory of rationality but no developed theory of the act of faith, Aquinas has both. Thus, by attending primarily and taking as primary Aquinas's views on the act of faith, Wolterstorff has an easy job showing that Aquinas was not an evidentialist. After all, the texts are clear. Indeed, Aquinas explicitly affirmed that the core of divine revelation exceeds the human capacity to understand and remains inevident even after divinely revealed. Aquinas explicitly maintained that the assent of faith, unlike the assent of reason, is not prompted by the evidence of its object, but the intellect has to be moved by the will. Furthermore, Aquinas also affirmed categorically that the assent of faith is unconditional, in spite of the lack of evidence of its object. All these Thomistic texts, however, as well as a number of other texts that could be brought in support of the same point, concern the domain of faith. And in all these points Aquinas differs from Locke, but not because Aquinas is a pre-Cartesian and Locke is a post-Cartesian, but because Aquinas has an analysis of the act of faith and Locke doesn't. But the question is whether in the domain of reason Aquinas was or was not a rationalist, as Locke certainly was. After all, it may have happened, as apparently it happened in Locke's case, that Aquinas' attachment to his faith prevented him from being consistent.         

Wolterstorff reply would be, I assume, that post-Cartesian rationalism is essentially inspired by evidentialism and foundationalism, and these are notions for which there is no room within the pre-Cartesian paradigm of rationality. In other words, Aquinas's very conception of rationality was paradigmatically different from that of the post-Cartesians. But was it really?

Of course, there are important epistemological differences between Aquinas and Locke, but not all differences, even if important, are proof of a paradigmatic chiasm. After all, there are important epistemologcial differeences between Descartes and Locke, too. For instance, Descartes was a rationalist whereas Locke was an empiricist, and the way in which each of them understood "ideas"and consequently "evidence" was vastly different. Yet, in Wolterstorff's view, this does not detract from the fact that Locke was an evidentialist and a foundationalist and, thus, that he worked within the Cartesian paradigm, which suppopsedly was radically different from the pre-Cartesian paradimg within which Aquinas worked. But why the epistemological differences between Aquinas and Locke are deemed paradigmatic, so to say, and the differences between Locke and Descartes non-paradigmatic? Why the migration of the theistic arguments from Aquinas to Locke implies an epistemological rupture, whereas the migration from Descartes' rationalism to Locke's empiricism is paradigmaticall non-significant?

Wolterstorff maintains that Aquinas was not an evidentialist.

Now, obviously, if Aquinas was an evidentialist, his evidentialism cannot be the evidentialism of an empiricst of Locke's kind, for Aquinas was not an empiricist but an intellectuaalist and a rationalist. For Aquinas, who was an intellectualist by contrast to a sensorialist, knowledge began with the senses but was a deed of the intellect and consisted concretely in an intellectual act of judgment. Furthermore, for Aquinas, who was a rationalist by contrast to an empiricist, evidence was not a matter of intuition of sensible qualities, but of intellectual grasp of sufficient reason for assenting. Granting these differences between Aquinas and Locke, the question remains whether Aquinas was an evidentialist in matters that fall within the domain of reason rather than of divine revelation.

Now, as just indicated, for Aquinas evidence consists in the intellectual grasp of sufficient reason for assenting. And when the reason for assenting is necessary, the assent is necessary and unconditional, that is, the affirming or denying judgment necessarily follows and the assent is unconditional. However, when the reason for assenting, the evidence, is not necessary, the assent is not necessary and cannot be unconditional, but conditional, as befits a probable object of knowledge.

Given this, and even granting that Aquinas was not an evidentialist in matters of faith, it seems that in matters that didn't fall within the domain of faith he was no less an evidentialist than the post-Cartesian Locke. Indeed, exactly as

Locke, he would have said that the rationality of the assent and the consequent certainty of knowledge depends on the evidence and ought to be proportionate to the evidence, so that unconditional assent without necessary evidence, much more without any evidence, would be irrational.

This, in fact, was the Aristotelian epistemological framework by reference to which Aquinas analyzed and came to understand the special case of revelation and faith. This, in its turn, led him to the very unAristotelian distinction of two domains: the domain of reason and the domain of faith. Let us briefly follow Aquinas in his exploration of the latter domain.

Divine revelation for Aquinas is that portion of God's own knowledge which God shares with us people because it is necessary or useful for our salvation. Insofar as it is revealed to us it is meant to be known by us and become our own knowledge within the limits that are possible, given our human nature and the actual conditions under which we live here on earth. Now, precisely because what God reveals is his own knowledge and is to become known by us, and orecisely because for the Aristotelian Aquinas knowledge is a matter of intellectual asent, Aquinas claims that God couldn't possibly have revealed Himself to animals or stones. Clearly, then, Aquinas understands divine revelation by reference to his epistemology, that is, to his very Aristotelian conception of knowledge and human reason.

However, the idea of divine revelation as understood by Aquinas would be not only alien to Aristotle, but simply absurd. Therefore, Aquinas will move on his own in this terrain, but without losing sight of his Aristotelian understanding of knowledge and reason, and using that understanding as his guiding thread for purposes of contrast. Thus, realizing that because the knowledge to be appropriated in the case of divine revelation refers in its central core to God's own essence, which surpasses the human knowing capacity, he claims that the assent of faith is not prompted by the evidence of its object but is motivated by God's authority, and conequently that in this particular case the intellect has to be moved by the will.

While carving a place for the domain of faith, Aquinas certainly limits, but doesn't suppress, the domain of reason.

Indeed, as is well known, he also defended the power and the autonomy of reason within its proper field, and he attributed to human reason the capacity to prove, that is, to come to know with demonstrative certainty, those revealed truths he called " praeambula fidei, " the most important of which truths is God's existence. Actually the so-called "Thomistic synthesis of faith and reason" implies the recognition of the two domains, of the autonomy of reason within its own domain, and of the harmony between the knowledge reason is capable of acquiring using its own powers, and the knowledge acquired through divine revelation. The two domains of knowledge complement each other, and their harmony shows precisely in the common field of the praeambula fidei . Indeed, these are both, divinely revealed so that they not be ignored even by the simplest of the faithful, as well as accessible to human reason so that they may be known by the intellectually developed Christians wsithout the assistance of divine revelation.

Now, this restrained field of the praeambula fidei in which faith and reason meet, for the truths comnprised by it are both divinely revealed and accessible to human reason without revelation, is par ticularly important for us, since we are trying to determinewhether and to what extent Aquinas was a rationalist in matters concerning to reason. Now, here we are in a field constituted by truths that are both revealed and rationally knowable, where therefroe the strngth of Aquinas' synthesis between his rationalism in matters of reason and his should Isay fideism in matters of revealtion should particularly shine.

Now, we already know that Aquinas was a rationalist in matters not pertaining to faith in that he was, like Locke, an evidentialist. But there is another aspect to Locke's poast-Cartesian rationalism which Westentorff identifies as his foundationalism. Indeed, given that not all truths are evident or equally evident, given that certain truths are directly evident, while others are only indirectly evident through inductive or deductive reasoning, and that much of the knowledge an individual possesses depends irremediably on authority rather than on personally verified evidence, one wonders on the rational acceptability of the whole corpus of knowledge possessed by an individual. Now Descartes, realizing this fact, concluded to the rational validity of a corpus of knowledge constituted by truths not all of which were the result of direct evidence but which was founded on certain clear and distinct ideas which because of their evidence couldn't be doubted. In this sense, Descartes was a "foundationalist," and Locke followed Descartes in his foundationalism. For Locke, a chain of knowledge is as strong as its foundational links, and for a chain of knowledge to be rationally acceptable it suffices if itsmost important and foundational links are evident.

This brings us back to rationalism and the Thomistic praeambula fidei .

,This name is itself significant, for it indicates Aquinas' conviction that the possibility of knowing these truths without the help of divine revelation constituted a sort of transcendental condition for the assent of faith to be rational rather than totally blind or purely arbitrary and, therefore, unacceptable to intellectually adult human beings.  True, he granted, that the acceptance of divine revelation was possible and meritorious without the rational knowledge of the preambula fidei . That, indeed, was admittedly the case with children as well as with most Christians who are not intellectuals and have no grasp of the proofs for God's existence. Yet the possible influence of Aquinas on the importance that anti-fideism came to acquire in the Catholic theological schools, which culminated with the famous or infamous Syllabus in the 19th century, the foundational role that natural theology and fundamental [foundational?] theology came to play in the theological formation of priests, as well as the unique place occupied kwithin the Catholic intellectual tradition by the discipline of philosophy as the necessary and appropriate antidote to fideism, particularly in the American colleges that were more independent than their European counterparts from the strictures of the official system of education, cannot be easily dismissed.

Most certainly, then, as already suggested, Aquinas was not an extreme rationalism of the post-Cartesian style, and he would have found such rationalism incompatible with the Christian faith. But would he have been as clearly opposed as Westertorf suggest to the ambiguous rationalism that prevailed in Catholic theology--a rationalism that at the same time rejected fideism and was willing to assent by faith, however provisorily, to 'truths of faith' that were apparently contradicted by 'truths of reason," as was the case concerning heliocentrism?  in short, can he, who introduced the notion itself of the praemabula fidei , who explicitly affirmed the harmonious complementarity between reason and faith and the impossibility of conflict between the truths of faith and the truth of reason, from which the Catholic opposition to fideism originated, can he be simlpy absolved of all contamination with rationalism, so that the rationalism which prevailed in the Catholic intellectual and theological tradition (represented by 'orthodox Thomism) until Vatican II may be considered an obvious deviation from Aquinas' own Thomism?

My thesis is that it cannot . To make persuasive my case, however, would demand a multilayered or multifaceted work. Here I will limit myself to study one particular, and particularly important, aspect of what I see as a powerful indicator of Aquinas' rationalism, namely, his explicit theory of the Scriptural language as presented in the first question of the Summa Theologica .         Aquinas on Scriptural language

In article 8 Aquinas asks WHETHER Sacred Doctrine is argumentative. It seems that not , for arguments are to be put aside where faith is sought , and "in this doctrine faith especially is sought." And then, arguments from authority seem unbefitting its dignity, for they are the weakest form of proof; and arguments from reason sem unbefitting its end, because faith has no merit in those things of which human reason brings its own experience. He answers that " this science doe not argue in proof ot is principles, which are the artcles of faith, but from them it goes on to prove something else , as the Apostle argues from the resurrection of Christ in proof of the general resurrection." "Sacred scripture, since it has no science above itself, disputes argumentatively only if their opponent will make some concession; but if he concedes nothing, it can have no dispute with him, though it can answer his arguments . Replying to obj. 2 , the argument from authority based on divine revelation is the strongest. "But sacred doctrine also makes use of human reason, not indeed to prove faith (for thereby the merit of it would come to an end, but to make clear other things that are set forth in this doctrine... Hence it is that sacred doctrine makes use also of the authority of philosopshers in those questions in which they were able to know the truth by natural reason, but uses these authorities as extrinsic and probable arguments, but properly uses the authority of the canonical Scriptures as a necessary demonstration...

In article 9 Aquinas asks WHETHER holy Scripture should use metaphors . It seems that not , for to proceed by the aid of various sismilitudes and figures is proper to poetic, the least of all sciences . And, then, " this doctrine seems to be intended to manifest the truth,' "but by such similtudes truth is hidden ." But Aquinas anwers that " it is natural to man to attain intellectual truths through sensible things, because all our knolwledge originates from sense. Hence in Holy Scripture spiritual truths are fittingly taught under the likeness of material things. It is also befitting that spiritual truths be expounded by means of figures taken from corporeal things, in order that thereby even the simple who are unable by themselves to grasp intellectual things may be able to understand them . Replying to obj. 1, poetry uses metaphors to produce a representation, for it is natural to man to be pleased with representations. But sacred doctrtine uses metaphors as both necessary and useful . To obj. 2 , divine revelation does not allow the minds to whom revelation has been made to rest in the likenesses, but raises them to the knowledge of intelligible truths ... Hence those things that are taught metaphorically in one part of Scripture, in other parts are taught more openly . To obj. 3 , it is clear that these things are not literal descriptions of divine truths, which might have been opened to doubt had they been expressed under the figure of noble bodies . Second, because it is more befitting the knowledge of God we have in this life. For what he is not is clearer to us than what He is .

In article 10 Aquinas asks WHETHER in Holy Scripture a word may have several senses . It seems that not , "for many different senses [hISrical or literal, allegorical, tropological or moral, anagogical, and parabolical, or, to follow Augustine, according to history, etiology, analogy, and allegory] in one text produce confusion... But Holy Scripture ought to be able to state the truth weithout any fallacy . But he answers that it is in God's power "to signify not by words only (as man can also do) but also by things themselves. So whereas in every other science things are signified by words, this science has the property that the things signified by the words have themweselves also a signification . The former is the first sense, the historical or literal . The latter is called the spiritual skense, which is based on the literal and presupposes it . This spiritual sense has a fourfold division... Since the literal sense is that which the author intends, and since the qauthor of H Scriputre is God, who by one act comprehends all things by his intellect, it is not unfitting if, even according to the literal sense, one word in H Scripture should have several senses . Replyint to obj. 1 , in H Scripture no confusion results, for all the senses are founded on one--the literal--from which alone can any argument be drawn , and not from those intended allegorically. Furthermore, nothing necessary to faith is contained under the spiritual sense which is not elsewhere put forward clearly by the Scripture in its literal sense . To obj. 2 , a sense is called history, as Augustine expounds, whenever anything is simply related . To obj. 3 , the parabolical sense is containefd in the literal, for by words things ane signified properly and figuratively . Nor is the figure itself, but that which is figured, the literal sense . When Scripture speaks of God's arm, the literal sense is not that God has such a member, but only what is signified by this member, namely, operative power. Hence it is plain that nothing false can ever underlie the literal sense .
