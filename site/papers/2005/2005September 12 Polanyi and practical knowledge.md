2005-September 12- Polanyi and practical knowledge

POLANYI AND PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE

We attribute knowledge to those animals whose activity, because it is adapted to new circumstances, shows that they  ‘intentionally,’ rather than mechanically, pursue their ends through their actions. So, at least in t heir case, we implicitly acknowledge that desire or need is an intrinsic constitutive of what we call knowledge. But there is also  such a thing as mechanical teleological action to satisfy a need. Such action is inherited – an automatic, biologically programmed reaction to a sensed or perceived feature of the environment that functions as a sign for the reaction. And that is the beginning of what in us, humans, will become knowledge when combined with language.

“Sensed” or “perceived” tacitly rather than objectively because, conjoined with need or desire, it functions as a sign FOR the reaction, not as an object. Since the sign is tacit, as all signs are, because t hey are signs FOR action, not signs OF anything, the subject’s awareness cannot be said or enunciated in words, for it literally has no object. It is not meant to terminate in linguistic enunciation but in action.

This is what the Platonic-Aristotelian epistemic tradition couldn’t grasp. Since its adherents approached epistemology with the preconception of  the knowing mind as a mirror, and of knowledge as a correct or faithful representation or reproduction of the object mirrored, they had to subordinate the action in response to the object to the correct representation or knowledge of the object. This subordination of action to knowledge is clearly manifested, for example, in Searle’s conviction that I can know that you ask me to lend you my pen and not lend it to you – hence knowledge does not depend on action– , but I cannot lend you my pen if I do not know that you ask me to lend it to you –hence action depends on knowledge. As another example, the same subordination is manifested in Aquinas’s matter-of-factly assertion that in order to pursue an end one first needs to know it.

The main problem with this subordination was pointed out by Hume regarding the impossibility of the passage from an objectively known state of affairs to an action, for no objectively known fact can motivate an action. As to the validity of the conception of knowledge as the correct representation of reality, Kant raised the never solved objection that, if such is indeed the case, then it is impossible to know whether or not we know, for there is no independent means of knowing whether or not the correspondence between the representation and reality  obtains in any particular case.

Of course these two unsolved problems did not prevent the scientific undertaking to go on. What is even more curious is that this development took place with the blessing of the two philosophers who raised the unanswered objections against the epistemological system. Indeed, however illogically, Hume explicitly excluded from his skepticism the sciences that dealt with mathematical relationships and the  sciences that affirmed facts on the basis of experiment, though these experimental sciences involved an act of faith in the resemblance of the future to the past. ^(F1) No less illogically, Kant continued to affirm the validity of the synthetic a posteriori judgments of the experimental sciences when supported by “intuition,” in spite of the impossibility of knowing whether they actually corresponded to the  thing in  itself.  Typical, in our days, of the same illogical exclusion by philosophers of the conclusions of the experimental sciences from their very objections against the epistemology they presuppose is Rorty’s sleight of hand through which

he continues to give his allegiance to those conclusions, in spite of the fact that their truth presupposes the very correspondence he systematically attacks, since he offers no alternative to that view of truth.

Of all the mentioned philosophers. Hume has the distinction of having suggested, at least in an indirect way, a way out of the epistemological quandary. Indeed, he distinguishes between philosophical or theoretical skepticism and human or practical skepticism. As a thinker, one can be a skeptic, but not as a human agent. No philosopher, no matter how skeptic he may be in matters of knowledge, ever attempts to get out of a second-floor room through the window. Be a philosopher, concludes Hume, but above all be a human being!

Hume’s epistemological alternative, though far from fully developed, consists in the primacy of action over knowing. Knowing is subordinated to action, not the other way around –as it has consistently been assumed by the Platonic-Aristotelian tradition. This is the alternative I want to explore.
