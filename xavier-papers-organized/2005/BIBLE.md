      ON BIBLICAL NARRATIVES AND THEIR INDISPENSABILITY
      "What modern philosophy understands by 'faith'  is  what  properly  is
      called an opinion, or  what  is  loosely  called  in  everyday  speech
      believing. Christianity is made into a doctrine, and the next stage is
      to comprehend this doctrine, and that is what philosophy does. On  the
      whole, this is quite right, in case Christianity were a doctrine;  but
      since it is not that, it is a crazy proceeding."  (Soren  Kierkegaard,
      The Offence).
      N.B. Given that Christianity is nolt a doctrine, what
      is it?

Frei on the shift from religious to scientific (objective),  from  narrative
to factual reading of the Bible
      Frei is clear as to the fact that a momentuous shift  in  the  reading
of the Bible took place in the seventeenth-eighteenth  centuries,  and  that
the latter reading has become paradigmatic, which entails as  a  consequence
the delegitimation of the traditional Christian reading of the Bible. He  is
much less clear as to the exact nature of the shift, for his  insistence  on
retrieving  the  narrative  genre  regardless  of  its  applicative  meaning
indicates, I think, a blind spot in his thinking. But about this later.  Let
us begin with one of Frei's multiple characterizations of the shift.
      "In the earlier Protestant interpretive tradition... the  literal  and
      religious meaning of  texts  and  the  judgment  about  their  factual
      accuracy had been wholly united" (The Eclipse of  Biblical  Narrative,
      p. 56).
      The situation changed radically in the 18th century in  the  following
way, if my understanding of Frei's position is  correct.   (1)  The  literal
sense of realistic narratives was separated from the  religious  meaning  or
significance  of  those  texts  (in  other  words,  from  their  applicative
meaning). This presupposes two things: that  what  the  narrative  literally
says is different and independent from what it means religiously  (in  other
words, that there are two meanings:  literal-theoretical  and  applicative),
and that this literal meaning  can  therefore,  and  should,  be  determined
independently of the significance the text has for Christians  (whereas  the
applicative meaning depends on the literal). In  other  words,  the  literal
sense of the text is the same for, and in principle equally  accessible  to,
Christians and to atheists.
      (2) Indeed, according to this  view,  realistic  narratives  in  their
literal  sense  mean  either  actual  facts  (in  principle  verifiable   or
ascertainable  in  total   independence   from   the   biblical   narratives
themselves), or religious doctrines or ideas or views  (whose  acceptability
is to be judged  by  the  extra-biblical  standard  of  rational,  universal
religion).*  [*This  independence  of  the  "facts"  from   the   narratives
presupposes that realistic narratives do not refer to an  inhabitable  world
(Ricoeur) with distances of exposure, but rather, as  scientific  statements
supposedly do, realistic narratives  refer  to  brute  facts  or  states  of
affairs without significance in  themselves,  and  that  the  world  of  the
speaker and the hearer, of the writer and the religious reader, is  a  world
made up of brute realities].      (3) The religious meaning or  significance
of realistic narratives, their "applicative" sense* [*Can brute  "facts"  or
objective "ideas" have applicative sense, when there  are  no  distances  of
exposure  and  no  inhabitable  world  ?],  depends  on  the  literal  sense
understood as defined above. If perhaps  it  could  go  beyond  the  literal
sense, it could in no case conflict with it. [Notice  that  the  applicative
meaning of historical events--not of  "facts,"  which  have  no  applicative
meaning, no significance--is  different  from  the  applicative  meaning  of
doctrines].
      To fully understand the above, let us apply it to a concrete  example:
the narrative of Abraham's sacrifice.
Abraham's Sacrifice
      In the post-critical age, the story of Abraham was  held  in  abeyance
before the eyes of the experts before its meaning was determined. [The  text
itself  became  "object  of  study,"  instead  of  something  to  be   heard
(transcended)  applicatively  in   practical   response].   That   is,   the
grammatical meaning of the words was understood  by  all  those  capable  of
reading the language in which the text was written, but what the  particular
narrative literally (that is, actually)  meant  remained  to  be  determined
through purely intellectual study.
      FURTHERMORE,  IT  WAS  TAKEN  FOR  GRANTED  THAT  NARRATIVES  MEAN  BY
REFERRING. MORE CONCRETELY, A NARRATIVE MEANS THAT TO WHICH IT REFERS,  that
which 'it says,' as statements supposedly do. Now, did Abraham's story  mean
(that is, refer to) real events, or  to  a  (religious)  idea  or  doctrine?
Since the narratives we are considering are by  definition  realistic,  that
is, history-like, the question cannot  be  answered  on  the  basis  of  the
realistic  character  of  the  text,  for  in  that  case  all  history-like
narratives would refer to actual facts. Theoretically, the  question  should
be settled on the basis of independent  evidence,  that  is,  of  historical
traces of the facts referred to, or the lack  thereof.  However,  given  the
remoteness in time of the events narrated, there is  practically  no  chance
of finding historical  traces  close  enough  to  the  time  of  the  events
(documents, monuments, etc.). Thus the question can be settled only  on  the
basis of the verisimilitude of the events narrated  and  of  the  conditions
prevailing at the time the biblical narrative was written.  Now,  given  the
cultural conditions prevailing at the time that the  story  of  Abraham  was
written and what we know from extra-biblical sources  about  the  people  of
Israel, there is every reason to think that the author  or  authors  of  the
narrative meant the events as real, for there is a strong tradition of self-
identification by the people of Israel as the children of  Abraham  and  the
inheritors of Yaweh's promise made to Abraham. If so,  however,  or  so  the
mythophiles who  represent  the  'modern'  attitude  argue,  the  author  or
authors and their intended audience obviously were primitive,
unsophisticated, naive people, for the events narrated in the  case  we  are
considering are utterly unbelievable to us,  sophisticated  people.  Indeed,
apart from the unlikeness of God speaking in such familiar way  to  a  human
being, the story portrays a cruel, undignified, odious God who asks  Abraham
to kill his own son, which is unacceptable to any civilized  mind.  To  make
matters worse,  the  story  continues  with  the  absurdity  of  a  God  who
arbitrarily picks up Abraham and his descendants  as  the  beneficiaries  of
His promises, to the exclusion of all other  peoples.  Obviously  this  does
not square at all with the universalism that  characterizes  the  monotheism
of Deism, that is, of the notion of God as conceived by reason.  Conclusion:
the story of Abraham, if meant as real, is false.  Hence,  either  believers
grant that the story does not mean real  facts  and  find  the  doctrine  or
religious message actually meant by the story (which is  what  Strauss  does
in the last, apologetic chapters of his  book  on  Jesus),  or  they  should
consider the story as false and give up their faith in the Bible.
      A few observations. (a) The 'scientific' or 'post-critical'  hermeneut
who reasons in the  manner  just  described  assumes  that  his/her  way  of
reading (Wittgenstein would say the interpretive  "game"  he/she  plays  and
into which he coopts those who until then had read the  Bible  in  the  pre-
critical way) is normative, that is, is the indisputably valid way to  reach
the true meaning of a narrative text.
      (b) Actually,  the  interpretive  game  played  by  the  post-critical
hermeneut and into which he/she coopts  the  believer  is  heavily  'loaded'
with  the  hermeneut's  own  culturally-shaped  presuppositions.   What   is
acceptable or unacceptable as fact, what God can  and  cannot  possibly  do,
what is  believable  or  unbelievable  amounts  to  what  the  post-critical
hermeneut finds to be so on the basis of  his/her  culturally-shaped  notion
of experience. Had this hermeneut belonged to the culture within  which  the
story of Abraham was produced, he/she would have found  the  story  as  true
and Yaweh's actions as believable as the authors of the story did.  In  that
case, however, the only reason  why  the  post-critical  or  scientifically-
minded hermeneut rejects the historicity of the events narrated  is  because
he/she is a member of a different culture, which he/she automatically  takes
as the valid  standard,  with  the  typical  a  priori  superiority  of  all
colonizers.
      (c) Yet--and this observation  is  particularly  important--the  post-
critical hermeneuts are honestly convinced that  the  interpretive  game  as
they conceive and play it is normative and should  therefore  be  played  in
that way by any rational, reflective person.
      Now, it is on this very point, I think, that Frei's  thesis  is  meant
to bear principally. For Frei thinks that  it  can  be  persuasively  argued
(not  demonstrated)  that  not  only  is  the   post-critical   reading   of
narratives, the biblical narratives included, not normative, but that it  is
positively wrongheaded. And this on the basis that realistic  narratives  in
general, that is, history-like narratives in general, not only the  biblical
narratives, are not meant to  be  heard,  nor  can  be  heard  appropriately
without misunderstanding them, in the way the post-critical hermeneuts  read
them and claim that they are to be read,  for  narratives  do  not  mean  by
referring either to brute facts or to ideas.+
      As for the significance of Frei's thesis, my  impression  is  that  he
attempts  to  rescue  the  appropriate  meaning  and  reading  of  realistic
narratives in general in order to relegitimize the Christian reading of  the
Bible which was automatically rendered  insignificant--that  is,  eclipsed--
when the post-critical reading took possession.
      But why does Frei  neutralize  the  issue  of  the  actual  historical
character of the Gospel narratives and focuses exclusively on their history-
like character? Because, I  think,  his  purpose  is  not  to  convince  his
readers that  the  Christian  reading  of  the  Bible,  which  involves  the
substantive historicity of the Gospel's narratives, actually is the  correct
interpretation (in which case he would have to convert  them,  if  they  are
not already Christians), but only that the Christian reading  of  the  Bible
which  has  been  eclipsed   by   the   post-critical   reading   has   been
illegitimately discarded. (N. B. I am not sure that  the  neutralization  of
the historicity of the Bible attempted  by  Frei  can  be  achieved  without
loss, for it amounts to the neutralization of the  applicative  meaning.  Of
course, he would claim that the story of Abraham  can  be  "followed"  as  a
story is  supposed  to  be  followed,  that  is,  with  concerned-curiosity,
whether or not one considers it substantially historical, which is  correct.
I doubt, however, that the story of Abraham so  read  remains  the  biblical
story, for it is meant to be heard applicatively.  In  other  words,  Frei's
strategy has the unavoidable result of converting  the  applicative  hearing
and meanining into something subjective and extrinsically added to the  text
by the hearer (and  perhaps  by  the  author).  This,  however,  contradicts
Frei's position about the author's intention being  manifested  or  embedded
in the text itself. See The Eclipse, pp. 280-281).*
The pre-critical reading of biblical narratives
      Why did Christians before this revolution read the  Bible  in  such  a
different way? The answer that came to me spontaneously: because  they  were
not  confronted  with  a  written  text,  much  less,  a  text  composed  of
statements to be believed, that is, intellectually assented to (as the post-
critical  hermeneuts  thought  they  themselves  were   amd   assumed   that
Christians were), but with the narrated events themselves told by  a  living
voice and as to be responded to.* [*Only Christians could have  written  the
Gospels, and their intention, obviously embedded  in  the  Gospels,  was  to
confront their readers with the narrated events as to be responded  to,  not
with facts to be assented to. However, only those who hear  religiously  the
Gospels  are  so  confronted.  Others--and  this  is  the  point  of  Frei's
neutralization of the history question--should  be  confronted,  though  not
necessarily exposed to, at least with a particular world inhabitable to  the
credulous authors though not to them. At any rate, since it is a  matter  of
realistic narratives, they should not  be  confronted  with  "facts"  to  be
dissented  from,  for  narratives  are  not  statements;  "facts"  are   the
correlates of "misunderstood statements"].
      The Christians of which we are talking did not read  a  written  text;
they heard God's voice, speaking to them,  narrating  to  them  through  the
sacred authors in order to expose them to the  dramatic  events  which  they
did not themselves witness but by which they  are  affected,  and  to  which
they have to respond, for the events are unfinished and  ongoing,  and  they
demand their cooperation. "The Word of the Lord," "God is the author of  the
Scriptures," that is, the 'interpealing' oral narrator.  As  Hauerwas  says,
"God is a character in a story."
      Furthermore, Christians did not hear the  Bible--remark  that  hearing
implies a living, somewhat effectively or practically  present  speaker  who
addresses--as a statement or a series of statements to be assented  to,  but
as storically structured, as opening the very dramatic  space  within  which
they and everybody else moved and breathed.
A theory of meaning
      "...'Subject-matter' hermeneutics was influenced by the identification
      of a theory of meaning with theory of knowledge,  which  meant  (among
      other things) that the sense  of  a  fact-like  statement  was  almost
      automatically  turned  into  its  ideal  or,  more  likely,  ostensive
      reference" (Frei, The Eclipse, p. 279).
      By the beginning of the seventeenth century, the Bible  had  become  a
(written)  text  to  be  deciphered  and  understood,  as   supposedly   any
linguistic  communication  is.  Rather  than  referring  practically  to  an
interacting God, the Bible became a text composed of  statements  about  God
that referred theoretically to a notion or idea of God that may or  may  not
be acceptable to the reader and that  would  in  the  best  case  result  in
assent to the judged-stated.*
      [* "(él, Juan) dice que yo (Pedro) me  vaya."  "(él,  Juan,  dice  que
Pedro se vaya." "(él, Juan) dice que la casa de  Pedro  se  está  quemando."
"(él, Juan) le dice a Pedro que su casa  se  esta  quemando."  All  this  is
reported by an overhearer. "Dice" = "says," = "utters  meaningful  words  or
sentences" = "words or sentkences that any competent user  of  the  language
in which they are pronounced would understand." From the  descriptive  point
of  view  of  a  contemplator-overhearer,  this  is   correct:   all   'say'
meaningful, that is, intelligible words or sentences; what is common to  all
the above cases is that all describe  Juan's  linguistic  conduct  from  the
point of view of an observer of Juan.  However,  concerning  what  is  said,
that is, concerning what is meant by Juan, the expressions "Juan  dice  que"
are not descriptive, for the first is, say, a command,  whereas  the  second
is not. This implies that the "dice" or "says" is equivocal,  for  the  same
word means different speech acts. Similarly, "The Bible  says  that  Abraham
went to the mount to sacrifice Isaac."  This  is  correct  if  "says"  means
"narrates"; it is false it "says" means "states." For a narrative, like  the
poet, affirmeth nothing].
      The view that the Bible contains God's revelation  about  God  in  the
form of theoretical statements which demand assent,  consecrated  by  Locke,
builds a theory  of  linguistic  communication  in  which  communication  is
reduced to statements, on the basis of written language held in abeyance  as
object of study, of written statements  in  particular  heard   or  read  by
people who are merely curious about their meaning and  their  truth  because
they do not have to respond nor do they think that statements are  primarily
to be responded to, and applies it to the Bible which  had  till  then  been
heard as a living voice [OR AS A  'TRUE,'  ON-GOING  DRAMATIC  STORY?].  The
theory itself presupposes that there is no difference  between  written  and
spoken language, or at least that written language  is  primary,  since  the
theory is based on written rather than on spoken language.
      The originators of this theory of communication,  which  seems  to  be
equivalent  to  what  Frei  calls  "general  hermeneutic,"  were   primarily
interested in truth and their  conditions.  By  the  same  token  they  were
exclusively interested in statements, for at  least  in  written  texts  the
only truth-conveying texts are statements (Cf. Aquinas on  Peri  Hermeneias:
\wp\chant\excor-3, arond p. 25). Consequently, because the  Bible  was  true
and insofar as it was true, it had to be made up of  statements.  Hence  the
narrative portions of the  Bible,  the  story-like  portions  on  which  all
others depended, had to be read as statements to be assented  to.  According
to them, God (through the writer) said  (stated)  that...,  (for  "the  poet
affirmeth nothing," the poet only expresses), whereas the Christian  reading
of the Bible had till then consisted in hearing narrated  dramatic  episodes
of the on-going history in  which  they  themselves  were  participants  and
meant to be participants. They could hear the  Bible  in  that  way  because
those episodes were spoken, that is, narrated, by  believers,  that  is,  by
participants  in  the  episodes  narrated  or  by  people  who   heard   the
participants themselves (that is, heard them  participatingly,  believingly-
applicationally) narrate  those  episodes.  [See  the  beginning  of  Lucas'
Gospel, the end of John's, and the first  verses  of  John's  first  letter.
This is why the early datation  of  the  Gospels,  which  insures  the  oral
transmission by the participants in the bulk of  the  stories  narrated,  is
important.* [* The difference between a written and  an  oral  communication
is not to be taken in a gross way.  John  the  Baptist's  father,  Zacharia,
become mute, communicated orally by writing when he came out of the  Sancta.
For he communicated in presence events in which he had participated  and  to
which he was responding in naming his son John, and  to  which  he  expected
those concerned to respond by abiding by his decision}.
      Statements, as this word is ordinarily understood in our  intellectual
tradition  after  the  Lockean  theory  became   canonical,   are   concrete
predications to be assented to, theoretical predications which  express  the
speaker's knowledge (judgments). The hearer or reader (for supposedly  there
is no difference between the two) who understands  language,  that  is,  who
understands both what is stated and that it  is  stated,  is  not  meant  to
respond to the reality to which the stated is resemblant (or with  which  it
is  identical,  in  the  Aristotelian  view)  but  to  assent  to  what   is
predicated.
      Now, if narratives are statements, narratives refer  either  to  facts
or to ideas, and in either case the appropriate  response  to  them  on  the
reader's-hearer's part, and the response actually intended by the  narrator-
stater, is assent.  But this reduction of narratives  to  statements,  which
was necessary in order to continue to  affirm  that  the  Bible  speaks  the
truth--for only statements are susceptible of being truth or false--may  not
do justice to narratives at all. After all, do narratives actually  mean  by
referring to facts or ideas? An alternative seems at least possible.
      Narratives are narratives, not disguised 'statements' as this term  is
generally understood in the Lockean tradition. Narratives are meant  by  the
narrator, whether rightly or wrongly matters litle, either as history or  as
story, and the narrator's communicative intention  is  to  have  them  heard
accordingly by narrating them rather than stating facts to be  assented  to.
For narratives are not statements nor  meant  to  be  heard  as  statements.
Narratives are not assented to, nor meant to be assented to. Narratives,  at
least history-like, realistic narratives, are heard either as history or  as
story. In Frei's opinion, the specific illocutionary effect  of  narratives,
be they heard as history or  as  story--an  illocutionary  effect  which  is
different and independnet from the perlocutionary  effect  intended  by  the
particular narrato, is to refer the hearer  to  a  world--to  use  Ricoeur's
idiom--that is,  to  an  inhabitable  spatio-temporal  concrete  setting  of
dramatic action, in which the characters of  the  narration  are  themselves
involved, a setting which they modify and by which they are  modified.*   [*
Whether or not  the  hearer  is  meant  to  participate  and  does  actually
participate in the events narrated depends on a  number  of  factors,  among
which  are  the  realistic  character  of  the  story   and   the   possible
relationship of the events narrated to the hearer].
      Now, before the paradigmatic shift, the Bible, as  already  suggested,
was read as a history--"Sicut liber historiarum  solebat  legi",  not  as  a
statement or a collection of statements--BUT  NOT  AS  A  NOVEL  EITHER,  IN
WHICH CASE IT WOULD NOT HAVE HAD THE RELIGIOUS MEANING IT ACTUALLY  HAD  FOR
CHRISTIANS. However, this shift occurred unwittingly and went, for the  most
part, unnoticed for a long time. In fact,  it  could  even  be  taken  as  a
progress to get a better hold on the meaning of the Bible,  though  actually
it resulted rather in an undermining of the authority of the  Bible  and  of
the interpreting Church. Indeed,  in  the  previous  dispensation,  that  is
before the eclipse of the narrative reading, before the shift  to  objective
statements-pictures, the "expert" interpreters, in case of  what  seemed  to
them to be an unacceptable particular  reading,  were  committed  Christians
submitted in faith to  the  ecclesiastical  authorities,  that  is,  to  the
hierarchical community of those who knew how to respond to  Christ.  In  the
new dispensation, however, after the eclipse, the experts became  those  who
understood  the  pertinent  theories  of  linguistic  communication  and  of
knowledge, regardless of their religious faith  or  lack  thereof,  and  the
standards they applied to the written text depended  on  whether  they  took
the God of  faith  or  the  God  of  reason  as  normative.  Theologians  as
scientists, rather than as fideles, became the authorities. In  both  cases,
though, they misunderstood the Bible for they read it  theoretically  rather
than being referred practically or applicatively. But that this  constituted
a misunderstanding,  which were the cultural forces that  prompted  it,  and
with what consequences, needs a deeper exploration than the  impressionistic
one carried so far.
An alternative to the Lockean theory
      Written  language  is  comparatively  recent,  perhaps  20,000  years.
However, people had been talking to each other for as long as they  existed,
long before language was written. Thus any  theory  which,  even  under  the
unwitting assumption that written and spoken  language  are  equivalent,  is
built on the  examination  of  written  language  is  prima  facie  suspect.
Moreover, making statements was only one among the many speech  acts  people
practiced both as competent speakers and hearers,  that  is,  as   competent
linguistic agents. Thus any  theory  that  implicitly  reduces  language  to
statements is prima facie suspect. It happens, however, that the  theory  of
meaning or communication which provoked, or at  least  contributed  to,  the
shift from the narrative to the  objective  or  scientific  reading  of  the
Bible is suspect on both accounts. Without, therefore, in any way  conceding
the philosophical primacy of written  language  and  of  statement,  let  us
begin by taking the theory on its own grounds and examine it critically.
      Whether or not a correct theory  of  linguistic  communcation  may  be
built on the basis of written language, or whether or  not  spoken  language
is reducible to, or identical with, written language,  we  are  entitled  to
start with the linguistic practice in which we  engage  everyday.   Let  us,
then, consider oral face  to  face  statements  about  particulars,  in  the
present tense  and in an everyday situation.
      According to the  theory  of  communication  I  have  perhaps  wrongly
baptized as "Lockean," statements are meant to  be  assented  to.  Actually,
however, or so I claim, that is not  the  case.  In  the  situation  we  are
considering statements are heard, and meant to be heard, applicatively,  for
what is stated  is  an  object  for  immediate  ordelayed  response  by  the
addressee, not a brute fact.
      "The pencil is on the table," "Your mother is  on  the  phone,"  "Your
house is burning," "You are stepping on my foot." The object  is  stated  by
the speaker, that is, meant by the speaker, as object for  response  by  the
addressee to whom the statement is addressed. Otherwise said, the  statement
is meant by the speaker  to  be  heard  practically  (that  is,  not  to  be
objectified but rather transcended instrumentally) by  the  addressee.  (And
the addressee is usually not intentionally present, as  the  speaker  is  or
was, to that which is stated. Since the statement  is  meant  applicatively,
not theoretically, for it does not state a judged fact  but  an  object  for
response, believing the statement (if one can properly speak  of  believing,
given that  the  truth  of  the  statement  is  not  at  stake)  amounts  to
responding to  the  object  for  response  as  stated,  or  at  least  being
practically disposed or bracing oneself to respond to it in that  way,  when
no other intervening factors  prevent  actual  action  in  response  to  the
object stated. (The object,  therefore,  as  object  that  demands  response
within the given field of action of the addressee, is not neutral but value-
laden in relation to the addressee, grasped  as  such  by  the  speaker  and
meant to be grasped as such by the addressee. If the  language  of  pictures
is to be used, the picture is practical). This is why, if no  action  ensues
on the addressee's part, the speaker  is  deemed  not  to  have  heard  (the
sounds), or not to have understood the statement.  And  an  addressee  whose
understanding were reduced to theoretical  understanding  and  assent  would
not be a competent user of language, that is,  would  not  be  a  lingusitic
agent.
      A statement, however, may be not believed by the addressee   that  is,
may fail to refer practically the  addressee  to  the  object  for  response
stated. Yet the statement was heard and grammatically understood.  In  fact,
it can be even repeated word by word by the  addressee  or  by  anybody  who
heard it. However, since it was not heard as it  was  meant,  for  practical
reference did not obtain and action did not ensue, it was  not  heard  as  a
statement, for oral statements in the situation described  mean  object  for
response by the addressee, and are to be heard as such.
      Now, when a grammatical statement such as "there is a  pencil  on  the
table" is uttered word by word, out of the situation of  exposure  in  which
it was made, it sounds as if it were the same  as  when  the  statement  was
made. It can even be written. And what is written appears to be  a  sort  of
verbal picture of the fact or state  of  affairs  referred  to,  and  to  be
decoded as a mental picture by any competent user of language. In  addition,
the picture may be resemblant or not resemblant. Hence  the  Lockean  theory
of knowledge and of statements. What many people, and most certainly  Locke,
fail to realize is that this written  statement  is  not  the  same  as  the
statement made by the speaker and heard  by  the  competent  hearer  in  the
situation we are considering, for it is not the same speech  act.  In  fact,
it is not even a speech act. Now what is stated (the  supposedly  resemblant
picture) is at best to be believed, that is, assented to, for it  cannot  be
heard practically out of the practical, existential situation  in  which  it
was uttered. Now it is not addressed to anybody in particular by anybody  in
particular, and it does not state the  object  as  object  for  response  by
anybody, much less for as object for response by  the  particular  addressee
or adressees. In fact, it is not a statement at  all,  if  by  statement  we
mean oral statements in situations of exposure. However, it is a  statement,
if we define statements what statements  are  according  to  the  theory  of
linguistic communication which maintains that statements state  facts,  that
they are true or false depending on whether the  content  of  the  statement
corresponds to the fact stated, and that they aim at eliciting assent,  that
is, to be believed.
      In fact, the written statement to  which  I  am  referring  becomes  a
statement of the predication (that is,  of  the  notion  judged)  as  to  be
assented to and agreed upon by any  neutral  observer,  not  of  object  for
response as  object  for  response  by  the  addressee.  Since  exposure  is
neutralized, the connection with emotion and action is severed.  Now  purely
objective evidence of what is predicated is required by the hearer of it  as
a theoretical statement, as a statement of the brute fact predicated.
      In addition, since statements are made by people who speak,  they  are
deemed to express  or  re-present  the  speaker's  view,  what  is  visually
(rather than practically) present to the speaker, what the speaker views  or
judges: "The pencil is on the  table."  However,  since  two  perceivers  or
speakers or contemplators may  disagree,  sufficient  reason  for  affirming
something (rather  than  responding  to)  is  required.  Hence  the  visual-
pictorial theory of judgment and of communication as  exchange  of  pictures
to be assented to, and of independent grasp of  the  evidence  (rather  than
implicit, applicative grasp of object for response). In this way the  theory
of knowledge and of linguistic communication that provoked the  paradigmatic
shift in the reading of the Bible in the eighteenth century is completed.
      However, as just indicated, from the standpoint of competent users  of
ordinary language in situation of exposure,  the  theory  is  mistaken,  for
statements in this situation are meant and heard applicatively, not  in  the
way assumed by the theory.
Connecting applicative statements with stories
      Now, to continue with the  idea  I  wanted  to  develop  in  order  to
connect applicative statements with stories. "Your house is burning"  refers
me--me the exposed agent who right now  do not see  the  house  I  love  and
need--not to a picture or to a predication but to my burning  house  (stated
by you) as object for response, within  the  world  in  which  I  am  agent-
patient. Believing your statement is responding to  the  object  as  stated,
given my present situation within the field of action in which  both  myself
and my house are. Thus I run to my house, call my neighbors and  with  their
help succeed or fail to extinguish the fire.
      When later I tell this I unavoidably tell it as a story, as  a  'true'
story (history), as a past episode in my  on-going  life  (that  is,  of  my
narrative identity?). "Yesterday I met John who told me that  my  house  was
on fire. I ran and with the  neighbors'  help  I  managed  to..."  All  this
sounds as a series of descriptive  statements.  However,  certain  important
remarks are in order.
      First of all, if we insist on speaking of pictures,  at  least  it  is
clear that we do not  have a number of pictures,  each  corresponding  to  a
statement, but one episode or 'dramatic picture teleologically organized  in
which I am involved,' one  story  with  its  typical  temporal-teleological-
dramatic unity  analyzed  by  Aristotle,  with  its  goal  orientation,  its
suspense (uncertainty concerning the future) created by actual  or  possible
obstacles ("peripetiae"), and its resolution in terms of success or  failure
estimated by the protagonist's standards and by  the  reader's  or  hearer's
standards. Dramatic unity is teleological, but teleologically structured  by
participants who have different, and perhaps conflicting, goals, so  that  a
resolution that is good from one of the participants' standpoint may be  bad
from another's.
       Secondly,  the  hearer  understands  the  story  because  of  his/her
experience as teleological, exposed agent-patient. Otherwise there would  be
no connection intelligible to the hearer of the story  between  "your  house
is burning" and the protagonist's action, and there  would  be  no  dramatic
unity followed by the hearer in terms of what is desired  and  desirable  in
the resolution, something that even young children are capable of  following
with their limited human experience as vulnerable agents.
      Thirdly, before being a told story the events  were  'history  in  the
making,' 'on-going history' in  which  agents-patients  were  involved  both
actively and passively. What is this? Not  brute  realities  or  facts,  but
some of them projected and desired and brought about  intentionally  by  the
desirous protagonist and by others, some of them suffered or  undergone  and
hated by the desirous protagonist, who  was  afected  by  them  and  had  to
modify his/her plans to bring about the desired outcome, and  each  and  all
of them structured teleologically around a subject or subjects. Which is  to
say that reality as lived by exposed agents is itself temporal-teleological-
dramatic, that is, quasi-storical,  potentially  storical  as  mere  natural
events are not. Now, not only does this explain why any event  in  which  an
agent-patient is involved becomes dramatic when told, but  also  this  makes
possible the continuity I  want  to  explore  between  reality  as  on-going
history and the biblical stories as read by--and written by--Christians.
      The Gospel stories were on-going history before  being  stories  told.
They are told in the form of stories because they  involve  agents-patients.
But the participants in the on-going history were  living--not  telling--the
on-going history they were making as agents-patients,  which  they  were  to
tell later as a (true or real-past) story. They were not making  theoretical
statements to be assented to and supporting them with  evidence,  much  less
making explanatory theories; they  were  making  decisions  (either  from  a
position of having the initiative, or  as  controlled)  according  to  their
teleologically structured perceptions, and changing  the  state  of  affairs
through their actions, and being changed by the changing state of affairs.
      For instance, the British captain. He saw the fire  to  which  he  and
his crew and his boat were exposed as fugiendum  rather  than  extinguendum,
much as Christians see Christ as adorandus.
      [NOTICE THAT THIS IS THE  SITUATION  TO  WHICH  ARITOTLE'S  NOTION  OF
KNOWLEDGE IN DE MOTU ANIMALIUM CORRESPONDS; IT IS  PRACTICAL  KNOWLEDGE.  BY
CONTRAST, SPECULATIVE KNOWLEDGE, WHICH CORRESPONDS TO THE STATE  OF  AFFAIRS
PRESENTED IN THE FIRST CHAPTER OF THE METAPHYSICS, AND  PRESUPPOSED  IN  THE
POSTERIOR ANALYTIC, RESTS ON A  THEORY  OF  KNOWLEDGE  AND  CONSEQUENTLY  OF
LANGUAGE IN WHICH THE PURELY THINKING--NOT  SPEAKING-SPOKEN  TO--SUBJECT  IS
NEITHER I NOR YOU OR SHE, NEITHER HERE NOR THERE, NEITHER NOW NOR THEN].
       Stories are the only literary genre that  preserves  not  only  space
but  hodological,  FUTURE-ORIENTED   space   populated   by   objects   with
coefficients of favorableness and adversity for subjects; not only time  but
teleological  time  toward  the  projected  and  striven-for  future,  while
placing the subject as patient-agent-subject-object, that is as exposed  and
vulnerable, within that space and time. (The hodological  space  of  agents-
patients becomes dramatic space in stories where the  significance-value  of
events is already clear to the author--because past--who is  himself/herself
dispelling that  signifcance  to  the  reader  together  with  the  events).
Furthermore, stories are singular--a singular subject or singular  subjects,
in a singular situation, at a singular time. They have the  irrepleaceabiliy
and unrepeatibility of real life. Because  stories  replicate  the  dramatic
structure of our lives as agents, they are connatural to  us,  that  is,  we
are natural tellers and hearers of stories. Furthermore,  only  stories  can
convey our existential reality. THEY  ARE  INDISPENSABLE  FOR  REFERRING  TO
DISTANCES OF EXPOSURE TO OBJECTS FOR RESPONSE.
The Gospel stories
      The teller of the story, however, is not, as  present  teller,  within
the space of the story when he tells the already copmpleted story,  nor  are
the hearers of the story, except if their own time (and  space)  are  (lived
as, and actually are) the time (and  space)  of  the  story.  This  is  what
happens in the case of the Gospel's stories as  heard  by  Christians.  They
are unique, though, in that, once distances  of  exposure  across  time  and
space are established in relation to Jesus of Nazareth--His time  being  on-
going time, the time of on-goping history, not mere past in relation to me--
 I find myself presently exposed to Him in  a  time  structured  around  Him
within which I belong.
      So that we see as clearly as possible how the passage  occurs  between
present statements and past narratives let us consider the  famous  case  of
the "World Wars"  broadcast.  Certain  people  heard  the  statements  of  a
supposedly  contemporary  witness  within  their  space   of   exposure   as
statements are  supposed  to  be  heard,  namely,  applicatively,  and  they
responded accordingly.  Certain  other  people  were  not  referred  by  the
statements and therefore did not respond to the object stated. This  is  on-
going history, history in the making. Stories are always heard by an  agent-
patient from his/her standpoint in relation to the events  narrated.  It  is
for this agent, as part of his/her hearing, to  situate  himself/herself  in
relation to the events, getting his or her clues from the narrative  itself,
and from his or her self- understanding  and  understanding  of  the  world.
Since in the case we are considering  the  contextual  clues  were  missing,
indeed, purposefully blurred, the mistake was easily made;  indeed,  it  was
provoked by the broadcasters, however unwittingly.
      In the case of the Gospels, at least in  one  case,  the  story  began
like this: Invenimus Messiam. By this announckement one person  was  brought
to exposure to Jesus of Nazareth. This person could have told his  story  to
an audience by referring them to Jesus of Nazareth as he had  lived  Him  as
object for response, namely, as  illi  adorandus,  by  virtue  of  the  very
events told and the character presented. If the audience had  been  referred
as this speaker meant to refer them, the members  of  this  audience,  given
the unique case of the temporality of Jesus as the Son of  God,  would  have
become part of the on-going history of the Church.  The  passage  from  past
history to present, on-going history open to the  future,  would  have  been
effected. And from then on these Christians would  have  heard  the  Gospels
and the Bible applicatively as Christians (not applicatively as Jews).
A note on the wrong theory of linguistic communication
      The Lockean theory of communication puts the cart  before  the  horse.
Instead of starting with linguistic oral successful communication  in  which
one oneself is engaged as the paradigmatic case, it begins with,  and  makes
paradigmatic  for  the  theory,  the  case  in  which  a   piece   of   oral
commuunication is overheard by a merely curious bystander. If  so  the  oral
communication, that is, what is overheard, is equivalent to a  written--that
is, objectified--text that supposedly says something that can be  understood
but does not refer practically, since the overhearer is not in situation  of
participation. Furthermore, because this theory  is  contaminated  with  the
central interest of the Greek philosophical tradition in truth and  falsity,
and because this tradition makes of judgments the exclusive place of truth--
and therefore of genuine  knowledge--as  far  as  the  knowing  activity  is
concerned, and of statements (which express judgments) the  exclusive  place
of  truth--and  therefore  of  genuine  knowledge--as  far  as  language  is
concerned, this theory makes of judgments and statements the basic  unit  of
both knowledge and language and, in any case, the only  interesting  portion
of "mental acts" and of "speech acts."
      According to this theory of linguistic communication, the  first  step
concerning a written text consists in deciphering it, in passing  from  non-
understanding  to  understanding  of  its  meaning,  in  moving   from   the
institutionally meaningful visible signs to the signified to be  understood,
according to the sedimented,  institutional  'language'  that  shortcircuits
speech-acts. Then from the signified to its truth, which excludes  from  the
field of interest all speech acts but statements and  makes  of  theoretical
statements the basic or at least the  philosophically  important  linguistic
unit. (Here is where a contemplative or  "speculative"  theory  of  language
connects with this speculative or  non-participatory  theory  of  language).
Then from its truth to its application. Thus, learning how  to  read,  which
is seen as equivalent to learning how to  speak,  is  learning  to  decipher
texts  (while  learning  to  speak  is  learning  how  to  encode  ideas  in
language), that is, learning to move from what is written or  said  to  what
is signified. When this is  achieved  success  is  achieved,  for  texts  by
definition say something to be understood and,  at  best,  in  the  case  of
statements, believed or, what is the same,assented to. The purpose of  texts
is to convey something intelligible by means of saying it. Thus for  a  text
to mean is to say something to be understood, as Searle has it.
      As indicated above,  the  Christian  readers  of  the  Bible  are  not
confronted with a text in the sense we  are  considering;  they  hear  God's
voice. However, if we apply the present view to the Bible's case, the  Bible
says something to be understood, so that the result of  succesfully  reading
the Bible is understanding what  the  text  says.  When  this  understanding
obtains, success is achieved. As if the narratives of  the  Bible  were  not
meant to refer their readers-hearer to objects for  response,  that  is,  to
the objects for response to which the participants  in  the  narratives  who
have an analogous situation to that of the readers-hearers responded.
      A conversation can  be  reported  in  writing  and  then  be  read  by
somebody as a text to be understood.  But this reader reads a  text,  he  is
not  engaged  in  a  conversation.  A  reported  conversation   is   not   a
conversation. The reader of the text of a conversation is neither  addresser
nor addressee, he can neither call  nor  be  called.  A  person  engaged  in
conversation calls and is called. A  conversation  read  neither  calls  its
reader nor allows the reader to call. By calling and  being  called  I  mean
being on the giving or on the receiving side, on the active or  the  passive
side of speech acts, as speaker or spoken to.
      In an oral  exchange  one  is  addressed  personally  by  someone  who
shares (is surrounded by) roughly the same immediate world, and  who  speaks
on a practical understanding of his/her and his own and  the  interlocutor's
respective roles and situations in regard to the surrounding world.
