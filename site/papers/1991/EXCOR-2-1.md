Whether or not this demand of allegiance is compatible with the claim of impartiality is highly doubtful , and there is not in the document the slightest attempt to allay the doubt with an explanation. In any case, it is my opinion that that the two are incompatible and, therefore, that an either-or choice has to be made. Secular universities have made their choice and defend and demand impartiality. The Church, obviously, has not made a choice between the two, since it purports to guarantee impartiality (that is, pure objectivity, non-commitment) in the acquisition of knowledge, while demanding a commitment to faith that is obviously partial and imposes limits on reason.

Needless to say, Ex corde Ecclesiae is radically vitiated by this internal incoherence. For it is simply incoherent to promise freedom from interference and guarantee impartiality, and to demand allegiance and impose limits.  

As already stated, confronted with the mutually incompatible demands of faith and reason, secular universities have opted for the absolute autopnomy of reason and given up faith and its supremacy. They therefore demand and grant both impartiality and demand freedom. The Enlightenment has become prescriptive in our culture, as faith was in the previous cultural dispensation, and demands the same from Catholic universities, which amounts to demanding that they cease being Catholic.

The Enlightenment has finally reached them and is already well entrenched within them under the guise of "academic freedom," which involves two undisputed claimns: the integrity of each academic discipline, which establishes the right of its practitioners to pursue knowledge in the domain of their discipline according to the method dictated by the nature of its object, without interference from any other interests, and the right of the faculty of the university to pursue their disciplinary research wherever it may lead and to publish and teach the conclusions reached whichever they may be , without fear of retaliation from those individuals or agencies to which their conclusions may be unpalatable. Needless to say, the acceptance of these rights amounts practically not only to the rejection of the authority of faith over reason, as these were conceived in the medieval synthesis, but to the subordination of faith to reason in matters of truth. Which amounts to saying that there is no possible harmony between faith and reason, that a choice has to be made between the two, and that in universities as institutions of learning there is no room for faith. In other words, the Apostolic Constitution Ex corde Ecclesiae comes precisely at the moment in which the Enlightenment has already penetrated the last bastion of the old Catholic dispensation and is on the verge of consecrating forever the incompatibility between reaons and faith. Reason is in and faith is out.

THE QUESTION IS WHETHER THE DEMAND FOR IMPARTIALITY IS

ITSELF REASONABLE . I do not think it is.

Why have modern, post-Enlightenment universities opted for, and staunchly defended the impartiality of research? Their rationale for this was already expressed above in connection with academic freedom and the integrity of the academic disciplines. The reason that the two terms seem to be mutually exclusive has to do with two features that most people today deem to be essential to the identity of the university and without which it cannot function as such, namely, academic freedom and the academic integrity of the disciplines (the autonomy of academia - of reason -of the academic disciplines) .  Roughly speaking, again, by the integrity of the academic disciplines is meant the autonomy of each of the scholarly domains. Concretely this amounts to the professional obligation of the practitioners of each of the disciplines to let themselves be primarily guided in their teaching and research by the disinterested desire to know, and

The argument presupposes that the only appropriate attitude to reach genuine knowledge, that is, the truth, is impartiality, and that reason left to itself is impartial. In other words, the disinterested desire to know must be primary and in any case exclude any other conflicting desires or interests that might prevent the object from showing itself as it is rather than as it is wished to be according to extraneous interests.

We may ask, however, whether the conception of knowledge here presupposed holds water .

The conception of knowledge we are talking about separates knowledge from action as two entirely different, though not unrelated, things or realities . They are different in that one can know without acting and act without knowing. Yet, they are not unrelated, in that both intentional and systematically successful action is conditioned by knowledge. Thus, to allude to the first chapter of Augustine's Confessions , in this view one cannot praise God (which is an intentional, object-directed action) without knowing God, whereas one can conceivably know God without actually praising Him. Furthermore, only on the basis of knowing can one act successfully on a systematic basis . For instance, if I am walking blindfolded in an unfamiliar room which has a door, I can once by chance pass through a door without hitting the wall, but it is on the basis of my seeing the door or knowing exactly where it is situated that I can systematically suceed in passing through the door without hitting the wall.

A further, important characteristic of this conception of knowledge is that it presupposes that knowledge is a matter of resemblance of what is in the knowing mind to the reality known , or--in another version of the same--of " adaequatio " or conformity of the knowing mind to the reality known.  The knowing mind is passive to the reality to be known-- adaequatio mentis ad rem --, not the other way around. But to what aspect or 'formality' of the reality to be known is the mind passive in knowledge? Not to its goodness or badness, its attractiveness or repulsiveness to the subject, but only to what is objective, to what the object is in itself, to what in the object is capable of satisfying the subject's pure, disinterested desire to know or curiosity. In other words, real or objective is what on the side of the object corresponds to the pure, disinterested desire to know on the side of the subject. This is why impartiality is essential for knowing , for any motivation other than the disinterested desire to know would distort the reality to be known to conforma it to the subject's desires. Therefore the subject who pursues knowledge has to make a deliberate effort to hush or neutralize any interests or desires that would interfere with the disinterested desire to know.

To sum up this conception of the knower, of the object known, and of knowledge . Real or objective is what is to be known by a subject guided by the disinterested desire to know, that is, by an impartial knower who neutralizes all other desires and adequates itself passively to the purely objective aspects of its object. Knowledge is the result of this adaequatio mentis ad rem, and is the condition of possibility of genuinely intentional and systematically successful action.   Does such a conception make sense?

It is noteworthy that, according to this conception, not only is knowledge different from action, but knowledge is in no case a sufficient condition for action . The reason is that there is nothing in knowledge capable by itself of motivating the knowing subject to act, for knowledge supposedly is purely objective, purely 'informative' about the object itself, purely revealing of the object itself independently of its goodnes or badness for the subject. Such knowledge finds its expression in the merely informative statement--what in his commentary on the Peri Hermeneias Aquinas calls "enunciativa oratio" "quae significat id quod mens de rebus concipit," "ipsum conceptum intellectus in quo est verum et falsum" by contrast to other "orationes" which "non significant ipsum conceptum intellectus in quo est verum et falsum sed quemdam ordinem ad hoc consequentem ". The entire paragraph deserves quotation :

"Sed quia intellectus vel ratio non solum concipit in seipsum veritatem tantum, sed etiam ad ejus officium pertinet secundum suum conceptum alia dirigere et ordinare; ideo necesse fuit quod sicut per enunciativam orationem significatur ipse mentis conceptus, ita essent aliquae aliae orationes significantes ordinem rationis secundum quod alia diriguntur... Quia istae quatuor orationis species non significant ipsum conceptum intellectus in quo est verum et falsum, sed quemdam ordinem ad hoc consequentem ; inde est quod in nulla earum invenitur verum vel falsum, sed solum in enunciativa, quae significat id quod mens de rebus concipit... Sed enunciativa oratio praesentis considerationis est. "True or false is said to be in the enunciation as in a sign of true or false thought; but true or false is in the mind as in a subject (as said in VI Metaphysicae E, 4, 1027b 17-1028a 5) and in the thing as a cause (as said in the book Predicamentorum Categ. 5, 4a 35--4b 9)--for it is from the facts of the case, i.e., from a thing's being so or not being so, that speech is true or false.

"Next he shows that this definition differentiates the enunciation from other speech, when he says, Truth or falsity is not present in all speech however , etc. In the case of imperfect or incomplete speech it is clear that it does not signify true or false, since it does not make complete sense to the mind of the hearer and therefore does not completely express a judgment of reason in which the true or false consists. Having made this point, however, it must be noted that there are five species of perfect speech that are complete in meanining: enunciative, deprecative, imperative, interrogative, and vocative (... as in 'O good Peter!') . Of these species of speech the enunciative is the only one in which there is truth or falsity, for it alone signifies the conception of the intellect absolutely and it is in this that there is truth or falsity.

" But the intellect, or reason, does not just conceive the truth of a thing. It also belongs to its office to direct and order others in accordance with what it conceives . Therefore, besides enunciative speech, which signifies the concept of the mind, there had to be other kinds of speech to signify the order of reason by which others are directed. Now, one man is directed by the reason of another in regard to three things: first, to attend with his mind, and vocative speech relates to this; second, to respond with his voice, and interrogative speech relates to this; third, to execute a work, and in relation to this, imperative speech is used with regard to inferiors, deprecative with regard to superiors. Optative speech is reduced to the latter, for a man does not have the power to move a superior except by the expression of desire.

" These four  species of speech do not signify the  conception of the intellect in which there is truth or falsity, but a certain order following upon this. Consequently truth or falsity is not found in any of them, but only in enunciative speech, which signifies what the mind conceives from things. It follows that all the modes of speeech in which the true or false is found are contained under the enunciation, which some call indicative or suppositive. The dubitative, it should be noted, is reduced to the interrogative, as the optative is to the deprecative.

" Then Aristotle says, Let us therefore consider enunciative speech , etc. Here he points out that only enunciative speech is to be treated; the other four species must be omitted as far as the present intention is concerned, because their investigation belongs rather to the sciences of rhetoric or poetics. Enunciative speech belongs to the present consideration and for the following reason: this book is ordered directly to demonstrative science, in which the mind of man is led by an act of reasoning to assent to truth from those things that are proper to the thing; to this end the demonstrator uses only enunciative speech, which signifies things according as truth about them is in the mind. The rhetorician and the poet, on the other hand, induce assent to what they intend not only through what is proper to the thing but also through the dispositions of the hearer . Hence, rhetoricians and poets for the most part strive to move their auditors by arousing certain passions in them, as the Philosopher says in his Rhetorica (I, 2, 1356a 2, 1356a 14; III, 1, 1403b 12). This kind of speech, therefore, which is concerned with the ordination of the hearer toward something, belongs to the consideration of rhetoric or poetics by reason of its intent, but to the consideration of the grammarian as regards a suitable construction of sounds".

Knowledge, in the conception we are now exploring, has a certain primacy over action in that it is said to be a necessary condition for action--but only for intentional action, for object-related action. And, as just explained, it never is a sufficient condition for action. Indeed, precisely because knowledge is purely objective, purely informative of what the object is in itself and independently of any relationship to the knowing subject, it has no motivating power. (This is what Hume noticed when he argued about the impossibility of passing from knowledge to action, from fact to value. And what long before him Aristotle had remarked in connection with practical reason by contrast to speculative reason).

Now, if knowledge is not a sufficient condition for acting, what more and else is required for the subject to act? Attraction or repulsion. However, this attraction or repulsion cannot be a mere 'feeling,', it has to be intentional in the sense of object-directed, of being perfused with evaluative knowledge of object, for otherwise there would be no action in response to the object and the problem of the passage from fact to value would reappear: why would such a feeling (as a pure fact) motivate action anymore than the knowledge of the object? Thus, what is needed as a necessary--though perhaps not sufficient--condition for acting is knowledge of a different sort, namely, evaluative knowledge, knowledge that is not merely informative about the object in itself but about the object in relation to the subject, about the object in its capacity to affect the subject favorably or unfavorably. (THIS IS WHAT ARISTOTLE CALLED "PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE") . AND, OBVIOUSLY, GIVEN THE INTRINSIC RELATIONSHIP OF THIS KNOWLEDGE TO THE GOOD OF THE SUBJECT , TO DEMAND THAT THIS KNOWLEDGE BE IMPARTIAL WOULD BE ABSURD. INDEED, THE DEMAND OF IMPARTIALITY MAKES SENSE ONLY IF KNOWLEDGE IS SUPPOSED TO BE PURELY OBJECTIVE, INFORMATIVE ABOUT THE OBJECT AS IT IS IN ITSELF, NOT AS IT IS IN RELATION TO THE GOOD OF THE SUBJECT. HENCE THE QUESTION BEFORE US IS WHETHER THE KNOWLEDGE PURSUED IN UNIVERSITIES (BE THEY CATHOLIC OR NOT), AND PARTICULARLY IN DEPARTMENTS OF THEOLOGY (AND PHILOSOPHY) IS THEORETICAL OR PRACTICAL. FOR IF IT HAPPENS TO BE PRACTICAL, IT CANNOT BE IMPARTIAL, IT HAS TO BE GUIDED BY THE GOOD OF THE SUBJECT.

Faith as knowledge

Faith, in a general way, is the ability to conduct oneself in the appropriate Christian way, that is, to respond to God and other people as a Christian in the concrete circumstances of the responder ought to respond. It is Christian faith because the central axis of the conduct, its motivating framework, so to say, is Jesus-Christ and His teaching (much as one would say that the central axis of the conduct of the protagonist of Kafka's The Castle is the Master of the castle). The more a life is structured around the Jesus-Christ axis, that is, the more the whole conduct of a person can be understood by reference to that motivating framework, the more it is Christian, the more it is the life of faith. The saints are the best of the faithful. In the Ancient Testament Abraham is the model, for his entire life was defined by obedience to God.

In relation to Jesus-Christ, faith is knowledge, for in a way Jesus-Christ is known. But what kind of knowledge?

Not theoretical knowledge . Theoretical knowledge is the knowledge of a pure contemplator of contemplated object, of an intelligent observer, not of an exposed subject. It is, therefore, purely objective, merely informative about the object itself. Furthermore, it abstracts the object from all relations and objectifies it, that is, considers it by itself, which amounts to conceptualizing it. By the same token, this conceptualized-known object demands no response on the knower's part, for neither is the knower exposed to it nor is an abstract, purely intelligible concept the sort of object that can motivate to respond. It is characteristic of theoretical knowledge to terminate in a judgment whose expression is a statement, a mere enunciation about the object.

By contrast, the knowledge in which faith consists is both personal and affective. Insofar, therefore, as faith is knowledge, it has to be in one way or another personal and motivating knowledge. Personal not only in the sense that all knowledge is a subject's knowledge, for knowledge is not something that floats around by itself, but personal in the sense of a relationship of exposure of the subject to the object in which the subject is at stake, which makes the knowledge affective.

Knowledge as judgment

"It is true that your house is burning." As Searle observes, this can be said appropriately only if someone has previously stated that your house is burning. In this sense, truth is a property of statements, which express judgments-that of the kind of "it is true that your house is burning." Judgments-that of this kind (COMMUNICATIVE SPEECH ACTS) are reflective acts based on reflectively grasped (explicitly justified) conformity of what is conceived by the subject to reality (SILENT NON-COMMUNICATIVE STATEMENTS = KNOWLEDGE). Thus, before being a COMMUNICATIVE statement [IT IS TRUE THAT YOUR HOUSE IS BURNING, THE KIND OF STATEMENT MADE IN PROFESSIONAL PAPERS SO THAT THE HEARERS ARE REFERRED TO THE BURNING HOUSE THROUGH ASSENT TO THE STATEMENT THAT YOUR HOUSE IS BURNING] your house is grasped and assented to as burning by the subject. House burning, therefore, as far as judgment is concerned, is mere object of assent, and truth consists in the conformity between the judgment and the reality judged by a subject voyeur . IN OTHER WORDS, THE SUBJECT SEES WHAT IS THE CASE (material truth of perception, even in animals): what is the case, then, is object of assent, not object for response . On the basis of this seen-assented to, when the evidence is explicitly acquired or reflectively verified as evidence, I communicate to others (judge) that your house is burning. If "it is true for me that your house is burning", I am referred to the house as real, that is, as object of assent . That is, a voyeur creates another voyeur.

The preceding paragraph has something to it but is muddled.

The point is that what I call "judgment-statement-that," which is reflective, is judgment of the truth of a previous judgment  (A CONCEPTION, OR perhaps what Lonergan calls verbum mentis that predicates something of something). Thus, this kind of reflective judgment is formally judgment of conformity of a previous judgment to reality ON THE BASIS OF EVIDENCE PROMPTING SAYING-ASSENTING (JUDGING), NOT OF REALITY PROMPTING RESPONSE. The judgment of reality is the non-reflective judgment, the inchoatus judgment. But in fact this inchoatus judgment is not judgment by pure contemplator who predicates but judgment by exposed subject who acts, practical judgment, applicative judgment. THE RADICAL MISTAKE is to take it for a theoretical, merely predicative judgment, and hence to attempt to cash it in terms of a fragment of a picture to be put together with, and completed by, other fragments to constitue a mosaic of the whole of reality, much as Aquinas thinks that faith and reason complete each other in their truths .

The fact is that we are not inserted in the world as invulnerable, curious voyeurs but as vulnerable, exposed, concerned subjects. "Your house is burning" does not convey to me, its owner, a piece of neutral information about a visible phenomenon, but the terror of a great determinate loss to be prevented if possible, that is, if the means are available. To be prevented-GERUNDIVE, APPLICATIVE IKNOWLEDGE . Neither was my burning house, when you discovered it, a piece of neutral information for you, since you ran to let me know that fact. In short, because we are needful, exposed agents,   we perceive emotively and  applicatively . We do not go around registering facts, neutral pieces of information. Only when not constrained to act,  (or when functioning in the 'scientific' gear), do we seem to accumulate pieces of information, but even these are accumulated as to be activated practically in the future. My house burning, under conditions of exposure, is not object of assent, but object for response, at the very least of emotive response when nothing is there to be done about preventing it. And so would it be for the Aris of the Rhetoric--THIS IS PRACTICAL REASON.

But how or why does Aris move from practical to theoretical reason, and why does he ultimately consider theoretical reason as at least de iure paradigmatic for knowledge?

For us, as for animals, objects--reality--are objects for response at the moment of exposure . [Objects for response are objects to be transcended in appropriate action (applicative knowledge). Like your mother is on the phone, like Christ is in the eucharist, like "Fire!"]. The fact, however, that WE ARE LINGUISTIC CREATURES gives us possibilities of communication and deliberation and action that animals do not possess. We can plan and invent (delayed) response, we can consider the object in itself (fall of bodies on inclined planes, which yields the basis of instrumental value for the exposed agent) to brace ourselves for action, we can exchange practical information (that is, information to be understood applicatively). But this linguistic structure of ours also makes possible gigantic epistemic mistakes when we become theoreticians of knowledge and, as reflectors, take our knowledge sedimented in statements for  pictures of reality and convert even the functioning subject-agent into contemplated object .

FOR WE REFLECT 'SCIENTIFICALLY' WHEN WE ARE NOT IMMEDIATELY EXPOSED, AND WE REFLECT NOT AS CONCERNED DELIBERATORS BUT AS CURIOUS KNOWERS. AND WHEN WE BUILD A THEORY OF KNOWLEDGE ON THIS BASIS, AS THE GREEKS DID, THEN WE ARE IN DEEP SPECULATIVE TROUBLE WITH PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS. FROM THIS RESULTS PURE OR THEORETICAL REASON, WITH ITS IMPLICIT NOTION OF REALITY AS TO BE CONTEMPLATED AND OF KNOWLEDGE AS PICTURE OF REALITY, SO CLEARLY INCARNATED IN KANT'S FIRST CRITIQUE.

Actually, the temporal reality (truth?) of "Fire!," as far as the agent is concerned, is to be cashed in terms of affective response and action, not in terms of picture seen or of assent to the statement that there is fire. But this applicative cashing is a personal, LINGUISTICALLY INTRANSFERABLE deed of the linguistic agent who himself or herself furnishes his or her situation of exposure and his or her project, and this in terms of his or her self-understanding within the world (which is partially furnished by his or her culture) , as it was the case with the Orson Welles's people. Thus, what in the subject-agent is as to be activated at the time of exposure and is linguistically transferable appears as an 'objective' 'picture' of what is the case, though it is to be applicatively cashed . It is not a picture, since it does not function a pictures do, namely, as to be contemplated.

A couple of comparisons may be helpful. Perceiving food is not just perceiving something to be seen, but perceiving something-edible and edendum under the appropriate circumstances. In other words, angels cannot perceive food. Thus, habitually knowing food is not having a mental picture of food, but being habitually disposed to eat that under the appropriate circumstances, and to 'understand' that as edendum by other agents. And, of course, food can be known only by exposed agents who need to eat. Similarly, as understanding an order is intrinsically related to obeying or disobeying it, which is an action, so knowing something real is intrinsically related to responding to it appropriately--that is, given its affective and instrumental identity .

The illusion of imaginative or intellectual pictures arises out of the 'objectification' [that is, phenomenalization?] by the curious contemplator of what actually functions as object for response for the exposed subject. In other words, it arises out of contemplating and identifying as for-the-exposed-subject what is for-the-Other-as-contemplator-- think of Sartre --, what constitutes the stated content of the judgment, which in  fact is meant kand heard by agents applicatively. Thus, the illusion of knowledge as imaginative or intellectual picture is unavoidable and insurmountable if one takes as paradigmatic and normative one's experience when one is not acting but reflecting as a curious knower. It is like assuming that A PAIR OF GLASSES , as such, is an object  whose proper intelligibility can be grasped by its sensible qualities without reference to its particular instrumentality. When this illusion becomes the basis for a theory of knowledge, the primacy of theoretical over practical reason--which really amounts to cutting the connection between cognition and action-passion becomes unavoidable.

[ Notice that a pair of glasses is something seen by others , but not something whose instrumental efficiency depends on its being seen, or is even an object to be seen, by the subject who uses it. [In fact, the pair of glasses functions very much as a certain part of the eye-subject , whose defect it corrects. Now this part of the eye has its objective side, understood and manipulated by the physiologist, but is part of the eye-subject and is understood and manipulated by reference to the seeing subject]. A pair of glasses does not function as a pair of glasses when it is seen as an object by someone who does not wear it; it functions as a pair of glasses when it is not seen but worn as a self-extension by a subject who sees the world through it and can consequently act competently in response to the surroundig world. Thus, though a pair of glasses has an objective dimension for Others-contemplators (which in fact allows the physicist-phisiologist to construct the instrument called pair of glasses), it cannot be understood and designed but by subject-agents and in relation to subject-agents. In fact, it cannot even be understood at all, if by understanding we mean a purely theoretical or objective and fully ARTICULABLE understanding independent of the hearer, PRECISELY BECAUSE IT IS NOT SOMETHING TO BE CONTEMPLATED BUT TO FUNCTION AS AN EXTENSION OF THE SUBJECT-AGENT.

Unfortunately, what the Greeks did was to develop an all-embracing, that is, purely objective theory of knowledge-- a systematic explanation of knowledge and its validity, as if knowledge were a thing, an object, rather than a living deed of the exposed subject. Consequently, they developed it not as the subject-agents who are knowing applicatively, but as objects who are contemplated (that is, known) by contemplators. In a way, this is like trying to understand a pair of glasses by seeing it rather than by wearing it and without any relation to what it makes possible for the wearer, by how it looks when seen rather than by what it allows the agent to do.

What the Greeks did will become more perspicuous if we consider what Descartes did , who only pushed the Greek direction to its ultimate consequences . Indeed, the Greeks saw knowledge as the mind becoming intentionally the known, and truth consisted in actual intentional conformity of the mind to reality. But this "intentional" amounts to "visual": the mind sees what is the case, becomes visually what is the case . All that Descartes added, by objectifying the mind in his reflection, was the realization that there was a gap between the mind and the world, and that what the mind saw was its own thought, so that the conformity had to be a matter of resemblance of the mental picture to reality. Descartes came to this realization by placing himself explicitly in the reflective position of someone who is curious, that is, not in the position of somebody who perceives and is exposed to the perceived but in the position of sombody who attempt to understand his own perceiving and to judge of the truth of what he or she perceives . Indeed, by neutralizing all his cares and reducing his ties to the world to the disinterested desire to know, that is, to mere curiosity, Descartes reduced himself to an invulnerable contemplator related to the world as object of contemplation, as pure spectacle . And his theory of knowledge is built on that basis . Of course, only then, and only because he had modified what Husserl would call "the natural attitude" (which, pace Husserl, does not involve belief-that but practical exposure, which is imaginable by the involved subject but not visualizable ) could Descartes doubt the reality (that is, the correspondence of something in the world similar to what he had in his mind) of everything. He had reduced himself to a thinker thinking thoughts who does not know whether the thoughts resemble reality. He had cut the connection between knowing and acting, on the subject's side, and, on the object's side, he had cut the connection between appearing and acting on the subject.

Thus, this is what the Greeks did: their conception of reality, knowledge and truth was contemplative-curious and cut the connection between the knower and the agent, between the world seen and the world as object of exposure.

AND WHAT ABOUT PRACTICAL REASON AMONG THE GREEK PHILOSOPHERS?

For a number of reasons, the question can be asked profitably only about Artistotle , for Plato, following Socrates' lead, collapsed practical reason into theoretical reason, whereas it was Aristotle's merit to realize that practical reason cannot be subsumed under theoretical reason, though perhaps it can be subordinated to the latter as far as human happiness is concerned. The contemplation of the immutable essences, though not of the eternal ideas, remains the telos even in the Ethics , however precariously combined with the life of action.

Aristotle came close to becoming independent from Plato and redressing his mistake. ( De Motu Animalium, the Rhetoric , the Nicomachean Ethics , partly De Anima ). But Aris never completed his independence from Plato. He never gave up the notion of episteme, and with it the works of theoretical reason, very specially the Metaphysics, as his ultimate point of reference . Yet, he came close, or at least opened the way for us to escape from Platonism. The practical syllogism, underpinned by desire and need, can lead to the realization of the primacy of action over assent. And deliberation introduces practical reason , a form of intentional action ( actus humanus ) of which animals are incapable. However, Aris did not undertake the debunking of theoretical reason and of "scientific knowledge" and its implied primacy (given his conception of knowledge and truth), nor does he seem to have even suspected the need to debunk it. He thought that the two were compatible. As a result, he bequeathed us, through the medievals, the philosophical mess in which we have been ever since. The scepticism of post-modernism is the result of the failure of theoretical reason in which we had placed our hope to live up to its promises, as well as of our failure to replace it with practical reason .   

The fact is that Aristotle has two different and mutually exclusive conceptions of knowledge: knowledge as theoretical judgment, and knowledge as practical judgment (which in animals is practical knowledge without judgment). To these correspond two different conceptions of the subject, that is, of the human being, not distinguished by Aristotle: the pure, invulnerable contemplator, and the exposed, needful, vulnerable agent-patient . "Reason" is the natural ability to know characteristic of the human being. However, since Aris views on knowledge correspond to two different and incompatible conceptions of the human being, he consequently has two different and incompatible conceptions of reason: theoretical reason, whose ideal is episteme, and practical reason, whose ideal is appropriate action .

Aris did not notice the incompatibility between theoretical and practical reason. In fact he kept alive both the primacy of theoretical reason and contemplation as the human telos. This is due to the fact that he placed truth in judgment and considered practical judgment capable of truth and falsity as if it were a matter of theoretical evidence and assent, whereas in fact it is a matter of affection and action. It is at this point that the fact that we are linguistic animals misled him, for his interest was concentrated in statements written and read by ('scientific') contemplators rather than spoken and heard by agents . (This without taking into account his Platonic interest in essences and essential truths.( Notice that Aris, like Plato, denies or at least distrusts the truth of statements of particular facts because their truth ceases to be--cf. Suzanne Mansion. This presupposes an idea of truth as intentional conformity of the mind to the real that comes very close to picture ).

In practical judgments there is no evidence (in the sense of Kantian theoretical intuition), and therefore no possibility of proof, because the hearer supplies in part with his or her own evaluative situation in relation to the object what would count as evidence (in other words, the evidence is not objective) , and anyway the point is not to assent to what is stated or can be stated, but to respond appropriately.

Practical reason is the involved agent's reason, or the witness's reason who may become involved in the future. Therefore practical reason is always toward the future and potentially deliberative , since the agent supplies his or her own situation (but on the basis of his or her comprehension of a factual situation taken for granted). However, history is possible, and stories (novels) too, when told from the point of view of agents and in the "narrative past" which imposes the appropriate accommodation of tenses. Unlike stories, history leaves traces, is related to the past of people as their history (roots) or not their history, and may have an explanatory value in terms of self-understanding and understanding of their present world.

Aris places truth in judgment . Truth is the conformity of the judging mind to reality through the understanding of sufficient reason for predicating. A judgment, therefore, is a reflective act, for it presupposes a direct act of (predicative) perception (seeing as?), plus the understanding that in this case  there is evidence for assenting to the prepredicative or simply-apprehensive predication. "The phone on the table" as perceived is neither true nor false, or at best only materially true or false (that is cognitional or intentional conformity of the mind that apprehends to the reality apprehended) . It becomes true or false, formally, when judged, that is, when evidence is grasped that compels the predication. [The evidence is evidence for the predication; that is, it authorizes--and when sufficient, prompts--the predicative act, the reflective act of judgment. Because the true or authorized judgment is deemed to be intentionally identical with the reality judged, assent to the judgment is assent to reality, that is, reality becomes known in itself through the judgment, much as an object becomes seen not in the pair of glasses that are not themselves seen but seen itself through them. [The evidence is for Aris, not for Descartes, sensible (?) or intelligible, immediate (intuitive) or mediate, inductive or deductive].

The important point in the preceding paragraph is that in the case of theoretical judgment, with which the paragraph deals, assent is the outcome of the cognitional process. By contrast, as we shall now see, in the case of practical judgment the outcome is affective action in response, and there is no room for truth or for evidence. ( IF HE HAD SEEN THE INCOMPATIBILITY, AQUINAS COULD NOT HAVE SAID THAT THEOLOGY IS BOTH A THEORETICAL AND A PRACTICAL SCIENCE) .

Let us then move to practical knowledge in Aris , which in people is rational (a matter of theoretical reason , of practical judgment ), and in animals non-rational (a matter of "situational (but non-linguistic) perception").

In De Motu Animalium Aris presents his views on practical knowledge under the form of the "practical syllogism ."

Characteristic of practical syllogism is (1) its connection with desire, and (2) its resulting in action rather than in assent. In other words, the agent who desires something as a goal and to whom the means for the realization of what he desires are available necessarily acts. (Camus put it in terms of authenticity: "For a man who does not cheat, what he believes to be true must determine his action. Belief in the absurdity...").

In NE Aris speaks of the practical syllogism in connection with deliberation, which is a 'rational' and, therefore, exclusively human operation of which animals are incapable. However, in MA he is not concerned with practical reason but with practical knowledge, which is common to people and animals. Animals, according to him, practice the practical syllogism , in a way, though, it seems, as a sort of non-linguistic enthymeme . * A lion wants to eat. A man is available (perceived within reach by the lion). Lion necessarily eats man. -  A dog wants to copulate. A she dog is available. Dog necessarily copulates, unless prevented. (As Nussbaum comments in MA, p. 193, In animals, no distinction between appearance and application: "they follow the appearance of the moment.").

[ * This is interesting because it furnishes the key to the shift from practical to theoretical reason . Since animals are non-linguistic, they cannot verbalize what they perceive-as. Because of this they can only perceive what is for them vitandum or adipiscendum. In other words, their perception can only be practical knowledge. Since they cannot verbalize what they perceive, they cannot verbalize predicatively what they perceive , much less perceive predicatively . Consequently they cannot objectify, they cannot put between themselves and the desired or feared object destances of mere contemplation rather than of exposure, they cannot put the world in abeyance (to use Sartre's expression). They live applicatively , in their present and, as directed by instinct, in their immediate future. They perceive practically, but they do not judge (kwhich is a personal, responsible activity) practically . In other words, they can only perceive by reference to themselves as exposed agents of a certain kind, but they cannot think and judge.  

And this constitutes a fundamental difference between them and people, with very significant consequences. Animals posses practical knowledge, but they do not possess practical reason. People do , because they are linguistic agents.

In De Motu Animalium Aris does not make a clear distinction between animals and people as rational and non-rational. Since his interest is in practical knowledge, in cognitive action that explains "why sometimes cognition leads to action and sometimes not" (MA, p. 185: THE QUESTION SHOUD, RATHER, BE: WHY THE PRESENCE OF OBJECT IS SOMETIMES FOLLOWED BY ACTION AND SOMETIMES NOT), he ignores the distinction between animals and people, for he wants to stress the continuity between animals and people as possessors of practical knowledge. However, the distinction between linguistic and non-linguistic agents is radical. Let us spell it out, not for the specific purpose of completing Aristotle's theory of action, but of detecting the origin of the myth of theoretical knowledge and of the primacy of theoretical reason.

Both animals and people perceive applicatively, but only linguistic agents can verbalize what they perceive . If a six years old child wants bread and there is bread within its reach, the child eats the bread, unless otherwise prevented. So would an animal do, too. This is what in MA Aris calls a practical syllogism. However, the child can do what the animal cannot: the child can verbalize its desire and its belief that the bread is available if the child does X. At the child's young age verbalization may make no significant difference as far as the concrete action of getting the bread is concerned. But let the child grow and become reflective. Thanks to its being linguistic agent who can verbalize (think by itself, without immediate relation to himself or herself, but as object of predication) the object experienced, even when not hungry the child will perceive "bread on the table," that is, will perceive bread not as mihi-edendum and start converting it into object of contemplative predication: "A piece of bread is on the table." Later bread will become for the child object of abstract thought and talk, that is, not as this or that piece but as abstract concept of bread, as abstract object of predication, as in itself independently of its desirability. Essential and accidental properties, empirical and explanatory properties will be distinguished.

As Aris realizes, animals of the highest species do not act according the a simple S-R model in which the availability of the object under conditions of desire prompts the action, always in the same way; they themselves create the conditions of availability, learn by trial and error to look for what satisfies their needs (sort of teleological action . Their improved learned skills, their diversified activities, their social interactions, their individualized ways of pursuing the goods and avoiding the evils determined by their respective biological programs show an ability to memorize and generalize and respond applicatively to the particular situation. Nonetheless, Aris denies that they KNOW (AS PEOPLE KNOW), in that they are incapable of theoretical and practical judgments . What does this mean?

As far as theoretical knowledge goes, Aris denies that animals know for two interconnected reasons: (a) because they cannot escape the sphere of practical interest, that is, the dimension of exposure, so that they only memorize and generalize what is useful for their future action; (b) because they cannot abstract and conceptualize the object to convert it into object of predication, so that they remain within the field of the imaginable. . The two reasons are interconnected. Indeed, to escape the sphere of practical interest, abstraction and conceptualization are required, for only in this way can the object be considered by itself, independently of its usefulness for the agent. But, conversely, to abstract and conceptualize the object one needs to cease considering it in terms of its usefulness for the agent, to want to grasp it by itself. Which is to say that people belong in an entirely different order than animals because they are linguistic agents and this renders possible for them what is impossible for animals, namely, Knowledge with a capital K.

In the practical sphere, Knowledge makes possible what Aris presents in his Ethics, namely an agent capable of decision and action, that is, an agent who determines himself or herself to desire and act rationally. Two questions need to be answered in order to understand this point. First, how and why does this differ from the way animals act? Second, what is involved in acting rationally?

(1) How does this differ from the way animals act?

One characteristic of practical knowledge by contrast to theoretical knowledge is that it is desirous knowledge, that is, knowledge informed by desire. Now, the action of the higher animals is not, according to Aristotle, purely mechanical, for it involves application based on past experience; thus there is in the higher animals a sort of knowledge inspired by desire that results in not purely mechanical actions but in actions guided by the passively received goal . Compared to human action, though, the action of even the higher animals has a uniformity that human action overcomes. And this in at least two ways . In animal action the concrete desired goals are determined by instinct and are determinant of action, though the means can be improved by experience. As Nussbaum says, "animals follow the appearance of the moment" (MA, 193). Now, people cannot only delay their response; they can abstain from responding in the way that instinctoid desire suggests, that is, people can actively invent their goal . A hungry person may abstain from eating when food is available: hunger strike. The appearance--desired food--is not followed; instead, another appearance is followed which is not passively received but invented as goal to be followed , as mihi desirandum et adipiscendum . For this kind of action the means are, objectively, as available to animals as they are to people, since the means are mere abstention; but they are not available subjectively, in that it is not possible for animals to avail themselves of abstention. To animals the desiderandum et adipiscendum when the means are available is perceptually given in the apupearance. By contrast, people invent the appearance to be realized. And if they just respond to the given appearance as animals do, they do it by choice, since they could abstain from responding and choose their own response.

Thus people can invent and choose goals, because they can abstain from responding according to instinctoid desire. And, as we shall see, they can also invent mekans that are not presently available, so as to render possible the realization of a goal that was previously impossible . But why can they, whereas animals cannot? Is this a surd brute fact?

No. Here their linguistic ability intervenes and makes possible what is impossible to animals. People can speak about that to which they respond even in its absence and when they are not exposed to it. This objectification creates a distance between the object and the response within which the appropriateness of the response habitually prompted by the object may be considered. In addition, most of the responses of linguistic subjects are learned and culturally conditioned, not biologically inherited. This cultural induction by which objects for response are defined and responses reinforced positively or negatively is for the most part linguistically done, so that the object itself is perceived as demanding or at least justifying the culturally induced response, and correlatively the culturally induced response is automatically taken for appropriate. Because the responses themselves, as well as their correlate objects, are informed by culturally induced attitudes which are not the result of irresistible and unmodifiable instinctive pulls, these acquired attitudinal desires and aversions are, at least in principle--because the force of habit combined with inherited predispositions may be overwhelming--controllable and modifiable. The human agent shaped by culture has, therefore, a lived experience of conflict between spontaneous desire or aversion and induced attitudinal desire or aversion that informs actual action and curves spontaneous desire and aversion and even supplants them through habituation. (Remeber Civilization and its Discontents , with its principles of reality and desire). ( REMEBER THAT "ANIMALS DO NOT BECOME AKRATIC" - Nussbaum, MA 201). Moreover, as part of his or her acculturation, the human agent not only experiences linguistically the reality to be responded to, but through the explanatory and justificatory process through which acculturation is reinforced is used to talk about objects and responses to objects objectively, by themselves, and to think of responses in terms of appropriateness.

But what is the standard by which appropriateness is adjudicated? There is not one single standard but a combination of them. In general, however, the relevant considerations have to do with the purely technical adequacy of the action to realize the goal pursued or  with the appropriateness of the goal chosen.

Let us consider the former first, then the latter .

The former has to do, in the first place, with the possibility of realization of the goal chosen . And this, in its turn, depends dialectically on the availability of the means to the agent who chooses the goal for his or her action. Only a goal that appears concretely possible because the means appear to be available to the agent can be chosen as a goal. But in certain cases the means have not only to be implemented because already available but invented, like agriculture for an instance, or the cure of aids . This invention depends on 'the nature of the object itself,' independently of desire. Here is where objective thinking enters and with it what might be called theoretical reason .

The desire of a cure for aids leads to looking for how and why aids produces its ill effects, and to what--and how and why--it reacts. If aids were not an illness to be done away with (observed syndrome), aids as its to be known cause to be suppressed would not exist for us. Thus the search is teleological and interested, informed by desire. But the search is objective, that is independent of desire, and therefore presupposes no desire in the researcher other than the desire to understand the object, for here the subject is totally passive to the object. The fit in this case is world to word, and there is no deliberation-declaration possible. It is only when the object is known and the means to do away with it are known and made available, that the action-conclusion of the practical syllogism will obtain, and it will be implemented not by the researchers but by physicians. In other words, the objective knowledge of the cause understood applicatively as to be suppressed led to the objective knowledge of the means, which understood applicatively led to their making them available, all of which understood applicatively led to the action by the physician.

What then is theoretical knowledge, at least in a case of this type? It is knowledge of the object by itself abstracted from all irrelevant relations and knowledge of the means to be employed to doaway with it, which renders possible choosing doing away with aids as an end. Certainly animals are incapable of this knowledge for they do not discover-invent ends (their ends are passively received), or means to ends not passively received.

But what is the status of theoretical knowledge when it is not applied? Is it a picture of aids and of the means to do away with it? Let us say that the conclusion of research is that aids is a virus of such a kind and that it responds favorably to such a dose of X. Notice that this kind of technical knwoledge is only knowledge (that is, actually functions as knowledge) for the technically prepared people capable of using it applicatively, not for us laymen who hear these statements as pictures of reality. Saying that aids is a virus means that it is to be treated as a virus rather than as something else, much as saying to you that your mother is on the phone is saying that she is to be so responded by you now. But is not saying that your mother is on the phone something true, independently of whether you respond to it or not?

If by something true we mean that that is where your mother is right now exposed to action, so that what knowledge yields and is supposed to yield for the knower is objects for response and that the truth of statements is to be heard applicatively and cashed in terms of action, then saying that your mother is on the phone is saying something true. In other words, there is no purely theoretical knowledge, for even the most theoretical knowledge is to be understood applicatively by exposed agents. Knowledge, then, is a sort of purposefully built pair of glasses that makes possible appropriate response to what is known. In short, theoretical reason is subordinated to practical reason.

A sticky point remains obscure. Concrete objects for response are always known as to be responded to surrounded by other objects and known by a subject who is also surrounded by other objects. In other words, the object of perceptual encounter is always a figure in a background for a subject-agent who also acts from a particular situation, that is, surrounded  by other objects as subject-figure in a bacground. Now, the background of the object for response is perceived but only subsidiarily (that is, is not known), and the subject's background is not known either, perhaps not even perceived but only subsidiarily sensed. Yet we attribute factual reality to all these traits. For instance, when "your mother is on the phone," I perceive the phone by the table at a certain distance from the chair and from me. This is another way of saying that perception is not only perspectival but practically-perspectival, that is, situational, that the object to be responded to is in a concrete hodological space where practical distances to be cleared unfold from the agent, where the background objects possess a coefficient of instrumentality that is favorable or adverse to the realization of the subject-agent, and that the subject-agent is himself exposed in the world. This, precisely, is the reason why, knowledge being essentially practical and by reference to a project to be realized by an exposed subject, that is, by a subject-object of exposure, knowledge is essentially relative and cannot be hypostasized and absolutized in itself, for what is object for response for me is background for you whose project is different, and what is object for response for me now can become background for me in a moment without itself changing except in relation to my project.

Appearances--that is, "contents' of experiences of objects by subjects--are obviously relative. They are appearances of this exposed object to this subject so situated, and they are not to be contemplated in themselves but to be instrumentally transcended toward action. In fact they are so 'read' when the subject is involved in action, not as pictures to be contemplated but as guiding maps. To attempt to identify the reality with its appearances in subjects does not make sense, much less to try to identify the reality with a selected series of its appearances, which in general tend to be visual appearances.

I have begun to fall into the very trap I am criticizing. I am now reflecting as a contemplator. I have isolated the knowing activity as if it were our only relationship to the surrounding world, and an instantaneous one at that. I have forgotten that we know as exposed subjects, that is as subjects-objects of exposure to other subjects-object of exposure to us. Action is temporal interaction, that is, involves passion. Successful action is not the one that succeeds immediately only to fail subsequently, but the one that has the best chances of succeeding all in all in the temporal, finite flux and reflux of reciprocal relations. Successful action across time demands successful passion across time, as successful a lived history as it can be in an imperfect world and insofar as it depends on me. My project of which you are part should involve taking you as subject affected, not as mere object instrument. So knowledge which is always embedded in a project is, to an extent, a matter of choice. The point is not to interpret the world but to build the world.

Where in this process does the mistake of the independence and primacy of theoretical reason take place? Let us begin by remarking that there is indeed, thanks to language, a bifurcation in human experience: from situational perception with distances of exposure unfolding from the needful-desirous subject, to objectification-conceptualization resulting in abstract object in itself without  distances, object of sensible and intelligible (explanatory) predications which may be true or false. By contrast, the field of practical reason does do have the structure of a world inhabited by the subject and by all that or those4 to which or to whom the subject is exposed.

It has to take place where the divorce between knowing and acting is consummated, that is, when the object is merely thought in absence and when, therefore, predication (that is, judgment) appears at best as true, at worst as false. Then arise the notions of reality as to be contemplated, of truth as conformity of judgment to reality, of judgment as justified theoretical predication, and of knowledge as consisting in this process disconnected from action. ]  

Why, then, does Aris speak of the practical syllogism only in connection with deliberation and as if it were an exclusively human conduct or way of knowing? He speaks of it in connection with deliberation because, in the particular context of the Ethics , Aris is interested not in desire but in rational desire, not in action but in action regulated by a conception of happiness that is object of rational desire. But does Aris speak of practical judgment only in connection with deliberation? I think so, for animals perceive practically, but they do not judge practically. What I above characterized as "non-linguistic enthymemes" of animas are really not enthymemes, which presuppose a linguistic agent who judges and combines judgments, but practical perceptions.

Thus, for Aris, there are two kinds of desires and two kinds of objects: animal desire and human desire; object of animal desire and object of human (either rational or irrational, but in any case freely chosen as an end, or at least freely adhered to) desire. In other words, man invents and brings about reality, though not arbitrarily but on the basis of the existing material (and cultural?) conditions. Here "real" is not object known (or knowable) but object feared or desired (or desirable), that is, what is capable of satisfying need or desire or of preventing the satisfaction of need or desire, and what is desired or avoided not for its own sake but for the sake of something else.
