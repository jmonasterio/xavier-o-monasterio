THE POINT OF VIEW OF EXTERIORITY

In the introduction to the chapter on "The Body"

Sartre wrote:

Therefore we have deliberately chosen  the physical point of view--i.e., the point of view of the outside, of exteriority--in order to study the problem of vision; we have considered a dead eye in the midst of the world in order to account for the visibility of this world. Consequently, how can we be surprised later when consciousness, which is absolute interiority, refuses to allow itself to be bound to its object? The relations which I establish between the Other's body and the external object are really existing relations, but they have for their being the being of the for-others; they suppose a center of intra-mundane flow in which knowledge is a magic property of space, "action at a distance." From the start they are placed in the perspective of the other as object. If then we wish to reflect on the nature of the body, it is necessary to establish an order of our reflections which conforms to the order of being. (BN, pp. 403-404).

What Sartre says of the problem of vision applies in general to the problem of reality and affects most particularly the proper understanding of the human being as well as the issue of the Other's existence. I will unpack this.

What does Sartre mean by "the point of view of exteriority"? The point of view, not of the eye that sees, but of the eye that is seen by another eye, by a seeing eye. As for Sartre, he wants to understand the eye that sees, which he thinks we have confused and wrongly identified with the eye seen. For this purpose he considers it essential to retrieve the point of view of the eye that sees. More concretely, Sartre wants to retrieve the subject who sees, not the object others see when they see the subject. This is what he means by "the point of view of interiority."

There are two ways, however, not distinguished by Sartre, of understanding the point of view of exteriority: the physicist's or the physiologist's, on the one hand, and the psychologist's, on the other (assuming that the psychologist functions as a genuine psychologist, not as a physiologist or a physicist). Insofar as the eye that sees is a mechanism, the point of view of exteriority is the appropriate one. In this case, the point of view of interiority would be not only inappropriate but inapplicable. (Since Sartre radically separates  consciousness, which is pure interiority for him, from the eye-mechanism, he ends up in a Kantian dualism). The psychologist, on the other hand, should not take the point of view of exteriority, for his object is a subject and cannot therefore be understood by taking on it the point of view that is appropriate for mere objects. But the psychologist is not himself the subject he attempts to understand and even help. Try as he may, he cannot become his subject. Therefore, he cannot help taking on his subject an exterior point of view. It is, however, not the point of view of a detached "observer," which is the physicist's and the physiologist's point of view, but of a concerned "witness." There are, therefore, two different ways of understanding the point of view of exteriority: as the point of view of the observer, and as the point of view of the witness. Sartre would have gained by distinguishing them.

It may be argued that Sartre did indeed distinguish them, since he distinguished "my body for me" from "my body for the Other," and "myself for me" from "myself for the Other." In this case, it would be a matter of terminology. Both the "witness" and the "agent" would belong within what Sartre calls "the point of view of interiority." Such, however, does not seem to be the case. For Sartre, there is no possibility of the witness's standpoint. Either I see you as object (however 'dangerous'), or I am seen by you as subject. Your eye seen is not you as subject seeing me, it is you object-for-me seen by me. That is why Sartre has to write different chapters on the Other and on the Body, because consciousness is pure interiority and the body pure exteriority. This lack of distinction by Sartre creates problems for him. However, let us provisionally ignore this confusion.

When Sartre complains that we have chosen the point of view of exteriority, what is he complaining about? That subjects have been taken for, and misunderstood as, objects, because they have been approached from the point of view of I defined as the point of view of the detached observer and identified with the point of view of the physicist or the physiologist. According to Sartre, the eye that sees should not be confused with the eye seen; the subject who acts should not be confused with the object whose behavior is observed. Why not? Because the object "in the midst of the world" is not surrounded by a world exposed to his action and is not himself exposed to the action of the surrounding world, which is what constitutes the agent as agent.

   (I cannot agree more with Sartre on this fundamental point.My differences with him concern his ontology. A step is missing in Sartre. He does not realize that an ontology (as distinguished from a metaphysics) is an abstraction from history (shared fundamental histories) and histories. In other words, the dramatic character of life is what has the primacy. And there is no such dramatic character without concrete lived histories).

It seems therefore, as a first approximation, that the point of view of interiority is the point of view of an exposed subject, and the point of view of exteriority is the point of view of a not exposed subject. But we need to be more precise. The point of view of exteriority is a point of view on something which is object for the person who has the point of view, it is not the point of view of this "something."  This is one characteristic of the point of view of exteriority. In Sartre's terms, it is not the point of view of the eye that sees, but of the eye seen. The second characteristic of the point of view of exteriority is that the person who has the point of view is not exposed to that which he or she sees, to the eye seen. The seen is in

his or her field of vision, not in his or her field of action. The seer himself is mere seer, not patient exposed to the action of the surrounding world. In other words, the person who has the point of view of exteriority functions as subject, but as subject who is just an observer and by whom the object is considered just as object of observation. (A good illustration of this is the Savage's reluctance to be used as guinea-pig, that is, as just an object of observation). It is in this way that the physicist or the physiologist are related to the eye seen, to their object of study. Sartre's main point is that this approach to vision will fail to understand the subject who sees. More generally, this approach to the human being will fail to understand the human being because it is doomed to see the human being as a mere object.

By contrast to the point of view of exteriority, which is a point of view on something, the point of view of interiority is the point of view of the "something," not of somebody else's on the something. Secondly, it is the point of view of "something exposed," that is, of someone who has, not merely a field of vision, but a field of action-passion.

Now, in what sense have we, as Sartre says, chosen the point of view of exteriority? In two senses, I think. First, sometimes, we have tried to understand subjects, beings who have a point of view, as things, which have no point of view. This is "scientism," an obviously bad mistake. It amounts to privileging as "the really real" what is only what a detached observer observes. Second--and more important for Sartre's purposes and my own--we have tried to understand subjects-exposed from the point of view of an unexposed-subject, of a mere observer; but this is not the point of view of involved subjects and, therefore, we are fatally bound to misunderstand them.

In what way is this the "perspective of the Other," that at best reveals the subject's being for the Other? Sartre says that "the relations I establish between the other's body and the external object are really existing relations, but they have for their being the being of the for-Others." Concretely, then, when you situate me as being "ten yards from the bench," it is a real relationship you are establishing, but not my relationship as subject but as object, as body-thing.  Needless to say, since for Sartre

"consciousness is absolute interiority," he is contradicting himself here, for a consciousness that is pure interiority cannot have any relationship of distance to anything, anymore than it can have a body that is genuinely its body. In fact, however, my distance from the table is very real and very much my relationship to the table (not merely the distance of my body to the table), because I am not a consciousness that is pure interiority mysteriously entrapped in a body that is pure exteriority, but a bodily-consciousness. This is why, as Descartes remarked, I do not need to observe my knee to realize that it has been hurt, whereas the captain of a sheep has to observe the sheep to realize that it has been pierced.

Thus, the only point Sartre has here--but a mighty important one--is that the understanding of objective distances established by a mere observer is, in the case of humans, a mere abstraction from their distances as subjects-agents, and an abstraction that ignores and, consequently, disfigures their actual subjective distances as agents as well as the distances of that which is distant from them, for these distances are dramatic distances . And what applies

to space applies to time too: the subject's temporality is

dramatic temporality .

Let us be more concrete. From a dog barking at me and ready to pounce on me I am separated by three yards and two seconds. But these are three yards and two seconds of terror. From the dog I want to get rid of by throwing a stone at it I am separated by three yards and by two seconds, but this distance is distance of dramatic possibility, underpinned by a counter-finality that may frustrate my plan. Certainly, the distance of three yards and two seconds that separate me from the barking dog is real and can be measured in the same terms as the distance that separates the door from the table. But the dramatic distance that separates me from the barking dog cannot be reduced to the neutral distance that separates the table from the door. And if it is so reduced, it is falsified and misunderstood. And the distance of dramatic possibility that separates me from the stoned dog can only be calculated by someone who sees me as a subject, by someone who reads my project in my gesture of threatening the dog with a stone. Now, in physics, space and time are "external relations"

established by an objective observer; they are distances of the table to the door, or of the door to the table (for these distances, being neutral or a-dramatic, are arbitrarily reversible) for the Other-observer . This is due to the fact that neither the door nor the table is subject of exposure; they can, at best, function as objects of exposure. But even this is beyond the concern of the physicist as such, because he studies entities as physical, that is, in their thinghood. Given the physicist's intention of understanding and the abstractive attention this intention introduces into the nature of the objects studied, "the point of view of exteriority" is the only possible one for the physicist as such. And a perfectly legitimate and fruitful one for the physicist as such . But is it necessarily the legitimate and fruitful point of view for the physicist (or the philosopher, for that matter, or the psychologist)) as human beings?

Certainly, to start with, it is not the only possible point of view, for the point of view of the involved agent and of the witness--both of which belong in the order of exposure--are possible too. But is it a legitimate point of

view? And is it a fruitful one?

It is legitimate, within its proper limits. The physicist takes it, considering the human body as any other kind of thing. And it is productive and fruitful for the physicist purposes and within the sphere of physics. But that does not make it necessarily fruitful in all spheres and for all purposes, unless we assume against all evidence that it is the only possible point of view. The question, therefore, is for what purposes and in what way can the point of view of exposure be legitimate and fruitful.

First of all, that is our point of view as agents. That is not a matter of choice. Whether or not we like it, we take ourselves as exposed agents-patients and the surrounding world as exposed to our action. And always our subsequent experience, even in the cases in which we are mistaken, confirms this self-understanding in relation to the world and of the world in relation to us.

Secondly, when we function as witnesses, we understand as witnesses, not as mere objective observers, and this by taking the agent's point of view with its dramatic structure. This means that as witnesses, too, we find ourselves situated within the same world as the agent,

because of our experience of exposure as agents, even though we may disagree with a particular agent in a particular case as to what the agent is exposed to, or as to the appropriate response to it, or as to the actual response of the agent to it.

Thirdly, if we attempt the approach of the point of view of exteriority to human action, we simply do not understand it. In other words, we cannot develop, as agents, any expectations  about other agents or brace ourselves to confront what might await us. By contrast, the point of view of interiority allows us to develop that kind of expectations, however fallible they may be, which is the confirmation of its legitimacy and fruitfulness.

Now I want to consider Sartre's charge that we have deliberately chosen, or at least privileged, the point of view of exteriority with disastrous results. Since this applies particularly to the issues related to God, I will take the Scholastics as my prime example.

The Scholastics and the Point of view of Exteriority

One complication that arises in the case of the Scholastics is that they  work immediately on a written

document--the Bible--not directly on the world around them. The world is already mediated for them by the Bible. Perhaps this complication can be cleared by comparing their case to Descartes'.

Descartes sees the table. The Scholastics are told that there is such a table. If Descartes functioned in the sphere of exposure, the being there of the table would motivate him to act in one way or another, depending on his particular project at the time. But he is not functioning in that capacity. His cares are taken care of, and he is just a curious observer. He objectifies or phenomenalizes the table. He will not judge that the table is there until and unless he finds sufficient reason (intellectually compelling 'evidence') for assenting to its existence. Moreover, Descartes makes no difference between the existence of the table and the existence of a human being he sees . The two are, for him, potential objects of assent--nothing less, but nothing more. How does the Scholastics' case differ from Descartes'?

For one, the Scholastics already assent to God's existence when they start their inquiry, unlike Descartes who does not assent to the existence of the table when he

starts his inquiry. Secondly, they assent, not because of intrinsic evidence, but because of God's authority, unlike Descartes who will come to assent (supposedly) only on the basis of intrinsic evidence. (However, though the Scholastics do not realize it,their assent is of the order of exposure; in other words, it is "consent" rather than "assent." For instance, they consent with Abraham's consent to God's demand to sacrifice Abraham. Now, although their reason for assenting differs from Descartes who does not consent to the existence of the table he sees because of God's authority, their consent is of the same order as Descartes' consent as an agent. In this regard, therefore, there is no fundamental difference. But there is no fundamental difference either in the way the Scholastics treat their issues, philosophically . By rendering, for instance, God's existence questionable, they reduce it to mere intellectual, hypothetical presentation. In other words, they objectify or phenomenalize it. And they look, as Descartes did, for intellectually compelling evidence to assent to it. There is, therefore, no fundamental difference between Descartes and the Scholastics concerning assent, as far as Scholastic philosophy goes. Furthermore, exactly as

Descartes, the Scholastics make no difference between the existence of a table and the existence of a subject--God, in this case .

Let us now come to our point: the Scholastics and the point of view of exteriority. When the Scholastics read the Bible as a history, they function within the same world as Abraham--a world to which both Abraham and them are spatio-temporally exposed. At that point, that is, as believers, they function as agents exposed to God, to the same God Abraham was exposed. But then, as philosophers, they phenomenalize or objectify the world and start functioning as mere observers. The God to whom they consent as agents becomes mere object of possible assent, a proposition.

They have taken the point of view of exteriority in both senses: first, their own point of view is not anymore of exposed agent toward (possible) object of exposure, but of mere contemplator; second, and by the same token, they have transformed God and Abraham who interact as agents into mere 'objects' from which no distances of exposure unfold . Only a story can preserve the distances of exposure . That is why the Bible is primarily historical. But by hearing it, as philosophers, not as a sequence of dramatically

interconnected stories but as a collection of statements, the Scholastics completely denatured it.

We can see how this shift from the order of exposure to that of contemplation affected the Scholastic reading of the Bible. The reader of the Bible, whether a believer or not, reads it as a story that s/he witnesses imaginarily, from the standpoint of a witness who, because of the spatio-temporal separation from the events, can function only as a witness. Since stories preserve or replicate the order of exposure, the reader understands the Bible as it is meant to be understood, as a narrative, that is, as reflecting the order of exposure with its dramatic character. Thus, when the reader reads "God said to Abraham", the reader does not read a statement about God, anymore than Abraham heard a statement about God when he heard God speaking to him. What the reader gets is God's linguistic action by which Abraham was addressed by God in the order of exposure. And, as a witness, the reader understands Abraham exposed to God's linguistic action. Similarly, when the reader reads that what God said was "Sacrifice Isaac," the reader does not read a statement about God, anymore than Abraham did. What the reader gets is God demanding Isaac's sacrifice from

Abraham and Abraham exposed to God's demand. (This can be easily realized by comparing a story to an actually witnessed play, where the spectator, hearing the voice of God, does not hear a statement about God, but God speaking to Abraham). And all this as a portion of the dramatic event of which God and Abraham are part, as a development tending toward a dramatic outcome, as part and parcel of a spatio-temporal-dramatic unit, which is what gives the narrative its unity as a narrative.

By contrast, when the Scholastic reads the same, he reads it as statement or statements about God saying and about what God said. In other words, the Scholastics deprived themselves of the possibility of understanding God as an agent, which led them to misunderstand revelation as primarily a matter of statements by a God-contemplator, as well as of the possibility of understanding the faithful as agents exposed to God, which led them to misunderstand faith as assent to statements made by a God-contemplator .

Dramatic spatio-temporality

A narrative possesses a dramatic unity, much as a geometric demonstration possesses a logical unity. In other

words, the elements involved in a narrative--agent(s), surrounding world, space and time--are interrelated, so that all of them together constitute a unified whole rather than a mere collection of scattered elements.  Obviously, however, the dramatic unity of a narrative is very different from the logical unity of a demonstration. In what, precisely, does the dramatic unity of narratives consist? It is constituted by a space which is a field of action-passion of an agent rather than a field of mere contemplation, a field of exposure with an open-ended temporality toward a future, while the present is co-defined by the agent's being present and by that to which the agent is present. Both the space and the time are dramatic in that they are structured by the final outcome(s) intended by the subject(s) involved and by the counter-finality or "index of resistance" of the world surrounding the agent(s). The future is open-ended in that what it will bring about is contingent and unknown to the agent(s) involved. Though open-ended, however, it is "hodologically structured" toward the outcome(s) desired by the agent(s) involved. And the temporality of a narrative is dramatic through and through because of the constant tension, until the final outcome obtains, between the

desired outcome(s) and the actual outcome, which may or may not coincide with each other.

The contingency of the future deserves attention in order to distinguish the temporality of a narrative from the temporality of actual action .

The tense of narratives is past; the tense of action is present. This means that narratives replicate action as if it were taking place in the present, that is, before the eyes of the reader as a witness of the action; but in fact the action witnessed by the reader has already taken place and its outcome is already known at least to the author of the narration.

The narrative, however, replicates the dramatic temporality of the action in that the agent is presentified in relation to his contingent, unknown future. But it is a replication 'as-if'. It is presentification of the past as if it were still future in relation to the participants, though it is past and known in relation to the author, and past and perhaps already known in relation to the reader.

Because the narrative is subtended by the agent(s)'s desire for a future and uncertain outcome, subject to the counter-finality of the surrounding world, it replicates the

dramatic character of human life. But because of the past-tenseness of narratives, it replicates it merely 'as-if.'

Why is this difference important? Its importance has to do with the question whether life is or is not dramatic.

One of the kings of France, Louis XIII, who kept a diary, used to write as the entry for most of his days: "nothing."

And there are many days in the lives of many of us the entry for which, if we kept a diary, would or should be "nothing."

Does this mean that our lives are not dramatic, and that a narration superimposes on life a structure which does not belong to it? I don't think so.In any case, if that were true, the point of view of exteriority would be the appropriate one. And, yet, we do not recognize in it the structure of our lives, and we recognize in the dramatic structure of narratives the structure of our lives.

The life of Louis XIII may have been a very dull life for several different reasons. Perhaps he was unimaginative and abulic, with very few desires and projects. But he had some desires, at least the desire to eat and go on living. What may well have happened to King Louis is not so much that he did not desire anything, but that his desires were satisfied as a matter of course without nay effort on his

part, that he did not have to act himself in order to bring about the desired outcomes because his desires were orders for other agents to act. In that case, the tension between desired outcome and actual outcome was lessened almost to the point of non-existence. For only when a desire can be frustrated can there be a dramatic tension between the outcome desired and the actual outcome. The acuteness of the tension depends on the intensity of the desire, on the importance attached to the realization of the desired outcome, and on the strength of the counter-finality of the surrounding world, that is, of the index of resistance of the world to the realization of the desired outcome. There are lives, and moments in many or even all lives, when there is nothing explicitly desired, or nothing desired intensely, or nothing important intensely desired, or no high index of resistance in the world. For all these reasons King Louis's life may have appeared to be a-dramatic, uneventful. Yet, these moments or even long periods are dramatic in that they constitute the background by contrast to which the dramatic ones take their meaning. And there is no conceivable life of a human adult that has no dramatic moments or periods, though many lives are not presided by a constant, over-

driving desire, but rather by a number of different, successive ones. Furthermore, there are many lives that would not make the subject of a good, that is, intensely dramatic narrative, or would need an exceptionally skilled narrator to be such. This shows that the dramatic quality of a narrative depends not only on the dramatic quality of the events narrated but also on the dramatic skills of the narrator. The most dramatic subject-matter of a story may make a dull story in the hands of a clumsy author, and the less dramatic story may be intensely dramatic in the hands of the right narrator. It is certain, therefore, that a real life is never exactly as narrated, for any real life involves an enormous amount of background elements that are never integrated into the narrative, which is obviously dependent on the choices of the narrator. But all this does not detract from the fact that every life possesses a dramatic structure, in that we live constantly a life structured by desired outcomes which are always potentially in danger of being frustrated by the counter-finality of the world, since the future is always contingent and unknown to the agent. The dramatic character of human life, which gives its unity to each of its episodes, consists therefore in the

tension between the outcome desired by the agent(s), which is always contingent and future while the action lasts, and the actual outcome which becomes known to the agent(s) (and, potentially, to others) and enjoyed or suffered by the agent(s), only when the action is over.

It seems clear, then, that the dramatic character of life is a structure of life itself, not an invention of literature. But this does not quite take care of the question that was bothering me in relation to narrative as a presentification of the past, as a replication 'as if' of the dramatic character of life, whereas action takes place in the present. It is one thing to say that life is narratable; it is quite another thing to say that life is a narrative, which I don't think it is. And that was the point of stressing that a narrative is presentification 'as if,' which life is not. But this needs unpacking.

If life is presentification, it is presentification of a real or imaginary past by an author to a reader. Obviously, life is not like that, for many reasons. First, life is in the present toward the future, which was the point that my remark stressed. Second, the agent is exposed; neither the author nor the narrator are. Third, at least in

the case of historical narration, the author knows the final outcome, which was unknown to the agent. Fourth, the agent was making history, encountering the surrounding world and trying to bring it to the desired state; not telling or writing or replicating history. It is clear, then, that life is not a narrative; it may well be the basis for a text, but it is not itself a text.

Yet, I want to maintain that life is dramatic and narratable. How so? Let us take the standpoint of the agent.

The agent is conscious of the surrounding world. But his consciousness is not a contemplator's consciousness, but the consciousness of an exposed subject. His distances are distances of exposure, and so is his temporality. In  this sense, it is consciousness of the possibilities of the surrounding world on him, on the basis of which the agent carves his own possibilities and co-determines (together with the possibilities of the world) his own project. This project, of course, may in certain cases be totally imposed on him by the possibilities of the world; for instance, my project of taking a leisurely walk may be changed into the project of escaping from my house if a fire erupts. In the order of exposure, in any given circumstance, the

possibilities of the world may be stronger than the agent's possibilities, or the other way around. This amounts to saying that the initiative is metastable and can, successively, change from the world to the agent and vice-versa. Now, consciousness of object of exposure is, by its very nature, consciousness of self-exposed-to-object. Which is to say that self-consciousness of exposure is the correlate of the consciousness of object of exposure. This is the basis on which the agent can recognize or not recognize himself in a narrative of the events in which he was involved, and cannot but fail to recognize himself and his world in a description that systematically neutralizes the order of exposure. Another way of putting the same: the self-consciousness of exposure is the basis on which the agent can function later as his own witness (autobiographer) and his own critic. Furthermore, the adult agent is aware, by virtue of the pluri-personal language he speaks and is spoken to and about, of living under the eyes of (potential) witnesses, that is, of potential authors of other, and perhaps different, narratives of the events in which he is involved--authors who may have different standards of appropriateness to criticize his actions. In other words,

although life is dramatic and narratable, this does not imply that only one narrative of the events in which an agent was involved is possible. But it at least establishes that the agent's understanding of himself and the world is dramatic understanding , (that is what " existential understanding " means) and that only narratives can do justice to the dramatic character of life. (By contrast, animals cannot invent their own projects, cannot be their own witnesses, cannot live under the eyes of potential or actual witnesses-critics, cannot be their own critics, and cannot narrate their lives).

Plurality of conflictive narratives, and Truth

We just touched the issue of the plurality of narratives of the same event. In this plurality, the narrative of the agent has no privilege over the narrative of a witness. Even the self-recognition of an agent in a narrative of the events in which he was involved amounts only to the recognition of his own intentions in relation to his own world,, not to a vindication of the appropriateness of his intentions and of the reality of his world. This seems to imply that there is no 'truth' to narratives. Is this correct? To answer this question we have to consider a

few issues: the importance of the last act or episode in a narrative, the connection between the last act and the existence of God, and the connection of the standard of appropriateness with both the last act and the existence of God.

The last act or episode in a narrative is particularly important in that it closes the narrative by closing its future and fixes definitively the shape of the character(s) involved as well as their success or lack thereof in achieving the desired outcome(s). In real life,  the last act is always death--a final defeat, insofar as it is an undesirable outcome. But death is the last act and a final defeat only if there is no life after death, if death is really the closure, and if the last act in this life is really the last act. Now, for the Christian, for instance, death, however undesirable, is not the last act nor a final defeat. Thus, the question of the last act, whose importance is undeniable, is closely connected with the question of God's existence and the kind of God that exists, for the question whether or not death is the closure of the last act is closely connected, at least in  the case of Christianity, with God's existence.

Furthermore, the agent, given the extent of his foreseeance and the intensity of his care, is irremediably concerned with the appropriateness of his actions. But appropriateness may be considered on different levels. On one level, appropriateness is measured in terms of its efficiency as a means to achieve the desired partial or final outcome. But appropriateness has to be considered also concerning the end, the subject who pursues the end, and the circumstances in which the end is pursued. The end is whatever final outcome is desired and pursued by an agent. Now, an agent's actions and their effects sometimes involve other people who also desire outcomes which may be in conflict with the outcome desired by the agent of whom I am talking. Is it appropriate for an agent at any time and in any circumstances to pursue an end just because he desires it, regardless of other agents and the effects of his actions on them? This is the question of ethical appropriateness which cannot be simply ignored. Finally, there is the question of the religious appropriateness of actions, which cannot, either, be simply dismissed a priori. Assume that God exists and participates in the fray, as Christians do. If so, God also has desired outcomes, whether

He pursues them actively or simply desires them. In that case, are not the other participants rationally and ethically constrained to take God's desires into account? Can they safely ignore God's desires? Are they entitled, as His creatures, to ignore them or pursue outcomes that conflict with God's desires? This is the question of religious appropriateness.

How does this relate to the question of the truth of narratives? The plurality of histories in the making and of histories told, when they are incompatible with each other, arises from the absence of standards of appropriateness, or from the unhierarchical plurality of standards of appropriateness. There is history rather than a mere succession of states of the world because there are outcomes desired with which the states of the world may or may not coincide, and because the participants do not have the desired outcomes dictated by instinct but conceive and pursue them as their own projects. But as long as there are  no common standards concerning the desires, the outcomes, and the pursuit of them, there is not one history but a plurality of individual histories, each of which ends with the individual's death, each of them lived and told from the

individual's point of view, and all of them on a par with one another. In other words, each individual will measure success or failure according to his or her own standards, and these standards will in turn depend on whether the individual is an aesthetic, and ethical, or a religious individual. Furthermore, the aesthetic individual will have no motivation (except if that happens to be his 'taste,' which is unlikely) to submit the outcomes he desires to ethical constraints, whereas both the ethical and the religious individuals will be constrained to restrain the outcomes they pursue by taking into account the outcomes desired by the aesthetic individuals, which will put them in constant disadvantage. Finally, between the ethical and the religious individuals, there is the difference on death as the ultimate outcome, which most certainly has a bearing on the kinds of outcomes that can be appropriately desired and pursued. What to make of all this?

It seems that, when we discuss whether life has or not a historical structure, we are discussing several things, and that we may have something to gain by distinguishing them in order to become fully aware of them. One of the questions involved is whether life has the form of one

history, whether there is such a thing as the history of humankind. In other words, is there a teleology to history, at least in the prescriptive sense? If so, does it consist in a final outcome represented by a concrete state of the world at the moment of the end of the world, or at the moment of the death of each individual, or does it rather consist primarily in the kind of character each individual was, defined by appropriateness of the state of the world he pursued and of his pursuance of it? And, if there is such a teleology in the prescriptive sense, is it prescriptive in a religious sense, or just in an ethical sense? In short, only if there is the possibility of a unified history under a prescriptive teleology can the question of truth be dealt with.

Lolita

To bring all this to a more concrete level, let us reflect on Navokov's novel Lolita , whose protagonist is a perfect example of what Kierkegaard called "the aesthetic individual." Now, by the end of the novel, the protagonist, for once, becomes reflective and says:

Unless it can be proven to me--to me as I am now, today, with my heart and my beard, and my putrefaction--that in the long run it does not matter a jot that a North-American child-girl

named Dolores Haze had been deprived of her childhood by a maniac, unless this can be proven (and if it can, then life is a joke), I can see nothing for the treatment of my misery but the melancholy and very local palliative of articulate art. (p. 285)

The protagonist--whom I will call P from now on--lived aesthetically. In the process he sacrificed the youth and the life of a young girl. Insofar as he came to possess her for his own satisfaction, he obtained the desired outcome. In this sense, the story told by him, is the story of his success. Obviously, however, if Lolita had told the story, it would have been a tragedy. For P, it did not matter that Lolita was sacrificed by him; but for Lolita, it mattered. The two versions cannot be true--in the sense of truth I am considering here, for they contradict each other. The question raised by P, when he at last becomes reflective, is the question of truth. Is his own version of the story true? Or is Lolita's version the true one? Or is neither of the versions true, and then, as he realizes, life is a joke?

Behind these questions lurks, of course, the issue of appropriateness: of ethical appropriateness, if Lolita's tragedy did matter at least at the time it took place; of religious appropriateness, if Lolita's tragedy remains forever a tragedy, if it continues to matter in the long run, that is, forever.

The issue of ethical appropriateness is an issue among people, whether or not God exists. Let us explore it in the first place.

Both P and Lolita tell their stories in English. But do they really share the same language? In other words, is an actually spoken language ethically neutral?

I am here referring to narrative language, specifically, for we are contrasting two narratives. Now, there is at least one sense in which narrative language is not neutral, for it is the language of desire and aversion; it is hodological language, orientated toward a future to be obtained or averted. In this sense, narrative language is not purely 'objective,' for it deals not simply with what is the case but with a desired or hated outcome that may or may not come to be the case. It is the language that expresses not only the desires of people but also the actions they undertake which at the same time express and pursue their desires, and the difficulties they meet in the pursuance of their desires. For a narrative to be intelligible, there must be a correspondence, in terms of appropriateness, between the outcomes desired and the actions undertaken in their pursuance. In this regard, the actions of both P and

Lolita are intelligible. But in P's narrative, the action is structured around P as subject, and Lolita appears only as object of desire and victim of P's actions. It is the story of P's instrumental success. L's narrative, by contrast, would be a tragedy, the story of L's failure, of her manipulation and victimization by P. In this latter story, which is structured around Lolita, L is subject only in the passive sense, mere subject of affection, that is, object for P. This is, in part, why it would be a tragedy, given her care (the intensity of her desire) and the importance of what she cared about (a life not wasted). P is more experienced, more intelligent, shrewder, and more powerful than Lolita. If the truth of a story is to be judged exclusively in terms of the final outcome that obtains, that is, that becomes true, and how closely it corresponds to what was desired, then L's desires were not true, because they did not count, and P's desires were true because they counted and resulted in the desired outcome. In a purely instrumental sense, P's actions were appropriate to obtain the desired outcome, and L's actions, all in all, were inappropriate, since they failed to realize her desires.  

As long as we only have the stories narrated by P and L, each of them being the author and the only reader of their own respective stories, no other judgment than an instrumental one is possible for each of them. And if each of them read the other's story, it would be just a matter of  their liking or disliking the outcome that actually obtained. But let a different author tell their story, and let a third reader read the stories told by P and L, and things become more complicated.

Consider first the case of a different author. This author will successively structure the narration around P and L as subjects for whom the other is object. But the author cannot modify the actual outcome. In the instrumental sense, P won the day and L lost. Like P and L, the author may like or dislike the outcome. But, if the author is not just an aesthetic individual but an ethical one, he will judge the characters by standards of ethical appropriateness. Even if he does not act the part of the ethical critic in his telling of the story, the way in which he tells the story--the tone of his 'voice'--will betray his ethical stance. And the same will occur, though perhaps less visibly, if the author is a religious individual.

There is, therefore, no neutral dramatic language . The only question is whether, at least prescriptively, there ought to be a common language all agents should speak. If we conclude that the common language is aesthetical, what we are saying is that the language to be spoken by agents is the language of sheer power. If we conclude that the common language ought to be ethical, there is the possibility of one history, at least prescriptively, where Lolita's tragedy matters for a time, that is, for as long as there is history in the making. If we conclude that the common language ought to be religious--Christian, specifically--, Lolita's tragedy matters forever and, then, but only then, life is not a joke. (Of course, a common language presupposes standards. And the ethical and religious standards, in particular, will vary with conceptions of ethics and religion).

SUMMARY OF THE ABOVE  

                      - I -

Sartre on the point of view of exteriority

- Quotation from Sartre: his criticism

- S wants to take the point of view of the seeing eye, not of the eye seen.

- Digression on the points of view of (1) the observer (appropriate for a subject who sees an object), (2) the witness (appropriate for a subject who sees an interacting subject), and (3) the agent (appropriate for a subject who interacts with either a subject or an object). Sartre does not distinguish these points of view, and does not make room for the witness' point of view, since a subject cannot encounter another subject as a subject.

- Sartre's point: The point of view of exteriority mistakes subjects for objects, for objects are not surrounded by a world exposed to their action and to whose action they are exposed.

- Two characteristics of the point of view of exteriority: (1) It is not the agent's point of view, and (2) he who has the point of view is not exposed.

- By contrast, two characteristics of the point of view of interiority: (1) It is the agent's point of view, and (2) it is the point of view of the exposed agent.

- The mistake criticized by Sartre: (1) We have tried to understand subjects as if they had no point of view, and (2) as if they were not exposed to the world (because the

observer does not realize his own exposure to the world).

- Sartre's own mistake: He wants to have it both ways: (1) Consciousness is pure interiority, and (2) nonetheless, the distance established by another between me and the bench is a really existing relationship.

- Sartre's valid point: The point of view of exteriority missess "dramatic distances" and "dramatic temporality." From the barking dog I am separated by two yards and one second of terror, and from the desired state of affairs I am separated too; my possibilities are dramatic possibilities underpinned by a counter-finality that does not depend on me. These dramatic distances cannot be reduced to the observer's neutral distances, which are "external relations."

- The physicist's point of view is legitimate and fruitful within its proper limits, but the exposed agent's point of view is also possible, legitimate (since, as agents, we have no other choice), and fruitful (since it is the only way we can develop expectations and brace ourselves for future action).

- II -

The Scholastics and the point of view of exteriority  

- A complication: The Scholastics do not work

immediately on the world but on the world mediated by a text--the Bible.

- Comparison with Descartes: Descartes sees a table; the Scholastics are told that there is such a table. But Descartes doesd not function as exposed agent; he functions as unexposed observer, he phenomenalizes the table, makes it into object for possible assent. And he makes no difference between the table and the man he sees from his window: the two are objects for possible assent. He takes on the two the point of view of exteriority. What is the difference between Descartes and the Scholastics?

- There is no difference, philosophically . True, while they inquire, the Scholastics consent to God's existence (though they think that primarily they assent to the statement that God exists because of God's authority). But they do not differ from Descartes philosophically: By rendering God's existence questionable, they phenomenalize it into object for assent, and they look for intellectually compelling evidence to affirm it. And, like Descartes, they make no difference bettween God's existence and the existence of a table. No distances of exposure unfold between God and them (for they are observers), or between God and Abraham (for both are objects for the Scholastics).

In short, like Descartes, they have taken the point of view of exteriority.

- How did this affect the Scholastics' reading of the Bible? The Bible is storical. And only stories preserve distances of exposure. But the Scholastics read the Bible, not as a sequence of dramatically connected episodes, but as a collection of statements. Now, the Bible was meant to be read as a story. Even the non-believer reads it so. "When the reader reads 'God said to Abraham,'the reader does not read a statement about Goid, anymore than Abraham heard a statement about God speaking to him. What the reader gets is God's linguistic action by which Abrahama was addressed by God in the order of exposure. And as a [distant] witness the reader understands Abraham exposed to God's linguistic [and practical] action" (pp. l5-l6). And all this is a portion of the dramatic event in which God and Abraham [and the believer] are involved. By contrast, the Scholastics reads the passage as a series of statements about God and about Abraham. Thus, " the Scholastics deprived themselves of the possibility of understanding God as an agent [as a character], which led them to misunderstand revelation as primarily a matter of statements by a God-contemplator, as well as of the possibility of understanding the faithful as agents exposed to God, which led them to misunderstand faith as assent to statements made by a God-contemplator " (pp. l6-l7).

- III -

Dramatic spatio-temporality

- The unity of a story is dramatic; on this it differs from, say. logical unity. Dramatic unity is constituted by a space which is a field of action-passion, a field of exposure with an open-ended temporality hodologically orientated toward a contingent and unknown desired future

conditioned by the counterfinality of the surrounding world. - The temporality of a narrative (past tense) differs from the temporality of life (actually lived).

- Nonetheless, life is dramatic, even in those days the entry for which in our diaries would be "Today, nothing." The reason is that, being vulnerable, we cannot help desiring outcomes. The dramatic intensity depends on the acuteness of the desire and its tension with the counter-finality of the world, all this underpinned by contingenty and our ignorance of the final outcome.

- Although life is narratable, for the reason just given, it is not a narrative; only the basis for a narrative. And becaue life is narratable, the agent's understanding of himself and the world, grounded on his thetic consciousness of the world and on his non-thetic consciousness of himself exposed to it, is dramatic understanding or, in other terms, "existential understanding."

- IV -

Plurality of narratives, and truth

- The agent's narrative has no absolute privilege over

other conflictive stories. His story aqmounts only to the recognition of his own intentions in relation to his own world, not to a vindication of the reality of his world and of the appropriateness of his intentions. Does this imply that there is no truth to narratives? To answer this we have to take several factors into account:

(1) The last act : which depends on whether God exists or not.

(2) The appropriateness of actions : Instrumental, ethical, and religious.

- The plurality of histories in the making and of incompatible histories told arises from the absence of shared standards of approrpiateness, or from diverse hierarchies of standards of appropriateness. The participants do not haqve desired outcomes dictated by instinct; they conceive and pursue them as their own projects. But as long as there are no common standards for desired outcomes, there is only a plurality of histories on a par with one another, depending on whether the individual is an esthetic, and ethical, or a religious person. The question of a universal history amounts to whether there is a teleology, at least in the prescriptive sense, and whether such teleology is ethical or religious.

- "Lolita" as an illustration

P (the protagonist of Lolita ) is an aesthetic individual. He obtained the desired outcome. His story is lived and predicated on the basis that it does not matter what happens to Lolita. Lolita's story is lived and predicated on the inverse assumption. On p. 285, P raises this question himself. If the ultimate horizon of action is aesthetic, "it did not matter," contrary to Lolita's claim. And all histories lived and told on the basis that something matters are false. If the horizon is ethical, it matters temporally. If the horizon is religious, it matters--period.
