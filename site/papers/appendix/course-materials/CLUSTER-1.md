Catholic Intellectual Tradition Cluster

Course Proposal

a) Xavier O. Monasterio

b) Existentialism - PHL 360

c) Description

This course focuses on Albert Camus, with very brief excursions into Sartre and Nietzsche. The reason for concentrating on Camus are, on the one hand, the comparatively concrete character of his philosophical works ( The Myth of Sisyphus and The Rebel ) and, on the other hand, the quality of his literary works ( The Stranger , Caligula , The Plague and The Just Assassins ) as well as their close relationship to his philosophical views.

The course follows Camus' philosophical evolution from

The Stranger (1940) through The Rebel (1951). This evolution is particularly enlightening to our students in that it starts where many of they are in matters of ethics and religious convictions, and forces them to rethink their position by offering more mature alternatives.

Camus faced with rare intellectual integrity such fundamental human questions as the sense of life, the problem of evil, the existence of God, and the way of living that befits human beings. All these questions are studied and discussed in class. Though Camus himself was an atheist, his openmindedness and constant self-questioning are exemplary and furnish an excellent opportunity to at least this aovwedly Catholic teacher to present and discuss with the students his own stands, which in some matters coincide and in other matters differ from Camus' own.
