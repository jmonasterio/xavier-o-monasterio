On Kuhn in relation to faith

“The academic culture in the Western world breathes an atmosphere of unbelief” (Ch. Taylor, see complete quotation at beginning of <99-aug-1).

“The paradigm as shared example is the central element of what I now take to be the most novel and least understood aspect of this book” (Kuhn, The Structure, p. 187, in the “Postscript” of 1969).

Kuhn thinks that the notion that “truth and falsity are uniquely and univocally determined by the confrontation of statement with fact” has resulted in “ a confirmation theory drawn predominantly from other sources” than the actual practice of science ( The Structure , p. 80).

“To borrow once more M. Polanyi’s useful phrase, what results from this process is ‘tacit knowledge,’ which is learned by doing science rather than by acquiring rules for doing it” ( Ibid., p. 191).

“Galileo was not one of the first men to suggest that stones fall with a uniformly accelerated notion. Furthermore, he had developed his theorem on this subject together with many of its consequences before he experimented with an inclined plane . That theorem was another of the network of new regularities accessible to genius in the world determined jointly by nature and by the paradigm upon which Galileo and his contemporaries had been raised. Living in that world, Galileo could still, when he chose, explain why Aristotle had seen what he did. Nevertheless, the immediate content of Galileo’s experience with falling stones was not what Aristotle’s had been” ( The Structure , p. 125). [N.B. Galileo measured,, Aristotle didn’t].

“Because they aim quickly to acquaint the student with what the contemporary scientific community thinks it knows, textbooks treat the various experiments, concepts, laws, and theories of the current normal science as separately and as nearly seriatim as possible. As pedagogy this technique of presentation is unexceptionable. But when combined with the generally unhistorical air of science writing and with the occasional systematic misconstruction discussed above,

one strong impression is overwhelmingly likely to follow: science has reached its present state by a series of individual discoveries and inventions that, when gathered together, constitute the modern body of technical knowledge. Many of the puzzles of contemporary normal science did not exist until after the most recent scientific revolution. Very few of them can be traced back to the historic beginning of the science within which they now occur. Earlier generations pursued their own problems with their own instruments and their own canons of solution. Nor is it just the problems that have changed. Rather the whole network of fact and theory that the textbook paradigm fits to nature has shifted . Is the constancy of chemical composition, for example, a mere fact of experience that chemists could have discovered by experiment within any one of the worlds within which chemists have practiced ? Or is it rather an element... in a new fabric of associated fact and theory that Dalton fitted to the earlier chemical experience as a whole, changing that experience in the process. .. - These questions are asked about what appear as the piecemeal-discovered facts of a textbook presentation. But obviously, they have implications as well for what the text presents as theories. Those theories, of course, do ‘fit the facts,’ but only by transforming previously accessible information into facts that, for the preceding paradigm, had not existed at all. And that means that theories too do not evolve piecemeal to fit facts that were there all the time. Rather, they emerge together with the facts they fit from a revolutionary reformulation of the preceding scientific tradition, a tradition within which the knowledge-mediated relationship between the scientist and nature was not quite the same” ( The Structure , pp. 140-141).

”Like ‘time,’ ‘energy, ‘force,’ or ‘particle,’ the concept of an element is the sort of textbook ingredient that is often not invented or discovered at all. Boyle’s definition, in particular, can be traced back at least to Aristotle and forward through Lavoisier into modern texts. Yet that is not to say that science has possessed the modern concept of an element since antiquity. Verbal definitions like Boyle’s have little scientific content when considered by themselves. They are not full logical specification of meaning (if there are such), but more nearly pedagogical aids. The scientific concepts to which they point gain full significance only when related, with a text or other systematic presentation, to other scientific concepts, to manipulative procedures, and to paradigm applications . It follows that concepts like that of an element can scarcely be invented independent of context. Furthermore, given the context, they rarely require invention because they are already at hand . Both Boyle and Lavoisier changed the chemical significance of ‘element’ in important ways. But they did not invent the notion or even change the verbal formula that serves as its definition. Nor... did Einstein have to invent or explicitly redefine ‘space’ and ‘time’ in order to give them new meaning within the context of his work. - What then was Boyle’s historical function in that part of his work that includes the famous ‘definition’? He was a leader of a scientific revolution that, by changing the relation of ‘element’ to chemical manipulation and chemical theory, transformed the notion into a TOOL quite different from what it had been before and transformed both chemistry and the chemist’s world in the process ” ( The Structure, pp. 142-143)

( The Structure , p. 80-81).

“Giving way ,as taking direction, belongs essentially to Dasein’s Being-in-the World. Dasein is always somehow directed and on its way...The [practical or instrumental sign or the instrument] addresses itself to a Being-in-the-World which is specifically ‘spatial’. The sign is not authentically grasped if we just stare at it and identify it as an indicator-Thing which occurs. Even if we turn our glance in the direction which the arrow indicates, and look at something present-at-hand in the region indicated, even then the [instrumental] sign is not authentically encountered. Such a sign addresses itself to the circumspection of our concernful dealings... This circumspective survey does not grasp the ready to hand. .. Signs of the kind we have described let what is ready-to-hand be encountered; more precisely, they let some context of it become accessible in such a way that our CONCERNFUL dealings take an orientation and hold it secure . A sign is not a thing which stands to another thing in the relationship of indicating; it is rather an item of equipment which explicitly raises a totality of equipment into our circumspection so that together with it the worldly character of the ready-to-hand announces itself. In a symptom or a warning signal ‘what is coming’ ‘indicates itself’, but not in the sense of something merely occurring, which comes as an addition to what is already present-at-hand; ‘what is coming’ is the sort of thing which we are ready for, or which we ‘weren’t ready for’ if we have been attending to something else . In signs of something that has happened already, what has come to pass and run its course becomes circumspectively accessible . A sign to mark something indicates what one is ‘at’ any time. Signs always indicate primarily ‘wherein’ one lives, where one’s CONCERN dwells, what sort of involvement there is with something ” (Heidegger, Being and Time, pp. 110-111. The common reaction to Kuhn, particularly among scientists, has been negative. The two main accusations leveled against him are that he does away with the certitude of science, and that his views lead to relativism or even skepticism by debunking the notion of truth as correspondence with the real. As for the post-modernists, who long ago gave up on the notion of truth as correspondence, they welcome Kuhn because they see him as a powerful ally, since he demythologizes the notion of truth on which the current interpretation of science depends.

For my part, I think that the negative and positive reactions to Kuhn focus exclusively on the debunking aspect of his views, but ignore totally their constructive aspect. This is not totally the fault of Kuhn’s interpreters, in that he had trouble explaining clearly this constructive aspect and even grasping it clearly.

Much to his own bafflement, Kuhn found himself constrained by his analysis of the actual practice of science to admit that the accepted scientific views of any given period (represented by the respective textbook view of science) do not correspond to reality, which is what infuriates his traditionalist enemies and rejoices the post-modernists.  Now, the fact that scientific views do not actually correspond to reality is seen as bad by the traditionalists and as good by the post-modernists only because both parties share the traditional conviction that in order to be valid and genuinely scientific those views ought to correspond to reality. Suppress this demand, and the fact that scientific views do not actually correspond to reality automatically becomes neither bad (as Kuhn’s enemies suppose), nor good  (as Kuhn’s  welcomers suppose) but strictly irrelevant,  Which is why Kuhn could never agree not only with his detractors, who accused him of skepticism, but even with the post-modernists who welcomed his supposed anything-goes relativism. Yet Kuhn failed to see that he was actually offering an alternative to the “Cartesian” epistemology from which both textbook science and post-modernism derive.

An historical remark may be enlightening. Though Hume couldn’t be certain that scientific views actually corresponded to reality, he nonetheless was willing to grant their validity. Such indeed is the reason why he didn’t throw the books of science to the fire. For his part, Kant, because he couldn’t bring himself to deny the validity of Newton’s views, given their obvious success, he assumed that they were valid, in spite of the fact that  he couldn’t demonstrate that they corresponded to the thing itself, and that his own position implied the impossibility of knowing whether any judgment whatever actually corresponded to the thing itself, and that his Copernican revolution rendered futile the idea of correspondence to reality.  What we have in both of these cases is something akin to Kuhn’s position, who believes in the validity of scientific views though he openly grants that they don’t correspond to reality –which implies that the demand of correspondence is to be discarded.  

My personal interest in Kuhn is related to the Christian faith. According to a long tradition within the Catholic Church,  faith is a peculiar variety of knowledge concerning God and his revelation. On the one hand, it is unlike scientific knowledge in that it doesn’t rest on evidence –what Aquinas calls “vision”– but on God’s authority. In terms of evidence, therefore, the knowledge of faith is inferior to scientific knowledge. On the other hand, however, the knowledge of faith is absolutely certain precisely because it rests on divine authority. In terms of certainty, therefore, the knowledge of faith is superior to scientific knowledge.

The tradition to which I am referring has three aspects. One, it deems faith to be knowledge of a certain kind. Two, it understands knowledge as conformity of the mind to reality. Three,  it deems scientific knowledge to be the highest exemplar of human knowledge. Accordingly, this tradition, a paradigmatic instance of which is to be found in the first article of Aquinas’ Summa Theologiae alluded to above. Indeed, while affirming the superiority of faith over science, Aquinas nonetheless understood faith by analogy to science. Moreover,  in its apologetic eagerness –for the assimilation of faith to science arises from an apologetic inspiration– this tradition has reinforced in Christian intellectuals the tendency  to assimilate as much as possible the knowledge of faith to scientific knowledge. Hence they have claimed (whether or not that was actually Aquinas’s position remains hotly disputed), that at least certain of the truths divinely revealed can be rationally demonstrated and constitute, therefore, strictly scientific knowledge. Moreover, in order to protect faith against the charge that it ultimately  rests on blindly accepted divine authority --the famous charge of  fideism--, they have claimed that though the central truths of faith –namely, the articles of faith-- are beyond the ken of reason and can indeed be accepted only on divine authority, faith  nonetheless rests ultimately on rational foundations –the famous praeambula fidei – in that the truths that make faith rationally possible (such as, for instance, God’s existence and the fact of divine revelation) are themselves not a matter of faith but of evidence. * On the other hand, following the same path, the trend among non-believers has been to attempt to disparage faith as knowledge by pointing out that evidence is an intrinsic component of  knowledge and, particularly, of scientific knowledge, so that the very idea that faith is a form of knowledge, and that it is comparable to scientific knowledge though it rests on authority rather than on evidence, is preposterous.   

* Aquinas clearly belongs in this tradition, for he sees faith as a form of knowledge superior to science because of its certainty, but inferior to science because of its lack of vision or evidence. Whether or not he is a rationalist, as he tended to be interpreted in the nineteenth century and later, is, for my present purposes, a secondary point, though I am sure he was not.

The ambiguous, not to say perilous, status of theology in the university as an academic, that is, scientific, discipline, witnesses to the persistence of both those who think that faith is a genuine form of knowledge as well as of those who reject that idea..[See Hans Frei, “ Theology in the University” ]. Moreover, the very fact that both sides are equally convinced of the validity of their respective position while unable to make it persuasive to the other side may be an indicator that the problem itself of knowledge and faith may be an ill-conceived problem that originates in the view of scientific knowledge as necessary or demonstrated or at least experimentally proven correspondence, and of faith as knowledge.

What has all this to do with Kuhn? The two sides in this endemic debate share the unexamined assumption that scientific knowledge is indeed knowledge because it corresponds to what is the case in reality, and that scientific knowledge is the supreme variety of human knowledge because of its guaranteed certainty. Hence the tradition among Christians of comparing faith to scientific knowledge in order to situate it precisely within the map of knowledge. Now, what Kuhn brings to the fore is that what goes under the name of scientific knowledge actually doesn’t correspond to, or mirror, reality, and that it doesn’t possess at all the certainty traditionally attributed to it, since it changes as paradigms shift. This seems to take the ground from under the feet of those Christians who attempted to assimilate faith as much as possible to scientific knowledge, but no less from under the feet of the non-believers who rested tranquil on the unshakeable conviction of the superiority of their cause because of the guaranteed correspondence of scientific knowledge to reality.

On the side of us believers there may be a facile reaction of gleeful victory. The atheistic rationalists have had us for a long time with our backs against the wall, for we accepted their model of knowledge, namely, science, while we futilely tried to convince them and ourselves that our faith was not irrational because it was not fideistic. After Kuhn and post-modernism, however, we can turn the tables on our former enemies by claiming that their king-science is naked, after all. Alas!, in that case our victory would be Pyrrhic, for it would tantamount to acknowledging that our queen-faith is equally naked. And, indeed, if knowledge consists in the correspondence between the mind and reality –Aquinas’s adaequatio mentis ad rem–, then that is necessarily the case with both science and faith, for neither is in that case genuine knowledge.

Certainly Kuhn doesn’t  take us out of the predicament in which his analysis of science places us, for he acknowledges that he doesn’t quite see an alternative to the Cartesian paradigm he debunks. I think, however, that he takes us more than half of the way out, if we know how to listen to him and to complete his job. That is what I intend to do here .*  

* The questions I want to raise are the following: If there is a point to Kuhn’s reinterpretation of science –and I believe there is–, where are we left in terms of knowledge now that our idol of science as the supreme model of knowledge has fallen? And which consequences has the Kuhnian revision for our self-understanding as Christians, which has been so dependent on the conception of faith as knowledge after the scientific model?   

Debunking the textbook image of science

The paradigm as shared example” was, according to Kuhn in his Postscript of 1969, p. 187,  “the most novel and least understood aspect” of his famous book.

To understand its novelty, we have to refer ourselves to the model of science which Kuhn was discarding, what he called “textbook science,” that is, the idea of science that scientific textbooks instill in the student. According to the textbook  model, which Kuhn calls “Cartesian,” knowledge consists in the correspondence between the mind and reality. Reality exists independently of the mind. The epistemic job of the mind consists in conforming to the real by  mirroring it. What distinguishes scientific knowledge from non-scientific so-called ‘knowledge,’ which actually amounts to mere opinion, is its objective certainty. Indeed, in the case of non-scientific knowledge the conformity of the mind to the real is merely putative, a subjective conviction lacking objective certainty, an ungrounded opinion. In the case of scientific knowledge, by contrast, the correspondence between the mind and the real, in which truth consists, is guaranteed by evidence. The great progress of modern science over what went under the name of knowledge before Galileo is its experimental character, for experiment yields the evidence that guarantees the correspondence between what the scientist claims to know and what is actually the case.

Let us analyze the textbook view of science in some detail. In the process of scientific discovery a hypothesis is by itself an opinion as yet lacking certainty. It differs, however, from non-scientific opinion in that it is reached on the basis of systematic observation and measurement of the phenomenon under study, thanks to a planned and controlled manipulation of nature. Thus, for instance, Galileo, who wanted to explore the fall of bodies on inclines, actually constructed an inclined plane whose angle of inclination could be modified at will, and he actually rolled marbles down the plane, at different angles of inclination, carefully measuring the time it took each time to a particular marble to roll down the incline at a particular angle of inclination. Nothing like this supports ordinary, non-scientific opinion, which is reached out of the blue, so to say. Galileo, by contrast, spent a long time in this tedious and fastidious systematic observation before being able to formulate to himself, on that basis, what appeared to him to be the law determining the fall of bodies on inclines. Yet, he still deemed  the mathematical formula in which he expressed his law a mere hypothesis lacking the certainty of genuine knowledge. In order to be certain that his formula was actually correct he needed its correspondence to reality to be guaranteed by experiment. Thus it was only when a large number of concrete experiments confirmed his precise predictions of the time it would take such and such a marble to roll down the inclined plane at such and such an angle of inclination that his certainty of the truth of his hypothesis became objectively guaranteed. In short, the truth of the hypothesis had been guaranteed or proven by experiment. Needless to say, if in what we ordinarily take for knowledge there is nothing like the systematic observation and measurement found in the scientific method, much less do we find in what we ordinarily take for knowledge anything like the experimental procedure by which the correspondence to reality of a hypothesis based on scientific observation becomes guaranteed.

This verificationism underlays the textbook view of the experimental method typical of the natural sciences. Now, convincing as it may sound,  Kuhn finds that it does not represent correctly what actually takes place in the practice of science. If experiment yielded absolute evidence, then every time that an observation doesn’t conform to the expectations created by a previous body of experiments scientists would automatically discard the theory that was previously taken for the truth. Yet, as shown abundantly by Kuhn, that is not the case at all. As he puts it rather caustically, “If any and every failure to fit [the facts] were ground for theory rejection, all theories ought to be rejected at all times” (p. 146). Indeed, rather than discarding the current theory because it doesn’t fit all the facts, physicists and chemists live with irregularities

Shaped by this false image of the experimental method which they themselves contribute to perpetuate, the scientific textbooks inevitably misinterpret the way in which sciences actually develop:

“... one strong impression is overwhelmingly likely to follow: science has reached its present state by a series of individual discoveries and inventions that, when gathered together, constitute the modern body of technical knowledge... One by one, in a process often compared to the addition of bricks to a building, scientists have added another fact, concept, law or theory to the body of information supplied in the contemporary science.

But that is not the way science develops . (The Structure , p. 140, see quotation above).

It is by systematic contrast to this textbook image of science that Kuhn sketches his own view.  Thus, concerning the way in which science actually develops, Kuhn claims, as is well known, that the genuine history of each science shows that sciences develop not by accretion but by revolutions, that is, by shifts of paradigms that transform completely the science in which the shift takes place. As a result, after a shift of paradigms, scientists practice their science in an entirely different world.  For instance, “What chemists took from Dalton was not new experimental laws but a new way of practicing chemistry... As a result, chemists came to live in a world where reactions behaved quite differently from the way they had before” (p. 134). Nor is the textbook image of science more accurate concerning observation and the gathering of facts, for facts are not discovered by neutral observation. Rather,  theory and facts emerge together and are co-dependent.

The paradigm as shared example

Kuhn’s general position has become so well known that it would be tedious and futile to try to present it in more detail. It should prove more productive is to go to its core, which is what Kuhn refers to as “the most novel and least understood aspect of his book, namely, the paradigm as shared example .” A certain scientific achievement becomes exemplary or paradigmatic for the scientific community in which it takes place, to the point of shaping from then on to THE PRACTICE of the community . What precisely is so novel in this, and in what way has it been misunderstood?

As to its novelty, let us contrast what Kuhn has to say with the textbook image of science.

According to the latter, after the hypothesis of al law has been reached on the basis of repeated  observations and measurements of a constant fact, the stage of experimental demonstration of the law begins . When a large number of previously unobserved cases has been correctly predicted on the basis of the hypothetical law, the law ceases to be hypothetical and becomes experimentally proven and therefore certain. In this view, the role of experiment in the natural sciences is similar to the role of demonstration in Euclidean geometry: it yields the evidence thanks to which the truth of a stated theorem, that is, its correspondence to reality, becomes scientifically certain.

For his part, Kuhn rejects this understanding of science. I have already alluded above to some of the main points on which Kuhn finds this textbook image or science clearly at odds with what goes on in the actual practice of science, as well as his own view of pre-paradigmatic science, normal problem-solving science under a paradigm, the shift of paradigms under the weight of irregularities, and the revolutionary rather than evolutionary change that takes place under a new paradigm. All that, however, leaves obscure what I take it to be Kuhn’s most important contribution, which is his understanding of science as what Aristotle would have called “practical knowledge” by contrast to the traditional, Aristotelian understanding of science as theoretical knowledge.  

According to the traditional, textbook image, science consists in the accumulation of  piecemeal successive discovery of certain and unmodifiable laws, which together give us the correct view of the universe. The process through which these scientific truths are acquired is the following. It begins with  systematic brute observation and measurement of regular facts that, taken together, end up by dictating the hypothesis whose truth in its turn will be guaranteed by repeated experiments. Once acquired, this truth, which takes the shape of a statement or, ideally, of a mathematical formula, in its turn leads to its successful, routine application in the problem-solving activity. Thus, in this view, the object that becomes known is the  stated law, and it becomes a scientific truth because its correspondence to reality is guaranteed by experiment . However, according to Kuhn,  this is not at all what actually takes place in the scientific activity.

First of all, the very idea that scientific knowledge corresponds to what is the case is a myth. Kuhn rejects “the generalization that truth and falsity are uniquely and univocally determined by the confrontation of statement with fact” (p. 80). He provides abundant evidence that scientific truths are not dismissed when they fail to be confirmed by experiment. In fact, he shows that the practitioners of all sciences, however uncomfortably, live with “irregularities” of this kind, until an alternative, more satisfactory paradigm emerges from a radically new paradigmatic example.

“Once it has achieved the status of a paradigm [that is, of canonical shared example that shapes the practice of the scientific community], a scientific theory is declared invalid only if an alternate candidate is available to take its place. No process yet disclosed by the historical study of scientific development at all resembles the methodological stereotype of falsification by direct comparison with nature . That remark does not mean that scientists do not reject scientific theories, or that experience and experiment are not essential to the process in which they do so. But it does mean – what will ultimately be the central point– that the act of judgment that leads scientists to reject a previously accepted theory is always based upon more than a comparison of that theory with the world ” (p. 77).

Kuhn thinks that the notion that ““TRUTH AND FALSITY ARE UNIQUELY AND UNIVOCALLY DETERMINED BY THE CONFRONTATION OF STATEMENT WITH FACT” has resulted in “a confirmation theory drawn predominantly from other sources” than the actual practice of science.  As to the nature of those other sources, he abstains from speculating, at least in this passage of his book, because the question how the textbook interpretation of science, so much at odds with the actual scientific practice, necessarily leads to the historical and critical elucidation of philosophy, and those topics are here barred” (p. 80).*

* To this point I will return later.   

“ But we can at least note two reasons why science has seemed to provide so apt an illustration of the generalization that truth and falsity are uniquely and unequivocally determined by THE CONFRONTATION OF STATEMENT WITH FACT. Normal science does and must continually strive to bring theory and fact into closer agreement, and that activity can easily be seen as testing or as a search for confirmation or falsification. Instead, its object is to solve a puzzle for whose very existence the validity of the paradigm must be assumed. Fai lure to achieve a solution discredits only the scientist, not the theory... In addition, the manner in which SCIENCE PEDAGOGY ENTANGLES DISCUSSION OF A THEORY WITH REMARKS ON ITS EXEMPLARY APPLICATIONS has helped to reinforce A CONFIRMATION THEORY DRAWN PREDOMINANTLY FROM OTHER SOURCES . Given the slightest reason for doing so, the man who reads a science text CAN EASILY TAKE THE APPLICATIONS TO BE EVIDENCE FOR THE THEORY, THE REASONS WHY IT OUGHT TO BE BELIEVED . BUT SCIENCE STUDENTS ACCEPT THEORIES ON THE AUTHORITY OF TEACHER AND TEXT, NOT BECAUSE OF EVIDENCE . What alternatives have they, or what competence? THE APPLICATIONS GIVEN IN TEXTS ARE NOT THERE AS EVIDENCE BUT BECAUSE LEARNING THEM IS PART OF LEARNING THE PARADIGM AT THE BASE OF CURRENT PRACTICE . If applications were set forth as evidence, then the very failure of texts to suggest alternative interpretations or to discuss problems for which scientists have failed to produce paradigm  solutions would convict their authors of extreme bias . There is not the slightest reason for such an indictment “ (p. 80-81)

  

To better understand what Kuhn means by “shared example” let us go to a passage (pp. 187-191)  in which, explaining  precisely “the paradigm as shared example,” he discusses the role of the problems given to the student in science textbooks. Problems “are thought to supply only practice IN WHAT THE STUDENT ALREADY KNOWS . He cannot, it is said, solve problems unless he has first learned the theory and some rules for applying it. SCIENTIFIC KNOWLEDGE IS EMBEDDED IN THEORY AND RULES; problems are supplied to gain facility in their application. I HAVE TRIED TO ARGUE, HOWEVER, THAT THIS LOCALIZATION OF THE COGNITIVE CONTENT OF SCIENCE IS WRONG” (P. 187).

    According to the textbook image of science, scientific knowledge concretely consist in knowledge of the particular law, for this is what the student needs to already know in order to solve problems. Furthermore, the application of the law to solve particular problems doesn’t add to the knowledge the student already possesses by knowing the law; it only allows the student to gain facility in problem-solving. However, Kuhn deems wrong this localization of the cognitive content of science in the statement of the law or the theory.

Let us illustrate the misunderstanding Kuhn opposes with an example borrowed from Euclidean geometry. In the interpretation Kuhn opposes, the student gains scientific knowledge when, thanks to the demonstration of a theorem in the textbook, he grasps the truth of the stated theorem. The rest will be mere application of the already acquired knowledge to the solution of particular problems to which the theorem applies, and that will not add to the student’s knowledge but only to his or her facility in solving problems. According to this interpretation, then, scientific knowledge is acquired by means of the demonstration that renders evident, and therefore guarantees, the truth of the stated theorem. The further application of the already known theorem doesn’t add or contribute anything to the scientific knowledge which consists in the stated law or theorem; it only will help the student to gain facility in problem-solving.*

* To use Galileo’s example, see below.

Not so, says Kuhn. Imagine a student who understands perfectly the meaning of the statement of the Pythagorean theorem, who is capable of demonstrating it, and who is convinced of the truth of the theorem as a result of the demonstration, but who is nonetheless incapable of applying his knowledge to the solution of even a single pertinent problem. Question: does this student possess geometrical knowledge?  According to the textbook view of science, the answer has to be affirmative, since the student knows the theorem, and that is where knowledge is localized in the textbook image of science. In actual practice, however, no teacher of geometry would grant that such a student really knows. Nor would Kuhn. This implies that scientific knowledge is not to be identified with knowledge of the law or of the theory or of the theorem. Where then does Kuhn think that scientific knowledge is to be localized?

In Kuhn’s view, what the student gains through repeated application , that is, through repeated solution of concrete problems, is not, at least not primarily, increased facility in solving problems:

“After the student has done many problems, he may gain only added facility by solving more. But at the start and for some time after, doing problems is learning consequential things about nature . In the absence of such exemplars, the laws and  theories he has previously learned would have little empirical content... A phenomenon familiar to both students of science and historians of science provides a clue. The former regularly report that they have read through a chapter of their text, understood it perfectly, but nonetheless had difficulty solving a number of problems at the chapter’s end. Ordinarily, also, those difficulties dissolve in the same way. The student discovers... a way to see this problem as like a problem he has already encountered. Having seen the resemblance, grasped the analogy between two or more distinct problems, he can interrelate symbols and attach them to nature in the ways proved effective before. The law sketch, say f=ma, has functioned as a TOOL, informing the student what similarities to look for, signaling the gestalt in which the situation is to be seen. The resultant ability to see a variety of situations as like each other, as subjects for f=ma or some other symbolic generalization, is, I think, the main thing a student acquires by doing exemplary problems, whether with pencil and paper or in a well designed laboratory. After he has completed a certain number..., he views the

situations that confront him as a scientist in the same gestalt as other members of his specialist’s group. For him they are no longer the same situations he had encountered when his training began. He has meanwhile assimilated a time-tested and GROUP-LICENSED WAY OF SEEING. . Galileo... Huighens... Finally, Daniel Bernoulli... That example should begin to make clear what I mean by learning from problems to see situations as like each other, as subjects for application of some scientific law or law-sketch. Simultaneously, it should show  why I refer to the consequential knowledge of nature acquired while learning the similarity relationship and THEREAFTER EMBODIED IN A WAY OF VIEWING PHYSICAL SITUATIONS RATHER THAN IN RULES OR LAWS. The three problems in the example... deploy only one law of nature... it was usually stated as ‘Actual descent equals potential ascent.’ Bernoulli’s application of the law should suggest how consequential it was. Yet the verbal statement of the law, taken by itself, is virtually impotent. For him [the contemporary student of physics] the generalization could begin to function only when he learned to recognize actual descents and potential ascents as ingredients of nature, and that is to learn something prior to the law, about the situations that nature presents and does not present. THAT SORT OF LEARNING IS NOT ACQUIRED BY PURELY VERBAL MEANS. Rather IT COMES AS ONE IS GIVEN WORDS TOGETHER WITH CONCRETE EXAMPLES OF HOW THEY FUNCTION IN NATURE; NATURE AND WORDS ARE LEARNED TOGETHER. To borrow once more Michael POLANYI’S useful phrase, what results from this process is ‘TACIT KNOWLEDGE’ which is learned by doing science rather than by acquiring rules for doing it (pp. 187-191).

There are several things going on in the preceding quotation, and I suspect some confusion there that needs to be dispelled: (1) K wants to show that applications in problem-solving do not function as evidence for the law. There may be here a confusion between what happens in the case of the discoverer and what happens in the case of those who are taught after the discovery.  (2) Paradigmatic examples are examples that generate a problem-solving practice and a group-licensed way of seeing the field that didn’t exist before. (3) Solving problems in which the law is applied results in a practical way of seeing that facilitates a disposition to respond, which itself cannot be stated because it is practical, tacit knowledge. The illustration by inverting lenses and inverted image in the retina applies here. This is what the new practice consists in (4) The law is not a matter of verbal learning. It can only be learned by concrete example and repeated application (as one’s native language is learned). (5) What is confusing in Kuhn’s approach is a matter of focus. Kuhn insists so much on the extension of a given paradigm that he blurs the all-important localization of scientific knowledge not on the contemplative understanding of the law but on the transformation of the practical field of the science thanks to problem -solving guided by the paradigmatic example.

I may address these possible confusions by answering the following question: How does Kuhn’s view, as presented in the preceding quotation, apply to Galileo’s case, at least  according to me, though not necessarily according to Kuhn? First of all, there is a difference between the discoverer and the ones who follow him or her. In the discoverer, Galileo, if my notion of the difference between judgment and statement is correct [see my “Kuhn and the Issue of Truth” pp. 9 -10; 2001-April-1-Kuhn] , the statement followed, and resulted from, the judgment, the judgment consisting in the disposition to deal with the object in a certain way. In Galileo, the solution of concrete experimental problems by prediction and verification resulted in the instrumental or practical statement as aide-memoire for himself and as teaching tool for others. In the student, the statement precedes, and favors “as a tool,” as a “sign FOR,” the development of the judgment as disposition to respond to the object as stated. In other words, the ‘seeing’ of which Kuhn speaks is practical seeing, disposition to deal with the object as stated. As a disposition, it develops through practice –the repeated practice of problem-solving. Because the statement does not mirror what is the case, the student who understands perfectly the statement (what is said) instead of having appropriated or interiorized  it under the form of a disposition to respond doesn’t know, for the localization of knowledge by the textbook interpretation of science is wrong. It rests on the ‘Cartesian’ assumption that knowledge consists concretely in judgment-statements that correspond to reality by mirroring or representing it.

To return to Galileo, the discoverer. The student, says Kuhn, accepts the statement on Galileo’s authority. In his turn, the student himself will transmit the statement-tool when he becomes a teacher until the student learns to use it as a tool. But Galileo didn’t accept the sketch of the law on authority. He created the statement as a result of his work as a discoverer. Unlike the student, Galileo didn’t move from the statement to the judgment-disposition, but the other way around. So, how did Galileo come to the judgment? There was the observational stage that led to the hypothetical statement. This led Galileo to apply the law in particular predictions. However, Galileo didn’t take the correctness of his predictions as evidence for the truth of the stated law; what Kuhn calls ‘the sketch of the law,’ that is, the statement. But the statement, in Galileo’s case, was a practical judgment, a disposition to see practically, that is, to deal with, to move in,  the world as mapped by the statement.  In Kuhn’s view, though, the predictive stage was part of the observational one, except that in this case the observation was already guided by the sketch of the law, but still in an insecure way that only through the practice of solving problems would result in a habitual practical disposition. This is manifested by the fact that he started predicting what would happen in the case of previously unobserved instances. Contrary to the textbook view, Kuhn doesn’t see the fulfillment of predictions as evidence for the truth of the statement. Indeed, the statement is practical,  it doesn’t mirror nature; therefore it is not true by way of reproducing what is the case. What Galileo developed was the habitual disposition to see nature practically and deal with it according to the stated law. Of course, we would like a statement that says explicitly what this disposition is and creates it automatically, as Searle imagines.. But that is an illusion that corresponds to the notion that knowledge is representational. The disposition can be known only practically by acquiring it through practice. It is a case of tacit knowledge, as all practical knowledge is. The best that can be said in this connection is suggested by Kuhn: the practical disposition is embedded as a sort of ‘program’ in the neural system. But a program doesn’t say the world anymore that a written musical score says the symphony.

Let me pause here, for I am not sure that I am interpreting Kuhn correctly. Euclid demonstrated the practical statements of practical geometricians and gave to geometry the appearance of a deductive, demonstrative science which became the ideal of science consecrated by philosophers. Kant would say that Euclid  brought to intuition, thanks to a manipulation of images, the necessity of the synthetic a posteriori judgments expressed in the stated theorems. But Kant realized that nothing guaranteed the correspondent necessity in nature. Kuhn goes farther. He argues, or could and should argue, that the Euclidian demonstration of theorems is irrelevant. The geometrician doesn’t need to know the demonstration, as long as he knows how to apply the theorems in actual problem-solving. As for Galileo, his experiments did not demonstrate experimentally the law, that is, the law’s necessity. That is what the textbook view of science wrongly maintains, because it is based on the Cartesian assumption of knowledge as representation, and because it is influenced by the Platonic-Aristotelian doctrine of scientific knowledge as necessarily corresponding to what is the case . Contrary to the common assumption, geometry is not a theoretical science. A geometrician who wouldn’t know how to apply geometry to the solution of problems in which the theorems are applicable is not a genuine geometrician, even if he or she is capable of demonstrating each of the theorems. Geometricians solve concrete practical geometrical problems, guided , Kuhn would say, by ‘the sketch of the theorem’ used as a tool, as one is guided by a map to reach a particular place. (But one develops the skill of reading maps by actually practicing their use by reaching places through using maps).  Their problem-solving practice is, so to say,  in-formed by the stated theorem. Initially the statement is remembered as such to guide the practice; later, when already interiorized, it is not even remembered, as one doesn’t remember a map one used at a certain time once one has interiorized it.

Even this way of speaking, however, may be misleading, for a map is a visual instrument; therefore it still suggests that –as Kuhn mistakenly expresses himself– practical knowledge is a sort of vision rather than a disposition, an attitude. A musical score may be a better metaphor. A musical score doesn’t say how the fingers are to be moved on the piano. It is to be ’read-obeyed’ by the fingers, for it is more like an order to be followed than like a statement to be understood theoretically. Yet, it may be argued, a verbal order can be understood and not followed. So understanding is essential and comes first. Wrong. A particular order cannot come to be understood as an order and not followed without being preceded by the practice of following orders. There are not two moments involved: understanding and following or not following. An order is not grasped by understanding what the order ‘says.’ An order is  grasped or cashed as an order by following it, or at least as to-be-followed, much as money cannot function as money but by being used. What I mean is that one doesn’t first grasp orders as to be understood, but as to be followed.   And one genuinely ‘understands’ orders by following them, for they are to be followed. In other words, understanding what an order “says” or “what is said” when an order is given, is not a condition for obeying or disobeying, for an order says nothing in the sense of stating something to be understood. An order commands, and the appropriate way to respond to an order is to obey it.). Understanding an order as if it were a statement that ‘says’ something is not, pace Searle, the appropriate way of responding to an order. Can a musical score be understood as such otherwise than by playing it, or by being disposed to respond to it by playing it in the appropriate situation? The temptation to answer ths question affirmatively comes from the fact that musical scores are written language, objectified language, that is, language out context, out of situation. However, one doesn’t learn to obey in that way. Originally orders are given orally, in the concrete situation in which they are to be obeyed, so that the child who is learning the language doesn’t have to supply the context. “Eat your egg” (here and now, always in the present tense) is a good example. “Go and apologize to your brother” is another. In short, the original language of orders is the language of interacting participants. And so, too, is the original language of statements, FOR STATEMENTS ARE TO BE FOLLOWED RATHER THAN TO BE UNDERSTOOD OR EVEN MERELY ASSENTED TO.. Assenting to a statement is ‘knowing’ what it says, but ‘knowing’ what a statement says is acting accordingly.

Thus, Kuhn would, or at least should,  say, the sketch of the law or of the theorem is  like an oral order to be followed rather than like a written statement read out of the original context, which can only be understood. However, unlike an order, a law or a theorem comes under the form of a statement, not of an order. Why? Because, unlike an order, a statement has, so to say, a referential charge or force. Indeed a statement, such as “the hammer (which you are looking for) is in the next room,” refers to ‘object,’ to the desired hammer being in the next room in a way in which a musical score and even a verbal order do not. Statements, however, Kuhn would, or at least should , say, have been traditionally interpreted to refer in the wrong way, that is, ‘objectively,’ independently of the subject’s dispositions and of his subsequent behavior. Such an interpretation, however, doesn’t square with our actual practice. “Your mother is on the phone” or “Your brother was taken to the hospital” are good examples. In both cases a conduct, not assent, is expected as the appropriate response, and mere understanding or even assent would be totally inappropriate.

Thus, implicit in Kuhn’s position is the view that, contrary to the traditional interpretation, statements in general –and of scientific statements in particular-- do not say or picture objectively what is the case, but are meant ‘to be followed.’ Unlike in the case of orders, however, in the case of statements the action that will constitute ‘the appropriate following’  is not specified. That it is not specified may be due to several reasons. In certain cases, because the conduct of following is too obvious to need specification. The cry “Fire!,” in a cinema, or to say, “Your mother is on the phone,” are cases in point. But it is not so obvious what the following conduct must be in the case of “The Martians have landed in New York.” However, every first-order statement is addressed, at least implicitly, “to whom it may concern,” be it one or many persons. And the concerned can be only exposed agents who can be favorably or unfavorably affected by the object for response and who therefore ought to respond accordingly, to ‘follow’ the statement. And, as in the case of perception, which is indexical, it is for the concerned agent to grasp that he or she is the addressee.

What, then, of scientific statements of the type of Galileo’s law? They are not first-order statements, not only for the general public (to whom they are not addressed) but even for the practitioners of the science to whom they are generally addressed, though not in specific circumstances. In this regard, they are like written statements. Not addressed by anybody in particular to anybody in particular in a particular situation, as first-order statements are. True, they arise out of observation of the world, but not directly of the world of interaction, for scientists are immediately guided by the desire to understand uniform events, which is the sort of ‘object’ they abstract. Hence their statements do not refer directly to objects for response, not only because they are universal rather than particular, but because scientists observe objects ex iis quae sunt propria rei and with the pre-conceptions appropriate to their epistemic task as they understand it, not in reference to vulnerable agents situated in relation to the object, not  per dispositionem agentis, not “to whom it may concern.” Their statements are, at least in intention, explanatory of a ‘phenomenon.’

From the field of action scientists abstract the object-event that interests them, something to be understood . This abstract object is, at least in the natural sciences, a regular event (not a ‘substance,’ a thing), such as bodies fall on inclined planes or the fall of bodies on inclined planes. If and when the scientists are successful, as Galileo was, they grasp ‘the law’ or formal cause of the regularity of the event, which is the to-be-understood. Such anyway, is the textbook view of science. Once the at least factual necessity (to use Lonergan’s expression) has been understood and guaranteed by experiment, future application to problems will add facility to the scientist’s problem-solving, not knowledge.

IT IS HERE THAT KUHN PARTS COMPANY WITH TEXTBOOKS, ON THE BASIS OF WHAT SCIENTISTS ACTUALLY DO. According to Kuhn, the law or the ‘sketch of the law’ does not mirror a necessity in nature, supposedly demonstrated or proven scientifically by experiment. The regularity of observed natural phenomena from which the ‘object’ is abstracted is itself contingent, as Hume realized it. The regular expectation of the same to which it give rise is grounded in nothing more than past repetition. Experiments do not prove or demonstrate the necessity of the regularity; scientists, as commonsensical people, assume it and take it for granted. As Kuhn says, the result of experiments is no evidence of the truth of the law, that is, that the phenomenon will necessarily be repeated because it corresponds to something necessary in nature. That sort of demonstration is required only within the context of the Platonic-Aristotelian tradition of science vs. opinion, but whether the demonstration does or  doesn’t take place in actual science is irrelevant .[See Kuhn, pp. 104-105, on the Aristotelian quest for natures or essences. And p. 119 on Aris’s and Galileo on pendulum. And 123-126]. In a parallel case, if the experiment or the manipulation of images in Euclidean geometry functioned as evidence, it would function as evidence of the content of the law as necessary. But that is not what scientists are actually after. If it were, the student who understands the demonstration of the theorem, that is, of the sketch of the law, and is capable of demonstrating it, would satisfy the scientists who are his teachers. However, as Kuhn says, the mere verbal statement by itself , that is, without any notion of its appropriate use or application, is virtually impotent, for scientific knowledge is not theoretical knowledge of the law . Thus the case of Euclidean, fully formalized geometry is misleading, for it exemplifies the Aristotelian ideal of science in the Analytica Posteriora, borrowed from Plato the admirer of mathematics. That the demonstration is not actually required is shown by the fact that the students accept the law on authority, and the fact that they don’t work on the basis of a demonstration doesn’t hamper their scientific work.  What is more important, the stated law is meant to function “as a tool informing the student what similarities to look for, the gestalt in which the situation is to be seen” (p. 189) –to be seen practically , that is–  not as an image of what is the case. And it comes to function as a tool only when it is repeatedly used as a tool in the solution of problems on paper or in a well designed laboratory, thanks to which it is interiorized as a disposition to respond, as tacit practical knowledge that is un-formalizable and in-articulable, to the point that Kuhn wonders whether it can be called knowledge.

Nonetheless, as in the previous paragraph, Kuhn repeatedly speaks as if the result of application were a certain kind of ‘seeing,’ the seeing licensed by the science at that point of its development, the seeing shared by the community of scientists. But when Kuhn speaks of this seeing as a result of problem-solving –of problem-solving, mind you, not of experiment–, he is speaking of the extension of a paradigm, as in the case of the extension of the pendular view from Galileo through Huighens and finally through Bernoulli. Now, however right or wrong Kuhn may be on the particular point of the intra-paradigmatic growth of science as a result of t he extension of the paradigm, he seems to confuse it with the case of acquiring the knowledge that Galileo acquired as a discoverer. Galileo created a paradigm, he didn’t extend it. In this latter case, though,  Kuhn doesn’t speak of it as seeing but as “tacit knowledge,” and he mentions explicitly Polanyi. Now, according to Polanyi, as according to Kuhn too, the words cannot be learned by themselves but through actual practice. And what is learned, unlike the verbal law and even the demonstrated verbal law in geometry, is tacit and cannot be expressed in words, for it is a practical skill of dealing with the regular behavior. *

* In this connection, as an illustration, Kuhn several times refers to the experiment of inverting lenses (see pp. 111 and127. Thanks to appropriate action, in spite of the ‘wrong’ retinal image, the (practical) vision of the practical field is reversed, so that appropriate action becomes again easy. This shows the subordination of vision to action, as well as the practical character of vision as “being ready for,” to use Heidegger’s expression.

Again on the point of application Kuhn needs to be corrected or at least completed. Indeed, interested as he is in science as such (as Aristotle was), he restricts application to problem-solving either “on paper” or “in a well designed laboratory.” That is, he is thinking of the student who is learning the science, progressively becoming, ‘a scientist,’ that is, a member of the scientific community who shares a paradigm. In other words, much in the Aristotelian tradition of the Analytica Posteriora, Kuhn focuses on science as such as an abstract object of study, isolated from its actual practice in the world of interaction. The rare cases in which he speaks of the application of the engineer, he seems to dismiss it as secondary or merely accidental to the science, not a component of it. But this is wrong, at least in my view. Ultimately, science is not theoretical but practical. It renders possible the practical vision that enhances our situation in the universe as vulnerable agents. And the ultimate applicators are not those who solve problems on paper or in the laboratory but in the field of our action and our exposure. Certainly, there is a division of work among the scientists, and those who work on paper or in the laboratory render possible the applications by engineers and technologists, without which applications the natural and physiological sciences would never have acquired the  uent allocations of money and the consequent prestige they enjoy today,  without which their progress would have stagnated long ago.

CONSIDER THE SIMILARITY BETWEEN KUHN’S TACIT  KNOWLEDGE AND ARIS’S PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE. AND CONCLUDE BY SHOWING THAT FAITH IS NOT TO BE UNDERSTOOD BY ANALOGY TO THEORETICAL KNOWLEDGE BY ‘CORRESPONDENCE,’ BUT BY ANALOGY TO PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE AS APPROPRIATE RESPONSE.
