\wp\monas\l'heureux

Your position as I understand it

       1.  You  reject  all  religions  that  conform  to  "the  traditional
approach," that is, which attempt to fulfill their fundamental  function  of
assuaging the human existential  anxiety  with  "answers."  The  traditional
Christian faith is, in your view, a prototype of such religions.

       2.  The  reason  why   you   find   religious   "answers"   radically
unsatisfactory is that they are "not information which we have access to  by
means of the ordinary use of the natural  human  faculties  of  knowing  and
understanding. The truths in question are said to have  been  supernaturally
revealed, so that the individual is required to believe them  on  the  basis
of some authority: the Church, or the Bible, or  some  combination  thereof.
In this perspective, then, faith is essentially synonymous with belief"  (p.
3).

      3. The alternative you propose and  find  satisfactory  is  "apophatic
faith," that is, a faith that assuages the  human  existential  anxiety  not
with answers but with an oceanic experience that all is fundamentally well.

My reaction

      1. You reject the conception that makes faith "essentially  synonymous
with belief." I agree with you.

      2. Because the UD documents on  the  Catholic  intellectual  tradition
presuppose this conception of faith you reject their conception of faith.  I
agree with you.

      3. You think  that  the  conception  of  faith  reflected  in  the  UD
documents  on  the  Catholic  intellectual  tradition  is   the  traditional
Christian faith. Therefore, you reject the traditional  Christian  faith.  I
disagree with you on both accounts.
            I think that  the  conception  of  faith  reflected  in  the  UD
documents does not represent the traditional Christian faith. It is,  in  my
opinion, a misinterpretation of the  Christian  faith  created  by  academic
theologians and philosophers deeply influenced by  Plato's  and  Aristotle's
intellectualism.
            I think that in identifying the traditional Christian  faith  as
"belief in answers" you  make  the  same  mistake  as  the  well-intentioned
authors of the UD documents.
             By the same  token,  I  do  not  think  that  your  reason  for
rejecting the traditional Christian faith and looking for an alternative  to
it is compelling.

      4. I disagree with your idea of  knowledge  as  "information  that  we
have access to by means of the ordinary use of the natural  human  faculties
of knowing and understanding." (By the way, notice the kinship of this  idea
with that of truths floating out there to be accessed, which  rightly  makes
you so uncomfortable in the case of the UD  documents).  To  make  my  point
through a concrete instance: the atheistic existentialists  claim  that  the
undeniable facts of our suffering and being  condemned  to  die,  which  are
accessible to everybody,  show   that  our  existential  anxiety  cannot  be
legitimately assuaged, for human life is  indeed  ultimately  senseless.  If
this is what you call knowledge, then your oceanic feeling is  inadmissible.
And if this is not what you call knowledge, why not?  On  what  basis  would
you say that this is not information to which we have  access  by  means  of
the  ordinary  use  of  the  natural  human   faculties   of   knowing   and
understanding? In short, I think that the conception  of  faith  typical  of
the traditional approach, which you rightly reject, stinks as  much  as  its
conception of knowledge, which is the one you espouse  here  and  which  the
supernatural  information  (religious  "answers")  which  you   reject   was
supposed to complement.

      6. At least from my standpoint,  apophatic  faith  is  an  essentially
unstable  refuge  between  the  courageous  lucidity  of  atheism  and   the
encounter with a personal loving God. The strength of reflective atheism  is
that it stays within the limits of ordinary experience. The strength of  the
Christian faith is that it is an encounter with a personal loving  God  (not
"belief in answers"). Your apophatic faith has  neither  strength:  it  goes
beyond ordinary human experience and it  encounters  nobody.  One  does  not
trust a feeling; one trusts a person. In short, I do not think  there  is  a
tenable middle term  between  encounter  with  a  personal  loving  God  and
atheism.

























      5. Insofar as you accept as valid  the  conception  of  knowledge  you
present and you see dealing with  existential  anxiety  as  the  fundamental
function of religious faith, you have to explain to yourself why you do  not
even consider atheism as a serious alternative to faith,  whether  apophatic
or not. For reflective atheism claims that the lack of factual  information,
that is, of "answers" that assuage  our  existential  anxiety  is  itself  a
valid answer to our ultimate existential questions. On what  basis  can  you
claim that your oceanic feeling is not just wishful  feeling,  much  as  you
claim that the  answers  of  traditional  religions  are  wishful  thinking?
According to you, the fundamental function of religion  is  to  assuage  our
existential anxiety. But on what basis do you dismiss the  possibility  that
precisely because there are no answers, that is, no valid  information  that
assuages our existential anxiety, the adult way of dealing  with  it  is  to
live courageously with it instead of attempting to suppress it?



      5. I disagree even more with your  procedure  of  taking  for  granted
that your notion of knowledge is indisputable and  that  what  qualifies  as
knowledge  according  to  that  notion  is  automatically  valid,  and  then
determining what faith is and the extent to which it is valid  by  reference
to 'knowledge.' I find this procedure as unacceptable as I  would  find  its
counterpart, namely, a procedure that would define faith  as  a  faculty  of
accessing divinely revealed information  and  would  consider  automatically
valid what qualifies as object of faith accordisng to that  notion,  and  th
en would determine what knowledge is and the extent to which it is valid  by
reference to what is divinely revealed.
                 The correct procedure, if one does not want to go about  it
aprioristically  and  discard  either  knowledge   or   faith   arbitrarily,
dialectical. Examine what faith iswhether

notion of faith, or at least of the Christian  faith,  that  defines  it  by
contrast to  gauge it by reference to a supposedly  indisputable  notion  of
knowledge is bound to misunderstand faith.

Precisely because this is the  idea  of  knowledge  presupposed  by  the  UD
documents, can those docuemtns present faith as complementary to knowledge.

            I think that any conception of faith (like yours  and  like  the
conception reflected in the  UD  documents)  that  takes  for  granted  what
knowledge is and defines faith by contrast to that supposedly  unassailable,
undisputable idea of knowledge is bound to result in a  misunderstanding  of
faith. Notice that what you call the traditional approach defines  knowledge
as you do and conceives of faith as a  sort  of  complementary  supernatural
knowledge. And your apophatic faith is just a  non-cognitive  complement  in
the area of ultimate concerns to that idea of knowledge.
 That is the view of knowledge to which the view of faith which  you  reject
presents itself as its supernatural complement. And  that  is  the  view  of
knowledge to which your apophatic faith presents  itself  as  its  (natural?
supernatural?) complement in matters related to existential  anxiety.  Since
you think  that  knowledge  furnishes  valid  "answers,"  that  is,  factual
information which can be verified by everybody,  and  that  religion  cannot
furnish that, what you look for is a complement to knowledge  that  consists
not in information but in a peaceful feeling that has  nothing  to  do  with
knowledge.
            I do  not  think  that  there  is  knowledge  of  the  kind  you
describe. Consequently, I do not think that faith is meant to function as  a
complement to what such knowledge does not furnish
