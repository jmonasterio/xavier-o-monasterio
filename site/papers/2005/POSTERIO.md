THE MEDIEVALS AND THE POSTERIOR ANALYTICS

My main reference here are Nussbaum's "Saving Aris's Appearances," her Love's Knowledge: "An Aristotelian Conception of reality," and Owen's "Tizenai ta Phainomena".

From Nussbaum's "Saving" I  get the idea that the medievals misread Aris's understanding of science in the Posterior Analytics . They thought there was an intuition of principles, whereas Aris did not. Owen does not refer to principles. But he does not seem to discard as un-aristotelian the idea of a deductive science [PLATONIC?]. Furthermore, he makes a distinction--which Nussbaum discards--between setting down phainomena perceptually (in a Baconian way) and dialectically, as Aris does in the Physics in contradiction with his own method . In fact, he starts by pointing out this contradiction in Aris. Nussbaum thinks that the method was the same in the Physics, in the Ethics, and always and everywhere, and that the method was dialectical. However, she underscores that different sectors of phainomena have to be approached in a different way, which seems to be one of Owen's points in his distinction (though perhaps he is wrong in his Baconism--a matter of empirical facts).

I sense a contradiction in Nussbaum. On the one hand, in Love's Knowledge , she defends the view that Ethics cannot be scientific both by Aris's own criteria (which she defines) and Aris's own acknowledgment. On the other hand, if the approach followed by Aris to set down ta phainomena is always the same and it is the dialectical approach, it seems that the Aristotelian Ethics is scientific. And, as a matter of fact, this is the view she suggests in The Fragility when she  speaks of Aris' "strange realism" and of her intention of keeping the philosophical approach together with the literary (or unscientific) approach.

Of course, the first step is to investigate whether there is indeed such a contradiction in Nussbaum. Assuming, however, that there is, what is its import concerning the Medievals' reading of Aristotle and their infatuation with the deductive method of the Posterior Analytics? (Curiously, I do not remember Nussbaum insisting on the point made by Aris that the purpose of Ethics is not knowing but acting correctly. This is important, though. After all, what makes ethics unscientific: the fact that it is practical rather than theoretical or speculative, or the fact that it is not observational but participational, that is, emotional-imaginational and in-presence?).

The import for the Medievals' reading is, first, that it makes more conceivable their understanding of Aris's scientific ideal. (Remark, however, that Aquinas seems to follow the dialectical method ("ta legomena" as his starting point), although ta legomena of theology are certain and indisputable, already settled down by God himself, though they are not evident for us. By contrast, the philosophical legoumena--for instance, the proofs for God's existence--are to be setted down, defined).

OWEN:

The Post A pictures a science as a formal deductive system based on necessary truths. But it is not only interested in the finished state of a science but in its essential preliminaries; it describes not only the rigorous deduction of theorems but the setting up of the archai , the set of special hypotheses and definitions from which the deductions proceed. The physics takes no pains to hold apart the finding and the applying of the principles. The real contrast between the Physics and the Analytics concerns the means by which the principles of the science are reached.

In the Prior A n "it falls to experience to provide the principles of any subject. In astronomy, it was astronomical experience that provided the principles of the science, for it was only when the ph ainomena were adequately grasped that the proofs in astronomy were discovered. And the same is true of any art or science whatsoever. Elsewhere he draws the same Baconian picture: the phainomena must be collected as a prelude to finding the theory which explains them, and the method is explicitly associated with natural science (phusike). But such is not the method followed by Aris in the Physics where everything comes down to more or less detailed analyses guided and illustrated rather than founded upon empirical data. In this sense, Aris in the Physics is not estalishing the principles upon a survey of the phainomena.

What Aris's critics forget is that in other contexts concerned with methods of inquiry phainomena has another sense : endoxa (the common conceptions on the subject, and what is commonly said on the subject (either by the many, or by the wise).

This ambiguity in "phainomena" carries with it a corresponding distinction in the use of such connected distinctions as Induction (epagoge ) and puzzles (aporiai ). Epagoge establishes the principles of science by starting from the data of perception (APst), but is also one of the cardinal methods of dialectic (topoi) and in this way it can be used to find the cardinal principles of the sciences.

Similarly with the puzzles: When the phainomena are empirical data like in biology the aporiai are questions of empirical fact or of explanations of such facts or of placing a recalcitrant fact within the empirical hypothesis, In the discussion of incontinence, on the other hand, where the phainomena are things that me are inclined or accustomed to say on the subject , the aporiai  are logical or philosophical puzzles generated by exploiting some of the things commonly said.

The latter is the case in the Physics both concerning the convictions or usage of the many or the wise (where the dokounta that survive are taken as premises for what follows, for if the difficulties are resolved and the endoxa are left standing this in itself is sufficient proof) as well as the epagoge, which proves to be not a review of observed cases but a dialectical survey of the legoumena; where the data are not for the most part of natural history but of dialectic, and its problems are accordingly not questions of empirical fact but conceptual puzzles.

This ambiguity in Aris's terminology explains why the methods of the Analytics cannot cover the method of the Physics. Furthermore, even within the second sense of phainomena--equated with endoxa and legomena- -some essential distinctions lie concealed. For an appeal to a legomenon may be either to common belief about matters of fact or to established forms of language or to a philosophical thesis claiming the factual virtues of the first and the analytic certainty of the second (examples ibidem).

At least the broader ambiguity about the two senses of phainomena Aris himself exposed, for when he wishes to restrict phainomenon to its first sense he calls it expressly a "perceptual phainomenon" and distinguishes it from an endoxon.

Yet Aris was entitled to take the formuula in the Analytics  to apply to the Physics and to the Historia animalium, for the two senses of phainomena have much in common. For certain phainomena in the first sense (observation of astronomers) are beyond accurate observation. And many a biological 'observation" is bound to be hearsay, legomena to be treated with caution; only properly established can they work as true data. Some endoxa as well pass the test and become firm data. Nor, if Aris associates the phainomena with experience (empireia), as he does in the text from the Analytics, must it be supposed that his words are meant to apply only to phainomena in the first sense. Endoxa also rest on experience, even if they misrepresent it. An endoxon that is shared by all men is ipso facto beyond challenge. "The phainomena to which the Physics pays most attention are the familiar data of dialectic, and from the context in the Prior Analytic it seems clear that Aris's words there are meant to cover the use of such data. For in concluding the passage and the discussion in which it occurs Aris observes that he has been talking at large about the ways in which the premisses of deductive argument are to be chosen; and he refers for a more detailed treatment of the same matter to the treatise on dialectic (APr. I 30, 46a28-30). He evidently has in mind the claim made in the Topics that the first premisses of scientific argument can be established by methods which start from the endoxa". "Ross seems to mistake the sense of the APr (46a28-30) when he writes: 'It is of course only the selection of premises of dialectical reasoning that is discussed in the Topics; the nature of the premisses of scientiific reasoning is discussed in the Posterior Analytics.' But in this passage Aris is concerned with finding the principles of scientific reasoning, and must be thinking of the claim made in the Topics to find such principles dialectically."

NUSSBAUM:

Saving aris's appearances

Owen was right in cvorrecting the view of a sharp distinction between science and metaphysics (?). He was wrong in retaining a Baconinan view concerning one sense of phainomena, and in attributing to Aris the ambiguity of two senses and two distinct methods. There is only the second sense connected with what we say and our common beliefs (273). There is in fact no case for crediting Aris with a Baconian picture of science based on theory-neutral observation. Instead, we find in Aris a loose and inclusive notion of  experience, or the ways a human observer sees or takes the world, using his cognitive faculties (all of which Aris calls kritika, concerned with making distinctions). The two alleged senses and methods are one. When Aris takes notes on shellfish he is doing something not far removed from his activity when he records akrasia. He will be describing the world as it appears to observers who are members of our kind. Certainly there are important differences between these two activities ; but there is also an important link, and it is legitimate for him to stress it. Against Parmenides who repudiated both the evidence of sense perception and the data of shared language, Aris defends a method thoroughly committed to human experience and its limits. There will be different and differently gathered appearances in each area. But all areas will include both a study of ordinary beliefs and sayings and a review of previous scientific or philosophical treatments of the problem, the view of the many and the wise (275). We must at the end of our account bring it back to the phainomena and show that it does preserve them as true--at least the greater number and the most basic. One has to consider practice. (277)

The importance of the expert emerges if we consider Aris's account of our basic linguistic practices of introducing into discourse and defining. In APost II 8, Aris develops an account of the transition from our initial use of a natural kind term to its scientific definition. The kind term enters our use on the basis of some communal experience. "Thunder" is a noise in the clouds, eclipse a privation of light. At this point we are able to semainein (NAMING) thunder and eclipse, to introduce them into discourse, to refer to them; but we do not yet have the scientific definition that states the nature of things of this kind. We move to this when we have some account or theory of the phenomenon. Thunder is the quenching of fire in the clouds. The expert, not the layman, uncovers this theory. We are bound to regarding this theory as defining and bounding (at least pro tempore) the nature of this being, even if some individuals whom we have previously included in the extension of the term have to be excluded. Our agreement in a commitment to scientific exploration proves more basic than our prima facie disagreeement with the scientist over the extension of the term. (WHY NO CATHOLIC PHYSICS?). (279-80).

The above account of Aris's philosophical/scientific method rests on the Metaphysics and the specific scientific treatises. How is it to be put together with the account of scientific understanding of the Posterior Analytics ? (281)

The Analytics demands that in the natural sciences (as opposed to ethics) the expert should in the end be able to validate his claim to undestanding by giving systematic demonstrations. This is compatible if the deductive ideal is seen as arising itself from the appearences, a commitment which we believe ourselves to undertake when we do science. This is  what Aris does in APos 71b9, where he begins with an account of the conditions under which we think we understand something. Similarly, the Physics discusion of explanation begins from the ways in which we ask and answer "why?" questions, and criticizes earlier scientists for insufficient attention to the variety of our usage. At every step Aris is concerned to show how his norm arises out of the appearances and embodies their requirements. (IT IS YET ANOTHER PROBLEM, OF COURSE, TO RELATE THIS NORM TO THE PRACTICE OF ARIS'S SCIENTIFIC TREATISES WHERE DEDUCTIONS OF THIS SORT ARE VERY RARELY PRESENT. THIS DISCREPANCY MAY INDICATE ONLY THAT ARIS DOES NOT BELIEVE HE IS READY TO CLAIM FULL EPISTEME; ON THE OTHER HAND, IT IS PLAUSIBLE, AS I ARGUE IN DE mOTU, eSSAY 2, THAT THE EVIDENCE UNCOVERED IN ACTUAL SCIENTIFIC WORK LED ARIS TO MAKE SOME REVISIONS IN HIS METHODOLOGICAL NORMS, ESPECIALLY WITH REGARD TO THE AUTONOMY OF THE SCIENCES). In the  Ethics, on the other hand, he takes pains to argue that our beliefs about practice do not yield the demand for a deductive system. He is evidently not interested in assimilating the appearances to a theoretical ideal where the appearances themselves do not reveal a commitment to such an ideal . (THERE WILL BE SOME SIGNIFICANT DIFFERENCE BETWEEN THE SCIENCES AND ETHICS (OF WHICH THERE IS, IN aRIS'S VIEW, NO EPISTEME) IN TWO AREAS ABOVE ALL: (1) THE DEGREE TO WHICH CONSISTENCY IS A REQUIREMENT, AND THE NATURE OF THE CONSISTENCY SOUGHT; (2) THE AMOUNT OF VAGUENESS OR INDETERMINACY IN THE SUBJECT-MATTER, WHICH WILL HAVE IMPLICATIONS FOR THE USEFULNESS AND FEASIBILITY OF GENERAL ACCOUNTS. THE ISSUES ARE DISCUSSED IN ESSAY 4 OF MY dE mOTU). (281-282).

More troublesome: the first principles of science in the Analytics have been thought  by centuries of commentators, via the medieval tradition, to be a priori truths grasped by special acts of intellectual intuition . Surely the finished structure of an Aristotelian science rests on these, and not, ultimately on appearances. Thus, if the scientific works rest on appearances, they depart from the ideal set forth in the Analytics (282).

Nussbaum thinks that the fact that the principles are true, undemonstrable, necessary, primary, and both prior and more primary than the conclusion  does not commit us to special acts of rational intuition, nor to the notion that the principles are true outside of all conceptual schemes, all language . The idea of a special intuition of the principles is derived from POst Analytics II, 19 . But recent work on"nous" (intellect) and episteme (understanding) in the Analytics by Kosman, Lesher, and Burnyeat belies this. We grasp the principles and use them inside our experience, as the text of II l9 asserts. We move from this grasp to the reflective ability to give accounts. There is no reason to posit two philosophical methods here, one dealing with the appearances, one resting on the a priori; DIALECTIC AND FIRST PHILOSOPHY HAVE, AS ARIS INSISTS IN METAPHYSICS IV 2, EXACTLY THE SAME SUBJECT MATTER. THE APPEARANCE, THEN, CAN GO ALL THE WAY DOWN. (282-283).

What, then, is meant by the claim that the first principles are true and undemonstrated; and where do we get our conviction of truth? The Analytics does not answer this question. We must turn to Metaphysics IV, where we shall see how Aris defends their claim against the sceptic's attack (283).

In Metaphysics IV, 4, Aris considers how we should deal with an opponent who challenges the principle of non-contradiction, which he calls the most secure starting point ( arch e) of all because it underlies all inquiry. Aris says: They demand a demonstration out of   apaideusia , for it is apaideusia not to recognize what needs demonstration and what does not. Apaideusia is, for example, the condition of Cyclops, humanoids who live in isolation from a human (linguistic) community. They lack the sensitive awareness, produced by education and experience, of the fundamental role this principle plays in all our practices, in all our discourse (284). Aris's way: Either the person says something, thereby using the principle, or nothing, and then is out of the community (285). He does not say that the principle is true apart from the appearances and from human conceptual schemes, true of the way the world is behind or beyond the categories of our thought and discourse. The principle is unassailable and undemonstrable. So, in a very important way, Aris does not answer the opponent's challenge; he does not offer him the Platonic certitude he wants . The same with the "lunatics" who deny movement and plurality. The same in Metaphysics IV, 4, where he extends his discourse argument for the principle of non-contradiction to cover cases in which the opponent, though possibly silent, reveals his commitment to the principle through his practices: why does he not throw himself off a precipice as though he holds that it is not good and good to fall in? [CF. HUME} one thing better and the other not better (286-7).

Aris would add that the demand of a demonstration of the principle asks us to stand outside language and is doomed to incohernce . In this sense, the principle is a priori, in that it cannot significantly be defended, explained, or questioned at all from within the appearance, that is to say, the lives and practices of human beings. But it is not a priori in the sense that the principle can be known to hold independently of all experience and all ways of life, all conceptual schemes. Plato asked for unhypothetical foundations of science. Aris understands "unhypothetical" differently: what is necessary for anyonewho understands anything at all to have, this is nota hypothesis (l005bl5-l6) (287-88).

Same refutation for the Eleatics who deny change . The Eleatic is saying what he is in no position to say. Just as a person blind from birth is in no position to use in an argument premises about colours, since he cannotg have had any experience of color, so the Eleatic is in no position to use premises having to do with the unitary, unchanging Being of the universe. Change and plurality are in everything we experience. The eleatic is comic because neither he nor anyboldy has had any experience of the unchanging (289).

Appearances and truth are not opposed, as Plato believed they were. We can have truth only inside the circle of appearances, because only there can we communicate, even refer, at all (290). Truth is one for  all thinking speaking beings (291).

To opt out of a basic 'appearance' will not always entail silence or inaction. Appearances come at different levels of depth ; the cost of doing without one will vary with the case, and must be individually scrutinized. To deny the prevalent belief in gods will lead to a certain loss of community; there will be a very real sense in which theist and atheist do not inhabit the same world or look at the same stars. But the gulf will not be totally unbridgeable, for the possibility remains of one showing to the other that some more basic appearances conflict with the appearances to which he holds and ought to lead him to abandon them (291-2).

What the method does is to explain to us how any radical or new view must commend itself to our attention: by giving evidence of its superior ability to integrate and organise features of our lived experience of the world . Sometimes it may remain unclear over a long period of time whether a bold hypothesis has or has not successfully made the return to the appearances--whether it is the truth, or just empty words (292) .

Love's knowledge : The discernment of perception.

(55) The word "scientific" will be used as Aris used it , to designate a family of characteristics that were usually associated with the claim that a body of knowledge had the status of episteme. Aris's attack on the scientific conceptions of [ethical] rationality is a family of attacks, directed at logically distinct positions--although these positions are in some forms mutually consistent and were combined into a single conception in certain works of Plato . Aris's attack has three distinct dimensions, closely interwoven. These are (1) an attack on the claim that all valuable things are commensurable; (2) an argument for the priority of particular judgments to universals; and (3) a defense of the emotions and the imagination as essential to rational choice.

N. B. NUMBER (3) CAN BE APPLIED TO THE MEDIEVALS' OBJECTION AGAINST THE SCIENTIFIC CHARACTER OF THEOLOGY, AS WELL AS NUMBER (2) IN A MORE QUALIFIED WAY.

FOR PLATO, there is only one scientific method which yields knowledge; the rest is opinion, belief. The method is unhypothetical, based on self evident principles . (Cf. Nussbaum).

FOR NUSSBAUM there is only one Aristotelian method. There is no science grounded on self-evident principles which would be purely deductive. And there is only one scientific method, though regional varieties impose differences.

FOR OWEN, there are two Aristotelian methods: the dialectical and the Baconian. The former employed in the metaphysical and philosophical works, the latter in the scientific treatises.

FOR THE SCHOLASTICS, possibly, there are three methods: One that uses self-evident,necessarily true principles, perhaps combined with other a posteriori true principles that involve the setting down of phenomena. A second, like in geometry and perhaps in the physical sciences, that involves a posteriori truths based on the settig down of phenomena and empirical confirmation by the phenomena.

OBSERVATIONS

- Greek reason is dominated by the Platonic ideal of science. Sharp opposition between doxa and knowledge. Strive for knowledge which is purely intellectual and necessary, not of  singular contingents which are mere shadows or perhaps imperfect participations. The ideal is unattainable even in mathematics.

- Apart from his opposition to the eternal separate forms, Arist places the forms in reality, as "natures". The forms are universal, necessary (or at least necessarily true because they are natures understood).

- The objections of the Anti-Aristotelians and the Non-Aristotelians are highly revealing of the way in which they understood "scientia". What tempted them in the Greeks was truth as knowledge of the real, and certainty as knowledge that is firm, that cannot be otherwise. This attracted them because they thought they possessed, concerning the divinely revealed, that kind of knowledge. This is why they were so confident and eager in their reception of Aristotle. Curiously, however, that was not what Aris furnished them. What he furnished them was a method to reach that insofar as possible through purely human means, a method which was, by the same token, exclusive of revelation. A method that, as far as science was concerned, presupposed an ideal knower that was a contemplating mind (be it an eye or an intellect). It is true that Aris also has an ethics, a practical reason. But this reason is inferior, for it does not reach natures, only what is true for the most part, which did not interest Christians. Is this the reason of an exposed agent? Not quite sure, for the Ethics is ambiguous: it looks for what is true for the most part about the (exposed?) agent interested in being happy or fully human. And, anyway, it has a level of generality that action does not have. It is an ethics of virtue, of the virtuous agent, not an ethics of actions.
