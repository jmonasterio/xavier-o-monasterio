\wp\chantill\thekey-3                     March 12, 1992

              Knolwedge and Predication, Desire and Action

This paper continues "WP\CHANTILL\THEKEY-2." (See its conclusion and the section after its conclusion).

The God I believe in is a motivating God. I live braced for his presence. Can the classical proofs for God's existence yield this kind of practical knowledge? Can they lead to a statement of the kind "Your mother will arrive tomorrow morning", that is, to a motivated-motivating belief--the belief of the exposed? By this I mean a belief that is meaningful for the agent, not merely leading to a blind automatic response but based on the grasped identity of the believed in, where part of the identity ofthebelieved in is its capacity to move and move to action.

The insufficiency of the proofs

Fundamentally, the deficiency of the classical proofs for God's existence, Saint Anselm's included, lies in their incapacity to motivate the agent, for they can lead in the best case to mere intellectual assent. But this needs some unpacking.

The proofs involve objectification. Objectification abstracts the object at stake, converts it into a mere explanandum and, in particular, cuts the ties of concern between the contemplator and the object to consider the object by itself. (When it is a class of objects rather than a singulr one it focuses on a particulr common trait--bodies fall, all observed entities exist contingently. It looks for the explanation of the trait, tht is, takes the trait as the explanadum).

Even if we were to assume that the proofs for God's existence work, they would at best demonstrate the existence of an ultimate uncaused cause, of a being whose existence is necessary or identical with its identity, of an intelligent ordinator of the world. Aquinas concludes each of his proofs with the words "And this we call God." God we may call it, if we so choose, but its necessity, its causality and even its intelligence do not in any way imply a personal concern on its part for the contingent, causally dependent universe or for its inhabitants. What, indeed, in the notion of a necessarily existent, supremely intelligent, uncaused and ultimately causating Being entails that it cares for the contingent and causally dependent? As the demonstration that the sum of the three angles of a triangle amounts to two straight angles demonstrates only what is stated and leads to assent only to that, so the proof that an uncaused cause exists proves only what is stated and leads to assent only to that. And nothing in the notion of uncaused involves that the uncaused cares at all, much less that it cares for what is causally dependent. On the other hand, again assuming that the proofs work, it is evident that the existence of such uncaused cause which, according to Aquinas, is what " we call God," is compatible with our suffering and death, for we actually suffer and die. Does not this rather suggest, as Bertrand Russell argued in his famous parable, that, if such Supreme Being exists as the proofs establish, this Supreme Being is either simply cruel and callous or at least totally indifferent to our suffering? If so, assuming that the intellectual assent to the His existence could motivate us to anything, how could it motivate us to filial submission and trust? Why whould such a Being be worthy of what we Christians mean by "adoration," the only appropriate conduct of a human being toward Him whom we mean by "God"? In short, leaving to Aquinas to speak for himself, I reject his "we," for the Supreme Being of his proofs is not what what we Christians call God. Pascal was right: the God of the proofs, the God  about whom philosophers discuss and whose existence they affirm or deny is not the God of Jesus Christ.

The statement by Aquinas that an uncaused cause exists, even were I to asssent to it, satisfies perhaps my curiosity but does not prompt in me any emotion and leads me to no particular action. By contrast, the announcement of my mother's arrival to my home tomorrow morning motivates my attitudes and conduct. That is, I experience my mother's imminent arrival not as a neutral fact to be assented to but affecting me personally, and I brace myself for that event. But none of the proofs yields anything for which I have to brace myself. Necessary, uncaused and ultimately causating Being are purely intelligible, rather than attractive or repellent predicates, susceptible perhaps of assent, but radically incapable of motivating conduct. Only a proof that demonstrated, that is, yielded as a necessary conclusion, the existence of a loving, caring God whom I could trust, to whom I could pray, and before whom I could kneel in adoration could bring me to say "And this we Christians call God." As far as I know, such a proof has never been offered. But would such a proof be possible? I think not.

God and the nature of the proofs

Necessarily existent, uncaused, ultimately causating, even intelligent are predicates of a Being who is neither attractive nor repellent but merely intelligible. In other words, the predicates themselves, and therefore the Being whose essential qualities they are, are neither attractive nor repellent and, therefore, do not lead to bracing ourselves for anything even if we assent to them.

In other cases, however, we make and understand attractive and repellent predications that, under circumstances to be defined, motivate us to act and lead us to brace ourselves for something. A case in point is the statement made to me "Your mother arrives tomorrow morning to your home." However, Kant's pure reason and Aris's speculative intellect are incapable of making and of grasping such predications. Why?

In spite of the imaginary nature of the character, one could make the statement "Santa is a good fellow." But to make or grasp such a statement one has to have experienced yearning for toys as a child and having that yearning satisfied as if by magic. Angels, who neither want nor have any use for toys, cannot understand or make this kind of statement, for they have no experience by reference to which the statement would be meaningful to them. Now, precisely of this kind are the predicates and statements capable of making us brace ourselves for..., that is, what I have called motivating predicates and statements. And this is the kind of statements and predicates that the proofs cannot yield. Why?

Certain people who believe in the Christian God lose their faith when confronted with the suffering and death that go rampant in the world, or at least feel strongly tempted to do so.

What is the logic involved here? Why do this rampant suffering and death motivate people to refuse to believe in God, whether or not they actually lose their faith?

If the proofs were to lead to God's existence they would have to be of this kind, for the same practical logic is involved in God's existence as in the loss of belief in God.

Believing in God is having a set of practical expectations motivated by the one believed in.  Animals can respond to and be braced for, but they cannot believe in. That is, they cannot have motivated practical expectations. For animals, it is either the present object that prompts the response, or the signs of its imminent presence that prompts the bracing. Animal behavior is caused, ours is motivated. This is a way of saying that no truth is involved in animal practice, whereas in ours there is.

Motivation involves truth. But the truth involved is not of the order of mere intelligibility, but motivating, practical. In those who lose their faith in God, the belief ceases to be truth when it ceases to be motivating, and it ceases to be motivating when it ceases to be true.

Motivation, however, is different from causation. A rapist is not caused to rape, but motivated to rape, whereas a dog is caused to copulate. This implies motivation and self-motivation, which may or may not coincide with each other. Motivation originates in the object, self-motivation in the subject. But they are not independent from each other. The motivating-identity of the object is necessary but not sufficient; I cannot motivate myself independently from the motivating-identity of the object. But the motivating identity of the object cannot motivate me (though in certain cases it can cause me); I have to make myself go along or abstain from going along with its motivating force. Motivation becomes effective when it leads to action.

Saying that there is truth involved in motivation--though, again, the truth is practical--implies a certain objectivity.

And saying that the truth involved is practical implies an objectivity different from that of mere intelligibility.

With these remarks as our basis, let us return to the logic involved in the loss of faith in God because of the evil rampant in our world. As already underscored, the motivating force of God vanishes together with the truth of his existence, but the truth of his existence vanishes together with his motivating force. Now, we tend to separate the truth from the motivating force--the "is" form the "ought"--to make the latter dependent on the former, and the former independent from the latter, thereby reducing truth to assent-prompting, and motivating force to behavioral causation. If this were correct, however, it would be not only conceivable but actually possible to believe in God by merely assenting to his existence, and then God's motivating force would be merely causating and unintelligible. In short, there would be no practical truth. Does this correspond to the data of our experience? Are attitudes rationally appropriate?

Are attitudes toward God rationally appropriate? Is the approppirateness of attitudes toward God independent of his existence? Is the belief that God exists independent from attitudes toward God? Is the belief in the Christian God independent from the attitudes toward him? Can the belief in the Christian God appropriately survive the absence of attitudes toward God?

Believing in Santa

"Desire is expressed by the caress

as thought is by language" (BN, 506).

Let us say that I believe in Santa. What is this? Under the appropriate conditions, I put my show by the chimney on December 24. Santa, for me, has motivating force, and the motivating force depends on Santa's identity.

"Santa exists"
