X. O. Monasterio

Discussion  questions  for  David  J.  O'Brien,  From  the  Heart   of   the
      American Catholic Church

Chapter 7

- "Faculty and administration alike agreed [at  Holy  Cross]  that  research
and publication held the key to the excellence that was the  college's  most
important goal, a goal to which everyone seemed committed" (p. 128).
      Questions: Would the same agreement obtain at UD? Should research  and
publication be the most important goal of UD?

- "Everyone without exception wanted Holy Cross to  remain  a  liberal  arts
college and a jesuit college, and almost everyone  agreed  that  Holy  Cross
has special responsibilities as a Catholic institution. But  there  was  and
there remains very wide disagreement about what each of those  terms  means"
(p. 131).
      Questions: Do you think that at UD almost everyone agrees that UD  has
special responsibilities as a Catholic institution? Do you  yourself  agree?
If so, what is your own idea of those responsibilities? Do  you  think  that
there is a wide agreement at UD as to what those responsibilities are?  What
is the value of agreeing that UD has special responsibilities as a  Catholic
institution if we cannot put any content to it?

Chapter 9

- The Catholic intellectual feels caught between doctrinaire  ecclesiastical
authorities and  religious  populism  or  evangelicalism.  The  gap  between
theology after "the critical turn" and popular piety is enormous  (pp.  161-
165). Compare this to the following: "Too often the  Catholic  college  goes
far beyond the authentic respect for the autonomy of the secular  recognized
by Vatican II. It marginalizes religion and institutionalizes political  and
social irresponsibility.  Here  far  more  than  in  the  area  of  supposed
secularization lies the real failure of nerve" (around p. 197).
      Questions: Are Catholic intellectuals somewhat  at  fault?  Have  they
created, or contributed to create, an  unjustifiable  gap  between  theology
and popular piety? Has  the  professionalization  of  theology  in  Catholic
colleges and  universities  as  one  discipline  among  others  marginalized
religion?

- In a poll the majority of theologians felt that  their  department  should
not have any special  responsibility  for  the  Catholic  character  of  the
institution.
      Question: What is your reaction to this?

- In graduate programs, questions of value only rarely find their  way  into
the classroom or comprehensive exam.  Questions  of  meaning,  religious  or
philosophical,  never  do.  Here,  on  graduate  education,  is   the   real
battleground. Yet, critics make no  suggestion  about  alternative  graduate
programs or faculty development (p. 181).
      Question: What do you think of this view of graduate programs both  in
theology and philosophy as well as in other subjects,  particularly  in  the
humanities? Any suggestions?

- The ideology of  modern  liberalism  relegates  religion  to  the  private
domain in an attempt to  construct  a  public  square  devoid  of  religious
conviction and independently of any particular conception of the  good.  All
sectarian beliefs will be tolerated, but only if those who  hold  them  that
all of them are equally valid and equally irrelevant to  matters  of  public
concern.  The  common  life  is  to  be  conducted  on  essentially  secular
principles to which we will all  subscribe  and  agree  byu  virtue  in  our
common share in reason (p. 183).
      Questions: Is this  ideology  acceptable?  Do  Catholic  colleges  and
universities live practically by this ideology? Any alternatives?

- Three options: One is to reaffirm  the  particular  values  of  particular
groups: the Church must be the Church, its options  must  inform  explicitly
Catholic schools. The second is to live and let live. Allow marketplaces  to
adjudicate all claims; the Church should concentrate  on  religion,  leaving
academic  life  alone.  The  third  is  to  take  up  the  shared  work   of
reconstructing the foundations of public discourse and thus the common  life
(p. 183).
      Questions: Are these three options exhaustive, or  there  are  others?
Is the second option the one that led to the  secularization  of  Protestant
colleges? Which of these options appears to you the  most  desirable?  Where
does UD fall today  in this spectrum?
