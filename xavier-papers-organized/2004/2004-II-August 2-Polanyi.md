Tacit Knowledge            

Michael Polanyi introduced the notion of “tacit knowledge,” by which he became known, to refer to those cases in which a speaker “knows more than he can verbalize.” He exemplified this situation with the experiment conducted by psychologists Lazarus and McCleary in 1949.

“These authors presented a person with a large number of nonsense syllables, and after showing certain of the syllables, they administered an electric shock. Presently the person showed symptoms of anticipating the shock at the sight of ‘shock syllables’; yet, on questioning, he could not identify them. He had come to know when to expect a shock, but he could not tell what made him expect it. He had acquired a knowledge similar to that which we have when we know a person by signs we cannot tell.” ^(F2)

On reflection, the very notion of tacit knowledge (from now on TK), as characterized by Polanyi, sounds nonsensical. Indeed, it seems to amount to an unconscious knowledge, for the subject who supposedly knows is not aware of what he knows, since he cannot say what he knows. Now, an unconscious knowledge is an oxymoron. Why? Because the very notion of knowledge, or at least the ordinary notion of knowledge that common sense seems to impose on us, implies on the subject’s part the awareness of what he knows. He knows what he knows thanks to the awareness he has of what he knows, so that he would not know what he knows without that awareness.

As an example of this ordinary notion of knowledge, let us consider the knowledge of which Searle spoke in the preceding chapter, since he used the word ‘knowledge’ in its ordinary meaning. According to Searle, in linguistic communication the hearer who understands what the speaker says thereby acquires the knowledge  –that is, becomes aware– of what the speaker intends to communicate to him. And it would be inconceivable that the hearer knew what the speaker intends to communicate if the hearer had no awareness at all of what he knew. In other words, what a subject knows becomes object known to him by the very fact of his becoming aware of it. Hence no knowledge of object without awareness of what is known, because knowledge implies and demands the awareness of the object known. Thus, when Polanyi defines KT as a knowledge in which the subject cannot say what he supposedly knows, we have to conclude that KT is a knowledge in which the subject is not aware of what he knows, which is a contradiction in terms.  

For his part, Polanyi would reply to this objection by referring us to the undeniable facts of the experiment he described: the subject of the experiment cannot say which are the shock-producing syllables because he does not know them, that is, is not aware of them, has no consciousness of them. Yet the subject’s behavior in reaction to those syllables shows that he knows them in a certain way –and this certain way of knowing is what Polanyi calls TK.

As the facts of the experiment seem undeniable, we cannot ignore them. Let us then try to see things more clearly. For this purpose, it might be helpful to compare Polanyi’s TK to what Sartre called “non-thetic or non-positional awareness” in an analysis that led him to his famous notion of “Bad faith.” ^(F3) According to Sartre, the “censor” of which Freud speaks and to which Freud attributes the task of preventing the psychoanalysand from acknowledging his own shameful impulses by rendering them unconscious , is nothing else than the psychoanalysand himself who prevents himself from acknowledging them precisely because he is conscious of those. How, indeed, Sartre reasons, could the Freudian censor censure only the shameful impulses and not others, if they were totally unconscious?

What happens actually, answers Sartre, is that the psychoanalysand, who is conscious of his shameful impulses, does not want to assume responsibility for them. Now, what renders possible this hypocrisy on the psychoanalysand’s part, who pretends to himself not to know at all the impulses he himself censures with such discrimination, is that he is conscious or aware of them only under the mode of not being explicitly aware of them, because he has not of them the kind of thetic or positional consciousness of object we normally have of what  we know . The psychoanalysand, in Sartre’s view, has only an implicit or not verbalized awareness of his shameful impulses, and his awareness is only implicit precisely because it does not reach the level of verbal formulation. In short, the psychoanalysand knows his shameful impulses because he is conscious of them, yet he can pretend not to know them because he is only non-positionally aware of them, that is, cannot verbalize what he is aware of.

In order to gain as precise an idea As possible on Polanyi’s tacit knowledge, let us now inquire whether Sartre’s non-positional consciousness is equivalent to Polanyi’s TK. Thanks to his notion of non-positional consciousness Sartre can show, against Freud, that the psychoanalysand is conscious of his shameful impulses, that is, knows them, and is therefore responsible for his refusal to assume responsibility for them – a responsibility of which he is discharged by Freud under the pretext that they are unconscious. Sartre’s reply to Freud is that in order  to be conscious of something and, hence, to know it, it is not required to verbalize explicitly that of which one is only non-positionally conscious. Consequently,  the psychoanalysand does not formulate to himself that of which he is implicitly aware simply because he does not want to. Indeed, that is why Sartre considers him responsible for his shameful impulses. But this very trait of Sartre’s non-positional consciousness, which he rightly equates with our ordinary notion of knowledge because it involves the consciousness of what is known (though not the explicit consciousness brought about by verbalization), cannot be equated with Polanyi’s TK. The reason is that the subject who has TK of the shock-producing syllables does not know at all, either explicitly or even implicitly, which are those syllables. Different in this from the Sartrean psychoanalysand, the subject of the experiment does not identify verbally the shock-producing syllables, not because he does not want to render explicit his knowledge of his shameful impulses in order not to have to assume responsibility for them, but because the subject of the experiment literally has no knowledge of them.    

Let us be clear. Sartre does not reject the ordinary notion of knowledge as consciousness of object. He simply claims that the consciousness in which knowledge is ordinarily deemed to   consist can take two forms: the form of  positional consciousness of object, thanks to verbalization, as well as the less common form of merely implicit or non-positional consciousness of object. In other words, verbalization is not a necessary condition for knowledge, because the consciousness in which knowledge is ordinarily deemed to consist is possible without verbalization. Verbalization only renders explicit the implicit consciousness of object that the subject possesses before verbalization, and implicit or not verbalized consciousness is sufficient to constitute knowledge in the ordinary sense.

Thus, Sartre’s subject is initially non-positionally aware of his shameful impulses, so that, if he so decides, he can express in words the characteristics of the object of which he is non-positionally aware. The subject who possesses TK, by contrast, cannot say which are the shock-producing syllables because he does not know them at all. In other words, if TK is knowledge, it is not knowledge in the ordinary sense of the word, for it does not consist in awareness of object.

For my present purposes it is irrelevant whether or not one accepts Sartre’s view of implicit consciousness as pre-verbal consciousness of object. His analysis was only helpful in that it shows that the commonly accepted notion of  knowledge, in whatever variety,  implies consciousness of object; TK, by contrast, does not involve consciousness of object.

How, then, can Polanyi claim that what he calls “tacit knowledge” is knowledge at all? If it is, it has to be knowledge in a different sense.

Polanyi’s alternative conception of knowledge

Two different conceptions of knowledge

The consciousness of object in which knowledge is ordinarily deemed to consist is an awareness of the object’s characteristics. That, indeed, is  what is rendered explicit when verbalization occurs. However, the subject’s awareness in the case of TK is not of the same type, for it does not consist in a consciousness of certain characteristics of the object that can be expressed enunciatively. What occurs in the subject of the experiment at the sight of the shock-producing syllables is not an awareness of the object’s characteristics, for he does not know the syllables,  but a blind fear that leads him to brace himself for the shock as if protecting himself from it. Now, the subject’s bracing himself  is not a verbal enunciation, not even a silent one, but a non-linguistic conduct or behavior motivated by the affection of fear – a conduct whose reason is totally obscure to the subject, since he doesn’t know which syllables produce shocks.  

Thus, the subject of the experiment makes no linguistic enunciation. Rather, he behaves in a certain way, and this behavior is observed by those who conduct the experiment. Actually, the behavior of the subject is precisely the basis on which Polanyi grounds his claim that  the subject ‘knows’ in one way or another, and without that behavioral expression at the sight of the shock-producing syllables and not at the sight of the other syllables Polanyi would have no basis for his claim of tacit knowledge. Now, this connection of action with what Polanyi call tacit knowledge , in the absence of verbalization –which is why the knowledge is said to be tacit rather than explicit–  is a precious clue. Indeed , it is a first indication that knowledge may consist in action of a certain kind rather than in a consciousness of the object’s characteristics expressed in verbalization , as it has been traditionally assumed. In which case Polanyi’s notion of tacit knowledge far from being as oxymoronic as it initially appeared, may even open a new epistemic avenue. Let us, therefore, review in detail Polanyi’s views, with special attention to the role played by action instead of  linguistic expression in TK.

In distinguishing the illocutionary from the perlocutionary effects, Searle’s attributes primacy to the illocutionary effect, which consists in knowledge, over the perlocutionary effects, which consist in actions. The underlying reason for this unique primacy of the illocutionary  speech act over all the others is that the illocutionary effect consists in knowledge, and knowledge is the necessary condition for the success of all the perlocutionary intentions of a speaker, whereas the success of the illocutionary intentions of a speaker does not depend on the success of any of the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker. To put it concretely, a speaker cannot say anything without intending to convey to the hearer the knowledge of what he means, but he can say something without producing or intending to produce any other effect than this knowledge. Correlatively, the hearer cannot fulfill the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker without knowing those intentions, that  is, without the illocutionary effect obtaining, but the hearer can know the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker, thereby insuring the success of the communicative act, without fulfilling the speaker’s perlocutionary intentions. Still more concretely, if I do not know what you mean when you ask me to lend you  my pen, I cannot lend you my pen, but I can know that you ask me for my pen, and not lend it to you. Moreover, you can produce in me an illocutionary effect without intending any perlocutionary effect. Thus, in Searle’s interpretation of saluting,  when you salute me, all you intend is to produce in me the knowledge that you mean to salute me, and the communicative exchange succeeds if only I gain that knowledge. So knowledge is independent from action, but action is dependent on knowledge.

Polanyi, for his part, construes the relationship between knowledge and action in a totally different way. He does not ignore that knowledge involves a relationship to action, for he identifies tacit knowledge with disposition to act and with the actual appropriate action when the right opportunity arises;  by the same token, he denies that knowledge is independent from action, as Searle claims.  As already  explained, Polanyi cannot conceive of the illocutionary effect as separated from action, as Searle does. For Polanyi, the knowledge involved in the communicative exchange is ESSENTIALLY practical, not a theoretical awareness of a fact, as it is for Searle. For example, knowing an order received does not consist, according to Polanyi, in becoming aware of that fact, but in the practical disposition to obey the order received, and in actually obeying it at the appropriate time. Similarly, according to Polanyi, knowing a promise received does not consist in becoming factually aware of that fact, as one becomes aware of a color when one sees it in the course of an experiment in a laboratory, but in actually bracing oneself for the fulfillment of the received promise at the appropriate time. If you promise to repay me the $100.00 you owe me when I come to pick them up at your office at 3.00 P.M., the appropriate understanding of your promise does not consist in registering that fact and then doing nothing, as Searle contends,  but in expecting the fulfillment of your promise and in the action of actually coming to your office at 3.00 P.M. to collect my money.

The Polanyian notion of knowledge related to action rather than to consciousness of an object’s characteristics and its linguistic expression is not new at all. Spontaneously, that is, when free from epistemological prejudices, most of us believe that the members of at least the higher species of mammals, say, dogs, possess the ability to know. At the same time, most of us believe that dogs are non-linguistic agents, that is, that they do not posses the linguistic ability we possess, for they do not express in words what they know. But this shows that we are implicitly assuming that knowing is not identical with the consciousness of an object’s characteristics or with the consequent linguistic capacity to enunciate what one knows or is aware of.

On what basis, then, do we attribute to dogs the ability to know? On the basis of their observed actions. Certain of their actions are, we say,”intentional.” They  reveal an agent’s purpose , in that the dog’s agency is not uniform but adapted by the dog to different circumstances for the sake of a desired goal that satisfies a natural need of the dog. By contrast, a marble’s fall down an inclined plane is a uniform action, not an adaptive action, because it is caused by the environmental circumstances, and thus is not intended by the marble for the sake of the satisfaction of a need and, therefore, not modified accordingly by the marble. For that very reason, we have no temptation of attributing knowledge to marbles: their action is not intentional, not for the sake of a goal intended-desired  by the marble, and the uniformity of their action shows no ability on the marble’s part to adapt the action to the circumstances in view of the realization of a goal. In a dogs’ actions, by contrast, we grasp the intention of a goal and, with their increased experience, an increasing ability on their part to adapt their actions to the circumstances and thereby make their actions more efficient for the realization of their goals. Hence we conclude that dogs know.

But are we assuming that animals know enunciatively, though silently? In other words, are we attributing to them an internal or cerebral linguistic ability, that only lacks the vocal organs to express externally in words that of which they are conscious? Or are we rather implying that they know in a non-enunciative, instrumental way? The correct answer is the latter. And the difference between the two answers shows in the different relationship in which knowledge stands to action in each of the two conceptions, which implies two different notions of knowledge irreducible to each other. In one of them –the one I reject, together with Polanyi– knowledge is conceptually or intrinsically independent from action, and therefore can obtain in the absence of any action; action, however, is dependent on knowledge, for representational knowledge of object seems to be a necessary condition for appropriate action. In the second version – the one I deem correct–  knowledge is not representational of object but instrumental for the realization of a desired goal, and is intrinsically (or “conceptually,” in Nussbaum’s parlance ^(F4)) related to action in that it is meant to terminate in action and will actually terminate in action unless prevented by circumstances alien to the agent – be those circumstances lack of opportunity to apply the knowledge, or external positive coaction on the agent that prevents his action.  

This analysis may be refined, but as it is it suffices for my purpose. For my purpose is simply to show that knowledge is not necessarily to be conceived, as Searle among many others does conceive it, as an awareness of object that mirrors or reproduces in a factual way, the object’s characteristics. Rather, our analysis of what – without any epistemological preconceptions – we recognize as knowledge shows that what we identify as knowledge, at least in the case of animals, involves intentional actions for the sake of desired goals, and that verbalization of what is known is not a necessary component of knowledge. In short, knowledge is not identified, at least in this case, with a factual awareness of the characteristics of its object, as implied by the ordinary or commonly accepted notion of knowledge,  but with an agent’s ability to realize a desired goal through intentional action,  that is, through action adapted by the agent to the realization of the desired goal.

That the former conception of knowledge was consecrated as canonical by the classical epistemological tradition is beyond doubt. That Searle’s philosophy of language reflects his own allegiance to that tradition is also beyond doubt, too. But, as we shall now see,  precisely therein lies the radical difference between Searle and Polanyi.  For the latter parts way with that tradition, since he sees knowledge as “ tacit. ” This means that knowledge does not consist in possessing or gaining an implicit or explicit awareness of the object’s characteristics; rather,  knowledge functions as an  instrument for appropriate action, that is, for the sort of action that is apt to the achievement of a goal desired by the agent.

It will be useful to preface my examination of Polanyi’s views on tacit knowledge by remarking that, unlike Searle, Polanyi never wrote a treatise on language. However, his shift to action instead of consciousness of the object characteristics and its consequent linguistic expression enunciation implies a different conception both of the way in which language actually functions and of its role or lack thereof in cognition.

    

Without explicitly speaking of the enunciative speech act, Searle privileges it among all the other speech acts by making it the necessary condition for the success of all the other speech acts and, at the same time, itself independent of the realization of the other speech acts. According to Searle, saying is primarily the linguistic action of enunciating in words what the speaker wants to communicate. Now, there are two kinds of things that a speaker may want to communicate. (1) One is the knowledge of what he intends to communicate. (2) The other is his desire of obtaining a certain action on the hearer’s part. The former is called by Searle the illocutionary effect. The latter, the perlocutionary effect. To succeed in achieving (1) the speaker has to express in words that enunciate what is to be known by the hearer. One enunciates what one wants to be known by the hearer or hearers. Enunciating is putting what one wants to communicate in words that in one way or another are resemblant to what one wants to communicate. The case of (2) is different. The words that demand an action on the hearer’s part are not resemblant to the action demanded.   

This difference between Polanyi’s and Searle’s conceptions of knowledge is that the former thinks that knowledge is instrumental or applicative, whereas Searle thinks it is representational. This difference may be exemplified by the case of geometrical  knowledge. Does a student of Euclidean geometry who is able to prove a theorem and who sincerely claims to understand the proof and can even formulate it on his own, but who is totally incapable of applying that understanding to the solution of pertinent problems, really understand? With most teachers of geometry, I would say no, because geometrical understanding is applicative and practical, not representational of an actually existing  Aristotelian ‘nature.’ ^(F5)

Having distinguished the common or traditional conception of knowledge, shared by Searle, from Polanyi’s own, I should now make clear that the two conceptions are not only different but are mutually incompatible. In other words, the two cannot be correct. Why not? If for no other reason, because of the esssential role desire plays in tacit knowledge,, which is positively excluded in the traditional conception of knowledge .  Indeed, not only does desire play no role at all in the traditional conception of knowledge, but it is positively excluded, for it is deemed to militate against the ‘objectivity’ of the cognition. The reason is that the consciousness of  object, in which knowledge supposedly consists, as well as its linguistic expression, have to represent factually or ‘objectively’ the object, that is, without addition or subtraction by the subject. Not for nothing, in his close commentary on Aristotle’s treatise on linguistic interpretation, did Aquinas contrast what Aristotle sees as the genuinely enunciative speech of the scientist, which is dictated exclusively by the object and leads to knowledge of the truth, with the non-enunciative speech of the rhetorician that appeals to the desires of the hearer and, therefore, distorts the object to be known. Aquinas’s passage is such a succinct formulation of the disturbing function of desire in matters of cognition that it deserves to be quoted in full:

“Then Aristotle says, Let us therefore consider enunciative speech, etc. Here he points out that only enunciative speech is to be treated; the other four species [to wit, deprecative, imperative, interrogative, and vocative] must be omitted as far as the present intention is concerned, because their investigation belongs rather to the sciences of rhetoric or poetics. Enunciative speech belongs to the present consideration and for the following reason: this book is ordered directly to demonstrative science, in which the mind of man is led by an act of reasoning to assent to truth from those things that are proper to the thing; to this end the demonstrator uses only enunciative speech, which signifies things according as truth about them is in the mind. The rhetorician and the poet, on the other hand, induce assent to what they intend not only through what is proper to the thing but also through the dispositions of the hearer. Hence, rhetoricians and poets for the most part strive to move their auditors by arousing certain passions in them, as the Philosophers says in t he Rhetorica. ^(F6)

Notice the contrast between the mind being led to knowledge by the object (“those things that are proper to the thing”), which is the case with genuinely enunciative speech, on the one hand, and by the desires of the knower (“through the dispositions of the hearer”). As long as knowledge is conceived as a factual replication of the object by the consciousness of it the subject has and/or by the linguistic expression by the subject himself  of what is known by him, then desire can only be seen as a disturbing element in the cognitive act. When, however, as is the case with TK, the point of cognition is not to represent the object but to function as an instrument for the agent’s appropriate conduct to achieve a desired end, then the desire of the end to be realized through action becomes an essential component of cognition. ^(F7)    

              

Keeping this important  difference in mind, let us revisit the objections I previously raised to Searle’s linguistic philosophy which is grounded in the representational conception of knowledge. And let  us consider as well the extent to which Polanyi’s instrumental conception of knowledge (whose aptness is immediately recommended to us by the fact that it squares with our spontaneous attribution of knowledge to at least the higher species of animals, even though they do not posses any linguistic ability) coheres with our linguistic practice and with the intuitions of competent users of language.

the fact that TK is intrinsically connected with action. Herein lies the radical difference between the ordinary notion of knowledge and Polanyi’s TK: the ordinary notion of knowledge involves the consciousness of object as vision of its object, with no direct  relationship to action.

Let us follow in detail Polanyi’s analysis of TK. According to the traditional notion, knowledge consists in the revelation of the object to the knowing subject thanks to the awareness he gains of the object. This awareness of object amounts to a purely factual vision of the object known. (Let us remember that the Greek word θεορια means vision as opposed to action.) TK, by contrast, does not involve any objective content, because it doe not function as awareness of object but as instrument for action.

The instrumental role of TK by contrast to the vision or revelation of object in which their ordinary notion of knowledge consists may be illustrated by means of a comparison to the way in which eyeglasses function. When the eyeglasses function as such, that is when they are worn by someone, not when they lie on a table and are looked at, the person who wears the eyeglasses does not see the eyeglasses, that is, has no awareness of them as object of knowledge, as is the case when they lie on a table and are looked at. When the eyeglasses are worn, they do not function as object known but as an instrument. Now, we have to resist the temptation to complete the preceding sentence by saying that when worn on the nose the eyeglasses function as instrument of knowledge , that is as instrument to acquire knowledge of the characteristics of the objects seen through the eyeglasses. But that is not what Polanyi would say. He would say that the eyeglasses function as instruments for action , that is, as instruments that guide the agent’s action. Such is the role played by TK, according to Polanyi, and therein lies the radical difference between our ordinary notion of knowledge and TK. However,  in order to fully grasp this difference we have to follow in some detail Polanyi’s analysis of TK in which the knowing subject of the classical epistemological tradition is replaced by the agent .  

Depuis le debut Polanyi avait placé l’agent au centre de son analyse de la CT, puisqu’il commencait par nous referer au cas ou “ the subject sait ou connait plus que ce qu’il peut dire.”

Cependant, ce n’est qu’avec la notion d’ “interiorisation,” introduite plus tard, ainsi qu’avec les exemples fournis par Polanyi pour illustrer cette notion, qu’il nous sera possible de parvenir a comprendre le deplacement du sujet connaissant de la tradition epistemologique classique par l’agent, impliqué par la CT.

In his initial analysis of the notion of TK, in The tacit Dimension ,  referring to the experiment with the shock-producing syllables, Polanyi’s emphasis was on the fact that the subject knows more than he can say. Let us remark in this connection, in the first place, that in affirming that the subject knows more than he can say Polanyi is by the same token taking his distances from the epistemological tradition that identifies knowledge with the verbal enunciation of the object’s characteristics. Secondly, that Polanyi shifts the emphasis from verbal enunciation of what is known to action. Indeed, if the subject cannot express in words what he knows, his knowledge can be manifested only through his actions. And, indeed, as we shall see, all of Polanyi’s examples of tacit knowledge refer to an agent actually engaged in action to achieve a desired goal, not to a contemplating consciousness that expresses its knowledge through statements about the characteristics of the object. Grasping this  shift from the consciousness of the knowing subject of classical epistemology to the agent’s actions is fundamental to understand Polanyi’s conception of knowledge. Knowledge, for Polanyi, is the deed of an agent, not of a contemplating consciousness. But this displacement of the contemplating subject of the classical epistemological tradition by the agent is insufficiently underscored, and perhaps even insufficiently grasped, by Polanyi himself. It will therefore by my task to bring this all-important point to the fore.

Let us begin with the example Polanyi employs to introduce the notion of tacit knowledge. That the subject in the experiment with the syllables knows which syllables produce shocks does not manifest itself  in words that express his awareness of those syllables, for the subject cannot say even to himself which are those syllables, but in his conduct of bracing himself for a shock when those syllables appear on the screen. Thus, in the sense of being aware of the shock-producing syllables and of being able to say which are the shock-producing syllables, the subject of the experiment does not know. Yet he knows the syllables in the sense of acting in response to those syllables by bracing himself for the shock to come. Action , therefore, rather than linguistic enunciation, is the way in which knowledge manifests itself.

As Polanyi’s analysis of tacit knowledge progresses, his emphasis shifts from the inability to express in words the characteristics of the object to what he calls “INTERIORIZATION.” Interiorizing something, Polanyi explains, is to identify ourselves with it by making it function as the immediate term of TK IN THE WAY WE USE OUR OWN BODY WHEN WE ACT, thereby transcending it toward something else.

To grasp the difference between verbal enunciation as expressive of the characteristics of an object known by the one who enunciates, on the one hand, and, on the other hand, as an instrument for action according to what is verbalized, let us reflect on what goes on in our daily experience with traffic lights. We are used to think of red and green lights as  signs. And we are not wrong. Where we go wrong is in thinking that signs are primarily signs OF something that can be enunciated, when in fact signs are meant to function as instruments FOR action. Red, in the case of a traffic light, is not necessarily a sign that a car is coming –something that can be enunciated–  but  a sign to perform the action of stopping; similarly, green is a sign to perform the action of moving ahead. And the same is the case with all signs. An arrow pointing in a certain direction on a road is a sign to perform the action of moving in the direction pointed at. Consequently, the correct comprehension of signs is manifested not in linguistic enunciations of what is signified, for there is nothing signified as to be known; rather it is manifested in the performance of the action signified. To appreciate this important difference, let us reflect. I just stated-enunciated that red signifies the action of stopping. However, what is stated is not what a red traffic light signifies, for it does not signify something to be known and enunciated ; it signifies of is a sign for the actual  performance of the action of stopping when the red light appears . Moreover, the enunciation is not even necessary. A child may learn to stop at red on a traffic light without being capable of enunciating the general ‘rule.’ And so can a dog that, even more obviously than a child, is radically incapable of making any linguistic enunciation.

This may help us to grasp the fundamental difference between Searle’s and Polanyi’s respective conceptions of language, that is, of t he way in which language functions. For Searle, language is a sign OF what is enunciated, because he thinks that the essential characteristic of language is a system of signs that say or express in words what is meant or signified by the speaker. Hence his privileging of enunciative speech understood as saying in words what it means, a well as his assimilation of speech acts having  perlocutionary force to speech acts having illocutionary force. Hence his anlaysis of the difference between language and games. Hence his denial that the American soldier performed a linguistic action even if he obtained the perlocutionary effect he intended, for the soldier did not say “I am an American soldier.” Hence Searle’s insistence on his principle of expressibility: anything that can be meant can (and, ideally, should) be expressed  in words that say what one means to be understood. And what one means may be either something to be understood, or an action to be performed. For Polanyi, by contrast, all speech acts, enunciative speech acts included, are meant to function, not as expressions of the characteristics of the object enunciated (for nothing to be understood is enunciated) but as signs FOR performing an action, as instruments that guide in the performance of the action meant. That is, they are meant to function as any other sign does – as SIGNS FOR acting in a certain way. The difference between enunciative acts and the others is that the enunciative ones do not specify the action meant but leave its determination to the linguistic agent according to his desires or needs and according to the circumstances, much as it happens in the case of perception. As to why this difference, the reason is that linguistic agents, as such, are capable of assuming responsibility for their actions and their consequences, since their actions are linguistic and words do not cause actions but actions conceived and meant by the subject.

Far from being what is signified by a red traffic light, the linguistic enunciation –if it occurs at all–  is only meaningful as a  practical direction for the agent in the performance of the conduct signified. This is obvious in the case of an order or of a request – that is, in the case of what Searle calls perlocutionary acts– , for an order is to be obeyed and a request is to be granted by performing the action requested, not by conceiving or enunciating or even assenting to the statement of the action to be obeyed or of the action requested.. But the same is the case the case with enunciations and statements. For, contrary to what Searle thinks, saying is not a sign OF what is said, and so it is not meant as a sign to conceive or imagine (as a copy of)  what is said, but a sign FOR linguistic action in response. Much as a music partition, the ultimate comprehension of which consists in, and is manifested in, the actual performance of the musical piece. ^(F8)

The stick, in the sense that rather than being perceived itself as an object in the environmnet,  it is assimilated to the agent’s body to the point that it ceases to count or function as an external object and functions as an extension of the agent’s body. Similarly, the blind man’s stick. Think of an agent in total darkness. His situation is comparable to that of a blind person. Instinctively, this person, in order to find his way, extends his arms so as to touch the objects that are on his way before hitting them, thus being able to avoid them, not by seeng them, but  by using the sensations in his hands as instruments toguide his steps.towards the place he wants to reach.

In connection with internalization Polanyi uses such terms as “inhabiting” or “indwelling.”

“The identification of tacit knowing with indwelling involves a shift of  emphasis in our conception of tacit knowing. We had envisaged tacit knowing in the first place as a way to know more than we can tell. We identified the two terms of tacit knowing, the proximal and the distal, and recognized the way we attend from the first to the second, thus achieving an integration of  particulars to a coherent entity to which we are attending. It now becomes a means of making certain things function as the proximal terms of tacit     knowing, so that instead of observing them in themselves, we may be aware of them in their bearing on the comprehensive entity which they constitute. It brings home to us that it is not by looking at things, but by dwelling in them, that we understand their joint meaning... By concentrating attention on his fingers, a pianist can temporarily paralyze his movement. ^(F9)

Now, “inhabiting” or “dwelling in” are different from “perceiving or “knowing,” which refer to the object perceived or known.  Polanyi’s notion of tacit knowledge would have gained much in precision if, instead of speaking of the proximal term of  knowledge he had specified that “inhabiting”, “indwelling,” etc., which  appear always in relation to interiorization,  refer to instruments used by an agent, not to objects known. Thus, the stick in the hands of the blind person in which the blind person “indwells” to find his way is not what the person knows but the instrument he uses to guide his steps to the place he wants to reach. Similarly, the eyeglasses worn by a myopic person. are not what the person who wears them  knows or sees or  but the instrument he uses to guide his action to achieve the purposes he desires.

But why does Polanyi use the word ‘interiorization’? Because a shift takes place, as it were, from something in the subject’s environment to the agent’s body. This shift may be illustrated by the example of eyeglasses. When a pair of glasses rest on a table and I look at them, they do not function as eyeglasses but as object of knowledge, as object known. I see the eyeglasses. When I wear the eyeglasses, however, I do not see the eyeglasses, I use them as instruments to find my way as an agent in the realization of my projects. The eyeglasses now function as an extension of my eyes, which I do not see but which I use as instruments for my action. My eyes only function as objet known when I look at them in the mirror, but they usually function as the instruments I use to act efficiently in the realization of my projects. Polanyi calls interiorization this shift of an object  from object known in the environment of the agent to its functioning as an extension of the agent’s own body when actually used as an instrument, to indicate that the object is, so to say, assimilated to the agent’s body. When worn, the eyeglasses are not known themselves; they are not seen, anymore than our eyes are seen when we use them as instruments to act in the world. ^(F10)

He ”inhabits” them, or “dwells in” them, as when we walk we use the sensations we experience on the plants of our feet at each step we make; we do not know them, rather we transcend them as “that from which” we make the next step. Thus, when  we walk we don’t pay attention to the sensations we experience on the plants of our feet every time they touch the ground; we “dwell” in those sensations to make the next step, we pass through them as “that from which” we make the next step, much as right now I pass through the sensations I experience at the tips of my fingers every time I type on the keyboard toward the word I want to write.

Polanyi had initially explained TK as knowing more than one can say. His conceptual shift to the “interiorization” involved in TK helps him to become more precise regarding the nature of  TK:

When playing the piano, the pianist is using it as an instrument to produce the desired music. In that case, the consciousness he has of the keys he touches is instrumental. If the word knowledge is to be employed in this context, it has to be described as “tacit,” for it is totally different from the consciousness of object with which the ordinary notion of knowledge (and saying-enunciating) is identified. So much so that, as just explained by Polanyi,  if, in the process of playing, that is, of having tacit knowledge of his fingers and of the keys they touch, the pianist turns his attention to his fingers and observes them, thereby turning them into objects of knowledge, automatically  his activity of playing becomes impaired. Similarly, if, while reading a text, we shift our attention from the meaning we are getting through reading to the letters that compose the text in order to know them as such, our reading becomes impaired. While reading we only have a tacit awareness of the written signs, for we are not attending to them as such but using them as instruments to get their meaning. If we turn our attention to the signs, we gain ordinary knowledge of their characteristics, but they cease to function as instruments that convey a meaning. The two forms of consciousness are not only different from each other but incompatible with each other, which is the reason why we cannot get the meaning if we try to know the letter themselves.  

To summarize, Polanyi calls “interiorization ” the special, non-objective, quasi oblique consciousness we have of what we we actually use as an instrument to achieve a purpose because we assimilate it to our own body, since the consciousness we have of our own body when we act is itself instrumental and the ultimate model of tacit knowledge:

“ But our awareness of our body for attending to things outside it suggests a wider generalization of the feeling we have of our body . Whenever we use certain things for attending from them to other things,  in the way in which we always use our own body, these things change their appearance. They appear to us now in terms of the entities to which we are attending from them, just as we feel our body in terms of the things outside to which we are attending from our body . In this sense we can say that when we make a thing function as the proximal term of tacit knowing, we incorporate it in our body or extend our body to include it –so that we come to dwell in it.” ^(F11)

The generalization of the awareness of our body at the time of action as the model of the interiorization involved in TK allows Polanyi to discover the mechanism of agency, which he finds it exemplified in a large number of particular cases. In the first place, in the case of the acquired bodily skills, such as playing the piano or typing, and in the case of those more complex skills such as driving a car.  These skills are fundamentally similar to the way a blind person ‘drives’ his own body with the  help of a stick. They are acquired as a result of along practice of interiorization through practice; thanks to repetition, the agent transforms the stick, the typewriter, the piano, the car into extensions of his own body by using them as he uses his own body. At the end of that long practice of interiorization the use of those objects becomes as natural to the agent as the use of his own body in, say, the action of walking. When a child learns to walk, he is guided by the sensations he experiences on the plants of his feet every time he makes a step, but the does not focus his attention on them, he does not observe his  sensations, they are only “that from which ” he makes the subsequent steps.

The typewriter, the piano, the car are examples of material instruments invented by human agents to multiply the efficiency of their agency and extend its reach in order to satisfy first their elementary needs and then the desires that emerge as the transformation of the surrounding world allows the emergence of such desires. However, in addition to the invention of material instruments is the invention of symbolic (that is, significant)  instruments , among which the most important are spoken and written language, because they make possible the invention of all other symbolic instruments . such as mathematical symbols, scientific theories, etc.

a child who has already acquired the skill of reading written language, is not thereby automatically capable of using a map to find his way in unfamiliar territory, much less of drawing a map to be used by others. The reason for this difficulty is that a map is not a picture of a territory, any more than a name is a picture of the object named. A map is a symbolic sign of a territory, but not of a territory to be contemplated or observed but of a territory to be walked through. In other words, not only is a map to be deciphered or ‘read,’ as all symbols are, but to be deciphered in relation to the action of moving through the territory to which the map refers, in order to reach a particular place within that territory. In other words, a map is a symbolic instrument to be used by an agent as an instrument to achieve his goal. That is why, if somebody claimed to understand a map but proved himself incapable of using it in practice, he wouldn’t know what he is saying. Indeed, since the map is an instrument for action, understanding it amounts to being able to manifest that understanding in one’s action. The mistake of this person would be comparable to the one Searle makes when he claims that a person who is given an order understands it if only he grasps objectively the meaning of the words, without realizing that an order is to be obeyed, not to be understood objectively. In short, the genuine understanding of a map is to be manifested in the conduct that the map is meant to render possible, that is, in the conduct of reaching the desired place within the territory to which the map refers, as the genuine understanding of a verbal joke is to be manifested in the affection of being actually amused by it.    

Polanyi’s view of the reading of maps of the kind we are referring to goes as follows. The agent who reads the map must “inhabit” the map as he inhabits his body when he acts to realize a project, for instance, when he is nailing a nail to hang a picture. In other words, he must make the figure on the function map not as an object to be looked at and observed in itself, but as “that from which” he must act in order to arrive at the desired place. In short,  TK is always practical, for it involves the use of an instrument an agent to achieve an intended goal, not a representation of object. ^(F12)

For his part, Polanyi argues that such an interpretation is wrong. In the following text,  Polanyi explains how theories function in the actual practice of science, that is, when the theoretical instrument is actually used rather than talked about:

“To rely on a theory for understanding nature is to interiorize it. For we are attending from the theory to things seen in its light, and we are aware  of the theory, while thus using it, in terms of the spectacle that it serves to explain. This is why mathematical theory can be learned only by practicing its application: its true knowledge lies in our ability to use it.” ^(F13)

This text, quoted  in isolation from its context, may be too dense and too cryptic to make us understand how, in Polanyi’s view, scientific theories function. What is clear is that Polanyi’s view contradicts the prevailing conception of science as a an exact, or at least an increasingly approximative, representational  view of reality. Thomas Kuhn, who on this fundamental point  repeatedly indicates his agreement with Polanyi, expressed it forcefully:

“A scientific theory is usually felt to be better than its predecessors not only in the sense that it is a better instrument for discovering and solving  puzzles but also because it is somehow a better representation of what nature is really like. One often hears that successive theories grow ever closer to, or approximate more and more closely to, the truth. Apparently generalizations like that refer not to the puzzle solutions and concrete predictions derived from a theory but rather to its ontology, to the match, that is, between the entities with which the theory populates nature and what is ‘really there.’ Perhaps there is some other way of salvaging the notion of ‘truth’ for application to whole theories, but this one will not do.... the notion of a match between the ontology of a theory and its ‘real’ counter part in nature now seems to me illusive in principle” ^(F14)

What Kuhn is pointing out here is that the statements in which scientific theories are expressed are not meant to function as representations of reality but as symbolic instruments to be used by agents to solve  problems that the surrounding world raises. In this regard, though, scientific statements constitute no exception. Indeed, if Polanyi is right, no statement –whether scientific or not– is  meant to function as a representation of its referent; rather, all statement are meant to function as  instruments for action, not as representations.

Searle and Polanyi on language

We are now well equipped to make sense of the differences between Polanyi and Searle concerning language.  At the end of chapter one I expressed my dissatisfaction with central points of Searle’s philosophy of language. Let us now contrast the two views, to find out both how they differ from each other, and whether that contrast throws any light on the points in which Searle’s position proved unsatisfactory.

Before coming to other points it might be helpful to explore the radical difference between Searle and Polanyi concerning what Searle calls the illocutionary force of speech acts. All speech acts, Searle claims, possess illocutionary force, whereas only some of them possess perlocutionary force. Why? Because in all saying, by the very nature of saying or speaking, the speaker necessarily  intends to communicate the knowledge of what he says, that is, of what he intends to communicate, and such knowledge constitutes the illocutionary effect. In other words, saying or speaking, amounts to the speaker’s expressing in words the knowledge of what he intends to communicate to his interlocutor, so that he cannot say or speak without charging what he says with illocutionary force, for that precisely is what saying amounts to. By contrast, not all saying involves the speaker’s intention of obtaining other effects than the knowledge of what he says, in addition to such knowledge. Hence, not all saying is necessarily charged with perlocutionary force.

.   .

The above indicates Searle’s conception of knowledge. By replicating in his own mind, thanks to his understanding of what the speaker says, what the speaker intends to communicate, the hearer gains the knowledge of what the speaker means or wants to communicate. Thus, according to Searle,  at least in the case of linguistic communication, knowledge consists in the correspondence between the object to be known and what the knower comes to have in mind thanks to his knowing activity. Such, needless to say, is the ordinary notion  of knowledge, which the Scholastics, following Aristotle, defined as adaequatio mentis ad rem : the conformation of the knower’s mind to the object. What Searle does is to extend to the field of linguistic communication the ordinary  notion of knowledge, that refers primarily to the conformity of the  knowing subject’s mind to the object in the perceptual world, that is, to “reality.” Now it is precisely regarding  this conception of knowledge that Polanyi parts ways with Searle. For Polanyi knowing is not a matter of becoming aware of the object to be known by acquiring a sort of reproduction of it that corresponds to the object, and that is true in the measure in which it corresponds to the object. Rather, knowledge is tacit because it is essentially practical, not theoretical. It does not aim at reproducing the object in the mind, in which case the knowledge of the object would be explicit rather than tacit. Knowledge consists, rather,  in a practical  disposition to ACT in the way appropriate to achieve the knower’s goal. Thus, in the case of the shock-producing syllables, the subject has no awareness of the individual characteristics of the syllables; for that very reason the subject cannot say or express in words which are those characteristics of which he has no visual awareness. What the subject acquires, thanks to his past painful experience of being shocked, is not an image of the syllables, but the practical disposition to avoid the shock, which manifests itself in his bracing himself for the shock when the shock-producing syllables appear again on the screen, in order to protect himself as much as possible from the shock.

Whose conception of the relationship of knowledge to action is right: Searle’s or Polanayi’s?

Regarding those acts in which a perlocutionary effect is intended by the speaker , I have already shown how unpersuasive I find Searle’s view that in every act of communication the speaker intends to produce the knowledge of his communicative intentions –that is, the illocutionary effect–  and that this knowledge suffices to insure the success of the communicative act. For this means, concretely, that when I give you an order, what I intend primarily is that you understand and thereby be informed or acquire factual knowledge that I give you an order, not that you obey my order. Searle is so convinced that in the case of an order what the speaker intends is that his order be understood rather than obeyed that he considers the communicative exchange successful in the case in which the order is not obeyed as long as the order is understood by the hearer.

Why do I think that Searle is wrong on this point? Because this aspect of Searle’s characterization of linguistic exchanges is  at odds with the practice of all competent linguistic agents. It is true that I cannot disobey an order without understanding it and thereby knowing it. But what is in contention is what constitutes the appropriate understanding of an order and, in general, of all those linguistic acts that possess perlocutionary force, such as threats, warnings, promises, demands, etc. According to Searle, the primary intention of the speaker in all of the them is to inform the hearer of what he means, and only secondarily and accidentally to achieve the perlocutionary effect typical of the particular perlocutionary act, such as obeying it in the case of an order, expecting the fulfillment of the promise in the case of promising, etc.

But the linguistic facts contradict Searle’s opinion. Indeed, Searle’s opinion does not do justice to the practice of linguistic agents concerning the use of the indicative mood, on the one hand, and of the subjunctive and of the imperative moods, on the other. If  Searle were right, there would be no difference in actual use between the grammatical imperative, used in the case of orders, and the grammatical indicative, properly used only when one wants to inform of a fact. Furthermore, the imperative mood  would be totally superfluous,  for imperatives should be understood as  indicatives. Indeed,   if Searle were right, giving an order (in the imperative) would be exactly equivalent to saying or enunciating (in the indicative) that one gives an order . But such is not the case. As I have already argued, when I give an order, I do not say – in the sense of enunciating– something to be merely understood, to wit: that I give an order; rather, I say the order I give in the sense of giving an order to be obeyed. When I say something in the sense of enunciating, I refer my hearer to what I enunciate as something to be understood, that is, I intend to produce in my interlocutor the knowledge in which, according to Searle, the illocutionary effect consists. Therefore, if in giving an order I were enunciating that I give an order and the order I give, I should be satisfied if the addressee responded  “I understand your order.” But that is not the case. When I give an order –and this is why I use the imperative, not the indicative–  I expect and intend my order to be obeyed, which is the appropriate ‘understanding’ of an order by a competent linguistic agent.

Moreover, Searle’s opinion is also at odds with the practice of linguistic agents even in the case of statements. For, as I have already argued, contrary to what Searle thinks, in a real-life situations, by contrast to the artificial classrooom situation in which people are learning a second language, linguistic agents do not consider their linguistic action successful if a purely theoretical  understanding of what is said obtains; success, for linguistic agents, is identical with linguistic action. Cf. GEOMETRY AS APPLICATIVE.           

Why does Searle identify saying with enunciating?  Because his linguistic characterizations and, hence, his entire conception of language,  is based not on his own experience as a linguistic agent who actually participates in communicative interactions, but as a curious philosopher who overhears the words said by those who participate in communicative interactions, or who reflects after the fact on the words said by himself and others when he participated in communicative interactions. I underline “the words said,” to indicate that Searle’s characterization of language, in spite of his disclaimers, focuses on the words said, independently of the conduct and the intentions of those who say or hear them as actual participants in linguistic exchanges. This amounts to identifying linguistic meaning with saying in the sense of enunciating something, and, consequently, it amounts to considering the conduct of the linguistic agents as accidental to linguistic meaning, rather than integral to, or constitutive of, linguistic meaning. Clearly, however, this is not the kind of interiorization through agency through which children learn to play baseball, without being able to enunciate the rules they supposedly follow.

Thus Searle excludes from linguistic meaning anything that in a linguistic exchange is not  words in which something is said or enunciated as to be known. Such is the case with  the actions and affections intended by the speaker in the communicative exchange, which consequently are excluded by Searle from being constitutive elements of linguistic meaning. That, indeed, was the reason why, in his example of the American soldier captured by the Italians , Searle explicitly denied that the action by which the American tricked the Italians into believing that he was a German soldier by uttering the sounds “ Ich komme von dem Land wo die Zitronen  blühen ” was a linguistic action, since the words expressed did not enunciated the action intended.

The exclusion from linguistic meaning of whatever is not explicitly said in words is reflected in the separation Searle postulates between the illocutionary and the perlocutionary effects, as well as in the secondary and merely accidental role he attributes to the latter. Searle himself , in explaining his principle of expressibility that  “whatever can be meant can be said,” implicitly acknowledges that whatever is not said in words cannot be meant, for he excludes from meaning the perlocutionary effects. ^(F15)

In short, because he identifies linguistic meaning with what is expressed in words that supposedly say or enunciate something to be understood or known, Searle automatically excludes from linguistic meaning those no less linguistic effects that do not consist in words that express something to be understood, namely, the perlocutionary effects. No wonder, then, that  the way in which language functions in the artificial context of a course of a language for foreigners, in which only understanding  what is said counts as linguistic, to the systematic exclusion of any of the affections or actions intended by the speaker , ends up appeariong as the model linguistic situation for Searle. Indeed it corresponds to Searle’s defective philosophy of language.

Now, to understand the differences between Searle and Polanyi, we have to realize that Searle’s linguistic philosophy amounts to an application to the field of language of the Platonic-Aristotelian epistemology inherited by the Western philosophical tradition. In that tradition,  knowledge consists, on the subject’s part,  in the representational conformity of his mind to reality, and, on the object’s part, in the ontological correspondence or conformity to what is in the subject’s mind. By contrast,  Polanyi’s conception of tacit knowledge constitutes an alternative to that tradition, for tacit knowledge is to be used instrumentally by the agent to achieve a desired goal, not a representation of what is the case.

As we shall now see, Polanyi does not contend that the Searlean perlocutionary effects as Searle conceives them, that is, as separated from the illocutionary effect, are a constitutive element of linguistic meaning, for the Serlean conception of the illocutionary element as different from the perlocutionary presupposes the legitimacy of his conceptual distinction between them. Polanyi contends, rather, that this Serlean distinction reflects the separation between the knowing subject and the agent that is inherent to the epistemology inherited from Aristotle and Plato that begins by reflecting on knowledge as different from and separable form action. The Searlean distinction between illocutionary and perlocutionary effects is possible only on the wrong assumption , common to Aristotle and Plato, that knowledge and action stand to each other in the relationship presupposed in the epistemology inherited from Plato and Aristotle by the Western philosophical tradition .

Quant a Polanyi, il dirait que la conduite des agents qui participent dans les echanges lingusitiques font partie  constituante, autant que les mots, de la signfication linguistique, de laquelle par consequent les effets perlocutionairess sont inseparables.

Le type de connaissance qui, d’apres Searle, resulte chez l’interlocuteur par la comprehension de ce qui lui est dit par le locuteur est une information purement factuelle sur les intentions communicatives du locuteur. Elle constitue une toute autre conception du langage que celle presupposée par Searle, puisque la distinction meme entre effets illocutionnaires et effets perlocutionnaires perd sa raison d’etre dans la perspective de Polanyi, pour la simple raison que dans cette derniere il n’y a pas de distinction entre sujet connaissant et agent. C’est l’agent lui-meme qui connait, mais qui connait tacitement, non objectivement, PARCE QUE SA CONNAISSANECE EST GUIDEE PAR LE DESIR DU BUT. En d’autres mots, pas de desir D’UN BUT PARTICULIER, pas de connaissance.

Dans la conception de Searle, par contre, la comprehension qui resulte dans la connaissance de ce que l’interlocuteur veut dire est le fait d’un sujet connaissant qui n’est pas lui-meme agent expose a l’action du monde environnant. De là la separation etablie par Searle entre, d’une part,  la connaissance, qui est le seul effet illocutionnaire et qui est independant de l’action et, d’autre part,  l’action, qui fait partie des effets perlocutionnaires est est subordonnée a la connaissance . Or, etant donne que, d’apres Searle, la realisation des effets perlocutionnaires n’est pas essentielle pour la reussite de l’action linguistique en tant que telle, il s’en suit que dans la conception de Searle il n’y a pas de place pour l’agent et la veritable agence.

Ce que je viens de dire peut sembler surprenant, puisque l’ecole linguistique des “s peech acts ” ou de l’action linguistique, a laquelle appartient Searle, est censée se distinguer precisement parce qu’elle voit dans le fonctionnement du langage une forme sui generis d’action, par contraste avec la connaissance. Cependant, quoi qu’il soit des autres membres de cette ecole, en separant le composant illocutionnaire des composants perlocutionnaires, en faisant de l’efffet illocutionnaire qui consiste dans la connaissance le seul element essentiel pour la reussite de l’action linguistique en tant que telle, et en faisant l’action dependre de la connaissance qui constitute l’effet illocutionnaire comme condition sine qua non pour que l’action ait lieu, Searle reduit effectivement l’action linguistique a la connaisance objective, a la connaissance comme pure vision d’objet . C’est cette vision d’objet purement theorique qui constitute l’effet illocutionnaire, laquelle suffit pour la reussite de l’action linguistique et sans laquelle les actions qui constituent les effets perlocutionnaires (par example, l’action d’obeir)  ne peuvent avoir lieu, tandis que la vision d’objet ne depend nullement des actions. Par contre, dans la conception de Polanyi, la vision d’objet ou connaissance objective disparait tout a fait du champ du fonctionnement du langage. Elle est substituteee par la CT, qui n’est pas connaisance objective mais instrumentale et donc en rapport intrinseque avec l’action. C’est pour cette raison que j’ai insiste plusieurs fois sur le fait que ce qui caracterise la conception de Polanayi, c’est le deplacement du sujet connaissant de la tradition epistemologique clasique par l’agent.

Que la conception Polanyienne elimine tout a fait la connaissance objective du champ du fonctionnement du langage je crois l’avoir deja etabli pour le cas des effets perlocutionnaires. J’en viens maintenant au cas de l’effet illocutionnaire . Dans la communication linguistique, ce cas est representé concretement par les assertions, qui expriment les jugements intellectuels ou perceptuels du locuteur. Pour ma part, ce qui m’interesse exclusivement  ici c’est les assertions sur des faits particuliers du monde environnant du locuteur et de l’interlocuteur, c’est a dire du monde qu’ils percoivent et dans lesquels eux-memes vivent et agissent, ainsi que tout le monde.

Aristote s’occupe des assertions an tant que faits linguistiques dans son traité sur l’interpretation, Ρερι  Ηρμενειας,.MAIS LA IL NE FAIT QU’APPLIQUER AU CHAMPS DE  LA COMMUNICATION LINGUISTIQUE CE QU’IL AVAIT DEJA DIT SUR LES ASSERTIONS EN TANT QUE JUGEMENTS, C’EST A DIRE, EN TANT QUE FAITS EPISTEMOLOGIQUES SUSCEPTIBLES DE VALIDITE GNOSEOLOGIQUE DE PAR LEUR REFERENCE A LA REALITE .D’apres lui, les assertions, aussi bien affirmatives que negatives, expriment LINGUISTIQUEMENT des jugements en tant qu’actes gnoseologiques du locuteur. Cependant, A LA DIFFERENCE DE CE QUE JE FAIS ICI, Aristote ne se limite pas a etudier les assertions sur des faits perceptuels particuliers, c’est-a-dire, sur des etats particuliers du monde environnnat, parce que son projet epistemologique personnel de justifier

la verite des jugements “scientifiques” l’obligeat a s’occuper de preference des jugements intellectuels qui trouvent leur expressions dans des assertions universelles plutot que dans des jugments perceptuels sur des faits particuliers. En tout cas, et c’est ce qui compte pour nous, ce qui est commun aux jugements de deux types et aux assertions linguistiques aussi bien afirmatives que negatives qui les expriment, c’est, d’apres Aristote,  que eux et elles referent a ce qui est le cas en realite. Par la meme occasion, que leur referent soit ou bien un fait perceptuel particulier, ou bien  la “nature’ universelle d’une ou plusieurs realites particulieres (ce qui est le cas dans les jugements “scientifiques), les assertions sont, ainsi qu”Aristote le souligne fortement, les seules formes linguistiques qui affirment leur propre verite et, par consequent, les seules formes linguistiques qui soient susceptibles d’etre vraies ou faussess. En effet, comme le remarque Aristote,  une requete faite, un ordre donné, un souhait exprimé, etc., ne peuvent etre ni vrais ni faux. D’où la place privilegiée que les assertions occupent aussi bien dans l’oeuvre d’Aristote qu’en general dans toutes les oeuvres du genre philosophique inspiré par Aristote. Ce n’est pas par hasard que  la Critique de la raison pure de Kant est entierement devouée a l’etude des jugements et leur verité.    

D’apres Aristote, la verite des assertions aussi bien affirmatives que negatives et des jugements exprimés par elles depend de leur conformite a ce qui est le cas en realite. En d’autres termes, a propos des jugements et des assertions linguistiques, Aristote soutient la theorie de la connaissance objective. En fait il est le philosophe qui, le premier, en exprimant sa t heorie sur le judgment, articula cette theorie epistemologique telle qu’elle allait devenir pour ainsi dire canonique dans la tradition philosophique occidentale.

Pour mon but, les details de la theorie epistemologique d’Aristote importent peu. Il est evident, par example, que l’idée si generale que j’en viens de donner omet de dire qu’Aristote distingue entre, d’une part, les jugements theoriques, qui ont a voir avec la vision correcte de ce qui est le cas, independamment de toute action, et auxquels s’applique directement la notion de connaissance objective, et, d’autre part, les jugements pratiques qui, eux, ont a voir avec la reponse appropriée que l’agent doit donner a ce qui est le cas et qui incluent le DESIR. Mais en me referant a Aristote, mon but n’etait pas de faire une etude de son epistemologie ou de sa theorie du fonctionnement du langage. Je voulais seulement suggerer la raison pour laquelle Searle adopte comme allant de soi,  et applique sans hesitation dans le terrain de la communication linguistique, l’idee de connaissance comme connaissance objective ou representation d’objet. La raison en est simple: Searle appartient a la tradition philosophique initiee par Platon et continuee et developee par Aristote.

Revenons a Searle. Dans mon apercu de ses vues au chapitre precedent, je n’eus pas a m’occuper de la facon dont il traite le rapport de verite ou faussete des assertions linguistiques, car il n’adresse pas ce sujet sous la rubrique de la communication entre locuteur et interlocuteur mais sous celle des assertions comme l’ acte linguistique particulier qui refere au monde environnant et qui comporte l’afirmation de sa propre verite. Cependant, quand il en vient aux assertions comme acte linguistique particulier, il les concoit a la maniere d’Aristote comme expressions de d’une connasissance objective possedée par le locuteur, comme expressions d’une vision d’objet qui est jugée etre vraie par le locuteur et qui l’est en fait, ou qui ne l’est pas, dans la mesure ou elle est conforme, ou ne l’est pas, a ce qui est le cas en realite. Bref, sous ce rapport, Searle n’est pas un disciple de Kant mais d’Aristote, pour lequel la realite extramentale ne faisait pas de doute. Par consequent, Aristote concevait la verite des jugements et de leurs expressions linguistiques par rapport a la realite extramentale, et par un rapport de subordination a elle, puisque c’est la connaissance qui doit se conformer a la  realité. et non pas la realité a la connaissance. La seule difference, d’ailleurs secondaire, entre Aristote et Searle, est que ce dernier separe la question de la conformité de la connaissance que l’interlocuteur acquiert des intentions communicatives du locuteur avec ces intentions, de la question de la conformité  des assertions perceptuelles a la realite, tandis qu’Aristote, qui n’a  pas de theorie explicite de la communication linguistique, ne distingue pas les deux questions. Pour Searle, par contre, une chose est la communication liguistique entre locuteur et interlocuteur; une toute autre chose est le cas particulier de la verite out faussete, disons “ontologique,” des seuls actes linguistiques qui se proclament porteurs de verite de par leur reference a la realite, a savoir, les assertions de ce qui est le cas ou ne l’est pas en realite. La communication en tant que telle comprend tous les actes linguistiques, aussi bien ceux dans lesquels l’intention du locuteur est exclusivement de communiquer une connaissance, que ceux par le moyen desquels, en outre d’une connaissance de ce qu’il veut dire le locuteur veut obtenir un effet perlocutionnaire. Dans le premier cas, les assertions tombent sous la rubrique de la communication, comme un acte linguistique parmi tous les autres. Dans le second cas les assertions sont etudiées comme le seul acte linguistique susceptible d’etre vrai ou faux. Or puisqu’Aristote ne traite pas de la communication en tant que telle, il n’a aucune raison de distinguer les deux questions ni de les traiter separement.

Il est grand temps de porter notre attention sur les actes linguistiques a intention exclusivement illocutionnaire, dont je ne me suis pas encore occupé. Demandons-nous donc si, au moins dans leur cas, Searle a raison de les concevoir a la maniere Aristotelienne comme expressions de la vision de ce qui est le cas , possedée par le locuteur en tant que sujet connaissant, c’est-a-dire, d’interpreter les assertions comme porteuses de connaissance objective. Quant a Polanyi, il pense qu’il s’agit , non pas de connaissance objective opu vision d’objet, mais de CT, c.a.d., d’action adaptee pour realiser un but desire. Autrement dit, tous les speech acts, les assertions inclues, ne doivent pas etre comprises comme des representations, même des representations symboliques, de ce a quoi elles referent; elles doivent etre employées comme instruments pour l’action, comme ce a partir duquel le sujet doit agir. Pour departager entre ces deux conceptions si differentes, considerons quelques examples.

Imaginez que letelephone sonne. Je reponds et, me tournant vers mon ami qui se trouve dans la meme piece, je lui dis:  “Ta soeur est au telephone.” Voila une assertion toute simple. Or, s’il s’agit de transmission de connaissance objective, ainsi que Searle l’entend,c’est-a-dire, s’il s’agit de communiquer a mon ami ma vision objective de ce qui est le cas, vision qu’il acquerra par la comprehension de ce que je dis etre le cas, alors une reponse parfaitement adequate de la part de mon ami serait de me dire: “J’ai compris que ma soeur est au telephone” et de rester la ou il est sans rien faire d’autre. Pour ma part, en tant qu’agent linguistique competent, je m’attend a une toute autre reponse et je serais interloqué si la reponse de mon ami etait celle que je viens de decrire. Je m’attends, en effet  (et je suis persuade que, contrairement a Searle, n’importe quel agent linguistique competent en langue francaise me donnerait la raison), je m’attends a ce que mon ami prenne le telephone et parle avec sa soeur. Pour le dire autrement, je m’attends a ce que mon ami fasse fonctionner mon assertion, non comme porteuse de la connaissance objective de ce qui est le cas, mais comme instrument pour son action, comme CT “a partir de laquelle’ il doit agir et prendre le telephone. De meme si je dis a mon ami qui cherche le marteau: “Le marteau est dans la salle a manger,” je m’attends a ce qu’il aille dans la salle a manger prendre le marteau, non pas a ce qu’il acquiere une vision purement theorique, c.a.d. sans rapport intrinseque avec l’action, du marteau dans la salle a manger, ainsi que Searle le pense (ou devrait le penser s’il croit vraiement a sa theorie linguistique). De meme, pour terminer, dans le cas de n’importe quelle assertion linguistique d’un etat concret particulier du monde environnant.

En fait, j’avais deja plus haut fait la critique de la position de Searle au sujet de la connaissance objective dans la communication linguistique, en disant que son modele du fonctionnement du langage est la maniere bâtarde dont le langage fonctionne dans le cadre artificel d’un cours de langue pour des etrangers. La seule difference est que dans le chapitre precedent je me referait au chapitre de son essaai où Searle parle de tous les actes linguistiques sous la rubrique de la communication, et ici  je me refere au chapitre oul il etudie lecas particulier des assertions dont le referent est ce qui est le cas en realite. En tout cas, pour mon propos, cette difference n’est pas significative, puisque Searle concoit les deux cas comme examples de connaissance objective ou connaissance d’objet, tandis que je soutiens avec Polanyi que ce que nous appelons ordinairement “connaissance” fonctionne comme instrument pour l’agent , non comme representation d’objet pour le sujet connaissant de la tradition epistemologique classique.

Quant a la fertilite de la substitution du sujet connaissant par l’agent dans la conception polanyienne de la CT, nous aurons l’occasion de l’apprecier au prochain chapitre dans son application dans le terrain de la science . Effectivement, ainsi que la conception polanyienne fournit une alternative a la facon ordinaire de comprendre le fonctionnement du langage, representée par la theorie de Searle, ainsi elle fournit egalement une alternative a la facon ordinaire de concevoir le fonctionnement de la science, representée par la conception classique de la science comme vision correcte ou du moins de plus en plus approximative de la  nature de la realité.
