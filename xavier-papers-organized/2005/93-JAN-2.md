\wp\chantill\93-jan-2 January 26, 1993

Authorial Voice in Philosophy

Kant writes from the first person's perspective, that is, about (and from?) his own experience as a knower, and induces us to place ourselves in the first person's perspective. More clearly so does Descartes, because he explicitly writes in the first person and on the basis of and referring to the experience he has privileged access too because it is his own.

Skinner, by contrast, writes in the third person. What does this mean? It means that Skinner is speaking as if he had no privileged access to his own experience and is in no way using or referring to his own experience, but rather is referring to himself as seen by another.

What interests me here is the exposed subject's perspective by contrast to the pure contemplator's perspective. Aristotle in the Ethics and Kant in Religion within the Limits of Reason think and speak from the former perspective. Kant in the Critique of Pure Reason and Descartes in the Meditations think and speak from this perspective. Aristotle in the Metaphysics and in the Posterior Analytics as well as Aquinas in at least certain portions of the Summa are problemmatic to me at this point.

All the above, however, though with different presuppositions, think and talk in the reflective mode--not as actually exposed--and for purposes of knowing or communicating knowledge. By contrast, the participants in deliberative rhethoric--Is Carthago delenda?--speak in the decisional mode.

Beginning again

There are two fundamental modes: curiosity (whose purpose is to know) and concern (whose purpose is to change the state of the world to which a subject is exposed).

The concerned subject is both active (wants and can) and passive (wants and cannot). All decision-action is from a given "comprehended" world within which the agent occupies a given situation of exposure (of which he is correctly or incorrectly aware).

The curious subject has no situation of exposure (abstracts from it) and no practical project (no state of the world to be brought about). By the same token the subject is not surrounded by a world within which s-he is exposed but simply fronted

with an object discerned by attention for the purpose of knowing

(understanding) it. If this subject reflects on herself, she can

only find herself as curious subject, as subject experiencing object in the mode of curiosity.
