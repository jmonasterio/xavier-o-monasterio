Sharing Stories

A culture is a very complex set of customary expectations on people, expectations which are for the most part unwritten

and which the members of the culture absorb automatically, as if by osmosis, as they grow up. These expectations are for the most part unconscious; they become conscious only when frustrated.

To the members of the culture the expectatios of their culture appear "natural," not only in the sense of 'this is the way it is,' but 'this is the way it ought to be.' As a result, deviants are not only considered as different, but disapproved of, ridiculed, and--in matters of importance--shunned and ostracized.

Within a culture there are subcultures , that is, minoritary cultural groups that share in the main culture but are significantly distinct in their customs in certain aspects. Examples, Catholics, Blacks, Latinos.

, Language is only the most visible part of this set: people are expected to speak English in USA, French in France, Spanish in Mexico. - I came to the USA when I was forty. I could read English very well, but I had never spoken it. The cultural shock in this case is brutal. Functioning as a child when one is in fact an adult.

Whatever is different (be it color of the skin, pronounciation of the language, religion, eating habits, physical traits) appears unnatural and to be suppressed. One of the benefits of having the chance to live in different cultures is to learn, if one is capable of learning, that one's culture is not more natural than, or automatically superior to, any other culture; that cultural differences do not necessarily constitute inferiorities ( there was a time when foreign immigrants did not dare to teach their children the language of their mother country) , and that sometimes one has much to learn from those who are culturally different.

This healthy relativization of one's culture as one way of being human among others does not come to us sponataneously. Observe how cruel children often are to those who are different.

It is something that one has to learn, to be taught, and not only in words but in actions.

Problems

- Subculture within a culture.

- The relativization of one's culture may lead to a loss of identity, if one is unable to distinguish the more important from the less important in one's culture of subuculture.

- Cultures and subcultures tend to consecrate as natural the beliefs and the values of the most powerful members of the culture and to suppress more ruthlessly the beliefs and the values of the groups that are weakest (be they weakest in economic terms, in terms of numbers of people who constitute a subculture, or in other terms).
