2006-January 16-Lindbeck

LINDBECK AND AQUINAS

“Western reading of the Bible in the days before the rise of historical criticism in the eighteenth century was usually strongly realistic, i.e., at once literal and historical, and not only doctrinal and edifying. The words and sentences meant what they SAID, and because they did so they accurately DESCRIBED   real EVENTS and real TRUTHS that were rightly put only in those terms and no others .” (First sentence of the Introduction of Hans W. Frei, The Eclipse of Biblical Narrative , New Haven and London, University Press, 1974,  p. 1.) N.B. Frei is here speaking of biblical narratives for they are strongly realistic, i.e. at once literal and historical.  However, he mistakes those narratives for statements that describe real events and real truths. He confuses the narrative-poetic genre, forgetting that “the poet affirmeth nothing,” with the historiographical genre, whose very point is to affirm as true what was really the case at a certain time in  the past.

In his influential article “Aquinas as a Post-liberal Theologian” ^(F1)  Bruce Marshall argued that Aquinas was in agreement with Lindbeck’s coherentist notion of the justification of religious knowledge. According to Lindbeck, a religious belief is justified as being true or putting the mind in correspondence with the real if it coheres with the total relevant context of linguistic utterances – linguistic coherence–   (p. 302), as  well  as with the practices that the religion sees as appropriate to that kind of belief and utterance – practical coherence. (Notice the ominous identification of religious belief or knowledge or faith with linguistic enunciation). It is on this basis that Lindbeck rejects the truth of the utterance “Christus est Dominus” when it is used to authorize cleaving the skull of the infidel.

To a number of Catholic Aristotelian Thomists, such as Fr. Colman O’Neill,  who were used to believe in the ontological truth of the statement about Christ, the denial of the truth of the proposition “Christus est Dominus”  sounded heretical. It was in this context that Marshall came to Lindbeck’s defense  by saying that Lindbeck’s position was substantially the same as Aquinas’s. Marshall’s argument was based on the distinction between truth and justification. He showed that, contrary to Marshall’s assumption, Aquinas himself, when it came to the justification of religious propositions, was coherentist.

In his response, which appeared immediately after Marshall’s article in the

same issue of The Thomist , Lindbeck declared himself in agreement with Marshall. This move protected  Lindbeck from the ire of those Thomists, if any, who were convinced by Marshall’s  argument that Aquinas was a coherentist. By the same token, because of his supposedly Thomistic position, Lindbeck was restituted to the home of Catholic orthodoxy, which he himself recognized as his religious home.

Here I will argue, however, that Marshall’s interpretation of Aquinas as a coherentist was wrong, for Aquinas was a representationalist, not a coherentist. That in the first place. Secondly,  I will argue that Aquinas’ actual epistemological representationalist, non-coherentist position, was wrong. Consequently, by declaring himself in agreement with Aquinas Lindbeck obscured considerably his own post-modernistic position and its great merits.

Lindbeck rightly identified his position as that of someone who “accepts the contemporary commonplace that sense experience is heavily dependent on linguistic and non-linguistic practices which are in part acquired through variable forms of acculturation and not simply through the actualization of genetically encoded propensities. ^(F2)”     

To pinpoint the radical difference between Aquinas and Lindbeck: it lies in the way each of them conceives the enunciative function of language. Aquinas thinks that a linguistic enunciation represents what is enunciated, so that its truth or capacity to put the mind in contact with reality depends on the accuracy with which the enunciation represents the real object. For his part, Lindbeck thinks that a linguistic enunciation puts the mind in contact with reality by disposing the subject to conform his linguistic conduct to the pertinent stated doctrines of the Catholic Church, as well as by disposing the subject to act in conformity with the pertinent ethical practices of the Catholic Church. This is to say that religious doctrines function not as an accurate representation of anything but as rules for linguistic and practical conduct.

Aquinas’s representational view of linguistic enunciation is borrowed explicitly from Aristotle’s Peri hermeneias or treatise on the interpretation of language, on which Aquinas wrote a very detailed commentary. What distinguishes enunciation from all other speech acts, according to Aristotle and to Aquinas in his commentary on Aristotle’s Peri Hermeneias , is that enunciative speech is the only speech act susceptible of being true or false because it means by saying or enunciating what is the case in reality. Thus a true statement  –and, in Aquinas’ Aristotelian view, statements express judgments, not perceptual experiences– possesses ontological truth by saying  what is the case in reality, that is, by putting the mind in contact with the real, by making evident to the mind not only what is actually the case but what the mind grasps when it judges prompted by sufficient reason that something is actually the case.

Evidence of what is judged and verbally affirmed  is all-important here, given that Aquinas will distinguish faith or religious knowledge from other knowledge by the presence of evidence in the case of knowledge and by its absence in the case of faith. In Aquinas’s eyes, faith is a very special kind of knowledge in that it is more certain than all other knowledge because it rests on God’s authority. In this respect, faith is superior to all other knowledge. However, it is inferior to scientific non-religious knowledge because of its lack of evidence. Lack of evidence of what? Not of what is enunciated, for the subject intends to affirm what he or she affirms, but of the ontological  correspondence of what is judged by the mind with what is actually the case. In the case of faith, though, Aquinas would claim,  the ontological correspondence or truth of what is judged with what is the case in reality  is certain but is not evident .

Suppose, however, with Lindbeck, that an enunciation doesn’t mean as a description of what is the case, but as a rule for speaking and acting at the appropriate moment (in the future). In this case, and in the area of religious knowledge, truth cannot be a matter of ontological correspondence, for there is no view involved; it has to be a matter of coherence with the enunciative language authorized by the Church in matters of faith, and with the way of life authorized by the Church in matters of conduct. Now, if Marshall is right about Aquinas’s linguistic postmodernism, this kind of coherentism constitutes Aquinas’s position.

Marshall’s argument goes as follows. The merit of faith lies in the willingness to believe, that is, to assent in the absence of evidence, under the will’s motion, to what is known in faith. In this case, though, the absence of evidence of what is believed is essential, for that is what distinguishes faith from plain knowledge. But doesn’t Aquinas grant that certain truths of faith, such as the preambles and in particular God’s existence, are accessible to us without God’s revelation? And doesn’t he affirm at the same time that what is believed and what is naturally known are mutually incompatible, because belief is possible only in the absence of evidence, and knowledge implies evidence? So God’s existence cannot be both believed and known . To solve this problem Marshall rightly  distinguishes with Aquinas between knowledge before faith and after faith. Before the acceptance of something through faith, knowledge of something revealed  –say, God’s existence– is compatible with faith. After its acceptance on the basis of evidence, it is not possible anymore to believe it on the basis of God’s authority, but this does not destroy the merit of faith if the person would be disposed to accept it even if it were not evident, resting only on God’s authority. So faith is not always incompatible with knowledge; actually, it is incompatible with faith only under certain temporal conditions and certain dispositions of the subject.  

MY MAIN PROBLEM: Aquinas’s representational conception of enunciative language presupposes the possibility of ontological truth. Lindbeck’s coherentist conception of enunciative language is an irreducibly different conception of the way enunciative language functions, which is not by representing accurately what is the case ontologically, but by disposing the subject to act in the particular way signaled by the encountered object. Since Lindbeck’s conception does not involve representation, then ontological truth, which consists in the correspondence between what is judged and what is actually the case is not only impossible but inconceivable. Yet Lindbeck fails to realize this, for he continues to accept the possibility of ontological truth in the case of God’s knowledge, as assumed by Aquinas . Here is Lindbeck’s view:

        The issue turns on whether a classical ‘correspondence‘ theory of truth can be combined with, to employ O’Neill’s terminology, the use of ‘coherentist’ and ‘pragmatist’ epistemological criteria in justifying beliefs. I am not sure that this is possible for those who exclude any reference to an ideal observer or knower (whether real or hypothetical) when defining truth, but for any theist for whom God is prima veritas , as he was for Aquinas, the answer is clearly in the affirmative. In God, and only in God, are knowledge and reality, not only in correspondence, but directly known to correspond. Only in him do truth and knowledge of truth, alethiology and epistemology, coincide. In human knowledge in via , in contrast, there is always a gap. Our beliefs may correspond to reality, but we are justified in holding that they do so, not by directly seeing the correspondence, but by some other means. That those other means might be in part or whole  be coherentist or pragmatist cannot be excluded a priori. ^(F3)  

Notice what Lindbeck is implicitly conceding in the last lines of the quotation, to wit: that the coherentist or pragmatist conception of truth is compatible with the representational conception, for ontological truth obtains in the case of God’s knowledge as Prima Veritas . Moreover, Lindbeck concedes that ontological truth may also obtain even in our own case; only we cannot know whether our beliefs actually correspond to reality, for we don’t see that. Hence the necessity we have of using other means to be sure that the correspondence obtains – be those means coherentist or other.* [* Aristotle didn’t see the need for any means because, unlike Kant, he trusted nature in the case of knowledge, when –in the case of judgment, which is reflective, not of perception which is not reflective– the mind grasps sufficient reason for affirming].

Thus Lindbeck implicitly concedes that his pragmatist conception of linguistic truth is compatible with Aquinas representational conception of linguistic truth. I reject this. In the pragmatist conception, statements are meant to terminate in appropriate action, not in vision. It is not a matter of seeing and assenting to what is the case but of responding to what is signaled by the reality encountered . Had Lindbeck grasped the irreducible difference between his pragmatist conception of language and Aquinas’s representationalist conception, he would have rejected  Marshall’s assimilation of his pragmatist position to Aquinas’s representational position. The purpose of enunciations in the appropriate perceptual circumstances is to function as rules for conduct, not to represent what is the case
