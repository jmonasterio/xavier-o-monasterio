PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE IN ARISTOTLE

“What one acquires here is not a technique; one learns correct judgements. There are also rules, but they do not form a system, and only experienced people can apply them. Unlike calculating rules. - What is most difficult here is to put this indefiniteness, correctly and unfalsified, into words” (Ludwig Wittgenstein, Philosophical Investigations, II, xi. Quoted by Nussbaum, epigraph to chapter 2 of Love’s Knowledge ).

           In his article “Particular and Universal in Aristotle’s Conception of Practical Knowledge” ^(F1), Daniel Devereux had a number of extremely  illuminating things to say concerning Aristotle’s conception of practical knowledge. To mention a few of them, (1) his distinction between what Aristotle deems genuinely universal judgments which are explanatory, and apparently universal judgments which are actually particular and not explanatory. Add to this: (2) his distinction between practical sciences which are complete and efficacious and practical sciences which are incomplete because inefficacious; (3) his distinction between the “untutored empiric” and the “medical scientist”; (3) his distinction between the science of medicine and the practice of medicine; (4) finally, his distinction between the theoretical scientist and the practitioner of science. Why do I find these points illuminating will be explained in the corpus of the paper  

Now, while acknowledging the very positive points just enumerated, still I find myself in disagreement with Devereux on the most substantive aspect of his paper, which is to rescue the Aristotelian conception of practical knowledge on the basis of  the retrieval by Devereux of the  genuine Aristotelian notions of universal and particular. . Indeed,  I am far less sanguine than Devereux concerning the notion of practical knowledge he attributes to Aristotle, and even less sanguine concerning the validity of that notion as genuine practical knowledge.

Devereux rightly claims that all of Aristotle’s predecessors,  Plato included,, confused practical knowledge with theoretical knowledge, whereas Aristotle’s succeeded in putting an end to that situation. For my part, I think that Aristotle failed as much as his predecessors. That failure, in my opinion, was tragic, because we have remained subservient to Aristotle in matters of theoretical and practical knowledge with disastrous results,

[[As a result of my analysisrpcessDevereux presents  –a conception that has been profoundly influential within the Western epistemic tradition. Devereux’s article, however, because of its systematic character, its precision, and the familiarity it shows with the pertinent Aristotelian texts, furnishes me an excellent basis on which to conduct my criticism of the particular stage in the evolution of Aristotle’s views on practical knowledge on which Devereux focuses]].

Aristotle’s notion of practical knowledge

To move now to Devereux’s article itself, he begins by attributing to Aristotle the definite discrimination between practical and theoretical knowledge, whereas Plato had assimilated practical to theoretical knowledge.  As Devereux puts it, referring to Aristotle’s predecessors,

“ all of them failed to grasp the fundamental distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge ... Platonic dialectic is a confusing blend of the practical and theoretical. Questions about value and how we ought to act are not regarded as different in kind from questions about the ultimate nature of reality.” ^(F2)

Aristotle’s break with Plato, however,

“... was a gradual process. The notion of practical knowledge did not spring all at once, fully formed. There was a time when he thought practical knowledge was much like theoretical, the only important contrast being the different aims of each .” ^(F3)

At this stage of the evolution of his thinking, then, Aristotle is still very close to Plato, who assimilated practical to theoretical knowledge. Indeed, if the only difference between theoretical and practical knowledge is, according to Aristotle,  that they are pursued for different aims, then, they differ only extrinsically. By contrast, as Devereux’s sees it, at a later stage, Aristotle will have succeeded in fully dispelling Plato’s confusion of practical with theoretical knowledge by showing that they are totally different from each other . At that later stage,

“Ethics and politics, the prime examples of practical knowledge, differ from such theoretical sciences as metaphysics and physics not only in their aims but in their methods and subjects as well. Indeed, Aristotle thinks the differences are such that we cannot regard practical and theoretical knowledge as two species of a single genus, for there is no common definition of knowledge which applies to both.” ^(F4)

For my part,  I will contend that far from having come to realize even at this later stage of his evolution Aristotle continued to confuse practical knowledge with theoretical knowledge through his inclusion of a ‘universal” component in practical knowledge. As to the important consequences this failure has had for us, the inheritors of the Aristotelian epistemic legacy, I reserve that issue for a later chapter.

After pointing out that initially Aristotle thought that the only difference  between theoretical and practical knowledge was purely extrinsic, a matter of the goals for which they are pursued, but identical in all other respects, Devereux continues his exploration of Aristotle’s progressive evolution away from Plato:

“ A second.... contrast between practical and theoretical knowledge has to do with subject matter. Theoretical sciences like physics and mathematics are concerned with necessary objects, with things ‘whose principles cannot be otherwise than they are.’  Practical knowledge, on the other hand, is concerned with the variable and contingent; since such knowledge is practical, it is concerned with actions in particular circumstances, and here there is ‘much diversity and fluctuation. ’” ^(F5)

In other words, Aristotle thinks that the kind of necessarily true knowledge to which Plato aspired may certainly be attained in the theoretical sciences, because they deal with necessary objects, with objects which in all circumstances are the same. By contrast, following Socrates, Plato wanted scientific knowledge, that is precise and necessarily true knowledge – episteme– in the area of human action. In Aristotle’s opinion, however, such knowledge is impossible in practical matters. Precisely because it is practical, because its purpose is not to gain knowledge about action but to result in action , it deals with actions in particular circumstances, and there can be no scientific knowledge of what  is variable and contingent. As forcefully underscored in a different context by Plato himself, who for that very reason had relegated sensible knowledge that dealt with particular objects to the realm of mere ‘opinion,” scientific or necessarily true knowledge is only possible of universal objects, of what is necessarily what it is. Thus Aristotle concludes in the NE that, “Practical knowledge is not scientific knowledge ( episteme) .” ^(F6)

We might say that with these words Aristotle puts an end to Socrates’ and Plato’s dream in their fight against the rhetoricians who pretended to educate the youth without themselves possessing the truth – the dream  of attaining necessarily true knowledge in matters pertaining to human action. The opposite danger, of course, would be to conclude that in matters of action everything goes because actions are particular and knowledge is only possible of the universal. Aristotle avoids falling into that error by showing that, though the practical disciplines deal indeed with particulars, they nonetheless  involve knowledge of the universal, too. Thus, though the practical sciences cannot be strictly scientific like the theoretical ones, for the latter deal with universal objects exclusively , nonetheless  the practical disciplines or “arts” can legitimately qualify as knowledge because they combine knowledge of the universal and the particular: “Nor is practical wisdom concerned with universals only –it must also know particulars ( ta  kaz’ekasta ), for it is practical.” ^(F7)

Having underscored these differences between theoretical and practical knowledge pointed out by Aristotle, Devereux concludes that

“Aristotle thinks the differences are such that we cannot regard practical and theoretical knowledge as two species of a single genus, for there is no common definition of knowledge which applies to both.” ^(F8)

Up to this point I have followed Devereux’s analysis. Now I will part  ways with him. If Devereux were right, Aristotle would have indeed succeeded, as Devereux contends,  in dispelling Plato’s confusion of practical with theoretical knowledge. For my part, I think that Aristotle failed to discern practical from theoretical knowledge. In order to support this view, I  shall  explore the Aristotelian  inclusion of knowledge of universals in practical knowledge. Devereux rightly thinks that Aristotle believes that, aside from knowledge of particulars, practical knowledge also involves knowledge of universals. What Devereux fails to see is that Aristotle is wrong on this point BECAUSE NO KNOWLEDGE OF PARTICULARS IS INVOLVED IN THE PRATICAL SCIENCES. . And this fact is, as we shall see, the reason why, contrary to Devereux’s view, Aristotle ultimately  failed in fully overcoming Plato’s confusion of practical with theoretical knowledge.

                                                        

Does practical knowledge involve knowledge of the universal AND KNOWLEDGE OF THE PARTICULAR?

First of all, what is Aristotle talking about when he speaks of the knowledge of the universal involved in practical knowledge? Consider the case of medicine, which Aristotle classifies among the practical sciences. As observed by Devereux, Aristotle distinguishes between the person who, through trial and error, learned on his own how to cure, and the person who learned medicine as an academic discipline taught by others. the person who learned medicine on his or her own and through personal practice rather than by attending school is identified by Devereux as “the untutored empiric”: [LEARNING THE UNIVERSAL THROUGH DESCRIPTIVE STATEMENTS AND LEARNING THE PARTICULAR THROUGH PRACTICE].

“He [the untutored empiric] investigates other individuals who are sick with symptoms A and finds the same correlation: the treatment is effective for those who are phlegmatic, but not for those who are not. He concludes that it is because of the presence of this characteristic in the one group that the treatment is effective... Aristotle would say that this man is no longer simply an untutored empiric; he has made an important step towards acquiring the art of medicine. In discovering a common feature in virtue of which a particular treatment is effective for a group of individuals, he has hit upon what Aristotle calls here  a ‘universal ,’ and it is knowledge of such universal that qualifies as art or science” ^(F9)

As Devereux remarks,

“The normal procedure (at least nowadays) is to acquire the universal knowledge before the particular. One first acquires general information about...symptoms of various diseases, methods of treatment, etc., and then, through a period of residency in a hospital, one gains the experience necessary to use the general knowledge.” ^(F10)

           Using as a concrete example of a “universal” the general knowledge that “light meats are easy to digest and wholesome,” Devereux informs us that Aristotle makes some important points in the following paragraph of the Nicomachean Ethics :

“Nor is practical wisdom concerned with universals only –it must also know particulars, for it is practical. That is why in other fields too some who do not know are more effective in action than those who know –they have experience. For if a man knew that light meats are easy to digest and wholesome, but did not know which sorts of meat are light, he will not produce health, but the man who knows that chicken is wholesome is more likely to produce health. Now practical wisdom ( phronesis ) is practical; hence one should have both [knowledge of universals and knowledge of particulars], of the latter even more than the former.” ^(F11)

One of the main characteristics of practical knowledge, then, is that it involves not only knowledge of particulars but also knowledge of universals. Indeed, as Aristotle points it out at the beginning of  the Metaphysics, what distinguishes an “art” or a “science”  from other practices is their grasp of a universal truth. ^(F12) Because that is the case with medicine, medicine is an “art” or a “science,” not just a skill.

What is extremely disconcerting in the passage of the Meta;hysics to which I just referred is that, at the same time, Aristotle tells us,–at least in equivalent terms, and apparently without realizing the equivalence– that in  medicine, precisely because it is a practical rather than a theoretical science, the knowledge of the universal is ACTUALLY superfluous . Indeed, as he explains, the untutored empirics who “do not know” because they possess only the knowledge of the particular gained through experience –namely, that chicken is a wholesome meat–  may be capable of curing, whereas “those  who know,” that is, those who  learned in the classroom the universals in isolation from the practice–namely, the general truth that light meats are easy to digest and wholesome– but ignore that chicken is wholesome, will be incapable of curing . And, as Aristotle will point it out later, what happens in medicine in this regard also happens in the other practical disciplines, SUCH AS ETHICS .AND POLITICS.

Thus it turns out that, after all, knowledge of the universal is not an essential component of practical knowledge, since it can be dispensed with. Why, then, did Aristotle think that it was? At this point I may interject my suspicion is that Aristotle required knowledge of the universal in the practical disciplines, and attributed importance to it, only because he had not quite overcome his dependence on Plato, whose epistemic model was the necessarily true knowledge involved in the theoretical disciplines, such as mathematics and metaphysics, and for whom, accordingly, the only kind of genuine knowledge was knowledge of the universal. Now, however,  Aristotle has stumbled on the quite unexpected fact –so unexpected, indeed, that he doesn’t seem to quite absorb it, ANYMORE THAN DEVEREUX DOES– that practical disciplines really do not REALLY require theoretical  knowledge of universals. If this is true, tough, then Aristotle’s conception of practical knowledge as a combination of knowledge of universals and of particulars proves to be wrong. Of course, this point is so momentous that it demands serious textual confirmation. I think that Devereux himself indirectly furnishes us that confirmation.

In a very dense paragraph ^(F13), clarifying and systematizing Aristotle’s thought, Devereux correctly distinguishes between the science or art of medicine  and the practice of medicine. As any Aristotelian“art” or “science,” the science or art of medicine is exclusively composed of universals. On the other hand, the practice of medicine, in addition to the knowledge of universals contained in the science or art,   also involves the knowledge of particulars that, according to Aristotle, comes only with experience. Devereux calls “medical skill” this combination of universal  and particular knowledge possessed by the successful practitioner of medicine. By contrast, the medical scientist who learned medicine theoretically listening to lectures or reading books only possesses knowledge of the universal. Now, Devereux continues, medicine is a practical, not a theoretical science, since its aim is not the acquisition of  knowledge for its own sake but the restoration  and maintenance of health; in other words , medicine is supposed to be practical in the sense of BEING EFFICACIOUS, ‘‘productive” of health. Clearly, then, the science of medicine is  “incomplete,” since it is exclusively concerned with universals and cannot achieve its aim of curing  without experiential knowledge of particulars . The practice of medicine, by contrast to the science ,  is “complete,” since APART FROM, THE UNIVERSAL KNOWLEDGE IT SHARES WITH THE MEDICAL SCIENCE, IT ALSO involves the experiential knowledge of particulars that is required FOR THE KNOWLEDGE TO BE EFICACIOUS, THAT IS,  to produce or maintain health.

If we are to take seriously what Devereux just said, we should conclude that in declaring that the science or art of medicine is “incomplete,” he means by implication that the medical science, as distinguished from the practice of medicine, really is not a practical science at all but a purely theoretical one, for it is not “efficacious” in any way. AND It is a purely theoretical science because it only contains knowledge of universals, such as “light meats are healthy,” and from such knowledge no action follows. Why not? Because if the medical scientist only knows the general truth that light meats are healthy, but not which particular meats are light –a knowledge that comes only with practice or “experience” OF THE RESULTS OF ACTION – then he cannot prescribe the particular meat that will be a remedy for the patient’s illness.  

Oddly enough, Devereux fails to draw the conclusion that, as a science or art, medicine is not a practical science. In this regard, he resembles Aristotle, who required knowledge of universals in medicine for it to be a science or an art, and then stated that the medical practitioner who possessed no knowledge of the universals included in the science or art, but who possessed a thorough knowledge of particulars thanks to experience, could be a successful physician . Nor does Devereux prove to be any more perceptive than Aristotle when it comes to his own (Devereux’s) observation that

“The successful practitioner of medicine has both knowledge of universals (the science of medicine) and knowledge of particulars, and the latter is relevant to his being a practitioner but not to his being a scientist. ” ^(F14)

What, one wonders, prevents Devereux from realizing that the converse is also true, to wit, that knowledge of universals is only relevant to the physician’a being a scientist [THAT IS TO THE PHYSICIAN AS A SCIENTIST} but not to his being a successful practitioner of medicine [BUT NOT AS A PRACTITIONER OF MEDICINE, AS AN EMPIRIC] given that he has acknowledged with Aristotle that the medical practitioner can be successful without knowledge of universals?

Let us pursue the argument further. From the consideration of the case of medicine Devereux moves to a comparison of other practical disciplines to medicine. He begins with knowledge in the field of ethics, and he asks himself whether practical wisdom in ethical matters is analogous to the science of medicine (whose knowledge of universals has been IMPLICITLY acknowledged to be superfluous to the medical practitioner),  or is rather analogous to medical skill (whose knowledge of particulars has been declared necessary to the practitioner of medicine) .

Devereux’s careful answer comes in two parts. ^(F15) The first refers to practical wisdom “ in the practically wise person ”; the second part refers to the component of practical wisdom that is concerned with universals, a concrete example  of which is the doctrine contained in Aristotle’s book of Ethics. Devereux determines that the knowledge possessed by the practically wise person is analogous to medical skill, but not to medical science, for “it is self-sufficient in the same way as medical skill, in that the practically wise person has what he needs to achieve his aims.” As for the knowledge involved in such practical sciences or arts as Aristotle’s Ethics , it is analogous to the science of medicine, but not to the medical skill of the successful practitioner. The reason is, of course,  that the Aristotelian ethical treatises contain no experiential knowledge of particulars [BUT JUST GENERAL OBSERVATIONAL STATEMENTS RATHER THAN EXPERIMENTAL, DISPOSITIONAL STATEMENTS THAT RESULT FROM ACTION RATHER THN FROM OBSERVATION ].

Now, from the fact that the ethical agent’s knowledge or “practical wisdom”  is not analogous to the knowledge involved in the medical science, whereas the knowledge involved in the practical sciences of ethics and politics is, it follows that the Aristotelian identification of the agent’s practical knowledge with the knowledge involved in the practical sciences is mistaken. Yet  Devereux does not cease following Aristotle on this misidentification. For instance, he writes without flinching:

 

“ Ethics and politics, the prime examples of practical knowledge , differ from such theoretical sciences as metaphysics and physics not only in their aims but in their methods and subject matter as well.” ^(F16)

However, if my previous analysis is right, the practical wisdom of the ethical agent is indeed a prime example of practical knowledge, but the science of ethics, that is, the  doctrine contained in Aristotle’s ethical treatises, is not an example of practical knowledge. The practical wisdom of the ethical agent, through deliberation and decision in particular circumstances, results in action , whereas Aristotle’s abstract ethical treatises do not . Actually, the difference between these two types of knowledge which Aristotle deems identical explains why Devereux found no analogy between the practical knowledge or wisdom of the agent who has to decide how to act , and the general statements contained in Aristotle’s ethical treatises . It explains, too, why Devereux found no analogy between the knowledge of the medical practitioner and the knowledge constituted by the general statements of the science of medicine. Conversely, it explains why Devereux  found an analogy between the practical knowledge of the medical practitioner, which terminates in actual action, and the practical wisdom of the ethical agent, which also terminates in action.   

Aristotle’s realization that the practitioner of medicine can be successful without any knowledge of the universals involved in the science or art of medicine should  have alerted him to

his mistake in identifying practical knowledge with the knowledge involved in what he calls “practical sciences,” which after all are not genuinely practical since they are not efficacious . Similarly, Devereux’s realization of the dysanalogy between the practical knowledge of the agent engaged in action and the knowledge found in the practical sciences should have led him to correct the mistake he makes in following Aristotle in this matter. Actually, Devereux seems to have come very close to the realization that the ethical agent has no need at all of  knowledge of universals, such as those that compose Aristotle’s Ethics , for he himself explains that

“it is not clear whether he [Aristotle] thinks knowledge of politics [of which ethics is a part] is a necessary condition of practical wisdom (would he say, for example, that individuals who have not engaged in political reflection about moral and political questions [the kind of reflection in which Aristotle engages in his ethical treatises] could not be practically wise?” ^(F17)

Obviously, Devereux suspects that his question is rhetorical and should be answered negatively. And rightly so. Indeed, in order to act prudently, that is, to exhibit the practical knowledge Aristotle calls “practical wisdom” and make concrete correct decisions in concrete circumstances an agent has no need to possess the kind of so-called “universal knowledge” about action [CONSIDERED AS OBJECT OF INQUIRY ANS RESULTING IN GENERAL OBSERVATIONAL STATEMENTS}  imparted by Aristotle in his ethical treatises . Were that the case, correct ethical decisions would be possible only for scholars and intellectuals. Unfortunately, Devereux does not pause to ponder the implications of the negative, appropriate answer to his question for the Aristotelian identification of the agent’s genuinely practical knowledge, which is efficacious in that it results in appropriate action, with the theoretical  knowledge involved in the practical sciences or arts.

An indication that Aristotle himself would have had to agree that the practically wise agent would not need to possess the sort of knowledge Aristotle imparts in his ethical treatises emerges from Devereux’s observation that, in the Nicomachean Ethics, “Aristotle warns us that his remarks are necessarily imprecise because of the subject matter...”  Now, as Devereux aptly comments,

“Matters of health and conduct ‘have no fixity,’ and therefore it would be futile to attempt to formulate precise statements about what we should do in various situations. One must speak ‘in outline,’ ( typo) and not precisely ( ouk akribos ). If one tries to be more specific and detailed, one’s statements will inevitably be inaccurate and unreliable. Such statements will at best be useful as rules of thumb; the experienced agent or doctor will be guided not so much [ELIMINATE THE SO MUCH, CHANGE IT FOR NOT AT ALL] by them as by his judgment of ‘what is appropriate to the occasion.’” ^(F18)

Incidentally, Aristotle own observation in the passage of the NE to which Devereux refers, about the “subject matter” of his ethical treatises, which has no fixity, may be taken as an indication of his confusion of the kind of knowledge he imparts in his ethical treatises, that is, the knowledge involved in the supposedly practical sciences or arts, with the practical wisdom of the agent actually involved in action. Indeed,  the practical sciences or arts do have a  subject-mater, as Aristotle claims, that is an object of study , however much the subject-matter of each of them may differ from that of the others. Now, if the agent’s knowledge is indeed to be identified with the knowledge involved in the practical sciences or arts, as assumed by Aristotle, it follows that the agent’s practical knowledge does have a subject matter. Such assumption, however, is unwarranted . For having a subject matter, as is evident from the case of the theoretical sciences, means having an object of study. Now, the author of an ethical a treatise may be said to have an object of study, for he or she is guided by the desire to gain knowledge. But such is not the case of the agent involved in action , who is not motivated by the desire to know. The agent’s knowledge is genuinely practical in that, through deliberation and decision, it results in appropriate action, which is what the agent aims at. But that is not the case with Aristotle’s general, imprecise statements in his ethical treatises .  As correctly observed by Devereux, the agent is not guided by such statements but by his judgment of what is appropriate to the occasion.

On the basis of the evidence discussed so far, it seems that we are entitled to draw two conclusions. First, that it cannot be said  without qualification, as Aristotle does, that the practical sciences or arts involve and require knowledge of universals. For the fact is that by practical sciences Aristotle means two different things, as implied by Devereux’s analysis. One thing is the  “ art ” or “ science ” as such,  which by definition requires knowledge of universals CONSTITUTED BY STATEMENTS, but which is not genuinely practical, for it cannot result in action for lack of knowledge of particulars. In this case, then, knowledge of universals is involved, but not knowledge of particulars. Quite a different thing is the practice of a science that is genuinely practical or efficacious, FOR IT IS CONSITUTED BY DISPOSITIONAL JUDGMENTS THAT BECOME PRACTICAL HABITSsince it terminates in action, but which does not require knowledge of universals, since it can be successful or efficacious without such knowledge, as granted by Aristotle himself. Thus, in this case, knowledge of particulars is involved, but not knowledge of universals.   Second, we have to reject the Aristotelian identification of the genuinely practical knowledge or “wisdom” possessed by the medical practitioner, by the ethical agent and, in general, by the practitioner of any of the Aristotelian “practical sciences,” with the THEORETICAL knowledge or doctrine contained in the treatises corresponding to those sciences, concrete examples of which are Aristotle’s Ethics, Rhetoric and Poetics . Indeed, the practical knowledge or poetical ability exhibited by the poet neither is identical to, nor requires on the poet’s  part, the knowledge of the theory contained in the Poetics. The theory is drawn from theoretical reflection on the poetical activity and the poetical product, but the poets themselves are not guided by the theory in their creative activity. And a parallel affirmation is to be made in respect of the wise ethical agent and of the persuasive orator.

*   To complete my point, I need to show that theoretical knowledge, which arises from “ABSTRACTION” (NE, 1142a17, Transl. Irwin) rendered possible by language, and   practical knowledge which arises from “EXPERIENCE,” have nothing in common. In the former case, the words of the statement are understood to function as mirroring what is the case ; hence the attempt to demonstrate the truth of the content of the statement. In  the latter case, the words mean practically or instrumentally , that is, refer the desirous or fearful agent –through desire or fear– to a  new projected situation to be realized or brought about through the action decided by the agent in concrete circumstances. Now, in that the Aristotelian “practical sciences,” among which politics and ethics, are not practical in any serious sense of efficaciousness , not only cannot the knowledge involved in them be equated with the agent’s knowledge, which is indeed efficacious, but they are strictly superfluous for action and should, perhaps, disappear.

This is why the experienced practitioner without universal knowledge can cure, whereas those who have universal knowledge but no practice or experience cannot cure .

We cannot, however, leave the analysis at this point without being seriously incomplete. Indeed, we appear to be saying that the knowledge of  the medical theory or doctrine contained in the medical treatises is superfluous to the medical practitioner, that is, to the physician. However,  this is something that few people, if any, would grant. In order to make full sense concerning these matters  we need to explore in some depth the role attributed by Aristotle to “experience” in the acquisition of practical knowledge.  

Practical knowledge as experiential knowledge

“To the extent that awareness of time is lacking, the capacity of other animals for generalizing is limited. All animals recognize at least some objects as instances of types seen before. Thus, their ability to survive requires the recognition of ‘food,’ of dangerous predators ( general schemata  which are probably innate ) and of members of their own species. But types of generalizing that require a sophisticated awareness of historical patterns or of social structures will elude them... Animals also vary in the degree to which they have causal concepts ; and again, most species have them to a much less marked degree than do human beings. Lupa clearly had many forms of causal thinking; she saw Pitcher as the person who fed her, she saw a stick as a likely cause of abuse, and so forth. To that extent, she could have emotions such as shame and anger, which seem to be dependent on forms of causal thinking (‘he did that to me,’ ‘I disappointed him in this way’). Many animals do not have the basis for such emotions. It is of course difficult to tell to what extent an animal’s reactions express a conditioned routine, and to what extent they express a more specific cognition with real causal content . But Pitcher’s history with Lupa shows, I think, that the dog was able to move from mere association between the men’s presence and the food to some thought more like ‘This man has brought me food.’ This clearly underlay her eventual change in demeanor toward Pitcher, and her subsequent expectations, demands, and so forth . Certain causal stories, however, Lupa was unlikely to tell herself: for example, those that point to a history of oppression and abuse beginning in childhood. She is unlikely to conceive of herself, as Pitcher does, as a dog who was once shamefully abused but who is now loved and cared for; certainly she did not believe that she was abused by a human because she was a dog. Nor would she, like Pitcher, trace her adult states of shyness and suspiciousness to a childhood history that explains them” (Martha Nussbaum, Upheavals of Thought: The intelligence of Emotions, Cambridge University Press, 2001, pp. 144-146)

When Aristotle is reflecting about the character of the theoretical and practical sciences, his reflection tends to pull him in the direction of Plato’s contemplative epistemic conception. This contaminates his notion of practical knowledge. Indeed, though he gives up on the Platonic ideal of scientific or necessarily true knowledge in practical matters, reserving its possibility to the theoretical field, his demand of a component of universal knowledge in the practical sciences or “arts”indicates that his independence from Plato is far from being complete. [The same dependence on Plato shows in Aristotle’s preservation of the Platonic ideal of scientific knowledge in the  theoretical field, reflected in the ideal of science he delineated in the Posterior Analytics , as a n ensemble of universal, necessarily true statements organically interconnected, all of which are either object of direct intellectual intuition or logically deducted from the intuited ones. Not for nothing, as we are reminded by Nussbaum, there is the story connected with Plato’s Academy, attended by the young Aristotle,  that  its door carried the inscription, “Let no one enter here who does not pursue geometry” ( The Fragility of Goodness , 1986, p. 163).

 Aristotle’s stance, however,  is different when  he explores, not the character of the content of the practical sciences, but the process through which practical knowledge originates. . Elusive as he obviously finds practical knowledge, he nonetheless drops tantalizing hints as to its character, all of which refer to the role played by experience in its acquisition by the agent. Thus, Aristotle tells us that the agent’s knowledge can only be acquired through “experience,” and that experience takes time, which is the reason that young people can be good mathematicians but not prudent agents. ^(F19) He also tells us that we should  “attend to the undemonstrated remarks and beliefs of experienced and older people... For these people see correctly because experience has given them their eye.” ^(F20) Finally, rather than attempting to determine the specific content of the knowledge possessed by the practically wise or prudent agent, he makes of the latter’s decisions, as manifested in his or her conduct, the model to follow, obviously because no learned doctrine, but  only “experience,” can teach us how to act in concrete circumstances.

A good strategy to understand what Aristotle means by this experiential knowledge may be to explore what he has to say about animals in connection with practical knowledge. Indeed, so far we have noticed Aristotle’s insistence that practical knowledge involves and requires knowledge of universals, a claim that I, for one, have found misguided. Now, as we shall presently see,  Aristotle attributes practical knowledge to both people and animals. Thus, by focusing on the practical knowledge of animals and on the practical knowledge of people we should have a good chance to understand what Aristotle has in mind when he refers to the universal component involved in practical knowledge. Moreover, given that Aristotle deems that animals are incapable of producing practical sciences or arts, in their case there will be no possible room for the confusion we detected in Aristotle in the case of people between the agent’s knowledge and the knowledge represented by the statements contained in the treatises on the rpactical sciences or arts produced by people.

In the first book of the Metaphysics , Aristotle defines theoretical knowledge as knowledge pursued for its own sake, and practical knowledge as knowledge pursued for the sake of its utility. Of course, the only type of knowledge he attributes to animals –and, at that, only to the superior species of animals that enjoy local motion– is practical knowledge, for they cannot pursue disinterested knowledge for its own sake. Animals only  want knowledge for the sake of its utility either to the singular animal or to its species. This is an inherited , instinctual need.

Thus Aristotle claims that the superior species of animals possess practical knowledge. But how does Aristotle know that animals possess knowledge, given that they do not express linguistically their knowledge, as we people do wen we state what we believer to be the case? By observing their non-inherited teleological actions in which concrete means to achieve their natural needs are progressively learned, as manifested in their progressively more successful actions to satisfy their needs.

The learning process as described by Aristotle involves “experience.” What is this?

Thanks to their being stored in memory, the animal ‘remembers’ which of its actions related to which particular environmental circumstances led to the successful fulfillment of its natural needs. As a result, under the appropriate circumstances, in the future the animal will automatically repeat those actions in the expectation of similar results. This grasp of useful environmental regularities coupled with the appropriate motor responses to fulfill successfully their natural needs constitutes “experience,” which in its turn results in the habitual behaviors in which practical knowledge consists. For instance, after finding by chance that pushing a particular button opens the door to food, a rat will in time develop the habitual conduct of touching the button to get food. Experience leads to this sort of instinctive generalizations by the animal after uniformly repeated events in which the animal is interestedly involved..  (The assumption of repetitive uniformity, which for the most part is sufficiently correct to insure the survival of the species and of the individual, is embedded in this automatism of animals. But practical knowledge is never certain, in the sense of necessarily true, for it is prospective, and there is no knowledge of the future. Hence prudentia, pro-videntia, based on past experience but after a model of playing chess as humans do, not as computers do).

        

First important point: Knowledge here is directly connected with systematically or repeatedly successful action. And for good reason, since it is a result of action . In other words, no amount of thinking can yield , without action , the experience that results in practical knowledge. In other words, no action, no “experience.” What Aristotle calls “experience” is the result of actual doing, not of seeing.  If the rat doesn’t happen to actually push the button, it will acquire no practical knowledge, that is, the ability to get food systematically rather than by chance, which is what Aristotle identifies as “practical knowledge,” anymore than if a child doesn’t actually use his or her hands on the piano, he or she will not become a skillful pianist, that is will not develop the ability to produce musical sounds systematically rather than once in a while by chance. In practical knowledge, something is used instrumentally (it may be t he agent’s own body) or as a means for the sake of achieving a desired goal in determinate environmental circumstances, and the action or doing involved, correlated or associated with the circumstances or situation in which the agent finds himself or herself –for all practical knowledge is tacitly self-referential– is ‘interiorized’ to the point of becoming habitual or practically automatic.

(Notice, however, Aristotle’s tendency to interpret animal knowledge after the theoretical model, in the first lectio of   Metaphysiucs, .He thinks in terms of theoretical, quasi-linguistic, objectively known generalizations rather than of practical, tacit, sensorimotor  dispositions and expectations. This is why he attributes to superior animals a quasi theoretical knowledge-quia, instead of understanding the knowledge quia of humans as a superior, more efficacious variety of the practical, tacit, sensorimotor dispositions found in animals as a result of experience .

    

Second: Knowledge is connected with action via desire of goal, which structures perception teleologically and organizes hodological space for the perceiver-agent’s action. Insofar as animals, by contrast to people, only have what Maslow calls “lower needs.” the teleology of their actions is easier to detect.

Third: Knowledge is essentially personal, as personal as the agent’s desire and satisfaction.

Fourth: Knowledge depends on regularity because what happens always in the same way creates regular expectation and habitual action. The Platonic explanation of the exact regularity is by reference to intelligible forms, whereas Aristotle refers to essences or natures that supposedly correspond in the object to what the mind grasps. Hence the Platonic distinction between knowledge or scientia and opinion or belief.

The regularities, though, can be of the order of objective, unexposed observation (and then either exact and measurable, or not measurable but exact, or not exact and not measurable) and of the order of exposed affection and action.

The order of objective observation involves one or more of the degrees of abstraction of which Aristotle speaks, plus the one of which Aristotle does not speak, namely, the abstraction from the subject’s interested situation in respect of the object, which is at the origin of all knowledge and is always “tacit,” because non-objective. Once again, Aristotle is sidetracked by Plato’s visual epistemic model –hence “theoria,” with its two possible varieties of correspondence–necessary and contingent– instead of sticking to the non-visual, dispositional epistemic model that begins to ctive. Without this, regularities and lack thereof would be irrelevant. This is to say that the order of objective observation has to be replaced in relation to the order of exposed affection, deliberation, and action. Hence the identification of the knowledge involved in the practical sciences with the exposed agent’s knowledge rests in a confusion between the unexposed contemplator’s ‘world,’ and the exposed agent’s dramatic world .

Is this knowledge found in the ethical agent or, what is t he same, in the practitioner of ethics, analogous to the knowledge found in the successful practitioner of medicine, or rather analogous to the knowledge found in the medical scientist? Devereux answers that “ practical wisdom, as Aristotle understands it, is analogous not to [the science of] medicine but to medical skill... [NOTICE THE IMPLIED EQUIVALENCE OF THE KNOWLEDGE POSSESSED BY, OR FOUND IN, THE ETHICAL AGENT AND THE KNOWLEDGE FOUND IN THE STATEMENTS CONTAINED IN THE MEDICAL BOOKS? HOWEVER, THIS IMPLIED EQUIVALENCE CANNOT BE CORRECT, FOR EXPERIENTIAL KNOWLEDGE, WHICH IS APPLICATIVE, CANNOT BE IDENTICAL WITH PROPOSITIONAL KNOWLEDGE. ONE HAS TO LEARN IT EXPERIENTIALLY, EVEN WHEN GUIDED BY THE STATEMENTS OF THE EXPERIENCED PEOPLE. THIS IS WHY YOUNG PEOPLE CAN BE GOOD AT MATHEMATICS BUT NOT AT ETHICAL EXPERIENTIAL KNOWLEDGE –“the knowledge of particulars that comes with experience [and only with experience]... for it is practical both in aim and efficacy, and it is complete or-self sufficient as medical skill; the practically  wise person has what he needs to achieve his aims.” However, Devereux adds that “there is a part of practical wisdom which is analogous to [the science] of medicine: this is the component that is concerned with universals. And, as Aris warns us, it lacks precision and self-sufficiency.” What the lack of precision shows is that the universals supposedly found in the practical sciences are not genuine universals. Not only does the ‘object’ of  the practical sciences lack the fixity of the object of the theoretical sciences, but the universal itself is superfluous for the skillful practitioner, since it is not used or known by the practitioner. The supposed universal is discovered by the practitioner, not by the theorist or scientist. And it is not meant to work for the practitioner as objective knowledge that mirrors what is the case, but as a vague map or recipe for action, to be internalized through practice by the disciple of the successful practitioner, as it was acquired by the untutored empiric.

 

This confusion of the knowledge involved in the practical sciences with the agent’s practical knowledge shows again in Aristotle’s mistaken conviction that his philosophical exploration in the Nicomachean Ethics will result in  right actions:

“Our present inquiry does not aim, as our others do, at study; for the purpose of our examination is not to know what virtue is, but to become good, since otherwise the inquiry would be of no benefit to us... First, then, actions should express correct reason.” ^(F21)

It is baffling that Aristotle could entertain such hope, given his realization that the agent’s practical knowledge involves deliberation and decision, which are only possible in reference to the particular circumstances in which the agent finds himself and which demand a decision, whereas Aristotle’s own knowledge in the ethics refers to no particular circumstances.

Let me summarize in some systematic way the results yielded so far by my exploration. If I have been right, it emerges that Aristotle gives the  name of  “practical sciences” to two different things which he amalgamates, and only one of which deserves to be called practical for only one of them results in action , that is, only one of them is genuinely practical or efficacious. Which are the two different types of what Aristotle calls “practical sciences”?  To one type belong the practical wisdom of the ethical agent and the practice of medicine of the practicing physician. These are the practical sciences among which Devereux rightly finds an analogy. To the other type belong ethics and politics and the science or art of medicine. These are the sciences which Devereux rightly finds dysanalogous with the practical sciences of the first type. Now, the former are not genuine sciences or even arts, if we gujide ourselves by Aristotle’s own definition, in that, contrary to Aristotle’s opinion, they do not require knowledge of universals. However, the knowledge involved in them is genuinely practical, in that it is efficacious. Indeed, the practical knowledge – prudentia , pro-videntia – of the good ethical agent results in appropriate,. that is, ethically righ, action, and the practical knowledge of the skillful medical practitioner results in the patient’s health. Herein lies the analogy between the sciences of the first type and their dysanalogy with the sciences of the second type. Furthermore, the practical knowledge involved in this type of practical sciences is experiential knowledge, that is, knowledge that results from experience. By contrast, the practical sciences of the second type, such as Aristotle’s ethics and politics and the science of medicine, are composed of imprecise universals whose knowledge, contrary to Aristotle’s view of these sciences as “ruling” or directing their respective practices, is not really indispensable for ethical practice or for medical practice. By themselves these supposedly practical sciences are  incomplete, that is, inefficacious or not genuinely practical, since by themselves they lack experiential knowledge without which no efficaciousness is possible. They are a bad composite, a spurious mixture of the genuinely universal (because conceptual) but purely theoretical knowledge that arises from abstraction and is possible only of abstract objects of thought that are always the same or invariable, and the agent’s efficacious or genuinely practical  knowledge that arises from experience.

The above text, like  note 8, concerns the contrast between theoretical and practical sciences.  Aristotle explains the difference between them in terms of their respective objects: one has the fixity of the necessary, the other the variability of the contingent. In note 23 below, Aristotle explains that the theoretical sciences pursue and terminate in objective knowledge of what is, whereas in ethical matters the point is to terminate in appropriate action, without which Aristotle’s practical science or art of ethics would be futile. Finally, on the coming page Aristotle will explain the difference between the theoretical and the practical sciences by reference to the method or way through which knowledge is gained: “abstraction,” in the case of theoretical knowledge, and “experience” in the case of practical knowledge. Question: Are these different explanations equivalent or at least compatible? (Yes, acording to Nussbaum, not according to me). Secondly, are all of them correct?.   

To complicate matters still more, two points. (1) It is evident by now that Aristotle

mistakenly identifies the agent’s practical knowledge with the knowledge involved in the practical sciences. (2) The name of practical sciences covers such different things as medicine, which may be said to be  fields of study and ‘to have an object,’ and ethics, which is participatory or situational rather than contemplative, and in which action (which is on ‘my-body-for-me side)

is not an object to be known.

What is experience? Why does the practical knowledge that can only be acquired through  experience –from now on I will use the expression “experiential knowledge” to designate this kind of knowledge– require a good deal of time? Aristotle mentions that an apprentice baker asked whether he should make the bread deeply or lightly baked. The answer was “Do it right.” But what is right in matters of baking, and  how does one ‘do it right’? Only the experienced baker knows how to do it right. Thanks to his experience in making bread, he can guide the apprentice with general rules of thumb. However, for all of his master’s directions, the apprentice can learn how to apply the rules contained in the directions only through personal experience, that is, through trial and the feedback of error and success, as his master actually learned. And this practical learning, this acquisition of mastery, takes time to become habitual or facile. Once the apprentice has learned ‘to do it right,’ he will be like his experienced master baker who, reflecting on his experience and in order to guide his disciple, can formulate rules of thumb –the “universals” that constitute the so-called practical science or art of baking–  but who does not himself need or follow such rules, for he has “experience.” Actually, the rules of thumb or “universals”come after experience. However, to be precise, they do not come as a result of what Aristotle calls  “experience,” but of theoretical reflection on experience. What results from experience is the practical ability to make bread.
