2001-March-2-Polanyi March 3, 2001

POLANYI AND BEYOND

“Our body is the ultimate instrument of all our external knowledge, whether intellectual or practical. In all our waking moments we are relying on our awareness of contacts of our body with things. Our own body is the only thing in the world which we normally never experience as an object, but experience always in terms of the world to which we are attending from our body. It is by making this intelligent use of our body that we feel it to be our body, and not a thing outside” (Michael Polanyi, The Tacit Dimension , Anchor Books, Doub.leday & Company, Inc., Garden City, New York 1967, pp. 15-16).

“A theory may be set out on paper as a system of rules... Mathematical theory reaches the highest perfection in this respect. But even a geographical map fully embodies in itself a set of strict rules for finding one’s way through a region of otherwise uncharted experience. Indeed all theory may be regarded as a kind of map extended over space and time” (Michael Polanyi, Personal Knowledge: Toward a Post-Critical Philosophy (Harper & Row, New York 1958, p. 4).

“[The strong textualist] recognizes what Nietzsche and James recognized, that the idea of method presupposes that of a privileged vocabulary , the vocabulary that gets to the essence of the object, once which expresses the properties with it has in itself as opposed to those which [are] read into it. Nietzsche and James said that the notion of such a vocabulary was a myth –that even in science, not to mention philosophy, we simply cast around for a vocabulary which lets us get what we want” (Richard Rorty, “Nineteenth Century Idealism,” pp. 167-8; quoted by Richard Bernstein, Beyond Objectivism and Relativism ,  U. of Pennsylvania Press, Philadelphia 1983, p. 201).

In his view of internalization or appropriation as a result of action (what Aristotle, speaking of practical reason, called “experience,” which is connected with action and sometimes repeated action, not with curious observation). Polanyi is for me one of those who actually transcend the contemplative epistemological tradition. I think he is carrying to its completion the line of thinking initiated by Aristotle concerning practical knowledge, a line that Aristotle couldn’t complete because his contemplative Platonism  made him view experience in terms of knowledge, that is, representation of what is.

What distinguishes Polanyi from others is that his discourse is discourse about the engaged agent, the vulnerable, desirous agent. It is not discourse that starts from the point of view of interiority and whose first business is to prove that the phenomenal world and the subject to whom it appears are real. Polanyi’s discourse intends to prove nothing. It relies on, and appeals in the reader’s case to, our practical experience of ourself and the world as engaged agents, which is what Aristotle called “experience” in the NE, and moves from there.

What I want to explore here is the originality of this discourse by contrast to the traditional epistemological philosophical tradition geared on knowledge, and on knowledge conceived as representation. The latter demands a passage from phenomenon (mere appearance) to noumenon (reality).

Modern philosophy is usually said to begin with Descartes while philosophy begins with the Greeks and especially with Plato. What distinguishes modern philosophy from plain or Greek philosophy? And why is modern philosophy still, in a sense, philosophy? Obviously there has to be  continuity and discontinuity between the two. To put it in a nutshell, I think that the continuity lies in viewing knowledge as representation and the philosophical endeavor as the attempt to gain genuine knowledge through contemplation. The discontinuity lies in the fact that the Greeks –specifically, Socrates, Plato and Aristotle– conceived philosophy as a practical endeavor that was supposed to result in appropriate human action. Therefore, they had to include desire, for without desire there is no motivation to act. This inclusion, however, was incoherent, for desirous knowledge, if knowledge is objective representation, is a contradiction in terms. Descartes solved the problem in his new radical beginning by excluding desire and completing the rift between theory and practice. The total separation of theory and practice in Kant’s two first Critiques shows that the rift has ben completed. And the rift has remained ever since, though Hume with his “expectations” that have no epistemic value because they are not theoretical but practical and they represent nothing, as well as withh his commonsensical decision to exit rooms through the door rather than through the window, came close to move in Polanyi’s direction.

As strict re-presentation of what is, purified from all deforming factors such as desire, ^(F1) the implicit model of necessarily true knowledge of the Greeks is object for an unsituated mind, however incoherently they may have made that mind desirous. Whatever the case with the Greeks, by definitely suppressing desire, which is not objective but merely subjective and interferes with objectivity, Descartes de-situated completely the agent and transformed the agent into a contemplative mind to which ‘objects’ appear –phenomena .

Polanyi makes us aware that the agent is situated body and relies on his or her non-objective ‘experience (of) itself as desirous (satisfied-dissatisfied) body’ in all his dealings with the surrounding world. Actually, however, Polanyi is not explicit about need and desire .

Piaget has studied the child’s awareness of the world from its beginnings. Initially, no intentional action. Then the child learns through experience, that is, through action that is successful by chance in satisfying desire, to find the nipple relying on the contact of his body with the mother’s body. And so on, until his action becomes intentional through a progressive coordination of sight and body-hand guided by desire, a process in which each successful action is internalized for future application when the object of desire is present and desire calls for action. Then the child learns to make the satisfying object present when it is absent. Finally, thanks to language, which signals the separation between child and ape, at the end of the process the person learns to invent ideally the non-existing satisfying object and to bring it to existence.

Now, this is practical knowledge in that it results in action. However, we should notice that action (initially mere movement prompted by need) occurs first . Then, when by chance the need becomes satisfied, the object that satisfied the need becomes object-of desire or desired-object connected to the interiorized bodily action that brought about the satisfaction. In other words, the vision of the nipple will obtain as a result of action , not the other way around, much as in the case of inverting lenses, where the ‘correct vision’ occurs and becomes fixated as a result of action, not action as a result of vision . Now, once the object is constituted as such under the pressure of desire, that is, as desirable nipple, its presence will provoke desire and action, or the presence of desire of nipple will prompt the action of looking for the nipple. In this way, as studied by Piaget in much detail, thanks to language, a permanent public world will be constituted that will become common object of thought independently of its presence and its immediate utility. Thanks to language, that world and its laws (which are laws for our action)  –as much those laws which arise “ex his quae sunt   propria rei” when the object is observed and abstractedly understood by itself, independently of its utility, as those laws which appeal to our needful disposition (“per dispositionem audientis”)--  will end up being objectified in language and discourse. And this ‘objective’ world supposedly independent of desire and action will be given the primacy over action and practical knowledge, much as the science of medicine (the “general’ knowledge that arises from the practice of the practitioners) is nowadays given priority to guide the practice.   Once this point is reached, we will seem to consent vero ex his quae sunt propria rei (that is, we will view our thought and our speech as corresponding to what is, as adaequatio mentis ad rem), and we will view the objects of desire as “quemdam ordinem ad hoc consequentem .” ^(F2) Hence theoretical knowledge will be a necessary –and mysteriously, as pointed out by Hume, even sufficient condition–for successful action.

How does all this refer to Polanyi ? What Polanyi shows is that if knowledge is knowledge of object by what is in the mind being a replica of what is the case, then, pace Aristotle’s, practical knowledge is not knowledge at all. Indeed, the experienced agent doesn’t see objectively in his mind an objective image of the object . And even if he saw that, the objective object wouldn’t prompt any action. What actually happens is that the experienced agent has gained a practical internalization of the object that under the appropriate circumstances, that is, under the appropriate physical and teleological situation of the agent in respect of the object, makes possible for him to act in the appropriate way to obtain the desired goal.

But, let us insist, is this practical internalization not a faithful, objective reproduction of the object? Most certainly not. Consider the case of the baby and the nipple. First of all, the vision of the nipple is the result of the satisfaction of  desire, not the other way around. Second, the vision of the nipple that prompts action is not objective nipple, but desired nipple with a practical disposition to suck it, much as the vision of a woman that provokes an erection is not objective woman but desired woman with a practical disposition to posses her. Thus the vision functions as a call for action, and the internalized experience of the experienced agent functions, under the appropriate circumstances, as a map for successful action to satisfy the desire (or to achieve the goal when, thanks to language, end and means have been separated). Of course, at a later stage of development this internalized map need not be the result of personal experience, for one can learn from other people’s experience thanks to linguistic teaching-learning. But even in this case, as Aristotle suggested, experience is needed, under the form of theoretical problems repeatedly solved so as to come to ‘see’ the object appropriately under the appropriate circumstances. And when it is a matter of skills, only the exercise of actual driving or cooking or playing the piano, which results in internalization or practical appropriation, does the trick that all the rules learned by heart won’t do. And once one has developed the skill, the rules are superfluous, for one doesn’t follow them. Indeed,  one can learn the skill without the rules, as all genuine inventors or discoverers do. The rules come later and are abstracted from the practice of the prudentes, the expert phronimoi, in the particular field. However, whereas the prudentes in the natural sciences don’t need to be good, for desire plays no part, since they deal with the object ex his quae sunt propria rei, in the field of ethics the object is object of desire or aversion and this involves the dispositio audientis. Moreover, as observed by Aristotle, there are things that always happen in the same way and things that don’t, and this results in disciplines that have or don’t have epistata as their objects. And, then, there are sciences and arts, the latter of which are ‘productive.’ And, among the latter, those that are productive of external effects or of immanent action. And, among the latter, those which are ethical and those which are not.

All this reminds us of Aristotle’s remarks on the role of “experience” in practical knowledge, and on the superiority of the practical internalization over the theoretical knowledge of the ‘universal,’ and on the actual superfluousness of the knowledge of the universal (that is, the merely theoretical knowledge of the rule). All this should have led Aristotle to the definite conclusion, which I don’t think he ever reached, THAT THEORETICAL KNOWLEDGE OF WHAT IS THE CASE IS NOT A REQUIRED CONDITION FOR PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE, AND THAT PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE IS NOT REALLY KNOWLEDGE AS HE UNDERSTOOD IT, FOR IT IS IN NO WAY GRASP OF WHAT IS OBJECTIVELY THE CASE.

It is this clear realization concerning the hard natural sciences that is nowadays taking place, but without the dismissal of the notion of knowledge as specular objective representation.

One thing seems clear. The Greeks of whom I am speaking, whose aim was practical --to act in the way appropriate to a human being-- included desire of what is good. But Plato thought that the Good was absolute, an eternal and immutable supreme Form or Idea, whereas Aristotle came to realize that the good was species-relative –what was genuinely desirable for human beings-- and he concentrated on the good for the human being. He named it happiness, and assumed that the name was univocal for human beings. He defined or gave it relatively concrete content according to his best lights, which of course happened to be the lights one would expect from an Athenian of his times who had internalized the Athenian ethos and who was reflective and very articulate. Aiming at those multiple goods and, with luck, achieving them reasonably well in a polis that favored the development of people disposed by training and habit to pursue those goods, was Aristotle’s answer to the philosophical quest inaugurated by Socrates.

Aristotle didn’t doubt that his account would be persuasive to any well-trained adult. And that, if that adult, was not akratic and was experienced enough and intelligent enough, that adult –with luck and guided by that account– would make his or her choices so as to be virtuous and happy. But does one really need to know Aristotle’s Ethics and Politics to be virtuous? This question is aptly raised by Devereux, who obviously thinks that the answer is negative. But, in that case, Aristotle’s Ethics and Politics are not practical sciences, in that the implicit precepts are mere general rules of thumb and their (theoretical) knowledge is not required.

There seems to be a connection between what I just said and the status of the scientific disciplines, that is, the sciences and the arts of which Aristotle speaks. The scientific disciplines are constituted by the ‘universals’ or, what is equivalent, the ‘particulars’ understood as general types (see Devereux). But these universals or particulars enter into the Aristotelian sciences or the arts as truths that represent what is the case, not as intelligent dispositions to act in a certain way, which the agent chooses and to which he  personally  consents as the appropriate response. As Aristotle suggests, only the “experienced” agent, the genuine expert, will be able to activate in the appropriate way and in the right circumstances the disposition to act. However, while in the natural sciences as such the disposition to act doesn’t require that the agent be good because tghe laws for action are  drawn ex his quae sunt propria rei to the systematic exclusion of the dispositions of the agent, and therefore interested or moral deliberation as to the means is not required, the case is different concerning the deliberation as to ends and the means in what Nussbaum calls “the stochastic sciences,” such as economics and medicine and navigation, and even more so in ethics and politics concerning the deliberation and choice as to the end and means.

In short, what I want to say is: (1) That all disciplines are potentially practical or applicative, and that it is a bad mistake to take their doctrines or theories or ‘laws” as representational of what is. They are to be understood by desirous agents experienced in the field and should result in appropriate action. (2) That the cultural and personal identity and the concrete situation of the desirous agent who applies the law irremediably count. (3) That given the multiplicity of human goods and of the identities and situations of different agents, the conflict among goods is unavoidable.

Now, it is obvious that the list of Christian virtues and the list of Aristotelian virtues do not coincide. Aristotle had no idea of the infused theological virtues, and even such moral virtues as humility would have no place in Aristotle’s list of virtues. Furthermore, the ultimate end of human life, which according to the scholastics is the beatific vision of the Trinitarian God, of whom the Son became man and suffered and died, is totally alien to Aristotle. This radical difference was the inebriating discovery of those thirteenth century students of philosophy who realized that the Aristotelian human life and the austere Christian life didn’t coincide at all and who wanted to live by the light of unbaptized Aristotelian reason rather than of revelation. In other words, the Christian’s practical self-understanding in the scheme of things, whose center is the Trinitarian God, is radically different from Aristotle’s, and it results in a totally different conception of human life and of its ultimate goal. Moreover, no abstract formulation will result by itself  in  actual Christian living, because experience –even communitarian experience within the Church– is open-ended and changes with the times in which the culturally-shaped agents have to make their decisions.   Which brings me to the point I want most to emphasize, to wit: the difference between the Greeks and us Christians because of what we usually call “Revelation” – the non-deductible, historical fact that God encountered us as one of us and talked to us desirous agents about Himself and us. Augustine was not wrong, indeed, when he said that the thing he missed in “philosophy” was Jesus-Christ.

Theory and Story

Let us explore the contrast between a theory and a story, particularly a historical narrative in which one is included, which has a deep analogy with the contrast between Greek rationalism  and Biblical historism . This contrast is suggested to me by Frei’s study of the migration of the narrative, religious reading of the Bible to the objectivist, rationalistic reading of the Bible, the latter of which readings became canonical in the Western world, due to the Greek philosophical heritage combined with the misinterpretation of the triumph of the natural sciences.   

A theory is like a map, a regional diagram of the object ex his quae sunt propria rei. Of course it is object seen or grasped by human beings and it is to be read or interpreted by desirous human beings. But, as is the case with maps, a theory doesn’t tell you where you stand in relation to the mapped and what you should do. A theory is not you, it is an instrument you use. As Aristotle realized, you have to find how to apply the theory to the particular circumstances, and this includes your desires. This may be presupposed by the theory, which after all is the result of the practice of practitioners and meant to be applied by future practitioners, but is not itself comprehended in the theory. In this sense, the theory is objective.

A story, though, doesn’t speak to you in the same way as a theory. You don’t agree or disagree with it. You are or you are not part of it, you have to situate yourself within it or out of it.

A theory is not you; it is an instrument you use at will . But your body is you, with its accumulated experience, its habits, its desires, its projects –all of which may imply a certain conception of the world and of your place in it, but you do not have or need that conception for you are its subjective side.
