12 - Significance of book and differences from other books

I wrote this textbook of introduction to philosophy because I could not find one that took students at the level they actually are when they reach college and engaged them in the kind of reflection capable of helping them to gain a personal sense of orientation in the maze of life. After all, this very practical result is what Socrates pursued in his attempt to lead an "examined life." Unfortunately, academic philosophy has become the most purely theoretical of the disciplines. And I hardly think that the approach ordinarily followed by introductory textbooks, which reduces philosophy to history of philosophy by treating it either as a series of theoretical problems or as a study of classical texts in chronological order, is apt to lead college students to realize the practical significance of the philosophical endeavor and to engage in it fruitfully.

The only somewhat similar book to mine in today's market is Leslie Stevenson's Seven Theories of Human Nature (Clarendon Press, Oxford 1974; approximate price $10.00). Though not explicitly conceived as an introduction to philosophy, Stevenson's work, like mine, is meant for beginners and focuses on the human being. Moreover, among the seven theories of human nature Stevenson studies are Skinner's, Sartre's and Marx's, which are three of the four philosophies I deal with in my book.

In spite of these similarities, the two books are very different. For one, I believe (quite immodestly, of course) that my presentation of each of the philosophies I deal with is more accessible and lively than Stevenson's. This is due, in part, to my systematic avoidance of unnecessary philosophical jargon, to the concrete examples I give to illustrate abstract concepts, and to the way I use Huxley's novel Brave New World to show that different philosophies result in different ways of living. But the real difference between the two books is much more than a matter of style; my book has a dynamic unity that Stevenson's book lacks. Indeed, To Be Human is designed in such a way as to help the student to reach a personal, critical, coherent set of convictions by reference to which it is possible to conduct one's life. While Stevenson's book offers just a succession of world-views, I present different philosophies in such a way that the study of one naturally leads to the next as its critical counterpart or as its needed complement. As a result, the student is forced into a critical process whose final outcome is the adoption of a personal philosophy, which is the whole point of the philosophical endeavor.  

10 - Abstract

To Be Human is underpinned by the conviction that philosophy is a practical, not a theoretical discipline. What people look for when they philosophize is a philosophy by which they can live because it satisfies their critical sense after careful personal reflection. This book attempts to engage college students in the reflective process required to attain that end.

A philosophy amounts to a particular understanding of oneself within the world in which one has, willy-nilly, to take one's risks. This is why To Be Human focuses on the human being. It confronts the student with four complete, significantly different philosophies: Skinner's, Sartre's, Marx's, and Maslow's. The choice of contemporary rather than ancient philosophies is meant to convey to the student the insight that

philosophies have a powerful, practical impact on the very world in which he or she lives. The four philosophies with which this book deals are presented in such a way that the study of one naturally leads to the next as its critical counterpart or as its needed complement. Aldous Huxley's novel Brave New World is used throughout the book both as a means of making palpable that different philosophies have different and very practical consequences, and as a means of furnishing the student with a concrete model to start testing the validity of the particular philosophy he or she feels tempted to adopt.
