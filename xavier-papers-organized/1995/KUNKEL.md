\wp\monas\kunkel November 12, 1994

CIT - REPLY TO KUNKEL

I have been friends with Joe Kunkel for as long as I have taught at UD, more than 28 years. Though sometimes, perhaps even often, in disagreement with each other, Joe and I have a very comfortable relationship. This is due to many factors which are not pertinent in the present context, but one is: we have for each other the  respect that arises from the realization that the other's convictions are really convictions, not mere façade.  

Before te public reading of his recent contribution to the ongoing discussion on the CIT Joe honored me by asking my advise. This shows, I think, the trust we have in each other. I told him that there was much in his paper with which I disagreed, but that I thought that his contribution was needed both as a blunt expression of important reservations concerning the CIT which might be representative of what a number of people think and which should be faced squarely, and as a reminder that the question of the identity of a Catholic university is not an abstract and purely theoretical problem--and should not be discussed as if it were--for it has possible implications for freedom of speech, academic freedom, etc.

I believe, Joe, that you really meant what you said in your paper: "my intent is not to raise heckles for the purpose of stirring controversy but... to elicit honest feedback." My feedback to you, which may be no less blunt than your contribution, is conceived in exactly the same spirit. Let us therefore do our best for our part so that our conversation does not lead to a polarizing polemic in which I have no desire to participate, but rather helps us to advance a little, if possible, toward a shared understanding of what the CIT in a Catholic university might mean.     

A last remark before coming to your paper. Not only will I be speaking as a Catholic, but as a Catholic speaking to a fellow Catholic. Thus I will appeal to you in ways in which I would not, and could not, appeal to a non-Catholic partner in the conversation on the CIT and the identity of a Catholic university.

----------------------

Your contribution touched on so many complex issues that to deal more or less adequately with each of them would demand much more time than we have available and more expertise than I possess. I will not aim at giving a complete answer to each of your points, much less at comment only those points that seem to me more important at least some of them, if only superficially. But first I want to make some remarks of a general character.

(1) Ventilating your frustrations with the Catholic hierarchy

You began your paper with the following characterization: "The Catholic intellectual tradition implies an established Truthfulness (with a capital T) about the Catholic Church's teachings and doctrines..." By contrast to this, you defined your own position: "My difference with the Catholic intellectual tradition.... stems from my understanding of the fallibility of the church teachings." From there you proceeded to deal with such things as the natural law tradition of argument in matters of morals, the disciplinary measure of dismissing Father Curran from a tenured position at a Catholic University, the refusal of certain Catholic Universities (UD included) to interview Fr. Curran for a teaching job, the interdiction of one bishop to priests and religious to participate in civil rights demonstrations, the intensity of the participation of UD through UDRI in military research, etc. Given your opening words concerning the Catholic intellectual tradition and your own position, the unavoidable implication is that these are things that the Church proposes as infallible and that you consider fallible, and that our faith, as understood by the Church, demands the same kind and the same degree of agreement with, and obedience to, any pronouncement or position taken by a member of the Church's hierarchy as if each and all of them were infallible.

I honestly cannot believe, Joe, that you think that the things I just mentioned are considered in the Church and by the Church infallible teachings or positions. However, by implying the contrary, or at least by expressing yourself so poorly on such a serious matter, you projected and contributed to reinforce a false image of our Church that may be common among those who are not Catholics and perhaps among certain Catholics who lack the most elementary Catholic culture. I am sure that you did not mean to be unfair to the Church, but you were. And I do not think that this kind of careless rhetoric can help us to see our way more clearly in matters related to the Catholic intellectual tradition and to the identity of Catholic universities.

(2) The Church and philosophy

Third indication. Not only do you fail to take into consideration this obligation of fidelity to Christ on the Church's part, but you seem to think that Church's teachings, at least in matters of morals, are derived not from its faith in Christ and its will to remain true to Him, but from a philosophical system, which you identify with natural law ethics. Actually, you take this so much for granted that you even seem to expect that the teaching Church either demonstrate to philosophers that natural law ethics is the only acceptable system, or that the Church yield to the arguments of those who favor other ethical systems or ethical pluralism. Indeed, this is what you write in this connection:

"If Church teachings are to be intellectual they have to reach out to philosophers, and in particular to Catholic and Christian philosophers. Unfortunately to my knowledge  there are only three applied philosophers who develop their positions out of a natural law position...

"Ethics in philosophy today is pluralistic... the Kantian duties tradition... the consequentialist tradition... a social contract position, an emerging ethics of care... also splinter groups... The Church may prefer to write off the reasoning of philosophers, but it does so, I believe, at its own peril" (pp. 2-3).

It is unclear to me, in the first place, what you expect the Church to do in the very chaotic situation you describe of disagreement among philosophers in matters of purely rational justification  of moral practices. You warn the Church not to write off the reasoning of philosophers. At the same time you blame the Church for using in certain of her official documents the idiom of natural law ethics, which is, as you rightly suggest, the idiom of one philosophical school among others. But how is the Church to enter in dialogue with philosophers in their own sphere, instead of writing them off? Can the Church speak to intellectuals without using one or another of the philosophical idioms available, be it that of natural law, or of Kantian duties, or of consequentialism, or of ethics of care? Would you be more satisfied if her attempt to talk to intellectuals the Church adopted the idiom of a different philosophical school rather than that of natural law? And would all other philosophers be equally satisfied, given that the first of their disagreements concerns the appropriate idiom for ethical matters, and this is why, by your own avowal, "ethics today is pluralistic"?  You say that the Church would ignore at its own peril the reasoning of philosophers. But  what is " the reasonig of philosophers" you are talking about, when philosophers themselves are in deep disagreement with each other, acording to your own description, and when the world at large (insofar as it hears what philosolphers have to say in mostly unintelligible terms) finds them as confused and divided in ethical matters as non-philosophers are? This, it seems to me, is another case in which your exasperation with the hierarchy of the Church induces you to argue unfairly.

But perhaps the gist of your objection is that you see the Church committed to a particular philosophical school from which it derives her teaching. If so, I believe you are wrong. The Church derives her teachings from the Gospel, not from a philosophical system. When talking to intellectuals, the Church in certain documents uses the idiom of natural law ethics because she finds it more easily intelligible and capable of conveying her moral teachings. But you and any other Catholic philosopher are perfectly entitled to use a different idiom, as long as you preserve the teaching. One can, for instance, argue against lying in terms of Kantian duties or of ethics of care or ethics of social contract or of natural law and be in perfect agreement with the Church's teaching on lying, though the argument in each case would be different.

3) The teaching authority of the Church

I have attempted to show you that, concerning a number of particular points, your exasperation with the Church because she teaches them as infallibly true or demands absolute obedience is ungrounded. There are however other more substantive points on which you disagree with the Church. They regard matters of sexual morality--"use of contraceptives, labeling homosexuality as unnatural, forbidding in-vitro fertilization, and rejecting divorce except by annulment" (p. 2)--as well as one issue regarding gender equality, namely, the exclusion of women from the priesthood. Now, Joe, you would have been even more uptodaty if you had added to your menu the right of homosexuals to Catholic marriage, the free acceptance of premarital sex, perhaps even the right of married people to discrete love affairs when unhappily married and not wanting to divorce for the sake of the children or for economic reasons, as well as the right to abortion before three months after pregnancy. Maybe you do not want the Church to go that far. But what if other less conservative people than you do want the Church to go that far because they find themselves in as deep a disagreement with the Church on these points and feel as strongly about them as you feel about yours? If you are exasperated by the "conservative hierarchy" with whom as a Catholic intellectual you do not want to be "gridlocked," on what basis would you propose that the Church refrain from changing her moral teachings to accommodate the demands of those who are more liberal than you?

Of course, you may answer that the Church's faith as well as reason impose limits beyond which the Church simply cannot go. The Church agrees with you. In fact, it is precisely out of fidelity to the Gospel as they see it that those entrusted by Christ to interpret the Gospel authoritatively and to guide the entire Church have so far abstained from complying with your demands and those of others more liberal than you. Curiously enough, not once in your paper do you show the slightest awareness that this may be so.  Might it not be, Joe, that your problem is not so much with this or that Catholic teaching as  with the teaching authority of the Church . Indeed, for all we know from your paper, it is as if you had no notionpersonal stake in that fidelity and had no sense of the importance of that trait in the identity of the Church in which you believe and of which you are a member. Yet we Catholics believe in the Apostolic Church. In other words, for us it is vital to keep the continuity of our faith with the faith of the primitive Church, with the faith transmitted by the Apostles and as sanctioned by the Apostles. knWhy do I suspect this, in spite of the fact that you yourself say "I have always respected the Church's teachings and I dissent from them only after careful reflection"? (p. 1).

One thing you do not say is why you respect the Church's teachings. Do you respect them as a Catholic ? Let us see. A Catholic's respect for the Church's teachings rests on such faith convictions as that we all Catholics, from the Pope down, are called and committed by our baptism to think and live as Christians, and that the bishops headed by the Pope and the Pope as head of the Church have a special Christ-given mission, and a corresponding Christ-given gift, to guide us in this regard. Now, if these faith convictions are not operative in us, the only reason we can possibly have to respect the Church's teachings is the same as the one non-Catholics may have, namely, whether the particular teachings make sense to them or not. And those teachings that don't, deserve no respect.

Well, Joe, I must say that in all your paper I see no positive indication that your respect for the Church's teachings differs from the respect that non-Catholics may have, and I see positive indications that it does not differ at all.

First indication, the exasperation with the teaching Church that transpires through your entire paper. When, after considering it, a particular teaching does not make sense to you, you automatically think that the Church should agree with you rather than the other way around; and if the Church does not change that teaching to tailor it to what makes sense to you, or does not change it immediately just because it does not make sense to you, you find the Church authoritarian.  

Second indication. Nowhere in your paper is there even an indirect allusion to your acceptance of the Church's obligation of fidelity to Christ, from which stem the authoritative (not authoritarian, at least not for a Catholic) character of the Church's teaching and the cautiousness with which the Church is obliged to proceed in her teaching.

In the second place, Joe, you seem to me to be confused about the identity of the Church and, consequently, about the character of its magisterium. If the Church connstituted a philosophical school of thought and its teaching in matters of morality were the result of philosophical reasoning, you would be right in refusing your allegiance to the Church's moral teaching whenever you have what you consider good philosophical reasons to think otherwise, as well as in demanding that either the Church persuade you with philosophical reasons or change its teaching for the philosophical reasons you find convincing. However, the Church is no philosophical school, and her views on how Christians ought to conduct themselves are not derived from philosophical theories--such as Kantian duties, consequentialism, social contract, not even natural law--but from her understanding of Jesus-Christ and his teachings and from a reflection on her own history in her attempt to remain faithful to Jesus-Christ while responding to changing circumstances. In other words, for the hierarchy of the Church as well as for us as members of the Church, the point is not whether a certain form of conduct makes or does not make sense according to a particular way of deriving obligations from a philosophical theory, or whether it fits better the temper of the times and the generally approved customs of a particular region at a given time, but whether it squares with Jesus-Christ's demands and the extent to which it is conducive to his service.  No dout, in this discernment a certain experiential understanding of what is possible and good for us as human beings is involved, but the ultimate determining criterion as to whether something is possible and good for us is not that it appears to be so according to our experiential understanding, but whether it conforms to Christ's teachings. [ In addition, we are nowadays aware of how culturally relative is the definition of what is possible and good for us as human beings. But to this I will come in a moment ]. And, no doubt, in this discernment of whether a certain conduct is compatible with Christ's demands and conducive to his service all the faithful, members of the hierarchy or not, are expected to participate according to their respective gifts and abilities. But, given the nature of the discernment (which is precisely about the conformity to...), their gifts and abilities are not, and cannot be, measured exclusively or even primarily in terms of intellectual brightness and professional skills, but in terms of the persons' allegiance to Jesus-Christ as encountered in the Catholic Apostolic Church, that is, in the Church spread throughout the entire world ( kata-holos ) and rooted across time in the faith of the Apostles. In other words, the professional skills of theologians and philosophers and economists and sociologists, etc., are welcome for the purpose of this discernment, but only when subordinated to the allegiance to Jesus-Christ. Furthermore, though all the faithful are expected to participate in this discernment according to their gifts and abilities, the ultimate decision, when and if the decision is to reach ultimacy and be accepted beyond dispute, is not left to the absolute majority of those who acknowledge themselves as members of the Church, not even to the absolute majority of those who take very seriously the conformity of a practice to the teachings of Jesus-Christ and to the conduciveness of the practice to his service, but to those to whom the direction of his Church was entrusted by Jesus-Christ, that is, to the bishops headed by the Pope.

This, Joe, is the tradition of faith that justifies and explains in our own eyes the respect we owe to the Church's teachings in matters of faith and morals, not the extent to which those teachings happen to agree or disagree with what makes sense to an individual, to a cultural group, or to a group of philosophers. And precisely because all the members of the Church, according to their gifts and abilities, are expected to contribute to the discernment of which I have been speaking and in the manner in which I have spoken, no teaching is infallible before the discernement is completed, and only when it is definitively completed.

Another point.

You end your paper asking for "a dialogue on each of the issues I have raised," and finish with the following words:

"As a lay group of intellectuals within the Church we can either forge new ground ahead of the increasingly conservative hierarchy or we can follow in lock step behind. If we choose to follow the hierarchy let us put to rest any idea of a truly Catholic intellectual tradition" (pp. 5-6).

I find your self-righteousness and lack of self-criticism a little suspect, when you are so suspicious and so critical of the hierarchy. Apparently, nothing good can be expected from the Catholic hierarchy, for they lack moral sensitivity and genuine moral concern, whereas you and those who agree with you supposedly posess both in high degree, and the positions you espouse, because you espouse them, need to be the focus of the university dialogue. Of course, you do not say this in so many words. But why are you so negative about the Catholic hierarchy if not because you think that nothing good can be expected from them? And why are you so convinced that the issues you raise and the positions you espouse are the ones that deserve attention, even immediate attention, if not because they represent the concerns of the truly morally concerned people?

  I would not pay much attention to this aspect of your talk, which giving you the benefit of the doubt could be attributed to careless rhetoric, were I not convinced that, because it is representative of a rather widespread attitude, it deserves to be discussed carefully. Practically all your substantive complaints are 'politically correct,' that is, they are popular among the members of the American academia, be they Catholic or not. (Take, for instance, the complaint about the exclusion of women from the priesthood. It did not originate) . Whatever the merits or demerits of each of your demands, the fact that they are popular shows to what extent our views tend to be shaped by our cultural environment. Your demands are not deduced or derived from a philosophical theory . Any and each of your demands can be argued for in terms of any of the philosophical schools you mention. How come, if, as you unhesitatingly assume, the concern of those who espouse the causes you espouse is really about what is morally right, rather than about what is popular or acceptable today in our cultural environment or about what is in their own interest, that Americans in general, Catholics and non-Catholics, find the freedom of the market so sacred--"what the market bears," in the consecrated phrase--, and that the American Bishops letter on minimal economic rights fell on deaf ears inside and outside the Church? Where were the vociferous pro-choice as well as the anti-abortionists, the feminists, the defensors of the homosexuals, all those people supposedly concerned about what is morally right, when it was a matter not of supporting what today is popular within their circle or what is in the interest of the particular group for which they care, but of supporting, or at least giving a hearing to, a position that is not popular because it goes against the grain of what they are used to and because it might hurt their interests and their pockets? And why, then, would the position or positions they espouse and their set priorities constitute the paradigm of morality?   

The purport of this reflection, Joe, is to tell you that you might gain something by applying to your own stands the dialectics of suspicion that to you so ruthlessly apply to the Catholic hierarchy  and to those Catholics who disagree with you. Your self-righteousness is surprising, at least to me. And the lack of self-criticism on the part of someone who by profession should be acutely aware of the insidious influence of the culture on its members' convictions and attitudes as well in moral matters as in everything else is not very comforting. I am not telling you that you do not care for the poor, for I know that you do. I am telling you that the fact that you did not include this concern, which obviously is not popular among the supposedly morally concerned Americans, among the concerns you expressed in your paper carries its own message. Perhaps, after all--just perhaps--the Catholic hierarchy is not as obtuse and morally insensitive as you assume, and perhaps--just perhaps--the Catholic hierarchy is not wrong in not automatically agreeing with your stands and your priorities, because perhaps--just perhaps--your stands and priorities do not necessarily constitute the unshakeable, indisputable standard of morality by which the validity of the stands and priorities of the rest of the world, and particularly of the Catholic Church, are to be determined.

In fact, I have news for you. The Church does not use natural law in the way you think she does, for she understands human nature from the revelation of which she is the depositary and by which her members are regulated. As Pascal said (pp. 145 and 148 of Krailsheimer's translation of Pensées) the conception  of human nature, of human good, of virtue, is, in the case of Christianity, religion-dependent.

Now, what makes many of the stands you espouse popular today is precisely that they are secular , that they have nothing to do with Christianity; on the contrary, they are supposed to offer an alternative to Christianity and bypass it by proposing a supposedly neutral standard, valid and obligatory for all rational creatures, that eliminates from consideration the demands of God as such, since what is valid in God's demands will be included in the system. In fact, the ARGUMENTS GIVEN APPEAL TO the abstract equality of individuals invented by the Enlightenment, which supposedly yields universal human rights while at the same time justifying factual inequalities in the name of freedom, and which imposes obligations in the name of that equality without motivating people to act in  that way.  

Now, you may rightly rejoin that what you propose is a dialogue on your stands, not the imposition of your stands on others. But the dialogue you propose is itself suspect. For not only do you demand that the dialogue be on your issues, that is, on the stands where you find yourself at odds with the Church, but you also assume that the dialogue should be on your terms . And what are your terms? The terms of "reason," not the terms of faith . Why the terms of reason? Because they are neutral. But are they, really? How come, then, that your agenda coincides so well with the agenda and the interests of privileged Americans? Indeed, your issues happen to constitute the popular agenda of the privileged in the American culture today.

Well, I have already told you why part of the dialogue should be on the issues that should constitute the agenda of the dialogue, lest those issues be excluded which are not popular either with you or with the majority. Why, we all know that we could get a very large crowd of students to discuss whether condoms should be made available to them and their sexual partners within the campus of this university, whereas medical insurance for those Americans who have no hope of affording it and who will never make it to higher education to acquire a face and a voice, and therefore to exist and participate and count in the university discussion of the supposedly morally concerned people, would not get much of an audience.   

A last point . Not only do you think that the Church's positions are as good as the arguments by which they are supported, and that the Church is a philosophical school, which you identify with natural law ethics, in free competition in the market of ideas with other philosophical schools, but you imagine that reason--the faculty supposedly at work in the university (and certainly not at work in the Church's deliberations)-- is a neutral disinterested faculty that reaches positions on human and ethical matters on the basis of objective evidence and judges the positions taken by people from a superior, neutral, and unassailable standpoint. Now this, precisely, is the conception of reason taken for granted, consecrated, and popularized by the thinkers of the Enlightenment , which yields the static conception of human nature as the multiplication of the selfsame identical concept of the individual who is neither man nor woman, neither rich nor poor, neither Catholic nor Protestant nor atheist, neither American nor French nor Chinese... In addition, this is the conception of reason by which today's commonly accepted views (which you definitely share) of academic freedom and of the university are shaped . Inded, given this view view of reason, the university is thought of as the legitimate free market of ideas, for it is the only arena in which views compete with each other and are accepted or discarded, taken or rejected, exclusively on the basis of evidence that leads to rational assent or dissent. Correlatively, academic freedom is seen as the necessary  mecanism by which it is insured that no other interest than the disinterested desire to know prevails among those whose positions compete, so that the evidence by which each position is supported be the only operative factor to determine the truth.

It is only because you share these views that you find unconscionable the Church's dismissal of Father Curran from teaching in a pontifical university. Given your conception of reason and of the university, what the Church should do, in case of disagreement with Curran on a particular position, is to submit her own and his position to the neutral tribunal of reason and to determine the case exclusively on the basis of evidence. Now, aside from the fact that you mistake the Church for a philosophical school, whereas the Church is not a philosophical school at all, much less a philosophical school among others, it is obvious that your solution would not work. Are you not aware that different philosophers reach different conclusions regarding, say, abortion? Well, contrary to what you seem to assume, the reason is not because they do not reach their respective and mutually incompatible positions on the basis of evidence, but because what counts as evidence for each of them is paradigm-dependent and they have different ethical paradigms. That evidence is paradigm-dependent seems to be the case even in the natural sciences, as Kuhn argued so persuasively already many years ago. But most certainly it is the case in matters of practical reason, which by the same token cannot be strictly scientific, that is, cannot yield universal laws, as Aristotle remarked many more years ago. Indeed, ethics is not a discipline in which disinterested reason, if such a thing existed, could function at all, because only those beings who have a definite interest in being happy can  engage in ethical reflection, and all those who engage in ethical reflection are already precommitted to a particular (though often implicit) way of being happy, for otherwise they would not have made any decision before they start philosophizing. Now, suppose that someone who engages in ethical reflection has acted all her life on the implicit conviction that whatever is conducive to her personal well-being as she feels it is the way to be happy. Obviously this person will see abortion as good, and will see as evidence for it the very fact that abortion is not conducive to her well-being, while the fact that abortion is conducive to one's well-being will not be persuasive evidence for somebody who conceives of happiness in a different way. This is to say that in ethical matters there is no disinterested reflection, that a reason not interested in being happy would have nothing to say, much less to decide, about what is good or bad, right or wrong, and that one cannot be interested in being happy without being interested in being happy in a particular, concrete way . This is to say that what counts as evidence for one's particular judgments is paradigm-dependent, that is, dependent on what one actually pursues (and at least implicitly conceives as) happiness .

Of course, paradigm-changes, that is, individual conversions, are possible in ethics as they are in physics, though for very different reasons, but they are as infrequent as they are in the natural sciences. Furthermore, as in the natural sciences, the shifts of pradigms are not, and cannot be, rational in the sense of depending upon evidence, since by definition evidence is paradigm-dependent, that is, can only obtain within a paradigm. If you want to see, Joe, to what extent evidence is paradigm-dependent and how difficult it is to change paradigms, you have only to think of how natural you find it to offer the dismissal of Father Curran as a convincing piece of evidence of the obtuseness of the Church. Indeed, for you it is natural and convincing because what counts for evidence is paradigm-dependent. For you it is a fundamental, a priori, paradigmatic assumption--what the scholastics used to call a first principle--that reason is a neutral, disinterested faculty that judges on the basis of objective, that is, not-paradigm-dependent, evidence, and that the university is consequently the right place for the Church to test the validity of its ethical positions by submitting them to the verdict of those who dream like you (and probably Curran, too) that their judgments are disinterested and based on objective evidence. For those of us who find your paradigm naive and ultimately deficient and who have a very different view of reason and of how universities actually work, the Church's action in Curran's case not only is not a piece of evidence of the obtuseness of the Church but rather of her practical lucidity.
