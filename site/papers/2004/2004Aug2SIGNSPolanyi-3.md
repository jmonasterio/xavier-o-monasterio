Preface on Meaning

- All signs mean, but not all signs mean linguistically, for not all signs are linguistic signs to be interpreted and understood..

- As Searle says, the characteristic effect of meaning is understanding.

But the understanding typical of linguistic meaning, which is the one Searle has in mind, is the result of deciphering or interpreting the meaning of the sign. This type of understanding of linguistic meaning, however,  is not the characteristic effect of non- linguistic signs,. In particular, it is not the characteristic effect of perceptual signs..Perceptual signs are signals-FOR action when connected with a desire on the addressee’s part. Consequently, their characteristic effect is the performance of the action signaled. at the appropriate perceptual time, which can only be determined by  the situated exposed agent..

- Linguistic signs are signs for conceiving what is said. Consequently, what is directly or immediately meant by them is a concept, not a reality– a concept of the conventional meaning connoted by the words used.

- Linguistic signs denote or refer to reality only when the perceptual signal is being encountered in presence by the agent acting in pursuit of a goal. If I am looking for a hammer and you tell me, “The hammer is in the next room,” what you tell me is actionable information, a signal-For going to the next room to get the hammer, and this action is the appropriate understanding of the signal, for the signal is a rule for immediate action, not a sign-OF a meaning to be interpreted.

Searle’s enunciative model of knowledge: Knowledge and verbalization

In The Tacit Dimension, Michael Polanyi introduced the notion of “tacit knowledge,” by which he became known, to refer to those cases in which a speaker “knows more than he can verbalize.” The assumption underlying the ordinary notion of knowledge is that knowledge is equivalent to verbalization, for otherwise there would be no reason for Polanyi to introduce the notion of tacit or not verbalized knowledge as an alternative conception of knowledge.

Polany explained tacit knowledge with the concrete example of the experiment conducted by psychologists Lazarus and McCleary in 1949.

“These authors presented a person with a large number of nonsense syllables, and after showing certain of the syllables, they administered an electric shock. Presently the person showed symptoms of anticipating the shock at the sight of ‘shock syllables’; yet, on questioning, he could not identify them. He had come to know when to expect a shock, but he could not tell what made him expect it. He had acquired a knowledge similar to that which we have when we know a person by signs we cannot tell.” ^(F2)

But why would knowledge be necessarily equated with its verbal formulation? The objection that John Searle would raise to the very notion of tacit knowledge explains why. Indeed, Searle would claim that the very notion of tacit or not verbalized knowledge is nonsensical, for in that case the subject who supposedly knows is not aware of what he knows, since he cannot say even to himself what he knows. Now, an unconscious knowledge seems to be an oxymoron. Why? Because the very notion of knowledge, or at least the ordinary notion of knowledge that common sense seems to impose on us, implies on the subject’s part the awareness of what he knows. He knows what he knows thanks to the awareness he has of what he knows, so that he would not know what he knows without the enunciative awareness of it.

As an example of this ordinary notion of knowledge , which seems so commonsensical, let us consider the knowledge of which Searle spoke in the preceding chapter, since he used the word ‘knowledge’ in its ordinary meaning. According to Searle, in linguistic communication the hearer who understands what the speaker says thereby acquires the knowledge  –that is, becomes aware– of what the speaker intends to communicate to him. And it would be inconceivable that the hearer knew what the speaker intends to communicate to him if the hearer had no awareness at all of what he knows. In other words, what a subject knows becomes object known to him by the very fact of his becoming aware of it. Hence no knowledge of object without awareness of what is known, because knowledge implies and demands the awareness of the object known. Thus, when Polanyi defines tacit knowledge (from now on TK) as a knowledge in which the subject cannot say what he supposedly knows, we have to conclude – Searle would argue– that TK is a knowledge in which the subject is not aware of what he knows, which is a contradiction in terms.  

For his part, Polanyi would reply to this objection to what he calls “tacit knowledge” by referring us to the undeniable facts of the experiment he described: the subject of the experiment cannot say which are the shock-producing syllables because he does not know them, that is, is not aware of them, since he cannot say which are t hose syllables. Yet, though the subject cannot say which are the shock-producing syllables, his BEHAVIOR in reaction to those syllables when they appear on the screen shows that he knows them in a certain way which does not involve verbalization –and because what is involved in this certain way of knowing does not involve verbalization of what is known Polanyi calls it TK.

Linguistic communication

Speech Acts –the book of Searle to which I have referred– is a study of the way in which language functions. That is why its subtitle is “An Essay in the Philosophy of Language.” And Searle deems language’s primary, though not unique, f unction to be the communication of what the speaker intends to communicate to the hearer through the enunciation of it, for, according  to him, language is the unique system of signs that say what they mean. Now, if language is essentially communicative of what the speaker intends to communicate, and communication obtains through enunciation of what is known by the speaker, it follows that language is primarily and essentially enunciative, which is exactly Searle’s conception of language, as we shall see.

For his part, Polanyi would respond that Searle is attending exclusively to the function of language in linguistic communication. Now, it is obvious, that communication of what one knows or is aware of demands the verbal enunciation by the subject who knows of what he knows. However, what Searle assumes is that before communicating to others what he knows the speaker has to enunciate to himself what he knows, which implies that all consciousness of object is necessarily linguistic-enunciative. But that is neither obvious nor indisputable. Actually, Polanyi would dispute it, for he claims that, contrary to Searle’ assumption, the consciousness of object in which knowledge actually consists is tacit, that is, not linguistic or not enunciative of what is known.

Who, then, is right: Searle or Polanyi? To refer again to the experiment with

the shock-producing syllables, the question is whether, as Polanyi claims, the subject can know the syllables that produce shocks without being linguistically or enunciatively aware of them, as Searle claims.

Let us first consider why Searle is so convinced that knowledge has to be enunciative.       

Polanyi speaks of “tacit knowledge” not only because what is known is not formulated or verbalized, but because, in his opinion and contrary to common belief, linguistic formulation of what is known plays no essential role in the knowing process,  however essential it may or may not be in the communication of what is known Indeed, Polanyi maintains against Searle that the knowing process does not terminate in a representational awareness of object, which  supposedly is enunciated linguistically either internally or externally. Rather, the knowing process, in the paradigmatic case of perception, terminates in the knower’s action without the mediation of verbalization .

Polanyi’s alternative conception of knowledge:  Knowledge and action

Searle assumes that the consciousness of object in which knowledge is ordinarily deemed to consist is an awareness of the object’s characteristics, an awareness that is expressed linguistically, at least internally and sometimes also externally to others. The subject’s consciousness of the object’s characteristics is, indeed,  what is enunciated when verbalization occurs. Polanyi objects, however, that the subject’s awareness of  object, in the case of TK, is not of the kind assumed by Searle, for it does not consist in consciousness OF the characteristics of the object, nor does verbalization consist in the descriptive enunciation of those characteristics. To refer to the experiment with the syllables, what occurs in the subject of the experiment at the sight of the shock-producing syllables is not an awareness of the object’s characteristics, for the subject does not know the syllables. It is, rather, a fear of certain syllables that leads the subject to automatically or instinctively brace himself for the shock as if protecting himself from it. Now, the subject’s bracing himself  is not a verbal enunciation, not even a silent one; it is a non-linguistic conduct or behavior motivated by the affection of fear – a conduct whose cause is totally obscure to the subject, since he cannot say which syllables produce shocks.  

Thus, the subject of the experiment makes no linguistic enunciation. Rather, he behaves in a certain way when the shock-producing syllables appear on the screen, and this behavior is observed by those who conduct the experiment. Actually, the observed behavior of the subject is precisely the basis on which Polanyi grounds his claim that  the subject ‘knows’ in a tacit or non-verbal way. Indeed, without that behavioral expression at the sight of the shock-producing syllables and not at the sight of the other syllables Polanyi would have no basis for his claim of tacit knowledge. Now, this connection of what Polanyi calls TK with action , in the absence of verbalization of what is known–which is why the knowledge is said to be tacit rather than explicit–  is an important clue. Indeed , it is a first indication that knowledge may terminate in action of a certain kind rather than in consciousness of the object’s characteristics enunciated in verbalization, as it has been traditionally assumed . In which case Polanyi’s notion of tacit knowledge far from being as oxymoronic as it initially appeared, may even open a new epistemic avenue.

Do animals know, though they do not verbalize?

It will be useful to realize that the Polanyian notion of knowledge as intrinsically related to action rather than to consciousness of an object’s characteristics and/or its linguistic expression is not new at all. Spontaneously, that is, when free from epistemological prejudices, most of us believe that the members of at least the higher species of mammals, say, dogs, possess the ability to know. At the same time, most of us agree that dogs are non-linguistic agents, that is, that they do not posses the linguistic ability we possess, for they do not express in words what they know. But this shows that we are implicitly assuming that knowing is not identical with the consciousness of an object’s characteristics nor with the consequent linguistic capacity to enunciate what one knows or is aware of.

On what basis, then, do we attribute to dogs the ability to know? On the basis of their observed actions. Certain of their actions are, we say,”intentional.” That is, they  reveal an agent’s purpose , in that the dog’s agency is not uniform but adapted by the dog to different circumstances for the sake of a goal that satisfies a desire or a natural need of the dog . For instance, a dog that follows the traces left by a rabbit adapts its action to the distribution of the traces left by the rabbit until it catches the rabbit, and then and there it stops, because its goal had been reached.. By contrast, a marble’s fall down an inclined plane is a uniform action, not an adaptive purposeful  action, because it is caused by the environmental circumstances (hence its uniformity), and thus is not intended by the marble for the sake of the satisfaction of a need and, therefore, is not modified accordingly by the marble . For that very reason, we have no temptation of attributing knowledge to marbles, because their action is not intentional, that is,  not for the sake of a goal intended-desired  by the marble, and the uniformity of their action shows no ability on the marble’s part to adapt its action to the circumstances in view of the realization of a goal. In a dogs’ actions, by contrast, we grasp the intention of a goal and, with their increased experience, an increasing ability on their part to adapt their actions to the circumstances and thereby make their actions more efficient for the realization of their goals. Hence we conclude that dogs know.

But cannot it be assumed that animals know enunciatively, though silently? In other words, cannot we attribute to them an internal or cerebral linguistic ability, that only lacks the vocal organs to express externally in words that of which they are conscious and verbalize internally? Against Searle, for whom verbalization is an essential component of knowledge, Polanyi maintains that animals know, but in a non-enunciative, tacit way. And the difference between these two answers shows in the different relationship in which knowledge stands to action in each of the two conceptions, which implies two different notions of knowledge irreducible to each other. In one of them –the one I reject, together with Polanyi, and attribute to Searle– knowledge is conceptually or intrinsically independent from action. Knowledge therefore can obtain in  the absence of any action (remember that, according to Searle, the illocutionary effect is the only essential effect, the only effect that cannot fail to obtain if the communication succeeds). Action, however, is dependent on knowledge, for representational knowledge of object is deemed by him to be a necessary condition for appropriate action. In the second version – the one I (together with Polanyi) consider correct–   knowledge is not representational of object but instrumental for the realization of a desired goal . It is therefore intrinsically or “conceptually” ^(F3) related to action in that it is meant to terminate in action, and will actually terminate in action unless prevented by circumstances independent of the agent.  

This analysis of animal knowledge may be refined, but as it is it suffices for my purpose. For my immediate purpose is simply to show that knowledge is not necessarily to be conceived, as Searle among many others conceives it, as an awareness of object that mirrors or reproduces without addition or distortion the object’s characteristics, which are reflected or reproduced in its verbal expression. Rather, my analysis of what –without any epistemological preconceptions– we recognize as knowledge shows that what we identify as knowledge, at least in the case of animals, terminates in intentional actions for the sake of desired goals. It shows, furthermore,  that verbalization of what is known is not a necessary component of knowledge, for animals are incapable of verbalization. In short, knowledge is not identified, at least in this case, with the awareness of the characteristics of its object, as implied by the ordinary and commonly accepted notion of knowledge shared by Searle with the Platonic-Aristotelian philosophical tradition, but with an agent’s ability to realize a desired goal through intentional action,  that is, through action adapted by the agent to the realization of the desired goal.

The notion of knowledge as awareness of its object’s characteristics has, indeed,  its roots in Plato and Aristotle and was adopted as canonical by the classical philosophical tradition. Searle’s philosophy of language reflects his own allegiance to that tradition. But, as we shall now see,  precisely therein lies a radical difference between Searle and Polanyi, for the latter parts ways with that tradition, since he sees knowledge as “ tacit. ” This means that knowledge does not consist in possessing or gaining an implicit or explicit awareness of its object’s characteristics, actually expressed or at least susceptible of being expressed in enunciative language.

Signs and signals, Linguistic signs of a meaning and perceptual signals for action

To elucidate the difference between Searle’s and Polanyi’s respective conceptions of the way language functions we should  first distinguish signs from signals. Indeed the difference between Searle and Polanyi is that Searle thinks that language functions as a system of signs OF a meaning to be deciphered or interpreted and, as a result,  understood , for “the characteristic effect of meaning is understanding.” Polanyi, for his part, sees language as a system of signals FOR performing an action that satisfies a desire ,without the mediation of an understood linguistic meaning.

Because Searle is primarily interested in language, he instinctively takes as his paradigmatic example of meaning the case of linguistic meaning, that is, the meaning made known by written or oral texts  And because he is interested in the connection of language with knowledge, he concentrates his attention on texts  that refer to the perceptual world as the simplest example of knowledge.

Now , Searle locates the difference  between linguistic signs and all other signs in the fact  that linguistic signs mean by saying or enunciating what is meant. Indeed, on this precise point Searle sees the dysanalogy between linguistic enunciations (which say what they mean) and games, ^(F4) in which moves mean but don’t say what they mean. For instance, a certain move in the game of chess means “checkmate” but doesn’t say it , while the verbal enunciation “checkmate” means and says it ^(F5). Again, the red traffic light means “stop,” but doesn’t say it, whereas the verbal enunciation “stop” says it. Again, an arrow drawn on a path means a direction to be followed but does not say that as the linguistic expression, “follow the arrow,” does.

Because linguistic signs man by saying  what they mean, they are superior in precision to all other signs, in Searle’s estimation. Thus, he explains, I can indicate with a gesture to somebody to leave the room in which we both are, and I can do  the same by saying to someone “Leave the room.” But I definitely need words to say certain complicated things. To quote Searle himself,:

“Some very simple sorts of illocutionary acts can indeed be performed apart from any conventional devices at all, simply by getting the audience to recognize certain of one’s intentions in behaving in a certain way. And these possibilities show us the limitations and weaknesses of the analogy with games. For one cannot, e.g., score a touchdown apart form invoking certain conventions (rules). But the fact that one can perform some illocutionary acts while standing outside a natural language, or any other system of constitutive rules should not obscure the fact that in general illocutionary acts are performed within language in virtue of ceratin rules, and indeed could not be performed unless language allowed the possibility of the performance. One can in certain special circumstances ‘request’ someone to leave the room without employing any conventions, but unless one has a language one cannot request of someone that he, e.g., undertake a research project on the problem of diagnosing and treating mononucleosis in undergraduates in American universities.” ( SA , p. 38)

From this it follows that Searle sees language primarily as a system of ENUNCIATIVE signs that say or enunciate what is meant or signified by the speaker in order to make his or her meaning known to the hearer . If, by understanding what the speaker says, the hearer becomes aware of, or understands, what the speaker means, the speech act is successful, for, if this obtains,  the essential function of language, which consists in making known what the speaker intends to communicate or make understood is fulfilled. This is what Searle calls the “illocutionary effect,” the effect that the speaker cannot fail to intend whenever he says something, for the speaker necessarily intends to make known to the hearer what he means. However, in addition to the illocutionary effect, sometimes the speaker may also intend  to produce a “perlocutionary effect” which does not consist in knowledge but either in an action of the hearer (for instance, the action of obeying in the case of a verbal command) or –a  possibility that Searle does not consider– in an affection of the hearer (for instance, the affection of feeling threatened in the case of a verbal threat, or the affection of being amused in the case of a verbal joke). However, to the difference of the illocutionary effect, which is essential to the success of the speech act as such, the perlocutionary effect is accidental, because a perlocutionary effect is not necessarily intended by the speaker in every communicative action, and because the success of the  communicative action is insured if only the hearer gains the knowledge of what the speaker intends to communicate, independently of whether or not the speaker intends a perlocutionary effect in addition to the illocutionary effect, and independently of whether or not the perlocutionary effect, if intended, actually obtains.

(2) Because Searle assumes that the fundamental function of language is to enunciate what is meant in order to make the meaning understood, the underlying idea of his “principle of expressibility, that whatever can be meant  can be said” is that the speaker ideally could and should say or enunciate exactly what he means, because only by enunciating exactly what he means can the speaker make known to the hearer exactly what he means. ^(F6)

(3) That Searle conceives enunciative or propositional language as representational of its meaning. This shows in his farfetched but revealing example of the American soldier faced by the Italians in World War II, whose point is ”to illustrate the connection between what the speaker mans and what the words he utters mean” ( SA, p. 44), Searle implicitly denies that the effect of the Italians’ believing that the speaker was a German soldier was linguistically obtained, for the words the American uttered mean “I come from the land where the lemon-trees bloom,” not “I am a German soldier,” which is the meaning the American soldier intended to convey. A fortiori Searle would deny that the effect of the American being treated as an ally by the Italians was linguistically obtained, for what he said doesn’t mean in German “Treat me kindly because I am a German soldier” (which Searle himself assumes is what the speaker intended the Germans to believe) but “I come from the land where the lemon trees flourish.” Ironically, the very fact that the American soldier expects that his saying something in German will terminate in his being treated kindly by the Germans, shows that, contrary to Searle’s representational conception of enunciative language, enunciative language terminates not in understanding a linguistic meaning, but in an action

         A fourth important point. Searle’s view that what is said by the speaker must ideally express or describe exactly what the speaker knows and intends to make known to the hearer, and that the hearer gains that knowledge by understanding the conventional meaning of what is said by the speaker, (4) rests on the assumption that enunciative language is a system of representational signs of what is meant . Hence the demand that one say precisely what one means. For what is said must represent as faithfully as possible what the speaker intends to communicate, and what the hearer understands must be as faithful a representation as possible of what the speaker intends to communicate. Thus what is said by the speaker is a sign OF what the speaker means because it is a verbal representation OF the speaker’s communicative intention, and the same goes for what is understood by the hearer.

Polanyi’s conception of the way in which language functions is totally different from Searle’s, because Polanyi thinks that signs –verbal signs included AND, SPECIFICALLY, PARTICULAR PERCEPTUAL STATEMENTS INCLUDED— do not mean by representing in any way what is meant. Rather –under the right conditions of desire and perceptual presence to be specified below– they mean as signals, that is, as signs-FOR performing an action conducive to the realization of a desire. If I tell you, “The hammer is in the next room,“ it is because I know or assume that you are looking for the hammer. And I expect you to proceed to the next room, not to assent to what I said. So it is not a matter of mental replication of a linguistic meaning, but of action that satisfies a desire.

Neither a red nor a green traffic light is in any way a representation of what is signified, Rather, each of those lights signals an action to be performed, not a meaning to be to be deciphered and understood , and capable of being expressed in an enunciative translation that represents the understood meaning . Similarly an arrow on a road signals the kind of action to be performed by the agent who encounters the sign, but does not signal the action to be performed by representing or enunciating the action signaled. Hence the appropriate understanding or decoding of the arrow encountered on the road consists in the actual performance of the action signaled, not in a mental or verbal representation of the action signaled. Similarly a certain noise in my house in the middle of the night is not a sign that means a meaning to be understood but a signal to respond to his or her uninvited presence by calling the police or by attacking the uninvited guest or by some other effective action...

Linguistic signs and perceptual signals

(Oral perceptual statements in presence refer to perceptual signals, like in the case of  “Your sister is on the phone.”)

Aristotle’s logic is composed of purely intelligible, not perceptual, a-temporal, universal propositions. It is supposed to lead from the combined knowledge of two propositions of the mentioned kind  to the previously unknown knowledge of a third proposition of the same mentioned kind. It is based on the notion that two things that are identical to a third one are identical to each other . When this identity is grasped in a particular case, the conclusion necessarily follows. If A = B, and B = C, the conclusion that A = C necessarily follows, and so does the mind’s assent to this conclusion. However, the assent is, in this case, to the identity of the verbal, that is, conceptual or connotational rather than denotational or referential meaning of the stated propositions . The concluding proposition A = C constitutes what Kant calls an analytic truth, because it suffices to understand what is meant by the premises to know what is said or meant by the conclusion. Analytic truths are purely conceptual because they are about verbal meanings, about the concepts connoted by the words, not about the world; not about anything denoted.. Thus they do not add to our knowledge of the world.. To come to know an analytic truth it suffices to analyze and understand the meaning of the words in which it is expressed; no experience is needed and thus, no sensory evidence is required.

Perceptual signals are given to us by actually perceived particular ‘objects’ that we encounter in the course of our acting to achieve a desired purpose. If I want to cross the road in order to reach my destination, and I see a car coming in my direction, the car  signals to me not to cross the road. It is not a sign OF a meaning to be understood. For It is meant to terminate not in understanding of a verbal meaning  but in an action , an action appropriate to satisfy my innate desire of surviving.

But is not language a unique case among signs? Is not Searle right that the uniqueness of language among signs is that it means or signifies by saying what is meant by the speaker? Not so. In Polanyi’s opinion, Searle is wrong on this point. The problem with Searle’s view of language is that he thinks of “saying” as enunciating a meaning to be understood, and he thinks of what is enunciated as a piece of information to be understood or to become aware of by the hearer, rather than, as is the case, as a signal FOR to be responded to by the desirous agent by performing at the appropriate perceptual moment the action signaled. Precisely because Searle is wrong on this point, he claims that the saying involved in a request, such as “Lend me your pencil,” succeeds as a speech act if only the hearer first becomes informed of, or understands, the speaker’s expressed linguistic meaning. As if in making a request the speaker intended to inform the hearer that he has a desire rather than bringing the addressee to comply with his desire by actually lending him his pencil. Searle confuses linguistic meaning, which is to be interpreted and understood, with perceptual signals that are to be responded to with action.

I have already argued against this Serlean view which ignores the grammatical difference between the subjunctive or the imperative moods in which desires are expressed, and the indicative mood in which pieces of information are imparted. This is the reason that, as I argued in the previous chapter, if Searle were right on this point, language would function in the bastard way English functions in a course of English for foreigners, where the request “Lend me your pencil” is understood as a piece of information to be acquired by the hearer rather than as a request to be complied with. In that artificial situation in which attention is not directed to the surrounding world, as it actually is when we are acting, but to the conventional meaning of words, all the sayings of speakers function as Searle wrongly thinks that sayings normally function in ordinary life – as mere factual enunciations. Clearly, this is not the way in which competent users of English understand requests, nor the way in which requests actually function in ordinary linguistic, face-to-face, oral exchanges among agents, nor the way in which a person who makes a request intends his or her requests to be understood. However, rather than merely repeating the arguments already given against Searle’s view, what we need is to explore further the alternative conception of language furnished by Polanyi.

The place of desire  in tacit knowledge: Desire is the operator

NB: A desire acted upon implies satisfaction or frustration.

Having distinguished the common or traditional conception of knowledge, shared by Searle with the classical philosophical representational tradition, from  Polanyi’s own, I should now make clear that the two conceptions are not only different but mutually incompatible. In other words, the two cannot be correct. Why not? If for no other reason, because of the essential role inherited need or acquired desire plays in tacit knowledge, whereas desire is positively excluded in the traditional conception of knowledge as representation, because it is deemed to militate against the ‘objectivity’ or exact representational value of the cognition. question:

Why did I introduce the theme of signs in particular? Because I want to show that the objects of our ordinary perceptual experience, which takes place when we  are actually engaged in action to achieve a goal –  not when we are just contemplating the surrounding world or thinking about it or trying to understand it –  function precisely  as SIGNALS FOR ACTING in a way that is appropriate to achieve the de sired goal, not as SIGNS OF A MEANING TO BE DECIPHERED AND UNDERSTOOD.

Thus, to refer to our ordinary perceptual experience, the car I see coming in my direction at a certain speed, when I am about to cross a road to reach my destination , is for me , but only insofar and because I naturally desire to reach safely the other side, a signal FOR not crossing the road. But if what I desire is to  die, the perception of the same car coming toward me is a signal FOR crossing the road at the approach of the car. And to someone who sees the same car coming toward me but who has no desire whatever, the car coming toward me is a signal for doing nothing at all, for it simply is not relevant OR MEANINGFUL at all for him, given that he has no desire to be satisfied in relation to the car coming toward me . Similarly , if I am thirsty and come across a stream, the presence of water, is for me, a signal FOR moving  toward it and drinking, but only insofar and because I desire to quench my thirst. For if I am not thirsty, the perceived water is no signal for me.  THUS DESIRE IS THE  OPERATOR THAT TRANSFORMS WHAT OTHERWISE WOULD BE A MERE meaningless “PHENOMENON”  INTO A DEFINITE SIGNAL FOR.  

The reason why we get easily confused concerning the representational meaning of perceptual statements, as Searle himself gets confused, is that in the case of enunciative language, –different in this from the speech acts possessing explicit perlocutionary force– the action signaled is not specified and doesn’t need to be specified precisely  because  desire  is the operator that transforms what otherwise would be a mere perceptual phenomenon into a definite rule for action to be used as an instrument to satisfy a desire . Thus (to refer again to Searle’s farfetched example), the American soldier rightly assumes the addressee’s habitual desire to treat well his German allies, which will automatically convert the enunciation into a signal and rule FOR the action of treating the American soldier kindly. (The same with “Your sister is on the phone”).

Perceptual encounter of a signal, and Reading or hearing statements that refer to perceptual objects

Wittgenstein distinguishes between“interpretation” of the linguistic meaning of texts –be they oral or written– from perceptual experience by the exposed agent. For  he claims that understanding a  linguistic meaning, which he calls interpretation, and which terminates in understanding of the verbal  meaning,  should not be confused with perceiving, which terminates in action appropriate to realize a desire .  

For his part, Searle falls precisely into that confusion, for he wrongly assimilates perception to what Wittgenstein calls  “interpretation,” that is, to  the reading and translating of the LINGUISTIC meaning of written and oral texts, by contrast to encountering perceptual signals that do not require understanding of any linguistic  meaning.

Like the reading and translating of oral or written texts, what Wittgeinstein calls “interpretation,”remains within the order of linguistic meaning, for it terminates in understanding the meaning of the text  and–in the case of translation– it terminates in the linguistic expression of the meaning of the text interpreted in order to make understood to others the meaning that the original speaker or writer supposedly intended to make understood.

.Because an oral text is heard and a written text is   seen , Searle assumes perceiving to be a  variety of reading or translating, and therefore meant to terminate in the kind of understanding involved in the interpretation of the meaning of texts rather than belonging, as it actually does, in the domain of non-interpretive and non-objective but instrumental perception of signals and action.

Actually –or so at least Polanyi would argue in agreement with Wittgenstein– perception, by contrast to the interpretive reading or the translation of a text, functions as a signal for action and is, therefore, meant to terminate in action, not in understanding and verbalization. Perception is the deed of an exposed agent actually engaged in action, and is therefore conjoined with desire. Perception addresses or “speaks to” the exposed, concerned agent who desires and projects a particular outcome of his or her action, not to the reader or interpreter (or to the mere unexposed “witness”). Remember that perception is ‘indexical,‘ that is, tacitly indicative of perceiving exposed agent.

This reflection on signs may help us to grasp the fundamental difference between Searle’s and Polanyi’s respective conceptions of the way in which language functions. Searle thinks that language –which he deems to be primarily enunciative and representational, and only secondarily and accidentally perlocutionary or

connected with action– functions as a system of representational signs OF a meaning to be understood and formulated linguistically , for, as he puts it, “the characteristic intended effect of meaning is understanding (SA, p. 47)” ^(F7) For his part,  Polanyi thinks that language is not primarily enunciative and representational, and that genuinely enunciative language is meant to function not as a sign OF a linguistic meaning to be deciphered and understood but as a signal FOR the performance of an action with no intervening purely theoretical understanding and/or enunciation of the action to be performed. Thus, according to Searle, in order to obey a command I have first to understand the command, i.e., what the command supposedly says, that is the connotational meaning of the command. In other words, the verbal command is a sign OF a meaning to be understood theoretically. According to Polanyi, by contrast, the verbal command is directly –that is. without intervening linguistic formulation of a meaning to be understood–  a signal FOR performing the action commanded. The understanding here involved is practical, in that it is meant to terminate, and be manifested at the appropriate time, in the actual performance of the action commanded, not in the theoretical understanding of the action commanded.

This confusion of perception with interpretation can be illustrated in the case of Newton . Indeed, at least in an indirect way, he fell victim to the same confusion as Searle, since he said that he did not invent his hypothese s –“ Hypotheses non fingo”– meaning that nature was a book in which he directly read the laws of nature that he translated linguistically in his mathematical formulas. In addition, influenced by Aristotle, he thought that his formulas described and represented the intelligible “natures” of what is the case in reality, as a meaning to be understood by a contemplating consciousness. This corresponds to the idea of science perpetuated by the textbooks and rightly criticized by Kuhn . Kuhn, for his part, would claim that Newton was wrong on this point, because his formulas were meant as signals-For action at the moment to be determined by the perceiving addressee as  appropriate to realize his or her desire, not as representational descriptions of observed phenomena..

That Newton’s work was better than his own misinterpretation of it shows in the essential function played by measurements of time and space (which are absent in Aristotle’ s scientific statements in his logical treatises). His logic is composed of abstract universal propositions interrelated exclusively by their conventional linguistic meaning that refers to connoted concepts. Aristotelian logic results of reflecting and comparing those concepts; [that the concept A is identical to B, and B to C}  This abstract character, too,  distinguishes Newton’s formulas from the SUPPOSEDLY universal and purely theoretical or purely logical theorems of Euclidean geometry , because it renders his formulas applicative at the particular time and place to be determined by the perceiving engineer. Rather than being representational of what is the case, Newton’s laws (much as the theorems of Euclidean geometry as well as  theological doctrines, when correctly understood), are rules for linguistic action, as indicated by Lindbeck..

Perceiving and Reading

Perceiving, then, is not a instance of interpretation of a text, to paraphrase Wittgenstein (cf. note 9 ). Interpretation of a text, when the text is understood, terminates in understanding,  interpretive understanding of propositional language, an understanding which itself can be translated for others in another text to be read and understood by them. Perceiving is rather –in Wittgenstein’s view– like following or obeying a rule,  like the practical comprehension of a signal for performing an action at the appropriate perceptual time, which may be now or in the future time of co-presence. It is, therefore, a non- semantic or not signifying “reading,” for it does not terminate in another text or in any understanding but in the action of following the rule without actual understanding of the meaning of the rule, as children follow grammatical rules when they speak, without being aware of or understanding the rules they follow. ^(F8) Thus, not only is it possible to follow a rule one doesn’t understand, but it is the normal case, for rules are not to be understood as linguistic statements but to be followed as guiding signals..

Like the  reading and translating of linguistic  texts , interpretation terminates in another text, that is, in the linguistic expression of the meaning of the text interpreted. Perception, by contrast, does not function as a sign OF a meaning to be understood and formulated. It is meant to terminate in action and, thus, functions  as a signal FOR action at the appropriate perceptual time. (For animals the time is always now, the ‘now’ of perception,  for, lacking connotational language, they have no means of distinguishing the signaling perceived object from the conceived object. Thus, they cannot put their desire in abeyance in order to determine when and how to act).

To return to human perception, perception is dramatic, for it is the deed of an exposed vulnerable agent, not of an invulnerable, disengaged purely contemplative intelligent consciousness. Therefore, it terminates not in understanding and formulation of what is understood, as interpretive understanding does, but in action to reach a desired goal by realizing it. Or,.to put the same in another way, what is perceived, if conjoined with desire, “speaks” to the desirous agent, not to the reader or interpreter (or to the mere unexposed “witness) , and is meant to terminate in action, not in interpretive understanding.

Falling victim, at least in part, to the same confusion of perception with interpretation as Searle, Newton said that he did not invent hypotheses. The reason is that he imagined  nature as a book in which he read-understood the laws of nature that he expressed linguistically in his mathematical formulas . This metaphor indicates that he thought that his formulas described and replicated what was the case in reality as a linguistic meaning written in the book of nature to be understood by a contemplating intelligent consciousness . In this interpretation of his own work – but not in his work itself– Newton was wrong, His formulas were meant by him, not by nature, and were supposed to function both for himself and for other interested or concerned agents  as signals-For action (thanks to the mediation of desire) at the appropriate perceptual moment, as suggested by Kuhn’s notion of science as problem-solving. Hence the essential role of measurements in Newton’s laws (totally absent from Aristotle’s purely logical conception of science). For Newton’s laws, as shown by the  measurements of time and spatial distance they include, are not like the supposedly purely theoretical or purely logical theorems of Euclidean geometry imagined by Aristotle in his treatises on logic and perfected in the modern treatises of symbolic or mathematical logic. Neither are they like the supposedly theoretical intelligible laws of physics whose meaning is to be understood, which are  IMAGINED by the authors of scientific textbooks criticized by Kuhn.  Rather  (much as theological doctrines, when correctly understood), Newton’s laws are meant to function as RULES for future action – once a particular problem is solved “on paper”–  and as SIGNALS FOR immediate action at the appropriate perceptual moment of application – when a problem is to be solved “in reality,” that is, when the desired state of affairs can actually be brought about through action.

The difference between a map conceived as a representational picture of a terrain and a map actually used to reach the desired place–as it is meant to be used,  i.e. as a guiding instrument for action–  is the difference between a sign and a signal , between a sign OF a meaning to be interpreted and understood, and a signal FOR performing an action to satisfy a desire. To return  to the experiment with the shock-producing syllables, it is the difference between observing the syllables that appear on the screen to become aware of their observable characteristics, and using the experienced sensations produced by the syllables as instruments to guide one’s action toward the realization of the inherited need to escape the expected shock Much as the blind person who carries a stick to find his way uses the sensations produced in his hand every time the stick hits an object on his path as an instrument  or map to guide himself toward the place he wants to reach. To a desirous agent, these sensations are not representations OF anything but signals and rules FOR action. Which action? An action conducive to the satisfaction of the particular desire the agent has. ( Compare this case to the case of the smoking lepers whose fingers are desensitized by their illness, who therefore receive no signal to throw the cigarette that is about to burn their hand..

The same instrumental role for the satisfaction of a need is played by the verbal enunciations or statements  made by one agent to another in a commonly shared situation. If I tell you “Your sister  is on the phone,” I expect you, not to become theoretically or contemplatively aware of the meaning enunciated, but to use what I enunciate as an instrument or a sort of map to guide  you  to the telephone and satisfy your pressumed desire of speaking to your sister. In short, Searle is wrong in attributing to verbal enunciations the representational role of signs OF what is said. Indeed,  –in conjunction with the project whose realization  they make possible–  they are meant to function as instrumental guiding signs or rules FOR action, as Polanyi (and Kuhn) implicitly claim.

The reason for the neglect– or, rather, exclusion–  of desire in traditional philosophy is that the consciousness of  object, in which knowledge supposedly consists, as well as its linguistic expression, have to represent the object factually or ‘objectively’– that is, to copy it without addition or subtraction. Not for nothing, in his close commentary on Aristotle’s treatise on linguistic interpretation , did Aquinas contrast what Aristotle sees as the genuinely enunciative speech of the scientist, which is dictated exclusively by the object and leads to exact representational knowledge (and therefore to the truth), with the non-enunciative, non-representational speech of the rhetorician that appeals to the desires of the hearer and, therefore– in Aristotle’s misinterpretation– necessarily distorts and falsifies the object to be known. Aquinas’s passage is such a succinct, though indirect,  formulation of the disturbing function of desire in matters of cognition, when cognition is understood as representation, that it deserves to be quoted in full:

“Then Aristotle says , Let us therefore consider enunciative speech, etc. Here he points out that only enunciative speech is to be treated ; the other four species [to wit, deprecative, imperative, interrogative, and vocative] must be omitted as far as the present intention is concerned, because their investigation belongs rather to the sciences of rhetoric or poetics. Enunciative speech belongs to the present consideration and for the following reason: this book is ordered directly to demonstrative [logical] science, in which the mind of man is led by an act of reasoning to assent to TRUTH from those things that are proper to the thing; To this end the demonstrator uses only enunciative speech, which signifies things ACCORDING AS TRUTH ABOUT THEM IS IN THE MIND. The rhetorician and the poet, on the other hand, induce assent to what they intend not only through what is proper to the thing but also through the dispositions of the hearer. Hence, rhetoricians and poets for the most part strive to move their auditors by arousing certain passions in them, as the Philosopher says in the Rhetorica. ^(F9)

Notice the contrast between the mind being led to knowledge exclusively by the observed object (“those things that are proper to the thing” independently of the dispositions of the hearer), which Aristotle sees as the mark of genuinely enunciative or purely objective speech, on the one hand, and the mind being led to knowledge by the desires or affections of the knower (“through the dispositions of the hearer”). As long as knowledge is conceived as a factual replication of the object by the consciousness of it the subject has and/or by the linguistic expression by the subject himself of what is known to him, then desire can only be seen as a disturbing element in the cognitive process. When, however, as is the case with Polanyi, the point of cognition and verbal expression of what is known is not to re-present the object but

– thanks to desire – to function as an instrument that makes possible the agent’s conduct in order to achieve a desired end, then the desire of the end to be realized through action becomes an essential component of cognition. ^(F10)    

Knowledge as a signal FOR action

One fundamental difference between Polanyi’s and Searle’s conceptions of knowledge is that, as we shall now see, the former thinks that knowledge functions as a signal FOR action, whereas Searle thinks that it functions as a representation of its object’s characteristics with no intrinsic and direct relationship to action. .

In his principle of expressibility, SEARLE DEMANDS EXPLICIT ENUNCIATION OF WHAT IS KNOWN, BECAUSE HE THINKS THAT THE FUNCTION OF ENUNCIATIVE LANGUAGE IS TO SAY or describe WHAT IS MEANT IN ORDER TO MAKE IT KNOWN, AND WHAT IS MEANT REPRESENTS WHAT IS THE CASE AS KNOWN BY THE SPEAKER. FURTHERMORE,  HE SEES THE ACTION IN WHICH THE PERLOCUTIONARY EFFECTS CONSIST  AS DEPENDENT ON UNDERSTANDING WHAT IS SAID OR ENUNCIATED (SEE BELOW). POLANYI, BY CONTRAST, INSISTS THAT KNOWLEDGE IS TACIT BECAUSE ITS FUNCTION IS NOT TO SAY OR ENUNCIATE or represent verbally WHAT IS THE CASE BUT TO TERMINATE IN ACTION BY FUNCTIONING, NOT AS A REPRESENTATION OF THE OBJECT KNOWN BUT, IN CONJUNCTION WITH DESIRE ON THE PART OF THE AGENT, AS A SIGNAL FOR PRE-PROGRAMMED OR FOR SELF-PROGRAMMED DELIBERATIVE ACTION.

It will be useful to preface my examination of Polanyi’s views on TK by remarking that, unlike Searle, Polanyi never wrote a treatise on language, nor was he primarily interested in language. Actually, his central idea is that knowledge is tacit , that is, not verbalized, and that what we call knowledge  functions as a sign FOR action. This implies not only that knowledge does not consist in the linguistic enunciation of its object’s characteristics, but that linguistic enunciation of the object’s characteristics is not even required for it would be superfluous. . Indeed, Polanyi’s shift from consciousness of the object’s characteristics and its consequent linguistic enunciation to action in the absence of linguistic enunciation of what is known implies a different conception both of the way in which language functions and of its role or lack thereof in cognition.

Unlike knowledge conceived in the ordinary way, Polanyi’s TK is intrinsically connected with action. Herein lies a radical difference between the ordinary notion of knowledge, inherited by Searle from the Platonico-Aristotelian philosophical tradition, and Polanyi’s TK.:The ordinary notion of knowledge is manifested in   KANT’S epistemology by the demonstrative function played by “ intuition” , for it involves the consciousness of object as visual manifestation of the identity of two concepts, with no intrinsic relationship to action if, at all. Polanyian TK, by contrast, functions as instrument for action.

The instrumental role of TK by contrast to the visual revelation of object in which the ordinary notion of knowledge consists may be illustrated by means of a comparison to the way in which eyeglasses function . When the eyeglasses function as such – that is, when they are used and by someone, not when they lie on a table and are looked at– the person who wears the eyeglasses does not see the eyeglasses, that is, has no awareness of them as object of knowledge, as is the case when the eyeglasses lie on a table and are looked at. In other words, when the eyeglasses are worn, they do not function as object known but as an instrument. As an  instrument for what? Polanyi would answers, or should answer: as an instrument for action , that is, as an instrument that guides or rules the agent’s action. Such is the role played by TK,  and therein lies a fundamental difference between our ordinary notion of knowledge and TK. However, in order to fully grasp this difference we have to follow in some detail Polanyi’s analysis of TK in which the knowing subject of the classical epistemological tradition is replaced by the agent .

The function of action in Polanyi’s tacit knowledge

In his initial analysis of the notion of TK, in The tacit Dimension ,  referring   to the experiment with the shock-producing syllables ,  Polanyi’s emphasis was on the fact that the subject knows more than he can say . Let us remark in this connection, in the first place, that  affirming that the subject knows more than he can say Polanyi was by the same token taking his distances from the epistemological tradition that equates knowledge with the awareness of its object’s characteristics and the consequent verbal enunciation of those characteristics. Secondly,   Polanyi shifts the emphasis from verbal enunciation of what is known to action . Indeed, as we shall see, given that the subject knows more than he can say or express in words, [OR, RATHER, –POLANYI SHOULD HAVE SAID–  KNOWS IN A WAY THAT IS MEANT TO TERMINATE IN ACTION AT THE APPROPRIATE PERCEPTUAL TIME, RATHER THAN IN UNDERSTANDING OR IN WORDS THAT EXPRESS WHAT IS UNDERSTOOD],  his knowledge is not manifested  in the verbalization of what is known by the subject, but in the subject’s action to realize a desired goal, a realization which is rendered possible by the perceptual presence of the object denoted. . This is why all of Polanyi’s examples of tacit knowledge  refer to an agent actually engaged in action to achieve a desired goal, and will consist in practical  skills, not– as is the case with Searle– referring to a contemplating consciousness that expresses its knowledge through enunciations of the characteristics of the objects contemplated

Grasping this shift from the consciousness of the CONTEMPLATIVE epistemic subject of classical epistemology to the agent’s actions is fundamental for understanding Polanyi’s conception of knowledge. Knowledge, for Polanyi, is the deed of a vulnerable, desirous agent exposed to the action of the surrounding world, not the deed of a contemplating-mirroring consciousness invulnerable to the surrounding world. Unfortunately, this displacement of the epistemic subject of the classical epistemological tradition by the agent exposed to the action of the surrounding world is insufficiently grasped or at least insufficiently underscored by Polanyi himself.. It will therefore be my task to bring this all-important point to the fore. “Interiorization” and action

Let us return to the example Polanyi employs to introduce the notion of tacit knowledge. The subject in the experiment with the syllables knows tacitly which syllables produce shocks. His knowledge is tacit because it does not manifest itself  in words that express his awareness of those syllables. Indeed, the subject cannot say even to himself , much less to others,  which are those syllables; rather, his knowledge of the shock-producing syllables manifests itself in his conduct of bracing himself for a shock when those syllables appear on the screen. Thus, in the sense of being aware of the characteristics of the shock-producing syllables and of being able to say which are the shock-producing syllables, the subject of the experiment does not know, for both the awareness of object and the linguistic enunciation of the object characteristics, in which knowledge is ordinarily supposed to consist, are absent in this case. Yet, Polanyi claims, the subject of the experiment knows the syllables, not in the sense of being aware of the observable characteristics of the syllables, but in the sense of acting in response precisely  to those syllables by bracing himself for the shock to come when those syllables appear on the screen. Those syllables function not as object known, but as a signal from which the subject acts. Action, therefore –the action of following the rule indicated by the perceived signaling object–  rather than interpretation or  linguistic enunciation of a meaning, is in Polanyi’s view the way in which knowledge manifests itself . As indirectly suggested by Wittgenstein, acting in obedience to a rule rather than understanding what the rule says or means, is the way in which knowledge manifests itself

.  

.”Interiorizing something,” Polanyi explains, is to identify oneself with it by making it function as the immediate term of TK IN THE WAY WE USE OUR OWN BODY WHEN WE ACT to realize a goal, THEREBY TRANSCENDING THE OBJECT TOWARD THE REALIZATION (THROUGH ACTION) OF THE DESIRED GOAL. To grasp the difference between the Serlean conception of knowledge as verbal enunciation (be it internal or external) of the characteristics of the object known, on the one hand, and, on the other hand, the Polanyian conception of knowledge as instrument for action, let us reflect on what goes on in our everyday experience of signs, which themselves function as instruments for action, as we shall now see. As a concrete example, consider our experience with traffic lights . We think of red and green lights as signs. And we are not wrong. Where we go wrong is in thinking, as Searle does, that signs are primarily signs OF a meaning to be understood and that this understanding is made explicit when verbally interpreted , when in fact signs function as signals FOR ACTION, directly, that is, without the mediation of verbalization .. Thus, red, in the case of a traffic light, has no meaning to be deciphered, a meaning that can be enunciated. Rather, it is  a sign FOR PERFORMING the action of stopping. Similarly, the green light is a sign FOR PERFORMING the action of proceeding ahead. And the same is the case with all signs. An arrow on a road  is a sign FOR performing the action of moving in the direction pointed at by the arrow. Consequently, the correct comprehension of signs is not to be manifested in linguistic enunciations of what is signified, for there is nothing signified as to be known or understood; rather it is to be  manifested in the performance of the action signified, for that is what signs signal or signify.

Excursus to Piaget’s Psychology of Intelligence

Traffic lights are examples of artificial signs, that is, of signs invented by people for the purpose of regulating the conduct of people in certain circumstances. But artificial signs are just an extension of the so-called “ natural signs,” which are not invented but inherited and characteristic of each species . A certain sound by a mother sheep  functions initially as a not learned but inherited signal for her ewe   to come and gather around her, which is an action – and only for her ewe, not for other sheep, for only in her ewe does the action satisfy  an inherited need, (which is why we can domesticate dogs but not cockroaches). But only for her ewe, for only the proximity of the ewe to their mother satisfies their need for protection and food. Once the ewe have  responded to the sign and gather around their mother, the sound is associated with the imminent presence of their mother and becomes a sign OF the desired imminent presence of exposure of the mother, but not as A MEANING to be understood theoretically but, rather, AS A SIGNAL FOR THE REALIZATION OF THEIR DESIRE OF BEING NEAR THEIR MOTHER-PROTECTOR This is an example of a natural , that is, inherited sign . There are, however, in addition, non-inherited, acquired signals. For instance, with some training , certain shepherds manage to direct their dogs with the sounds of whistling to behave in a way that controls the direction in which their flock is supposed to move. Now, the sound of whistling is not a natural sign for the dogs to direct the flock, for it is not inherited but acquired through special training. In this sense, not only is it an acquired rather than inherited sign , but it is an artificial rather than natural sign for the trained dogs for behaving in a certain way . All artificial signs are extensions based on natural signs. That is, we people can invent new signs only for those species that have the natural ability to respond to natural signs as signs.

The mechanism through which an acquired sign becomes and remains a signal for behaving in a particular way, in the case of animals, is complex. Many theories have been proposed to explain that phenomenon, (cf Piaget, Psychology of intelligence . ^(F11) What is certain is that the inherited  conducts elicited through signals vary widely from species to species, THE REASON IS THAT the inherited needs differ form species to species, and so do the behaviors that satisfy the particular need. For instance, very few species, comparatively, can become genuinely domesticated, that is, trained to behave as domestic animals, even under the best trainers. We cannot make pets out of cockroaches, but we can domesticate dogs, because cockroaches are not so constituted biologically as to enjoy our company or profit from it

Now, to come to humans, the most salient way in which we socialize or domesticate the young members of our own species and teach them certain conducts that we consider preferable, once they have reached a certain level of developmental maturity, is through the semantic system we call language , which varies from culture to culture . Lindbeck is right concerning the way in which children are acculturated into Christianity through teaching of the Christian stories and ritual participation in the stories within which they come to situate themselves by playing their appropriate roles. (See The Nature of Doctrine, chapter 2) . Unfortunately we are as confused as to the way in which language itself as a system of signs functions as we are concerning the way in which other signs function. This is manifested by the multiple explanatory theories of language. (See note 10 above).

The linguistic species: Superior, or just different?

Animals by contrast to us humans are non-linguistic creatures. What difference does this make? That we, people, can transform our environment to tailor it to our needs. As a consequence, unlike animals, each new generation of humans do not necessarily live as their ancestors lived, excepting the changes in the environment caused by nature.

It is important to realize that we do not observe or become objectively aware of the characteristics of the thing that is a sign. Rather, as pointed out by Polanyi, we just act from the sign   toward the satisfaction of a desire. In other words, my perceiving a sign shows not in objective or disinterested awareness OF  the perceptual characteristics of the sign or even in intellectual awareness OF a meaning signified, but in my behaving in the way signaled by it. Thus, the subject of the experiment with the shock-producing syllables had no awareness OF the observable qualities of the syllables that produced shocks; he was affected by the shocks and responded to them automatically as signals with the self-protective behavior they signaled of bracing himself for a shock and, if possible, excaping it.

This connection with action is typical of genuine perceptual experience ^(F12),which takes place in the course of acting, not of thinking or reflecting or contemplating.

Previously I mentioned that all our acquired and artificial signs are “extensions” of natural signs.   By that I meant that the objects that function as natural signs have to be  related, at least indirectly, to the satisfaction of the natural  needs of the species for which they function as signs.(cf. The case of the Photinus Pyralis “whose flashing signs are obviously understood by the prospective mate” as an invitation to copulate” (Werner Gitt, In the Beginning was Information , First Master Books Printing February 2006, Copyrigth Wernert  Gitt, p. 256).

Thus, sensed humidity – which is an affection, not a perception of objet–  functions for us as a signal to start looking for water only because we have the natural need for water, without which we cannot survive. Had we not that need, sensed humidity would signal nothing to us, as the signals sent by the photinus female would signal nothing to the male. Again, only those animals that have a natural need, say, for the affection of comfort or security that living with people provides them can learn to live with people and be trained to perform certain tasks because they inherit those needs, of which the mentioned behaviors acquired through training are, so to say,”extensions.”

Now, the human species seems to be the most malleable or adaptable species by far, in that it can be domesticated –or domesticate itself– to the most varied “domi” or houses (domesticate comes from the Latin word “domus,” which means “house,” “home.” In other words, we can find our “home” or come to  feel “at home” practically in any environment How come?

I think that the answer to this so important question has to do, on the one hand, with (1) the fact that our human needs do not function as instincts, as they do in the case of animals, that is, they do not dictate or cause definite, invariable, unmodifiable, uncontrollable conducts connected with the survival of the individual or of the species. On the other hand, the answer has also to do with two other interrelated facts, to wit: that we humans are linguistic creatures and, as such, are capable of (2) deliberative reflection and also (3) of applicative knowledge of the objective conditions on which successful action depends .

Let us begin with (1 )  instinctive conducts , which is the simplest element. An instinctive conduct is like a response in the S-R model of behavior, only more complex than a response. A reflex response is a uniform, inherited, automatic, uncontrollable  and  unmodifiable behavior prompted by a certain feature of the environment of a living creature. If there is intense light in my environment, my eye responds by contracting  the pupil and producing tears. If there is a certain intense scent in my environment, my stomach responds by vomiting. Well, an instinct or instinctive conduct is equally inherited or not learned, automatic, uncontrollable and unmodifiable as a response, but is a much more complex conduct than a response. Examples of this are the complex pattern of behavior of bees in building hives, of birds in building their nests, etc. These behaviors vary from species to species. They are complex, goal-directed, and highly skilled, unlike responses, which are simple and automatic.. Now, we humans, by contrast to animals, inherit some simple reflex responses but no instinctive conducts which are instrumental in creating a transformed environment adapted to the needs of the species, We build houses, but we have no instinct to build houses. We build our houses in the most different styles. Moreover, our houses may not have the extremely sophisticated ventilating system of a hive, which seems to be a marvel of engineering, according to the experts. Thus, the difference between the house built instinctively by bees and the house built by an architect, is not that the human builds better than the bee, but –as Marx comments– that the human builds in a totally different way, for the human invents and chooses what he is going to build and how, whereas the bee, determined by instinct, just executes blindly and automatically the plan of nature as dictated to it by instinct. ^(F13) To the difference of a response, which aims at correcting or getting rid of a disagreeable state of the organism , a conduct aims at transforming in a desirable way the environment of the organism

I suspect that this different ability –but not necessarily superior in every sense– of the human is connected with the fact that language involves a conceptualizing ( CONNOTATIONAL ) capacity, as suggested by Searle’s remark on complex projects that can only be communicated through linguistic signs [see note 11 below,  SA pp. 38-39. And see Lonergan, Verbum, (pp. 2-3) on the primary meaning of external words being nt anobject but an internal word, that is.  a concept or the connotation of the word ] , thanks to the sedimentation of dictionary or conventional meaning which render possible cooperative conducts. .

 

To complete my answer to the question why we humans have more varied forms of behavior than animals or any other living species, which allows us to adapt to practically any environment and even, within limits, to plan and choose our future environment, I return to the other two factors I mentioned, to wit: that (2) we are linguistic creatures and that, (3) as such , are capable of deliberative reflection to choose our conduct whose success in achieving our goals depends on objective conditions which are independent of our desires..

REFLECTION is often mischaracterized in the epistemological literature, by contrast to perception , as a second-order activity , in that it is about the past moment of perception-interaction and presupposes it, whereas perception confronts us with the present (in view of the Future ) and presupposes nothing. For instance, if you tell me that you offer me a job, I may answer that I will reflect on your offer. I can reflect on your offer because you already have made me an offer, but I cannot possibly  reflect on something which has not taken place yet and of which I did not become previously aware at the perceptual moment.

Wherein lies the MIS-characterization of reflection? In that perception does not confront us with the present as an object known or to be known; that is, to become aware OF. Rather what is perceived is that to whose action we are exposed and which is itself exposed to our action. It functions as a signal  FOR immediate or future (or not so immediate) action, depending on the perceptual conditions that make the action possible, and  refers us to a desired future . For instance, concerning your offer of a job, which takes place at the interactive or encounteirng moment of perception, I am not objectively aware of your offer, but only tacitly, as a sign For action . Consequently,   I will accept your offer, if I want to have a job, or reject it, if I don’t want a job. By contrast, the very comparison of the perceptual moment with the PAST object of reflection presupposes that we perceive in the way in which we grasp the past object of reflection, that is, objectively, for we are not anymore or not yet exposed to its action (cf.scientific .truths) Under the weight of the representational epistemology characteristic of the Western philosophical tradition, enunciative language is deemed to function as a (descriptive) sign OF what is enunciated . My conduct in response to your verbal offer of a job presupposes, it is argued by Searle,  that I am OBJECTIVELY aware of your offer. However, that is not the case, for I am not aware of your offer as object known or observed, but only tacitly and practically or instrumentally, as a sign-For action, as I am aware of an object encountered in perception.   

But there is, in addition to this objectifying reflection, a different conception of reflection, which unfortunately occupies a very secondary place in our philosophical tradition, obsessed as it is with objective representational  knowledge. Let us call it deliberative reflection –the reflection of the agent actually engaged in action, by contrast to the spurious objectifying reflection whose impossible purpose is to know the real through ITS linguistic representation . The purpose of deliberative reflection is  not to know objectively what is the case, but to plan and determine how to act either now or in the DISTANT  future – the way the British captain reflected or deliberated to determine whether to abandon ship or fight the fire. Deliberative reflection, which may take place during the perceptual-interactive moment, presupposes the capacity to neutralize or put in abeyance the signaling force of a sign FOR, thereby putting in abeyance one’s own conduct in response to it, instead of responding  automatically to the perceived signal, as animals cannot help doing under the pressure of the prevailing desire or fear, given their lack of linguistic conceptualizing ability . It also presupposes the capacity to know the world and its signs in an only potentially instrumental way through conceptualized regularities of its action-events in order to adapt the means to the desired-chosen ends . Both of these capacities depend on our linguistic ability as their condition. For –by itself and in the perceptual absence of the objective conditions for action– enunciative language refers us not to real objects but to  to concepts of situations in respect of potentially dramatic events, not to real objects .

Now, animals do not reflect in any sense, either objectively or deliberatively. All their dealings with their surrounding world are participatory or applicative, immediately practical, tied to perception.. Why? Because they are not linguistic creatures capable of conceptualizing – not ”objects,” as it is usually assumed, but rather events and situations in which the agent is or can be involved . The conduct of animals is predetermined by heredity and previous training or previous experience; they neither have to, nor can, plan their conduct nor the consequent modifications of the surrounding world. Since they have no language to conceptualize events and situations and regularities of action in the world in which they are situated , they cannot invent new goals-means to satisfy their needs and desires when applicable, at the appropriate perceptual time . That is why each species of animals, aside from the evolutionary modifications that happen to them, lives in approximately the same way as their ancestors lived. Not so the human species. Based on the observed and linguistically conceptualized regularities of action, that is, of events in which we may be involved, we invent means to transform the world to adapt it to our needs (agriculture is a case in point), and in the process we transform ourselves to adapt ourselves to the world we have transformed. But what has this to do with language? Thanks to conceptualization and mathematization of regularities, language makes possible delayed, planned action meant to obtain at the appropriate perceptual moment .

The judges of the British captain mentioned above determined that he could, and therefore should, have saved his ship instead of abandoning it to the fire. Yet they were not present at the time of the events. This shows that conduct in response to a situation can be planned “on paper,” as it were,  according to the known regularities of action in the world, while the planner is not actually involved in the situation and is not being pressured to act by the urgency of the situation. This is a variety of deliberative reflection, that involves putting the world in abeyance by conceptualizing it through language and, by the same token, putting oneself in abeyance (or disengaging oneself) before acting.  However, to put the world in abeyance in a complex situation like the one just described one needs language to enunciate (based on past experience or experiment), the regular reaction to fire of the different materials of which the ship was made, under the climatic conditions that prevailed at the time. Abstract enunciative language that refers to conceptualized regularities in the action of objects in their perceptual absence makes possible these complex, ideal objectifications or conceptualizations of regularities. Only a linguistic species, like ours, can do that, for enunciative language renders possible to think of, or conceptualize , observed objective regularities in their absence, in pure hypothesis.    

Notice that these judges were not deliberating, they were considering whether the desirable action of fighting the fire could be successful given the objective conditions, because the realization of projects through action depends on natural regularities that are objective in the sense of being  independent of desire. Whether or not we like it, it takes time for the sugar to melt, as Bergson observed. Now, these conditions can be very complex, as Searle indirectly suggests ^(F14), and only the use of language –particularly the language of science, which, as a problem-solving activity is ultimately applicative– makes possible their knowledge and the storage of that knowledge in writing, destined to the satisfaction of those desires concerning which the stated information will be pertinent at one time or another. But also much of our everyday action is problem-solving (cf. Buber).

This is the activity to which Aristotle referred by the name of episteme, in Latin scientia. However, he misunderstood it as yielding necessarily true representational knowledge, because it was knowledge per causas , which were intelligible and, therefore, not contingent but necessary. Moreover, unlike myself, Aristotle was an objectivist (hence his “categories” of the objects known), an intellectualist (hence his intelligible  “substances”), and a realist concerning “really existing natures. ” But the very notion of sign-FOR skips the Serlean and Aristotelian-Thomistic step that requires theoretical representational linguistic understanding as a condition for action As indicated by Wittgenstein, following a rule or obeying an order (or, I add, responding to a signal) is not an interpretation or a verbalized understanding of the rule as an ‘object” of knowledge ^(F15); rather it is a result of the inherited tendency to respond to signs as signs-FOR, becausse perception is pespectival-practiocal . The Aristotelian “causes,” which are necessarily regular, are signs-For,  when conjoined with desires, because they can be used or interiorized as instruments to satisfy our desires . If I know that cold air, under certain conditions, regularly causes an undesirable cold, I can invent the means to raise the temperature in my home or to cover myself to avoid the cold..

What the activity we call “science” does is not, then, as Aristotle thought, to grasp intelligible and necessary laws or objective “natures.” Rather, through careful observation and measurement, to grasps regularities in the activity and the effects in the world in which we live, These remembered regularities, at the perceptual-interactive moment of application and under the appropriate conditions of desire, will function as signs-instruments FOR problem-solving action. (Notice that the important or remembered regularities enunciated by science are related to common, permanent human desires, such as health, security, etc, for the effects of the regular actions affect us favorably or unfavorably.).

To consider now Aquinas own example in I Meta, Lect. 1, as developed by Peghaire in his article, “A Forgotten sense, The Cogitative,

” Plato has been sick; his pulse was rapid, his temperature too high, his tongue coated – these are so many external sensations whic h I have made and noted in my memory. Some doctor, as I have seen for myself or been told, gave him a dose of a certain herb – more external sensations which I have simply noted. Now the sick man’s  pulse is back to normal, his temperature is lower, his tongue is cleared, and he is cured – a third series of external sensations also noted in my memory. Thereupon I said that Plato was cured of his fever by this medicine. I have made the same observations in the case of Socrates, Phaedo, Critias. and so forth... – These various observations concerning Plato, Socrates, Phaedo, Critias, were successively recorded in my memory, perhaps at widely different times. But now, I place them together in my actual, present consciousness; I remember them. Then, going from one to the other, I note the concrete similarity of concrete symptoms in the case of each of the sick men; I note that the four doses of medicine which cured tem show  a similarity of concrete characteristics;  I see that the concrete effect in the four cases was the same.  I have therefore a concrete knowledge of these similar instances under a common nature . Now this last named knowledge is what St. Thomas calls experimentum . He sees it as a collatio , that is to say, a gathering together, a collection of singular data going to make up a singular whole. - In this knowledge, the first thing I have is a series of what St. Thomas calls judgment of the senses.” ^(F16)

So far, Aristotle would say, we are on the level of perception-interaction, of “judgments of the senses.” We still do not know why the herb cures the illness. Now, the physiological  regularities with which physicians deal are more complex and less exactly measurable than the physical regularities with which physicists deal. The former cannot be enunciated in a simple mathematical statement, like the law of falling bodies on inclined planes. The mathematical law of physics, however –contrary to Aristotle’s assumption about “natures”– doesn’t explain or make intelligible why bodies fall the way they do on inclined planes, nor does it have ontological existence that corresponds  to what is known by a subject; it only renders their fall exactly predictable at the particular time of application, thanks to the measurements of time and space involved in the law. As a consequence, the law makes possible the instrumental use of the grasped regularity to achieve our human purposes.

Searle suggests that language makes possible complex conceptual objectifications ( Speech Acts, pp. 38-39, note 11 below) that combine a number of observed regularities under one enunciation. These are based on remembered regularities of the world, which are conceptualized in an enunciation and, when and if conjoined with a particular desire and the appropriate perceptual conditions, will become signs FOR action. In the absence of desire the registered regularities are only concepts,  potential signs FOR. Anyway those complex regularities couldn’t be remembered if they were not linguistically registered not as statements of fact but to serve as maps For action at the right perceptual moment when a desire can be realized. Thus, in conjunction with our human ability to plan action, instead of acting immediately and automatically according to the strongest desire, as animals do when perceptually confronted with signs, enunciative language makes possible complex delayed actions through non-representational but instrumental conceptualizations of regularities . In the case of animals, desire, given its overwhelming power over them because of their incapacity to conceptualize, literally causes their action, leaving no room for deliberation, as is the case with us humans.

But doesn’t this show that, after all, enunciative language is representational OF what is enunciated?   No, not in the only important sense of being objectively representational of something to be understood , but in the sense of referring to regularities to be appropriated by the concretely situated agent, regularities made relevant by desire, something that will function as a sign FOR action under the appropriate perceptual-interactive conditions . As indicated by Kuhn’s problem-solving, instrumental, applicative conception of science (by contrast to the representational “ontological ” conception involved in what he calls “textbook science”), this is the way in which scientific enunciations mean and in which their meaning is to be cashed.  If cashed, however, as if they were a representation of what is the case in reality, it results in the metaphysical conundrum that has infected Western philosophy from Plato and Aristotle through Kant and beyond . - This  understanding of enunciations as representations of what is the case is at the roots of the error into which philosophical objectifying REFLECTION has traditionally fallen. (From it resulted Aristotelian abstract metaphysics, a well as traditional metaphysical RATHER THAN REGULATIVE doctrinal theology, both of which are hopeless, misleading ventures). ^(F17)

An important observation is pertinent in this respect. Contrary to Searle’s conviction, enunciative language is no exception in functioning  as a sign OF something to be known rather than as a sign FOR performing an action. This is to say that all linguistic expressions that refer to particular objects are tacitly perlocutionary if conjoined with desire. Indeed, in its original usage, that is, in oral face-to face communication among agents who, by the very nature of oral communication, share the same perceptual situation, even enunciative language does not function as a sign OF something to be understood, namely, what is said or what is referred to. Rather, as is the case with all other signs, these expressions, too, functions as a signal FOR performing an action.

Ironically, this shows in Searle’s own example of the American soldier in Italy during World War II. In that example the enunciation “Ich kome...” is intended to substitute for “I am a German soldier.” However, conjoined with the assumed desire of the Italian soldiers to please their allies, this enunciation was actually  meant to elicit from the Italians the action of liberating the American, rather than, as Searle surmises, to terminate in the theoretical or purely objective awareness that the American was a German soldier

The reason why we get easily confused on this point, as Searle himself did, is that in the case of enunciative language –different in this from the speech acts possessing an explicit perlocutionary force– the action signaled is not specified, because (to refer again to the same Serlean example) the speaker rightly assumes the addressee’s desire to treat well his German allies, which will automatically convert the enunciation into SIGN FOR or instrument for action.

Like all other animals we come to the world ‘wired’ to REact in certain ways in response to certain stimuli.objects in order to satisfy our needs. The objects that are instrumental in satisfying  needs vary from species to species, as well as the needs and the response. The original complexes object-need-response characteristic of each species are hereditary. Both chicken and ducks instinctively follow the first animal that takes care of them at birth, but ducks follow it into the water, whereas chicken stop at the border of the water. Instincts (or instinctual conducts by contrast to responses) are complex, teleological  forms of behavior, inherited rather than learned . They are teleological in that they are meant to fulfill the purpose of satisfying a need of the animal, but they are not intentional, in that the animal doesn’t choose the behavior nor intends the purpose. A step lower in complexity than the just mentioned “conducts” are those inherited automatic “movements” characteristic of a species that we call “reflexes” or reflex rsponses . Such are the sucking reflex in newborn children at contact with the nipple, the vomiting reflex in response to certain scents or tastes, the toad’s reflex of sticking its tongue in the direction of whatever moves near its mouth and is of a size that can be ingested by the toe . Reflex movements are simple responses to a stimulus, which obtain automatically whether or not in the particular circumstances they are appropriate to satisfy a need. Instinctive conducts are complex and not purely automatic, in that the animal may adapt its conduct, according to the perceptual signs-FOR,  to obtain the desired result. In contrast to reflex movements and instinctive conducts, in which the goals to be obtained are inherited, stand those complex conducts in which both goals and means are invented. Agriculture is an example.   

Polanyi’s alternative conception of language

To begin with,  both Searle and Polanyi agree that language is a semantic system, that is, a system of signs. They disagree, however, concerning the way in which signs function when they are actually experienced as signs, that is, when we encounter them and are actually referred by them to what they signal. Searle takes his model from a misinterpretation of the way written language functions, which supposedly refers to a “meaning” to be understood and silently formulated .

Now, the first thing to realize regarding signs is that being referred by a sign is not the same as observing and becoming aware of the characteristics of the thing that is a sign. In the case of written language this is obvious. If I have a book in a language I do not know, I see perfectly the signs on the page, but I do not experience them as signs , for  they do not refer me to what they signal.

Secondly, regarding the experience of signs as signs  Polanyi claims that when a sign functions as a sign, the sign is not itself observed or perceived as such,  for it signals “tacitly” or practically rather than explicitly or enunciatively . This is why, in the case of the experiment with shock-producing syllables, Polanyi claimed that the subject didn’t know –in the sense of observing or becoming explicitly aware of the syllables that produced shocks; consequently, he could not say which were those syllables. Actually, the subject ‘knew’ those syllables only in their referring or signaling function, only in his actually being referred by them to their shock-producing  virtue.

Thirdly, not only was the subject’s knowledge of the shock-producing syllables “tacit,” in that the subject was not able to say which were those syllables, but the verbal identification of the syllables would have been  superfluous, according to Polanyi. The reason is that, contrary to Searle’s assumption, the syllables did not signify something to be understood, that is, the knowledge of the verbalizable fact that those syllables produced shocks; rather, they signaled to the subject to perform an action– the action of protecting himself as much as possible from the shock to come, hence the subject’s bracing or preparing himself for the shock to come...

Generalizing his misinterpretation of linguistic signs, Searle thinks that signs in general function as signs OF something to be understood and thereby known by the hearer. For his part, Polanyi claims that all signs, linguistic signs included , are signs FOR performing an action – the action signaled, that is. In other words, signs are meant to terminate in an action on the subject’s part, not in what Searle calls understanding of something signified.

Searle’s own farfetched example of the American soldier in Italy shows that –contrary to his own view–  this is indeed the case . For what the American soldier intended by saying “ Ich kome...,” was not that the Italians understand what the German sentence means, but that they perform the action of setting him free . In short, even in the case of linguistic enunciations , enunciations do not mean as a sign OF something to be known by the hearer, namely, what is enunciated, for the purpose of face-to-face enunciating is not making anything known (in the sense of understood), as Searle claims, but to elicit an action. In other words, according to  Polanyi, saying or enunciating is a signal FOR performing an action, not a sign of a meaning to be understood.  Which is to say that, pace Searle, enunciations mean perlocutionarily, that is as signals, not illocutionarily or enunciatively . The only difference between enunciations and other perlocutionary acts is that enunciations do not specify the action meant (as, for instance, commands and requests do),  but leave its determination to the linguistic agent according to his desires or needs in the circumstances in which the agent experiences the sign as a sign. As to why this difference, the reason is that genuinely l inguistic agents, unlike animals, are not inescapably led by their needs or desires but are capable, within limits, of deciding how to act, tanks to their capacity to conceptualize.

Far from being what is signified by a red traffic light, the linguistic enunciation –if it occurs at all–  is only meaningful as a  practical direction or rule for the agent to follow by performing  the conduct signaled. This is obvious in the case of an order or of a request – that is, in the case of what Searle calls perlocutionary acts– , for an order is to be obeyed and a request is to be granted by performing the action requested, not by conceiving or enunciating or even assenting to the representational statement of the action to be obeyed or of the action requested.. And the same is the case with oral enunciations and statements. For, contrary to what Searle thinks, saying is not a sign OF what is said, and so it is not meant as a sign to conceive or imagine (as a copy of)  what is said, but a sign FOR linguistic action in response . Much as is the case with a music partition, the appropriate comprehension of which consists, and is manifested in, the actual performance of the musical piece, not in any linguistic formulation. ^(F18)

Interiorization

After this long introduction, we are well prepared to understand Polanyi’s own presentation of his views, by contrast t o Searle’s.

What, precisely, is interiorized here? The stick, in the sense that rather than being perceived itself as an object in the environment,  it is assimilated to the agent’s body to the point that it ceases to count  as an external object and functions, rather, as perceived themselves, but function as an extension of the eyes of the agent to guide him in his action. an instrumental extension of the agent’s body, much as is the case with eyeglasses . When worn by an agent, the eyeglasses are not seen or Similarly, the blind man’s stick. Think of an agent in total darkness. His situation is comparable to that of a blind person. Instinctively, this person, in order to find his way, extends his arms so as to touch the objects that are on his way before hitting them, thus being able to avoid them, not by seeing them, but  by using the sensations in his hands as instruments to guide his steps .towards the place he wants to reach.

“The identification of tacit knowing with indwelling involves a shift of  emphasis in our conception of tacit knowing. We had envisaged tacit knowing in the first place as a way to know more than we can tell. We identified the two terms of tacit knowing, the proximal and the distal, and recognized the way we attend from the first to the second, thus achieving an integration of  particulars to a coherent entity to which we are attending. Since we were not attending to the particulars in themselves, we could not identify them; but if we now regard the integration of particulars as an interiorization , it takes on a more positive character. It now becomes a means of making certain things function as the proximal terms of tacit knowing, so that instead of observing them in themselves, we may be aware of them in their bearing on the comprehensive entity which they constitute. It brings home to us that it is not by looking at things, but by dwelling in them [FOR PURPOSES OF ACTION], that we understand their joint meaning.. By concentrating attention on his fingers, a pianist can temporarily paralyze his movement. ^(F19)

In connection with internalization Polanyi uses such terms as “inhabiting” or “indwelling.” Now, “inhabiting” or “dwelling in” are different from “perceiving” or “knowing,” which refer to the object perceived or known.  Polanyi’s notion of tacit knowledge would have gained much in precision if, instead of speaking of “the proximal term of  knowledge” he had specified that “inhabiting”, “indwelling,” etc., which  appear always in relation to interiorization,  refer to instruments used FOR A PURPOSE by an agent, not to objects known. Thus, the stick in the hands of the blind person in which the blind person “indwells” to find his way is not what the person knows but the instrument he uses to guide his steps to the place he wants to reach. Similarly, the eyeglasses worn by a myopic person. are not what the person who wears them  knows or sees, but the instrument he uses to guide his action to achieve the purposes he desires.

But why does Polanyi use the word ‘interiorization’? Because a shift takes place, as it were, from something perceived in the subject’s environment to the agent’s body as instrument for action to satisfy a need or a desire. . As already indicated, this shift may be illustrated by the example of eyeglasses . When a pair of glasses rest on a table and I look at them, they do not function as eyeglasses but as object of knowledge, as object known. I see the eyeglasses. When I wear the eyeglasses, however, I do not see the eyeglasses, I “inhabit’ them, that is, I use them as instruments to find my way as an agent in the realization of my projects, in the way I use my body when I act . . The eyeglasses are “interiorized” in the sense that they now function as an extension of my eyes, which I do not see but which I use as instruments for my action . My eyes only function as object known when I look at them in the mirror. But those are not my eyes that see , but my eyes seen as any other observed object. When my eyes see they  function as the instruments I use to act efficiently in the realization of my projects. Polanyi calls interiorization this shift of an object  from object known in the environment of the agent to its functioning as an extension of the agent’s own body when actually used as an instrument. When worn, the eyeglasses are not known themselves; they are not seen, anymore than our eyes are seen when we use them as instruments to act in the world. ^(F20)

            When a blind person uses a stick to find his way to the place to which he wants to go, he is guided by the sensations he experiences in his hands every time that the stick hits an object. However,  these sensations are not known in themselves by the blind person; rather, they function as “ that from which ” the person finds his way towards the place he wants to reach . Thus, if the blind person is asked to what he pays attention, he won’t mention the sensations he experiences in his hand, for he has no objective awareness of them. Yet he is aware of them in the sense that he uses the sensations transmitted by the stick as instruments to find his way. He ”inhabits” them, or “dwells in” them, much as when we walk we use the sensations we experience on the plants of our feet at each step we make; we do not know them, rather we transcend them as “that from which” we make the next step. The blind person doesn’t know the sensations he experiences in the palm of his hand, he knows them only tacitly in that he uses them to make his next step .  He ‘inhabits” them as we inhabit our body when we use it as an instrument to realize our projects. Thus, when  we walk we don’t pay attention to the sensations we experience on the plants of our feet every time they touch the ground. Rather; we “dwell” in those sensations to make the next step, we pass through them as “that from which” we make the next step, much as right now I pass through the sensations I experience at the tips of my fingers every time I type on the keyboard toward the word I want to write.

In The Tacit Dimension Polanyi initially explained TK as knowing more than one can say. His conceptual shift to the “interiorization” involved in TK helps him to become more precise regarding the nature of  TK. When playing the piano, which is an action, not a perception, the pianist is using the piano as an instrument to produce the desired music. In that case, the consciousness he has of the keys he touches is instrumental toward the realization of the desired music . If the word knowledge is to be employed in this context, it has to be described as “tacit,” for it is totally different from the objective consciousness or consciousness of object known, with which the ordinary notion of knowledge is commonly identified. So much so that, as just explained by Polanyi,  if, in the process of playing the piano, that is, of having tacit knowledge of his fingers and of the keys they touch, the pianist turns his attention to his fingers or to the keys and observes them, thereby turning them into objects of what we take for knowledge, automatically his activity of playing becomes impaired. Similarly, if, while reading a text, we shift our attention from the meaning we are getting through reading to the letters that compose the text in order to know or become aware of them as such, our reading becomes impaired. The reason for this is that while reading we only have a tacit awareness of the written signs, for we are not attending to them as such but using them as instruments to get their meaning. If we turn our attention to the signs, we gain what we ordinarily take for knowledge of their characteristics, but they cease to function as instruments that convey a meaning. The two forms of consciousness are not only different from each other but incompatible with each other, which is the reason why we cannot get the meaning of a text if we try to know the letter themselves.  

To summarize, Polanyi calls “interiorization” the special, not objective but instrumental, quasi oblique consciousness we have of what we  actually use as an instrument to achieve a particular purpose through action . And he calls it “interiorization,” because we assimilate it to our own body in action, since the consciousness we have of our own body when we act to achieve a  purpose is itself instrumental and the ultimate model of tacit knowledge:

“But our awareness of our body for attending to things outside it suggests a wider generalization of the feeling we have of our body. Whenever we use certain things for attending from them to other things,   in the way in which we always use our own body , these things change their appearance. They appear to us now in terms of the entities to which we are attending from them, just as we feel our body in terms of the things outside to which we are attending from our body. In this sense we can say that when we make a thing function as the proximal term of tacit knowing, we incorporate it in our body or extend our body to include it –so that we come to dwell in it.” ^(F21)

The generalization of the awareness of our body at the time of action as the model of the interiorization involved in TK reveals to Polanyi the mechanism of agency, which he exemplifies with  a large number of particular cases of ACTION. In the first place with the case of acquired bodily skills, such as playing the piano or typing, and with  the case of those more complex skills such as driving a car.  These skills are fundamentally similar to the way a blind person ‘drives, ’so to say, his own body with the help of a stick toward the place he want to reach. These skills  are acquired as a result of a long practice of interiorization. Thanks to repetition, the agent transforms the stick, the typewriter, the piano, the car into extensions of his own body by using them as he uses his own body, until the use of these instruments becomes habitual. At the end of that long practice of interiorization the use of those objects becomes as natural to the agent as the use of his own body in, say, the action of walking, which doesn’t require any other instrument than his own body. When a child learns to walk, he is guided by the sensations he experiences on the plants of his feet every time he makes a step; however, he does not focus his attention on those sensations, he does not focus his attention on those sensations, they are only “that from which ” he makes the subsequent steps towards the place he wants to reach..

The typewriter, the piano, the car are examples of material instruments – the technology invented and created by human agents to multiply the efficiency of their agency and extend its reach in order to satisfy first their elementary needs and then the desires that emerge as the transformation of the surrounding world allows the emergence of such desires. However, in addition to the invention of material instruments is the invention of symbolic (that is, semantic or signifying  instruments , among which the most important are spoken and written language, because they make possible the invention of all other symbolic instruments . such as mathematical symbols, scientific theories, etc.

After the case of acquired bodily skills as examples of TK, Polanyi refers us to the use of   maps by an agent who, with their help, attempts to find his way in an unfamiliar territory. Before analyzing this case, let us remark that the reading and actual use of maps are far more complex operations than they seem to be to those who have already mastered them. Actually, a child, even a child who has already acquired the skill of reading written language, is not thereby automatically capable of using a map to find his way in unfamiliar territory, much less of drawing a map to be used by others. The reason for this difficulty is that a map is not a picture of a territory, any more than a name is a picture of the object named. A map, when it actully functions as a map, is a symbolic SIGNAL of a territory, but not of a territory to be contemplated or observed but of a territory to be walked through reach a particular place.. In other words, not only is a map to be deciphered or ‘read,’ (but not interpreted) as all symbols are, but to be deciphered in relation to the action of moving through the territory to which the map refers, in order to reach particular places within that territory. In other words, a map is a symbolic instrument to be used by an agent to achieve his goal. That is why, if somebody claimed to understand a map but proved himself incapable of using it in practice, he wouldn’t know what he is saying. Indeed, since the map is an instrument for action, understanding it amounts to being able to manifest that understanding in one’s action. The mistake of the person who claimed to understand a map while being unable to use it would be comparable to the one Searle makes when he claims that a person who is given an order understands it if only he grasps objectively the meaning of the words, without realizing that an order is to be obeyed, not to be understood objectively. In short, the genuine understanding of a map consists in the performance of the conduct that the map is meant to render possible, that is, in the conduct of reaching the desired place within the territory to which the map refers, as the genuine understanding of a verbal joke is to be manifested in the affection of being actually amused by it.

Polanyi’s view of the reading of maps of the kind we are referring to goes as follows. The agent who reads the map must “inhabit” the map as he inhabits his body when he acts in order  to realize a project, for instance, when he is pounding a nail to hang a picture. In other words, he must make the nail function not as an object to be looked at and observed in itself, but as “that from which” he must act in order to arrive at the desired place, that is, as a SIGN FOR performing the action of pounding. In short,  TK is always practical, for it involves the use of an instrument an agent to achieve an intended goal, not a representation of object. ^(F22)

By now we won’t be surprised by Polanyi’s’ view  that scientific theories function instrumentally rather than representationally . In other words, we tend to consider the statement in which, for instance, a physical law is expressed as a symbolic representation of the intelligible ‘nature’ of the particular phenomenon to which the law refers. For his part, Polanyi argues (as does Kuhn) that such an interpretation is wrong. In his view, the statements of scientific laws are meant to be used by the scientific agent as instruments to guide his or her conduct in the solution of concrete problems related to the portion of the surrounding material world the law refers to, much as a map is meant to functions as an instrument for the person who uses it to find his way within the mapped territory.

In the following text,  Polanyi explains how theories function in the actual practice of science, that is, when the theoretical instrument is actually used rather than talked about :

“ To rely on a theory for understanding nature is to interiorize it. For we are attending from the theory to things seen in its light, and we are aware  of the theory, while thus using it, in terms of the spectacle that it serves to explain. This is why mathematical theory can be learned only by practicing its application: its true knowledge lies in our ability to use it.” ^(F23)

This text, quoted  in isolation from its context, may be too dense and too cryptic to make us understand how, in Polanyi’s view, scientific theories function. What is clear, at any rate,  is that Polanyi’s view is at odds with  the prevailing conception of science as an exact, or at least an increasingly approximative, representational  view of reality. Thomas Kuhn , who on this fundamental point  repeatedly indicates his agreement with Polanyi, expressed it forcefully:

“A scientific theory is usually felt to be better than its predecessors not only in the sense that it is a better instrument for discovering and solving  puzzles but also because it is somehow a better representation of what nature is really like. One often hears that successive theories grow ever closer to, or approximate more and more closely to, the truth. Apparently generalizations like that refer not to the puzzle solutions and concrete predictions derived from a theory but rather to its ontology, to the match, that is, between the entities with which the theory populates nature and what is ‘really there.’ Perhaps there is some other way of salvaging the notion of ‘truth’ for application to whole theories, but this one will not do.... the notion of a match between the ontology of a theory and its ‘real’ counter part in nature now seems to me illusive in principle” ^(F24)

What Kuhn is pointing out here is that the statements in which scientific theories are expressed are not meant to function as representations of reality but as symbolic or signifying instruments to be used by agents to solve practical problems. In this regard, though, scientific statements constitute no exception. Indeed, if Polanyi is right, no statement –whether scientific or other– is  meant to function as a  linguistice representation OF its referent; rather, all statements are meant to function as instruments or signs  FOR action at the appropriate time.

Searle and Polanyi on language

We are now well equipped to make sense of the differences between Polanyi and Searle concerning language.  At the end of chapter one I expressed my dissatisfaction with central points of Searle’s philosophy of language. Let us now contrast the two views, to find out both how they differ from each other, and whether that contrast throws any light on the points concerning which Searle’s position proved unsatisfactory.

The above indicates Searle’s conception of knowledge. By replicating in his own mind what the speaker intends to communicate,  thanks to the hearer’s understanding of what the speaker says, the hearer gains the knowledge of what the speaker means or wants to communicate. Thus, according to Searle,  at least in the case of linguistic communication, knowledge consists in the correspondence between the object to be known and what the knower comes to have in mind thanks to his knowing activity. Such, needless to say, is the ordinary notion of knowledge, which the Scholastics, following Aristotle, defined as adaequatio mentis ad rem : the conformation of the knower’s mind to the object. What Searle does is to extend to the field of linguistic communication the ordinary  notion of knowledge, that refers primarily to the conformity of the  knowing subject’s mind to the object in the perceptual world, that is, to “reality.” Now it is precisely regarding  this conception of knowledge that Polanyi parts ways with Searle. For Polanyi, knowing is not a matter of becoming aware of the object to be known by acquiring a sort of reproduction of it that corresponds to the object, and which is true in the measure in which it corresponds to the object. Rather, knowledge is tacit because it is essentially practical, not theoretical. It does not aim at reproducing the object in the mind, in which case the knowledge of the object would be explicit rather than tacit. Knowledge consists, rather,  in a practical  disposition to ACT in the way appropriate to realize a  goal of the subject. Thus, in the case of the shock-producing syllables, the subject has no awareness of the individual characteristics of the syllables; for that very reason the subject cannot say or express in words which are those characteristics of which he has no visual awareness. What the subject acquires, thanks to his past painful experience of being shocked, is not an image of the syllables, but the practical disposition to avoid the shock, which manifests itself in his bracing himself for the shock when the shock-producing syllables appear again on the screen, in order to protect himself as much as possible from the shock.

Whose conception of the relationship of knowledge to action is right: Searle’s or Polanyi’s?

Regarding those acts in which a perlocutionary effect is intended by the speaker, I have already shown how unpersuasive I find Searle’s view that in every act of communication the speaker intends to produce the knowledge of his communicative intentions –that is, the illocutionary effect–  and that this knowledge suffices to insure the success of the communicative act. For this means, concretely, that when I give you an order, what I intend primarily is that you understand and thereby become informed or acquire factual knowledge that I give you an order, not that you obey my order. Searle is so convinced that in the case of an order what the speaker intends is that his order be understood rather than obeyed that he considers the communicative exchange successful in the case in which the order is not obeyed as long as the order is understood by the hearer.

Why do I think that Searle is wrong on this point? Because this aspect of Searle’s characterization of linguistic exchanges is  at odds with the practice of all competent linguistic agents. It is true that I cannot disobey an order without understanding it and thereby knowing it. But what is in contention is what constitutes the appropriate “understanding” of an order and, in general, of all those linguistic acts that possess perlocutionary force, such as threats, warnings, promises, demands, etc. According to Searle, the primary intention of the speaker in all of  them is to inform the hearer of what he means, and only secondarily and accidentally to achieve the perlocutionary effect typical of the particular perlocutionary act, such as obeying it in the case of an order, expecting the fulfillment of the promise in the case of promising, etc.

But the linguistic facts contradict Searle’s opinion. Indeed, Searle’s opinion does not do justice to the practice of linguistic agents concerning the use of the indicative mood, on the one hand, and of the subjunctive and of the imperative moods, on the other. If  Searle were right, there would be no difference in actual use between the grammatical imperative, used in the case of orders, and the grammatical indicative, properly used only when one wants to inform of a fact. Furthermore, the imperative mood  would be totally superfluous,  for imperatives should be understood as  indicatives. Indeed,  if Searle were right, giving an order (in the imperative) would be exactly equivalent to saying or enunciating (in the indicative) that one gives an order. But such is not the case. As I have already argued, when I give an order, I do not say – in the sense of enunciating– something to be merely understood, to wit: that I give an order; rather, I say the order I give in the sense of giving an order to be obeyed. When I say something in the sense of enunciating, I refer my hearer to what I enunciate as something to be understood, that is, I intend to produce in my interlocutor the knowledge in which, according to Searle, the illocutionary effect consists. Therefore, if in giving an order I were enunciating that I give an order and the order I give, I should be satisfied if the addressee responded  “I understand your order.” But that is not the case. When I give an order –and this is why I use the imperative, not the indicative–  I expect and intend my order to be obeyed, which is the appropriate ‘understanding’ of an order by a competent linguistic agent.

Moreover, Searle’s opinion is also at odds with the practice of linguistic agents even in the case of statements . For, as I have already argued, contrary to what Searle thinks, in  real-life situations, by contrast to the artificial classroom situation in which people are learning (to interpret) a second language, linguistic agents do not consider their linguistic action successful if a purely theoretical  understanding of what is said obtains; success, for linguistic agents, is identical with linguistic action. Cf. GEOMETRY AS APPLICATIVE.           

Why does Searle follow Aristotle in identify saying with enunciating?  Because in the case of both their entire conception of language is based not on their own experience as linguistic agents  who actually participate in actual linguistic interactions. Rather,  as  curious philosophers who overhear the words said by those who participate in communicative interactions, or who reflect after the fact on the words said by themselves  and others when they  participated in communicative interactions. I underline “the words said,” to indicate that Searle’s characterization of language, in spite of his disclaimers, focuses on the words said in their conventional-dictionary meaning, regardless of the conduct and the intentions of those who say or hear them as actual participants in linguistic exchanges. This amounts to identifying linguistic meaning with saying in the sense of enunciating something, and, consequently, it amounts to considering the conduct of the linguistic agents as accidental to linguistic meaning, rather than integral to, or constitutive of, linguistic meaning . Clearly, however, this is not the kind of interiorization through agency through which children learn to play baseball, without being able to enunciate the rules they supposedly follow.

Thus Searle excludes from linguistic meaning anything that in a linguistic exchange is not  words in which something is said or enunciated as to be known. Such is the case with  the actions and affections intended by the speaker in the communicative exchange, which consequently are excluded by Searle from being constitutive elements of linguistic meaning. That, indeed, was the reason why, in his example of the American soldier captured by the Italians , Searle would deny that the action by which the American tricked the Italians into believing that he was a German soldier by uttering the sounds “ Ich komme von dem Land wo die Zitronen  blühen ” was a linguistic action, since the words expressed did not enunciate the action intended.

The exclusion from linguistic meaning of whatever is not explicitly said in words is reflected in the separation Searle postulates between the illocutionary and the perlocutionary effects, as well as in the secondary and merely accidental role he attributes to the latter . Searle himself , in explaining his principle of expressibility that  “whatever can be meant can be said,” implicitly acknowledges that whatever is not said in words cannot be meant, for he excludes from meaning the perlocutionary effects. ^(F25)

In short, because he identifies linguistic meaning with what is expressed in words that supposedly say or enunciate something to be understood and thereby known, Searle automatically excludes from linguistic meaning those no less linguistic effects that do not consist in words that express something to be understood, namely, the perlocutionary effects. No wonder, then, that  the way in which language functions in the artificial context of a course of a language for foreigners, in which only understanding  what is said counts as linguistic, to the systematic exclusion of any of the affections or actions intended by the speaker, ends up appearing to Searle as the paradigmatic model of the linguistic situation for Searle. Indeed it corresponds to his defectie philosophy of language.

Now, to fully understand the differences between Searle and Polanyi, we have to realize that Searle’s linguistic philosophy amounts to an application to the field of language of the Platonic-Aristotelian epistemology inherited by the Western philosophical tradition. In that tradition,  knowledge consists, on the subject’s part,  in the representational conformity of his mind to reality, and, on the object’s part, in the ontological correspondence or conformity to what is in the subject’s mind. By contrast,  Polanyi’s conception of tacit knowledge constitutes an alternative to that tradition, for tacit knowledge is not a representation of what is the case but an instrument  to be used y the agent to achieve a desired goal,

As we shall now see, Polanyi does not contend that the Searlean perlocutionary effects as Searle conceives them, that is, as separated from the illocutionary effect, are a constitutive element of linguistic meaning, for the Serlean conception of the illocutionary element as different from the perlocutionary presupposes the legitimacy of his conceptual distinction between them. Polanyi contends, rather, that this Serlean distinction reflects the separation between the knowing subject and the agent that is inherent to theWestern epistemology inherited from Aristotle and Plato that begins by reflecting on knowledge as different from and separable form action . The Searlean distinction between illocutionary and perlocutionary effects is possible only on the wrong assumption , common to Aristotle and Plato, that knowledge and action stand to each other in the relationship presupposed in the epistemology inherited from Plato and Aristotle by the Western philosophical tradition.

In this process, through interiorization , the so-called ‘object’ plays the role of instrument for the realization of a desired end, not of representation of anything. Interiorization is practical in the sense that it is achieved and becomes fixed  or habitual through  practice of the game not through verbal formulation of the rules. A child learns to play baseball, not by formulating the rules repeatedly ( for he doesn’t  know them), but by ‘following’ them, that is,  by imitating again and again in his own practice of the game the practice of those who play the game competently ,until following the rules becomes habitual. Later, through reflection, the adult may be able to formulate the rules of the game. However, neither is this belated formulation necessary for the correct performance by the child, nor is the formulation equivalent to the performance.

Let us, therefore, review in detail Polanyi’s views on TK, with special attention to the role played by action instead of enunciation of the characteristics of the object of which the subject is aware.

The contrast between Searle’s and Polanyi’s respective conceptions of the way in which language functions as well as of the place action occupies therein will prove enlightening.

In distinguishing the illocutionary from the perlocutionary effects, Searle isolates the illocutionary effect from the perlocutionary ones and subordinates the latter to the former. The underlying reason for this unique primacy attributed by Searle to the illocutionary  speech act over all the others is that the illocutionary effect consists in knowledge, and knowledge of what the speaker means is, in Searle’s opinion, the necessary condition for the success of all the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker, whereas the success of the illocutionary intentions of a speaker does not depend on the success of any of the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker. To put Searle’s point concretely, a speaker cannot say anything without intending to convey to the hearer the knowledge of what he means, but he can say something without producing or intending to produce any other effect than this knowledge. Correlatively, the hearer cannot fulfill the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker without knowing those intentions, that  is, without the illocutionary effect obtaining, but the hearer can know the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker, thereby insuring the success of the communicative act, without necessarily fulfilling the speaker’s perlocutionary intentions. Still more concretely: if I do not know what you mean when you ask me to lend you  my pen, I cannot lend you my pen, but I can know that you ask me for my pen, and not lend it to you. Moreover, you can produce in me an illocutionary effect without intending any perlocutionary effect. For instance, in Searle’s interpretation of saluting,  when you salute me, all you intend  to produce in me is the knowledge that you mean to salute me, and the communicative exchange succeeds if only I gain that knowledge. So, Searle concludes, knowledge is independent from action, for it may obtain in the absence of any action,  but action is dependent on knowledge

Polanyi, for his part, construes the relationship between knowledge and action in a totally different way. He does not ignore that knowledge involves a relationship to action. On the contrary, he sees action as an intrinsic component of knowledge. Indeed,  he identifies tacit knowledge with disposition to act and with the actual appropriate action when the right opportunity arises . Given this intrinsic relationship of knowledge to action, Polanyi would necessarily deny that knowledge is independent from action, as Searle claims. In other words, for Polanyi, the particular knowledge involved in the oral communicative exchange is ESSENTIALLY practical, that is, bound to terminate in action, not in a purely theoretical awareness of a fact, as it is for Searle. For example, knowing an order received does not consist, according to Polanyi, in becoming aware of the order as an objective fact, but in the practical disposition to obey the order received, and in actually obeying it at the appropriate time. Similarly, according to Polanyi, knowing a promise received does not consist in becoming  aware of that fact, as one becomes aware of a color when one sees it in the course of an experiment in a laboratory, but in actually bracing oneself for the fulfillment of the received promise at the appropriate time. Thus, if you promise to repay me the $100.00 you owe me when I come to pick them up at your office at 3.00 P.M., the appropriate understanding of your promise does not consist in my registering that fact and then doing nothing, as Searle contends,  but in expecting the fulfillment of your promise and in the action of actually coming to your office at 3.00 P.M. to collect my money.

Not all saying is enunciative

Neither Searle nor Aristotle explicitly claims that it is. However both of them do that implicitly, for both of them attribute a  foundational primacy to the enunciative speech act over all the others. Searle, because he  thinks that enunciative saying is the necessary condition for the success of all the non-enunciative or perlocutionary speech acts. Hence the primacy he attributes to the enunciative speech act in communication. For, in Searle’s view,  this speech act enunciates what the speaker attempts to communicate as something to be understood, and if the hearer doesn’t understand first what the speaker attempts to communicate he cannot possibly execute any of the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker. As for Aristotle, who is not primarily interested in communication but in the certainty of knowledge, his primary interest  in the enunciative speech act is due to the fact that it is the only speech act (NO!)   that can be true or false, in the sense that by enunciating what is the case it implicitly claims to correspond to what is the case, that is, to express genuine knowledge rather than mere opinion. In other words, it claims that what is said is true and, therefore, can be a valid link in a logical or demonstrative chain of statements. Notice that Aris, being a realist, assumes that the representation of reality in the mind automatically corresponds to what is the case –an assumption that Kant will dispute..

In both cases, enunciation means  a valid description or replication , be it of what the speaker has in mind and intends to communicate (Searle’s case), or of what is the case extra-mentally (Aris’s case). In other words, in both cases the assumption is that knowledge functions by similarity or correspondence between what the knower is aware of and what the hearer becomes aware of, or what is in the mind of the knower and what is the case in reality. But what if knowledge or linguistic enunciations are not supposed to represent what is the case but rather , in conjunction with a desired end, to function for the needy agent as a signal for action without any linguistic translation, as is the case with animals?
