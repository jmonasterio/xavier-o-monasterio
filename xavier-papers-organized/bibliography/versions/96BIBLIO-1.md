78: Athens, 5th BC: A change towards a society in which what people thought and said was beginning to be more important than what was actually the case. It was this widening gulf between rhetoric and reality which led Plato in the Gorgias to contrast rhetoric and philosophy, and to condemn the practice of the first, and then later in the Phaedrus to argue en favor of a reformed rhetoric based on dialectic and psychology as a possible servant of philo.

80-82: Gorgias held that things cannot be apprehended nor communicated by human beings, for we communicate through speech or logos, and this logos cannot be the things themselves. Radical gulf between logos and things referred to. To the extent that a logos claims faithfully to reproduce reality it is deception. Yet there are two kinds of logoi, one better than the other. The superiority of one logos depends on the presence of specific features. The study of these is the study of the art of rhetoric, and their successful development is the source of the power of logos over souls which is entitled "Psychagogia" or the winning of men's souls in Plato's Phaedrus (261a).

85: Diogenes Laertius quotes the famous statement, apparently from the beginning of Protagoras' writings: 'Man is the measure of all things, of things that are as to how they are, and of things that are not as to how they are not."

86-87: The problem of likeness and correspondence : Protagoras would say that the wind is cold to the one who feels  cold, and not to the other. This rejects the everyday view that the wind in itself is either cold or not cold, and that one percipient is right and the other wrong. But at least three possibilities remain: (1) There is no wind at all, but two private winds, my wind which is cold and yours which is not. (2) There is a (public) wind, but it is neither cold nor warm. The coldness only exists privately for me when feeling the cold. The wind itself exists independently of my perceiving, but its coldness does not. (3) The wind in itself is both cold and warm. Warm and cold are two qualities which can coexists (causally?) in the same physical object: I perceive the one, you the other. - [Add to this the problem of correspondence in the case of negative statements and of "négatités."]

94-95: There has recently been a reassessment of the philosophical interpretation of the verb "to be" in Greek, both before and after the Sophists. Charles Kahn reduced both the predicative and the existential use to a more ultimate use which is nearer to the predicative than to the existential. Then G.E.L. Own argued that in Plato's Sophist the argument neither contains nor compels any isolation of an existential verb, and that it turns out to be primarily an essay on reference and predication. Then Mourelatos concluded that Parmenides was not concerned primarily with existence and non-existence, but with predication. All this amounts to a massive shift of emphasis, away from the view that much of Greek philo was concerned primarily with problems of existence.

97: Gorgias: White objects, if thought, require white thoughts, and objects that are require, if thought, thoughts that are. Correspondence between thoughts and things .

102-104 : Xerox In the Phaedo (discussion of the new method: 89c11-102a1) Plato gives his answer to the antilogikoi on the subject of the two-opposed-logoi doctrine. As it is expressed in terms of the theory of Forms (where the contradictions between appearences do not obtain) it is not likely to have been expressed in such developed form by Socrates himself. But the phrase "the logos judged to be the strongest" (100a4) is strongly reminiscent of the best or strightest logos in sophistic discussions.

136-137 : Xerox Plato on whether virtue can be taught. It can, if it is knowledge. But perhaps it is only right opinion ( orthe doxa ), which can be as good a guide as knowledge for the purpose of acting rightly. Right opinion is acquired, but not by teaching since teaching is to be related to knowledge only. The position of statesmen, who act on right opinion, is no different from the position of prophets and tellers of oracles, who under divine inspiration utter many things that are true but have no knowledge of what they are saying . Plato, of course, cannot bring himself in the Meno to grant that the Sophists act under any such inspiration.

163-167: The Sophists, religion, and the gods. By a shallow analogy between the age of the Sophists and the 18th Aufklärung, the sophistis movement has been taken as one of revolt against religion and divine authority and towards a rationalist and humanistic outlook. But Greek religion was never unified, it was regional, had no creeds, and no definite notion of divine revelation like that contained in the Bible. Like Plato, some Sophists rejected myths in which the gods are portrayed as thieves, cheats, adulterers, gluttons and seducers. And  Protagoras was agnostic as to the nature (or even the existence?) of the gods: "Concerning the gods I am not in a position to know either that (or how) they are or that (or how) they are not, or what they are like in appearance; for there are many things that are preventing knowledge, the obscurity of the matter and the brevity of human life." We are told that the statement came at the beginning of one of Protagoras' writings.

192: "The Eleatics, by their primitive limitation of the term 'being' to what is one, unchanging and timeless, had driven practical people like Protagoras to the opposite extreme of subjectivism, a denial of all being in the Eleatic sense. Plato, convinced that any explanation of phenomena must still allow for an eternal and changeless being over and above them, contrasted Sophists as 'those who take refuge in the darkness of non-being' with philosophers who are 'devoted to the nature of being' ( Soph . 245a). He meant, as Aris pointed out ( Metaph . 1026b14), that the Sophists recognized only accidental as opposed to essential being, that is, the conditional and relative as opposed to the self-existent or absolutely existent. The way to these useful distinctions had b een closed for a time by the blunt antithesis of Parmenides, and they were only established by Plato and Aris. Obviously Protagoras's 'what appears to me and is for me' had not existence in the Eleatic or Platonic sense (in which 'what is' was completely inaccessible to the senses), and Gorgias brought this opposition fully into the open, and took the Eleatic bull by th horns, by boldly proclaiming that 'nothing exists.'"
