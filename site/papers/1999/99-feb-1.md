- I -

Desirous knowledge       

“Our body is the ultimate instrument of all our external knowledge, whether intellectual or practical. In all our waking moments we are relying on our awareness of contacts of our body with things. Our own body is the only thing in the world which we normally never experience as an object, but experience always in terms of the world to which we are attending from our body. It is by making this intelligent use of our body that we feel it to be our body, and not a thing outside” (Michael Polanyi, The Tacit dimension , 1965, pp. 15-16).

“A theory may be set out on paper as a system of rules... Mathematical theory reaches the highest perfection in this respect. But even a geographical map fully embodies in itself a set of strict rules for finding one’s way through a region of otherwise uncharted experience. Indeed all theory may be regarded as a kind of map extended over space and time” (Polanyi, Personal Knowledge: Toward a Post-Critical Philosophy (Harper k& Row, New York 1958, p. 4).

Aris distinguishes between a   knowledge-process that terminates in rational assent to what is the case, that is,  a knowledge-result that consists in correspondence or identity with the real, and a   knowledge-process that in addition to correspondence or identity and on the basis of it moves to and terminates in action as a different kind of knowledge-result. He also distinguishes between the pursuit of knowledge for its own sake , that is, for the sake of coming to grasp what is the case, and the pursuit of knowledge for the sake of its utility for something else to be attained through action . This implies a third distinction, namely, between those objects of knowledge which are only desirable as objects of knowledge for their own sake, as all objects of knowledge are, and those objects of knowledge which are useful and can therefore  be desired for the sake of something else.

  For Aris, knowledge is always knowledge of what is the case–adaequatio mentis ad rem–for he has no other notion of knowledge. In this sense, there is only one kind of knowledge–specular knowledge. But the knowledge of some of what is specularly known or to be  known may be useful as a means to satisfy the needs or desires of the human being or of animals, and therefore such knowledge may be pursued for the sake of achieving that satisfaction, while some of what is known may not be useful and is simply desirable for its own sake.

People can pursue knowledge either for its own sake, for they naturally desire to know, or for the sake of its utility to satisfy other needs and desires. Animals, by contrast, pursue knowledge only insofar as it is useful for the satisfaction of the inherited needs of their species. In other words, they don’t desire knowledge as such; they only desire the objects which can satisfy their needs.

This is to say that, as far as people are concerned, knowledge has always value as an end and, in addition, sometimes,  instrumental value as a means. For animals, though, knowledge is only valuable as a means, it only has instrumental value, it cannot be desired or pursued for its own sake.

However, animals do not know theoretically or specularly that a certain piece of knowledge has instrumental value. Therefore they don’t desire or pursue knowledge at all, not even for the sake of something else. What they desire and pursue is what can satisfy their natural, passively received needs, in concrete situations.

For example, a lion desires food. If  in the past it found food near a lake, it will go back to the lake in search of food, which is the desired object pursued through its action. This going back to the lake is made possible by memory of the past success, which guides the desirous imagination’s project toward the future success.* The lion, however, doesn’t know theoretically the instrumental value of going to the lake; its action is automatically guided toward the lake as a result of past experience , in a Skinnerian way. For the animal, to know means to pursue successfully through action  guided by desire. Desire, however, as Aris suggests, is not only prompted by the presence of the object desired but also, in locomotive animals, by memory and imagination based on past experience of successful action. Hence animal “prudence.’ Thus, the duck follows the leader as its natural protector by instinct, that is, by inherited desire, and this works successfully most of the time; but the chicken also learns to become a leader on the basis of imitation and past successful action. This is what Aris calls “practical knowledge.”

*[ THUS AN ‘IMAGE,’ PAST AND PRO-JECTED  (WHICH MAY NOT BE VISUAL OR PERCEPTUAL AT ALL IN ANIMALS, BUT MERELY A HABITUAL ‘MAP FOR ACTION’ INSCRIBED IN THEIR BODIES, AS OUR DRIVING SKILL IS) IS INVOLVED IN THE PROCESS. The image, however, is not ‘corresponding’ in the sense of replicating or being similar to the desired object. Rather, it functions like a practical map that guides the body’s motion toward the realization of the project when desire is present, much as a seen map guides me to my desired destination, or a  formula in physics guides the student toward the theoretical solution to the problem (which functions as a map for the practical solution), and this theoretical map guides the engineer toward the practical solution to the problem. - The importance of this ‘image’ is that, as supposedly resembling to or replicating the object, it is at the roots of our notions of knowledge, truth, and reality. In us people these wrongheaded notions arise out of our linguistic capacity as manifested in statements. Now, spoken statements by desirous agents to other agents function as maps, but   written , reflected upon, objectified statements, or disputed, not believed, objectified, reflected upon oral statements are easily interpreted as being  pictures.   On the other hand, our linguistic capacity , by objectifying our successive observations and making them systematic, the way Galileo did with his inclined plane, allows us to reach explanatory theories which, when formulated, function as practical maps which guide our actions, though we mistakenly tend to confer on them the value of ontological re-presentations. This latter ‘scientific’ activity is the one referred to by Aris under the name of “ ars ” in the first lesson of the metaphysics, which makes it possible for us the discovery of “causes,” on which Platonizing Aris mistakenly confers ontological value. Lacking linguistic, objectifying capacity, animals cannot enhance the span of their successful action through explanatory maps of the behavior of things, much less tailor the environment to their needs through genuine invention and technological instrumentality.]

Because people need food, they, too, may happen to go to the lake in their search for food. If this move is particularly successful in terms of getting food, they will, like animals, return  to the lake in search for food, mechanically.  That is, their action will automatically integrate the pursuit of the means into their pursuit of the food–going to the lake will become a portion of their practical map toward food. However, because people are linguistic, they can also reflectively judge-state to themselves that there is food by the lake and that going to the lake is the means to get it. Moreover,  they can communicate their useful knowledge to others who have a practical interest in acquiring it, because it is useful to them. Here appears the distinction between  speculative or theoretical knowledge and practical knowledge . The judgment-statement “There is food by the lake” supposedly corresponds to what is the case, grasps what is the case. And this is factual,  independent of its practical value, and can be verified by others. The notions of knowledge as correspondence to what is the case, as adaequatio mentis ad rem, and of truth as reflective realization of correspondence have appeared, too. Now judgments and statements refer to objects of contemplation, not to instrumental complexes in which the subject is related with distances of exposure.

Before reaching this theoretical or merely specular stage, however, a practical stage took place in which going to the lake was grasped as a means of satisfying hunger, as instrumental for the satisfaction of the need for food. And this instrumentality was revealed only because food was desired. Initially, going to the lake was not a means. When, by chance, going to the lake happened to lead to the satisfaction of desire, it became means, thanks to memory and imagination. As Sartre says, there needs to be a project, a desire to be satisfied or a fear to be allayed, for the coefficient of instrumentality–positive or negative–of things to be revealed. Thus going to the lake as instrumental to get food in the practical map of the organism precedes the factual and objective. And it interests or is relevant only to desirous creatures who need food.. So what appears to be purely objective is potentially instrumental, and it is not important in itself but because of its potential instrumentality.  For angels who have no material needs there would be no factual perceptual gestalt of “there is food (or animals) by the lake,” because all these supposedly objective ‘entities’ are abstracted and degraded from desirous interaction and exposure. Hodological space, instrumental space, refers to the desirous body center of instrumentality. And each body as center of projects and as exposed to action by others is temporally and spatially situated–both in a passive and in an active capacity--toward the future.  

This shows the primacy of the practical over the speculative, that is, of the interested desires over the desire to know. However, when things are under control, people, unlike animals, can reverse their dependence on the environment. That is, they do not merely respond to existing conditions, but tailor environmental conditions to their needs through invention, create conditions favorable to the satisfaction of their needs..

It is when people reflect on themselves as knowers, when they attempt to see the eye seeing, when they convert the subject-agent who faces the future by way of decision into object seen, that the correspondence theory of knowledge and truth arises and the intrinsic connection between ‘image-map’ connected with action through desire vanishes from sight.

There is, however, a point concerning people’s natural desire to know on which Aristotle is right. Animals are not contemplative in any way; they are always in the mood of exposure, connected exclusively to what is relevant to their needs as members of a particular species. People, by contrast, read, listen to narrations, watch plays and television... for the pleasure of it.  And people are not satisfied with grasping what is the case; they want to control what will be the case. They ask for the explanatory reason of what is the case; they want to understand, they look for the intelligibility of what is the case.  Thus, they reflect on what is the case in order to understand it. Now, all reflection takes place in a moment of disengagement, of what Sartre calls “nihilation.”  But the subject who reflects is not a timeless, neutral, disinterested object. Aris speaks abstractly of the natural desire to know, as if it were not the desire of this or that spatially and temporally and culturally situated subject, and as if the desire to know were always and primarily of the scientific type–about ideal things to be asented to and explained, without distances of exposure, and therefore as if it were always disinterested and objective desire. This, indeed, is Aris’s model of knowledge–‘scientific’ knowledge. Actually, however, the subject who reflects  belongs to a particular culture that shapes his or her self--understanding and his or her understanding of the world. He or she finds himself or herself in particular circumstances, playing particular cultural roles, and has a unique personal past and acquired habits. Furthermore,  there is reflection and reflection. There is the reflection of deliberation in order to act (be it functional or properly reflective reflection), and there is the aesthetic reflection in order to enjoy as a distraction, and there is the scientific reflection in order to understand and explain the regularities of the universe as a purely ideal causal order in which the subject himself or herself is not immediately situated , etc.  

- II -

Desirous knowledge

According to Nussbaum, Aris wondered why not all knowledge terminated in action . His conclusion, at least at a certain point in his development,  was that, unlike speculative knowledge, only practical knowledge was informed by desire–that is by interested desire. NOW THIS TRAIT OF PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE IS NOT SERIOUSLY TAKEN INTO CONSIDERATION BY DEVEREUX , according to whom what came to distinguish practical from speculative knowledge for Aris at his most unPlatonic is (a) that it is pursued for different goals –for the sake of knowing as an end, or for the sake of knowing as a means to the end of acting appropriately --and (b) that it deals with particulars, namely, actions. [IT IS PURSUED FOR DIFFERENT ULTIMATE GOALS, BUT IN BOTH CASES IT AIMS AT KNOWLEDGE THAT CONSISTS  IN CORRESPONDENCE OR INTENTIONAL ‘IDENTITY.’ MOREOVER, IT DEALS WITH PARTICULARS RATHER THAN WITH UNIVERSAL AND NECESSARY OBJECTS. IN BOTH CASES, HOWEVER,  THE OBJECTS ARE OBJECTS OF KNOWLEDGE, THOUGH THEY ARE NOT KNOWN  WITH THE SAME PRECISION, NOT OBJECTS OF DESIRE] . Perhaps desire is included by Devereux under practical interest, but this is not explicitly said or even taken into account by Aris. In addition, since practical knowledge is still deemed to be knowledge, the realization that it does not involve truth-correspondence is not reached. Let us then begin again with the Metaphysics.

Aris distinguishes between knowledge that terminates in grasp of what is the case, and knowledge that terminates in action--“desirous knowledge,” Nussbaum calls it. It is in the latter context, I think, that Aris speaks of animal and human prudence : Ex praeteritorum memoria de futuris providentia, aestimatio de convenientibus prosequendis et fugiendis nocivis. In hominibus quidem est prudentia secundum quod ex ratione deliberant quid eos oporteat agere–recta ratio agibilium. Iudicium de rebus agendis non ex rationis deliberatione, sed ex quodam naturae instinctu prudentia in aliis animalibus dicitur. Unde prudentia in aliis animalibus est naturalis aestimatio de convenientibus prosequendis et fugiendis nocivis, sicut agnus sequitur matrem et fugit lupum.

In both cases there is positive preference for what is ‘conveniens’; and negative preference against what is ‘nocivum.’ Only preferential, desirous beings can be motivated to act, either by actively looking for the desired object in the case of locomotive, prudent animals, or by actually being exposed to the desired or naturally harmful object by chance. Animal preference is partly inherited (sicut agnus sequitur matrem et fugit lupum) and partly acquired through experience and memory (as to the successful means to attain the natural ends). But animals inherit their ends to which all their actions are applicative means, some of which means are learned through experience and some genetically transmitted. As for people, as Maslow suggests, most of their preferences are culturally shaped on the basis of inherited needs, rather than merely inherited. But what is important is that, within limits, people can deliberate because their desires (whether inherited, or culturally shaped, or reflectively invented) are not–at least not always--determinant of conduct. People can choose their ends or goals and, consequently and correlatively,  their means , which is what they do through deliberative thinking, though their deliberation itself may be heavily shaped by their culture. Furthermore, their means are not passively received but learned through trial and error or through scientific experiment and explanation. Furthermore, as clarified by Marx, some ends and means are historically conditioned, in that they become possible in the  process of people transforming their environment to tailor it to their needs.

[The point that people ex ratione deliberant quid oporteat eos agere–recta ratio agibilium– might suggest that there is a natural, objective order of ends or goals to be discovered rationally and, consequently, to be  pursued as goals by all people. Aris’s own investigation of what people as people ought to do, the goals they ought to pursue and the standards by which they ought to regulate  their preferences and their conduct, seems to suggest such a thing. And, I think, the tradition of ‘natural law’ is based on the understanding that there is such a natural, objective order. Now this issue is all-important for my investigation.]

[I think the just mentioned view is typical of the confusion between speculative and practical knowledge, where ‘human nature’ is misunderstood as paralleling the explanatory and supposedly specular necessary nature of things . However, in the order of exposure–and goals belong in that order, as much as means–a goal is a goal only if and when it is chosen and actually pursued as a goal. To say, for instance, that God is the goal of human life, or the goal of all humans, is, in a certain way, meaningless, a pious desire by someone who believes in God and attempts to subordinate his or her conduct to Him. Indeed a goal that doesn’t actually function as a goal by shaping the conduct of a person in its pursuit is no goal at all. Furthermore, I think that Aristotle himself suggested the contrary when he argued that only the morally well trained would ‘see’ the goals that ought to be pursued. That is, only those who are trained to believe in God practically, or who through grace come to believe in God, can ‘see’ in a practical way that He ought to be the ultimate goal in all their actions. ]    

Aris also distinguishes between knowledge as a goal for its own sake (propter ipsum congnoscere, propter ipsum scire, cuiusmodi sunt scientiae mathematicae [scientiae autem dignitatem ostendit per hoc quod naturaliter desideratur ab omnibus tamquam finis], and knowledge as a means for its usefulness for something else (propter necessitatem actionis ad utilitatem vitae, propter voluptatem, ad introductionem in aliis scientiis sicut scientiae logicales). Symptomatically, Aris considers the first kind of knowledge superior to the second and the  speculative sciences superior to the practical ones : Illi artifices sunt sapientiores quorum scientiae non sunt ad utilitatem inventae, sed propter ipsum scire, cuiusmodi sunt scientiae speculativae [ See “\wp\98\apr-2" and “Monas\bible-5 "].

Knowledge satisfies people’s natural, disinterested desire to know, but it also is sometimes conducive to satisfy their need to act successfully. The assumption is that knowledge, which consists in correspondence or intentional identity with the real ( for Aris has not two conceptions or definitions of knowledge ), apart from being a good in itself, is also a good in that it is necessary for successful action. THIS ASSUMPTION IS AT THE ROOTS OF THE PROBLEM.

Animals spontaneously desire knowledge only for the sake of acting successfully. [Rather, animals desire their natural, passively received goals, and means become automatically integrated into that instrumental complex).  People, too, much like animals, sometimes spontaneously desire knowledge for the sake of acting successfully; and sometimes, unlike animals,  deliberately pursue knowledge for that purpose. In addition, however, people sometimes do what animals cannot do, to wit: they spontaneously desire knowledge for its own sake, for the sake of knowing the truth.

Concerning the desire of knowledge for the sake of successful action, animals always, spontaneously rather than deliberately, desire knowledge in order to attain their natural ends, whereas people can desire this useful knowledge deliberately. In both cases, however, the Aristotelian assumption is that knowledge is necessary for successful action. This amounts to saying that in order to act correctly one has to see specularly  what is the case before acting, in which case practical knowledge would require and would be made possible by speculative knowledge. But such assumption is wrong. In fact, prudence involves no correct seeing, no specular knowledge at all, since both the action and the effect are still future; so there cannot be any specular correspondence whatsoever between what is decided and projected and what is actually achieved. Prudence consists in choosing to act and actually acting in the appropriate way to achieve the desired end. In the case of learned prudence in animals, that is, of ‘discovered’ means to achieve their natural ends, the initial discovery may be by chance or imitation; after that, as Skinner says, it is just a matter of automatism: the successful conduct will tend to be repeated under similar (perceived) circumstances.

The case of prudence in people is far more complex. In people, too, prudence consists in acting in the appropriate way to achieve the desired end. Hence, no specular knowledge involved. But people possess language and they are members of a culture that teaches them linguistically which ends are to be pursued, that is, which desires are to be satisfied and how, and members of a culture that rewards and punishes them for their conduct, and explains why. This is an education of desires. However, there may be conflict between the desires and goals instilled by the culture and the individual’s desires, as well as conflict between the goals and desires of individuals (cf. Freud’s Civilization and its discontents ). Hence discussion and persuasive rhetoric and deliberation about goals. (Animals have no conflict of desires to be resolved ).  

The possession of language is a key element in the shift toward the necessity and the primacy of speculative knowledge–of which Socrates’ obsession with essential definitions and Plato’s needed postulation of unchangeable essences as the certain correspondent of knowledge are prime examples . Indeed, without language there would be no room for deliberation. Animals are directly or immediately exposed to what they desire or fear and, therefore, respond to it directly, invincibly sollicited by the object. We people, however, are only indirectly exposed, for language mediates our exposure to what we desire or fear. In other words, our perception is linguistic. Almost from the beginning our practical, desirous  experience of the world is linguistically directed by others who are members of our culture.

Analysis of Devereux’s Article

- Theoretical Science

- (a) Its goal is knowledge. May have applications, but not for their sake (484)

- (b) Deals with necessary, universal objects (484)

- Examples: Physics, Mathematics. Geometry (486)

- Young people can become masters, for experience is not needed (486)

- Practical Science

- (a) Its goal is what we can do with the acquired knowledge (484)

- (b) Deals with what we can do with the acquired knowledge, with the variable and contingent, with action, with the particular  pursued for the sake of action  (484)

 - Example: The science of medicine (493)

 - Cannot be mastered by young people for lack of experience (486)  

Universal and Particular

Particular may mean either (a) specific types of action for specific types of situation, or

(b) knowledge of individual acts and situations.

The universals in a theoretical science are precise; it is the particular or individual which is

imprecise. If there are discrepancies between the universals and the particulars falling under them, the defect is on the side of the particulars. In the case of practical knowledge it is the reverse: the universals are indeterminate while the judgments about particular acts in particular circumstances are precise and determinate. If there is discrepancy between the particular judgment of the practically wise person and a universal rule which applies to the situation,

the defect is on the side of the universal (497-498).

The role of experience in the theoretical and practical spheres.

In a theoretical science like physics, experience has the single role of serving as a stepping stone to our grasp of universals. In practical knowledge, experience has not only this role but also the more important one of providing the salient features of particular situations in which decisions are to be made. The knowledge of particulars provided by experience ‘completes’ practical knowledge assuring that it achieves its end in action. (498)

Practical wisdom

- involves knowledge of universal and particular, but even more of particular

- it is not scientific knowledge; there is no episteme of the particular (487)

- Experience is knowledge of particulars.

(Medical) Untutored empiric

- Has experience but lacks the art of medicine (489).

- Cures certain individuals because he knows certain facts about them: the regular connection in the past between symptoms A and treatment B. This is knowledge of

particulars, not of universals. It is knowledge of facts based on experience (491-492). [HUME]       

Art or Science of Medicine

- Knows why treatment B succeeds in Peter and Paul but not in John: because they are phlegmatic (490).

- This is explanatory universal , the strict universal [knowledge of causes] that interests Aris; unlike “fire is hot” and “chicken is wholesome,” whose knowledge is a matter of experience and is, for Aris, particular knowledge [knowledge of observed regularities].

– Aris speaks of medicine as an art ( techne) or science ( episteme ); as such it is concerned exclusively with universals. But he clearly believes that to practice medicine one also needs the knowledge of particulars which comes with experience (493). Let us call the combination of these two in the practitioner of medicine “ medical skill .” (493)

- The science of medicine is not a theoretical science, since its aim is not knowledge but

the restoration of health. It is not “self sufficient,” since it cannot attain its aim without the help of experience and knowledge of particulars. Medical skill is practical both in its aim and in its efficacy in action, and it is self sufficient insofar as it needs nothing further to achieve its aim (495).

Practical Wisdom is analogous not to medicine but to medical skill ; it is practical in aim

and efficacy, and is self-sufficient in the same way as medical skill. The practically wise person has what he needs to achieve his aims. But there is a part of practical wisdom which is analogous to medicine and, like medicine, it lacks [precision and self sufficiency . This is the component of practical wisdom that is concerned with universals [AND WHICH ARIS, BY ANALOGY TO THE SCIENCE OR ARS OF MEDICINE, SHOULD CALL “ THE SCIENCE OF ETHICS ” (495). “Practical wisdom is not a practical science like medicine or rhetoric’; it is more like medical or rhetorical skill; it is an instance of practical knowledge” (497).

- The science of ethics (quotation 5, p. 494)

  The inquiry in Aris’s treatises on ethics is not for the sake of knowledge ( theorias) , for we do not inquire in order to know what virtue is [AS SOCRATES DID?], but in order to become good. [DO WE NEED THIS UNIVERSAL RATHER THAN APPLICATIVE KNOWLEDGE IN ORDER TO BE GOOD? ARIS, LIKE SOCRATES AND PLATO, SEEMS TO THINK SO, FOR PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE MAKES POSSIBLE TO ACT CORRECTLY]. We must examine matters concerning actions and how we OUGHT TO perform them. The whole discussion of matters of conduct must be in outline and not precise, for matters of conduct and what is good for us have no fixity, any more than matters of health. The universal account being of this nature, the account of particulars is even more lacking in precision. For these do not fall under any art (techne) or rule, but the agents themselves must in each case consider what is appropriate to the occasion, as happens also in medicine (494). The statements made here will at best be useful as rules of thumb; the experienced agent or doctor will be guided not so much by them as by his judgment of what is appropriate to the occasion (495). This doesn’t mean that the person with practical wisdom or medical skill will have to resort to guesswork. Their knowledge of particulars built up from experience ( and their ability to deliberate well ) make up for the inadequacy of their universal knowledge. The science of medicine and the universal component of medical skill may lack in precision and self sufficiency, but the medical skill and practical wisdom do not. The doctor’s or wise person’s judgment [DECISION?] about how to act in a particular case is perfectly determinate and appropriate to the situation; such judgments have the precision which universals lack (496).  

The full-fledged doctor (by contrast to the untutored empiric) sees particulars as instances of universal (492)

Medical Skill combines universal and particular

Precise and self-sufficient sciences don’t involve deliberation because the application of

the universal doesn’t need experience [OR. RATHER, DOESN’T NEED VIRTUE, BECAUSE DESIRE IS NOT INVOLVED?] (492). Once someone has learned how to form a particular letter of spell a particular word, he will have no difficulty following the rule (493). But  in things that are brought about by our efforts , like medicine and making money, one needs experience and, in complex cases, deliberation (493). Since medicine and certain other arts are not precise and self-sufficient sciences, one needs experience before one can effectively exercise the knowledge; and even if one has a good store of experience, there will often be situations in which careful thought and deliberation in order to see how the general knowledge should be applied in particular cases (494).
