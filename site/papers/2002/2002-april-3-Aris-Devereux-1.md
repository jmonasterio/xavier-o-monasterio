Aristotle’s notion of practical knowledge, as well as his evolution concerning this subject, were carefully studied by Daniel T. Devereux in his article “Particular and Universal in Aristotle’s Conception of Practical Knowledge.” ^(F1) I will use his article as a foil to express my views.

My interest in this subject springs from my conviction that a good deal of the epistemic mess in which we find ourselves today is due to our dependence on our highly confused Aristotelian heritage regarding theoretical and practical knowledge, as well as on the closely related distinctions between the theoretical and the practical sciences, and  between scientific and non-scientific knowledge. In the previous chapter I explored some of the pertinent material concerning the relationship of Aristotle to Plato in these matters. In this chapter I want to focus more directly on Aristotle’s own contribution.

Let me begin by summarizing the main results already reached. For all the modifications that Plato’s epistemic views underwent in Aristotle’s hands, Aristotle remained close to his master in certain important ways. (1) He did not give up on the Platonic notion  of knowledge as correspondence with the real. The significant modification he introduced in this connection was that he dropped the Platonic requirement of necessary correspondence as a condition for genuine knowledge. This resulted in the Aristotelian division between necessarily true or “scientific” knowledge, and contingently (or factually) true knowledge, whereas Plato had excluded the case of contingent correspondence from the domain of genuine knowledge and had relegated it to the realm of “opinion.” This move allowed  Aristotle to include perceptual or sensible knowledge in the domain of genuine knowledge, whereas Plato had excluded it.  (2) For lack of distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge Plato pursued necessarily true knowledge in the domain of human action. I located on this point the main conflict between Aristotle and Plato. Indeed, as we shall see it more clearly in this chapter, Aristotle came to be convinced of the impossibility of  “scientific” knowledge in the practical domain.

Before moving to Devereux’s article to study it systematically, I want to begin with some preliminary, rather intuitive observations based on my reading of the article.

(1) I am bothered by the confusion by Aristotle and Devereux between knowledge in the knowing person, be it a scientist or an agent, and knowledge as found in an already systematized  corpus of propositional knowledge, such as a textbook of geometry or physics, or Aristotle’s Rhetoric or his Nicomachean Ethics . When Aristotle and Devereux distinguish practical from theoretical knowledge they often are referring to already systematized theoretical disciplines (such as mathematics) and the already systematized practical disciplines (such as Aristotle’s Rhetoric and his Ethics and Galen’s medical treatises ). Moreover, they make the fateful, mistaken  assumption that such systematized knowledge is identical with the knowledge possessed by the practicing mathematician, rhetorician, ethical agent, and practicing physician. Yet:

(a) Aristotle wavers in his identification of his Politics, of which (his?) ethics is a part, as a science. Why this hesitation? I suspect that Aristotle senses that...

(b) In Devereux’s opinion, Aristotle would probably hesitate to say that the ethical agent needed to study a treatise like his Politics in order to act prudently.  Why, if not because the agent’s ethical knowledge is not the same as the knowledge contained in the book called Politics ?

(2) Desire plays no part in Devereux’s analysis. As shown by Nussbaum, though, desire s is, according to Aristotle in the NE [???] and in De motu animalium , the answer to the question why only certain judgments result in action –and only under the appropriate circumstances to bring about the desired effect. The reason why the statements of medical books are efficacious, when appropriated by an agent, is because medicine is studied for the sake of curing or maintaining health. - N.B. Thus, contrary to what Sartre says, my-body-for-the-physician is not my-body-for-the-objective-anatomist or physiologist, but my body implicitly compared to a human body capable of performing as a human body should. This is the desired effect for physicians.  

To move now to Devereux’s views , he rightly attributes to Aristotle the notion of practical knowledge as different from theoretical knowledge.  As he puts it, referring to Aristotle’s predecessors,

“all of them failed to grasp the fundamental distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge... Platonic dialectic is a confusing blend of the practical and theoretical. Questions about value and how we ought to act are not regarded as different in kind from questions about the ultimate nature of reality.” ^(F2)

As Devereux points it out, however,  Aristotle’s break with Plato

“... was a gradual process. The notion of practical knowledge did not spring all at once, fully formed. There was a time when he thought practical knowledge was much like theoretical, the only important contrast being the different aims of each.” ^(F3)

At this initial stage of the evolution of his thinking, then, Aristotle is still very close to Plato, who did not distinguish practical from theoretical knowledge. If the only difference between theoretical and practical knowledge is, according to Aristotle,  that they are pursued for different aims, it follows that they differ only extrinsically. That is, the same piece of knowledge may be a case of theoretical and of practical knowledge, depending only on whether it was pursued  exclusively for the sake of knowing or for the sake of satisfying other interests or needs. By contrast, as Devereux’s sees it, at the end of his evolution Aristotle will have succeeded in dispelling completely Plato’s confusion of practical with theoretical knowledge by showing that they are totally different from each other. In Devereux’s own words,

“Ethics and politics, the prime examples of practical knowledge, differ from such theoretical sciences as metaphysics and physics not only in their aims but in their methods and subjects as well. Indeed, Aristotle thinks the differences are such that we cannot regard practical and theoretical knowledge as two species of a single genus, for there is no common definition of knowledge which applies to both.” ^(F4)

For my part,  I will contend that far from having come to realize that practical knowledge has nothing in common with theoretical knowledge, Aristotle continued to confuse practical knowledge with theoretical knowledge through his inclusion of a ‘universal” component in practical knowledge. As to the important consequences this failure has had for us, the inheritors of the Aristotelian epistemic legacy, I reserve that issue for the end.

My strategy will be to follow closely the portions of Devereux’s article that are pertinent to my subject, and on that basis argue that my interpretation makes more sense than that of Devereux. As to why I decided to zero in on Devereux’s article to deal with the notion of practical knowledge, the reason is that Devereux’s perspicuousness, his systematic treatment of a such a complex subject, and his constant appeal to the Aristotelian text helped me to find my way to reach the conclusions I draw here.

I begin by saying that I have nothing but admiration for the first section of Devereux’s article, in which he clarifies Aristotle’s notion of “particulars,” so central to his conception of practical knowledge. To that section I will pay explicit attention in a second article.  Devereux himself summarizes his conclusions as follows: PAGE 491-2.

“We began by noting the emphasis placed on particulars by Aristotle’s conception of practical knowledge, and asking whether by ‘particular’ he meant specific types or concrete individuals. Our examination of some of the relevant passages has shown that neither of these answers is correct by itself. Knowledge of particulars turns out to be knowledge of facts based on experience, and these facts may or may not be about specific individuals. Two important features of this kind of knowledge are: (i) it does not involve any explanation of the facts known, and (ii) it can serve by itself as a perfectly adequate guide to action in many circumstances.” ^(F5)

After pointing out that initially Aristotle thought that the only difference  between theoretical and practical knowledge was purely extrinsic, a matter of the goals for which they are pursued, Devereux continues his exploration of Aristotle’s progressive evolution away from Plato:

“A second.... contrast between practical and theoretical knowledge has to do with subject matter. Theoretical sciences like physics and mathematics are concerned with necessary objects, with things ‘whose principles cannot be otherwise than they are.’  Practical knowledge, on the other hand, is concerned with the variable and contingent; since such knowledge is practical, it is concerned with actions in particular circumstances, and here there is ‘much diversity and fluctuation.’” ^(F6)

In other words, Aristotle thinks that the kind of necessarily true knowledge to which Plato aspired may certainly be attained in the theoretical sciences, because they deal with necessary objects, with objects which in all circumstances are the same. However, following Socrates, Plato wanted scientific knowledge, that is precise and necessarily true knowledge – episteme– in the area of practical knowledge. For his part, Aristotle thinks that such knowledge is impossible. Precisely because it is practical, because its purpose is not to know about action but to result in action, it deals with actions in particular circumstances, and there can be no scientific knowledge of what  is variable and contingent. As forcefully underscored by Plato, who for that very reason had relegated sensible knowledge that dealt with particular objects to the realm of mere ‘opinion,” scientific or necessarily true knowledge is only possible of universal objects, of what is necessarily what it is. Thus Aristotle concludes in the NE that, “Practical knowledge is not scientific knowledge ( episteme) .” ^(F7)

We might say that with this words Aristotle puts an end to the Socratic-Platonic dream of attaining necessarily true knowledge in matters of action. Aristotle senses that the danger lurking now is falling into the opposite extreme of denying that in matters of action everything goes because actions are “particulars,” and knowledge is only possible of the universal. Aristotle avoids falling into that extreme by showing that, though the practical disciplines deal with particulars, they nonetheless  involve knowledge of the universal, too. Thus, though the practical sciences cannot be strictly scientific like the theoretical ones, which deal with universal objects exclusively, nonetheless  the practical disciplines or “arts” can legitimately qualify as knowledge because they combine knowledge of the universal and the particular: “Nor is practical wisdom concerned with universals only –it must also know particulars ( ta  kaz’ekasta ), for it is practical.” ^(F8)

Aside from the important fact that one of the differences between theoretical and practical knowledge is that the former deals exclusively with universal objects while the latter combines knowledge of universal objects with knowledge of particulars, here Aristotle also lets us know  that the knowledge of particulars, in the case of practical knowledge, is due to “experience.” This is an important point to which I shall return in a second article, where I intend to reflect more deeply on the Aristotelian view of practical knowledge. Before undertaking that reflection, though, we may finish this overview of the Aristotelian conception of practical knowledge by mentioning Devereux general conclusion, forcefully expressed in the very first words of his article:

“Aristotle thought his predecessors in general, and Plato in particular, made a serious mistake in failing to mark the boundaries separating the different sciences and branches of philosophical inquiry. All of them failed to grasp the fundamental distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge... Aristotle thinks the differences are such that we cannot regard practical and theoretical knowledge as two species of a single genus, for there is no common definition of knowledge which applies to both.” ^(F9)

If Devereux were right on this point, Aristotle would have fully succeeded in overcoming Plato’s confusion of practical with theoretical knowledge, since they differ not only specifically but even generically as two totally different things having in common only an equivocal name. Whether or not this is the case, though, remains to be seen.

Up to this point I have followed Devereux’s analysis. Now I will part ways with him . He has shown the important place that knowledge of “particulars” occupies in Aristotle’s view of practical knowledge, and his careful analysis has convincingly shown what Aristotle means by “particulars.” Moreover, he rightly thinks that Aristotle believes that practical knowledge ALSO involves knowledge of the universal. What Devereux fails to see is that Aristotle is wrong on this point. And the fact that Aristotle thought that knowledge of the universal was involved in practical knowledge is, as we shall see, the reason why, contrary to Devereux’s view, Aristotle ultimately failed in fully overcoming Plato’s assimilation of practical to theoretical knowledge. To proceed with some order, however, let me first show that both Aristotle and Devereux are wrong in thinking that practical knowledge involves knowledge of  universals.

                                                        

Does practical knowledge require knowledge of the universal?

First of all, what is Aristotle talking about when he speaks of the knowledge of the universal involved in practical knowledge? Consider the case of medicine, which Aristotle classifies among the practical sciences . Devereux distinguishes between the person who learned to cure on his own, through trial and error, and the person who learned medicine as an academic discipline taught by others. In the following passage, explaining the knowledge of the universal involved in practical knowledge, the person who learned to cure through sheer practice is initially  identified by Devereux as “the untutored empiric”:

“He [the untutored empiric] investigates other individuals who are sick with symptoms A and finds the same correlation: the treatment is effective for those who are phlegmatic, but not for those who are not. He concludes that it is because of the presence of this characteristic in the one group that the treatment is effective... Aristotle would say that this man is no longer simply an untutored empiric; he has made an important step towards acquiring the art of medicine. In discovering a common feature in virtue of which a particular treatment is effective for a group of individuals, he has hit upon what Aristotle calls here  a ‘universal ,’ and it is knowledge of such universal that qualifies as art or science” ^(F10)

As Devereux remarks,

“The normal procedure (at least nowadays) is to acquire the universal knowledge before the particular. One first acquires general information about...symptoms of various diseases, methods of treatment, etc., and then, through a period or residency in a hospital, one gains the experience necessary to use the general knowledge.” ^(F11)

           Using as a concrete example of a “universal” the general knowledge that “light meats are easy to digest and wholesome,” Aristotle makes some important points in the following paragraph:

“Nor is practical wisdom concerned with universals only –it must also know particulars, for it is practical. That is why in other fields too some who do not know are more effective in action than those who know –they (the former) have experience. For if a man knew that light meats are easy to digest and wholesome, but did not know which sorts of meat are light, he will not produce health, but the man who knows that chicken is wholesome is more likely to produce health. Now practical wisdom ( phronesis ) is practical; hence one should have both [knowledge of universals and knowledge of particulars], of the latter even more than the former.” ^(F12)

One of the main characteristics of practical knowledge, then, is that it involves not only knowledge of particulars but also knowledge of universals. Indeed, as Aristotle makes it abundantly clear in the Metaphysics, what distinguishes an “art” or a “science” from other practices is that it involves not only knowledge of the particular but also of the universal. ^(F13) In other words, without knowledge of the universal, the medical practice cannot be an art or a science. Now, what is extremely striking and important in this passage is that Aristotle tells us, at least in equivalent terms, that in the science or art of medicine, precisely because it is practical, the knowledge of the universal is superfluous . Indeed, the untutored empirics who “do not know” because they possess only  knowledge of the particular –namely, that chicken is wholesome– acquired through experience, may be capable of curing, whereas “those  who know,” that is, those who  learned in the classroom the universals independently of practice or experience–namely, that light meats are easy to digest and wholesome– but ignore that chicken is wholesome, will be incapable of curing. And, as remarked by Aristotle, what happens in medicine in this regard also happens in other practical disciplines. In a moment we will come across some concrete illustrations of this point.

Thus it turns out that, after all, knowledge of the universal is not an essential component of practical knowledge. Why, then, did Aristotle think that it was? At this point I may interject my suspicion that Aristotle required knowledge of the universal in the practical disciplines, and attributed importance to it, only because he had not quite overcome his dependence on ( the early, Socratic ) Plato, for whom the only kind of genuine knowledge was knowledge of the universal, and whose epistemic model was the knowledge involved in such (theoretical) disciplines as mathematics and metaphysics. Now, however,  Aristotle has stumbled upon the quite unexpected fact –so unexpected, indeed, that he doesn’t seem to quite absorb it– that the practical disciplines really do not require theoretical  knowledge of universals. If this is true, tough, then Aristotle’s conception of practical knowledge as a combination of knowledge of universals and of particulars is radically wrong.

Of course, this point is so momentous that it demands serious textual confirmation. I think that Devereux himself indirectly furnishes us that confirmation.

In a very dense and important passage ^(F14), in which he attempts to clarify and systematize Aristotle’s views, Devereux distinguishes between the science or art of medicine and the practice of medicine . As any Aristotelian“art” or “science,” Devereux remarks, the science or art of medicine is exclusively composed of universals. On the other hand, the practice of medicine, in addition to the knowledge of universals contained in the science or art,   also requires the knowledge of particulars that comes only with experience. Devereux calls “medical skill” the combination of universal  and particular knowledge found in the successful practitioner of medicine. In the medical “scientist ,” by contrast, no knowledge of particulars is required, for it is not relevant to his being a scientist. Now,  medicine is a practical, not a theoretical science , since its aim is not the acquisition of  knowledge for its own sake but the restoration  and maintenance of health; in other words, medicine is supposed to be practical in the sense of ‘‘productive” of health. Clearly, then, the science of medicine is, as a practical science, “incomplete,” since it is exclusively concerned with universals and it cannot achieve its aim without experiential knowledge of particulars. By contrast, the practice of medicine  is “complete” as a practical science, since apart from the universal knowledge contained in the medical science it involves the experiential knowledge of particulars which is required to produce or maintain health.

If we are to take seriously what Devereux says, we are to conclude that in declaring that the science or art of medicine is “incomplete,” he means by implication that the medical science, as distinguished from the practice of medicine, really is not a practical science at all but a purely theoretical one, for it is not “efficacious” in any way, that is, it does not terminate in action. It is a purely theoretical science because it only conveys knowledge of ‘universals,’ such as “light meats are healthy,” and from such knowledge no action follows, because no knowledge of  particulars is involved. Indeed, if the medical scientist only knows that light meats are healthy, but not which meats are light, he cannot prescribe the particular meat that will be a remedy for the patient.  

Oddly enough, Devereux, like Aristotle, fails to draw the obvious conclusion that the knowledge of the universal is not required for practical knowledge. Nor is Devereux more perceptive when it comes to his own observation that

“The successful practitioner of medicine has both knowledge of universals (the science of medicine) and knowledge of particulars, and the latter is relevant to his being a practitioner but not to his being a scientist.” ^(F15)

What, one wonders prevents Devereux from realizing that the converse is also true, to wit: that knowledge of universals is only relevant to the physician’s being a scientist but not to his being a successful practitioner, given that he has acknowledged with Aristotle that the medical practitioner can be successful without knowledge of the universals?

Let us pursue the argument further. From the consideration of the case of medicine Devereux moves to the comparison of other practical disciplines to medicine. He begins with the practical knowledge involved in ethical matters, and he asks himself whether practical wisdom is analogous to the science of medicine (whose knowledge of the universal has been acknowledged to be irrelevant to the medical practitioner),  or rather analogous to medical skill, whose knowledge of particulars is necessary to the practitioner of medicine.

Devereux’s careful answer comes in two parts. ^(F16) The first refers to practical wisdom “ in the practically wise person ”; the second part refers to the component of practical wisdom that is concerned with universals, as is the case with  the doctrine found in Aristotle’s book of Ethics. Devereux determines that the knowledge possessed by the practically wise person is analogous to medical skill, but not to medical science, for “it is self-sufficient in the same way as medical skill, in that the practically wise person has what he needs to achieve his aims.” As for the knowledge involved in such practical sciences or arts as Aristotle’s Ethics , it is analogous to the science of medicine, but not to the medical skill of the successful practitioner. The reason is not only that the supposedly practical science of ethics  involves knowledge of universals exclusively,  but also that, like the supposedly practical science of medicine, it lacks precision and self-sufficiency, as Aristotle warns us concerning the character of his remarks in the Nicomachean Ethics.

Now, from the fact that the ethical agent’s knowledge or “practical wisdom”  is not analogous to the knowledge involved in the medical science, whereas the knowledge involved in the practical sciences of ethics and politics is, it follows that the Aristotelian identification of the agent’s practical knowledge with the knowledge involved in the genuinely  practical sciences is mistaken. Yet  Devereux does not cease following Aristotle on this misidentification. For instance, he writes without flinching:

 

“ Ethics and politics, the prime examples of practical knowledge , differ from such theoretical sciences as metaphysics and physics not only in their aims but in their methods and subject matter as well.” ^(F17)

If my previous analysis is right, the practical wisdom of the ethical agent is indeed a prime example of practical knowledge, but the same cannot be said, as Devereux here does, of the science of ethics and politics . The agent actually engaged in action has to deliberate and make decisions. By contrast, when Aristotle is writing his ‘science’ or ‘art’ of   Ethics and, for that purpose, is reflecting about the ethical agent and ethical action, he has no decision to make, and what he writes will not result in action either on his part or on his reader’s part. In writing his Ethics Aristotle is engaged in theoretical reflection on action, not in action. Actually, the difference between these two types of knowledge, namely, reflection on action for purposes of understanding, and practical knowledge for purposes of acting, explains why Devereux found no analogy between the practical knowledge or wisdom of the agent who has to decide how to act , and the general statements found in Aristotle’s Ethics.   It explains, too, why Devereux found no analogy between the knowledge of the medical practitioner and the knowledge constituted by the general statements of the science of medicine. Conversely, it explains why Devereux did find an analogy between the practical knowledge of the medical practitioner and the practical wisdom of the ethical agent , both of which terminate in action.   

Aristotle’s realization that the practitioner of medicine can be successful without any knowledge of the universals involved in the science or art of medicine should  have alerted him to his mistake in identifying practical knowledge with the knowledge involved in what he calls “practical sciences,” which after all are not practical since they are not efficacious. Similarly, Devereux’s realization of the dysanalogy between the practical knowledge of the agent engaged in action and the knowledge found in the practical sciences should have led him to correct the mistake he makes in following Aristotle in this matter. Actually, Devereux seems to have come very close to the full realization that the ethical agent has no need at all of the knowledge of the universals that compose Aristotle’s Ethics, for he writes:

“We should note that Aristotle is somewhat hesitant about describing politics as a science (cf. 1094a26 with 118-b31-32). ^(F18)  Also it is not clear whether he thinks knowledge of poli tics [of which ethics is a part] is a necessary condition of practical wisdom (would he say, for example, that individuals who have not engaged in political reflection about moral and political questions [the kind of reflection in which Aristotle engages in the Ethics ] could not be practically wise?” ^(F19)

Obviously, Devereux suspects that his question is to be answered negatively, for it sounds very much like a purely rhetorical question. At any rate, he should not only have suspected but fully realized that in order to act prudently, that is, to exhibit what Aristotle calls “practical knowledge,” an agent has no need to possess the kind of “universal knowledge” about action and virtue imparted by Aristotle in the Nicomachean Ethics. Because he failed to do that, Devereux does not have to pause to ponder the important implication of a negative answer to this question for the mistaken identification by himself and Aristotle of the agent’s genuinely practical –that is, efficacious– knowledge with the supposedly practical knowledge involved in the so-called practical sciences or arts. The most immediate implication being, as far as Aristotle’s own ethical works are concerned, that these works are of no use to the ethical agent.

As this is a rather scandalous claim, for it contradicts Aristotle’s own pronouncement that the purpose of his ethical inquiry is not theoretical but practical –“not to know what virtue is but to become good,” ^(F20) as well as the understanding  of the practical character of Aristotle’s ethical works by a long chain of commentators who have taken him at his word, I want to finish with some further elaboration of this implication.

“With a view to action, experience seems in no respect inferior to art, and men of experience succeed even better than those who have theory without experience. The reason is that experience is knowledge of individuals, art of universals, and actions and productions are all concerned with the individual ( Metaph. 981a13-17).

In the Nicomachean Ethics , we are reminded by Devereux,

“Aristotle warns us that his remarks are necessarily imprecise because of the subject matter... Matters of health and conduct ‘have no fixity,’ and therefore it would be futile to attempt to formulate precise statements about what we should do in various situations . One must speak ‘in outline,’ ( typo) and not precisely ( ouk akribos ). If one tries to be more specific and detailed, one’s statements will inevitably be inaccurate and unreliable. Such statements will at best be useful as rules of thumb; the experienced agent or doctor will be guided not so much by them as by his judgment of ‘what is appropriate to the occasion.’” ^(F21)

If Devereux’s interpretation is right --and I believe it is–,  Aristotle definitely thinks that the practical sciences are like the theoretical sciences in that both have a subject-mater. No doubt, Aristotle realizes that the respective subject-matters of these sciences are different; that, indeed, is the reason why knowledge, in the case of the theoretical sciences, can be scientific or necessarily true, but not in the case of the practical sciences. Now the attribution by Aristotle of a subject-matter to both the theoretical and the practical sciences clearly indicates that Aristotle fails to realize that the agent’s genuinely practical knowledge, which through deliberation and decision results in action, is of a different kind than the knowledge involved in what he calls “the practical sciences .” Indeed, contrary to what Aristotle thinks, unlike the ethical scientist who produces a treatise of the type of Aristotle’s Ethics , the agent has no subject-matter, that is, no object of study. The reason for this is that the agent is engaged in action (which involves deliberation and decision), not engaged in thoughtful reflection for the purpose of knowing about action. The reason why the agent has no use for the supposedly practical knowledge conveyed by the ethical scientist in his treatises is that it results from theoretical reflection about action as a subject-matter of study.

But, then, what sort of knowledge, if any, is the genuinely practical knowledge possessed by the agent? Or is the agent’s decision totally blind and arbitrary? Aristotle does not think so, most certainly. He may not be very clear himself as to the nature of the agent’s knowledge. However, when he is not theorizing about the theoretical and practical sciences, a reflection that tends to pull him in the direction of Plato’s epistemic conception which privileges theoretical knowledge,  he drops tantalizing hints about the baffling nature of the agent’s knowledge. For instance, he says that the agent’s knowledge can only be acquired through “experience,” which, he points out, is the reason why young people can be good mathematicians but not prudent agents. ^(F22) He also tells us that we should  “attend to the undemonstrated remarks and beliefs of experienced and older people... For these people see correctly because experience has given them their eye.” ^(F23) Finally, rather than giving us any specific content of the knowledge possessed by the practically wise or prudent agent, he makes of the latter’s decisions as manifested in his conduct the model to follow, obviously because only “experience” can teach us how to act in concrete circumstances.

But what, precisely, is “experience” ? And why does the practical knowledge that can only be acquired through  experience  require a good deal of time? Aristotle mentions that to an apprentice baker who asked whether he should make the bread deeply or lightly baked, the experienced baker answered “Do it right.” But what is “right” in matters of baking, and  how does one ‘do it right’? Only the experienced baker knows. Thanks to his or her experience in making bread, the expert or experienced baker can guide the apprentice with general rules of thumb, and can correct the practicing apprentice when the apprentice goes wrong.. However, for all of his master’s directions, the only way the apprentice can learn how to apply the rules contained in the directions is through personal experience, that is, through practice, through trial and the feedback of error and success. That is how the apprentice’s master actually learned the art of making bread. And this practical learning, this acquisition of mastery, takes time, for it takes practice to develop the habit of correct application of one’s action to the task. Once the apprentice has learned ‘to do it right,’ he will be like his experienced master baker who, reflecting on his experience, can formulate rules of thumb –the “universals” that constitute the so-called practical science or art of baking–  but who himself  is in no need of, for he has “experience.” Actually, the rules of thumb or “universals”come after experience. To be more precise, they do not come as a result of what Aristotle calls  “experience,” as does the practical knowledge of the expert; they result from theoretical reflection on the expert’s experience. What results from experience is the practical ability to make bread. *

When Aristotle proposes his ideal of science in the Posterior Analytics , the problem with that ideal is not, as the commentators have generally said, that Aristotle is talking about an already completed science rather than about the process of discovery that leads to the completed science. The problem –not even mentioned by the commentators, because they don‘t see it as the problem at all, anymore than Aristotle does– is that Aristotle thinks that the result of the thinking scientific activity is a view that corresponds (if possible at all, necessarily) to what is the case rather than in a habitual disposition resulting from past successful repeated action to act in a certain way whose concrete application will be determined by the agent in the concrete circumstances. Hence the demonstrative tenor of his outline of the ideal science and of his concrete realization in the logic, in order to guarantee the correspondence of the view to the real. Hence, too, Aristotle’s ill-disguised regret that the strictly scientific character cannot be realized in the science of ethics because of the fluid character of its ’subject-matter,’ which is action in concrete circumstances. - Parallel to the idea of correspondence of the intellectual judgment to the intelligible reality is the conception of perception as a concrete picture that corresponds to the sensible object. Because Plato thought of knowledge as necessary and, therefore, permanent correspondence with its object, the impossibility of a more than  instantaneous correspondence between the perception and the sensible object, given the particularity and contingency of the sensible, he despaired ( as Aristotle did in the case of action ) of the possibility of genuine

to become an intellectualist and invent the eternal intelligible Forms to guarantee correspondence. Perception, in Aristotle, is more complex, or rather more ambiguous that in Plato.

Experience is sometimes picture (be it image or verbal description) of a particular perceived object as it is in the mind of a pure disinterested contemplator, and at other times –at least incipiently or inchoatively, if not formally– disposition to act in a certain way in order to satisfy the agent’s need or desire, as a habitual result of past interaction with the surrounding world. In the former sense, perception or perceptual knowledge is not practical, for, as clearly seen by Hume, it is not intrinsically connected with action. In the latter sense, action is a constitutive moment of the experience of a situated needful or desirous agent. When Aristotle speaks of experience in connection with knowledge, he is referring to the latter conception of perception as purely objective grasp of object, without reference to the needs and desires of the exposed subject. Unfortunately, he is not quite aware of the ambiguity of his conception of perceptual knowledge, anymore than his commentators are, and falls victim to it, and bequeaths it to the western epistemic tradition. This is the ambiguity that requires exposure and dissolution in connection with practical knowledge.

When in the NE Aristotle says that scientific knowledge in the domain of action is not possible because of the lack of fixity of action, he clearly is thinking of action as a subject-matter of a supposedly practical study, for such study will result in the production of the appropriate action. But the agent, as such, is not an observer of his or her own action.

Practical knowledge, then, is experiential knowledge.

Now, a good strategy to understand what Aristotle means by this sort of knowledge may be to explore what he has to say about animals in connection with knowledge. Indeed, Aristotle attributes to them practical experiential knowledge . Moreover, in their case there should be no room for the confusion between the knowledge possessed by animals and theoretical knowledge, for Aristotle deems animals incapable of theoretical knowledge.

In book II, ch. 19 of the Posterior Analytics as well as in Book A, ch. 1 of the Metaphysics , Aristotle deals with animal knowledge. In both cases, however, he deals with animal knowledge only incidentally, by contrast to human knowledge, which is his real quarry . For his purpose is not to examine the practical knowledge possessed by animals, but to show that the grasp of universals involved in human knowledge has its origin in sense perception, which of course people have in common with animals. Why does Aristotle say that animals partake but little in this systematizing ability of people? One hypothesis seems prima facie plausible: because animals, even the members of the highest species, systematize automatically, by instinct,  without knowing what they are doing. By contrast,  if initially they too systematize by instinct, people can reflect and, hence, come to know objectively or predicatively the universal, at first merely as a sensed or perceived cause –that fire is hot, knowledge quia ), and later, on that basis, as a reason why– knowledge propter quid . In the latter case they really know classes, that is, natures or forms, as a result of their systematization, not merely factual universals, so to say. Thus they can reach Wisdom, which is the highest form of knowledge. So they can teach others. Animals, by contrast, only have what Sellars, in a different context,  called  “discriminative behavior.”

Is this what Aristotle is trying to say?  If so, his position is defective. First, this interpretation doesn’t do justice to the superior species of animals whose behavior is genuinely adaptive. that is, modified according to circumstances in order to achieve the satisfaction of their needs. By contrast, the behavior of the rat that pushes the button is merely repetitive, as reflexes are. [In short, it is not that the animal remembers and INFERS. Rather, the animals is genetically ‘wired’ to repeat any action that is accompanied by the satisfaction of a need. It is a matter of acquired or conditioned reflexes]. Superior animals have a sort of prudence, though not deliberative. One has only to see a tiger hunting to realize how “plastic” (to use Nussbaum felicitous expression) its behavior is, and how much it changes and improves with practice or “experience” and is adapted to changing circumstances, for it is prospective rather than retrospective . Hence –in later writings and concerning people, Aristotle speaks of prudentia, pro-videntia, based on past experience but after a model of playing chess as humans do, that is, deliberately adaptive or applicative , not automatic and merely responsive, as computers do. This is the adaptive type of conduct in which the superior species of  animals partake, but only ”a little,” for they do not invent. Their adaptive behavior is prescribed to them by instinct; they do not deliberate as to how they will act. As Nussbaum indicates. they will always follow the strongest desire at the time. That is the reason that, unlike people, each generation of animals always lives as their ancestors lived, for they do not invent ways of tailoring the environment to their needs.

Secondly,  and this is most important, Aristotle sees a certain continuity between animals and people in their common systematizing ability , however inferior it may be in the case of animals. In fact, he explains that people’s systematizing ability, that is, their capacity of grasping the universal in the particulars and reducing them to classes, has its origin in the very systematizing ability shared by animals. In other words, Aristotle sees the systematizing ability of people as a more sophisticated form of the same ability that animals possess in an inferior form. Yet, only a few lines later, Aristotle realizes that the systematizing ability he attributes to people is superfluous for action, for the person who knows the universal but not the particular cannot cure, whereas the person who knows the particular but not the universal can cure, that is, can act in the appropriate way to achieve the goal of curing. In other words, the knowledge of universals is not genuinely practical knowledge, for it does not result in action. Obviously, however, the systematizing ability of animals is efficacious. Indeed, the only way in which it is manifested, since they possess no language, is in their actual efficacious behavior. * Thus, pace Aristotle, the continuity between people and animals must be located on the side of action, not of seeing or understanding . People are superior to animals not because people know abstract universals and can reach scientific knowledge, whereas even the best of animals only know factual sensible ‘universals’ like fire is hot, but because people are superior agents in that they decide and, to an extent, invent their action to transform the environment to tailor it to their needs.

* As the only way in which the (practical) knowledge of the student to whom Kuhn alludes is expressed and demonstrated in his actual ability to solve problems. Indeed, it is identical with that habitual ability.

Aristotle’s realization that the (theoretical) knowledge of  universals of which he speaks is superfluous for action should have prompted him to become aware of his ’s mistaken tendency, in the above quoted passages of the Metaphysics and the Pasterior Analytics, to interpret animal knowledge after the model of theoretical knowledge, rather than interpreting human knowledge after the  model of the efficacious practical knowledge animals possess, a knowledge that is intrinsically connected with action. In short, there is indeed a continuity between animals and people, but not of the type defined by Aristotle. He thinks in terms of theoretical, quasi-linguistic, objectively known, predicative generalizations rather than in terms of practical, tacit, sensorimotor dispositions developed through action. He attributes to superior animals an incipient, quasi theoretical but not verbalized knowledge-of causes, “a little” generalizing power in the theoretical sense,   instead of understanding the knowledge quia of humans as a superior, more efficient variety of the practical, tacit, sensorimotor dispositions found in animals as a result of action or”experience ” helped by language.

Indeed,  how does the medical practitioner acquire his genuinely practical knowledge? Through practice, through “experience,” by internalizing his or her own successful activity in curing a particular ailment (or, to use another instance, to interpret radiographies correctly, so as to apply the appropriate remedy to the patient) until the production of the appropriate conduct becomes habitual. That is, the medical practitioner acquires his ability to cure in the same way in which the superior species of animals acquire their practical knowledge, that is, the ability to produce the appropriate behavior to achieve their natural, inherited goals. Within this continuity between animals and people, the difference and superiority of people resides in the fact that the goals of animals are prescribed by their inherited needs, whereas – thanks to the reflective ability that language confers on them and to their ‘freedom’ to desire –  people can invent the goals they will choose to pursue as well as the means to achieve them. The progress in science and technology, with the new possibilities they open, supports this point.

It is to be remarked, however, that even in the case of people the internalization involved in genuinely practical knowledge is tacit and dispositional rather than predicative and objectifying. Consequently, application cannot be taught directly, however much its learning may be favored by example and rules of thumb . Practical knowledge is not predicative or mirroring knowledge, that can be formulated in statements, but knowledge –if that is the correct word– that consists in the habitual ability to produce the appropriate conduct in a given set of circumstances.

Thanks, then, to their being stored in motor -memory (and the consequent disposition to act in a determinate way), the animal ‘remembers,’ in a motor or dispositional rather than in a predicative way, which of its actions related to which particular environmental circumstances led to the actual satisfaction of its needs . First important point: Knowledge, in my pseudo-paraphrasis, is directly connected with systematically successful action. And for good reason, since experience is a result of action guided by inherited need (instinct) or acquired  desire . In other words, thinking cannot substitute for action, as far as gaining experience is concerned. No action, no “experience” that results in habitual practical knowledge and correct action. What Aristotle calls “experience” is the result of actual doing, not of seeing or understanding. If the rat doesn’t happen to actually push the button, it will acquire no practical knowledge, that is, no ability to get food systematically rather than by chance, anymore than if a child doesn’t actually use his or her hands on the piano, he or she will not become a skillful pianist, that is will not develop the ability to produce the desired musical sounds systematically rather than once in a while by chance. In practical knowledge, something is done which, because it was – retrospectively –  successful in satisfying the particular desire, later becomes a means and is manipulated  instrumentally as a means – prospectively – for the sake of achieving a desired goal in determinate environmental circumstances. The involved action or doing, correlated or associated with the circumstances or situation in which the subject finds himself or herself –for all practical knowledge is tacitly self-referential– is ‘interiorized’ to the point of becoming habitual or practically automatic. It is not that something is done because something else is previously seen or understood; rather something is seen or understood and formulated –in the case of people– because something is done that results in satisfaction of a need. As Kuhn suggests, no application, no understanding. And the understanding is tacit, general disposition to act in a certain way; not predicative understanding.

Second: Knowledge is connected with action via desire of goal, which structures perception teleologically and organizes hodological space –that is the field of action– for the perceiver-agent. * Desire is absent in the Aristotelian passages of the Metaphysics and the Posterior Analytics.

*Of course, insofar as animals, by contrast to people, only have what Maslow calls “lower needs.” the teleology of their actions is easier to detect, and they do not undergo moral conflicts as to how to act.

Third: Knowledge is essentially personal, as personal as the agent’s desire, action, and satisfaction. It is not a quality of the statments themselves in which t he “science” is expressed.

Fourth: Knowledge depends on regularity because what happens always in the same way makes possible regular expectation and habitual successful action. The Platonic explanation of the exact regularities is by reference to understood intelligible forms, whereas Aristotle refers to grasped essences or natures or formal causes of actually existing things or events, that supposedly correspond in the object to what the mind grasps . Hence the Platonic-Aristotelian distinction between knowledge or scientia and  mere opinion or belief, although the distinction doesn’t cover the same territory in both cases.

The regularities, though, can be of the order of objective, unexposed observation (and then either exact and measurable, or not measurable but exact, or not exact and not measurable) and of the order of exposed affection and action involving what later Aristotle will call ‘phantasia’. (Cf.  last portion of Essay 5 of Nussbaum’s De Motu Animalium) .

The order of objective observation involves one or more of the degrees of abstraction of which Aristotle speaks, plus the one of which Aristotle does not speak, namely, the abstraction from the subject’s interested situation in respect of the object, which is at the origin of all knowledge and is always “tacit,” because non-objective . Once again, Aristotle is sidetracked by Plato’s visual epistemic model –hence “theoria,” (modified by Aristotle’s ‘linguistic turn’ toward judgment-statement), with its two possible varieties of correspondence –necessary and contingent– instead of sticking to the non-visual, dispositional epistemic model that begins with action. Without desire and satisfaction or frustration, regularities and lack thereof would be irrelevant. This is to say that the order of objective observation has to be replaced in relation to the order of exposed affection, deliberation, and action.

Hence the identification of the knowledge involved in the practical sciences with the exposed agent’s knowledge rests in a confusion between the unexposed contemplator’s ‘world,’ and the exposed agent’s dramatic world.

Not only does the ‘object’ of  the practical sciences lack the fixity of the object of the theoretical sciences, but the universal itself is superfluous for the skillful practitioner, since it is not used or known by the practitioner. The supposed universal is discovered by the practitioner, not by the theorist or scientist. And it is not meant to work for the practitioner as objective knowledge that mirrors what is the case, but as a tacit map or recipe for action, to be internalized through practice by the disciple of the successful practitioner, as it was acquired by the untutored empiric.

 

........

One of the reasons that made Aristotle hesitate about describing politics as a science was the difference he sensed between the vague universals of the practical sciences, which function as mere “rules of thumb,” a fact that  he correlates with the variable nature of their object, which is action,  and the precise explanatory universals of the theoretical sciences, a fact that he correlates with the invariable nature of the object of the theoretical sciences.  

..............................................

This confusion of the knowledge involved in the practical sciences with the agent’s practical knowledge shows again in Aristotle’s mistaken conviction that his philosophical exploration in the Nicomachean Ethics will result in  right actions:

“Our present inquiry does not aim, as our others do, at study; for the purpose of our examination is not to know what virtue is, but to become good, since otherwise the inquiry would be of no benefit to us... First, then, actions should express correct reason.” ^(F28)

It is baffling that Aristotle could entertain such hope, given his realization that the agent’s practical knowledge involves deliberation and decision, which are only possible in reference to the particular circumstances in which the agent finds himself and which demand a decision, whereas Aristotle’s own knowledge in the ethics refers to no particular circumstances.

...................................................

Let me summarize in some systematic way the results yielded so far by my exploration. If I have been right, it emerges that Aristotle gives the  name of  “practical sciences” to two different things which he amalgamates , and only one of which deserves to be called practical for only one of them results in action , that is, only one of them is genuinely practical or efficacious. Which are the two different types of what Aristotle calls “practical sciences”?   To one type belong the practical wisdom of the ethical agent and the practice of medicine interiorized or appropriated tacitly by the practicing physician. These are the practical sciences between which Devereux rightly finds an analogy. To the other type belong the theoretical treatises of ethics and politics and the science of medicine. These are the sciences which Devereux rightly finds dysanalogous with the practical sciences of the first type. Now, the former are not genuine sciences or even arts, by Aristotle’s definition , in that, contrary to Aristotle’s opinion, they do not require knowledge of universals. However, the knowledge involved in them is genuinely practical, in that it is efficacious. Indeed, the practical knowledge –prudentia, pro-videntia– of the genuinely prudent ethical agent results in right action, and the practical knowledge of the skillful medical practitioner results in the patient’s health. Herein lies the analogy between the sciences of the first type and their dysanalogy with the sciences of the second type. Furthermore, the practical knowledge involved in this type of practical sciences is experiential knowledge, that is, knowledge that results from experience. By contrast, the practical sciences of the second type, such as Aristotle’s ethics and politics and the science of medicine, are composed of imprecise universals whose knowledge, contrary to Aristotle’s view of these sciences as “ruling” or directing their respective practices, is not really indispensable for ethical practice or for medical practice. By themselves these supposedly practical sciences are  incomplete, that is, inefficacious or not practical in any genuine way, since by themselves they lack experiential knowledge without which no efficaciousness is possible. They are a bad composite, a spurious mixture of the genuinely universal but purely theoretical knowledge that arises from abstraction and is possible only of abstract objects of thought that are always the same or invariable, and the agent’s efficacious or genuinely practical  knowledge that arises from experience.

To complete my point, I need to show that theoretical univesal  SCIENTIFIC knowledge, which arises from “abstraction” (NE, 1142a17, Transl. Irwin) (as well as supposedly practical particular NON-SCIENTIFIC knowledge which arises from sensible perception or observation) , and   practical knowledge which arises from “experience,” have nothing in common. In the former case, the words of the statement are understood to function as mirroring or conforming to what is the case; hence the attempt to demonstrate the truth of the content of the statement. In  the latter case, the words mean practically or instrumentally, that is, refer the desirous or fearful agent –through desire or fear– to a  new projected situation to be realized or brought about through action decided by the agent in concrete circumstances. Now, in that the sciences of the second type, among which politics and ethics as conceived by Aristotle, are not practical in any serious sense of efficaciousness, for not only cannot the knowledge involved in them be equated with the agent’s knowledge, which is indeed efficacious, but they are strictly superfluous and should, perhaps, disappear.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The above text, like  note 8, concerns the contrast between theoretical and practical sciences.  Aristotle explains the difference between them in terms of their respective objects: one has the fixity of the necessary, the other the variability of the contingent. In note 23 , Aristotle explains that the theoretical sciences pursue, and terminate in, objective knowledge of what is, whereas in ethical matters the point is to terminate in appropriate action, without which Aristotle’s practical science or art of ethics would be futile. Finally, on the coming page Aristotle will explain the difference between the theoretical and the practical sciences by reference to the method or way by which knowledge is gained: “abstraction,” in the case of theoretical knowledge, and “experience” in the case of practical knowledge. Question: Are these different explanations equivalent or at least compatible? Secondly, are all of them correct?   

To complicate matters still more, two points. (1) It is evident by now that Aristotle mistakenly identifies the agent’s practical knowledge with the knowledge involved in the practical sciences. (2) The name of practical sciences covers such different things as medicine, which may be said to be a field of study and ‘to have an object,’ and ethics, which is participatory or situational rather than contemplative, and in which action (which is on the side of ‘my-body-for-me) is not an object to be known.

This would be comparable to a textbook of  Euclidean geometry that contains the universal statements that constitute the academic discipline called geometry. Secondly, many strands are confused in Aristotle’s  hesitation about describing politics as a science, t he same strands that are confused whenever Aristotle speaks of genuine or strict universals and of genuine scientific knowledge. One strand concerns the notion of truth as correspondence and of scientific knowledge as necessarily true. Now, the correspondence is meant to obtain between the judgment and the object judged. At this point, there is a shift from the object as conceived (in the mind) to the real or extra-mental object, from the order of conception to the order of exisrtence. For the correspondence to be necessary the real object must necessitate the judgment, that is, make the judgment necessary, by virtue of its evidence, that is of the object’s being necessarily so as judged. (Notice the identification of essential, predicative judgment with existential judgment, which is not predicative) . This is what Aristotle calls scientific truth or “episteme.” Such scientific truth can obtain only in the realm of the intelligible, of what cannot possibly be otherwise than it appears, of what is what it is by necessity. When the intellect grasps that necessity with evidence, the judgment becomes necessary and necessarily true. As realized by Plato, this excludes from the realm of scientific truth (a) the sensible, for it is changeable by nature and therefore cannot make evident to the intellect that it is necessarily what it appears to be; (b) the particular or individual, for it has no intelligibility and, therefore, cannot appear as necessary; (c) the existent, for the actual correspondence between what is in the mind as judged and what is real cannot be known.

Now, whatever has to do with knowledge as correspondence, with universal knowledge, and with necessarily true knowledge is a legacy of the Platonic epistemic conception, which constrained Plato to limit the sphere of knowledge to the essences and to relegate to the realm of mere ‘opinion”the domains of the sensible and of action. That Aristotle remained tributary to the Platonic tradition shows in his preservation of the domain of s cientific knowledge in the area of the theoretical disciplines like geometry and metaphysics as different from the domain of the practical disciplines , as well as in his persistence in attempting to include knowledge of the universal in the practical ‘sciences’ or ‘arts,’ and in his confusion between the practical knowledge as found in the practical sciences –which are defined a la Plato by their variable, imprecise object and which do not result in action– with the practical wisdom of the ethical agent and of the medical practitioner -- which, as we shall see, are organized by desire, and involve deliberation and terminate in actual action. Now it is my opinion that as long as we do not give up completely the Platonic tradition related to theoretical knowledge and its related conception of knowledge as visual correspondence with what is and as necessary correspondence with what is, and as long as we do not purify Aristotle’s conception of practical knowledge from its Platonic adherences, we will remain in the epistemic conundrum we find ourselves today.
