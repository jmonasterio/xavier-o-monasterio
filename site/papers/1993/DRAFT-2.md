The Greek notion of truth
      Knowledge is  adaequatio  mentis  ad  rem  (intelligible  or  sensible
reality).
      Truth resides in judgments, for only judgments predicate.
      Perfect truth--scientific  truth--belongs  to  essential  predication,
for such judgments are universal (necessarily exceptionless)  and  eternally
so. Factual predication is at  best  contingently  and  temporarily  true--a
matter of Platonic  opinion  (doxa),  not  of  knowledge  (episteme).  Hence
sensible 'natures' and individual events, with their reduced and  contingent
intelligibility, are of secondary importance in terms of knowledge.
      Knowledge is the deed of a contemplative, immaterial,  unexposed  mind
encased in a body--a spiritual substance born to understand what  is  to  be
understood  and  to  be  beatified  by  this  disinterested   contemplation.
Therefore real is what can be  known,  that  is,  what  can  be  object  (of
predication) for this mind. [But, as Sartre would  argue,  can  the  knowing
mind itself be known? A body-object, understood as a  material  substance--a
thing--is united in the Greek  view  with  a  mind-object,  understod  as  a
spiritual substance, a spiritual thing with a particular  nature.  But  this
is not the knowig mind nor the body subject; it is the known mindn  and  the
body object].
      Theoretical and practical knowledge. Practical  knowledge  presupposes
a knower who has needs to be fulfilled and which can be frustrated.  By  the
same  token,  the  correlate  of  this  knower  is  not  real-as  object-of-
predication but real as,  let  us  say  provisorily,  object  of  desire  or
repulsion, as desirable or aversible. - This knower is  nothing  other  than
exposed  subject,  exposed  agent-patient.  This  is  the  subject-agent  of
Aristotle's Ethics and the world to which this subject is  exposed.  In  the
Ethics  this  subject  makes  general  reflective   judgments   about   what
constitutes happiness (what is in general to be aimed at  by  human  beings)
and the kind of virtues that should characterize the agent if the  agent  is
to achieve its 'natural' aim insofar as it depends on the agent and  not  on
luck.  Such  judgments  are  not  scientific   (because   not   exact,   not
exceptionless, true only for the most part). In the realm  of  praxis  there
are no scientific judgments. But what exactly is the realm of praxis?  -  Is
the subject of the Ethics the same  as  the  subject  who  makes  scientific
judgments in the Metaphysics and in  the  De  Anima?  (And  is  the  subject
spoken of in the De Anima as  an  hylemorphic  composite  the  same  as  the
subject who speaks as a pure contemplator of objects of predication  in  the
De Anima and in the Metaphysics, and the subject who speaks  as  a  rational
desirer (exposed to the world as an object of exposure) in the Ethics?
      In answer to these questions: Aristotle considers medicine  as  partly
theoretical and partly  practical.  Partly  theoretical  and  suceptible  of
strictly scientific  knowledge:  universal,  factually  necessary  judgments
(concerning, for instance, the nature of tuberculosis--a particular  virus--
and its cure--antibiotics. Partly practical: is the  particular  illness  of
this particular person tuberculosis,  and  should  antibiotics  be  employed
given his or her reaction to them? What is what makes  these  two  judgments
practical? And does that fact render them unscientific, as Aris suggests  in
the Ethics? - What makes them unscientific is that they are  factual,  about
singular individuals here and  now,  not  about  natures  as  such.  Thus  a
judgment can be theoretical--that is, of  object  of  predication,  and  not
susceptible of scientific knowledge. - But what makes  judgments  practical?
One candidate is that they are of object of desire or  aversion,  judgements
that determine that something is ethically good  or  bad.  If  so,  however,
they have to be judgments of the exposed agent in the first person,  who  by
judging chooses to pursue or avert.  This coheres with Aris' notion  of  the
practical syllogism. But the practical syllogism results  in  action.  So  a
practical judgment cannot be the deed  of  a  non-exposed  agent,  for  that
would be theoretical. If the  practical  judgment  results  in  action,  the
judgment has to be about particular object for response here  and  now,  for
only particular judgements can result in  particular  actions.  Furthermore,
correct practical judgments can be made only by wise, prudent, good  people,
for only good people can desire the good to the point of pursuing it.
      Acording to Aris, certain objects of study, because of  their  nature,
do not lend  themselves  to  scientific  knowledge.  For  instance,  ethical
matters. Why  do  ethical  matters  not  lend  themselves?  Because  ethical
judgments are  always  of  particular  (and  particulars  as  such  are  not
intelligible--universal   and   necessary--but   sensible--contingent    and
temporal, or because they are practical ( are of good  or  bad  morally  and
aim at and normally result in correct action, not in  mere  knowledge,  that
is, in contemplative knowledge?
      N. B. The very expression "ethical judgments" seems suspect.
Judgments are by  definition  predicative  and,  therefore,  true  or  false
depending on thether or not they equate the mind  to  reality.  But  ethical
judgments are practical knowledge, that  is,  knowledge  that  aims  at  and
results in (or is  identical  with)  correct,  appropriate,  'human-natural'
action. [The person who does what is right without knowing that it is  right
and because it is right has no practical knowledge. But also the person  who
'knows' what is right but does not  do  what  is  right  (the  akratic,  the
inauthentic  person)  has  no  practical  knowledge,  for  the  purpose   of
practical knowledge is the doing, the action, not the adaequatio  mentis  ad
rem. Here the "res" is good, and the way to know the good is  by  responding
to it as good, by pursuing it, not by assentig to it, for the  good  is  not
object of assent.
Yet, paradoxically, the prudent knows, does  not  act  by  mere  habituation
like a child. This is why prudence is, for  Aris,  an  intellectual  virtue.
Thus  there  is  knowledge  of  a  certain  kind,  "desirous  knowledge"  or
"knowledgeable desire",  as  Nussbaum  says,  desire  of  good  informed  by
knowledge of appropriateness to human nature, to what befits a human  being;
not mere instinctive desire of good.
      Let us clarify the knowledge of the exposed agent.  Good  or  bad  are
relative to an affectible, to a center of affectivity, to an  exposed  agent
patient. They have to do with action that produces  effects.  A  person  who
loves another wills the good of the loved. This means  that  the  lover  has
some understanding of the other, of what is good  for  the  other.  This  is
practical  knowledge.  Why  practical?   Because   under   the   appropriate
circumstances it results in  action,  in  action  invented  (hence  'known",
'projected') and chosen by the agent, and because the whole purpose of  such
knowledge is to result in action. It is knowledge (project of state  of  the
beloved and of action to be undertaken for that purpose) that only  a  lover
can  invent,  it  is  loving  action  that  caresses  the  lover   lovingly,
willingly, intentionally. Notice that this knowledge-action is in  the  flow
of exposed interaction, not objective and abstract.
      Then, what, exactly, are we talking about when  we  speak  of  ethical
judgments and ethical knowledge? Sometimes Aris is speaking  of  goodies  to
be desired and of actions to be engaged in accordingly by the one who  knows
those goodies as desirable;  sometimes  of  the  person,  that  is,  of  the
character, of the virtues to be possessed by the  person  who  would  desire
and pursue such goods. But in what person is this  talk:  in  first,  or  in
third? Exposure, by its very nature, is  in  first  person,  and  it  is  as
object (patient) and as subject (agent).  The  person  exposed  desires  and
fears this or that desired or feared in these  particular  circumstances  of
initiative or lack thereof. When I say desires or  fears  I  mean  primarily
the  actions  that  correspond  to  desiring  or  fearing,   the   practical
attitudes, whether easily and spontaneously done  because  of  the  feelings
toward the object or not. Now,  this  desiring  or  fearing  may  be  purely
passive--a matter  of  instinct--as  it  is  in  animals,  or  a  matter  of
conditioning, as it is in children and childish  adults.  Or  it  may  be  a
matter of  rational  decision,  a  choice.  If  the  choice  is  not  merely
arbitrary, as it is in the early Sartre, or if education   (habituation)  is
not to be purely conventional, as it was for the sophists, then  it  has  to
be on the basis of a theory of what is good for the human being, OR  on  the
basis of a historical comprehension. Let us consider  the  two  successively
and in contrast to each other to see if they are really different and how.
      (1) A theory à la Aristotle:
      A theory has to yield the  rationally  desirable,  what  ought  to  be
desired, whether or not the agent actually desires it.  First  we  have  how
the admirable people behave. But they  are  admirable  because  they  pursue
goods and exhibit virtues  approved  of  by  the  community,  and  they  are
approved of by the community because their effects are  desired.  Thus  what
we actually have is canonical stories of heroes from  which  the  theory  of
what is natural-humanly desirable is abstracted. Those  habituated  in  that
system and brainwashed into seeing certain virtues and actions as  desirable
will consider them natural-human. Those habituated  in  a  different  system
will have different canonical stories with different heroes. As I  explained
it in a previous paper and as Aris suggests it, there is  a  vicious  circle
involved: I judge virtuous that person whom I admire as good and pursuer  of
the good, and I admire that person as good and pursuer of the good  becauseI
have been habituated in this system. Thus what is knatural human can  appear
only within a system of desires which is a culture.
      The status of  judgments  of  existence  [different  from  existential
judgments of an  exposed  agent]  is  highly  ambiguous  for  at  least  two
reasons: (1) because existence is not a predicate [Did the  Greeks  and  the
medievals realize this fully?], and (2) because  existents  [what  Heidegger
calls essents?] are singular, concrete, contingent.
