The role of Xtian philosophy in politics: A response

In the Winter Issue of the American Catholic Philosophical Quarterly James V. Schall published an article entitled "The Role of Christian Philosophy in Politics." His claims about the

philosophical resources of Christianity in ethical matters and, consequently about its potential contribution to the world in these matters, though far from new, are worthy of the attention of any serious Catholic intellectual for two very different reasons. On the one hand, if those claims are correct, we Catholic intellectuals are seriously called to the appreciation, which perhaps has been eroded by the present climate of the Western culture, of what our faith offers us as intellectuals as well as the world and large in matters of morality. On the other hand, if Schall's claims happen to be incorrect, they deserve our attention for a very different reason. As already indicated, Schall's claims are not new. Actually, they amount to a mere

repetition of claims that have been made by Catholics for centuries, that have can be traced back to Catholic thinkers of the thirteenth century--to Aquinas in particular--and that have been repeated so often and so uncritically that they have become fundamental in the way we Catholics tend to see ourselves and demand to be seen by others. However, if such claims happen to be wrong, an assumption that is not a priori impossible, then we may be seriously harming ourselves and others by such a misrepresentation, and the sooner we get rid of it the better.

I must regretfully I confess that believe that Schall's claims are incorrect. Thus, my purpose in this article is to contribute to displace ...

I will begin with a summary of Schall's position. Then I will explain why I think that his claims are wrong. I will end up with an adumbration of an alternative view.

Schall's position

Schall's article is complex and higly ambiguous on  fundamental points. *  My intention is not to address the details of Schall's article, but to deal with what I see as its underlying ideology. This ideology is constituted by a view of faith, of reason, and of their relationship which is historically conditioned and by now has produced enough bitter fruits to     , but whose indisputability Schall assumes and expects others to assume as a matter of fact.

Schall's article sounds like a pep talk to sophomoric Catholic intellectuals. Its gist is the following: Because we possess the richness of divine revelation, we Catholic intellectuals--Catholic philosophers in particular--are in a unique position to offer to the world a contribution which it more than ever needs. The contribution is nothing less than the correct and in principle universally acceptable political philosophy. By political philosophy Schall seems to understand a rationally justified, normative conception of the human being, from which derive both the obligatory code of conduct of the  citizens of the well ordered polis as well as the kind of institutions needed to sustain that conduct. The modern world needs very urgently that contribution because it is the victim of what Samuel Johnson called a "'violated morality' that results in a perverse substitute for religion.Activities and institutions contrary to reason and to the understanding of reason that is embodied in a given human nakture, that, as such, has no specific origin in any human making, replace the activities and institutions said to manifest and support classical morality and religion" (p. 8).

 

Schall uses the name "Politics" in its classical, Aristotelian sense. The word refers to the polis, that is, to society as concretely constituted by its members and its institutions. Politics is a given conception of the well ordered society and of the institutitions it should develop to make possible the conduct of genuinely well ordered people. The role of Christian philosophy in politics, to which the title of Schall's article alludes, is to contribute the correct and universally acceptable conception of the desirable conduct of the citizens of the well ordered society as well as of the institutions that would make possible or facilitate such conduct. In short, what Schall is talking about is what today we call "ethics" rather than politics, namely, the obligatory code of conduct of the members of a well ordered society. And his main claim is that the code of conduct of Christianity is the correct and in principle universally acceptable "politics" by which the conduct of the people of all nations ought to be regulated and to which the institutions of all societies ought to conform. Contributing such politics is what Schall defines as "the role of Christian philosophy in politics."

Notice that this role devolves, according to Schall, not to Chriustianity as such but to Christian philosophy

   Xtian philosophy has a role in relation to politics (that is, the mores and the institutions of the polis).

This role is the affirmation and rational justification of the fundamental mores of the well ordered polis, which coincide with the obligations that Christians recognize (without needing to justify them rationally in order to accept them as obligations) because they are Christians .

We Christians actually have--or at least possess the resources to produce by means of philosophical reflection --the only valid and universally acceptable ethical doctrine by which the conduct of the citizens of the well ordered polis should be regulated, and in conformity with which the institutions of the well ordered polis that make possible and facilitate such conduct should be developed. Yet, not only are we not listened to in the cacophonous moral dialogue of today's world, but our voice, unlike any other, is systematically silenced and dismissed. As to why our voice is silenced and why our valid and in principle universally acceptable ethical doctrine is not actually accepted and does not regulate the conduct of the citizens in the modern world, Schall's explanation seems to be that the large majority of today's people, except for those Christians who remain faithful to Christianity and try to live as Christians, are victims of a sort of inverted religion that inspires an exclusively self-concerned conduct which is totally at odds with the valid ethical doctrine.

Alas!, I cannot guarantee that my summary of what I understand to be the gist of Schall's complex article does full justice to his intentions. Perhaps it does not do him justice at all. However, I do not think it is my fault, for Schall's claims are so general and so unclear as to give one pause. All I can say is that my summary is an honest version of what I understood to be the thrust of Schall's article, and why I found his article worthy of attention. After all, I am a Christian, too, and am consequently deeply interested in the ethical resources of my tradition, which according to Schall are extraordinary and which I obviously had so far missed.

Now, when one encounters an article of this nature, one is naturally weary. After all, already Plato had claimed that philosophers possessed the resources to produce the blueprint for the well ordered polis, and we are still waiting... But could it not be, one thinks as a Christian, that Christianity as the revealed way of living furnishes the valid and in principle universally accepatble general pattern for the conduct of the good citizens and for the institutions of the well ordered polis? That, of course, is what Schall thinks. Why then would this other Christian, myself, pass this opportunity to find out how good Schall is at making his promise good, given the all important nature of what is promised? Indeed, if Schall's claims about what we Christians possess and can offer to the world at large to create as well ordered a polis as it is compatible with our human weaknesses, then I am a Christian--and I am far from being alone--who has failed to understand Christianity. This role, however, is denied in the contemporary political dialogue . In fact, the voice of Christianity is not even allowed to be heard. (Why? Because the appeal to religion is obscurantist, it does not respect the neutrality of reason).

Schall's claims

Johnson thought that the consolation of philo and of Chistianity were different and incompatible, and that the Christian way to face death was superior. In other words, reason and faith, in his view, did not yield the same practical truths.

Boethius, without realizing what he was doing, preferred the consolation of reason, and in this regard Johnson disapproves of him.

Schall : The truths of faith, in "political" matters, are the truths of reason. "By carefully reflecting on the exact Xtian doctrines... to grasp what they mean, one is a better philosopher, as philosopher" (p. 2) . For the more reason is rational the more it agrees with faith in matters of conduct. In other words, one starts with the Xtian doctrines, with the truths of faith. One justifies them to oneself and to non-Christians by reason, by appeal to human nature, to the intention written in things . Boethius therefore was not wrong, for the consolations of Christianity may be more, but are partly the same as the consolations of philosophy .  

Myself : I agree with Johnson who saw Christianity, that is, baptized reason, and reason without revelation, as motivating two different conducts.

My objection

- If reason and religion coincide in ethical matters, then the voice of religion is not needed, for the voice of philosophical reason ( which is neutral and independent of religion ) suffices. In fact, the voice of religion as different from the voice of philosophical reason cannot but interfere. This is why the voice of religion is silenced: in a pluralistic society, that is, in a society whose members have a different conception of the telos, of the way to be happy, the voice of religion has no title to be heard, for reason is supreme, the standard, certainly the only universally acceptable standard.  

- Why then does the voice of religion appear to be needed?

Among Christians, because not everybody can philosophize. Among non-Christians and certain Christians who have been colonized by non-Christians, because they have an inverted religion, ethical solipsism., which naturally motivates them to act egoistically. If only they were neutral, that is, had no distorted interests , had the genuinely human interests at heart, they would come philosophically, as a matter of natural knowledge, to the same views we Christians have (by revelation, by ecclesiastical authority, by a rational reading of human nature which is negatively limited by revelation?).

[But which are these views, given that the Gospel is silent for the most part? The dignity of the human person . But why is the dignity of the human person respected by keeping the fetus alive rather than by getting rid of, say, an accidental pregnancy that will break a marriage? Whose human dignity is to be respected? Why is the dignity of a beginning life with little or no consciousness of self, with no perrsonal fears and desires and projects, with no acquired responsibilities, to be preferred in any conceivable case? [I am not necessarily saying that this should not be the case. I am suggesting as a possibility to be considered that perhaps Christians can see that the new life should never be suppressed because they have resources--the providence of God, the sacredness of the life of a child of God, the hope that this life is not where happiness is to be fully achieved--that non-Christians do not have . In which case they may be right in avoiding abortion, but they cannot expect non-Christians to understand that obligation, and cannot demonstrate it. In short, human nature--like Aristotelian happiness--is an abstraction incapable of grounding obligations; it needs to be supplemented by a religious or a cultural vision which is not itself rationally deducible . HOW ARE CHRISTIANS SUPPOSED TO LIVE IN A PLURAL AND PLURALISTIC WORLD? THIS IS THE QUESTION. And we are the first victims of the illusion of "Christian philosophy."

My diagnosis :

We invented, or at least perpetuated, the myth of neutral reason--and with it we invented philosophy as a scientific discipline --as a tribunal without interests, or in which only the truly rational or genuinely human interests count because they are dictated by human nature (the papal appeal is to a theory of this kind), or in which the participants hush their interests under the veil of ignorance, and agree on the fundamental, genuinely human interests--but he does not like the theory of rights!). And some, like Schall, continue to believe in that nonsense, without realizing that it is the appeal to that nonsense that neutralizes the voice of Christianity in the political dialogue about public mores and social institutions that support it .  

Better to recognize that we Christians bring to the dialogue different interests, a different horizon of possibilities, different capacities, different standards.

Let me then take as good, as serious, as honest a look as I possibly can to what Schall has to say.

     I begin with his claim concerning what we possess in ethical matters and can share with the world at large . Schall proceeds by indirection, but his point is clear: we possess a vision of the worth and meaning of the human person that is in accordance with the natural structure of human worth and dignity--a vision which is made explicit in the statements of Catholicism about itself and its understanding of the worth and meaning of the human person (3-4). For example? Schall confesses that in connection with Christian thought and morality "abortion is my least favorite topic and I hate even to allude to it" (5) NOTE.

Nonetheless, and perhaps not accidentally, the issue of abortion, as presented by John Paul II in an address to the Irish Bishops, constitutes his only concrete example of the Christian vision of the worth and meaning of the human person. The passage quoted by Schall reads as follows:

Schall's commentary is important for our understanding of his position:

"What is to be noted about these remarks in particular... is that they claim to be spoken not merely in the name of religion but rather by religion in the name of reason. Indeed, they are made by a religion that insists that reason is not contradictory or alien to its doctrines and practices, one that insists that it must give valid reasons to show this consistent relationship" (4).

Indeed, there is no doubt that the Holy Father is here stating that the right of life and and the consequent interdiction of abortion as a way of killing can be known and convincingly demonstrated in a purely rational way, without any appeal to revelation. And this is central for Schall's argument. For his argument is that Christianity has unique resources in political matters, that is, in matters pertaining to human conduct to produce a well ordered polis and pertaining to the institutions necessary in a well ordered society to facilitate such conduct.

But what is what makes Christianity unique? After all, did'nt the Holy Father just say that in matters pertaining to human dignity human reason suffices itself, so that revelation is not needed? True. However, this does not detract from the fact that, though not absolutely needed in political matters, that is, in matters of mrality, revelation--Christian revelation, that is--gives us infallible guidance on the basis of God's authority. In other words, we Christians have two ways of access to the truth in ethical matters: revelation and faith. Revelation teaches us infallibly what is right and wrong in matters of human dignity, and reason independently confirms what revelation teaches. In this way, supported by revelation, the christian thinker--what Schall calls the Christian philosopher--knows through faith what is right and wrong, and can therefore look securely for the reasons of the natural order that demonstrate what revelation teaches, and thus enlighten those who do not count with the help of divine revelation. For, by contrast, those who do not enjoy the benefit of revelation concerning political matters, can only count on reason, which though sufficient is not as sharp and secure in the knowledge it reaches as revelation. Thus, the Christian philosopher, enlightened by faith as to what is to be demonstrated by reason concerning political matters, can steadfastly an unflinchingly hold to the truth and make it more clearly known to those who have access to it only through reason.  [ON THE COMPARATIVE WEAKNESS OF REASON LEFT TO ITSELF SCHALL FOLLOWS ST THOMAS: P. 6].

Now the distinction between reason and revelation , on which the theory of the two sources of knowledge rests, has been with us inm the Western world for a long long time. Not without reason is traced back, though not exclusively, to Thomas Aquinas among others of his contemporaries. It has become part of the cultural furniture of the Western world. We all tend to take the distinction for granted. In fact, Schall and Pope John Paul II take it for granted, for only on the assumption of that distinction does it make to say, as the Pope does, that "the argument against abortion is based not only on the data of faith but also on reasons of the natural order," and only on that basis coan Schall sensibly comment that the Pope's remarks "claim to be spoken not merely in the name of religion but rather by religion in the name of reason." In fact, "revelation" and "reason" are reciprocally defining terms, like circumference and cneter. "Revelation" means what is revealed by God and is accepted on the basis of Gods authority, because revealed by God. "Reason" means what humnan intelligence left to its own devices comes to know and is accepted on the basis of its evidence to human intelligence.

What has happened in the modern world is not that the meaning of the terms revelation and reason has changed, but that, on the very principle of the autonomy and self-sufficiency of reason within its own limits, revelation has been displaced by reason as the supreme standard.
