Response to Dennis M. Doyle's "Toward a Working Definition

 of the 'Catholic Intellectual Tradition'"

First of all, I want to thank Dennis for having "taken the challenge" I posed, for I posed it in a positive spirit, in the hope of our coming to define a viable notion of the CIT, if indeed there can be one. I admire Dennis' courage, for his task, compared to mine, is the truly difficult one. Destroying is always easier than creating.

Five minutes is an impossibly short time to do justice to such a multifaceted paper as Dennis' is. I will therefore limit myself to a few substantive remarks.

(1) Dennis agrees with me on the most important point of my paper.

The central purpose of my paper was to criticize a particular version of the CIT to which in the past Catholic universities appealed either implicitly or explicitly. Now, as I read Dennis' paper, not only does he not attempt to defend that version, but he joins me in discarding it, for he writes: "I would first of all try to argue that the relationship between faith and reason in the particular way that Xavier indicated is a view associated with nineteenth century Catholicism, especially with the document on faith and reason Dei Filius , issued by the First Vatican Council in 1870. ... The main thrust of my approach--this is Dennis speaking--would be to demonstrate that the Second Vatican Council, while retaining key essential elements of the Vatican I definition, moved significantly beyond this view" ( ibid. ). According to Dennis, "Underlying the Vatican I approach to faith and reason is a view of revelation that has come to be labeled "propositionalism." Those who hold this view speak as though God had delivered a preset package of doctrines... In its most extreme forms, propositionalism is a type of Catholic fundamentalism. It is ultimately a failure to take seriously the elements of human experience and personal witness that underlie particular formulations of beliefs" (p. 11).

Since all of the above amounts to a rejection on Dennis' part of the very understanding of faith and of revelation I rejected and on which the version of CIT I criticized rests, I feel entitled to conclude that we are in agreement as to the main point of my paper, which I take as an important step in the right direction.  

(2) On  the epistemological presuppositions of the CIT.

Two comments concerning what Dennis calls "the epistemological presuppositions that underlie any definition of the CIT" (p. 10).

Rather than in Vatican I, Dennis grounds his version of the CIT in Vatican II, where he finds a more "existential" and, to him as a Catholic, a more congenial view of faith and of revelation. I find the same, as I stated it in a note of my paper. Now, whatever Dennis may mean by more 'existential,' I definitely mean 'less intellectualistic.' I am strongly opposed to a notion of the Christian faith that makes it consist primarily in an intellectual act, and to a notion of revelation that makes it consist primarily in the transmission of truths. Now, did Vatican I conjure out of the blue these epistemological presuppositions? Was not Vatican I relying on six centuries of meditation on the self-understanding by Catholics that took shape in the thirteenth century? And who but Aquinas consecrated that view, who defined the act of faith as assent of the intellect to the truths revealed by God? By isolating Vatican I from the past Dennis may succeed in absolving Aquinas, but at the price of rendering unintelligible Vatican I and six centuries of  intellectual history of Catholicism.

Secondly, for all my sympathy with the general direction taken by Vatican II, I am less sanguine than Dennis as to its ability to ground directly a viable version of the CIT. It seems to me that Dennis is not sufficiently aware of the real status of the epistemological presuppositions of Vatican II. While retrieving from the past of the Church a more existential view of faith and of revelation, Vatican II nonetheless kept practically intact the rest of the intellectual framework of Vatican I, constituted by such pieces as the division into two domains--the domain of faith and the domain of reason--, the autonomy of faith and the autonomy of reason within their proper domains, and the supremacy of faith over reason in the terrain that is common to both, except that in case of conflict in the latter, to use Dennis' words, "it may be the particular expression of the faith that needs to change, not always the process of reasoning" (p. 13).

In my opinion, in terms of its epistemological presuppositions, Vatican II is only half-cooked and, thus, indigestible, because its notions of faith and of revelation are inconsistent with the rest of the epistemological presuppositions it kept intact. What Vatican II changed was not those presuppositions but the willingness to force the actual life of the Church and of Church affiliated institutions into the mold defined by them. Since the mold remains, however, though--thank God!--not the will to shape life to it, we are, for the time being, in the lurch. For this reason I persist in thinking that, even if we consider the alternative offered by Vatican II, today we Catholics have no operative intellectual tradition worthy of the name.

Dennis obviously disagrees with me, since he proposes a version of the CIT grounded in Vatican II. Let us, then, consider how operative his version can be.

3) Concerning Dennis' own version of the CIT .

Dennis' version of the CIT, compared to the version I criticized, is minimalist. The version I criticized was sufficiently substantial and well defined as to result in relatively clear prescriptions concerning the functions of theology, of philosophy, and of the other disciplines, and the respective roles of theologians, philosophers, and the practitioners of other disciplines. A Catholic university was, in that version of the CIT, an institution in which such functions and roles were carried accordingly and whose members consequently knew what was expected of them. By contrast, Dennis' version is minimalist in that the functions defined are extremely general and the roles float in a vacuum, with the result that anybody may be equally considered to be or not to be acting according to the CIT and what is expected of him or her.

Indeed, the first of the three functions of Dennis' version is the integration of "what Catholics 'know' according to their faith with 'knowledge' from various other sources" (p. 4). But what do we Catholics know according to our faith, if we can always appeal to the distance between faith and its expression? Is not Curran's case a perfect example? Furthermore, whose role is it to carry this integrating function: the Catholic theologian's, the non-Catholic theologian's, the Catholic philosopher's, the non-Catholic philosopher's, the practitioners' of all disciplines--be they Catholic or not? Since this role floats in a vacuum, and since different Catholics know different things according to their faith, anybody can equally be considered to be fulfilling his or her role, or not fulfilling it.

For obvious reasons, the same applies to the second function of Dennis' version of the CIT, namely "the study of how this process of integration [of what Catholics know from their faith with what they know from other sources] has actually taken place in history" (p. 4).

The third and last function of the CIT is "the study of anything worthy of study for its own sake, using methods arising from Catholic sensibilities and intellectual tendencies" (p. 4). I assume that anything worthy of study for its own sake includes all the disciplines. But are all the practitioners of those disciplines expected to possess a Catholic sensibility? And what if they don't, as many obviously won't? Is it fair to expect a Catholic sensibility from, say, an agnostic theologian, an atheist philosopher, a Unitarian professor of literature, when we welcome them as they are and regardless of what they are?

How can a Catholic university fairly or even realistically expect from its members a Catholic sensibility when it explicitly

discards religious affiliation from its screening process in the name of legitimate pluralism?

For these reasons among others, I am rather skeptical about Dennis' minimalist version of the CIT. It seems to me that such minimalism amounts practically to a confirmation of my claim that, at least for the time being, there is no such a thing as an identifiable Catholic intellectual tradition capable of operating as such.

Yet, I do not want to finish on this purely negative note.

The purpose of my original paper, I repeat, was not negative but positive, and I have important positive things to say. First, I am encouraged by the fact that other Catholics like Dennis share my perception that the version of the CIT I criticized is dead and not to be resuscitated. I see this as positive, for I think that this recognition forces us to see ourselves more honestly and to strive in a different direction. For instance, though I do not think that Dennis' "Catholic sensibilities," by itself can do the job he wants it to do, I also think that it is a promising area to explore.

Secondly, though I am often irked by the wording concerning the "CIT" in the official documents of the University, which make it sound as if we Catholics had a definite intellectual tradition, and though personally I would prefer as a basic point of reference just one , more humble and concrete and realistic document concerning not the intellectual tradition of the Catholic Church but the Catholic tradition and the way in which in today's pluralistic world this Catholic institution of learning struggles to live from and represent that tradition,

I have a profound sympathy for the intention of this University to remain Catholic and to proclaim it. Why? Because Catholic universities today are about the only place in the academic world in which intellectuals who are Catholics and proud of being Catholics can take avowedly Catholic stands in the classroom without being automatically banished or at least systematically ostracized as proselytizers, and in which students have a chance to be exposed to those stands. Indeed, the academic world is far less pluralistic than it makes itself appear to be. Religious agnosticism, atheism, purely intellectual interest or total disinterest in religious matters are considered to be unprejudiced, whereas any position that smacks of religious commitment, much more the position of someone who openly confesses a particular religious commitment and works from it, is considered prejudiced and, therefore, unscientific and to be excluded from academia. Thus, for all my reservations concerning a Catholic intellectual tradition and what I see as clumsy documents of this University in that connection, I am behind those who want UD to remain an avowedly Catholic University. Indeed, I doubt that the conversation we are having here and Dennis' courageous effort would be possible at all, and even welcome by some of us, in a secular or even in a non-Catholic university.

XAVIER O. MONASTERIO
