Santoni on Bad Faith and Sincerity: A Vindication of Sartre
      Ronald Santoni's book, Bad Faith,  Good  Faith,  and  Authenticity  in
Sartre's Early Philosophy, appeared recently.[i] It is a work that has  been
long in the making. Indeed, Sartre's views  on  bad  faith  and  the  issues
related to it have constituted  one  of  Santoni's  life-long  philosophical
interests, as evidenced by his article of more than thirty years ago.[ii]
      Were my intention to write a review of this  book,  I  most  certainly
would have positive things to say about  Santoni's  systematic  exploration,
in the  latter  part  of  his  work,  of  the  notions  of  good  faith  and
authenticity which in Being and Nothingness Sartre  left  almost  untouched,
or at least seriously undeveloped. However, my interest here  is  simply  to
deal  with  Santoni's  criticisms  of  Sartre's  views  on  bad  faith   and
sincerity, which Santoni  feels  compelled  to  improve  upon  in  order  to
"salvage" them. For my part, I am convinced that Sartre's views stand in  no
need of being  rescued.[iii]  In  any  case,  Santoni's  book  furnishes  an
excellent opportunity to review and reevaluate an  important  piece  of  the
Sartrean heritage that, I think, is quite worthy of attention.
Bad faith: A cynical lie?
      Sartre defines bad faith as a lie to oneself, a definition with  which
Santoni ends up agreeing, not without sweating much  about  the  possibility
of such a lie. However, when he considers  Sartre's  claim  that  bad  faith
neither is nor can possibly be a cynical  lie,  Santoni  replies  with  what
appears to be an unqualified rejection:
      "In the present chapter,  I  wish  to  show  that,  in  spite  of  his
      insistence to the contrary, the deceptive  consciousness  that  Sartre
      characterizes as bad  faith  is  a  cynical  consciousness;  that  the
      project of bad faith is a cynical project; and that bad faith... is  a
      form of cynical 'lying'."[iv]

      Why does Sartre think that the lie to oneself by which  bad  faith  is
constituted is not--and cannot possibly be--a cynical  lie?  Simply  because
such a lie would be impossible if it were  cynical.  After  all,  as  Sartre
observes in this connection, bad faith is not an ordinary lie; it is  a  lie
to oneself, whereas in the case of the ordinary lie we lie to other  people.
Now, in the latter case my lie can be utterly cynical, that  is,  I  can  be
coldly and blatantly aware of the truth I am hiding from the person to  whom
I lie, precisely because I am not the person to whom I lie and  because  the
person to whom I lie ignores my intention to deceive  him  or  her.  In  the
case of bad faith, however, I lie to myself. Here the liar and the  lied  to
are one and the same person. Well, this, precisely, is why Sartre  does  not
think that bad faith is or can be a cynical lie. For if I were  fully  aware
that I am lying, then  the  possibility  of  believing  my  own  lie,  which
grounds my attempt of lying to myself, would be absent:
      "We must agree in fact that if I deliberately and cynically attempt to
      lie to myself, I fail completely in this undertaking;  the  lie  falls
      back and collapses beneath my look; it is ruined from  behind  by  the
      very consciousness of lying to  myself  which  pitilessly  constitutes
      itself well within my project as its very condition."[v]
      I find Sartre's reasoning totally convincing. However,  since  Santoni
has so unequivocally expressed his unqualifiedly  disagreement  with  Sartre
concerning the cynicism of bad faith,  one  expects  him  to  disagree  with
Sartre on this point. Instead of doing that, however, Santoni  now  declares
himself willing to concede that, after all, Sartre is right at least  up  to
a point: "I urge that Sartre is justified in concluding only that  bad-faith
cannot be cynical  in  exactly  the  same  way  that  lying  in  general  is
cynical."[vi]  But isn't this, precisely, the only  sense  in  which  Sartre
affirms that bad faith cannot be a cynical lie, as shown by  the  reason  he
gives in support of his claim? And, if  so,  doesn't  conceding  this  point
amount to fully agreeing with Sartre?
      Anyway, it seems that Santoni is determined to find  Sartre  at  fault
in one way or another, for after making  the  essential  concession  I  just
discussed he nevertheless states:
      "In focusing on the single  and  unified  consciousness  of  lying  to
      oneself..., Sartre has failed to  acknowledge  the  blatantly  cynical
      aspects of the kind of lying that, by his own admission,  goes  on  in
      bad faith and self-deception.
      "In what follows, I shall try  to  make  clear  some  of  the  cynical
      aspects in bad faith as 'lying to oneself'."[vii]
       It would seem that showing some cynical  aspects  of  bad  faith,  if
they are not the only one denied by Sartre, can hardly be  construed  as  an
objection to  Sartre.  However,  since  I  am  taking  this  opportunity  to
revisit, on the occasion of Santoni's book, the issues  connected  with  bad
faith, it might be interesting to find out whether he really  has  a  point.
After all, by showing that bad faith can be cynical in certain ways  Santoni
might be adding a significant complement to Sartre's analysis of bad  faith.

      Where, then, does Santoni find the  blatantly  cynical  aspects  that,
supposedly, by Sartre's own unnoticed admission, are involved in bad  faith?
Santoni grounds  his  argument  in  the  fact  that  in  analyzing  concrete
instances of bad faith, such  as  those  of  the  "coquette"  of  Being  and
Nothingness and of Stekel's "frigid woman,"  Sartre  uses  expressions  that
clearly denote on their part a  cynical  will  to  lie  to  themselves.  For
instance, Santoni points out, the frigid woman, as her case is  analyzed  by
Sartre himself,
      "makes 'knowing preparations' for self-deception:  she  'deliberately'
      chooses to think about domestic chores. By choosing  the  'distracted'
      consciousness, she 'disarms' in advance any counterbeliefs..."[viii]
      Thus, Santoni concludes, "Who  would  want  to  say  that  this  inner
conniving to prove to herself that she is frigid is not cynical...?[ix]
      What escapes Santoni is that the reason why  Sartre  uses  expressions
of that sort in each of his analyses of particular cases of bad faith[x]  is
that there was no other possible way to make clear
to his readers that the person in bad faith is really lying  to  himself  or
herself rather than being merely mistaken. Indeed, if it  is  not  pointedly
stressed that the person knows the truth and is hiding it  from  himself  or
herself, it would appear that the person does not lie but is just  mistaken.
In addition, Santoni fails to notice  (or  in  any  case  to  bring  to  his
readers' attention) that Sartre compensates the  expressions  that  indicate
that the person in bad faith  knows,  with  corresponding  expressions  that
indicate that the person does not know, which expressions do  not  favor  at
all Santoni's argument for cynicism. Thus, in the case  of  the  "coquette,"
Sartre tells us that "she knows very well the intentions of  her  companion"
(a "knows very well" that Santoni would take  as  evidence  of  her  cynical
lying to herself), only to add immediately  that  "she  does  not  apprehend
this conduct as an attempt  to  achieve...  'the  first  approach'."  Again,
Sartre tells us that "she is  profoundly  aware  of  the  desire  which  she
inspires" (a "profoundly aware' that Santoni would take as evidence  of  her
cynical lying to herself), only to add immediately that "she  recognizes  it
[the  desire]  only  to  the  extent  that  it  transcends   itself   toward
admiration, esteem, respect..."[xi]  The same, of  course,  happens  in  the
examples of the frigid woman and of the homosexual.
      In  short,  Santoni  takes  at  their  face  value  and  in  isolation
expressions that in other contexts would indeed indicate  blatant  knowledge
of the truth and blatant disregard of it, without realizing that  here  they
are qualified by other  expressions  that  indicate,  in  the  same  person,
ignorance of the truth. Now, this interplay of expressions of knowledge  and
ignorance is, I repeat, the only way in which Sartre could  make  clear  the
subtle mixture of knowledge and ignorance of the truth involved in lying  to
oneself. Indeed, if there were blatant knowledge of the  truth,  as  Santoni
seems to think, then the lie would indeed be cynical, but  bad  faith  would
be impossible, for one would in no way be able to believe one's lie. If,  on
the contrary, there were full ignorance of the  truth,  then  one  would  be
mistaken in what one believes but could not possibly be  lying  to  oneself,
since in that case one has no inkling of the truth. Thus, only if one  knows
and does not know at the same time is bad faith possible. This  is  what  in
each of his analyses of particular examples of bad faith Sartre  points  out
to his reader, and rightly so.  Thus,  to  pay  onesided  attention  to  the
expressions that denote knowledge, and  to  construe  them  as  an  implicit
"admission" by Sartre that bad faith  is  cynical  in  certain  regards,  is
simply a bad mistake.
      I have, therefore, to conclude, after this examination  of   Santoni's
criticisms concerning the non-cynicism of bad faith, that Sartre's  position
stands in no need of his salvaging efforts.  Let  me  now  turn  to  another
rescuing operation undertaken by Santoni, this one regarding sincerity.
The bad faith of sincerity
      "I submit," Santoni boldly announces, "that equivocation lies  at  the
root of Sartre's analysis of  sincerity  and  of  what  I  take  to  be  his
erroneous depiction of sincerity as bad faith."[xii]
      Why is Sartre's depiction of sincerity wrong?  According  to  Santoni,
Sartre  borrows  the  word  "sincerity"  from  ordinary  language.  Now,  in
ordinary language the word means the quality of "being what one is," but  in
a sense that differs  from  Sartre's  idiosyncratic,  technical  meaning  of
"being what one  is."  Unfortunately,  Sartre  confuses  his  own  technical
meaning of "sincerity," which is a form of bad faith, with  the  meaning  of
the  word  as  used  in  ordinary  language.  As  a  consequence   of   this
equivocation, Sartre concludes that  whoever  is  sincere  in  the  ordinary
sense of the word is in bad faith, which is an unacceptable conclusion.
      Lest I be suspected of having missed the point of  Santoni's  argument
against Sartre, let us have Santoni speak for himself:
      "But  my  contention  is  precisely  that  Sartre's  crucial  step  is
      philosophically illegitimate. It is  based  on  an  equivocation.  The
      meaning of 'to be what one is' as intended by ordinary language cannot
      be identified with the meaning of that expression when it is  used  by
      Sartre technically either to define the 'in-itself' or to characterize
      human reality's bad faith goal to tie down or objectify consciousness.
      But Sartre appears either to ignore or to exploit this ambiguity.  And
      he makes no effort to justify this move from one universe of discourse
      to  another.  Left  as  is,  his  procedure  seems  to  be  arbitrary,
      unwarranted... And any inference he draws about sincerity on the basis
      of his having inserted 'being what one is' (as  an  ordinary  language
      expression) into his own distinctive system of  ontological  meanings,
      would likewise appear presumptuous, questionable, and  philosophically
      illicit.[xiii]
      I will be no  less  blunt  than  Santoni.  He  has  completely  missed
Sartre's point. It is true that Sartre borrows  the  word  "sincerity"  from
ordinary language, and that he endows it with a different meaning,  his  own
technical meaning. But it is false that Sartre  confuses  the  two  meanings
and infers that whoever is  sincere  in  the  ordinary  sense  of  the  word
necessarily is in bad faith. What Sartre affirms, as we shall see,  is  that
whoever is sincere in his own technical sense is  in  bad  faith,  and  that
there is a particular case--but  not  all  cases,  as  Santoni  contends--in
which being sincere  in  the  ordinary  sense  of  the  word  fulfills  that
condition and is, therefore, a case of bad  faith.  And  now,  to  turn  the
tables on Santoni, I contend that in his eagerness to rescue those  who  are
sincere in the ordinary sense of the word from a supposed  universal  charge
of bad faith that Sartre does not make, Santoni assumes  that  there  is  an
absolute incompatibility between being sincere in Sartre's  technical  sense
and being sincere in the ordinary meaning of the word. As a consequence,  he
draws the wrong inference that those  who  are  "sincere"  in  the  ordinary
sense of the word can never be in bad faith. This mistake, as we shall  see,
has the unfortunate effect of obscuring completely the special  subtlety  of
the lie involved in the form of bad faith that Sartre calls sincerity.
      Rather than conducting my exploration  by  way  of  trying  to  follow
Santoni in his elaborate--and, in my opinion, wrongheaded--attempt  to  show
how mistaken Sartre is concerning sincerity, I prefer to take a  fresh  look
at the Sartrean  text  itself,  to  see  whether  it  can  possibly  support
Santoni's interpretation.
      Sartre introduces us to  his  own  technical  meaning  of  "sincerity"
through the example of "the champion of sincerity," the homosexual's  friend
who wants him to avow what the homosexual 'knows very  well,'  namely,  that
he is a homosexual. Apparently the homosexual's friend is wholeheartedly  in
favor of sincerity in the ordinary sense of the word, since all he seems  to
want from the homosexual  is  a  truthful  avowal  of  what  the  homosexual
actually is, namely, a homosexual.  Yet,  at  this  point,  Sartre  suddenly
turns the tables on the champion of sincerity: "We ask here: Who is  in  bad
faith? The homosexual or the champion of sincerity?"[xiv]
      We may be surprised. If the champion of sincerity  is  in  bad  faith,
then it seems that whoever wants to be told the  truth  rather  than  a  lie
would be in bad faith, which does not make sense. Indeed, it does  not.  But
that is not at all what Sartre means by "the champion of sincerity,"  as  he
explains immediately. What  characterizes  the  champion  of  sincerity--and
here Sartre comes to his own technical  meaning  of  the  word--is  that  he
wants from the homosexual not just a truthful avowal of what he is (that  is
what sincerity in the ordinary sense of the word means), but an avowal of  a
particular kind. Indeed, as  Sartre  explains,  the  champion  of  sincerity
wants his friend to confess that he is a homosexual  "as  this  table  is  a
table, or as this red-haired man is red-haired."[xv]
      "Sincerity," therefore, in Sartre's technical sense--and such  is  the
sincerity championed by the champion  of  sincerity--is  the  acknowledgment
that one is what one is in the same way as a  thing  is  what  it  is.  Now,
according to Sartre this is false and, therefore,  such  an  acknowledgement
would be a lie.  Indeed,  the  fundamental  difference,  in  Sartre's  eyes,
between a table and a homosexual  is  that  in  people  "existence  precedes
essence." This is to say that human beings do not come to the world  with  a
pre-given identity; they define themselves,  they  choose  their  individual
identity and are responsible for it. The homosexual, in  Sartre's  view,  is
not  born  predestined  to  be  a  homosexual,  he  chooses  himself  as   a
homosexual. Thus, if he remains a homosexual, he will remain so  by  choice;
and if, on the contrary, he decides not to be a homosexual anymore, he  will
not be a homosexual anymore. The table, by contrast, did not  choose  to  be
what it is and cannot choose to become a different  table.  Thus,  what  the
champion of sincerity wants from his friend, is not merely  an  avowal  that
would be "sincere" in the ordinary-language sense of the word,  namely,  the
honest acknowledgment that he is a homosexual,  but  an  avowal  that  would
imply that his homosexuality is something which is as little his  choice  as
it is the table's choice to be the table it  is.  That  is  why,  as  Sartre
points  out,  when  asked  for  this  kind  of  avowal,  "The  homosexual...
struggles with all his strength against the crushing view that his  mistakes
constitute for him a destiny. He does not wish to let himself be  considered
as a thing."[xvi] What this homosexual refuses, in short, is the  bad  faith
that the avowal he is demanded would imply, for it would amount to lying  to
himself about his responsibility for  what  he  is  as  well  as  about  his
freedom to be in the future what he decides.
      Now, Santoni accuses Sartre of confusing "being what one is"  in  this
technical sense with the "being what one is" of sincerity  in  the  ordinary
sense of the word and, consequently, of concluding, or  at  least  implying,
that one cannot be a sincere person  in  the  ordinary  sense  of  the  word
without ipso facto being in bad faith. Afflicted by  this  mistake,  Santoni
helpfully   comes   to   Sartre's   rescue   with    "a    suggestion    for
reconstruction"[xvii].
      "I now wish to suggest that, if Sartre had been more attentive to  the
      ordinary-language meaning of 'to be what one is,' he might  well  have
      avoided this equivocation and come up with a translation of the 'being
      what one is' as used in the ordinary language that  does  not  condemn
      sincerity to bad faith...
      "That is to say, sincerity is often intended to mean that one is 'what
      one is' in the mode of being of self-consciousness, not in the mode of
      the objectification or thingification of consciousness, or of  fleeing
      one's freedom."[xviii]
      In short, Santoni will show that, if only Sartre had not confused  the
two different meanings of "being what one is," he could have  preserved  his
valuable technical notion  of  sincerity  without  unfairly  and  mistakenly
condemning to bad faith those who are sincere in the ordinary sense  of  the
word.
      Unfortunately for Santoni, far from confusing the two meanings of  the
word "sincerity," Sartre himself points out that the fact  that  people  are
not what they are as things are what they are (his technical sense of  being
what one is) does not imply that they are not what they  are  in  the  sense
presupposed by the use of  the  word  "sincere"  in  its  ordinary  language
meaning. In other words, Sartre maintains that people can be what  they  are
in the sense referred to by the ordinary word "sincere,"  without  being  in
bad faith:
      "Yet there is no doubt that I am in a sense a  café  waiter--otherwise
      could I not just as well call myself a diplomat or a reporter? But  if
      I am one, this cannot be in the mode of being of the in-itself. I am a
      waiter in the mode of being what I am not."[xix]

      It is my conviction  that  against  the  rock  of  this  crucial  text
Santoni's accusation that Sartre condemns to bad faith  all  those  who  are
sincere in the ordinary sense of the word is irremediably  wrecked.  Indeed,
as this text shows, Sartre does not deny at all that I am what I am  in  the
ordinary sense of the expression "being  what  one  is."  If  I  am  a  café
waiter, I am a café waiter and cannot sincerely say or believe that I  am  a
diplomat. The only thing I can say sincerely is that I  am  a  café  waiter,
which I am. All that Sartre denies is that I am a café waiter as a thing  is
what it is, which is his technical sense. And it is only the  acknowledgment
that one is what one is in his technical sense that he considers a  form  of
bad faith, not the sincere acknowledgment, in the ordinary  meaning  of  the
word "sincere," that if I am a café waiter I am a café waiter.
       Needless  to  say,  what  Sartre  says  here  fulfills  exactly   the
conditions of what Santoni proposes as a remedy to Sartre's  supposed  flaw,
for it is nothing else than "a translation of the being what one is as  used
in the ordinary language that does not  condemn  sincerity  to  bad  faith."
Such being the case, it is obvious that Sartre did  not  have  to  wait  for
Santoni's  suggestion  for  reconstruction,  for  his  position  needed   no
reconstruction.
      Having shown that Santoni's rescuing operation of Sarrtre's  views  on
sincerity is uncalled for, I  could  finish  here.  However,  given  that  I
decided to avail myself of this opportunity to revisit Sartre's  chapter  on
bad faith, I want to highlight his rich  contribution  concerning  sincerity
as  a  particularly  subtle  form  of  bad  faith.  This  seems   especially
appropriate in the present context, given that, in his eagerness to  absolve
from bad faith those who are sincere in the  ordinary  sense  of  the  word,
Santoni unavoidably obscured the subtlety of the lie involved  in  the  form
of bad faith that Sartre calls sincerity.
The sincerity of bad faith
      Santoni  resents  Sartre's  use  of  the  "noble  word  sincerity"  in
connection with bad faith, for  in  that  use  he  sees  the  basis  of  the
supposed equivocation that led Sartre to believe  that  "sincerity"  in  the
ordinary sense of the word  is  a  form  of  bad  faith.  Now,  contrary  to
Santoni's contention, Sartre does not think that whoever is sincere  in  the
ordinary sense is  ipso  facto  sincere  in  his  technical  sense  and  is,
therefore, in bad faith. Sartre thinks that if I am a homosexual  and  I  am
sincere in the ordinary sense of the word  by  acknowledging  that  I  am  a
homosexual, I may or may not be in bad faith, depending on the way in  which
I acknowledge to  myself  that  I  am  a  homosexual.  This  is  why  Sartre
explicitly says that the homosexual harassed by the  champion  of  sincerity
is entitled to deny that he is a homosexual in the technical sense of  being
what one is, without thereby being insincere in the ordinary  sense  of  the
word:
      "He [the homosexual] would be right  actually  if  he  understood  the
      phrase 'I am not a paederast" in the sense of 'I am not  what  I  am.'
      That is, if he declared to himself, 'To the extent that a  pattern  of
      conduct is defined as the conduct of a paederast  and  to  the  extent
      that I have adopted this conduct, I am a paederast. But to the  extent
      that human reality can not be finally defined by patterns of  conduct,
      I am not one.'"[xx]
       Santoni, then, is clearly wrong in  charging  Sartre  with  the  view
that one cannot be sincere in the ordinary sense of the word  without  being
in bad faith. Yet it would be equally  wrong  to  attribute  to  Sartre  the
opposite view, to wit, that whoever is sincere in the ordinary sense of  the
word is ipso facto exempt from being in bad  faith.  That  is  the  position
which Santoni considers correct and would  have  liked  Sartre  to  espouse,
without realizing that the form of bad faith Sartre calls "sincerity"  would
be impossible if sincerity in the ordinary sense of the  word  were  totally
incompatible with sincerity in Sartre's technical sense.  Perhaps  the  fact
that Sartre does not consider the two kinds of sincerity  incompatible  with
each other--for a person can at the  same  time  be  in  bad  faith  and  be
sincere in the ordinary sense of the word--is what led Santoni  to  believe,
wrongly, that Sartre equivocated between the two meanings.  And  perhaps  it
is  Santoni's  own  conviction  that  the  two  kinds   of   sincerity   are
incompatible with each  other  that  prevents  him  from  understanding  why
Sartre had borrowed from ordinary language "the  noble  word  sincerity"  in
connection with bad faith.
      In order to understand why Sartre thinks that  being  sincere  in  the
ordinary sense of the word is not totally incompatible  with  being  in  bad
faith we have, in the first place, to grasp  the  difference  between  plain
bad faith and the  form  of  bad  faith  constituted  by  sincerity.  Sartre
distinguishes implicitly the form of bad faith  he  calls  "sincerity"  from
the form he calls simply "bad faith." Indeed, he accuses "the  coquette"  of
bad faith, but he does not accuse her of "sincerity."  The  reason  is  that
the two forms of bad faith are not identical. The difference is that  simple
bad faith involves a DENIAL of what one is,  whereas  sincerity  involves  a
POSITIVE ACKNOWLEDGMENT of what one  is,  which  constitutes  precisely  the
connection with the ordinary sense  of  the  word  "sincerity"  as  used  in
ordinary language. Thus, the "coquette" lyingly DENIES to herself  that  she
is actually flirting,  that  she  leaves  her  hand  in  the  hands  of  her
companion, so as not to have  to  assume  responsibility  for  her  conduct.
Similarly, the frigid  woman  lyingly  DENIES  to  herself  that  she  makes
herself adopt a frigid conduct. These are  examples  of  what  Sartre  calls
simply "bad faith." In the case of "sincerity," by  contrast,  there  is  no
denial involved, but a lying AFFIRMATION  or  POSITIVE  ACKNOWLEDGMENT.  The
person who is "sincere" in Sartre's technical sense positively  acknowledges
or affirms that he or she is what he or she is; the lie resides in the  fact
that this person means that he or she is what he or she is in the  same  way
in which things are what they are, that is, without choosing to be that  and
without being responsible for it. Now, as we shall see in a moment,  because
sincerity involves a positive acknowledgment of what one is, it  is  a  more
subtle variety of bad faith, that is, a  less  easily  detectable  lie  than
simple bad faith.
      Let us return  to  the  homosexual  and  the  champion  of  sincerity.
Sartre's technical notion of sincerity may seem highly esoteric, for  it  is
not very clear to the untrained eye what is involved in the kind  of  avowal
that the champion of sincerity is attempting to extract from his  homosexual
friend. After all, who wants to extract from anybody the confession that  he
is a homosexual as a table is a table, or as a glass is a  glass?  Yet,  the
practical meaning of what Sartre calls "sincerity" will begin dawning on  us
if we relate that term as well as the sort  of  "sincere"  avowal  that  the
champion of sincerity wants to extract  from  the  homosexual  to  something
with which we are quite familiar from everyday's experience.
      Why do we people dislike intensely, and resist, "being labelled"?  The
reason, Sartre would say, is that we  are  people,  not  things,  and  being
labelled amounts to being equated with things. Let us see how.  A  butterfly
pinned up to the board of a collector with  its  label  apposed  beneath  is
just what the label says it is, a "Monarch Butterfly." However, a student  I
have  labelled  as  "Lazy  Student"  resists  her  being  so  labelled,  not
necessarily because she thinks the label false and wants to  deny  that  she
is lazy if in  fact  she  is  lazy,  but  because  she  wants  to  deny  the
implication of the label, namely, that she is what she  is  the  way  things
are what they are, that her laziness is like  the  monarch-butterflyness  of
the butterfly--something which in no way depends on her and to which she  is
predestined. It may be comfortable for me to label this student, for then  I
will not have to read with an open mind her future papers, or even  to  read
them at all, since those will be by definition the lazy  papers  of  a  lazy
student. And, if I play the pitiful role of champion of sincerity,  I  might
even attempt to mislead this student by trying to  convince  her,  and  have
her confess to me, that she is lazy as a  monarch-butterfly  is  a  monarch-
butterfly, that is, as something to  which  she  was  predestined  and  from
which she cannot escape. Such an avowal would  be  immensely  gratifying  to
me, poor champion of sincerity, for it would prove me right,  and  it  would
save me the effort of having to deal with this student as a human  being  in
charge of her open future. But if the student assented to it, it would be  a
lie on her part, for she actually is in charge of her future and her  future
is indeed open.
      We now are in a position to understand more clearly what Sartre  means
by sincerity, in his own technical sense. It  is  nothing  else  than  self-
labelling on the part of a human being.
      An example. Imagine that, in  the  early  stages  of  a  term,  having
received a disastrous paper from a student who is obviously  intelligent,  I
tell him: "You are lazy, my friend. You could do  so  much  better!"  To  my
pleasant surprise, the student does not evade the truth;  on  the  contrary,
he openly  concurs  with  me:  "You  are  right,  Monasterio;  I  am  lazy."
Naturally and, as it turns out, naively, I expect that his future work  will
show much improvement. The sad fact, though, is that the  student  does  not
even bother to write one of the assigned  papers,  and  his  two  tests  are
miserable. By chance, at the end  of  the  term,  after  having  given  this
student his richly deserved "F," I happen to meet  him  again  and  to  talk
with him about his work. In reply, he  simply  says:  "Well,  what  did  you
expect? Didn't I tell you myself that I am lazy?"
      Now, this student is sincere in the  ordinary-language  sense  of  the
word, for he acknowledges that he is  lazy,  which  is  actually  the  case.
However, at the same time, he also is sincere in  Sartre's  technical  sense
and, consequently, he is in bad faith. Indeed, he is using  his  being  lazy
as  an  excuse  to  absolve  himself  (and  be  absolved  by  me)   of   all
responsibility for what he is. He  means  that  he  is  lazy  as  a  monarch
butterfly is a monarch butterfly. And since his laziness is  a  destiny,  he
cannot be  blamed  for  it  and  does  not  even  have  to  contemplate  the
uncomfortable possibility of changing  himself  or  assuming  responsibility
for choosing to go on being lazy. After all,  his  laziness  is  not  really
his.
      Because this student who is sincere in Sartre's technical  sense--and,
therefore, in bad faith--is nonetheless sincere in  the  ordinary  sense  of
the word, too, he gives the impression of  being  honest;  consequently,  he
can cheat himself (and others) all the more easily. Now the  coincidence  in
the case of this student of sincerity in the technical sense with  sincerity
in the ordinary  sense  of  the  word,  far  from  being  an  exception,  is
characteristic of the variety of bad faith that Sartre calls sincerity.  For
instance,  if  I  am  a  homosexual  and  I  lyingly  tell  myself  that  my
homosexuality  is  for  me  "a  destiny,"  not  something  for  which  I  am
responsible, then I am I sincere in Sartre's technical  sense.  But  at  the
same time I am sincere in the ordinary  sense  of  the  word,  for  I  am  a
homosexual  and  I  acknowledge  it.  This,  precisely,  is  what  makes  of
sincerity a more subtle variety of bad faith than simple bad faith.
      All this, unfortunately, gets totally obscured when, as Santoni  does,
the person who is sincere in the ordinary sense of the word  is  ipso  facto
declared  to  be  exempt  of  bad  faith.  In  that  way,  of  course,   all
equivocation  between  the  two  senses  of  the  word  "sincerity"  becomes
impossible, but the variety of bad  faith  that  Sartre  distinguishes  from
plain bad faith becomes impossible, too.
