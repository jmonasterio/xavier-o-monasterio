                                  DEPTH
      "More directly than the other dimensions of space, depth forces us  to
      reject the  preconceived  notion  of  the  world  and  rediscover  the
      primordial experience from which it springs; it is, so to  speak,  the
      most 'existential' of all  dimensions  because  (and  here  Berkeley's
      argument is right) it is not impressed  upon  the  object  itself,  it
      quite clearly belongs to the perspective  and  not  to  things....  It
      announces a certain indissoluble link between  things  and  myself  by
      which I am placed in front of them,  whereas  breadht  can,  at  first
      sight, be taken as a relationship between things themselves, in  which
      the perceiving subject is not implied. By rediscovering the vision  of
      depth, that is to say, of a depth which is  not  yet  objectified  and
      made of mutually external  points,  we  shall  once  more  outrun  the
      traditional alternative and elucidate the relation between subject and
      object." (Maurice Merleau-Ponty, The Phenomenology of  Perception,  p.
      256).
      As far as the history of philosophy is concerned,  the  whole  project
of the Phenomenology of Perception,  to  speak  only  of  one  of  Merleau's
works, is explicitly conceived as an alternative to the  interpretations  of
perceptual experience constituted  by  "Intellectualism"  and  "Empiricism."
Though the varieties of these two historical positions  are  multiple,  they
can be traced, respectively, to Descartes with his  thinking  mind,  and  to
the  British  Empiricists--particularly  Locke   and   Berkeley--and   their
voyeuristic consciousness. It is Merleau's contention that neither  position
does justice to our experience as perceivers in the  natural  attitude,  and
that a more  refined phenomenological description of  such  experience  will
reveal a totally different relationship betwkeen  subject  and  object  from
those presupposed by Intellelctualism and Empiricism.
      Since in The Phenomomenology Merleau moves throguh stages  that  cover
the same field, namely, our perceptual experience, through diferent  angles,
any of its chapters would ground equally my contention. I  have,  therefore,
chosen almost arbitrarily the chapter on space for  what  I  intend  to  do,
which is  twofold.  OIn  the  one  hand,  I  want  to  stress  that,  though
undoubtedly  offering  a  notion  of   the   relation   subject-world   that
constitutes an alternative  to  Intellectualism  and  Empiricism,  Merleau's
analysis remains nonetheless at least partially  influenced  by  the  visual
notion  of  experience   that   characterizes   both   Intellectualism   and
Empiricism, and that his analysis would have gained if he had  escaped  more
completely the visual paradigm of his predecessors. On  the  other  hand,  I
want to show, on the basis of the very conclusion reached by Merleau in  the
chapter on Space his own  awareness  of  the  unsurpassable  limitations  of
philosophy as an epistemological project.
