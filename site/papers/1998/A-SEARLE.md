For chapter 1 on Searle

(I) "The reason for concentrating on the study of speech acts is simply this: all linguistic communication involves linguistic acts. The unit of linguistic communication is not, as has generally been supposed, the symbol, word or sentence, or even the token of the symbol, word or sentence, but rather the production or issuance of the symbol or word or sentence in the performance of the speech act. To take the token as a message is to take it as a produced or issued token... More precisely, the production or issuancce of a sentence token under certain conditions is a speech act, and speech acts... are the basic or minimal units of linguistic communication. A way to come to see this point is to ask oneself, what is the difference between regarding an object as an instance of linguistic communication and not so regarding it? One crucial difference is this. When I take a noise or a mark on a piece of paper to be an instance of linguistic communication, as a message, one of the things I must assume is that the noise or mark was produced by a being more or less like myself and produced with certain kinds of intentions. If I regard the noise or mark as a natural phenomenon like the wind in the trees or a stain on the paper, I exclude it from the class of linguistic communication, even though the noise or mark may be indistinguihable from spoken or written words. Furthermore, not only must I assume the noise or mark to have been produced as a result of intentional behavior , but I must also assume that the intentions are of a very special kind peculiar to speech acts . F.e., it would be possible to communicate by arrangeing items of furniture in certain ways. The attitutde one would have to such an arrangement..., if one 'understood' it, would be quite different from the attitude I have, say, to the arrangement of furniture in this rooom, even though in both cases I might regard the arrangement as resulting from intentional behavior. Only certain kinds of intentions are adquate for the behavior I am calling speech acts. These kinds of intentions will be explored in chapter 2 John Searle, Speech Acts , Cambridge U Press, 1970, pp. 16-17).

(II) "When I say 'Hello' and mean it, I do not necessarily intend to produce or elicit any state or action in my hearer other than the knowledge that he is being greeted. But that knowledege is simply his understanding what is said , not an additional response or effect. Furthermore, there is no perlocutionary effect of, f. e.., promising which will distinguish promises from firm statements of intention and emphatic predictions. All three intend to create expectations in the heraer about the future, but 'I promise' does not mean 'I predict' or 'I intend.' Any account of meaning must show that when I say 'I promise' or 'Hello' and mean it, I mean it in exactly the same sense of mean as when I say 'Get out' and mean it . Yet Grice's account seems to suit only the last of these three sentences , since it is the only one whose meaning is such that in the ordinary cases the speaker who utters and means it intends to produce an 'effect' on the hearer of the kind Grice discusses" ( Speech acts , p. 46).

(III) "... there is a close connection between saying and the constative class of illocutionary acts. Saying fits statements but not greetings . Indeed, Austin's original insight into performatives was that some utterances were not sayings, but doings of some other kind . But this point can be exaggerated. A man who says 'I hereby promise' not only promises but says he does . That is, there is indeed a connection between sayings and constatives, but it is not as close as one might be inclined to think" ( Ibid. , p. 68).

Searle on speech-acts in general and statements in particular

S separates the illocutionary from the perlocutionary and makes the latter accidental. As a native speaker of English, he tells me (also a native speaker of English) that when I communicate linguistically my intention is always that what I want to communicated be understood or known . This applies to all cases of communication. In addition, sometimes I may want to produce other effects aside from having what I say understood. For instance, when I give an order I intend two things : as in any other case of communication, I want my giving the order I give to be understood, but I also want my order to be obeyed, executed. Now, though the latter is a locutionary or genuinely lingusitic effect--a per- locutionary one--for it is parasitic on understanding what is said, neither the occurrence of a perlocutionary intention nor the susccessful realization of the perlocutionary effect intended are essential, for not all cases of communication involve a perlocutionary intention aside from understanding, and the communication itself is successful if unterstading obtains, independently of whether the realization of the perlocutionary effect intended by the speaker obtains. By contrast, the intention of the illocutionary effect is common to all cases of complete linguistic communication (represented by speech acts). *

* There is an ambiguity here concerning "understanding." "I want you to study the diagnosis of mononucleosis." I want you counts as a request, that is, constitutes you the linguistic addresse as requested. But not automatically. By saying that I constitute myself as requester in regard to you, and intend to constitute you as requested. But, in linguistic exchange, this requires your collaboration. Now, I don't intend to inform you that I request you, but to have you requested. But, according to Searle, that is always the case: communicating is communicating something to be understood, by which understanding the addresse is informed of what the speaker wanted to communicate. This I call speculative understanding, speculative knowledge . (This may be a case of confusion between contemplative understanding and applicative understanding. Searle separates the two and makes the latter parasitic on the former).

Now, pace S , as a linguistic agent, as a native speaker of English, I know that when I tell you 'I want you to study...' I have no intention to inform you, but to get you to study ... And if you get just "informed," as when you read something in the paper, you misunderstood me in the sense that my communicative intention failed. Informing and requesting are different speech acts, for they express diferent intentions and intend linguistically different effects. [S's mistake is equivalent to the mistake of those who, taking the Bible for divine revelation, read the Bible as implicitly prefaced by " God says that ," as if God informed us of something to be merely understood, or at best believed or asented to because He says it, rather than as the continuation of an exposed relation with Him.] If S were right on this point, the illocutionary force, when the principle of expressibility is applied to this case, would be "I inform you that I request that you study..." This doesn't make sense.  "Kennst du..': the speaker is certainly not atempting to inform the Germans that he is a German soldier, as S assumes, but attempting to have them respond to him as a German, which is a perlocutionary effect.

Supose that this guy had known German and told the enemy "I am a German soldier." According to Searle, whether or not he would have been believed, the communication would have been successful if the Germans understood what he said and that he said it .

Suppose now that that the Germans understand what the guy says and say that they believe what the guy stated, yet treat the guy as a foreign, enemy soldier, and imprison him. This is not possible. Why not? Because it is a matter of practical, applicative understanding in a situation of encounter. Here the statement's referent is not  purely conceptual to be understood, as in a geometrical theorem. Nor is it, as in the case of the hanging bag of potatoes about to break the branch, to be understood purely objectively by a pure observer without existential relationhip to it. [This is what Aquinas in his commentary about Peri hermeneias (cf. wp\chant\excor-3 ) says that "animus hominis per rationem inducitur ad consentiendum vero ex his quae sunt propria rei," independently of any relationship of exposure of an affective, desirous subject to the thing. (Notice the difference between observing the behavior of lions in the zoo behind a protective barrier, and encountering lions in Africa). Here, by contrast, the addressee is a participant, separated from the speaker to whom he is referred by existential distances of exposure. (In Aquinas's words, "Sed rhetor et poeta inducunt ad assentiendum ei quod intendunt, non solum per ea quae sunt propria rei, sed etiam per dispositionem audientis. Unde rhetores et poetae plerumque movere auditores nituntur provocando eos ad aliquas passiones").

PRACTICAL UNDERSTANDING is existential understanding by an exposed, affectible agent-patient, separated from the speaker by reversible distances of mutual exposure. Hence the dispositio audientis to the speaker and to what he is referred to by the speaker. In this case the Germans are referred applicatively or practically to the speaker as a German soldier. And, if the reference suceeds, if they accept or allow their being so referred to the speaker as German soldiers to a German, they have to treat the guy as a German, for that precisely is what "believing" means in this case, much as believing in the Biblical God is accepting that one is related to Him with distances of exposure, and therefore falling on one's knees before Him, among many other concrete ways of being dispositionally or actually related to Him. Sartre and Buber have spoken of this in less clear ways.

To return to S. His supposed understanding of language is understanding of speech acts, of words, as they may sound in aclasroom situation to someone whi is learning the language, not in the real situation of linguistic commerce.    

S misunderstands the perlocutionary because he separates it from the illocutionary. What he needs is a sort of combination of both, which is practical understanding, a practical-attitude-project-action informed by the speaker's intention "freely" accepted and transcended by the addressee, who is a genuine speaker-hearer himself. Suppose that as a result of his intention of being treated as a German the soldier had been so treated. He certainly, though not Searle, would have counted that as a success, for that--not informing--was his intetnion . The point concerning which Searle is right--and I will deal with this bkelow--is that the communication that obtains in this case cannot be considered paradigmatic, for it is parasitic on the use of the German language.

My comments:

1 - As a native speaker of English myself, I maintain that when I give an order my intention is to be obeyed, not to be understood (or my order to be known) in Searle's sense. The understanding of which S speaks is the observer's of language understanding, not the understadning of the participant in language.  Language doesn't function as an object to be observed. Language is a very special kind of tool (not a tool that one uses but that one is), in that it means practically and is to be understood practically . We learn first to speak and then to read. This order cannot be inverted. Yet the uderstanding of which S speaks is the understanding typical of reading, to which S wrongly reduces linguistic understanding.

2 - "Saying fits statements but not greetings." If by saying we understand uttering a propositional content which can be understood in S's sense, then S is right. "Hello" is not something to be understood as "I am a German soldier is" [p. 44] (which involves predication and reference), for it does not involve predication and reference. But isn't "Hello" a linguistic tool, an expression of free recognition of on the speaker's part of the addressee as his or her fellow-human being which produces its intended effect when it is freely accepted as such by the addresse in the very act of reciprocating the salutation? Only a linguistic agent capable of saluting is capable of being saluted.

S suggests that animals perform certain simple locutionary, that is, communicative, acts:

"One can in certain special circumstances 'request' someone to leave the room without employing any conventions, but unless one has a language one cannot request of someone that he... undertake a research project on the problem of diagnosing... mononucleosis... Furtermore, I wish to argue, some system of rule governed elements is necessary for there to be certain types of speech acts, such as promising or asserting. My dog can perform certain simple illocutionary acts. He can express pleasure and he can ask (request) that he be left out. But his range is very limited, and even for the types he can perform, one feels it is partly metaphorical to describe them as illocutionaary acts at all" (pp. 38-39).

The idea that this is "metaphorical," that this kind of communication is really not linguistic arises from  S's realization that animals don't "say" what they intend to communicate. In Langer's idolect, they just "signal."

Think of "Kennst du das Land wo die Zitronen blühen" (pp. 44). "But does it follow from this account  that when I say 'Kennst du...,' what I mean is 'I am a German soldier'? Not only does it not follow, but in this case I find myself disinclined to say that when I utter the German sentence what I mean is 'I am a German soldier...,' because what the words mean and what I remember that they mean is 'Knowest thou...' Of course, I want my captors to be deceived into thinking that what I mean is 'I am a German soldier," but part of what is involved in that is getting them to thinnk that that is what the words I utter mean in German... whgat we can mean is, at least sometimes, a function of what we are saying. Meaning is more than a matter of intention, it is also at least sometimes a matter of convention" (pp. 43-44).

There are two things involved here" "saying" and "illocutionary and perlocutionary".

First, concerning saying . Animals, Searly implies, communicate certain things but don't say. And because they don't say, their range of communication is very reduced and their meaning probably metaphorical only.
