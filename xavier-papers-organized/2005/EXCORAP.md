(2) pp. 17-23: Impartiality and the medieval conception of reason

- Both Excorde and opponents demand impartiality because both share the medieval view of reason that excludes desire. (Theoretical) reason is meant to be impartial.

- The medieval view:

(a) Faith and reason in the Summa.

(b) Knowledge and action are different though not unrelated.

(c) Knowledge is passive to reality

(d) Knowledge as resemblance (?). (ARIS, LIIKE pLATO, DENIES OR DISTRUSTS THE TRUTH OF STATEMENTS OF PARTICULAR FACTS BECAUSE THEIR TRUTH CEASES, WHICH PERHAPS SUGGESTS A CONFORMITY OF THE MIND TO THE REAL THAT COMES CLOSE TO IMAGE: SEE PP. 48-49).

(e) Knowledge excludes desire: impartiality

(3) pp. 24-29: A criticism of the medieval notion of reason

- Reasons for contesting this conception:

(a) Reason starts functioning as already culturally committed: the Greek and Jewish and Christian appearances are different.

(b) Reason is taken both as a faculty and as the product of its work IN WRITTEN CONTEMPLATED STATEMENTS: LOGIC.

(c) The presupposed relationship between knowledge and action is impossible if knowledge is what the conception says it is.

- Such knowledge finds its expression in the purely informative judgment (ENUNCIATIVA ORATIO): LONG QUOTATION.

- But precisely because knowledge is purely objective and informative of object as independent of any relationship to the knowing subject, it has no motivating power.

- What is required is knowledge of another kind, desirous knowledge, knowledge related to the good of the subject--which cannot be impartial. Thus if the knowledge pursued in departments of theology is practical, it cannot be impartial, it has to be guided by the good of the subject.

(4) pp. 30-37: Speculative and practical reason: "Nihil volitum quin praecognitum"

- The dictum can be understood in two ways: (1) Nothing can be pursued or averted unless first assented to. (2) Nothing can be pursued or averted without being known, because pursuing or averting are constitutive of knowing something practically.

- The first version is unacceptable because:

(a) The object merely assented to is not the object that moves; the latter has to be singular and with distances of exposure.

(b) How can a known that does not provoke desire be related to the volitum? Only extrinsically. But death is undesirable; it is reasonable to fear it, and someone who does not fear it does not know death anymore than someone who does not adore God knows God. (Cf. Nussbaum on Hecuba and literature).

- Why theoretical knowledge does not motivate?

(a) The object of episteme--abstract, universal--speaks to the intelligence alone, not to the imagination and the passions.

(b) The non-scientific, non-phronetic:

Approaches: curiosity and concern

Siuations: exposure and non-exposure

Kinds of object: desirable and non-, subject of possible concern and thing. MISTAKE: The Analytics and AQUINAS' long quotation level all knowledge down to curiosity, non-exposure, non-desirable and thing. Yet they make of such knowledge the "antecedent" of action, when such object cannot motivate action.

But cannot there be a connection in terms of "calculation" of action? After all, even scientific knowledge (Aids) is applicative.

Clarification: Desiring and averting are neither modes of knowing theoretically, nor blind feelings, nor a combination of theoretical knowledge + feeling. They are desirous-aversive- knowledge terminating not in assent but in action toward the future. Eucharist , Fire are only known when practically known at least dispositionally. Yet fire can be theoretically known. MISTAKE: But let us not get things backwards Platonically: this does not entail the primacy of theoretical knowledge, or that knowledge is a picture.

AQUINAS' MISTAKE : he maintains that theology is a theoretical and practical science. He does not realize that, if knowledge and reality are what they are supposed to be in the Analytics and the Summa , then no passage from knowledge to action, and then faith (like theology) is a purely speculative endeavor. But, as suggested by de Lubac, Aquinas' position has problems: dead faith, charity is extrinsic, the faith of the knowledgeable and the faith of the vulgar. For related reasons Aris introduced the practical Nous, the deliberative intellect of the exposed agent--the order of practical reason.

Conclusion: An equivocation. If the intellect is non-appetitive, it understands that something is good without desiring it. But how can it understand what it is not proportionate to?

(6) pp. 39-47: Knowledge as judgment: "It is true that your house is burning"

Jugments-that are reflective intellectual acts based on grasped conformity of the conceived to reality. The kind involved in professional philosophical papers. Constitutive of the judgement- statement is the assent to house burning. So house burning (or whatever the object) is object of assent expressed in predicative statement. And the statement refers you and others to your house burning as object of assent, not of reality motivating action in response. This other, motivating apprehension of reality is practical judgment, applicative judgment. Hence, THE MISTAKE is to take the practical judgment for a theoretical, merely predicative judgment, to attempt to cash the judged as a fragment of a picture to constitute a mosaic of the whole of reality, much as Aquinas thinks of faith and reason. Actually, we are not inserted in the world as invulnerable contemplators but as vulnerable agents. "Your house is burning" does not intend to convey to me a piece of information to be assented to, a visual phenomenon, but the terror of a great, impending, applicative, determinate loss to be prevented if possible. Gerundive, applicative knowledge because we are needful agents tied to the surrounding world. My house already burnt becomes the background from which certain projects are possible and others impossible.

But how does Aris move from practical to theoretical reason and to theoretical reason as paradigmatic? For us as for animals, objects for response. But we are lingusitic creatures . This opens possibilities for us not available to animals, but makes reflective epistemological mistakes possible. For we reflect detachedly, and not as concerned people but as curious contemplators who desire to know, when not immediately exposed.

THE MISTAKE consists in building a theory of knowledge on the basis of this reflection. From this results the medievals' "Reason" and Kant's pure reason. - Actually, the temporal reality of " Fire " to which we are temporally exposed is to be cashed in terms of affective response-action. POSSIBLE ORIGIN OF THE MISTAKE: But this linguistic cashing is the speaker's and hearer's deed who himself furnishes his situation, and it is linguistically intransferable : either you are a competent hearer (rather than reader) and speaker (rather than writer of statements), or you are not. Thus only the "content" of the judgment appears as a sort of objective picture of what is the case.

Illustrations : food is perceived as food only when edible and edendum by agents who need to eat. Similarly, understanding an order is intrinsically related to obeying or disobeying. So knowing something real is intrinsically related to responding to it appropriately. It is like a pair of glasses , not seen by the subject who wears them but only by others. Like the seeing eye and the seen eye . WHAT THE GREEKS DID was to develop  a theory of knowledge corresponding to an all embracing, objective reality where there is no place for the subjective body and eye. They developed it not as subject-agents who know applicatively, but as contemplated objects. This is like trying to understand a pair of glasses by seeing it rather than wearing it . WHAT DESCARTES DID , following the Greeks, was to place himself explicitly in the shoes of an invulnerable curious contemplator and to build a theory of knowledge on that basis. He cut the connection between knowing and acting, on the subject's side, and between appearing and acting-being acted upon, on the object's side.

(7) pp. 48-52: The incompatibility of Aris' notions of theoretical and practical reason

Aris realized that practical reason cannot be subsumed under speculative reason, though perhaps it can be subordinated to the latter as far as contemplative beatitude is concerned. Though he never completed his independence from Plato, he was on his way, as shown by the practical syllogism underpinned by desire and need. However, Aris did not undertake the debunking of speculative reason, or at least the redressal by showing the primacy of practical reason.

Aris' MISTAKE: he has two mutually exclusive conceptions of knowledge (ONE FROM THE POINT OF VIEW OF LOGIC AND THE STATED (WHICH LEADS TO SYSTEM: STATEMENTS WRITTEN AND READ BY SCIENTIFIC CONTEMPLATORS, RATHER THAN SPOKEN AND HEARD BY EXPOSED AGENTS), THE OTHER AS PRACTICAL JUDGMENT (WHICH IN ANIMALS IS PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE WITHOUT JUDGMENT).  To these correspond two different conceptions of the subject: the invulnerable contemplator and the vulnerable exposed agent (later explained as agent seen rather than as agent acting). He did not realize, as pointed out above, that practical judgment is not a matter of evidence and assent but of affection and action.

In practical judgments there is no evidence because the perceiver or hearer supplies with his situation in relation to the object what would count as evidence. However, history is possible, and stories and novels too. History, unlike stories, leaves traces, is related to the past of people (roots), and may have an explanatory value in terms of self-understanding by reference to the past and the present.

MISTAKE: The difference between theoretical and practical judgment is such that to call both knowledge creates an equivocation that hurt the medievals and has plagued us ever since. If AQUINAS had seen the incompatibility between the two, he would not have said that theology is theoretical and practical.

(8) pp. 53-68: Practical knowledge in Aris

In people it is rational, but not in animals.

Thanks to language , there is a bifurcation in human experience: from situational perception with distances of exposure to objectification-conceptualization-abstract object without distances.

Aris presents his views on practical knowledge under the form of THE PRACTICAL SYLLOGISM :

- Connected with desire and resulting in action rather than in assent. (Camus: "For a person who does not cheat...").

- In NE, connection with deliberation--a rational and purely human operation. In DMA, connection with desire action, common to people and animals. Animals practice the practical syllogism as a sort of non-linguistic enthymeme: no distinction between appearance and application, 'they follow the appearance of the moment" (Nussbaum).

- MISTAKE OR AT LEAST CONNECTION BETWEEN THEORETICAL AND PRACTICAL REASON . Animals cannot verbalize what they practically perceive as vitandum or adipiscendum (cf. Commentarium in Metaphysics ). Consequently they cannot objectify-conceptualize, they cannot put between themselves and the desired or feared object distances of mere contemplation, they live applicatively. People possess practical intelligence, practical reason because they are linguistic agents.

In DMA Aris does not distinguish between linguistic agents--people--and animals. Let us make clear the distinction. The verbalization "The piece of bread is on the table" leads to bread independent of its desirability, in itself. Aris realizes that the higher mammals do not act according to a simple S-R model; they learn by trial and error which actions are likely to result in satisfaction of their needs (sort of teleological action). - But they are incapable of theoretical and even practical judgments. What does this mean? If animals could judge practically, they could escape the dimension of practical exposure and be interested in what does not satisfy their needs, and they would be able to abstract and conceptualize instead of just imagining. Practical knowledge makes possible deliberation, decision and action--that is, action not necessarily in

accordance with spontaneous desire but with reason (the function of man) [or even with history: Xtianity]. - In animals the concrete desired goals are determined by instinct and determinant of action, though the means can be improved by experience in the higher animals. People can invent their goal: a hunger strike. This is a mihi adipiscendum state of the world anmd character (appointment of myself with myself in the future). hus two radically different kinds of desire: Human and animal desire.

In the NE Aris speaks of the practical syllogism as if it were an exclusively human conduct because there he is interested not in desire but in rational desire, not in action but in action regulated by a conception of happiness that is rationally desirable.

The linguistic ability of people makes deliberation possible. Talking about in absence. Furthermore, cultural induction of objects for desire, with verbalized justification of required response. But this culturally induced objects of desire conflict with spontaneous desire ( Civilization and its discontents : reality-principle). Thus the human agent is used to talk about objects and responses to objects objectively and by themselves, and to think of responses in terms of appropriateness.

The standard of appropriateness has to do with the technical adequacy of the action to realize the goal chosen (possibility of realization) and with the appropriateness of the goal chosen. In certain cases the means have to be invented--agriculture, cure of aids. Here is where objective thinking and 'theoretical reason' enter.

The desire for a cure of aids leads to look for the means, and looking for the means leads to the study of aids in itself. The latter study is, in a sense, theoretical, independent of desire, purely intellectual and passive to reality.

What is the status of theoretical knowledge when it is not applied? Is it a picture of aids and of the means to do away with it? That aids is a virus is not a picture of aids, except for the non-scientists who do not understand what virus means; for it means applicatively: virus of aids (different from virus of TB) to be suppressed. Theoretical knwoledge is a sort of ad hoc pair of glasses that makes possible appropriate response (not mere visualization).

Object for response and background. Hodological, dramatic space, practically perspectival perception by exposed agent with own projects. Hence, insofar as there is picture,  the 'picture' is essentially relative and dramatic. The point is not to interpret the world but to build the desired world on the basis of the presently practically, applicatively inhabited world.

(9) pp. 69-71: Faith as knowledge

Faith--oi pistoi--is the ability to respond to God and other people in the Christian way, given the circumstances. Like in The Castle .

In a way, faith is knowledge. But not speculative, abstract, purely informative, unrelated to subject, demanding no other response than assent. But practical, personal, affective, motivating, relating subject and object. Here the standard of comparative foundationality arises from a history in which the agent is involved, not from a system. THE MEDIEVALS' MISTAKE was to assimilate faith to theoretical knowledge, and to attempt to establish the truth of the statements of the faithful, which are practical statements to be heard applicatively, as if they were scientific statements of pure contmeplators.
