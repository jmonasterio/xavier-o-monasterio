                             ORAL EXAMS : MENO

 [Essential definitions, immortality of the  soul-Recollection,  distinction
between right opinion and knowledge}.

- Subject of the dialogue: "Cn virtue be taught? Or  is  it  the  result  of
practice? Or do men possess it by nature or in some other way?

- Socrates confesses that he does not even know what virtue is.
And he thinks nobody knows.

- You, Meno, what do you say that virtue is?

- First answer: The virtue of a man is managing the city  well;  the  virtue
of a woman is managing the household well; the virtue of a child...
      BUT as there is only one health for man, woman  and  child,  so  there
must be one virtue. Socrates is "seeking  one  description"  that  fits  the
case of all, (the genus that covers all the species). Like  what  is  common
to all particular colors, all particular shapes.  For  instance,  "shape  is
that which alone of existing things always follows  color."  Or  better,  in
order not to have to define color, "a shape is the limit  of  a  solid."  On
the basis of this one definition as an example,  give  me  a  definition  of
virtue.

- Second answer: "Virtue is, as the poet says,  to  find  joy  in  beautiful
things and have power"--that is, to desire beautiful  things  and  have  the
power to acquire them."
      BUT "beautiful" amounts to "good," And does anybody  ever  desire  bad
things? Does any one wish to be misreable and unhappy?

- Third answer: "Virtue is the power to secure good things."
      BUT what if one secures gold unjstly?

- Fourth answer: "Virtue is the power to secure  good  things  with  justice
(justly)."
      BUT justice is part of a virtue. Then "what is virtue if every  action
performed with part of a virtue is virtue?

- Socrates is like the torpedo fish. Why? Because  not  on,ly  is  he  in  a
state of perplexity but makes any9one who comes close and touches him  numb.
MENO AKCKNOWLEDGES THAT HE, WHO HAS MADE  SO  MANY  SPEECHES  ABOUT  VIRTUE,
DOES NOT KNOW WHAT VIRTUE IS.

      SOCRATES PROPOSES TO START ALL OVER AGAIN, GUIDING MENO

- BUT how can one look for something when one does not know at all  what  it
is?
      Answer: The soul is  immortal.  It  has  learned  everything.  It  can
recollect. For searching and learning  are   recollection.  So,  after  all,
socrates, who knows nothing,  knows  something,  namely,  thatthe  solul  is
immortal and has preexisted? No, he "trusts" that this is true, rather  than
the argument that we cannot search for what we do not know, for  that  would
make us idle, while this hypothesis  makes  us  nergetic  and  keen  on  the
search.
      The example of the slave who,  under  Socrates'  guidance,  recollects
that a square double the size of another square is  not  a  square  each  of
whose sides is double the length of the  sides  of  that  square.  "Somehow,
Socrates, I think that what you say is right. I think so  too,  Meno.  I  do
not insist that my argument is right in all  other  respects,  but  I  would
contend at all costs both in workd and deed as far as I could that  we  will
be better men if we believe that one must search for the thins one does  not
know, rather than if we believe that it is not possible to find out what  we
do not know and that we must not look for it." SO SOCRATES IS NOT SCEPTIC.

      PRESSURED BY MENO, SOCRATES ACCEPTS TO FIND OUT WHETHER VIRTUE CAN  BE
TAUGHT. HE WILL DO THIS BY MEANS OF A  HYPOTHESIS  AS  GEOMETRISTS  DO:  "If
virtue is knowledge  (or  recollectable)  it  can  be  taught;  if  not,  it
cannot."
- Is virtue a kind of knowledge?
      If all goods are reducible to knowledge, then if virtue is a  good  it
is knowledge.
      It seems that virtue is  knowledge.  For  virtue  is  beneficial.  But
neither the qualities of the soul (such as character)  nor  exter4nal  goods
(such as wealth opr beauty) are beneficial unless  accompanied  or  directed
by wisdom. And this wisdom is learned, for otherwise we  would  be  able  to
know which of the young are good by nature.
      BUT if virtue could be taught, there  would  be  teachers  of  virtue.
Now, inspite of all his efforts, Socrates has found none.
      Anytus criticizes the sophists--and especially  Protagoras--who  claim
to teach virtue to the young and in fact corrupt  them.  Socrates  does  not
contest this verdict. BUT THEN,  to whom in  Athens should Meno go to  learn
virtue?
      In fact, the examination of  particular  cases  of  good  people  like
Themistocles, Pericles, Thucydides who could not  teach  goodness  to  their
children and who found teachers of other things for them  but  not  teachers
of virtue shows that virtue cannot be taught.
      This does not mean that there are no  virtuous  people.  But  they  do
what is right by right opinion rather than by knowledge (knowledge why).  "I
too speak as one who does nopt have knowledge but is  guessing.  However,  I
CERTAINLY DO NOT THINK I AM GUESSING  THAT  RITHT  OPINION  IS  A  DIFFERENT
THING THAN KNOWLEDGE. IF I CLAIM TO KNOW ANYTHING  ELSE--AND  I  WOULD  MAKE
THAT CLAIM ABOUT FEW THINGS--I WOULD PUTTHIS DOWN AS ONE  OF  THE  THINGS  I
KNOW."
"As retgards  knowledge,  they  [virtuous  people]  are  no  different  from
soothsayers and prophets. They too say many true things when  inspired,  but
they have no knowledge of what they are saying."

- CONCLUSION OF THE WHOLE DIALOGUE: "BUT IF WE ARE  RIGHT...,  VIRTUE  WOULD
BE NEITHER AN INBORN QUALITY NOR TAUGHT, BUT COMES TO THOSE WHO  POSSESS  IT
AS A GIFT FROM THE GODS WHICH IS  NOT  ACCOMPANIED  BY  UNDERSTANDING...  WE
SHALL HAVE CLEAR KNOWLEDGE OF THIS WHEN, BEFORE WE INVESTIGATE HOW IT  COMES
TO BE PRESENT IN MEN, WE FIRST TRY TO FIND OUT WHAT  VIRTUE  IN  ITSELF  IS.
BUT NOW THE TIME HAS COME FOR ME TO GO."
