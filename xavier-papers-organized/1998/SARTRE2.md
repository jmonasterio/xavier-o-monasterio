Sartre on the existence of others

Katherine Morris's article in the latest issue of Sartre Studies International furnishes me a welcome opportunity to go back to Sartre's treatment of the Other's existence, which I have always found fascinating, but about whose actual import and philosophical value I have wavered through the years. Why not attempt to make up my mind once and for all?

Morris takes Gregory MaCulloch to task for having misunderstood "not just what Sartre is saying but what Sartre is doing " concerning the Other's existence in Being and Nothingness . ^(E1)  Though an analytic philosopher herself, Morris thinks that McCulloch "imposes our conceptions onto Sartre's dicussion and criticises him on that basis." ^(E2). In Morris's opinion,

Sartre does repay analytical treatment: that is to say, he himself is a philosopher, and a good one. But it does not follow from this that he is engaged in exactly the familiar sort of enterprises that we analytical philosophers are engaged in". ^(E3)  

Morris's view of what Sartre is saying and doing

Sartre is saying that the Other appears to me in two different ways: as an object and as a subject. As an object in my perceptual experience, my knowledge of the Other as another like me, that is, as an experiencing subject, is only probable, since in this mode I do not experience the Other as experiencing subject but as experienced object, though, admittedly, as a special kind of object. Therefore I can never be absolutely certain that this object that looks and acts like me who am a subject is in fact a subject, however probable that may be. But let this Other-object I have been observing look at me, let this Other catch me peering through a keyhole, and in my very shame of myself before this Other I experience myself as object experienced by the experiencing Other, that is, by the Other as subject.  Shame is, in other words, the experiential revelation of my objectivity for the Other as subject or, correlatively, the experieneial revelation of the Other's subjectivity for me. Thus, I know the Other as subject in my very experience of being known as an object by the Other.

As for the certainty of my knowledge of the Other as subject, Sartre maintains that it is absolute. The reason for this, as Morris explains, is that, unlike in the case of my knolwledge of the Other as object, where knowledge involves an extrapolation from what is intuited--namely the appearance and the behavior of this human-looking body--to what is not intuited--namely, the Other's subjectivity--, in the case of my knowledge of the Other as subject there is no extrapolation to be done; rather, the Other's subjectivity is intuitively or directly experienced in the very experience of my being object of experience for the Other, in my being ashamed of being object for the Other.

The intuitive immediacy to me of the Other as subject, argues Morris, is, in Sartre's view, of the same type as the intuitive immediacy of my own existence as subject to myself, expressed in the Cartesian cogito . Consequently, for the same reason, the existence of the Other as subject is as infallibly certain as my own existence as subject.  

Morris's criticism of McCulloch

In Morris's view, McCulloch has been betrayed by his analytic philsophical tradition and thus interprets Sartre s if Sartre were an analytic philosopher, which of course Sartre is not.  The Sartrean distinctions between the Other as subject and the Other as object as well as between knowledge and certainty are alien to the analytic tradition. As a result, McCulloch completely misses them. This leads him to a totally wrongheaded interpretation of what Sartre is saying and doing.  

McCulloch expects Sartre's 'proof' of the Other's existence, that is, of the existence of the Other as subject, to function as a proof of the existence of an object would. Indeed, when Sartre speaks of "knowledge," the analytic philosopher understands "justified true belief." Hence McCulloch thinks that the experience of shame is offered by Sartre as an empirical reason for believing that the Other exists.

To which Morris retorts:

... the fact that he [Sartre] feels shame in these circumstances does not give him a reason for believing that Others exist, it does not prove their existence, as if before he was uncertain of his own existence and only afterwards he was certain... Shame simply discloses a certainty that was already there. ^(E4)

When Sartre speaks of "certainty," Morris continues, the analytic philosopher understands "'very strong belief' or 'subjective conviction' (which can therefore be false)". ^(E5)

To which Morris retorts:

But the distinction Sartre draws between knowledge and certainty is not that between justified true belief and mere strong conviction, but between empirical knowledge and what Descartes called 'intuitive' 'knowledge.' The claim that I am certain of something (say my own existence) is thus not a 'weaker' claim  than that I know something; certainty of something is definitely not compatible with the thing's being false. ^(E6)

Since McCulloch misunderstands what Sartre is saying and ddoing, he unsurprisingly ends up accusing Sartre of some times simply assuming the Other's existence on the basis of a naïve, merely subjective feeling of certainty, and some times claiming to know with absolute certainty the Other's existence on the basis of an experience of shame that cannot, even in the best case, yield more than probability. Thus, in summary, while Morris concludes that Sartre furnishes us a "watertight" argument ^(E7), McCulloch finds Sartre's proof of the Other's existence "pretty hopeless." ^(E8)

Is Sartre's treatment of the Other's existence successful?

A very annoying feature of Morris's article is her pretended non-evaluative stance. Supposedly, she is just doing what linguistic analysts do best, namely, analyzing. In other words, she is just explaining what Sartre is actually saying and doing against the background of McCulloch's wrongheaded misunderstanding, so that a fairer evaluation of Sartre's achievement than McCulloch's own may be made on the basis of her explanatory analysis. However, Morris claims that she herself is not at all engaged in an evaluative undertaking:

I do not mean to suggest that there are no criticisms that could be made of Sartre's arguments or procedures, though I have not chosen to pursue them here. But any such criticisms must surely be based on a fair-minded understanding of what he is actually saying and doing. ^(E9)

Why should this non-evaluative stance be annoying? In the first place, because in fact Morris doesn't observe it. Indeed, she is not only explaining where and how McCulloch misunderstands Sartre; she is also making an evaluation--and clearly a positive one--of Sartre's position on the Other's existence.  After all, doesn't she categorically state that Sartre's argument based on the experience of shame, when correctly understood, is "watertight," that it yields absolute certainty--indeed, the very certainty of the Cartesian Cogito ?  The second reason why Morris's supposedly non-evaluative stand is annoying is that it gives her full immunity.  Indeed, since supposedly she is making no evaluation of Sartre's position but only defending Sartre against McCulloch's particular misinterpretation, she herself cannot be challenged on such an important issue as the validity of Sartre's results, though actually she claims that Sartre's argument yields absolutely certain knowledge of the Other's existence as subject.  

As we just heard, however, Morris doesn't discard the possibility of finding fault with Sartre's arguments or procedures, though she herself chose not to engage in that task in her article. We may then ask whether Sartre's position on the Other's existence is as watertight as Morris makes it sound.

Let us go directly to the critical point. Why precisely does McCulloch reject Sartre's contention that he, or anybody else for that matter, can know with certainty the Other's existence? Because Sartre claims, on the one hand, that the experience of shame infallibly reveals to the ashamed person the Other's existence as the subject before whom the person is ashamed, and, on the other hand, Sartre concedes the possibility that the ashamed person be mistaken in believing that he or she was Looked-at.  Indeed, as Sartre himself writes,

Nevertheless, someone will say, the fact remains that I can discover that I have been mistaken.  Here I am bent over the keyhole; suddenly I hear a footstep. I shudder as a wave of shame sweeps over me. Someone has seen me. I straighten up. My eyes run over the deserted corridor. It was a false alarm. I breathe a sigh of relief (BN 277). ^(E10)           

Now, McCulloch reads this passage as an admission on Sartre's part that the experience of shame is compatible with the non-existence of the Other, given that one can be ashamed when in fact there is no Other before whom to be ashamed.  However, Morris rightly argues that there is no such admission on Sartre's part, since Sartre presents the case of the mistaken person not as his own view but as the view of an objector, as shown by Sartre's own reply to the just mentioned objection:

What then is it which falsely appeared... when I discovered the false alarm? It is not the Other-as-subject... It is the Other's facticity , that is,

the contingent connection  between the Other and an-object-being in my world. Thus what is doubtful is not the Other himself. It is the Other's being there ; i.e., that concrete, historical event which we can express by the words "There is someone in the room (BN 277. ^(E11)

Thus Morris's explanation of Sartre's actual claim and, correlatively, of McCulloch's misunderstanding is quite accurate:

Although it is evidently tempting to suppose that the claim is that I can believe that I am being Looked-at when in fact I am not, this is not Sartre's claim. The claim he makes here is simply that I can ( in fact ) be Looked-at even where there is no concrete set of eyes to do the Looking. That he is being Looked-at in this example is never called into question. ^(E12)

There is no doubt, then, that McCulloch misunderstood Sartre on this particular point. And the point is far from minor. For if McCulloch were right and Sartre had indeed conceded that I can be ashamed when there is no Other before whom to be ashamed, then McCulloch would be right in claiming that the Sartrean absolute certainty of the Other's existence is a myth, and that Sartre's argument is therefore "pretty hopeless." But does it follow from this, as Morris seems to implyc, that Sartre's argument is "watertight"?

Morris's verdict on McCulloch's mistake, which confirms her view that McCulloch is blind to those Sartrean distinctions that are not current in the analytic tradition, is the following:

McCulloch has, in short, confused that Other as subject with the actual concrete presence of the Other, the presence of the Other as a possible object in my world." ^(E13)

Now, Morris is technically right: McCulloch thought that Sartre was trying to prove the actual concrete presence of the Other in our world, and that was not the case. And it was not the case, Morris suggests, because a subject who would be actually and concretely present in the world would be, in Sartre's view, not a subect but an object, in which case, of course, the knowledge of it could not be absolutely certain but only probable.

Now, Morris attributes McCulloch's expectation that Sartre

was going to prove the actual and concrete presence of the Other in the our world to the insensitivity of an analytic philosopher to Sartre's distinction between the Other as object and the Other as subject and the correlative distinction between the respective probability and certainty characteristic of each of those two kinds of knowledge. I propose, however, an alternative explanation, to wit: that the Sartrean distinction between the Other as object and the Other as subject, when actually applied concretely in the case of the Other's existence, results in a nonsensical existence of the Other. And, given that result, I for one would rather side with McCulloch's conclusion that Sartre's argument is hoipless than with Morris's view that it is watertight. How so?

, or rather to his sensitivity to the kind of Other whose existence can and should legitimately be expected from any so-called proof. If Morris is right in her interpretation of Sartre--and she is, for on this poiint she is only parakphraksing Sartre himself--what Sartre has proved is the existence of a subject by whom one can be Looked-at even when there is no concrete set of eyes to do the looking. But is this eyeless ghost by whom I am supposedly Looked-at from nowhere a truly viable alternative to the solipsism of the realists and the idealists criticized by Sartre at the beginning of the chapter on the Other? Can one blame McCulloch for not having taken seriouusly this phantasmagoric Other, or for not even having suspected that such could be the Other whose existence Sartre wanted to prove?  As to where exactly Morris stands on this issue we don't know, for though she doesn't mean that no criticisms could be made of Sartre's arguments or procedures he has chosen not to share them with us.  One is entitled, however, to wonder whether Morris herself, who shows that McCulloch is technically wrong about Sartre, is herself really willing to side with Sartre who coldly maintains that one can be ashamed before an Other by whom one is Looked-at even when there is no set of eyes to do the looking.  In other words, does she really believe in the existence of the ghostly Other-subject yielded by Sartre's argument? And does she really think that Sartre has made the question of the Other's existence "disappear"? ^(E14)

My point can be made from a slightly different angle.

Sartre cautions us against 'a confusion... between two distinct orders of knowledge and two types of being which cannot be compared' (BN 276); McCulloch, I submit, has not heeded this caution. ^(E15)

True, McCulloch has given in to the evident temptation Morris also feels, though without giving in to it. But why should the temptation to think that when Sartre speaks of the Other by whom I am Looked-at he is at the same time speaking the Other whose eyes I see? Because the the Sartrean distinction between two distinct orders of knowledge and two types of being which cannot be compared holds as little water, and for related reasons, as the Kantian distinction between the phenomenal and the noumenal subject and the consequent mutual independence of what the phenomenal subject undergoes in the order of causality and what the noumenal subject chooses to do in the Kingdom of ends. Even Kant himself, as is well known, couldn't be consistent with his distinction and came to dismiss it as a mere concession to the schools.  

Now, it is interesting to note that Morris herself acknowledges how "evidently tempting" it is to side with the objector, as McCulloch does, and conclude that Sartre's proof of the Other's existence fails miserably, rather than siding with Sartre . Morris gives no explanation for this evident temptation. She just assumes that McCulloch, because of his analytic blindness, is incapable of understanding Sartre concerning the difference between the Other as subject and the Other as object, as well as the difference between the infallible certainty of the intuitive knowledge of the existence of the Other as subject and the  mere probability of the empirical knowledge of the Other as object. Because the only alternative is the one proposed by Sartre. And this alternative is so nonsensical that one can excuse McCulloch for not having taken seriously the existence of the ghostly, eyeless Other-subject by whom one is supposedly Looked-at from nowhere.

"Sartre's notion of being-for-others is intricate and, I think, of great philosophical importance; that I have said nothing about it reflects, not lack of concern, but the fact that the 'logic' of the argument concerning the existence of others doesn't require a full exploration of the 'content' of the concept of being-for-others." (Note 29, p. 62).

When analytic philosophers speak of "knowledge" they mean "justified true belief."

On this conception, what counts as justification can be empirical, logical, conceptual, intuitive or anything you like. Sartre's conception of knowlege is much more specific and delimited... Only empirical justification yields knowledge in his sense. At the same time, McCulloch interprets Sartre's term 'certainty' as simply meaning.... ^(E16)   

Then, to compound his misunderstanding, McCulloch ends up saying that, after all, the failure of Sartre's proof is of no great consequence, for Sartre was not engaged in addressing the traditional sceptical arguments concerning the Other's existence but was doing something different. In McCulloch's own words, "Sartre is developing a non-Cartesian framework, and so it is far from clear that sceptical worries are inevitable or urgent..." ^(E17)      

  A curious aspect of the dispute between Morris and McCulloc h is that McCulloch, who thinks that Sartre's argument for the Other's existence fails and, therefore, that Sartre simply assumes the Other's existence, finds "Sartre's procedure... to be above reproach" ^(E18), whereas Morris thinks that if McCulloch's criticism of Sartre's were justified it would be serious to the point of being "damning:" "after all, if Sartre is simply assuming that we encounter Others, then he is simply assuming, that Others as subjects exist, and to call this an 'assumption' is surely incompatible with calling it certain in Sartre's sense of the term." ^(E19)  Obviously they see Sartre doing very different things in his treatment of the Other's existence.  Unlike Morris, McCulloch considers his own criticism of Sartre's argument justified; yet he doesn't think that it is fatal to Sartre's undertaking because McCulloch doesn't think that Sartre is trying to respond to the classical skeptical arguments against the knowledge of the existence of other minds but is, rather, "developing a non-Cartesian framework . ^(E20) For her part, Morris thinks that, if justified, McCulloch's argument would be fatal to Sartre because she obviously thinks that Sartre is actually responding to the classical skepticism about the existence of other minds.
