III

KUHN’S CONTRIBUTION

(Cf. my <2001-April-1-, revised on March 2002, particularly pages 7-9>, of which the present paper is a further development).

With Thomas S. Kuhn already dead and the intense attention paid to his work for so many years, it is time to try to define his contribution. As expressed by him in the very first page of his famous book, The Structure of Scientific Revolutions , his ambition was to substitute the defective “textbook image” of science, perpetuated by the textbooks from which the students of science learn their trade, with a new conception of science. Subjacent to this textbook image, as Kuhn points out, is the notion that science grows by cumulative accretion of facts and laws, much as a pyramid develops by accretion of single bricks and of successive superposed layers of bricks.  Each genuinely innovative scientist, in this metaphor, contributes a brick or two to the edifice, and each generation of innovative scientists contributes another layer of observed facts and laws and theories, and so on. From this process results the organic progress of science in the knowledge of the universe.

Kuhn’s idea of the development of science is radically different. According to him, “normal science” appears after a pre-paradigmatic period in which there is no fundamental agreement among the practitioners. Each scientist moves on his or her own, following his or her own best hunches. Such continues to be the case until a “paradigm” emerges that gains general acceptance. “Normal science” is paradigmatic.  More precisely, it is a problem-solving activity characterized by a shared view of the nature of the entities that constitute the particular field and, correspondingly, by a shared manner of solving the problems typical of the field. Some of the entities that constitute the particular field show irregularities irreconcilable with the paradigm. These irregularities threaten the shared view. However, rather than renouncing the shared view because of the irregularities, scientists live with those irregularities in the hope of being able to assimilate them to the shared view. This happens to be the case with some irregularities, which prove, in the long run, to have been  merely apparent, when a solution to accommodate them within the paradigm is found. However, some other of the  irregularities that threaten the paradigm because of their incompatibility with it  may in the long run provoke a crisis. The crisis may lead to the abandonment of the current paradigm, but this occurs only when an alternative view of the nature of the entities that constitute the field and, correspondingly, a new manner of solving the problems typical of the field emerges and rallies the practically universal allegiance of the practicing scientists. Such an event – that is, the shift of paradigms– constitutes what Kuhn calls a  “revolution.”

Herein, at least in part, lies the explosiveness of Kuhn’s position. According to the classical view of science, represented by the textbook image, science genuinely grows . The truths discovered  by each generation of innovative scientists are preserved, and the next generation of scientists adds to the previously known scientific truths constituted by fact and laws. Consequently, there is genuine scientific growth in scientific truth. In other words,  science is asymptotically moving toward total possession of the truth. Not so, in Kuhn’s opinion. Within a given paradigm, there is  growth, indeed, in that the problem-solving activity progresses further and further. ^(F1) With the shift  of paradigms, however, there is no preservation of the previous scientific knowledge and, consequently, no genuine addition to it. There simply is a revolution, a new scientific beginning – a new science, literally, defined by a new paradigm . Indeed, what was previously taken for genuine knowledge ceases to be accepted. That, in Kuhn’s opinion, is what happened when the Copernican paradigm was substituted by the Newtonian one, and again when the Newtonian paradigm was substituted by the Einsteinian. In other words, Copernican, Newtonian, and Einsteinian physics are three entirely different physics in which the putative ‘truths’ of the previous physics were replaced by new and different, but no less putative, ‘truths,’ since in the short or long run they happened to be superseded by other ‘truths.’ And, if past history is to be taken seriously, there is no reason to think that this process reached its end with Einsteinian physics; so Einsteinian physics is bound, in its turn. To be superseded by a different physics in the future. Hence the optimistic idea that science is asymptotically moving toward the total truth is an illusion. In Kuhn’s own softer words to the same effect,

“We may... have to relinquish the notion, implicit or explicit, that changes of paradigm carry scientists and those who learn from them closer and closer to truth.” ^(F2)

Is this the new conception of science that Kuhn intended as his lasting contribution? If so, his contribution seems to have amounted to a debunking of science rather than to a new conception of science. No wonder, then, that very few practicing scientists, whose very enterprise had been debunked by Kuhn, were to be counted among his followers. His admirers, indeed, were. for the most part, philosophers, sociologists, historians of science, and students of literature, who either found the notion of the paradigmatic character of science applicable within their own disciplines, or sensed that the Kuhnian  demotion os science from is long held pedestal amounted to an implicit promotion of their own disciplines to the level of science.

Now, this situation was not modified by Kuhn’s death. Who today, among the scientists, thinks that Kuhn’s conception of science is of lasting value? But what if the just sketched characterization of the scientific activity is wrong? What if Kuhn conceived science in a different and truly promising way? Such is my thesis in the present paper.

What did Kuhn himself expect his most important contribution to be? His answer comes in the very first paragraph of the first chapter of The Structure of Scientific Revolutions. Kuhn attacks “the image of science by which we are now possessed,” whose perpetuation he attributes to a large extent to the textbooks through which the students of science learn their trade, and at the same time specifies his own purpose of substituting the “textbook image” with a anew image or conception of science.:

“that [textbook] image has previously been drawn... mainly from the study of finished scientific achievements as these are recorded in the classics and, more recently, in the textbooks from which each new scientific generation learns to practice its trade. Inevitably, however, the aim of such books is persuasive and pedagogic; a concept of science drawn from them is no more likely to fit the enterprise that produced them than an image of a national culture drawn from a tourist brochure or a language text. This essay attempts to show that we have been misled by them in fundamental ways. Its aim is a sketch of the quite different concept of science that can emerge from the historical record of the research activity itself” ^(F3)  

The view of science presented by the textbooks that teach beginners their scientific trade  focuses on the chain of the most salient achievements of the scientific activity in terms of observations, scientific instruments, crucial experiments, laws and theories, together with the names of those scientists to whom each of the main contributions is to be attributed. Often the content of the first chapter is a statement of the scientific method, whose top exemplars are the natural sciences. In the successive chapters, the scientific method is illustrated by the experimental techniques used in gathering the data pertinent to the particular subject-mater of the textbook, together with the logical operations involved in relating those data to the theoretical generalizations found in the textbook. In addition, across the textbook, a series of concrete problems is furnished, whose solution by the student will serve as experimental confirmation of the validity of the laws involved, and will contribute to increase the student’s facility in applying the laws.

Of course, nothing more is needed to understand why Kuhn has been accused of relativism and even of skepticism the furious, or the furious attacks of which his view of science has been the object by all those who think that the very function  the experiment fulfills in the experimental sciences is to demonstrate the validity of the scientific laws and theories, or at the minimum to falsify those that are wrong . Whether or not such accusations and attacks are justified is a question that I will leave for later. As for Kuhn’s views themselves on the paradigmatic character of science, on the paradigm-dependency of the scientific facts and theories, and on the paradigmatic revolutions, they are so widely known and have been explored and discussed to such an extent and for so long, that any detailed presentation of them would be superfluous. At any rate, whatever may need to be said concerning them, I will explain it as the opportunity requires.  

Science as a problem-solving activity

Kuhn himself has told us that he wanted to propose an alternative conception of science to the one  perpetuated by the textbooks.  I have already indicated that the Kuhnian image involves the paradigmatic character of normal science, by contrast to pre-paradigmatic or primitive science, as well as the periodic shifts of paradigms, with the threatening implication concerning the development of science through revolutionary changes. Nothing like this is involved in the textbook conception of science. And as the notion of science sketched by Kuhn amounts for all practical purposes too the debunking of science, the temptation to reject the Kuhnian view is irresistible. However, these characteristics of the Kuhnian view of science are only consequences that flow from what I take it to be his most important contribution, to wit: the shift from the traditional image of science as a knowledge-acquiring activity that grows progressively to a problem-solving activity , together with the abandonment this entails of the traditional conception of knowledge and truth presupposed by the commonly accepted textbook image. According to the textbook image, what science is supposed to yield is knowledge of the world, a knowledge that increases and grows, as new genuine “laws of nature” proved by experiment are discovered.

Where, then, precisely, is scientific knowledge localized, according to the textbook view? In what the textbooks transmit to the students, to wit: the formulation of the laws discovered by the genuinely innovated scientists, together with the experiments that proved their scientific validity. In this view, then, scientific knowledge is localized in the mathematical formulation of the laws, and in the repeatable experiments through which the formulated laws gained their scientific status. This is what the student of science is supposed to learn and understand as scientific knowledge In addition, it is true, the textbooks furnish a number of concrete related problems through with the student is  supposed to gain facility in applying the knowledge carried by the laws. It is here that Kuhn takes his distances from the textbook conception of science, which localizes scientific knowledge in the formulated laws, while relegating the problem-solving activity to the merely accidental function of contributing to gaining facility in the application of the laws. Kuhn protests because he localizes scientific knowledge, not in the understanding of the formulated laws proven by experiment, but in the very application of the laws through solving concrete problems:

“Philosophers of science have not ordinarily discussed the problems encountered by a student in laboratories or in science texts, for these are thought to supply only practice in the application of what t he student already knows.  He cannot, it is said, solve problems unless he has FIRST learned the theory and some rules for applying it. Scientific knowledge is embedded in theory and rules; problems are supplied to gain facility in their application. I have tried to argue, however, that this localization of the cognitive content of science is wrong. After the student has solved many problems, he m ay gain only added facility by solving more. But at the start and for some time after, doing problems is learning consequential things about nature. In the absence of such exemplars the laws and theories he has previously learned would have little empirical content. ^(F4)

Why does the textbook view localize knowledge in the formulated law? Because the law  is supposed to represent what is the case in reality and correspond to it.. But Kuhn disagrees with that conception of knowledge:

“ A scientific theory is usually felt better than its predecessors... because it is somehow representation of what nature is really like . One often hears that successive theories grow closer to, or approximate more closely to the truth. Apparently generalizations like that refer not to the puzzle-solutions and concrete predictions derived from a theory but rather to its ontology, to the match, that is, between the entities with which the theory populates nature and what is ’really’ there. Perhaps there is some other way of saving the notion of ‘truth’ for application to whole theories, but this one will not do. There is, I think, no theory-independent way to reconstruct phrases like ‘really there’; the notion of a match between the ontology of a theory and its ‘real’ counterpart in nature now seems to me illusive in principle. Besides, as a historian, I am impressed by the implausibility of the view. I do not doubt, for example, that Newton’s mechanics improves on Aristotle’s and that Einstein’s improves on Newton’s as instruments for puzzle-solving. But I can see in their succession no coherent direction of ontological development. On the contrary, in some important respects, though by no means in all, Einstein’s general theory of  relativity is closer to Aristotle’s than to Newton’s. Though the temptation to describe that position as relativistic is understandable, the description seems to me wrong.” ^(F5)

Thus Kuhn’s rejection of the localization of knowledge by the textbook view of science is due to his rejection of the very conception of knowledge on which the textbook view rests, to wit: that knowledge is genuine knowledge only if and because it represents and corresponds to what is the case in reality.

Kuhn denies that the scientific activity yields knowledge in the sense of accurate representation of the world; as implicitly assumed by the textbook view; what the scientific activity yields or terminates in is the way to solve concrete problems when actually applied.

In rejecting the notion of knowledge as correspondence with the real Kuhn is fighting  a long and deeply ingrained epistemic tradition in which we all have been steeped. Indeed, the commonsensical notion, based on a superficial reflection on visual perception, that knowledge consists in the match or exact correspondence between what the knower sees or  knows and what is the case in reality, received its philosophical consecration from Plato. But how can one be sure that what one sees actually corresponds to what is the case? After all, everyone has had the experience of having sometimes been wrong concerning perceptual facts after being convinced of being right. In other words, the notion of knowledge as correspondence to what is the case, coupled with the obvious fact that putative knowledge proves some times to be false, that is, not to be genuine knowledge, created the problem of discerning between merely apparent and genuine knowledge. To distinguish the two, at least in name, Plato called the former “opinion” and the latter “knowledge,” and he tackled the problem of discerning the two in the following way. Only those realities can be object of “knowledge” which are absolutely unchangeable or permanent because they exist necessarily and are necessarily as they are. For if the knower grasps such necessity, then the knower grasps that what he or she knows necessarily corresponds to what is the case. Now, no sensible or perceivable object is of that kind, for all of them are contingent rather than necessary, that is, they are subject to change in the flux of time. Thus my opinions about them, even assuming that my perceptions actually corresponded at a certain instant to what is the case, ceases to be true as they change. Consequently Plato felt constrained to relegate all the particular objects of sensible perception to the realm of opinion or merely putative knowledge. Furthermore,  he determined that only the intellect (as opposed to the senses), which was capable of grasping what is necessarily the case, could yield knowledge (as opposed to opinion), and only in the case of the intelligible (as opposed to sensible) realities, for by the very fact of being purely intelligible rather than material, they were not subject to temporal change. The notion of “scientific knowledge,” that is, of a knowledge whose unshakeable, objective certainty is superior to the merely subjective feeling of what is ordinarily taken for knowledge, has its origin in Plato.

As to the avatars of the notion of knowledge as correspondence with what is the case in reality, and the intense scrutiny and criticism to which it has been subjected for centuries, the story has been told a hundred times. For my purpose, it may suffice to say that Kuhn is not alone, by far, in finding such notion defective. It is fair to say that Kant, who gave up on the notion of knowledge as correspondence with the real on the basis that, since we have no independent means of access to the real other than knowledge, we cannot possibly find out whether or not what we take for knowledge actually corresponds to “the thing itself,” is, in this particular regard, Kuhn’s direct predecessor. Whether, however, Kant proposed a viable alternative to the notion of knowledge as correspondence, or simply left us in the abyss of skepticism, is not my question. Or, rather, my question is exactly the same, but concerning Kuhn. Does he, or doesn’t he leave us  a viable alternative to the traditional notion of knowledge as correspondence ?

The paradigm as exemplar of the practice of problem-solving

A new paradigm inaugurates a new view of the field structured by the paradigm. This was, initially, the common understanding of “paradigm” and of paradigmatic science. And for good reason, for the language used by Kuhn to explain these notions was, for the most part, visual.

The metaphor of “seeing” as the explanation of “paradigm” is omnipresent in Kuhn’s book. Together with it, though, he smuggles a preconceived idea of visual perception –what he calls the philosophical or the epistemological paradigm which he attributes to Descartes following Newton (pp. 120-121): the corpuscular theory of vision, represented by the notion of visual “stimuli” sent by the real object resulting in sensations that represent the object. About this theory K is highly ambivalent. It has served us so well that he cannot abandon it;, and actually uses it  ( Postscript,  pp. 193); yet the irregularities he discovers, such as the misperception of cards, and the discovery that perception is influenced by expectations and paradigmatic theory tempts him to discard it. But for that he needs an alternative, which he doesn’t have. He doesn’t realize that his image of science as a problem-solving activity and of the paradigm as exemplar (of a practice of solving problems) is the alternative. Theories are instruments or maps for the scientific practice, not views of objects.

Practical animal knowledge is not representational: Ortho-doxy and ortho-praxis

Do animals know? To Plato, for whom genuine knowledge couldn’t be but a deed of the intellect, and who ,consequently had an acute sense of the unbridgeable difference between animals and people, such question would have sounded not only trivial and uninteresting but simply wrongheaded. Not so to Aristotle, who in time came to distinguish practical knowledge from the theoretical knowledge which Plato had explored, as well as to see some kind of continuity between animals and people. The interest of Aristotle’s exploration of the issue whether animals know is that it seems to open the way to an  alternative to the traditional notion of knowledge as faithful representation of the real –the sort of alternative Kuhn was obscurely groping for in order to give full shape to his understanding of science.

In my <2002-April-3-Aris-Devereux: Practical Knowledge in Aristotle> I argued that Aristotle failed to distinguish cleanly practical from theoretical knowledge. The reason for that failure is that, under Plato’s influence,  he still could not help looking in practical knowledge for an element of universality characteristic of Plato’s conception of theoretical knowledge, though practical knowledge is radically particular. In Aristotle’s view, the distinctive trait of practical knowledge is its connection with action. Let us reflect on this important point in more detail.

The connection with action is not found in theoretical knowledge, whose intrinsic purpose is knowledge for its own sake, the mere grasp of the truth. Thus, animals look for, and are capable of attaining, only the knowledge that is useful to the preservation and well-being of the individual or of the species to which the animal belongs, while people are capable of both theoretical and practical knowledge. Now the fact that practical knowledge is connected with action cannnot detract from the fact that it is knowledge. If it is knowledge, though, it is grasp of what is the case, for that is what knowledge consists in. Hence, on the basis of their respective past experience, both lions and people may come to grasp the useful ‘universal’ fact that, say, eating such a plant has beneficial purgative effects, though lions only know that fact practically, that is, for the purpose of applying that knowledge to obtain the benefits of the practice, whereas people know it practically and also theoretically, as a truth independent of its practical utility.          

Such  is Aristotle’s conception of practical and theoretical knowledge in the particular version to which we are presently referring. In this version, the difference between practical and theoretical knowledge is purely extrinsic. Indeed, the fact known by both the lion and the human is the same, though the lion knows it only because it is actually useful and only for the purpose of applying the knowledge, whereas the human knows the ‘universal’ fact also theoretically. There is, however, another possible version of practical knowledge. In the one we have so far considered, the connection between knowledge and action is extrinsic, in that the knowledge of the same fact may be applied or not, and it is knowledge whether or not it is applied.. But think, in the first place, of the lion’s case. The lion can attain the knowledge of the purgative effects of the herb only through personal experience of those effects, while the human may come to know the purgative effects of the herb by being told of them by somebody else Now, the lion’s experience is not only experience of the purgative effects, but also experience of eating the plant. In other words, it is experience of both the purgative effects and of the action that is followed by the purgative effects. Thus the lion learns not only the objective side, so to say, namely, the purgative effects the plant produces, but learns also the subjective side, namely, what to do to obtain the effects. Eating the plant becomes the means to obtain the effects.

This learning is experiential in that it is gained through experience . It is a matter not only of effects, but of effects felt by the agent and learned because felt. The purgative effects of the action of eating this plant are undergone and experienced, not observed, by the agent who eats the plant. Because the purgative effects are experienced as favorable by the agent who experiences the effects, the action of eating the plant will automatically tend to be repeated under the appropriate circumstances in the expectation of similar effects. Actions followed by rewarding effects tend to be repeated; actions followed by unpleasant effects tend to be avoided. And from objects that look similar similar effects are expected. These are inherited mechanisms of living, sensitive bodies. And these mechanisms are at the origin of all further purposive action initiated by living sensitive bodies and of all future learning about the world as our abode.  

As realized by Hume, these are mechanisms directly connected with action. In other words, the animal doesn’t know-that all plants of that kind are purgative. The animal just has learned to desire and look for that kind of plant when purgation is desirable.   There is memory of the plant as desirable for purgation. Whenever in the future purgation is desired the animal will actively look for that kind of plant. This is purely practical recognition of the plant for purposes of action, for the animal has no theoretical idea-that there is a kind of plant that uniformly produces purgative effects in such a kind of animals.

Though the recognition is practical and certainly not verbalized by the animal, Aristotle saw it as akin to the cognitive, objectifying  universalization involved in reflective human judgments, to which universalization Socrates and Plato had given epistemic supremacy in their quest for absolute certainty, and of which Plato thought geometry was the supreme model.
