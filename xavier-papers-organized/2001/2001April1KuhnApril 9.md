KUHN AND THE ISSUE OF TRUTH

“At no stage do we cease to participate in the life of the individual under observation. For we comprehend a living being at all levels by our subsidiary awareness of its particulars. These particulars were never observed in themselves; we read them as manifestations of an individual. We rely on them as pointers, as we rely on a probe or a written text, by making them parts of ourselves for reaching beyond them. Thus our understanding of living beings involves at all levels a measure of indwelling ...” ( Knowing and Being: Essays by Michael Polanyi , Edited by Marjorie Grene, The U of Chicago Press, 1969, p. 136).

“ The existence of this strong network of commitments–conceptual, theoretical, instrumental and methodological– is a principal source of the metaphor that relates normal science to puzzle-solving. Because it provides rules that tell the practitioner of a mature specialty what both the world and his science are like, he can concentrate with assurance upon the esoteric problems that these rules and existing knowledge define for him... Normal science is a highly determined activity, but it need not be entirely determined by rules. That is why, at the start of this essay, I introduced PARADIGMS RATHER THAN SHARED RULES, assumptions, and points of view as the source of coherence for normal research traditions. Rules, I suggest, derive from paradigms, but paradigms can guide research even in the absence of rules ” (Thomas S. Kuhn, The Structure of Scientific Revolutions . Second Edition, Enlarged, Volumes I and II of Foundations of the Unity of Science, University of Chicago, 1970, p. 42).

“ Many readers will surely want to say that what changes with a paradigm is only the scientist’s interpretation of observations that themselves are fixed once and for all by the nature of the environment and of the perceptual apparatus. On this view, Priestley and Lavoisier both saw oxygen, but they interpreted their observations differently ... - Let me say at once that this very usual view of what occurs when scientists change their minds about fundamental matters can be neither all wrong nor a mere mistake. Rather it is an essential part of a philosophical paradigm initiated by DESCARTES and developed at the same time as Newtonian dynamics. That paradigm has served both science and philosophy well... But as the example of Newtonian dynamics also indicates, even the most striking past success provides  no guarantee that crisis can be definitely postponed. Today research in parts of philosophy, psychology, linguistics, and even art history, all converge to suggest that the traditional paradigm is somehow askew. That failure to fit is also made increasingly apparent by the historical study of science to which most of our attention is necessarily directed here. - None of these crisis-promoting subjects has yet produced a viable alternate to the traditional epistemological paradigm , but they do begin to suggest what some of the paradigm’s characteristics will be. I am, for example, acutely aware of the difficulties created by saying that when Aristotle and Galileo looked at swinging stones, the first saw constrained fall, the second pendulum. The same difficulties are presented in an even more fundamental form by the opening sentences of this section: though the world does not change with a change of paradigm, the scientist afterward works in a different world .” ( Ibid. , p. 121).

“ But is sensory experience fixed and neutral? Are theories simply man-made interpretations of given data? The epistemological viewpoint that has most often guided Western philosophy for three centuries dictates an immediate and unequivocal, Yes! In the absence of a developed alternative, I find it impossible to relinquish entirely that viewpoint. Yet it no longer functions effectively , and attempts to make it do so through the introduction of a neutral language observation now seems to me hopeless” ( Ibid. , p. 126).

“In the sciences there need not be progress of another sort. We may, to be more precise, have to relinquish the notion, explicit or implicit, that changes of paradigm carry scientists and those who learn from them closer and closer to truth ... - But nothing that has been or will be said makes it a process of evolution toward anything. Inevitably that lacuna will have disturbed many readers. We are all deeply accustomed to seeing science as the one enterprise that draws constantly nearer to some goal set by nature in advance” ( Ibid. , pp. 170-171).

“ The PARADIGM AS SHARED EXAMPLE is the central element of what I now take to be the most novel and least understood aspect of this book .... Philosophers of science have not ordinarily discussed the problems encountered by a student in laboratories or in SCIENCE TEXTS, for these are thought to supply only practice in the application of what the student already knows. He cannot, it is said, solve problems unless he has first learned the theory and some rules for applying it. Scientific knowledge is embedded in theory and rules; problems are supplied to gain facility in their application. I HAVE TRIED TO ARGUE, HOWEVER, THAT THIS LOCALIZATION OF THE COGNITIVE CONTENT OF SCIENCE IS WRONG. After the student has done many problems, he may gain only added facility by solving more. But at the start and for some time after, doing problems is learning consequential things about nature. IN THE ABSENCE OF SUCH EXEMPLARS, the laws and theories he has previously learned would have little empirical content. [Follows excellent example on pages 188-189] - A phenomenon familiar to both students of science and historians of science provides a clue. The former regularly report that they have read through a chapter of their text, understood it perfectly, but nonetheless had difficulty solving a number of the problems at the chapter’s end. Ordinarily, also, those difficulties dissolve in the same way. The student discovers... a way to see this problem as like a problem he has already encountered. Having seen the resemblance, grasped the analogy between two or more distinct problems, he can interrelate symbols and attach them to nature in the ways proved effective before. The law sketch, say f=ma , has functioned as a TOOL , informing the student what similarities to look for, signaling the gestalt in which the situation is to be seen. The resultant ability to see a variety of situations as like each other, as subjects for f=ma or some other symbolic generalization, is, I think the main thing a student acquires by doing exemplary problems, whether with a pencil and paper or in a well designed laboratory. After he has completed a certain number, which may vary widely from one individual to the next, he views the situations that confront him as a scientist in the same gestalt as other members of his specialist’s group. For him they are no longer the same situations he had encountered when his training began. He has meanwhile assimilated a time-tested and group-licensed way of seeing... - GALILEO found that a ball rolling down an incline acquires just enough velocity to return it to the same vertical height on a second incline of any slope, and he learned to see that experimental situation as like the pendulum with a point-mass for a bob . Huyghens then... Finally, Daniel Bernoullli...  - That example should begin to make clear what I mean by learning from problems to see situations as like each other, as subjects for application of some scientific law or law-sketch. Simultaneously it should show why I refer to the consequential knowledge of nature acquired while learning the similarity relationship and thereafter embodied in a way of viewing physical situations rather than in rules or laws . The three problems in the example, all of them exemplars from eighteenth-century mechanicians, deploy only one law of nature. Known as the principle of vis viva , it was usually stated as “Actual descent equals potential ascent.” Bernouilli’s application of the law should suggest how consequential it was. Yet the verbal statement of the law, taken by itself, is virtually impotent . Present it to a contemporary student of physics, who knows the words and can do all these problems but now employs different means. Then imagine what the words, though all well known, can have said to a man who did not know even the problems. For him the generalization could being to function only when he learned to recognize actual descents and potential ascents as ingredients of nature, and that is to learn something PRIOR TO THE LAW, about the situations that nature does and does not present . That sort of learning is not acquired by purely verbal means. Rather it comes as one is given words together with concrete examples of how they function in use, nature and words are learned together. To borrow once more MICHAEL POLANYI’S useful phrase, WHAT RESULTS FROM THIS PROCESS IS ‘TACIT KNOWLEDGE’ WHICH IS LEARNED BY DOING SCIENCE RATHER THAN BY ACQUIRING RULES FOR DOING IT ” ( Ibid. , p. 187-191).

“ What I have been opposing in this book is therefore the attempt, traditional since DESCARTES but not before, to analyze perception as an interpretive process, an unconscious version of what we do after we have perceived . - What makes the integrity of perception worth emphasizing is, of course, that so much past experience is embodied in the neural apparatus that transforms stimuli into sensations . An appropriately programmed perceptual mechanism has survival value. To say that the members of different groups may have different perceptions when confronted with the same stimuli is not to imply that they may have just any perceptions at all. In many environments a group that could not tell wolves from dogs could not endure. Nor would a group of nuclear physicists today survive as scientists if unable to recognize the tracks of alpha particles and electrons. It is just because so very few ways of seeing will do that the ones that have withstood the tests of group use are worth transmitting from generation to generation . Equally, it is because they have been selected for their success over historic time that we must speak of experience and knowledge of nature embedded in the stimulus to sensation route - Perhaps ‘knowledge’ is the wrong word , but there are reason for employing it. What is built into the neural process that transforms stimuli to perceptions has the following characteristics: it has been transmitted through education; it has, by trial, been found more effective than its historical competitors in a group’s current environment; and, finally, it is subject to change both through further education and through the discovery of misfits with the environment. These are characteristics of knowledge, and they explain why I use the term. But it is strange usage, for one other characteristic is missing. We have no direct access to what it is we know [REGRESSES TO KANT’S THING IN ITSELF, WHICH PRESUPPOSES ONTOLOGICAL CORRESPONDENCE!!], no rules or generalizations with which to express this knowledge. Rules which could supply that access would refer to stimuli not sensations, and stimuli we can know only through elaborate theory. In its absence, the knowledge embedded in the stimulus-to-sensation route remains TACIT. ” - Though it is obviously preliminary and need not be correct in all details, what has just been said about sensation is meant literally... But talk like this of seeing and sensation here also serves metaphorical functions as it does in the body of this book. We do not see electrons, but rather their tracks or else bubbles of vapor in a cloud chamber. We do not see electric currents at all, but rather the needle of an ammeter or galvanometer. Yet in the preceding pages, particularly in Section X, I have repeatedly acted as though we did perceive theoretical entities like currents, electrons, and fields, as though we learned to do so from examination of exemplars, and as though in these cases too it would be wrong to replace talk of seeing with talk of criteria and interpretation. The METAPHOR that transfers ‘seeing’ to contexts like this is scarcely a sufficient basis for such claims. In the long run it will need to be eliminated in favor of a more literal mode of discourse. - ... Seeing water droplets or a needle against a numerical scale is a primitive perceptual experience for the man unacquainted with cloud chambers and ammeters . It thus requires contemplation, analysis, and interpretation (or else the intervention of external authority) before conclusions can be reached about electrons and currents. But the position of the man who has learned about these instruments and had much exemplary experience with them is very different, and there are corresponding differences in the way he processes the stimuli that reach him from them. Regarding the vapor in his breath on a cold winter afternoon, his sensation may be the same as that of a layman, but viewing a cloud chamber he sees (here literally) not droplets but the tracks of electrons, alpha particles, and so on. These tracks are, if you will, criteria that he interprets as indices [SIGNS] of the presence of the corresponding particles, but that route is both shorter and different from the one taken by the man who interprets droplets.  - Or consider the scientist inspecting an ammeter to determine the number against which the needle has settled. His sensation probably is the same as the layman’s, particularly if the latter has read other sorts of meters before. But he has seen the meter (again often literally) in the context of the entire circuit, and he knows something about its internal structure. For him the needle’s position is a criterion, but only of the value of the current. To interpret it he needs determine only on which scale the meter is to be read. For the layman, on the other hand, the needle’s position is not a criterion of anything except itself . To interpret it, he must examine the whole layout of wires, internal and external, experiment with batteries and magnets, and so on. In the metaphorical no less than in the literal use of ‘seeing,’ interpretation begins where perception ends. The two processes are not the same, and what perception leaves for interpretation to complete depends drastically on the nature and amount of prior experience and training ” ( ( Ibid. pp. 195-198).  

“ The practice of normal science depends on THE ABILITY, ACQUIRED FROM EXEMPLARS, to group objects and situations into similarity sets which are primitive in the sense that THE GROUPING IS DONE WITHOUT AN ANSWER TO THE QUESTION, ‘SIMILAR IN RESPECT TO WHAT? ” One central aspect of any revolution is, then, that some of the similarity relations change . Objects that were grouped in the same set before are grouped in different ones afterward and vice versa. Think of the sun, moon, Mars, and earth before and after Copernicus; or of free fall, pendular, and planetary motion before and after Galileo...  Not surprisingly, therefore, when such redistributions occur, two men whose discourse had previously proceeded with apparently full understanding may suddenly find themselves responding to the same stimulus with incompatible descriptions and generalizations ... - SUCH PROBLEMS, THOUGH THEY FIRST BECOME EVIDENT IN COMMUNICATION, ARE NOT MERELY LINGUISTIC, AND THEY CANNOT BE RESOLVED SIMPLY BY STIPULATING THE DEFINITIONS OF TROUBLESOME TERMS. BECAUSE THE WORDS ABOUT WHICH DIFFICULTIES CLUSTER HAVE BEEN LEARNED IN PART FROM DIRECT APPLICATION TO EXEMPLARS, the participants in a communication breakdown cannot say, ‘I use the word ‘element’ (or ‘mixture,’ or ‘planet’...) in ways determined by the following criteria .” They cannot, that is, resort to a neutral language which both use in the same way and which is adequate to the statement of both their theories or even of both those theories’ empirical consequences. Part of the difference is prior to the application of the languages in which it is nevertheless reflected”” (Ibid. , pp. 200-201)

“To translate a theory or world view into one’s own LANGUAGE is not to make it one’s own. For that one must go native, discover that one is thinking and working in, not simply translating out of, a language that was previously foreign . That transition is not, however, one that an individual may make or refrain from making by deliberation and choice, however good his reasons for wishing to do so. Instead, at some point in the process of learning to translate, he finds the transition has occurred, that he has slipped into the new language without a decision having been made. Or else, like many of those who first encounter, say, relativity or quantum mechanics in their middle years, he finds himself fully persuaded of the new view but nevertheless unable to internalize it and be at home in the world it helps to shape. Intellectually such a man has made his choice, but the conversion required if it is to be effective eludes him. He may use the new theory nonetheless, but he will do so as a foreigner in a foreign environment, an alternative available to him only because there are natives already there. His work is parasitic on theirs, for he lacks the constellation of mental sets which future members of the community will acquire through education. - The conversion experience that I have linked to a gestalt remains, therefore, at the heart of the revolutionary process. Good reasons for choice provide motives for conversion and a climate in which it is more likely to occur. Translation may, in addition, provide points of entry for the neural reprogramming that, however inscrutable at this time, must underlie a conversion. But neither good reasons nor translation constitute a conversion...” ( Ibid., p 204).

“ A scientific theory is usually felt to be better than its predecessors not only in the sense that it is a better instrument for discovering and solving puzzles but also BECAUSE IT IS SOMEHOW REPRESENTATION OF WHAT NATURE IS REALLY LIKE. One often hears THAT SUCCESSIVE THEORIES GROW EVER CLOSER TO, OR APPROXIMATE MORE AND MORE CLOSELY TO THE TRUTH. APPARENTLY GENERALIZATIONS LIKE THAT REFER NOT TO THE PUZZLE-SOLUTIONS AND THE CONCRETE PREDICTIONS DERIVED FROM A THEORY BUT RATHER TO ITS ONTOLOGY, TO THE MATCH, THAT IS, BETWEEN THE ENTITIES WITH WHICH THE THEORY POPULATES NATURE AND WHAT IS ‘REALLY THERE.’ - PERHAPS THERE IS SOME OTHER WAY OF SAVING THE NOTION OF ‘TRUTH’ FOR APPLICATION TO WHOLE THEORIES, BUT THIS ONE WILL NOT DO. There is, I think, no theory-independent way to reconstruct phrases like ‘really there’; the notion of a match between the ontology of a theory and its ‘real’ counterpart in nature now seems to me illusive in principle. Besides, as a historian, I am impressed with the implausibility of the view. I do not doubt, f. e., the Newton’s mechanics improves on Aristotle’s and that Einstein’s improves on Newton’s as instruments for puzzle-solving. But I can see in their succession no coherent direction of ontological development. On the contrary, in some important respects, though by no means in all, Einstein’s general theory of relativity is closer to Aristotle’s than either of them to Newton’s. Though the temptation to describe that position as relativistic is understandable, the description seems to me wrong. Conversely, if the position be relativism, I cannot see that the relativist loses anything needed to account for the nature and development of the sciences” (Ibid., pp.206-207).

“ A few readers of my original text have noticed that I repeatedly pass back and forth between the descriptive and the normative modes, a transition particularly marked in occasional passages that open with “But that is not what scientists do,’ and close by claiming that scientists ought not to do so.   Some critics claim that I am confusing description with prescription, violating the time honored philosophical theorem : ‘Is cannot imply ought.’ - That theorem has, in practice, become a tag, and it is no longer everywhere honored. A number of contemporary philosophers have discovered important contexts in which the normative and the descriptive are inextricably mixed. ‘Is’ and ‘ought are by no means always so separate as they have seemed .  [Stanley Cavell, Must we mean what We Say? (New York, 1969, ch. i)]. But no recourse to subtleties of contemporary linguistic philosophy is needed to unravel what has seemed confused about this aspect of my position. The preceding pages present a viewpoint or theory about the nature of science, and, like other philosophies of science, the theory has consequences for  the way in which scientists should behave if their enterprise is to succeed. Though it need not be right, any more than any other theory, it provides a legitimate basis for reiterated ‘oughts’ and ‘shoulds.’ Conversely, one set of reasons for taking the theory seriously is that scientists, whose methods have been developed and selected for their success, do in fact behave as the theory says they should. My descriptive generalizations are evidence for the theory precisely because they can also be derived from it, whereas on other views of the nature of science they constitute anomalous behavior. - The circularity of that argument is not, I think, vicious” ( Ibid. , pp. 207-208).

“Scientific knowledge, like language, is intrinsically the common property of a group or else nothing at all” ( Ibid. , p. 210).

Lessons learned

- The representational view of knowledge and the notion of truth as corresponding to the real out there are over. What supported them as their last bastion was their presumed instantiation in the natural sciences, which were taken as the supreme epistemic model . Kuhn shows that this view of the natural sciences doesn’t correspond to their practice, and he considers the Cartesian philosophical paradigm wrongheaded. With the exemplary instantiation in the natural sciences gone, the notions should go too.

- What prevents the Cartesian epistemic model from disappearing is the lack of a viable alternative. Perhaps “tacit practical knowledge,” which is not knowledge in the sense of corresponding to reality (see below), will do.

- The most important sense of “paradigm” is paradigmatic examples through which science is learned by students through the practice of solving problems (and for the purpose of solving problems). Thus it is not a matter of static vision acquired through theoretical understanding of the ‘law,’ but of practical application . By repeatedly solving problems of the same kind the students learn nature by learning to ‘see-it-practically,’ that is, to see it in the optimum way to respond to it appropriately as physicists or chemists.... Through solving problems they acquire the appropriate practical gestalt , much as, thanks to action , the person wearing inverting lenses reacquires the appropriate practical gestalt, that is, the optimum way of ‘seeing’ in order to function practically as a body in relation to its surroundings . (See my explanation of “situation” in my article “The Body in Being and Nothingness”). - It is, therefore, as agents bodily inserted in the world --as exposed, vulnerable agents-patients-- that we come to know. A pure Cartesian mind, unsituated physically and affectively in relation to the potential known, would know nothing, literally . Furthermore, the knowable is necessarily relative to the knower. The potential known of animals and humans is radically different, for the ‘known’ is that to which they have to respond, and their physical and affective exposure to it is radically different, depending on their interests and their respective range or scope of interests.

- Another important point is that this practical ‘knowledge’ is TACIT. In other words, it is not known by the agent in the sense of being seen; it is simply inscribed in the body, in the neural system, and reactivated under the appropriate circumstances in terms of Humean practical expectations and dispositions to respond. This is the reason why Kuhn hesitates to apply the term ‘knowledge’ to this un-known, un-formulated and un-seen . As an example, consider a skill, like driving a car. Little by little the skill, which may be initially directed by instruction, is ‘internalized’ in the body thanks to practice, much as the problem-solving of the physicist, for the physicist doesn’t know as object this internalization. Neither is it to be known as object. It functions instrumentally, as a map that is transcended practically in its being used to find one’s direction and reach the desired place. In the same way the law or formula of the physicist. It guides the student as a map as long as it is not fully internalized (as a new language that is not yet fully assimilated), but one doesn’t need the map or the formula once it is fully internalized. By then one has learned to ‘see’ the world in the optimal i nstrumental gestalt. Kuhn explains that the purely verbal understanding of the ‘law’ is useless, that is, not genuine understanding, for the ‘law’ refers to object-for-response; hence only those really understand who, thanks to practice, are disposed to respond correctly to the object under the appropriate circumstances. Much the same is the case with ‘ordinary language’ perceptual statements under conditions of exposure. If someone reacts to the word ‘Fire!’ in a movie theater as if it referred objectively, that is, as to a reality independent of the subjects exposed to it –as an ‘is” rather than as an ‘ought,’ as a neutral fact to be informed of rather than as a   fugiendum --one doesn’t understand language, for that is not the way language is spoken and heard, pace Searle [N.B. One difference between ordinary language and the technical language of the sciences is that the only type of speech-acts used in the sciences is statements (cf. Aquinas commentary on Peri Hermeneias) , and the only literary genre used is the “paper,” which is demonstrative in intention rather than persuasive. A scientific paper singles out one object and refers to it as object of specialized response for technicians, that is, for specialized people. By contrast to the ‘paper,’ the ‘story’ involves the participants in the story, and it forces the hearers or readers to situate themselves in relation to the story, as those who heard Orson Wells’s famous broadcast of “The Martians are coming” situated themselves in relation to the story. (Cf. Frei on the way Christians used to read the Bible by situating themselves in relation to the story).

Appendix on judgments and statements

“Thinking of religion as a cultural product thus directs attention to the process of its mediation; in other words, to how the content of religion is produced, transmitted, and received. Since the transmission of religion or any other symbolic form involves the packaging of meanings, the mediation process is a thoroughly interpretive activity... Interpretive activity, as we know from scholars of the mass media, is an active and creative process, one that is socially, historically, and locally contextualized... In this sense, as Dorothy Smith (1990b: 197) observes, ‘all texts are indexical’ since ‘their meaning is not fully contained in them but completed in the setting of their reading. ’ In making sense of the meanings packaged by a producer, the interpreter creates new meanings. Accordingly, the ‘reception’ or interpretation of meaning is itself part of the meaning production process... In practice, therefore, a sharp dividing line cannot be drawn between the production and reception of symbolic content, and this includes religious doctrine” (Michele Dillon, Catholic Identity: Balancing Reason, Faith, and Power , Cambridge University Press, 1999, p. 26).

Having reread Aquinas’s commentary on Aristotle’s Peri Hermeneias with its view of demonstrative science by contrast to persuasive science -- [“ Peri Hermeneias directe ordinatur ad scientiam demonstrativam , in qua animus hominis per rationem inducitur ad consentiendumn vero ex his quae sunt propria rei, et ideo demonstrator non utitur nisi enunciativis orationibus” (see wp-chant-excor-3, p. 28)] -- it dawned on me something that may be important. It concerns the difference between judgment and its expression in a statement. They are neither the same nor equivalent . Judgment precedes the verbal statement in which it is expressed, either as an aide-memoire for oneself or for purposes of teaching other people through verbal communication.. Judgment is  practical in that it refers the judge to future application and consists in a practical disposition in the judge to act in a certain way in response to what is judged. Though most likely we cannot judge without verbalizing, the internal wording of the judge doesn’t function for the judge in the same way as the external words function for the addressee . In the judge the words follow and express the judgment which is constituted by a practical disposition to respond to the object as seen practically. The judge doesn’t need the statement to guide him to action anymore than a person who knows the place and draws a map for others needs the map to reach the place, but the person taught needs the statement or the law until he or she develops through practice the practical disposition in which judgment consists. Thus, even for the person who receives the statement from the judge, the statement is meant to function as an instrument, as a map, not as a representation of reality or as reality itself.  

This relates to Kuhn’s implicit distinction between science in the discoverer, science in the practicing scientists who learned it already discovered and already made, and science in the textbooks of science. (Cf. September 12, 2005)

Galileo could write the law of falling bodies on inclined planes only because he had already discovered it through application to previously unobserved cases of fall. In him, as a discoverer and before teaching others the law,  the law functioned applicatively, as a practical disposition to see and cope with the related problems in a certain way. Now, thanks to Galileo’s direction, his already formulated law can be ‘demonstrated’ experimentally in the laboratory , which would lead other people to assent to it and then, through the practice of problem-solving, to develop the practical disposition Galileo acquired, which in Galileo was something “ prior to the law ” (cf. Kuhn’s quotation above from his pp. 187-191) But they could as well accept the law on Galileo’s authority, as students of physics accept it on the authority of their teachers, and, again through the practice of problem-solving, acquire the practical disposition. THUS, pace Kant, WHAT IS IMPORTANT IS NOT THE DEMONSTRATION OF THE LAW by which it becomes object of intuition, be the demonstration experimental or visual or logical. As a matter of fact, geometry was practiced in Egypt long before it was formalized by Euclid as a demonstrative science . Notice, by the same token, that the mere assent to the words of the law as a portrait of reality, be it by demonstration or by authority, doesn’t constitute understanding, for the addressee of  the statement of the law is the agent, and the statement means practically, not representationally .  As Kuhn observes, the student may have the impression that he understands the law when he understands a chapter of the textbook, that is, the words in which a law is expressed, and yet be incapable of solving problems (cf. above, quotation from Kuhn’s pp. 187-191).  In short, understanding is practical and results from internalization through practice .

As for the already constituted science found in the textbooks, Kuhn finds it misleading. Indeed, it is presented as if the law were itself the object of science, and as if it were object of contemplation and assent as representation of the real rather than an instrument that leads the agent to the appropriate response. . As an instance of textbook science, the student will be directed to ‘prove’ experimentally in the laboratory the law of falling bodies on inclined planes, and this experimental demonstration will be used as evidence in support of the positivistic view of science according to which the content of the statement that expresses the law is what is to be understood, and that content is ‘scientific’ because there is experimental or (in geometry) demonstrative evidence for it. This sounds very much like the syllogistic ‘papers’ of scholastic philosophers, and like the conception of science of Aristotle’s Posterior Analytics , which is indeed like the textbook science criticized by Kuhn. That is objectified science, not genuine heuristic or problem-solving, living science.

Objectified science (like Sartre’s “eye seen”) results from the demonstration that the law or the theorem is true; genuine, living science (like Sartre’s “seeing eye”) doesn’t know the law, it transcends the law in applying it. Hence the wrongheadedness of demonstrating the truth of the law, and of thinking that what distinguishes science is its certainty.

Scholastic natural theology as “textbook science”

Saint Anselm Proslogion, within its own different genre, looks like a prime example of what Kuhn criticizes as ‘textbook science.’ Anselm’s undertaking explicitly consists in finding out how much of what he knows through faith can be demonstrated , much as Euclid found out how much of what the Egyptian geometricians knew practically could be demonstrated. For Anselm, believing is a matter of faith, demonstrating is a matter of reason, but only because he sees belief as assent-quasi knowledge on the basis of infallible divine authority, and knowledge as assent on the basis of (demonstrative) rational evidence. In other words,   Anselm thinks that believing in God’s existence is assenting on the basis of God’s authority to the judgment-statement that God exists, and that knowing is assenting to the same statement but on the basis of compelling rational evidence. I think Anselm would agree with Lonergan: “Thus, our knowledge of God’s existence is just our knowledge of the truth of the judgment, Deus est” (cf Sum. Theol , I, q. 3, a. 4\, ad 2m) (Verbum, p. 8).

Anselm’s implicit assumption is (like Kant’s, incidentally –and unsurprisignly, since they inhabit the same intellectual tradition) that knowledge is speculative and that the judgment functions in the agent as a speculum of the real, though Kant transformed the view of correspondence with his Copernican revolution. Hence Anselm’s interest in demonstrating the truth of the judgment of faith, that is, in demonstrating that the judgment that God exists necessarily corresponds to the real.

Now, the failure of Anselm’s demonstrative enterprise is not only the one argued by Kant, that there can be no intuition that guarantees the validity of the a priori judgments of reason (therefore no science of God), and that existence anyway is not an attribute. The failure is far more radical and it obtains along Kuhn’s lines rather than Kant’s, to wit, that the very undertaking of demonstrating the content of a statement is wrongheaded, for it is inspired on a misunderstanding of knowledge as speculative grasp of object and of science as an organic collection of demonstrated or experimentally proven statements.

Yet, it may be argued that in geometry and in physics there is such a thing as experimental or rational ‘proof’ by which the theorem or the law is brought to intuition, to speak Kant’s language. But the question is not whether this is done but what is gained by doing it. In Kant’s interpretation, it is thanks to that intuition that the science is constituted as a science. It is therefore an essential moment and much is gained in it. In Kuhn’s interpretation, the experimental proof of, say, Galileo’s law, doesn’t play the role attributed to it by Kant and the textbooks of science, according to which the demonstration guarantees that the law is true and renders it “scientific.” In fact, Kuhn would argue, the grasp of the law reached by Galileo thanks to his experiments was a practical understanding, a disposition to apply, a disposition to respond to the world in the way involved in the practical grasp of the trait of the world stated in the law, not a representation of the world. A student who says that he understands the law or the theorem and yet fails to apply it in the solution of problems actually misunderstands the law or the theorem, for he thinks, as the authors of textbooks of science do, that the law represents the way the world is,  that having an accurate representation of the way the world is is what science is about, and that having compelling logical or experimental evidence in support of that view of the world is what constitutes a science as a science.  

The point is not to deny that Newton’s repeated observations were the basis on which he eventually reached the law of falling bodies on inclined planes. But the shape that this grasp of the law took in Newton was not the shape it takes in the student who supposedly understands because he understands what the law ‘says,’ while being incapable to apply it in actual problem-solving. By grasping the law Newton became capable of ‘seeing’ any particular case of fall on inclined planes in the appropriate way to solve the problems related to that kind of fall, much as the person who wears inverted lenses all of a sudden sees the world in the appropriate way to deal with it practically .  Now, that is what scientific knowledge consists in, potentially; and it becomes actual knowledge when actually applied.

What, then, is actually achieved by  the experimental proof of the law or the geometrical demonstration? Behind the idea of demonstration, be it experimental or logical, is the Greek aspiration to certainty of correspondence between vision and reality, that is, necessary truth, by which knowledge differs from opinion and science or genuine knowledge differs from mere belief . (Even Kant, who suppressed transcendent correspondence, introduced intuition to guarantee truth). In other words, there is no science or scientific knowledge without proof or demonstration, and both proof and demonstration consist in factual or logical evidence that compels and justifies assent. Now, TEXTBOOK SCIENCE IS PRECISELY OF THIS KIND. In this view, then, the demonstrative moment is absolutely essential for the constitution of a science as genuinely scientific. This presupposes, however, the Greek notion of knowledge as correspondence and of truth as justified correspondence, which Kuhn rightly wants to discard, though he avowedly has no clear alternative to propose.

In spite of the contingent constancy of the world in the past (remember Hume!), the same gestalt of the world will be expected, and is unavoidably expected by agents exposed to the world as we are, without any demonstration or proof that the case will actually be such. Our reflective demonstrations are mere securizing, wrongheaded attempts.  

To apply Kuhn’s views to the domain of faith , faith in the faithful is a practical relationship of the faithful to God by which the faithful is practically disposed to respond appropriately to God as grasped in the very act of responding to Him. Stating what God is for the believer is a useful and even necessary thing for the transmission of faith, among many other purposes, as stating the law of falling bodies on inclined planes is useful and even necessary for its transmission in teaching. This we do in the symbola fidei , in which we rightly do not simply say “There is God the Father”, etc., but we confess the God in whom we believe, that is, to whom we are –or at least should be, as everybody should be-- practically disposed to respond. It is by gaining practice within the community of the faithful in relating to God as Father, Son, and Holy Spirit that we ‘become native’ in the Christian faith, in a comparable way -- mutatis mutandis -- to that in which the student of physics becomes native in physics through practice under the supervision of his teachers. However, taking the ‘practical judgments’ of faith for objective statements, as Anselm and the practitioners of natural theology do, is to return to the view of knowledge as specular reproduction and of the real as something to be known speculatively. And to think that the God whom we confess can or should be proven as if it were a purely objective “fact,” and that anything would be gained by such a proof is wrongheaded. Not for nothing did Anselm end up his Proslogion expressing his dissatisfaction with the kind of objective, factual knowledge he had supposedly acquired by his ontological proof.

Another important point. A science, say, physics, is constituted not by a single law but by a coherent system of laws –another sense of  “paradigm”–  that defines the practical world in which the physicist works. Thus, there is Copernican, Galilean, Einsteinian physics. Each of these different paradigms constitutes a different hodological space inhabited as physicists by those who share the paradigm. In a parallel way, on the level of what Husserl called “the natural world,” Christians inhabit the historical world whose corresponding “theory” is the biblical neotestamentarian narrative which makes possible their expectations and their activity.    

Our knowledge, therefore, does involve something “seen.” But what is seen is not a picture of the real; it functions as a motivator to act in a certain way. In this sense, knowledge is essentially practical, meant to terminate in efficient action. This is why the existentialists –rightly– demand authenticity as defined by Camus: what a person believes to be true must determine his or her action. Nietzsche was particularly keen on that demand, especially when it came to Christians: If they truly believe that Christ rose from the dead, how come it doesn’t show in their faces? The reason, I suspect, is that   we are not only biologically committed, but also culturally committed, to certain goals . Now, while it is true that, unlike animals, we are capable of reflection and of choosing not only means but also goals, this is clearly within limits. Nietzsche, like the young Sartre, thinks that an alcoholic needs only to reflect in order to be able to choose in all freedom whether or not to stop drinking and even to choose whatever no matter what the consequences!
