AQUINAS

In the XIIIth century, with the solution worked out by Thomas

Aquinas, a fundamental controversy that had raged for about a century among theologians came, for all practical purposes, to an end. The subject of the controversy had been the nature of theology. Under the influence of the rediscovered works of Aristotle, the question had arisen whether and in what way theology could figure within the map of scientific disciplines traced by Aristotle, or whether--given its particular, revealed object--theology had rather to be excluded from the field of scientific knowledge. Aquinas won the day for the view that theology was indeed a science, though of a special kind--a view which has since prevailed.

Though the central point of the medieval controversy concerned the nature of theology, a number of other closely related and very important issues were dealt with in the process. Worthy of mention among them are the nature of divine revelation, the nature of the act of faith, the identity and importance of the literary genre of the Bible, the nature and scope of human reason, and the interplay between faith and reason and between revealed and natural knowledge. To the extent that the views of Aquinas on the nature of theology came to prevail, so did his views on the other issues just mentioned, which until our day continue to exert a powerful influence on Catholic theology. To the extent, also, that the Scholastic tradition inaugurated by Aquinas gave origin to the problems faced by Descartes, the father of modern philosophy, the positions taken by  Aquinas on the momentous issues involved in the XIIIth century controversy continue still to exert a powerful though unacknowledged influence within the contemporary philosophical establishment. This is true, very particularly, in the realm of philosophy of religion. It suffices to open any of the numerous current anthologies used as textbooks in the courses of philosophy of religion to realize it. Indeed, the texts anthologized invariably share Aquinas's fundamental assumptions concerning the scope and nature of human reason, the nature of philosophy, and the nature of the distinction between theology and philosophy.

My discomfort, both as a philosopher and as a Catholic, with the way in which religious issues are dealt with by professional philosophers, and with the way in which students are induced to reflect on such important matters in their courses of introduction to philosophy and of philosophy of religion, has led me in the last five or six years to investigate the roots of this problem and to look for a better alternative. As a parcel of this project,  which I expect to result in a book of which several chapters are already  in rough shape, I would like to spend some time studying the XIIth-XIIIth centuries theological controversy because of its determinant importance.

Fortunately, a good deal of the material that concerns me was put together by Marie-Dominique Chenu, O. P., in his authoritative work La Theologie comme Science au XIIIe Siecle . The rest of the pertinent material is available at the splendid theological library of Chantilly (France), where I would like to conduct my research the coming summer. (I visited that library in the summer of l990 to ascertain this fact).

I have already had the opportunity to get acquainted with much of that material and to reflect on it. I, therefore, have reasonable confidence that a summer would suffice me to test systematically the validity of my interpretation against the textual data and, if my intuitions are verified, to start giving shape to one of the central chapters of the book on philosophy of religion I have in mind.

Essentially, my thesis comes to the following. The Bible is, at its core, a narrative work. Now, narrative works possess a characteristic unity of their own that is completely different from the logical unity of a demonstration. The logical and epistemological works of Aristotle on which Aquinas and his supporters in the controversy took their basis are concerned with logical unity and equate knowledge with logical demonstration.

Having accepted this Aristotelian equation and convinced, as they were, of the truth of the Bible, Aquinas and his supporters mistook the Bible for a loosely constructed demonstrative work (because it was written neither by specialists nor for specialists), and mistook their own Summae Theologicae for  logical translations of the Bible (which they are not, since there is no possible one to one correspondence between a narrative and a logical demonstration). In this way, theology was made to qualify, in  Aquinas's own words, as a quasi scientia , that is, as a special kind of scientific discipline--more certain than any other scientific discipline because of its basis on divine revelation, though less humanly satisfying to the intellect, because of its lack of intrinsic evidence. As for philosophy, which worked on the data of reason, not of revelation, it was meant to fulfill an ancillary work to theology by supplying it with intrinsic evidence in those cases (for instance, the existence of God, the existence of the soul, the immortality of the soul, etc.) in which natural reason was capable of attaining truths which had also been the object of divine revelation. In the process, revelation was equated by Aquinas and his supporters with God's knowledge shared with us, and the act of faith with an intellectual act of assent akin to the act of assent to a logical demonstration, though based on God's authority rather than on intrinsic evidence.

Such was the view that became current as a result of the controversy that raged in the XIIth and XIIIth centuries. And such is the view that, in spite of superficial modifications

and significant reservations, still prevails and is operative in our days for lack of a better, more persuasive alternative. Don't

all the textbooks of philosophy of religion--to give an obvious instance of my point--deal still with the question of " proofs " for God's existence, for immortality, for life after death, whatever the side which the authors of the textbooks may personally favor on each of those issues? And does this not correspond exactly to Aquinas's conception, whether or not the authors of the textbooks are aware of the fact? After all, does this not necessarily imply, and create in the students who are so introduced to philosophy of religion the impression that, religious convictions are a purely intellectual matter? And that, at least ideally, the work of human reason in religious matters is to offer proofs for the object of revelation?

Assume, however, that there is some validity to the view sketched above that the Bible is indeed, at its core, a narrative work, that narratives possess a structure of their own irreducible to the logic of demonstration, and that narratives are nonetheless capable of conveying knowledge of a kind that cannot be reduced to the 'scientific knowledge' Aristotle was interested in. It is obvious, in that case, that the cluster of fundamental notions bequeathed to us by Aquinas concerning the nature of theology, of philosophy, of divine revelation as a sort of Aristotelian knowledge, and of faith as intellectual assent akin to the assent involved in a demonstration are wrongheaded and need to be substituted by totally different notions. Well, that is very precisely, the assumption on which my project is based and which I would like to substantiate the coming summer.

Of course, I am not the sole author of that assumption. I have been enormously helped by the development that has taken place in the last twenty-five years in the study of the so-called 'logic of narratives' and by the movement identified as 'narrative theology.' To cite only some of the authors and works I am particularly indebted to: Erich Auerbach, Mimesis: The Representation of Reality in Western Literature ; Paul Ricoeur, Temps et Recit; Hans W. Frei, The Eclipse of Biblical Narrative: Eighteenth and Nineteenth Century Hermeneutics; Robert Alter, The Art of Biblical Narrative ; Henri de Lubac, S. J., Exegese Medievale ; Stanley Hauerwas, A Community of Character: Toward a Constructive Christian Social Ethic ; Hans Georg Gadamaer, Philosophical Hermeneutics , and Truth and Method; Alasdair MacIntyre, After Virtue ; Julius A. Elias, Plato's Defence of Poetry ; John Searle, Speech Acts .

A final note on the scope of my immediate research. Strictly speaking, my project for the coming summer is limited to an analysis and a criticism of the position taken by Aquinas in the medieval controversy. In this sense, my work can be seen as a sort of tracing back to its medieval roots the loss of the narrative character of the Bible studied by  Hans Frei in the hermeneutics of the XVIIIth and XIXth centuries. I reserve for a further time the development of the positive alternative to the route taken  and consecrated by Saint Thomas.

I. STATEMENT OF PURPOSE

The immediate purpose of my investigation is to show that

the enormously influential thesis of Hans Frei concerning the loss of the narrative dimension in biblical interpretation, which he placed in the eighteenth century, can and should be traced back, for the full intelligibility of its nature and consequences, to the thirteenth century controversy on the nature of faith and reason from which Scholastic theology and philosophy were born. Since this is only a chapter of a book I am working on,I should explain the context of my study.

As a philosopher, as a Catholic, and as a teacher of philosophy I am distressed by the way in which the issues concerning God and religion are approached by professional philosophers. Practically all the textbooks of philosophy of religion as well as the contemporary philosophical literature on that subject focus on whether or not God's existence can be proved or disproved, and on  particular proofs and counter-proofs. There is nothing very new to this philosophical approach; already in the thirteenth century Aquinas had proposed his famous 'five ways' to prove God's existence. Which means that the model Aquinas created for dealing philosophically with religious issues remains alive and kicking today, at least in philosophy. Indeed, both those philosophers who believe in God and those who do not believe in God share the implicit presupposition of Aquinas's philosophical approach, to wit: that religious convictions are essentially intellectual convictions (after all, Aquinas defines faith as intellectual assent). What is questioned today by many is not the presupposition on which the approach rests but the sufficiency of the reasons for religious assent. What disturbs me, by contrast, is not the sufficiency or insufficiency of the arguments, but the approach itself. For neither do I think that the God in whom people actually believe is akin to a geometrical theorem, nor do I think that the religious convictions of people are essentially intellectual in character, though they are not thereby a matter of blind emotions either.

Xavier O. Monasterio, Ph. D.

B. 2: "Such is the character of this basically unitary content of divine revelation that the reality it affirms is, in a peculiar fashion, identical with the act of revelation and also with the witness. Earlier in this book it was said that this is a situation almost without parallel... to leave room for the situation in which a person says 'I love you.' That statement is not primarily supposed to inform another person of an objective fact separable from the speaker. Rather, it is a kind of self-witnessing; and the witnessed subject-matter is given reality solely by having been spoken in such a manner. - ... the same rule applies to divine revelation. In speaking to men God does not cause them to know objective facts, but he does throw open to them his own Being.The subject-matter which forms the essential content of revelation--that man has been elected to participate in the divine life; that that divine life has been offered to him, in fact already given--this subject-matter owes its realityu to nothing but the fact that it is pronounced by God. Rather, the Incarnation of God and the revelation in Christ are one and the same reality. Divine revelation is not an announcement of a report on reality, but the imparting of reality itself. That imparting takes effect, however, only upon the believer." (Pieper, pp 89-91)
