    Thomas Kuhn: Tacit Knowledge in the experimental sciences  

Preface to Kuhn’s views

IT IS MY CONVICTION THAT Kuhn’s notion of “paradigms” and of  the paradigmatic character of “normal science,” important and enlightening as it is, prevented him and us from being enriched by his real intended contribution, which was to overcome the Cartesian “philosophical paradigm” reflected in the textbook view of science by which we all are contaminated. His dissatisfaction was with the “textbook view of science” because he found it at odds with the actual practice of science. His aim was to substitute for that image the correct one.

To speak of TACIT KNOWLEDGE amounts to saying that there is no way of expressing in enunciations that say what is ‘known,’ precisely because knowledge is tacit, and consequently is not objective or representational of object nor equivalent to it. In the case of tacit knowledge, what is meant is not to be understood or comprehended as a representation of what is known by the speaker but as a signal for action. As understanding money is being capable of using money to perform the exchange operations or transactions  which money as an institutional instrument renders possible, and as the only way to ‘understand’ what is meant by the  signs on a musical partition is being capable of using the signs to produce the music meant, so understanding a scientific law consists in applying it appropriately to the solution of concrete problems of physics which the enunciated law  renders possible. Objective knowledge is an illusion that results from curious, epistemic reflection, similar to the illusion that, in the case of statements, identifies the oral communication between agents with its written version –that is, with what is said independently of the circumstances in which it is said as well as of the communicative intentions of th speaker – and reduces the former to the latter. Which is why Searle identifies linguistic meaning with the bastard way language functions in a classroom of a school of language for foreigners. (Monasterio)

Preface to the study of Kuhn’s views

 Thomas S. Kuhn’s name is generally associated with “paradigms.” Indeed  most of the discussion of his views has focused on pre-paradigmatic science, the paradigmatic character of “normal” science, and the scientific revolutions that a result from a shift of paradigms. Now, while there are good reasons for this focus, it seems to me that this fixation with paradigms has obscured Kuhn’s attempted and very important contribution to the correct understanding of  the scientific activity. Such will be the subject of this paper.   

In the very  opening paragraph of the first chapter of The Structure of Scientific Revolutions Kuhn attacks the view of science projected and perpetuated by the scientific textbooks –what he calls “the textbook image of science” –  and states his own purpose:

“that image has previously been drawn... mainly from the study of finished scientific achievements as those are recorded [THAT IS TOLD, STATED, ENUNCIATED] in the classics and, more recently, in the textbooks from which each new scientific generation learns to practice its trade. Inevitably, however, the aim of such books is persuasive and pedagogic; a concept of science drawn from them is no more likely to fit the enterprise that produced them than an image of a national culture drawn from a tourist brochure or a language text. This essay attempts to show that we have been misled by them in fundamental ways. Its aim is a sketch of the quite different concept of science that can emerge from the historical record of the research activity itself” ^(F1)  

This terse  introduction summarizes Kuhn’s entire undertaking: It consists in the disismissal of the textbook image of science because it is at odds with the actual scientific practice, and in an attempt to substitute for it the conception  of science that Kuhn finds in harmony with the historical record of the actual scientific practice.

Of course, the general idea is that the textbook image of science is that it is at odds with what goes on in the actual practice of science. But is there a common underlying point to the multitude of Kuhn’s particular arguments against the textbook view? I think there is. And Kuhn’s basic point is that the textbook view takes for granted that scientific knowledge consists in a view of the real world, and that the real world corresponds to that view. However, Kuhn is convinced that the actual practice of science is at odds with both assumptions.  A quick glance at Kuhn’s argument will suffice to realize that such is his conviction.

Let us begin with scientific observation. the textbook view conceives scientific observation as an extension and a refinement, thanks to technologically sophisticated instruments of observation and measurement, of the ordinary perceptual vision by means of which, SUPPOSEDLY,  we gain our awareness of the sensible aspects of the surrounding world. What is no less important, the textbook view assumes that the perceptual awareness we gain through ordinary vision, and then through the more sophisticated approach of scientific observation, corresponds to what is the case in reality. Kuhn points out, however, that  the way in which scientific observation  functions in the actual practice of science does not confirm that the awareness of the observed objects gained by the scientist as a result of observation and measurement represents, AND corresponds to, the sensible aspects of the  objects observed. For one example among many, Kuhn informs us that “Lavoisier saw oxygen where Priestley had seen dephlogisticated air...” ^(F2)

A more pointed example of sensible observation:

“We do not see electric currents at all, but rather the needle of an ammeter or galvanometer. Yet in the preceding pages... I have repeatedly acted as though we did perceive theoretical entities like currents, electrons and fields, as though we learned to do so from examination of exemplars, and as though in these cases too it would be wrong to replace talk of seeing with talk of criteria and interpretation. The metaphor that transfers ‘seeing’ to contexts like this is scarcely a sufficient basis for such claims. In the long run it will need to be eliminated in favor of a more literal mode of discourse. ” ^(F3) *

[A more literal kind of discourse is, of course, a discourse that reproduces more exactly what is the case. Do we need a more glaring example of Kuhn’s own representational conception of the language of statements, the very conception he is trying to overcome!?].

Naturally, Kuhn foresees the spontaneous objection of most of his readers to examples of this sort:

“Many readers will surely want to say that what changes with a paradigm is only the scientist’s interpretation of observations that themselves are fixed once and for all by the nature of the environment and of the perceptual apparatus. On this view, Priestley and Lavoisier both saw oxygen, but they interpreted their observations differently; Aristotle and Galileo both saw pendulums, but they differed in their interpretations of what they both had seen.” ^(F4)

For his part. Kuhn disagrees. The reason for the perceptual differences, according to him, is that far from being neutral that is,  purely dictated by the object, and therefore resulting in  an exact reproduction of the observed object, scientific observation is –and must be in order to fulfill its instrumental rather than reproductive function-- paradigm-dependent or theory-laden.

However, IF AS IS ACTUALLY THE CASE IN KUHN’S VIEW,  SCIENCE IS A PROBLEM-SOLVING ACTIVITY RATHER THAN A REPRODUCTIVE ACTIVITY AIMING AT RESULTING IN A FAITHFUL COPY OF THE WORLD AND IF THE POINT OF OBSERVATION IS TO YIELD A SIGNAL FOR PROBLEM-SOLVING ACTION, THEN THE PARADIGM-DEPENDENCY OF THE OBSERVATION MAY NOT BE A DEFECT BUT A VIRTUE, IF INDEED IT MAKES POSSIBLE THE SOLUTION OF PROBLEMS. (3) If I take a book, I place it in the position that is most favorable to my reading it. If th book is inverted in relation to me, the agent, no reading is possible (4) In the case of Euclidean geometry . As long as the task is misconceived as demonstrating the necessary validity of the truth of the stated theorems by bringing it to intuition in a way that renders superfluous the logical deduction of their truth, the inspective observation of the figures appears as the most apt means to achieve the goal. As soon, however, as it is realized that the goal of geometry is not to bring to intuition the truth of the theorems but to use the theorems as instruments to solve  concrete problems, the strategy changes from inspection of the figures to the practice of problem-solution, as suggested by Kuhn’s view of science.

Whether or not paradigm-dependency is the correct explanation, we may ignore that point for the moment.  What interest us is Kuhn’s reaction to the idea that the observations of different scientists are actually the same, and that their disagreements are to be explained as differences in interpretations of the same observed facts or data, which of course would be consonant with the textbook view. Kuhn sees in this idea “an essential part of a philosophical paradigm initiated by Descartes and developed at the same time as Newtonian dynamics.” ^(F5) According to Descartes and Newton, what actually exists out there is particles of matter. As to what exactly that Cartesian philosophical paradigm is, which underlies the textbook image of science, it becomes clear in the immediately following pages that it amounts to a version of epistemological realism, which postulates a reality that exists independently of observation and  is out there to be observed in its objectively observable aspects. It is this “philosophical paradigm” that Kuhn feels reluctantly constrained to contest, beginning on the level of scientific observation:

“But is sensory experience fixed and neutral? Are theories simply man-made interpretations of given data? The epistemological viewpoint that has most often guided Western philosophy for three centuries dictates an immediate and unequivocal, Yes! In the absence of a developed alternative, I find it impossible to relinquish entirely that viewpoint. Yet it no longer functions effectively, and the attempt to make it do through the introduction of a neutral language of observation now seems to me hopeless.” ^(F6)  

NO NEUTRAL LANGUAGE OF OBSERVATION

In contrast to this, one has only to remember that Kuhn rejects the idea that scientific knowledge involves or implies epistemological realism, that is, the ONTOLOGICAL CORRESPONDENCE of what is known-SAID with what is the case in reality, as well as the correlative notion of the SIMILARITY of knowledge to its object. But it is important to realize that the reason why Kuhn finds it impossible to accept the similarity between what the scientist is aware of and the object to which it supposedly refers as object known is THAT SCIENTIFIC KNOWLEDGE IS INSEPARABLE FROM THE LINGUISTIC APPLICATION IN WHICH THE AGENT  'COMPREHENDS' DISPOSITIONALLY OR HABITUALLY HOW THE OBJECT IS TO BE DEALT WITH. NO OBSERVATION IS INNOCENT OF ASSUMPTIONS PRECISELY BECAUSE IT IS IRREMEDIABLY LINGUISTIC-PRACTICAL, AND CONSEQUENTLY THERE IS NOT – AND CANNOT BE – A NEUTRAL LANGUAGE of observation, SINCE THE LANGUAGE OF STATEMENTS MEANS PRESCRIPTIVELY RATHER THAN DESCRIPTIVELY. CONSEQUENTLY, the meaning of linguistic formulations, statements included, is not to be assessed in terms of their correspondence to an object but in terms of the appropriateness of the agent’s conduct they motivate or prompt. This is why Kuhn is skeptical about the attempt to devise a neutral scientific language. Such a language, indeed, if it were possible, would be totally transparent in order not to  subtract or add  anything to what the object dictates, thereby making perfect the resemblance between the object and the knowledge of it possessed by the knower. Morever. –and  more importantly–  the very demand for correspondence is wrongheaded because language does not mean by resemblance but practically, so that a language that meant by resemblance between what is said and what is referred to would not be a language, for it would not mean or function as language does. Language is cognitional not by resemblance of what is said to that of which it is said, but by the  instrumentality of what is said for the apropriate response–which is precisely the radical difference between Searle’s and Polanyi’s views on language and knowledge . All this, however, is highly abstract. Let us render it concrete by returning to Kuhn’s views.

Attacking the textbook image of science, Kuhn asserts repeatedly that there is no neutral language of observation by reference to which scientists can reach firm agreement as a starting point. Moreover, however reluctantly, he rejects the “philosophical paradigm” he attributes to Descartes, according to which the world is there independent from observation and available for scientific observation that resembles it without modifying or affecting it. By the same token he rejects the idea that the statements in which scientists formulate the results of their observations, when correct,  enunciate and thereby communicate to others what is the case in reality. In other words, he rejects the view he attributes to Descartes that the real exists independently of all observation, that it is observable in itself, and that knowledge and language, when correct,  express and communicate, etiehr literally or at least equivalently,  what is the case in reality .

Whether or not Descartes is the originator of this conception of knowledge and language is a moot point. At any rate, Kuhn is clearly attacking the classical epistemological conception of reality, and of the relationship of knowledge and language to reality, which he sees implicit in the textbook image of science, and his undertaking cannot be understood, much less, judged correctly, unless seen in this light .

I have developed  in some detail what Kuhn says  against the notion of scientific observation implicit in the textbook image of science because it establishes the pattern of his criticism of the textbook view. Indeed, no less than the sensible aspects observed, the existence itself of the ‘realities’ postulated by scientists are paradigm-dependent . One concrete example should suffice:

“An investigator who hoped to learn something about what scientists took the atomic theory  asked a distinguished physicist and an eminent chemist whether a single atom of helium was or was not a molecule. Both answered without hesitation, but their answers were not the same. For the chemist the atom of helium was a molecule because it behaved like one with respect to the kinetic theory of gases. For the physicist, on the other hand, the helium atom was not a molecule because it displayed no molecular spectrum (note 7). Presumably both men  were talking about the same particle, but they were viewing it [INSTRUMENTALLY] through their own research training and practice (p. 50) ^(F7)

But what about experimental proofs of scientific laws and theories? Modern scientific knowledge is often  hailed because, by contrast to the aprioristic, rationalistic science of the ancients, it is experimental , and when confirmed again and again by experiments it supposedly acquires the unshakable, imperishable certainty of what is rationally demonstrated. According to Kuhn, however, that, too, is a misconception. Indeed, the shifts of paradigms, however infrequent, render obsolete the experimentally proven and supposedly  permanent ‘truths’ of the preceding scientific practice, and inaugurate an entirely new ‘normal’ science.

Nor is the textbook image concerning scientific theories any less at odds with what goes on in the actual scientific practice. Indeed, if scientific theories had to fit the facts in order to be acceptable, as they are supposed to by the textbook view, scientists would abandon a theory as soon as they discover irregular facts that do not square with the theory. But Kuhn finds that such is not the case at all. ^(F8) Indeed, not only has there never been a prevailing scientific theory that was not confronted with serious irregularities, but the irregularities themselves create the “esoteric” problems whose solution defines the most important task of the normal science of a given period.

Certain irregularities prove to be recalcitrant for a long period to their subsumption under the prevailing theory ; yet scientists cling to the theory against the evidence of the irregularity,– and they do this in the hope of squaring the rebellious facts with the theory, rather than the other way around, as the textbook image presupposes. Moreover, even in those comparatively infrequent cases in which the observed irregularities prove to be totally recalcitrant, and end up by becoming unbearable to some of the scientists and provoke a crisis, that does not amount to a falsification of the theory or theories. Rather it results in the emergence of a new paradigm with its correlative new normal science, that simply renders obsolete the old theories, in the sense that they are not employed anymore. Moreover, even in the case of the new science the correspondence between theories and facts, postulated by the textbook image, will fail to obtain, for the new theory or theories will again depend on the new paradigm and will again confront irregularities that in their turn will define the esoteric problems to be solved by the new normal science. In this way, a new developmental cycle begins, in which the textbook view will find as little definitive verification as in the preceding historical epoch or period. Not for nothing is Kuhn reluctantly forced to conclude that he finds in the process of scientific evolution no sign that science moves toward an increasing approximation to the truth, that is, toward an increasing correspondence with what is the case. To quote his actual words,

“We may... have to relinquish the notion, explicit or implicit, that changes of paradigm carry scientists and those who learn from them closer and closer to the truth...

“The developmental process described in this essay has been a process of evolution from  primitive beginnings – a process whose successive stages are characterized by an increasingly detailed and refined understanding of nature. But nothing that

has been or will be said makes it a process of evolution toward anything. ^(F9)

It is clear, then, that Kuhn’s array of arguments against the textbook image of science, to a few of whose most salient points I just alluded, shows that what he is attacking in that image at every step is the representational and ontological correspondence conception of knowledge by which it is inspired. It is Kuhn’s view that  the characteristics of the actual scientific practice simply do not square with that epistemological interpretation.

In the introductory paragraph of The Structure of Scientific Revolutions , quoted above , Kuhn told us that he was going to propose his own alternative to the textbook view of science. And he did. Indeed, his view of science is significantly different from the textbook view. Yet I am of the opinion that a very important piece is either totally missing or not made sufficiently explicit in Kuhn’s entire argument , and to that extent his undertaking remains incomplete and much less persuasive than it could and should be. For if Kuhn’s understanding of science is indeed an alternative to the textbook view, and if the reason why Kuhn rejects the latter is that it is underpinned by the representational conception of knowledge by which  epistemological realism is inspired, then  Kuhn’s own view has to imply an alternative to epistemological realism and to its correlative representational notion of knowledge. Now, what precisely is Kuhn’s alternative to epistemological realism? That is the missing piece, for nowhere does Kuhn specify it precisely and unhesitatingly. Moreover, since the only alternative to epistemological realism seems to be epistemological idealism, that is, that no reality corresponds to what the subject takes for knowledge, which is what baffles Kuhn when he uses the word ‘knowledge’ to characterize his own position, then by not fully  specifying his alternative Kuhn opened himself to the accusations of idealism and relativism of which he has been the object.

The problem is compounded by Kuhn’s own hesitations, which suggest that he himself was confused as to the nature of his alternative to epistemological realism. For instance, in an already quoted text concerning scientific observation, after criticizing “the epistemological viewpoint that has most often guided Western philosophy for three centuries,”  which underpins the textbook view of science, he uncomfortably confesses that “in the absence of a developed alternative, I find it impossible to relinquish entirely that viewpoint.” But was not Kuhn’s own position supposed to constitute the very alternative that he misses? Moreover, there are reasons to believe that Kuhn did not in fact abandon entirely the epistemological viewpoint he attacked. For one,  he could not prevent himself from assuming that observed objects send “stimuli” to the observers, without apparently realizing that stimuli are postulated entities needed to support precisely the epistemological realism he rejects in the textbook. To top this, he feels constrained to assume that an object sends the same stimuli to different observers, though at the same time he claims, in defense of his own view of science, that “two men with the same retinal impressions”– produced, one has to surmise, by the same stimuli–  “can see different things.” ^(F10) All this leads me to conclude, not only that Kuhn himself never reached a firm grasp of the alternative view of science he proposed, but that he could not quite rid himself of the epistemological realism he criticizes in the textbook image of science. In the hope of clarifying and rendering more persuasive Kuhn’s position, I will try to complete Kuhn’s job, following his traces..     

Science as a specialized language SCIENCE AS A PROBLEM-SOLVING ACTIVITY

In the first two chapters of this book I presented two radically different views of language. It is time now to explain what was the point of my having begun in that roundabout way before moving to Kuhn’s work on the nature of scientific knowledge. It will be my contention in this chapter that the textbook image of science rejected by Kuhn , which presupposes epistemological realism and its correlative representational or correspondence conception of knowledge *, coincides with the Serlean view of the relationship of the language of statements to the real, statements being conceived as objective representations of what is the case in reality . ^(F11)  Similarly, it will be my contention that Kuhn’s own view of science implies a Polanyian view of language, in which speech acts in general and statements in  particular are conceived to function not as objective representations of what is the case but as symbolic  instruments for the agent’s action. ^(F12) Now if this interpretation of  the textbook view of science rejected by Kuhn, and of  Kuhn’s own position happen to be correct, then my first two chapters will turn out to work as a highly  illuminating  preface to the nature of Kuhn’s undertaking, which I will study in this chapter.

∙ * Strictly speaking, the representational and the correspondence view of knowledge (or of language) are not identical. In both cases there is correspondence, but not of the same kind. Representational correspondence is pictorial correspondence by similarity between the representation and the object represented. In the case of  a mirror, the image reflected in the mirror represents exactly and, in this sense , corresponds to the object mirrored, so that a person who sees the image reflected in the mirror may be said to know in that image the object represented. But this correspondence by similarity can obtain only in the case of  visible objects. A sound, for instance, is perceptible but not visible. In its case, therefore, there can be no correspondence by similarity. Yet, when I hear the sound of, for instance, a word, I deem that what I heard corresponds to the word uttered,, but not in the sense that what I heard is visually similar to, or represents exactly, what was uttered, for a sound is not visible and cannot be represented visually. Here the correspondence is, rather,  causal, correspondence between cause and effect. It may be said that in knowing the sound I hear I know the sound that was produced, but “knowing” in this case is not used in the same sense as in the case of knowing the object represented by knowing its mirrored image, for there is no visual similarity here between the sound I heard and the sound uttered. Now if we understand knowledge as knowledge by similarity, then hearing is not a form of knowing, for what I hear is my auditive sensation and there is no similarity between it and what is the case. The lack of knowledge by similarity in the case of “secondary qualities” is what led the British empiricist to raise the question of the validity of perceptual knowledge in the case of secondary qualities, for he rightly realized that the original, paradigmatic notion of knowledge demanded visual similarity between the object known and the subject’s sensation. The same lack of similarity is what led Kant to deny that we know the thing in itself, that is, the object as it exists independently of our sensations, for it is impossible to know with certainty whether what is out therew corresponds to our sensations.

From what I just said it is evident that my interpretation of Kuhn’s view of science involves two assumptions: (1) that Kuhn thinks that science functions as a language, and (2) that he thinks that scientific language, that is the language of scientific statements, functions not according to the Serlean representational model, but according to the Polanyian instrumental model. Both assumptions, however, are far from being obviously true, and thus are in need of explanation.  

Regarding the first assumption, many readers may be startled by the idea that science functions as a language because they do not see any intrinsic relation between knowledge and language. Consequently, they fail to see what the scientific enterprise, which deals with knowledge, may have to do with language. In short, knowledge and language are, for them, two totally different things, for the same reason that they deem knowledge as a view of what is the case that is totally different from action and independent from action. Now, that, precisely, is the conception of knowledge implicit in Searle’s theory of language. How so?

We get an important clue to answer this question in Kuhn’s own constant comparison, sometimes explicit and more often merely implicit but clear nonetheless, of the practice of science to the practice of a language. For us, who in the two previous chapters explored the way in which language functions, this approach to the functioning of science may prove to be particularly  fruitful. I will go even farther along this line and say that we shall get an enormous amount of light if we realize that the textbook view of science rests on the same assumption on which Searle’s conception of language rests, to wit: that knowledge is to be equated with “objective knowledge.” . In a parallel way, I say that the alternative view of science that Kuhn struggles to articulate rests on the understanding of knowledge as “ tacit, that is, instrumental knowledge ” on which Polanyi’s implicit conception of language as I developed it in chapter II rests. Moreover, as Searle’s view of language appears initially persuasive to us because it is determined by the Cartesian-Newtonian philosophical  paradigm under whose influence both Searle and we live, and yet we realize that it does not reflect the way in which we as linguistic agents actually act, so does Kuhn find it difficult to get rid of the textbook view of science because it is determined by the Cartesian-Newtonian philosophical paradigm under which he lives, while at the same time realizing that this view is at odds with the way in which he and all other scientific agents actually act in the practice of their scientific trade. Finally, as the notion of “tacit knowledge” appears initially improbable if not outright unintelligible to us, because it is at odds with the philosophical paradigm under which we live, and yet, as we apply the idea of instrumental knowledge to the analysis of the actual functioning of language, this notion seems to fit the facts of the case in a way in which the objective knowledge of the Serlean analysis does not, and hence we begin to feel at home within it, so I predict that in the long run both ourselves, and Kuhn too, if he were alive, would find ourselves at home within this instrumental conception of knowledge and of scientific knowledge.

LANGUAGE AS INSTRUMENT OF COMMUNICATION OF ALREADY ACQUIRED  KNOWLEDGE BUT NOT REQUIRED FOR THE ACQUISITION OF KNOWLEDGE

Linguistic communication, according to Searle, consists in informing the addressee of the  of the speaker’s communicative intentions thanks to the expression of those intentions through saying. In this sense, all linguistic communication consists in the communication of knowledge of the speaker’s intentions. However, whereas in the case of all other speech acts what the speaker intends is to achieve an effect through the addressee’s action (what Searle calls perlocutionary effects), only in the  only in the case of statements does the speaker intend to communicate the knowledge he possesses, and he communicates it by stating what he knows. The illocutionary effect consist in informing the addressee of what the speaker intends co communicate to him or her Thus, while all other speech acts are related to action, statements are the only exception, for the only intention of the speaker in stating is to communicate to the addressee the piece of knowledge he states. On this point Searle agrees with Aristotle who observed that statements are the only speech acts that can be true or false, depending on whether or not the knowledge the speaker means to convey through them corresponds to what is the case in reality.

Now

It is obvious, then, that according to Searle, language plays an essential  role in the communication of the speaker’s already acquired knowledge. Indeed, in order to communicate to others what he knows, the speaker has to express in statements what he knows. But the question I want to explore is whether, according to Searle, language plays an equally essential role in the acquisition of knowledge, that is, in   the acquisition by the speaker of the knowledge he or she will eventually communicate to others through statements.

If Searle had thought so, he certainly would have included that very important function of language in the acquisition of knowledge in  “An Essay in the philosophy of language .” However, since Searle is totally  silent on that point, we are entitled to conclude that, for all practical purposes, he reduces the function of language, as far as knowledge is concerned, to the communication of previously acquired knowledge. This implies, however, that he matter-of-factly makes the very momentous assumption that knowledge is separable from all linguistic expression. and is acquired independentlyly of linguistic expresion – be it acquired, as most philosophers have traditionally surmised, through direct perception, or through intellection, or through  a combination of both. Why does Searle make this assumption? Because he conceives knowledge as a subject’s view of what is the case that corresponds to what is actually the case. and he sees no reason why a view could not be wordless. Don’t dogs, who possess no language but who are endowed with organs for visual perception, obviously know the world around them thanks to the view whey acquire of it?

Michael Polanyi, for his part, has a very different notion of  knowledge and of its relation to language and action. This difference can be best appreciated by considering the way in which each of them understands the relationship between knowledge and action. Let us begin with Searle.

How does Searle understand the relationship between knowledge and action? We can gather it from the way in which he establishes the distinction between illocutionary and perlocutionary effects, for the illocutionary effect is nothing else than the knowledge transmitted to the addressee of what the speaker intends to communicate. Now, Searle specifies that when the speaker intends to obtain perlocutionary effects, the latter can obtain only on condition that the addressee understand what the speaker means. In other words, the perlocutionary effects depend on the realization of the illocutionary effect. By contrast, however, the illocutionary effect may obtain even if the perlocutionary effect does not obtain. Indeed, the addressee may, for instance, understand a command, and not obey it, that is, not perform the action commanded by the speaker. It is evident, then, that Searle sees knowledge as independent from action, whereas he sees action as dependent on knowledge, for the addressee cannot obey an order unless he understands the order received. Actually, this relationship between knowledge and action is  the very basis on which Searle establishes his distinction between illocutionary and perlocutionary effects. From this it follows that, in the case of statements, the same relationship between knowledge and action obtains. In other words, given that, according to Searle, knowledge is independent from action and different from action, the knowledge that the speaker intends to share with the addressee in the case of statements is totally independent from action, as far as the speaker’s communicative intention  is concerned. Thus, for instance, the speaker may share with a child the knowledge that the toy the child is looking for is in the next room, without necessarily intending that the child go to the next room in search of the toy, for the speaker is simply stating,  not expressing a wish. and what the speaker states is a simple fact concerning the location of the toy.  

The same example will help us to understand the difference between Searle and Polanyi concerning the relationship between knowledge and action. Searle conceives knowledge as different from action because he understands knowledge as  a mental representation of the object. Thus what the speaker intends to communicate to the child by stating that the toy is in the next room is a purely factual view of a state of affairs. According to Polanyi, though, that is not the way the language of statements functions. Linguistic communication is a relationship between desirous agents exposed to the surrounding world. The speaker who states means as an agent speaking to an agent. and the addressee is meant to understand as an agent who desires the toy he is looking for. Hence the speaker refers the addressee not to a fact but to a desired toy.

Esoteric problems and application

Kuhn uses the word “problem” in two different ways to designate two different things: (a) concrete problems, like those that appear in textbooks, in whose solution the theory or ‘law’ applies. This “problem-solving,”  whether on paper or in reality through action, is genuine “APPLICATION,” for the function of the theory is to guide the agent in its successful application in view of actual or projected action , much as a map does; (b) esoteric problems or “puzzles” created by irregularities within a paradigm that do not correspond to what the prevailing theories lead to expect to be observed. In this second case, the problem --or rather the ‘puzzle,” to avoid confusion– is not practical but theoretical. Moreover it arises from the assumption that the theory represents the nature of  what is the case and therefore has to correspond observationally to it, which is precisely the textbook view that Kuhn rejects . Hence, when the theory and the phenomenon are at odds with each other,  either the theory or the phenomenon is deemed to be wrong and has to  change. This, in spite of the fact that in the original version of the representational view of knowledge, there is no possible ’either-or’ because knowledge is supposed to correspond to reality, not the other way around. In spite of the fact, too, that Kuhn himself will acknowledge that there is never complete correspondence between theory and phenomenon.

In the case of (a), not only the theory is not endangered, but it positively helps, in that it leads instrumentally to the solution of the problem. In this case, the student has to learn through practice of application guided by the theory the instrumental use of the stated theory, i.e., its meaning, which is practical . In the case concerning (b), the theory itself  is threatened: either the irregularity will be shown to fit the theory after all, or the whole paradigm will crash when replaced by a different paradigm.

What is highly significant in ths passage is that Kuhn attributes the primacy of importance to (b) rather than to (a), since he says that the solution of the esoteric problems of a given paradigm defines the most important task of the normal science of a given period. Why does he think that this is the most important task? Because it is the one that brings about what he sees as scientific progress, either by extension of the field covered by the theory, or by a shift of paradigms. The other task, by contrast, that is the solution of concrete problems by application of the theory, he sees, from the scientific point of view, as mere dull routine –the work of the engineer, not of the scientist. Yet, avowedly, progress in theory is not progress in truth-correspondence; rather, by implication, it is progress in efficiency in finding the way to satisfy our needs and desires in the material world. So theory is fundamentally instrumental, like the instrumental ‘vision’ with inverting lenses after the shift of gestalt thanks to action and in terms of future action. After a shift of paradigms, Kuhn tells us, physicists “move in a different world.” Move, that is, act confidently and efficiently to realize their projected goals. Thus scientific progress turns out to be progress in the instrumental value of a theory or of a paradigm or matrix of theories, for even within the new paradigm there will be no closer approximation to what is the case.  This is to say that the ‘match’ is not representational correspondence between what the scientist conceives or has in mind os ir objectively aware of and what is the case in reality. The match is, rather, instrumental,  between the scientists as human agents with needs and projects and the world in which the projects are to be realized. HOWEVER, AS SHOWN BY THE SECONDARY PLACE KUHN ATTRIBUTES TO CONCRETE PROBLEM-SOLVING BY CONTRAST TO THE SOLUTION OF ‘PUZZLES,’ HE FAILS TO SEE THIS CLEARLY. In other words, he fails to realize that, once the myth of truth-correspondence to what is outside is eliminated, the magic power of the idea of seeing the world as it IS, of intuiting the pure spectacle of  the entities that populate the world, loses its ground of validity and that, consequently, there is no reason for Kuhn’s own implicit exaltation of theory as the most important task of science, which rests on the conception of theory as representational of what is the case. This lack of realization is evident in Kuhn’s conviction that the metaphor of vision can be replaced by “a more literal form of discourse,” that is, a mode of discourse that corresponds more exactly to what is the case! (see pp. 196-197 and note 10). Not realizing that what he proposes is the epistemological alternative to realism, as far as science is concerned, Kuhn cannot walk firmly on the ground he has opened.

Inverting lenses

In my opinion, it is not a matter of eliminating the metaphor of seeing, but of realizing that knowledge –that is, the seeing–  is practical, instrumental rather than literally representational, exactly as the written notes in a musical partition are signals for action rather than signs Of the musical piece.. Hence, the one who ultimately ‘sees’ is the situated agent with particular goals, not an ethereal contemplating consciousness but the exposed agent. Hence that the seeing is instrumental-adaptive, not representational .  Not for nothing, in the case of inverting lenses , when the ‘correct’ vision is retrieved even when wearing inverting lenses , the correct vision is not ‘objective,’ but happens to be the appropriately instrumental one, the one that renders possible and more facile the agent’s action, the one that, so to say, through the disposition to act which it brings about favors the future coordination of vision with prehension (in the active mode), and the coordination of vision with preferred expected affection in a given situation (in the passive mode) . That is why everybody’s preferred position of a photo of a room is the one in which the roof is on the top and the floor on the bottom, which implies reference to the bodied agent whose space, as Sartre says, is ‘hodological.’ ^(F15) That is why, too, we don’t perceive the world inverted, as we should according to the laws of optics, given that the image in the retina is inverted. In short, what we call vision is the instrumental-adaptive vision characteristic of the needful, desirous or interested bodied agent .

To return to Kuhn . In the above quoted text, what puzzles him is that  what he calls knowledge lacks the representational character that we assume knowledge has to possess, the vision of a viewed that is typical of ‘objective knowledge’ by contrast to ‘instrumental knowledge.’* Well, exactly the same is lacking in what Aristotle called “practical knowledge,” for practical knowledge is not representional. Unlike Kuhn, however, Aristotle was not puzzled by the non-representational character of practical knowledge,  for he did not quite realize it, because theoretical knowledge was for him the paradigmatic model of knowledge. Hence, he finally made practical knowledge parasitic on, and subordinated to, representational knowledge.  If, however, unlike Aristotle, we begin with the practical knowledge of animals, which is neither verbal nor representational but instrumental, and we realize that human knowledge is a refinement of animal knowledge thanks to the instrumental or applicative character of language, then our whole conception of science is radically modified in the direction suggested by Kuhn’s work , though not fully realized by him.*

Knowledge and action

Sealre imports into his view of lingusitic communication a preconceived  notion of knowledge. According to this conception, knowledge is totally independent from action, though action may presuppose knowledge as its necessary condition . Indeed, in Searle’s opinion, all speech acts aim at communicating knowledge, for through  all of them  the speaker intends to inform the addressee of what he or she means. This obtains when, thanks to the comprehension of what is said, the addressee gains the awareness of what the speaker means. No connection with action is invovled. In addition, however,  there is the special case of statements . A statement is the only speech act in which,  in addition to the knowledge of the perlocutionary effects the speaker intends to have linguistically accomplished, either directly by means of his own lingusitic action (affections), or indirectly through the addressee’s actions,  the speaker intends to communicate a particular piece or pieces of  the knowledge he or she already possesses of a feature or features of the world, by saying or enunciating what heknows. However, in this special case, too, Searle conceives knowledge as the exact correspondence between what the speaker knows and communicates and what is the case in reality. And in this case, too, knowledge is totally independent from action. In short, two conclusions follow from this brief analysis. First, in every case in which  Searle refers to knowledge he conceives it as reproducing  or corresponding to its object. Second, given his notion of knowledge as vision,Searle believes that language, including statements, is totally independent from action.     

D oes one need words in order to come to know something? . Yes, according to Polanyi . The reason is that, in his opinion,  knowledge does not consist in a theoretical vision of its object independent from action and without intrinsic connection to action, a vision that corresponds to what is the case, but in “knowledge acquired through practice and which cannot be articulated explicitly,” as Kuhn very compactly puts it. It can be acquired only through practice because it results from the internalization by the agent of his own practice, and this internalization is practical, that is, is instrumental for the agent’s action in the appropriate circumstances. Hence knowledge cannot be articulated in statements, it can be manifested to others only through action, and disposition to act in a certain way.It cannot be articulated explicitly because it involves no object known, for it is not about anything as object known. Scientific knowledge is linguistic

Scientific knowledge is linguistic in two ways: it is acquired linguistically (as all human knowledge is, for nothing in science is or can be known directly or without the mediation of language , and it is transmitted linguistically, by means of statements that refer to the world of our experience as agents. Thus the way we mean through language is of central importance for our interpretation of science.

If it were explicitly articulated, it would have to  be articulated in a statement or statements that would refer objectively –and this would amount to a return to Searle’s view and to the abandonment of Polanyi’s conception of knowledge as instrumental and practical.. According to Polanyi , knowledge is manifested not in words that say the object but in the  appropriate conduct of the agent in respect of the object, in concrete circumstances. What the agent learns in knowing is not what the object is either essentially or descriptively, but how the object behaves in the world of exposure and, implicitly, how to conduct himself or herself in respect of the object under concrete circumstances to achieve his or her goals.. Now this can only be obtained through practical internalization , and can only reach fruition and be manifested in an agent’s conduct. In short , the reason  why knowledge is tacit and can only be acquired through practice is that it is not theoretical but practical-instrumental , essentially connected with action .

Now, Searle separates completely knowledge from action. He makes the understanding by which something becomes known the distinctive effect of ALL linguistic acts, whereas he makes the understanding that consists in an action (or an affection) totally different from knowledge and  the exclusive effect of those particular speech acts called perlocutionary. Moreover, he claims that this latter effect is parasitic on the former, that is, on knowledge,  whereas the illocutionary effect is independent from the perlocutionary one, that is, from action or affection. However, in chapter II I showed that objective knowledge of what the speaker means is not the effect intended by competent speakers and ‘understood’ by competent hearers even in the case of statements, which because of their enunciative appearance are the best candidates to represent Searle’s contention that in all cases saying expresses the intention of making something known. In showing that, my purpose was to point out that the illocutionary effect, the mere intention of making known what the speaker has in mind to communicate, is an illusion. In other words, the proper understanding or’ reading’ of all ORAL OR DIRECTLY COMMUNICATIVE speech acts, statements included , is applicative and is fulfilled or realized either by the addressee’s actual action intended by the speaker or, in case of delayed application for lack of the appropriate opportunity, by the habitual disposition to act in that manner at the appropriate time.

Concerning the case of speech acts in which perlocutionary effects are intended by the speaker , my point was that the correct ‘comprehension’ of such acts does not consist in an objective awareness that reproduces or represents what the speaker means-says, but an action or an affection in which the awareness is not focused upon as object to be known, but functions instrumentally to produce the affection or the action intended by the speaker .   Concerning the case of statements, the only speech act in which the transmission of knowledge (of object) appears to be exclusively intended, my point was that even in this case, at least in perceptual statements, the appropriate understanding consists in an action, too, not in a representational vision of object. Thus , when I claim that Kuhn rejects..., what I have to show is that the underlying conception of knowledge in the textbook view is contemplative and representational rather than instrumental, whereas Kuhn shows that scientific knowledge is, instrumental,  practical-applicative – a program or map for action to be judged by its effectiveness, rather than a theoretical view of object to be appraised by its resemblance.

S peaking of Searle in chapter I, I said that he had confused the theoretical interiorization he makes of the rules of language or of baseball when he reflects and thereby makes of the players’ conducts an object of study, thereby taking in respect of them the position of an observer rather than that of a player, whose result is the formulated rules, with the practical interiorization of the practice by the agent, which results from practicing the game, and whose result is not anything said but the habitual ability to play the game manifested in the actual playing of the game, not in the ability to state rules but in the ability to practice successfully and even easily the scientific game. The former kind of interiorization results in judgments-statements that supposedly correspond to what is the case. The latter kind of interiorization results , not in the enunciation of rules but in the agent’s habitual ability to play the game. That is the way children learn to play baseball, and to speak  their native language, and to read – by practicing its application. That, too, is the way in which students learn physics (and even geometry): by solving concrete problems , that is, by doing science the way scientists do it.

Application is action or project of action at the appropriate time

One of the most distinctive features of Kuhn’s view of science is that he sees it not as an activity that yields a view of the universe that corresponds to what is the case, but as a problem-solving activity. In other words, the scientific activity is not for the sake of gaining knowledge as traditionally conceived, that is, as a view that exactly or at least with increasing approximation reflects and corresponds to what is the case, but as an activity that functions as an increasingly more efficient instrument for problem-solving.  Now, as we shall see, the difference between these two conceptions of science is the fundamental difference between the textbook image of science and Kuhn’s own view.  

Classical epistemology conceives knowledge, and perception in particular, as the activity by means of which, either through the senses or the intellect, the knower becomes aware of what is there as it actually is, without addition or subtraction that modifies it, that is, without interpretation. However interpretive the epistemic activity may be, its end result has to be an epistemic reproduction – be it a pictorial copy or a merely symbolic but equivalent interpretation–  of its object, thanks to which the knower becomes aware of the object as it is. In other words, the ideal of knowledge is to be perfectly transparent. However, knowledge as such is a private property of the knower, in the sense that only the knower becomes aware of the object known. Fortunately, there is such a thing as language to break the ominous solipsistic circle. Indeed, language, when employed appropriately, communicates what the speaker intends to communicate. In other words, in every case of successful communication, language communicates, without addition or subtraction, the knowledge of  what the speaker has in mind and intends to communicate. Such is the case with every speech act, for communication amounts to the acquisition by the addressee of knowledge of what the speaker intends to communicate. However, whereas in all speech acts the speaker intends to communicate the knowledge of what he or she means, only in the case of statements that refer to what is the case in the surrounding world does the speaker intend to communicate the knowledge or awareness he possesses , not of what a speaker says, but of what is the case in reality. And here again, the language of statements has to be perfectly transparent or neutral to communicate without addition or subtraction what is the case in the surrounding world. Now, in opposing the textbook image of science Kuhn is opposing precisely this conception of the functioning of knowledge and of language that he sees implicit in that view.

Now, the conception of knowledge and of language Kuhn rejects corresponds point by point to the conception I ascribed to Searle in the first chapter of his book, and the conception of knowledge and language he adopts corresponds to the one I ascribed to Polanyi. For Searle contends that, in every case of successful communication, the linguistic communicative act consists in the acquisition by the addressee of the REPRODUCTIVE or REPRESENTATIONAL knowledge or awareness of what the speaker intends to communicate, and in the particular case of statements referring to the surrounding world what is gained by the addressee is the REPRESENTATI0NAL and CORRESPONDING knowledge of the surrounding world possessed by the speaker. For my part, as the reader will remember, I found that Searle’s characterizations of both knowledge and linguistic agency are at odds with the actual conduct of the linguistic agents they are supposed to reflect, both in the case in which the speaker intends to obtain perlocutionary effects, as well in the particular case of statements in which the speaker is supposed to intend to transmit exclusively  his objective knowledge of what is the case in reality.

Science as a problem-solving activity

Nowhere in The Structure of Scientific Revolutions does Kuhn explicitly say that science functions as a language, though often he indirectly suggests that it does. ???  Anyway, the fact is that in order to determine the way science functions, throughout his book Kuhn focuses his attention on the statements in which scientific knowledge is concretely embedded, as well as in the way in which those statements function within the scientific enterprise. Are they meant and actually understood representationally or instrumentally-applicatively?   .

focus on scientific statements. ^(F16)

Let us remember from our second chapter that, whatever the case may be with scientific statements, in ordinary language, according to Polanyi, statements function not as objective representations of what is the case but as instruments for (intentional) action. In other words, if statements do indeed enunciate and convey knowledge, then  knowledge does not consist in a theoretical vision of its object but is, rather, intrinsically related to action as its application through practical- instrumental internalization . To remind the reader of a couple of  the examples I gave in chapter II to explain and support Polanyi ’s view of the way in which  statements function in ordinary language, the appropriate linguistic response to the statement, “Your sister is on the phone,” is not, as Searle claims, to comprehend theoretically what is stated, by getting the picture of what is stated (on the assumption that the picture represents and corresponds to what is the case in reality), or even to assent intellectually to the truth of what is stated; the appropriate response is, rather, to act on the instrumental basis of what is stated , that is, to take the phone and talk to your sister. The statement is meant to function as a map “from where” one acts.  Similarly, if I am looking for the hammer and I am told, “the hammer is in the next room,” the appropriate linguistic response is not to get the theoretical picture of what is the case (as postulated by the Serlean representational  model, which separates the illocutionary from the perlocutionary elements, that is, knowledge from action), but to actually go to the next room and take the hammer, as postulated by the instrumental Polanyian model.

These are examples of the way in which ordinary language perceptual statements  function. But the question confronting Kuhn is how statements function in scientific language. That Kuhn does not think that they function according to the Serlean representational model  we already know from his systematic rejection of the textbook image of science, precisely because it assumes the representational model. But where does Kuhn lead us to believe that he finds that the Polanyian model of tacit knowledge corresponds to the actual practice of science? The answer is that this appears in his treatment of science as a problem-solving activity, by contrast to the traditional conception of science as an activity that yields knowledge as correct vision of reality .  

It was only progressively, and rather late, that Kuhn became fully aware of the central importance of problem-solving in science. While the idea was already  there in the original edition of The Structure , published in 1962 (see pp. 47-51), only in the Postscript to the 1969 edition did Kuhn come to the full realization that the notion of science as a problem-solving activity constituted the core of his innovative understanding of science.

Kuhn tells us in the Postscript that “the paradigm as shared example is the central element of what I now take to be the most novel and least understood aspect of this book .” ^(F17) Shared example of what?  A few pages before Kuhn had observed that

“... the term ‘paradigm’ is used in two  different senses. On the one hand, it stands for the entire constellation of beliefs, values, techniques, and so on shared by the members of a given community. On the other, it denotes one sort of element in that constellation, the concrete puzzle solutions which, employed as models or examples, can replace explicit rules as a basis for the solution of the remaining puzzles of normal science ... Philosophically, at least, this second sense of paradigm is the deeper of the two... terms like ‘subjective’ and ‘intuitive’ cannot appropriately be applied to the components of knowledge that I have described as tacitly embedded in shared examples .” ^(F18)

Thus the answer to the question, of what is the paradigm a shared example, is: of the puzzle solutions which, employed as models or examples, can replace explicit rules as a basis for the solution of the remaining puzzles of NORMAL science. For this knowledge is embedded as TACIT KNOWLEDGE in the exemplary puzzle solutions . The somewhat obscure sense of this formulation is clarified a few pages later, where Kuhn explains the relationship between scientific knowledge and the practice of problem-solving through which the students of science learn their scientific trade :

“ Philosophers of science have not ordinarily discussed the problems encountered by a student in laboratories or in science texts , for these are thought to supply only practice in the application of what the student already knows . He cannot, it is said, solve problems at all unless he has first learned the theory and some rules for applying it. ” ^(F19)

The reason why the activity of problem-solving involved in the learning of science by the students has received scant attention is the secondary or subordinate function generally attributed to that activity by the commonly accepted textbook view. According to the textbook image, in order to solve concrete problems in whose solution  a particular scientific law applies, the student first has to know the law (as well as some rules for applying it to the solution of concrete problems ). (Notice how reminiscent this is of Searle’s idea that the knowledge of what is said is primary, and that the perlocutionary effects are dependent on it. Notice also that Hume’s objection to the passage from is to ought, from vision of object to action, goes to the jugular of Searle’s view, which presupposes the possibility of this passage. Finally, notice that, if knowledge is applicative rather than representational, if it functions instrumentally, there is no such passage. The child looking for his toy doesn’t understand theoretically (in t he sense of acquiring a factual  view disconnected from action) that the toy is in the next room; rather his understanding itself consists in and is identical with the practical disposition to go to the next room in search of his toy at the appropriate moment). The reason why the textbook view demands that the law be first understood theoretically is that, since the law supposedly  states what is the case in the real world, the law itself constitutes the piece of knowledge to be acquired by the students. If so, once the students grasp the law, they already possess the  knowledge of the aspect of the universe to which the law refers. Consequently, all that can be gained by the repeated  solution of concrete problems on paper or in the laboratory is mere facility in applying the already acquired knowledge. In other words, in this view, scientific knowledge is prior to, and independent from, its application, that is, from ACTION,whereas action is parasitic on theoretical knowledge.

Kuhn summarizes this aspect of the textbook view as follows: “ Scientific knowledge is embedded in theory and rules; problems are supplied to gain facility in their application. “ Then, by way of contrast , he presents his own position:

I have tried to argue, however, that this localization of the cognitive content of science is wrong . After the student has done many problems, he may gain only added facility by solving more. But at the start and for some time after, doing problems is learning consequential things about nature. In the absence of such exemplars, the laws and theories he has previously learned would have little empirical content .” ^(F20)

A concrete example. f = ma is a linguistic statement. Of course, it differs from the statements of ordinary language in that it is a scientific statement. But it is a statement nonetheless. Therefore it has to function as linguistic statements do. But how do linguistic statements function, according to Kuhn? If statements say or enunciate what is the case in reality because what is said represents and corresponds to what is the case, as Searle claims, then what is stated in the scientific law,   f = ma, itself constitutes the piece of knowledge to be acquired by the student, and the student gains it by “comprehending’ what is stated –to use Searle’s expression. In which case, the knowledge meant by f =  ma is acquired by the student before applying it to the solution of concrete problems and independently of that activity. Such is the understanding of the language of statements assumed by the textbook view of science, whose implicit representational notion of the language of statements is Searlean. If, however, as claimed by Polanyi, statements are not meant to be understood as representations of what is the case, but what is said is rather meant to be internalized and used as an instrument for action, then the instrumental use of what is stated which consists in applying it in the solution of problems constitutes itself the practical knowledge to be gained by the student of physics.

But how can it be determined which of the two views is the appropriate one for the case of scientific statements? By finding out whether, according to Kuhn, scientific statements function as objective or factual representations of what is the case, as implicitly assumed by the Serlean textbook view, or rather as instruments for problem-solving, as Polanyi maintains.

Let us refer again to the statement of Newton’s second law of motion , f = ma. According to Searle, as in the case of any speech act, so too in the case of scientific statements what is said by the speaker needs to be comprehended, in order for the statement to succeed in conveying the knowledge of what the speaker intends to communicate. However, since the speech act at stake is not just any speech act but  a statement, and as statements  happen to be the only speech act that not only enunciates what the speaker intends to communicate but which, in addition,  refers to an actual  state of the world and claims to correspond to what is the case in reality, the statement f = ma demands not only the comprehension but also the assent of the student  to its truth.  Now, as assumed by the textbook view, in the case of scientific statements the assent cannot be given on the basis of authority, not even of Newton’s authority, much less on the teacher’s authority; it has to be given on the basis of compelling evidence of the truth of the law. That is, precisely, according to the textbook view, the necessary function fulfilled function by experiment in science: it proves or renders evident the truth of the stated law, and thereby compels the assent of the scientist to the truth of the law.

Supposedly, then, if the textbook image is correct, it was on the basis of experimental evidence that Newton himself reached the certainty that his hypothetical grasp of f = ma in the case of constrained fall on inclined planes was actually true. Supposedly, too, according to the textbook view, the student will gain in the laboratory, through experimental demonstration, the certain knowledge of the truth of the law.  After that, all that will remain for the student to acquire is facility in applying the law to concrete problems in whose solution the law applies, and the facility will be acquired through the solution of  multiple problems of the kind of those furnished in the textbooks. Well, it is precisely  this localization of knowledge in the assent-compelling experimental proof of the law – a knowledge that is supposedly achieved independently of all application in problem solving and that precedes application – that Kuhn rejects.

Why does Kuhn reject the localization of knowledge by the textbook view? Because he finds  the actual practice of science at odds with this characterization. To begin with, the textbook view collapses into one the two supposedly different  steps: observation and experiment.  The experiment is required to prove the truth of the scientific law. But another step has to precede the experiment, namely, a hypothesis, for otherwise there would be nothing whose truth would have to be proved by experiment, and there would be no point to the experiment. Now, since at the stage of the first step the statement of the law is just a hypothesis, which therefore can be false, before its proven by experiment the stated hypothesis it is not yet knowledge of what actually happens in nature. Moreover, since the hypothesis to be proven by experiment precedes the experiment, it does not result from experiment. From where then does it result? It  results, in the particular case of f = ma , from observation of the phenomenon of mobiles falling down on inclineds, plus understanding by the scientist of a particular constant or regular interrelation among the factors that will be involved in the final formulation of the law, once its truth is proven by experiment. That mathematical formula will state that the critical characteristic of this case of naturally accelerated motion (Galileo’s second theorem) –whatever the cause of the acceleration may be, which is not Galileo’s concern at all– is that the spaces described by a body falling from rest with a uniformly accelerated motion are to each other as the squares of the time periods employed in traversing these distances (Buckley, p. 108).

This analysis shows that the textbook image fails to distinguish the step from which the hypothetical understanding of the law originates from the experiment that proves the truth of the hypothesis, and thereby renders the experiment pointless, which is t he case with the textbook view. Indeed, if the stated law meant as a verbal representation of what is the case in nature, as assumed by the textbook view, then the experimental proof of its truth ought to constitute the first and only necessary step in the teaching and learning of science. The first step, for pedagogical reasons, because it would impress on the students, from the very beginning, the fundamental difference, assumed by the textbook image of science, between scientific knowledge, based on assent-compelling experimental evidence, and all the other kinds of so-called knowledge, of the surrounding world, which actually amount to ungrounded unscientific opinions. The only necessary step, because the law itself constitutes the piece of knowledge to be acquired by the student, and experiment achieves this by proving its truth; the rest is a matter of mere practice in applying the law. However, Kuhn finds, in the actual teaching and learning of science, neither does the experimental proof of the law constitute the first step, nor is it even a required step. Indeed, students do not gain their knowledge by hearing or reading the law enunciated in a statement that they first understand and whose truth is then proven by experiment. In fact, argues Kuhn, THE WORDS THEMSELVES IN WHICH A LAW IS STATED REMAIN PRACTICALLY MEANINGLESS TO THE STUDENT UNTIL THE STUDENTS repeatedly apply the law TO THE SOLUTION OF CONCRETE PROBLEMS. To put it otherwise, the actual meaning of a law is learned not by considering and  comprehending its abstract formulation, as it would be the case if Searle ’s view of the language of statements were correct, but by means of practice in solving problems to which the law applies, which corresponds to Polanyi ’s view:   (see pp. 47-51).

“Scientists, it should already be clear, never learn concepts, laws, and theories in the abstract and by themselves. Instead, these intellectual tools are from the start encountered in a historically and pedagogically prior unit that displays them with and through their applications...the process of learning a theory depends upon the study of applications, including practice [of] problem-solving both with a penl and paper and with instruments in the laboratory. If, for example, thestudent of Newtonian dynamics ever discovers the meaning of terms like ‘force,’ ‘mass,’ ‘space’amd ‘time,’ he does so less from the incomplete though sometimes helpful definitions in his texts than by observing and participating in the application of these concepts to problem-solution. That process of learning by finger exercise or by doing continues throughout the process of professional initiation. ” ^(F21)

The case of Newton’s second law of motion is a particularly telling example, since the way in which f = ma applies to different cases differs for the case of free fall, for the simple pendulum, for a pair of interacting harmonic oscillations, etc. ^(F22) And since f = ma does not itself state how the law applies, expressed in the different mathematical formulae for each of the cases, the way in which the law applies can only be learned through practice in solving problems.

“It is not quite the case that logical and mathematical manipulation are applied directly to f = ma . That expression proves on examination to be a law-sketch or a law-schema.   As the student or practicing scientist moves from one problem situation to the next, the symbolic generalization to which such manipulations apply changes .” ^(F23)

Indeed, as Kuhn observes, the mathematical formulae for the case of free fall, for the case of the simple pendulum, and for the case of interacting harmonic oscillations are different .”Yet, while learning to identify forces, masses, and accelerations in a variety of physical situations not previously encountered, the student has also learned to design the appropriate version of f = ma through which to interrelate them, often a version for which he has encountered no literal equivalent before. How has he learned to do this?” ^(F24) Through the practice of solving problems that teaches the student how they attach to nature:

“That sort of learning” –to use one of  Kuhn’s numerous expressions of the same point– “is not acquired by exclusively verbals means. Rather IT COMES AS ONE IS GIVEN WORDS TOGETHER WITH CONCRETE EXAMPLES OF HOW THEY WORK IN USE, NATURE AND WORDS ARE LEARNED TOGETHER . To borrow once more Michael Polanyi’s useful phrase, what results from this process is ‘tacit knowledge’ which is learned by doing science rather than by acquiring rules for doing it.” ^(F25)  

Without this practice it is impossible to know how f = ma, (or any other symbolic generalization, for that matter) applies to nature, and without knowing that one does not know what the statement of the law really means. The lay person who would imagine that he or she understands Newton’s second law of motion  because he or she supposedly understands the terms involved in the formula f = ma , but who is incapable of solving concrete problems in which the law finds application, is badly mistaken, for understanding the meaning of the statement is understanding how it applies to nature, and this understanding manifests itself not in a theoretical vision of what is stated and is supposedly the case, but in the student’s ability to apply the law appropriately in the solution of concrete problems.

An analogy taken from the field of Euclidian geometry might help us to clarify Kuhn’s thought on this matter . How do teachers test whether or not a student knows his geometry? Not by having the students recite the theorems or even demonstrate their truth on paper, but by having the students solve problems in whose solution the theorems apply. Imagine a student who honestly says and believes he understands perfectly the statement in which any theorem is enunciated, and who can even demonstrate on paper the truth of the statement, but who turns out to be incapable to solve any concrete problem in whose solution that theorem applies. That student will be deemed  by any teacher not to know . But, as Kuhn argues for the case of physics, this is flatly at odds with the textbook image of science, which localizes scientific  knowledge in the grasp of the law and in assent to it based on evidence.  On the other hand, it coheres with Kuhn’s Polanyian conception of science, for whom  the meaning of scientific statements cannot be cashed in terms of a theoretical understanding or comprehension of what is stated, or even in assent to its truth, but only in terms of its application, for the knowledge is meant to be applied and manifests itself in actual application, much as the knowledge conveyed by the statement, “Your sister is on the phone,” is meant to be used instrumentally and applied in the actual conduct of the agent. For what is expressed in a scientific statement  is not theoretical knowledge that... , which enunciates objectively what is the case in reality, but, rather, instrumental  knowledge how  made possible by the practical internalization of what is stated. In short, a statement like, for example, f = ma ,  does not mean as a  representation of what is the case, but as an instrument for the agent’s conduct in solving particular problems using the formula f = ma as an instrument, be it in the solution on paper of an hypothetical problem, be it in the solution of a real problem by the engineer who applies directly to nature his knowledge of physics using the internalized formula.

Some reflection on the analogy or dysanalogy between geometry and physics may help us to understand why Kuhn is not bothered at all by the fact that the students of physics begin by accepting the validity of the statements of physical laws on the authority of their teachers, not on the basis of experimental evidence. The reason is that this is not what happens even in geometry, which to all appearances is the exemplary science – on which Plato first and then Aristotle modeled their views on science, characterized by necessarily true statements obtained through immediate intuition or derived from intuitive statements by strict logical deduction . Now the analogy between physics and  Euclidean geometry  helps us here, for geometry, even more clearly than physics, seems to conform exactly to the textbook view of science rejected by Kuhn. Indeed, the textbooks of Euclidean geometry begin by stating the theorem that enunciates what is the case, and then proceed to the demonstrate its truth. The demonstration constrains the student’s rational assent to the truth of the statement. In this case, though, the demonstration precedes, and is totally independent from , any application of the theorem to the solution of concrete problems. This corresponds exactly to the textbook conception of science.   But the question we have to consider with Kuhn is whether the intuitive demonstration of the truth of a theorem or the experimental proof of a physical law have to precede the application of the theorem of the law to the solution of concrete problems or even whether they are necessary at all. And the answer is that they are not pre-required or even required at all in the entire process. Nor should demonstration be required, according to Kuhn,  for the appropriate response to statements is not assent (which is purely theoretical) to what is stated,, which is all that a demonstration or a proof can produce, but applicative conduct, that is, conduct in which what is stated is internalized by the agent and employed as an instrument to map his or her conduct. By the way, that is why, in the actual practicee of science, a teacher doen’t gaudge the comprehension of the student by the student’s statements or even proofs, but by the students ability to solve problems*  Imagine, indeed, that the student of geometry were taught by authority the Pythagorean theorem , and that the student accepted it on the word of his or her teacher. Would the absence of demonstration prevent the student from applying the theorem to the solution of problems with the same efficiency? No; the teacher’s authority suffices to guide the student as to what to do or expect, as it suffices to guide the child who is learning tho to read. Thus the demonstration of the truth of the theorems, which results in assent,  is superfluous for the practice of geometry, that is, for the application of the theorems to the solution of concrete problems . However, as long as stated theorems or stated physical laws are supposed to enunciate what is the case in reality, the demonstration of their truth appears to be absolutely required.* But once it is realized that the stated theorems or laws do not mean as representational enunciations of what is the case but as instruments to be internalized by the agent and  used in the solution of problems, the demonstration of their correspondence to what is the case becomes superfluous.

Mutatis mutandis: Dramatic statements and scientific statements

Referring to f = ma I said, “much as is the case with any other statement.” But that needs qualification, the qualification I suggested by adding mutatis mutandis . What I wanted to say is that the statement is ultimately to be cashed, as any other ORAL statement of a worldly fact, in terms of practical expectation and corresponding conduct of the engineer who applies the law to the realization of a project that satisfies a desire, rather than in terms of theoretical assent to what is stated . Analogously, the statement “Your toy is in the next room” is to be cashed by the child in terms of actually  going, or being disposed to go under the appropriate circumstances, to the next room to get the toy he wants. This, I think with Polanyi, is the case with all statements: they, ultimately,  mean instrumentally and, therefore, tacitly . Only through practice does the student learn how the law applies, that is, the concrete meaning of the law.

On the other hand, however, it is undeniable that there  are significant differences between the statement, “Your toy is in the next room,” and the statement, f = ma. For one, the discovery that the toy is in the next  room is not discovery of any regularity or constancy but of a purely accidental event registered by exposed agents who understand toys and children and who do not abstract from their living with others in the space of exposure. By the same token, the discovery  is not the result of a systematic, scientific, that is, ABSTRACTIVE, quest that leaves out of consideration the ordinary perception of involved agents , but of ordinary linguistic perception by exposed agents with inherited expectations modified by experience. By contrast, the immediate connection between knowledge and conduct effected by need and desire and inherited mechanism is systematically relaxed in the case of science by systematic ABSTRACTION FROM EXPOSURE, which results in attention to the (regular or constant) behavior of the thing observed by itself without immediate relationship to desire and fear .

In this context, it is to be remarked that, in the case of humans, by contrast to animals, the immediate connection between perception and applicative action is relaxed thanks to QUASI CONCEPTUAL LANGUAGE, as indicated by the difference between connotation (which refers to the quasi concept) and denotation (which, under the appropriate conditions, refers to the presence of the concrete object of desire or fear), as well as the actual relationship between agent and world made conceivable by the rift between concept and reality, or more concretely between potential and actual . (As noted by Piaget in The Psychology of Intelligence (pp.6-97), even in the case of rather primitive animals action is not automatically repetitive; its repetition is  dependent upon the actual exposural or dramatic perception of the object of desire and fear to which it is associated. That is why, as I observed in To Be Human , not even B. F. Skinner can condition oxygen and hydrogen not to combine to form water, for they are insensitive, that is, cannot be affected by what happens to them or is done to them). People, however, have an advantage over animals, which shows glaringly the practice of science. Because enriching the dramatic situational ‘concept ’ is possible for them through connotation, ‘disinterested curiosity’ through abstraction becomes possible.

What do I mean by quasi conceptual language, and by the dramatic situational concept?    Pace  Plato , and Aristotle, and scholastics in general, the concept is not concept of the object as object of perception or of predication (cf. the Aristotelian categories of SUBSTANCE , and the consequent stated in the formula (for no reason is grasped why the relationship should be so rather than otherwise). There is just practical habitual   expectation  –an extension of the inherited expectation revealed by the behavior of animals– that precisely such and such will happen under such hypothetical conditions, for expectation and the consequent conduct is what the statement of the relationship among the three factors means to produce (as in the case of what Searle calls “perlocutionary effects”) . In other words, Galileo did not himself understand or mean his law as something stated  to be assented to –for neither did he grasp, nor did he mention– any intelligible reason why it should be so– , but as a sort of map for practical application ,  much as is the case with distinction between natural and accidental properties, one of which happen to be action and passion). Within this conceptual constellation belong the notions  of objective existence and of realistic correspondence between knowledge and what is the case, and from this constellation is excluded all relation to desire and fear, which is replaced by the notion of “object’ or, at best by that of   behavior of the seen-observed. Desire and fear (and all other emotions) belong in the conceptual constellation of exposure and interaction, first in immediate relation to the exposed agent with inherited expectation of regularity of that by whose action the agent is affected, and then, thanks to abstraction, of  action or interactive behavior of that with other behaving animate or inanimate agents, and of all objective agents among themselves, but without immediate relation to the exposed agent. In the latter case, the immediate relationship to the agent is provisionally relaxed through abstraction from exposure , but it remains implicit in the grasp that the ‘objective’ (situational?) interaction, which is the abstract object of science, ultimately takes place in the dramatic space we inhabit as agents-patients, which is sometimes purposefully tailored by scientific application.*

* The type of abstraction involved in the case of maps of  particular places is enlightening concerning scientific statements. For, like scientific statements, a map refers to object in that it is the map of a place, but it is addressed only to those who are actually or at least potentially interested in or desirous of reaching a place within the space mapped, which is the field of their action and exposure . In order to make it actually function as a map, the interested agents have to situate themselves within the map in relation to the place they want to reach. This is APPLICATION. AND APPLICATION IS TACIT, in that no map can make explicit these data or furnish the desire to reach a particular place. The same with physical laws. As  map, the laws mean applicatively, not representationally, and they mean under the provisional regime of relaxed connection with action, created by abstraction. As for whole theories by contrast to laws, they are general maps, like a mapa mundi, not in order for an agent to reach a particular place but to get a general idea of posibilities.  

What now about the difference between perceptual statements of ordinary language, such as “the toy is in the next room,” and scientific statements, such as   f = ma ? To deal with this issue we have to consider the role that desire (or fear) play in the case of ordinary language statements and in the case of scientific statements . Let us consider first the case of ordinary language perceptual statements which, by virtue of the indexicality of perception, point to the subject ass exposed agent.

As realized by Aristotle in relation to his question why statements are followed by action only in certain cases,  the child of my above example has to desire to find his toy and be actively looking for it in order for the statement, “the toy is in the other room,” to constitute pertinent knowledge and lead the child to action. For without desire or fear of something no state of the world, even if seen, can motivate the agent to act, as realized by Aristotle in De motu animalium. ^(F26) In other words, for a piece of knowledge to actually function as knowledge, that is, practically, instrumentally, the referent has to be desired as a means for an end desired by the agent. Only on this condition will the piece of knowledge function as knowledge., that is, instrumentally rather than objectively. Now, since the child lives immediately in the perceptual world of exposure, when mediated by desire the knowledge constituted by the child’s understanding of the statement will function as the instrument that  results in the  action of going to the next room. But the adult, thanks to connotation , can think of the object without desiring it, or independently of whether or not he desires it, and independently of having or not having the means to cope with the object.

This is a case of Aristotle’s practical syllogism , except that being practical the syllogism is tacit, and is valid only for the desirous agent in the appropriate situation . ^(F27) However, animals do not participate in this quasi syllogistic dimension “ nisi parum ,” (in a limited way), as Aquinas puts it commenting on Aristotle’s notion of practical knowledge. ^(F28) For in the case of the adult human agent his syllogistic dimension is mediated by language, and this allows for a reflective distance or “nihilation” (that makes possible  the choice of a goal)  between the instrumentally known and action, a distance that is not found in animals. Indeed, in the appropriate circumstantial setting, as observed by Nussbaum, the animal will always act according to the strongest current desire. This intrinsic connection between desire and knowledge implies that the animal can only learn what is in direct relation with its desires or needs. In the  animal’s case, knowledge is directly wired to the motor centers in the appropriate circumstances. But language changes this direct and immediate relationship to actual action in the case of people . Indeed,  language makes possible for people to acquire potentially instrumental knowledge of the world of perception even in the absence of a direct relationship of what is known to the needs or desires of the human agent. That is why a dog immediately wags its tail at the mention of its master’s name, in expectation of his imminent presence, whereas thanks to potentially instrumental connotation we people can always ask, “what about him?” referring to the named person, and thus learn about that person in his or her absence, when what is learned has no immediate instrumental relationship to the satisfaction of our needs.*

* However, what is learned or known in the case of a particular observable is never an object-substance (like Plato’s ‘Ideas,’ and like Aristotle’s abstract ‘substances’ or objects of strictly theoretical predication), but the way in which the objects of present or potential exposure act and

t he effects they produce (which may potentially affect the agent, given that perception is practical-indexical).

ABSTRACTION : So much for the role of desire in the case of ordinary language statements . Let us come now to the role of desire in the case of scientific statements. Let us keep in mind, in the first place, that knowledge is never objective, that is, a pure neutral  representation of the object, but always instrumental. Therefore it is always knowledge of objects of at least possible exposure, of objects that can be connected with desire or fear in relation to the agent’s goals . By the same token, these objects are never abstract Aristotelian substances as subjects of abstract predicates, but concrete things whose predicates are the ways in which those perceived things act in the agent's dramatic space and time, and to whose action we actually are exposed or at least can be exposed . Let us keep in mind, too, that there is such a thing as linguistic connotation. In our particular case, connotation is TACITLY inscribed in our bodies as centers of practical indexicality and amounts to a practical tacit schema of the ways in which things act. Now, in the case of higher animals, by a HEREDITARY MECHANISM that involves association thanks to desire or fear, the connection of the actual exposure to the desired or feared object to the motor centers is direct. In people, though, even in the case of the immediately exposed agent, the direct connection is to the connoted way of acting of the object, and then, through the connoted way of acting, to the motor centers and action, if and when mediated by desire or fear. Hence the provisional separation or distancing of knowledge from action (of connotation from practical denotation of the presence or imminent presence of the object) , which opens the way to the apparently purely curious observation of the scientist. In the process of scientific observation, the scientific agent is not directly exposed, or at least does not take his or her exposure into consideration. AS A SCIENTIST. HE ABSTRACTS FROM HIS IMMEDIATE EXPOSURE TO CONCENTRATE ON THE WAY THE observed object ACTS INDEPENDENTLY OF HIS DESIRES OR FEARS. (Think, for instance, of the people who track tornados to gain observational data on them). This amounts to a neutralization of desire and fear, which consequently will not guide the observation, at least not directly. In the case of perceptual statements in pressence (oral statements), all the connoted traits, which will be later stated in communicative statements to others, will refer to the way the object  acts; they will not be essential or even accidental   predicates proper to ‘substances .’ Moreover, at least in physics (for this will vary from science to science, and is in fact very different in human biology and in medicine), the observation will preferentially be of uniform behaviors of things, on the Humean assumption that the future will resemble the past, and in the hope of being prepared to deal with the future.

Nonetheless, the separation between knowledge and action is only provisional, for at least two reasons . Firstly, the original observations of the way things behave are observations of the behavior of ‘real’ concrete things, that is, of things in the space of exposure, of things to whose action we can be either directly or indirectly exposed, however much the intervening abstraction will seem to isolate them. Secondly, since knowledge is always instrumental, not representational,  it is always at least  potentially applicative and its fruition will come with application, not with vision. Now the application will, ultimately, involve a return to the agent’s dramatic space . In the case of scientific knowledge, this return will be gradual: first, through the symbolic solution of problems on paper (which are symbolic projects of action), then through the actual application in the dramatic space by the engineer, the physician, the applied geneticist. In the latter case the return to dramatic space will be accompanied by the correlative re-emergence of desire or fear, which will show that the separation of knowledge from desire was merely provisional and, thus, merely apparent.

But, then, why the insistence in rational demonstration in geometry, as well as in experimental proof in physics and in the other so-called experimental sciences? Because of a preconceived idea of knowledge, and of scientific knowledge in particular, uncritically accepted for centuries, and  extended from logic and geometry to the empirical sciences when the latter emerged. The originator of the idea I am referring to was Plato, who followed on the footsteps of his master Socrates, who himself wanted to substitute the blind opinions of the sophistic rhetoricians concerning the way to educate the youth of Athens, with genuine knowledge, that is, necessarily true knowledge. Socrates failed, for near his death he had to confess the he did not know, but that he was superior to his adversaries at least in that he knew that he did not know, whereas his adversaries believed that they knew when in fact they had only blind opinions. Plato held to the Socratic dream of attaining necessarily true knowledge, which he called scientific knowledge ( sophia , which Aristotle later transformed into episteme ), in order to distinguish it from all other kind of so-called knowledge. The many varieties of non-scientific or non-necessarily ture knowledge Plato called  – doxa – (belief or opinion)  which, in his eyes, was merely apparent knowledge because of its lack of necessary truth. Like Plato, those who hold the textbook image of science assume that scientific statements enunciate and necessarily correspond to what is the case. Moreover, like Plato, they believe that the difference between scientific statements and any other statements is that the former are necessarily true by virtue of their evidence (be it deductive by virtue of evident principles, or experimental) that constrains assent, which of course contrasts with the assent without sufficient evidence or on the basis of mere authority of any other so-called knowledge. Unlike in geometry, however, in the experimental sciences the truth of their statements cannot be demonstrated a priori (or as Hume claims, by mere combination of a priori ideas), but only a posteriori, that is empirically or by experiment.

Helen Keller

Perceptual statements of particular states of the perceived world refer the subject to the object practically, not theoretically. But human agents are linguistic. Thus they refer themselves, or are referred to the world, practically but linguistically, that is, not by immediate connection to their motor centers, but by means of a connoted concept that they enrich not only through their own experience but through other people’s experience transmitted linguistically. Helen Keller learned simultaneously that the object she had touched and of which she now had a ‘concept’ was water (the thing she desired to drink when thirsty and used for her bath, etc.), that the thing had a name mediated by a concept , that she could refer herself to that object in its absence and without immediate and explicit relationship to action, in the thinking, abstractive mode, that other things had a CONNOTATING  name she could learn, and that she could refer other people and be referred by other people to that object and to all the other possible objects of her experience that had or could acquire a name . Until then Keller’s experience of water had been animal experience related, thanks to memory of the object experienced, to actual or imminent presence and action in response in the appropriate circumstances. This action was either dictated by instinct or by selective past experience. Now her action could be directed by the experience of others that gave her orders for action, or by herself.       

This is how Helen Keller acquired her knowledge of water together with the word. ^(F29) As we all learn the use of language and the world together . Statements, Polanyi told us, the speech acts in which the speaker expresses what he or she knows, do not mean representationally; they mean applicatively, that is, they are intrinsically connected with action. And that is the case, not only when others intend to communicate the knowledge of their communicative intentions or of the surroundign world to us, but also when we express or enunciate  to ourselves what we ourselves discover through application. For the only way we linguistic agents know is linguistic, by expressing or enunciating  in silent or internal  words-judgments that mean applicatively (when they refer to particular states or events in the world)  what we came to know through application. If a child who is looking for a toy hears or even repeats to himself the words, “your toy is in the next room,” but does not go to the next room to get the toy, that is, does not hear instrumentally what is said to him by others or what he says to himself, this child is not, at least not yet. a competent linguistic agent, that is, does not understand either language or what language refers to. In this regard, Kuhn tells us, scientific statements [ALWAYS PARTICULARIZED IN THE CASE OF PROBLEM-SOLVING] are like ordinary language statements, for they  mean applicatively, too, that is, refer potentially the agent to objects of interaction, however much scientific statements may differ from ordinary language statements in other regards. When Newton told himself, f = ma, he was telling himself  tacitly as a competent linguistic user (and later telling others) how the universal statement, --universal  because of the factual constancy of nature-- applied to nature, as when I tell a child who is a competent speaker of English,"Your toy is in the next room," I am telling him TACITLY how the statement applies to nature. [FOR LEARNING TO STATE AND TO BE THE ADDRESSEE OF STATMENT IS THE SAME AS LEARNING HOW STATEMENTS APPLY]. For Newton's statement meant applicatively, and Newton had learned that through a series of applications of the law in solving concrete problems . This is the way Newton reached the formulation of the law, as the law of his own practical expectations conerning a natural phenomenon or event..   Because the statement of the law meant applicatively rather than representationally, it implied, for instance (whether or not Newton himself  worked out that particular implication through the solution of concrete problems) that it had to be expected  that a mobile falling down an inclined would reach in an opposite inclined the same vertical height it initially occupied in the first inclined, whatever the inclination of the opposite inclined might be. Understanding this, however, as Kuhn observes, ^(F30)is not a matter of understanding what the words themselves mean, but being able to foresee and predict what to expect, as a competenent user of language, in a variety of situations not previously seen as instantiations of the law, given such and such hypothetical conditions of mass or of acceleration –  conditions that on paper can be symbolically modified at will. That is problem solving on paper. Problem solving in real life is either foreseeing and predicting the height a comcrete mobile will reach, given such conditions of mass and acceleration, and being prepared to deal with that fact in a given particular situation in order to obtain the desired outcome, or creating the appropriate mass and acceleration (for instance, by hydraulic means) to have a mobile reach the desired height.

Kuhn and Polanyian tacit knowledge

The mention of Polanyi’s name precisely in connection with tacit knowledge is far from casual. Nor is it, on Kuhn’s part, a matter of simply borrowing the name of tacit knowledge from Polanyi for his own different purposes. In fact, Kuhn mentions Polanyi’s name several times throughout his book, ^(F31) and always to acknowledge that his own views on scientific knowledge coincide with Polanyi’s notion of tacit knowledge. Actually, Kuhn coins as good and compact a definition of tacit knowledge as I have ever met: “ knowledge acquired through practice and that cannot be articulated explicitly. ” ^(F32)  Which fits exactly Kuhn’s own notion of knowledge in the practice of physics, the very notion we have been studying in the last two pages or so. Therefore, to linger a little longer on the kinship between Polanyi’s and Kuhn’s views might throw some light on the most important point of Kuhn’s book, to wit: the way in which he conceives scientific knowledge.

PARADIGMS ARE TACIT: UINDERSTANDING THAT RESULTS FROM PRACTICAL HABITUAL INTERNALIZATION OF THE ACTION, WITHOUT EXPLICIT RULES, LIKE FINDING ONE’S WAY WITHOUT A MAP THROUGH MERE PRACTICAL FAMILIARITY WITH A TERRITORY, VS. THEORETICAL, OBJECTIVE UNDERSTANDING: The paradigm is not a specific view of what is or what has to be the case in every observable instance, supplemented by a set of specified or even specifiable rules to apply the law to the solution of concrete problems on paper, in the laboratory, or in engineering reality. Paradigms are undefinable and scientists may not be in agreement even as to what exactly constitutes the vision in which the paradigm supposedly consists. Students do not learn the laws by becoming acquainted with their abstract formulation, and not even by experimental demonstration of the truth of the law in the laboratory. Rather,  the validity of the abstract formulation is accepted by the students on the authority of their teacher, and they learn its practical meaning by the protracted practice of applying it to the solution of concrete problems. For the meaning of a law does not refer to an observable phenomenon whose actual observation  leads to intuition-assent to what the law states. Rather, the law functions as a tool that guides the scientist in its application to the solution of concrete problems.

Hence the most important meaning of “paradigm” is concrete instances of solution of a n exemplary problem –a solution that becomes a model  of the way the law applies, for a particular scientific community that learns the tacit paradigm through the same kind of applications. Even though  all the members of the specialized scientific communities of physicists learn quantum mechanics, they learn it through different applications and, thus, have different paradigms that guide their applications (pp. 49-50).  Hence the paradigm is not a view but an exemplary case of problem-solving. The exemplary or paradigmatic case, in a tacit way and learned through practice, tacitly indicates how it applies.  But it does not TELL or ENUNCIATE how. That is to be learned through the practice of solving concrete problems. Like driving a car, applying a law in problem-solving is a practical skill. The experiment, like the demonstration in geometry, creates the illusion of rendering intuitive the validity of the stated law or theorem.  

(3) RULES: The paradigmatic law demands application in practice (by the agent who has the practical expectations created by it), but  not observational correspondence, for it doesn’t mean objectively but instrumentally, applicatively . Understanding the law is knowing how to apply it to the solution of problems. The paradigmatic law guides the scientists in their application of the paradigm to the solution of particular problems, but not by telling or enunciating how to apply it.

A different meaning of paradigm as a general view arises from the reduction of different cases as similar, to one unifying law. This meaning is possibly connected with the kind of entities that populate the universe. Its guiding role is similar to that of  the practical paradigm of playing chess in the case of an expert player. The expert player follows no rules, for he or she doesn’t know them. It is the role of the tacit habit acquired through practice.

(X) THE ROLE OF PROBLEM-SOLVING, THAT IS, OF HOW AND TO WHAT THE LAW OR THE THEORY APPLIES, IS MISINTERPRETED AND DEEMED SECONDARY IN THE TEXTBOOK VIEW, AS THE ILLOCUTIONARY PERLOCUTIONARY EFFECTS WHOSE REALIZATION MANIFESTS THE APPROPRIATE LINGUISTIC UNDERSTANDING IS DEEMED SECONDARY AND LINGUISTICALLY ACCIDENTAL BY SEARLE . For the textbook view, knowledge consists in the correspondence of what is stated and what is actually the case, on the level of intuitive perception or innocent observation, on the level of hypothesis-directed prediction, and on the level of the observed universe of our common experience (WHICH SEEMS TO BE THE ONE OF THE ‘IRRREGULARITIES.’

If this location of knowledge is correct , the student who has understood the stated law already knows what is to be known. Hence application to problem solving is epistemically superfluous. A mere luxury in the scientific practice. BUT, claims Kuhn, this localization of knowledge is belied by the facts. The law is not descriptive of what is the case, for it does not say or enunciate what is the case, but applicative, a matter of TACIT KNOWLEDGE. No passage from is to ought is required , for the knower is not a contemplator but an exposed, involved, interested agent who wants to be in control of the surrounding world as much as possible (rather than being at the mercy of it), and who has learned the dilatory trick of useful abstraction of immediate exposure in preparation for the real moment of application not in paper or in the laboratory but in real life.

This may be illustrated by an example. According to Searle ’s conception or notion of the

linguistic activity , as we saw it in the first chapter, linguistic communication between speaker and addressee consists in the acquisition by the addressee of knowledge of  what the speaker intends to communicate when he says something. By “knowledge” Searle means accurate view or consciousness of object gained by the addressee of what the speaker intends to communicate by comprehending, that is,   understanding or gaining the representational awareness of what is said-enunciated by the speaker . Such is the idea of linguistic communication reflected in Searle’s general “characterization” of the linguistic activity as well as in his particular characterizations of what is going on,  in the case of the diverse speech acts, when a speaker says something to an addressee. Polanyi , for his part, has a very different conception of the linguistic activity , for he does not think that the speaker’s intention in saying is to communicate the factual objective knowledge of what he intends to say when he says something. The speaker’s intention is, rather, either to affect in a particular way the addressee, or to induce the addressee to act in a certain way.

Now, it might seem that the difference in the way a practice –in this case, the linguistic communicative practice–  is conceived by the people who engage in the practice has no importance at all in that it does not affect the actual practice. Indeed, though their respective conceptions of the linguistic practice differ, nevertheless as linguistic agents both Searle and Polanyi seem to act linguistically in the same way. But such is not the case. It is true that, to take a particular instance, both Searle and Polanyi expect to be obeyed when they give an order to their young children, though Searle thinks that what he intends is to communicate the knowledge of the order he is giving, not directly his interlocutor’s obedience. The addressee’s obedience is conditioned by the knowledge of what the speaker means, and  whether or not the obedient action  obtains is irrelevant to the success of the linguistic act as such, for it aims essentially  at communicating the knowledge of what the speaker means.

However, it is not true that the fact that Searle’s idea of the linguistic practice differs from Polanyi’s makes no difference. If Searle were consistent with his conception of the linguistic activity, he should expect his child to understand that in giving an order to his child Searle intended to say or enunciate an order to be understood and known, not directly an order to be obeyed. Accordingly, Searle should consider his child’s obedience as irrelevant, as long as the child acknowledged the order received. In fact, my argument against Searle in Chapter I was that, given his conception of the linguistic practice, Searle should be perfectly satisfied as a linguistic agent if, instead of obeying his order,  his child just told him that he understood the order he had received, for Searle intended to obtain nothing else and nothing more by his linguistic command. Of course, my claim as a competent linguistic agent is that  neither Searle nor any other linguistic agent in the described situation would be satisfied with that response.

But how, concretely, do physicists do physics? The whole of Kuhn’s argument shows that

Let us be as precise as possible. KUHN LIMITS HIS EXPLORATION TO THE FIELD OF THE EXPERIMENTAL SCIENCES. One of his main conclusions is that, in this particular field, there is no such thing as knowledge as accurate representation of reality. Whether or not the conception of knowledge as accurate representation of what is the case finds application in other fields is not his business at all. He is not doing what might be called general epistemology or theory of knowledge. He is not attacking the epistemological notion or knowledge as accurate representation of reality in general. His point is simply that the notion of knowledge as accurate representation of reality finds no application in the domain of the experimental sciences, and of physics in particular. Now, though Kuhn is not doing epistemology or general theory of knowledge, nonetheless his position has great epistemological significance for at least two reasons. First,  the physical experimental sciences constitute the supreme model of scientific knowledge. Consequently, if the traditional notion of knowledge finds no application in what is supposed to be its supreme instantiation, there is little chance of its functioning in other fields. Two, the traditional notion of knowledge expelled by Kuhn from the  field of the experimental sciences has, as a matter of fact, been under attack, and has already been banned by many, from other fields, among which Kuhn himself mentions “parts of philosophy, psychology, linguistics, and even art history,” ^(F35) in which one would expect the traditional  notion of knowledge to be functional and  find application. PLATO ON KNOWLEDGE ADN OPINIONT. To distinguish the two, at least in name, Plato called the former “opinion” and the latter “knowledge,” and he tackled the problem of discerning the two in the following way. Only those realities can be object of “knowledge” which are absolutely unchangeable or permanent because they exist necessarily and are necessarily as they are. For if the knower grasps such necessity, then the knower grasps that what he or she knows necessarily corresponds to what is the case. Unfortunately, no sensible or perceivable object is of that kind, for all of them are subject to change in the flux of time. In other words, all sensible objects are contingent rather than necessary. Thus my knowledge of them, even assuming that my perceptions actually corresponded at a certain instant to what is the case, ceases to be true as the perceived objects change. Consequently Plato felt constrained to relegate all the particular objects of sensible perception to the realm of opinion or merely putative knowledge. Furthermore,  he determined that only the intellect (as opposed to the senses), which was capable of grasping what is necessarily the case, could yield knowledge (as opposed to opinion), and only in the case of the intelligible (as opposed to sensible) realities, for by the very fact of being purely intelligible rather than material these intelligible objects are not subject to temporal change. The notion of “scientific knowledge,” that is, of a knowledge whose unshakeable, objective certainty is of a different kind than the merely subjective feeling of certainty that accompanies  what is ordinarily taken for knowledge, has its origin in Plato.

As to the avatars of the notion of knowledge as correspondence with what is the case in reality, and the intense scrutiny and criticism to which it has been subjected for centuries, the story has been told a hundred times. For my purpose, it may suffice to say that Kuhn is not alone, by far, in finding such notion defective. It is fair to say that Kant, who gave up on the notion of knowledge as correspondence with the real on the basis that, since we have no independent means of access to the real other than knowledge, we cannot possibly find out whether or not what we take for knowledge actually corresponds [ontologically and representationally? There is a difference, though, between the signs on a musical partition and  linguistic signs, be they written or spoken. Such, at any rate, is Searle’s conviction, who expresses the difference  as follows: words are the only signs that not only mean, that is, refer to something, which is the distinctive characteristic of all signs, but the only signs that mean by saying or enunciating what they mean. [THAT IS, BY MAKING OBJECTIVELY KNOWN WHAT THEY MEAN]. Linguistic communication, therefore, amounts, on the speaker’s side, to saying what he or she means, and, on the interlocutor’s side, to gaining the knowledge of what the speaker means by comprehending it. True, by saying something the speaker may want to produce or obtain a number of other effects in addition to making known what he or she means, as is the case with all the speech acts that intend perlocutionary effects on the different speech acts, but the only effect the speaker cannot fail to want to produce when he or she says something is making known what he or she means, which is Searle’s “illocutionary effect.” Not only is this effect essential in the sense that this is what communication is primarily about, to wit, making known what one means to communicate, but also in the sense that the illocutionary effect is the necessary condition for the realization of  all the other effects the speaker may intend to obtain. Indeed, if I make a request, my request cannot be granted by the addressee unless he or she knows what I say, the request that I make. Having understood my request, the addressee may or may not grant it, but certainly he or she cannot grant it if he or she does not know my request. Saying, therefore, is intending to make known what one means. And this implies that what is said is always something to be known. Now, since the distinctive characteristic of language is expressing what it means, it follows that saying is always an enunciative speech act that expresses what the speaker intends to make known . Sometimes, though not always, but only in the case in which perlocutionary effects are also intended, the enunciation also comprehends other effects intended by the speaker which are not merely to be known by the addressee but to be executed or suffered by the addressee, such as granting what is requested, or being amused in the case of a joke told.

My point is that Searle makes of all speech acts enunciative acts, for what he primarily  understands by saying is “enunciating” what one means in order to make it known. Thus, requesting is enunciating a request, threatening is enunciating a threat, promising is enunciating a promise... in order to make them known to the addressee. Hence what Searle means by saying that the distinctive feature of linguistic signs is that they say what the speaker means is that they enunciate what the speaker means.

Does it follow from this that Searle reduces all the speech act to statements? No, because ‘saying’ and ‘enunciating’ are the same thing, but not ‘saying’ and ‘stating.’ What distinguishes the enunciative sentences of which Aristotle speaks in his Peri hermeneias is that not only do they enunciate or say or put in words what the speaker wants to communicate by making it known, but that, in addition ,  they convey the speaker’s claim that what is said is true . Now for that claim to be possible the sentence has to refer to something as being or not being the case in reality. This is what Aristotle called, on the speaker’s part as a knower, a “judgment.” Obviously, however, as manifested by multitude of perlocutionary speech acts, such is not the case with the majority of the meaningful sentences through which a speaker intends to make known to the addressee what he or she wants to communicate; only statements fulfill this condition. In short, all meaningful speech acts express or enunciate what the speaker intends to communicate or make known, but only statements express what is the case in reality as judged by the speaker, as well as the speaker’s commitment to the truth of what he affirms or denies.

Let us contrast this –that is enunciations and statements– with the signs on a musical partition to distinguish the latter from linguistic signs . In Aristotle’s and Searle’s view, what is stated corresponds to what is the case in the sense of saying it so as to make it known, for that is what the truth of judgments consists in. Certainly musical signs, unlike linguistic signs, do not say what they mean, for they do not enunciate the music. Actually, they don’t say anything , for they do not enunciate anything. YET THEY MEAN SOMETHING, for they are signs. Now we tend to think that the pianist understands and interprets the signs by actually producing the music meant by the musician. If so, what the signs on the partition mean is the music played by the musician. The musician understands the signs when he ‘reads’ them by imagining the music, as Beethoven.... But such is not the case, for the signs don’t mean in the sense of saying the music, of enunciating something to be known. Rather “reading, “ in this case, consists in the production by the reader, that is, the pianist, of the action that will create the music meant, for such is the intention of the writer of the partition. . The same in the case of “reading” a map that was drawn to give me directions from where I am to where I want to go. The same in the case of “reading” a traffic light: stopping when the light is red is the correct reading or understanding, for such was the intention of the person who set the sign. In all these cases, then the reading is applicative, NOT REPRESENTATIONAL OF WHAT IS THE CASE, AND NOT EVEN IMPERATIVE AS THE LANGUAGE OF A RECIPE THAT ENUNCIATES IN A RULE WHAT IS TO BE DONE. By this I mean that  ‘understanding’ consists in an action, not in a knowing or an understanding thanks to which something becomes objectively known. And such is the case with all the speech acts that aim at producing  a perlocutionay effect. From the speaker’s point of view, the proper understanding of a request is, not the understanding or knowing that a request was made , but the granting of what is requested; the proper understanding of a command is, not the knowledge that a command was made, but the execution of what was commanded. Well, according to Polanyi, the same is the case with statements: they are meant to produce an action.

Thus in its negative aspect Kuhn’s argument can be fairly summarized as a dismissal of the notion of objective knowledge in the area of the experimental physical sciences, which after all is the area in which that notion of knowledge seemed bound to find its field of application par excellence.

But what is the positive aspect of Kuhn’s view of science? In other words, what is the view with which he proposes to substitute the textbook view? Here Kuhn’s answer is bound to appear to be  less definite, for tacit knowledge being tacit, it cannot be enunciated in words to be understood objectively; there the understanding is instrumental . True, he maintains that he is neither a skeptic nor a relativist, but without giving any fully explicit explanation for his denial. In all fairness to him, however, we may acknowledge that implicit explanation may be gathered from the text of The Structure... He is not a skeptic, though he denies that science progresses towards the truth, that is towards ontological correspondence with what is the case, because he is nonetheless convinced that the succession of interpretive paradigms is accompanied by a progress in our problem-solving ability. He is not a relativist, either, because, although he grants that our views of the physical universe change radically with the successive shifts of paradigms, he is convinced nonetheless that not any view will do. As he puts it,

“To say that the members of different groups may have different perceptions when confronted with the same stimuli is not to imply that they may just have any perceptions at all. In many environments a group that could not tell wolves from dogs could not endure. Nor would a group of nuclear physicists today survive as scientists if unable to recognize the tracks of alpha particles and electrons. It is just because very few ways of seeing will do that the ones that have withstood the tests of group use are worth transmitting form generation to generation.” ^(F36)

Kuhn’s entire argument in The Structure consists in showing that the way in which physics is actually practiced is at odds with the way the practice of physics is conceived by the authors of the scientific textbooks. The reason those authors misconceive physics is that they unwittingly work under the constraints of the paradigm of classical epistemology, which determines what Kuhn calls  the textbook view of science. According to this view , the purpose of each science is to gain knowledge, and knowledge is concretely constituted by judgments-statements that represent correctly what is the case in reality. Now, as Plato became acutely aware of it, what appears to be genuine knowledge, which he called episteme, may turn out to be merely apparent. The latter he called doxa , commonly translated as “belief” or “opinion.” What distinguishes scientific knowledge from other species of knowledge is the quality of its certainty, of the certainty, that is, of its correspondence to what is the case. In the particular case of  the experimental sciences the correspondence between the judgments-statements of the scientists to what is actually the case is insured, as much as that is possible, in the first place by the rigor of the observation of the facts on the basis of which the explanatory hypotheses will be reached, a rigor which is constantly being enhanced by the increasing quality of the instruments employed for the observation and measurement of the phenomena; in the second place, the correspondence of the scientific hypotheses to what is the case is insured  by the repeated experimental confirmation of those hypotheses, which as a result are taken for  laws of reality.

Now, “reality,” in this view, is conceived as that which is self-subsistent

and, more generally of any practice–  may be incorrect, and that, misguided by it , the linguistic agent who believes an incorrect conception to be correct may sincerely continue to cling theoretically to that second-order conception based on reflective or objective  rather than practical interiorization. That is, to affirm and assent to the objective formulations dictated by that  conception,  while continuing, as a linguistic agent to practice or act linguistically in a way that is at odds with his theoretical conception . By contrast,   Hume’s argument, which is similar to Kuhn’s in the field of the experimental sciences, against the skeptics who don’t get out of a room through the window but through the door focuses on a particular case of this inconsistency and underscores the primacy of action over vision, that is of practice over theory. A primacy we all take for granted in our everyday life, for we all agree that “What you do speaks so loud that I cannot hear what you say .”

Now, what does my example about Searle and Polanyi’s different conceptions illustrate concerning both Kuhn’s conception of science and the conception of science of those who buy the very different notion represented by textbook image of science? Several points.

(1) That there is, or at least ought to be, a coherence between the conception and the practice of those who deem  the conception correct.

(1) Notice that we are speaking not of any sort of conception or idea, which would apply to the case of “ Objekts, ” but exclusively of conceptions of a practice , that is, of a way of acting in which living organisms engage (initially by hereditary reflex or by acquired reflex, later by imitation, still later by project, and still  later by inventive project) .

(3) That reflective conceptions or ‘theories’ or ‘philosophies’ are not irrelevant, since they result or ought to result in a particular practice on the part of those agents who are consistent with their conceptions.

(2) That significantly different conceptions resulting from applicative pracitce dictate different and incompatible practices, and that different practices imply different conceptions on the agent’s part.

(3) Conceptions dictate, or ought to result in, particular actions characteristic of a practice. They are dictated primarily to the agent who engages or participates in a particular practice. If the practice is social rather than purely individual, as most (or perhaps all) practices are, they are reflected in the habitual actions of the community and expressed in the “symbola fidei” of the community. These symbola cohere with the foundational (or at least exemplary) narratives of the community because they result from reflection on those narratives. The practices dictated by different conceptions are incompatible because each conception results in a different practical self-understanding of the practitioner agent in relation to his or her world. Now, because the self-understanding is practical, it is to be incarnated in action rather than in objective statements-about or in blind recipes for action,. Hence conceptions are necessarily “tacit. ” Thus, for instance, the self-understanding of the agent Galileo in relation to the world, that underpinned  his law of falling bodies on inclineds, was tacit. It could not be expressed as self-understanding otherwise than by his applicative practice in particular cases, and application can only be learned through practice. In Galileo’s case, THE ACTION INFORMED BY THE PRACTICAL JUDGMENT AS DISPOSITION TO RESPOND, NOT BY T HE STATED THEORETICAL JUDGMENT ABOUT OBJECT, came first and it generated the explicit vision of object for response, that is, the law for action. In the case of Galileo’s  followers, the quasi-blind action comes first, and in time it generates the practical vision or judgment, that is, the habit to see the objects as objects for a particular response. Pascal’s advise in the order of faith may be an illustration of this: If you want to believe, start practicing by imitation of those who believe, reciting their words and reproducing their gestures blindly, until the ‘vision’ or ‘understanding’ follows. What else does the child who is learning to make additions do but to replicate blindly the words and applicative gestures of his teacher until the tacit understanding comes which is manifested in the child’s ability to make additions when appropriate? The same with learning how to speak, and later how to read. *

* HELEN KELLER:

Words, when they function as words, connote. But they do not connote a concept, strictly speaking. Certainly not a concept in the sense of an explicit, abstract, essential, disinterested definition of object. And not even in the sense of the pure image of the object named . Keller didn’t gain the verbal definition of water, nor even the visual or even purely affective-aesthetic image of how water ‘feels.’ Rather, AS VERBAL, her experience was linguistic ; that water had a name represented by the telegraphed word, and that she could refer others to water and  refer herself in verbal thought to the concept in the absence of the object, and could also refer to water denotatively in its presence. But she learned water as an interested agent, not as a pure contemplator. That is, her concept was instrumental, applicative, implicitly related to action. Not primarily representational in a theoretical way , but referring water to her and others as interested agents. This is the conception of water she had when she thought about and talked to others about water. Before her linguistic experience of water, she already had a non-linguistic experience of water, as animals have, as object of desire and fear, as Gegenstand to which she might be exposed as existent under certain particular circumstances of which he had experience, so that she wouldn’t expect to have to deal with water, for good or bad, but under similar circumstances. But the fact that she could think and talk about water in its absence, without necessarily meaning its imminent presence, gave her the possibility of enriching the concept thanks to her successful experience, and then to reflect on her experience and to plan how to use her body-instrument to avoid or obtain, counting implicitly on the uniformity of events, as instinct originally directed her to do. Repeated experience created habitual disposition to act. Explicit planning or projecting, in the case of regular phenomena, is made possible by language, for it is symbolic action, rehearsal for application at the appropriate time.

(4) AND VERY IMPORTANT: That conceptions may be implicit or “tacit” (as “paradigms” actually are); that even when they are explicit or stated they are to be ‘understood’ not theoretically, as resemblant reproductions, but practically, as guiding, dictating or at least authorizing an action, under the appropriate circumstances.. Hence, genuine understanding is practical; it therefore shows in applicative action, not in mere words that supposedly resemble what is the case.

(4) Most of what needs to be said in this respect has already been said under (3). I will only add, for the sake of being more clear, that instead of “conceptions,” a word that suggests purely static images of what is the case or ideas about what is the case, we should speak of “convictions,” for this word suggests or connotes  a connection with an action in which they are to be incarnated or realized. In this vein, Camus wrote: “For a man who does not cheat, what he believes to be true must determine his action.”  ( The Myth , “An Absurd Reasoning,” p. 5). However, thinking of the always possible inconsistencies and of the primacy of action over vision or thought, that is, of praxis over theory or doxa , Camus cunningly added: “It is a common place to compare philosophical theories and the behavior of those who profess them... Schopenhauer is often cited as a fit subject for laughter, because he praised suicide while seated at a well-set table... In the face of such contradictions and obscurities must we conclude that there is no relationship between the opinion one has about life and the act one commits to leave it? Let us not exaggerate in this direction. In a man’s attachment to life there is something stronger than all the ills in the world. The body’s judgment is as good as the mind’s and the body shrinks from annihilation. We get into the habit of living before acquiring the habit of thinking . In that race which daily hastens us toward death, the body maintains its irreparable lead ” (Ibid. pp. 6-7).  - What I like about this formulation is that it places the instinctive and purely reflex bodily act or unthinking response, and then the purely customary unthinking behavior subterraneously guided by desire or fear, at the origin of the ‘cognitive’ process, which is in fact an extension of the instinctive practice, while radically transforming the instinctive by making it intentionally teleological.

(5) That explicit or stated conceptions are reflective reconstructions. They should function as tools for action for a an interested agent, much like maps. When seen as representational rather than as particular symbolic instruments, maps are totally misconceived..

(5) For the reasons explained under (3) and (4), conceptions are always tacit, for they are instrumentally-in dexicalm to-be-applied; not representational . Thus, to speak of explicit or stated conceptions makes no sense. What my statement actually refers to is the common misunderstanding of conceptions that takes them for implicit or explicit objective statements. [Cf. my paragraph on judgments and statements in my “Kuhn and the issue of truth,” p. 9 <2001-April-1-Kuhn>. I cited it at the beginning of this article.] These are reflective reconstructions.

Thus Kuhn’s own case reflects the situation described by him, of the scientists involved in the crisis that occurs when a shift of paradigms takes place, who cannot anymore function comfortably within the old paradigm as map for action, who see the need of replacing it, and who nonetheless cannot bring themselves to abandon the old paradigm for lack of a fully developed alternative.

Kuhn’s own crisis as well as his despondency about the philosophical paradigm in which he finds himself trapped is no less clearly expressed a couple of pages later. Referring again to the misconception by the traditionalists of what actually is the result of a new paradigm for a mere different interpretation of the same brute facts, he replies:

“But is sensory experience fixed and neutral? Are theories simply man-made interpretations of given data? The epistemological viewpoint that has most often guided Western philosophy for three centuries dictates an immediate and unequivocal, Yes! In the absence of a developed alternative, I find it impossible to relinquish entirely that viewpoint. Yet it no longer functions effectively, and the attempt to do so though the introduction of a neutral language of observations now seem to me hopeless.” ^(F37)

At least we are more clear now than we initially were about the nature of the historical situation in which both Kuhn and ourselves as members of his own historical  generation are, and of the outcome searched by Kuhn, to wit: a developed alternative to what he mistakenly identifies as the Cartesian-Newtonian philosophical paradigm that weighs on us into viewing science according to the textbook image. On the basis of this understanding, we may now ask whether, or to what extent, was Kuhn himself successful in developing that alternative.

Practical animal knowledge is not representational but instrumental: Ortho-doxy and ortho-praxis

Do animals know? To Plato, for whom genuine knowledge by contrast to mere belief or opinion ( doxa ) couldn’t be but a deed of the intellect, and who, consequently, had an acute sense of the unbridgeable difference between animals and people, such question would have sounded not only trivial and uninteresting but simply wrongheaded. Not so to Aristotle, who, unlike Plato, in time came to distinguish practical knowledge from the purely theoretical knowledge which Plato had exclusively explored, and who therefore could not exclude the possibility of some kind of continuity between animals and people . My interest in Aristotle’s exploration of the issue whether animals know is that it seems to open the way to an  alternative to the traditional notion of knowledge as representation of the real –the sort of alternative Kuhn was obscurely groping for in order to give full shape to his understanding of science.

In my <2002-April-3-Aris-Devereux: Practical Knowledge in Aristotle> I argued that, at least at the stage of his philosophical evolution to which the texts studied by Devereux belong, Aristotle failed to distinguish adequately practical from theoretical knowledge. The reason for that failure is, I surmise,  that, still very much under Plato’s influence, Aristotle could not yet  help looking in practical knowledge for a universal component which was an essential characteristic of Plato’s conception of  knowledge, though practical knowledge is unavoidably particular. By contrast, in a later view, already less dependent on Plato’s, the distinctive trait of practical knowledge, which is common to animals and people, is, according to Aristotle, its direct and immediate connection with action thanks to desire , its being bound to terminate in action without the mediation of any previous theoretical knowledge. ^(F38)

The connection with action, in Aristotle’s view,  is not found in theoretical knowledge, whose intrinsic purpose is knowledge for its own sake, the mere grasp of the truth, of what is the case. Therein lies, in Aristotle's opinion at this stage, the differencee between theoretical and practical knowledge as well as the consequent radical epistemic difference between people and  animals: animals are capable of attaining only the knowledge that is useful to the preservation and well-being of the individual or of the species to which the animal belongs, while people are capable of both theoretical and practical knowledge. However, at this stage of Aristotle's evolution the connection of knowledge with actionis extrinsic. In this view, the fact that practical knowledge is connected with action does not detract from the fact that it is knowledge, that is,  grasp or ‘vision' of what is the case that corresponds to what is the case, for that is what knowledge consists in. To give an example, on the basis of their respective past experience, both lions and people may come to grasp the useful ‘universal’ fact that, say, eating such a plant has beneficial purgative effects. However, the difference between people and animals is that lions only know that fact practically, that is, for the purpose of applying that knowledge to obtain the benefits of the practice, whereas people know it practically and also theoretically or predicatively, as a stated or statable truth about nature, independent of its practical utility.

         

Such  is Aristotle’s conception of practical and theoretical knowledge in the particular version with which Devereux deals. In this version, the difference between practical and theoretical knowledge is purely extrinsic . Purely extrinsic, as far as the piece of knowledge is concerned, in that its usefulness may or may not be exploited in action by the human knower. Purely extrinsic, too, in that what is known by both the animal  and the human is the same, even though the animal grasps it only from the point of view of its usefulness and only for the purpose of using what it knows, whereas people know it both from the point of view of its usefulness and also as an objective universal factual property of the plant. In other words, the fact known by both the lion and the human of my example is the same, though the lion knows it only because it is actually useful and only for the purpose of applying the knowledge, whereas the human knows the ‘universal’ fact also theoretically or predicatively, independently of its utility and its connection with action. There is, however, in Aristotle's writings, another version of practical knowledge. In the one we have so far considered, the connection between knowledge and action is, as I just said,  extrinsic, in that the knowledge of the same fact may or may not be applied in action, and it is knowledge whether or not it is applied.

Aristotle deems this kmnowledge to be "experiential." Whatdoes this mean? Think, in the first place, of the lion’s case. The lion can attain the knowledge of the purgative effects of the herb only through 'personal' experience of those effects, that is by experiencing them, by being actually affected by them,  while humans may come to know the purgative effects of the herb without any personal experience of them, by being told of them by somebody else, or by observing the purgative effects of the plant in somebody else. To be exact, the lion’s experience is not only experience of the purgative effects, but also experience of eating the plant. In other words, it is experience of both the purgative effects and of the action that is followed by the purgative effect. Thus the lion learns not only the purgative effects the plant produces, but learns also  what to do to obtain the effect. Eating the plant becomes the means to obtain the effects.

This learning is experiential in that it is not inherited but gained through experience . It is a matter not only of effects, but of effects felt (not   observed ) by the lion and tacitly connected with eating such a plant. The purgative effects of the action of eating this plant are undergone and experienced, in the sense of being felt; they are not observed or perceived, by the agent who eats the plant. Because the purgative effects are experienced as favorable or pleasant or satisfactory by the agent who experiences the effects, the action of eating the plant will automatically tend to be repeated under the appropriate circumstances in the expectation of similar effects. Actions followed by rewarding effects tend to be repeated; actions followed by unpleasant effects tend to be avoided. And from objects that look similar similar effects are expected. (The expectation is expressed by the animal, and manifested to us, in the animal’s conduct of repeating the action, not in statements, of which animals of course are incapable).* These are inherited mechanisms of living, sensitive bodies, particularly and more clearly of superior mammals. And these mechanisms are at the origin of all further purposive action initiated by living sensitive bodies and of all future learning about the world as our abode, as our concrete dramatic space.

* The same applies to people, though they are capable of making statements. For expecting and stating that one expects are not the same; so the conduct of expecting, not the statement, is the genuine expression of expectation. Indeed, I can make a statement of expectation without really expecting, thas is, when  my expectation is not incarnated or expressed in the conduct of expecting. Example: I can say,  “Expecto resurrectionem mortuorum,” when my conduct throughout life and especially just before death belies my statement. (Once again, the primacy of praxis over doxa ).  

Like the inherited reflexes on which they are based, these acquired expectations are directly connected with action without the mediation of any theoretical knowledge, for expectation is not stated expect ation. In other words, the animal doesn’t know-that all plants of that descriptive kind are purgative. The animal just has learned to expect purgative effects from that kind of plant, and to look for that kind of plant when purgation is desired. There is memory of the perceivable plant but only as desirable for purgation . Whenever in the future purgation is desirable for the animal’s survival or well-being, the animal will actively look for that kind of plant. This is purely practical recognition of the plant for purposes of action, for the animal has no theoretical or purely facutal idea-that there is this kind of plant that uniformly produces purgative effects in such a kind of animals.

Though the recognition involved in animal knowledge is purely practical in the form of virtual or actual expectation, and though certainly it is not verbalized or stated by the animal, nonetheless, at least at a certain stage of his philosophical evolution, Aristotle saw this practical recognition as akin to the cognitive, objectifying  universalization involved in reflective human judgments, instead of seeing the apparently objective, linguistic human ability involved in practical knowledge as akin to the animals' instrumental knowledge intrinsically related to action. In other words, he confused expectation with stated or objectively known expectation . It seems, though, that Aristotle changed his mind, at least partially, concerning practical knowledge, when he realized that practical knowledge is mediated by desire and, thus, intrinsically connected with, or bound to terminate in, action. This is reflected in his De Motu Animalium and in the sections of his Ethics that refer to the practical syllogism .*

* Martha Nussbaum  identifies Aristotle’s notion of practical knowledge with this second view, exclusively , ignoring the texts that reflect the first view. By contrast, in his article on “Practical Knowledge in Aristotle,” Devereux considers exclusively the first view as reflecting Aristotle’s convictions in this matter. For my part, I think that Devereux's position represents an earlier, and Nussbaum's position a later, stage in Aristotle's evolution.

What precisely is the difference between the notion of practical knowledge that Devereux attributes to Aristotle (position "a") and the one that Nussbaum atrtributes to him (position "b")?

In the case of (a), the practical knowledge animals possess is akin to the theoretical (linguistic, predicative) knowledge of which people are capable, in that a certain grasp of the universal by the animal is supposedly involved. Secondly, the connection of knowledge with action is extrinsic, in that action may or may not follow because practical knowledge is not bound to terminate in action. This amounts to saying that practical knowledge is subordianted to theoretical knowledge in that it preupposes theoretical, predicative knowledge as its necessary concondition. (By the way, this is how Searle construes the connection between the illocutionary and the perlocutionary effects: if the adressee does not know theoretically the illocutionary meaning of what the speaker says, the adressee cannot perform the perlocutionary action expected by the speaker. That is to say that in order to actually do what the speaker commands, the child needs first to know factually the order given. Moreover, the addressee may comprehend theoretically the perlocutionary force of what the speaker says and not perform the action intended by the speaker, because the action is purely accidental to the linguistic meaning; in other words, the connection betwen the knowledge of the perlocutionary meaning and the action is extrinsic.

The case is different in (b). Here the connection of knowledge with action is intrinsic. Practical knowledge is practical in that, being informed by desire, it is bound to terminate in action. No theoretical knowledge is prerequired because practical knowledge is genuinely practical, not theoretical knowledge accidentally relaated to act ion.  In (b), if there is knowledge, there is action, when the appropriate circumstances obtain, unless the action is prevented by factors beyond the agent's control. To use Nussbaum's example, if I am in Smirna, and if I want to see my friend John and I know that John is in Athens, I will take a boat to go to Athens if and when there is a boat to Athens. Similarly, if a hungry dog knows that there is food available in a certain place, the dog will go to that place. If a child desires a toy and knows where the toy is, the child will go to that place, if at all possible, unless he has a stronger diferent desire. (By the way, this is how Polanyi conceives tacit knowledge: what is known functions instrumentally, as the "from where" for action , but it is not known theoretically or objectively by the agent).

However, Nussbaum rightly observes a difference between animals and people, concerning knowledge. In the case of the animal, if there is conflict of desires,the knowledge connected with the strongest desire at the time will necessarily (as a matt ter of concept ual necessity) result in action; in the case of people, though, the action is not absolutely certain, because they can deliberate and choose their course of action. To this I want to add another observation that completes the one made by Nuyssbaum. Thanks to language and its connotational character , people are capable of abstraction from their own exposure. This results in abstract knowledge in which the connection with action is temporarily relaxed. From this arises the illusion of disinterested knowledge, of knowledge for its own sake, with which Artistotle identified theoretical or objective knowledge, which he wrongly interpreted as knowledge of the object through the concept of of the object.  However, theoretical knowledge, as conceived by Aristotle, is an illusion, in that the potential connection with action of what is situationally known (rather than conceptually known ) action remains implicit, as shown by the return to dramatic space at the moment of application. Language is, therefore, the clue. But language understood as it actually functions , tacitly and instrumentally, that is, dramatically, in the case of exposed agents, not explicitly and objectively, as Aristotle thought when he invented his categories that made possible his metaphysics.  

How, concretely, is language the clue? What precise difference or differences does it make between people and animals?

- By relaxing the connection between knowledge and action, linguistic connotation  makes abstraction from the subject's immediate exposure possible. This opens the space and time of the natural sciences, with its observation of the interactions of things. The relaxation, however, is only provisional, for agency takes place in dramatic space where agents attempt to achieve their goals, and the return to that space take place at the moment of application to reality. Application on paper is a sort of map. Application to reality takes place at the time art which the map is activated by the agent, when the appropriate conjunction of desire and circumstances obtains.
