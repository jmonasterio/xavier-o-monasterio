UNO

  FAITH AND REASON, SCIENCE AND OPINION

   (\WP\monas\uno-1)

In a celebrated lecture "On the Art of Misunderstanding Thomism" (McAuley Lectures, 1966), Etienne Gilson distinguished between "Thomism" as "the doctrine of Thomas Aquinas, as he himself understood it," and "Thomism" as "what most people think it is," that is, what most people think the doctrine of Thomas Aquinas as understood by himself was. While acknowledging that the answer of the historians to the question "What was Aquinas own thought?" is not unanimous on particular points, as is always the case with any profound and complex thinker, he feels nonetheless entitled as a historian to denounce three fundamental mistakes committed by Thomists in the second sense of the word. In the first place, there are those Thomists who think that because Aquinas was primarily a theologian and was convinced that theology rests on faith, Thomism as understood and practiced by Aquinas is a theology that has nothing to do with philosophy and the natural use of reason. Realizing that this variety of Thomism is a very rare species because it is so obviously at odds with the bulk of Aquinas's works, Gilson disposes of it quickly to come to grips with the two other varieties of Thomism he considers really alive and profoundly mistaken. One of them interprets Aquinas as having carried out two simultanoeus projects: a theological one, based on the data of faith but using reason to reach an understanding of faith, and a philosophical one in which reason works by its own devices but is indirectly guided by faith to explore in a purely rational way those particular domains or issues in which revelation needs to be  complemented by philosophy. As for the other variety of Thomism, it interprets Aquinas as having considered philosophy as the exclusive domain of natural reason left to its own devices, without any guidance from faith and even positively excluding such guidance as an illegitimate interference in a domain in which reason is entitled to, and capable of, reaching truth by its own stregth. The difference between these two ways of misunderstanding Aquinas's doctrine as understood by himself, that is, the Thomism of Aquinas, is relatively insignificant. What both have in common is that they attribute to Aquinas a conception of faith and of reason as complementary to each other in their ultimate map of reality, but as self-sufficient but autonomous within their proper respective domains.

There is a very curious feature of Gilson's position concerning Aquinas's Thomism. On the one hand, Gilson is sure of what Aquinas rejected. On the other hand, he never attempts to justify Aquinas's rejections or to show positively that Aquinas has an intelligible and consistent position on the issues at stake. Thus, Gilson tells us, speaking of certain Thomists who misunderstand Aquinas's Thomism:

"... in order to reconcile their own brand of Thomism with the current notion of philosophy free from theological entanglements, they divide it into two distinct parts: a theology exclusively based upon revelation and faith on the one hand, and on the other hand, a philosophy exclusively derived from the natural light of reason.

"Whether that is a correct notion of both theology and philosophy, is in itself a problem with which I am not prepared to deal. The discussion has been going on for centuries and is still going strong. My own question is: do such notions of philosophy and theology fit the thought of Thomas Aquinas? (ibid. p. 36).

Now, one might surmise that Gilson is here speaking as a mere historian of philosophy. As such, he has not to deal with the problem whether the way in which those mistaken Thomists conceive of philosophy and of theology is right or wrong, only with the problem whether the notions of philosophy and theology they attribute to Aquinas fit with Aquinas's own notions of theology and philosophy--and this regardless of whether Aquinas's own notions of theology and philosophy make sense or not. But surely Gilson was not speaking as a mere historian of philosophy in this case, for he had previously said in the same lecture:

"As a professor of the history of philosophy, I have had to make myself the mouth piece of many other philosophers besides Thomas Aquinas. In doing that, I have always been careful to present each particular doctrine such as, to the best of my knowledge, it had been in the mind of its author. Nothing is more irritating to dogmatically minded philosophers that such a purely historical attitude. Thomas himself once said that he was not interested in what other men have said but, rather, in the truth itself on every question. And, of course, that is how a true philosopher should feel. But before deciding whether what a certain philosopher has said is true or not, the first question to ask and answer is: did he say that? Now that is a straight historical question and, as a professor of the history of medieval philosophy, I had to make sure of what Thomas had really said before pretending to repeat it.

"The problem was the same with  all the other philosophers whose doctrines I had to teach. But something happened to me with Thomas Aquinas that did not happen with the other ones. Little by little, while I was understanding him better and better, I realized that not only he truly said so, but that what he had said was true . He was a man of his time and in matters of science, for instance, he did what we ourselves usually do: he followed the teaching of the scientists whom he thought he could trust. But on all the leading metaphysical problems, which are the properly philosophical ones, I came to the conclusion that Thomas Aquinas was right . So, without parting company with the others, I took more and more pleasure in staying with him" (ibid., p. 34).
