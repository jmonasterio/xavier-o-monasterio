(15) Dupré, Louis, A Dubious Heritage: Studies in Philosophy of Religion after Kant (Paulist Press, N. Y. 1977).

The "dubious heritage" seems to be the way philo of religion has been practiced after Kant. K left three challenges to all subsequent speculation about religion (p. 3). Aside from Scheliermacher and Kierkegaard, who may be deemed to have dealt successfully with the challenge concerning autonomy, the other two challenges have not been met successfully. The systematc study of the failures of everybody, Blondel and Duméry included, constitutes part III, which is a useful study of the 'proofs' of God's existence. THE FOLLOWING ARE MY REFLECTIONS :

- "For Socrates and Plato, whom we generally regard as the initiators of purely autonomous thinking, philo remained religious in form (the myth) and in content. In fact, the mystical trend of Plato's philo became the very heart of Plotinus's thought. - In the Middle ages the relation between philo and religion was drastically altered. Revelation became the proper object of the science of theology as such. As a result, except for a few isolated attempts, revealed religion ceased to be an obect of philosophical inquiry. The study of revealed religion was not to be taken up again until after Kant. And not until the contemporary phenomenological movement was it generally accepted that philo might have something positive to say about the entire realm of revealed religion. Even so, phenomenology remained primarily descriptive. Duméry, however, wants to restore

philo to its full critical right. The task of philo is not merely to describe and analyze but also to evalute critically on all levels of consciousness, the formation and structure of religious symbols, including the ones which the religious man considers to be strictly revealed" (p. 113).

Concerning Socrates (and Plato) : I think Nietzsche was more lucid: "Our whole modern world is entangled in the net of Alexandrian culture. It proposes as its ideal the theoretical man equipped with the greatest forces of knowledge, and laboring in the service of science, whose archetype and progenitor is Socrates. All our educational methods have originally this ideal in view... ( The Birth of Tragedy ). Even Dupré, in Transcendent Selfhood (The Seabury Press, N. Y. 1976), grants that Nietzsche "never retracted his early conviction that the rise of the scientific spirit in the particular Gestalt which it adopted in our tradition bore much of the responsibility for the present crisis" (p. 12).

Concerning the Middle Ages : Leaving aside whether or not Socrates and Plato were religious as philosophers, I agree that " in the Middle Ages the relation between philo and religion was drastically altered ." In fact, the objectivism Dupré regrets in Transcendent Selfhood became institutionalized with the adoption of Aris's scientific ideal. The separation between philo and theo comes from there. So does the autonomy of philo. So does the claim of philo to judge faith. So does the notion of faith as

a matter of irrational feeling. Both Kant, and  Dumery who is just a post-Kantian, a member of the "dubious heritage," remain within the tradition. - Because of his concern with scientific knowledge and his assumption that truth is an exclusive property of judgments and therefore that the only possible truth is propositional, Aquinas reduced revelation to propositions and faith to assent to those propositions. This resulted in a a view of reality as composed of what is knowable through revelation alone, what is knowable through both faith and revelation, and what is knowable through reason without revelation. Consequently, it resulted in a view of theology as dealing with the first and the second, and in a view of philo as dealing with the second and the third. - For Aris reason was secular and all-embracing. Aquinas, like Kant, attempted to make room for faith by limiting the domain of reason while making it autonomous within its proper domain and preserving for faith the domain of the revealed, of the sacred. For those who, after Aquinas, accepted his view of reason as autonomous but not his faith, things changed into the modern picture, which is the Aristotelian picture of reason with a vengeance: Reason is positively secular and positively all-embracing. If the revealed cannot be known , then it is not. Consequently religion is purely blind feeling. And this is where we are today, since even Dumery--"the greatest philosopher of religion" failed. The heritage, therefore, is not dubious: it is conclusive; there is no room for faith and religion, at least as long as we remain within the Greek paradigm wich is objectivist. AND WE CATHOLICS CELEBRATE HIM AS THE FOUNDING FATHER OF THE CHRISTIAN INTELLECTUAL TRADITION, FOR THIS ALLOWED US TO BE OPEN-MINDED TO REASON WITHOUT FEAR, WITH THE CERTAINTY THAT FAITH AND REASON ARE IN HARMONY WITH EACH OTHER AND THAT THERE CAN BE NO MORE THAN APPARENT CONTRADICTIONS BETWEEN REASON AND FAITH. - pp. 6-7: " Heidegger developed this discovery... Existence is essentially to be in time and its temporality alone provides the transcendental horizon in which the meaning of Being can manifest itself. Time had ceased to be a mere a priori form of cognition from which reflection could take its distance, instead it became the primary determination of Being itself. It is hard to exaggerate the significance of this theory for the understanding of our culture. Heidegger first fully recognized the need to attribute to the subject a content of its own. In doing so he overcame the basic principle of objectivism, that the subject is no more than the constitution of the object. But his philo also brings to a close the subjectivism which, in the wake of the critique of objectivity, had simply reversed the relation between knower and known without rethinking the content of the determining subject. Hermeneutic philo succeeded in attributing a creative role to the interpreter while at the same time maintaining that the interpreter is part of the interpreted. It thus fulfilled the condition indispensable to our culture for becoming aware of its objectivism. - But it also allowed us to understand another factor which had reinforced this objectivism and accelerated the loss of transcendence: the fundamental historicalness of human existence.... Until the advent of historical criticism the Christian had seldom faced the consequences of his own thoroughly historical faith. Though regarding his life and that of the nations a pilgrimage through time, he had consistently secluded the content of revelation into a realm of unchangeable permanence. When that content was finally subjected to the same treatment as all other historical phenomena, believers reacted either by submitting to the judgment of history and lowering the status of their faith to the common plane of secular events, or by safeguarding that faith from the contingency of history in a closed cycle of divinely foreordained decrees. The former alternative jeopardized transcendence, the latter sacrificied history, including the historical character of faith itself. - Philo provided no solution, since on the matter of history it was itself hopelessly divided between a relativist historicism and such secular versions of Christian providentialisma as Hegel's theory of the Absolute Spirit. Lacking was a perspective in which history would retain its full contingency while yet being founded in ontological necessity. The possibility of absolute transcendence required a significance beyond that of a self-repeating rhythm of birth and decline. Yet history could remain truly historical only if it remained contingently open toward the future. Hermeneutic philo provided both conditions by founding history in the essential temporality of existence itself. More than a particular entity, existence is that in which Being itself becomes transparent. If that existence is by its very nature historical ('temporalizes itself' Heidegger would say), then history may yet reveal Being as transcendent. Hermeneutic philo accounts for both the contingency of being in time and its ontological necessity. Without such and understanding either time is the merely subjective condition through which the objective order is being perceived or it is itself the objective succession of that order. In both cases the historical consciousness reduces the existence in time to the objective and leads to, or accelerates, objectivism."

- p. 10: "A philosophical explanation requires a grasp of the fundamental significance of history itself and this depends on our understanding of existence itself as essentially temporal. An investigation of this sort belongs to metaphysics rather than to history. Yet metaphysicians have not been the only ones to reflect on the meta-historical principles of history."

- p. 17: "Our predicament is due not to lack of faith but to a lack of inwardness. To profess a belief in God and to observe certain rules of ritual and moral conduct is not sufficient to regain it. Faith itslef is permeated by objectivism. What is needed is a conversion to an attitude in which existing is more than taking, acting more than making, meaning more than function--an attitude in which there is enough leisure for wonder and enough detachment for transcendence. Culture requires freedom but freedom requires spiritual space to act, play, and dream in. Such a space is not provided by leisure alone: leisure itself becomes suffocating without spiritual content. The space for freedom is created by transcendence. What is needed most of all is an attitude in which transcendence can be recognized again. The question of actual faith is entirely secondary to the recovery of freedom by detachment from the purely objective."

Somme comments:

H is attempting to escape the Greek conceptual space within which he finds himself a prisoner. This is the space of the judgment-statement (as distinct from feeling, desiring, willing, deciding) as exclusively cognitional and potential bearer of truth. This is rationalism, of which irrationalism is just a variety, a negative reaction against, for it constitutes a no more viable alternative. But H's singing of being, poetic disclosure of being is no alternative either. And yet his attempt to escape is justified, for a rationalism in which reason as thought is the measure of being is kaput. I share his reaction against the Greeks. By Greeks I mean the Socratics and post-Socratics, for H's pre-Socratics are a fancy name for H's hazy, failed alternative.

The Greeks invented the "reason" of rationalism. This reason takes its initial meaning over against authority, not over against religious faith.

Authority here means the weight of ethos, of culture, of custom. Ethos, however, is not a philosophical system, not even a collection of statements, but a prescriptive comprehension of the world incarnated or embodied in a set of practices characteristic of a human group. Insofar as the members of the group internalize those practices together with the comprehension of the world incarnated in them, the practices are 'natural' for them, not authoritarian. Ethos appears authoritarian only when it is contested, when it ceases to be part of the identity of one or more of its members, when it ceases to be part and parcel of what they are and becomes externally imposed on them.

One way of contesting the ethos, when one of its practices becomes authoritarian, is practical deviation from the customary practice, which depending on the importance attributed to the practice by the members of the ethos will result in lighter or heavier punishment--the heaviest punishments being suppression by death, ostracism and exile. For instance, a Catholic remarries in a traditionally Catholic society. This deviation does not necessarily involve a contestation of the practice of non-divorce with its proscriptive character of divorce as a no-no, as an undesirable. However, it may be prompted by a contestation of the validity of the practice itself and of the comprehension of the world embodied in it, of its 'grounds.' This is what happened in Greece.

Two alternatives arose which conflicted with each other:

the Sophists' and the Philosophers'. It seems that their very confrontation polarized them and hardened their respective positions. What both had in common was the conviction that the contested practices of their culture were conventional rather than natural. What distinguished them is that the Sophists, in the course of their conflict with the Philosophers, came to see all social practices as conventional, whereas the Philosophers insisted on the possibility of a natural culture.

MY REFLECTIONS

- The scholarly consensus changes, and his own opinions change. Why should we go along with them more than with the faith of the early communities?

- The modern worldview prevented him from believing in the J of the gospels, not only of John (for, after all, the other gospels do not present the historical J either, and are closer in substance to the J of John than to the J of scholarship. But the modern worldview did not prevent him from moving to the God-Spirit, while realizing that this is not scientifically serious. His view of Jesus is a bad mixture of "critical age" and vague religious instinct.

- There was an almost instinctive reluctance among Jews to identify a human being with Yaweh (see the horrifies reactions of Paul and Barnabas and of Peter when people attempted to adore them, in the Acts). Why would the early xtian community invent something about Jesus that went so much against the grain of their culture?

- The God of Jesus was not a vague, all-abiding Spirit; He was personal, to be entered with in a person-to-person (I-Thou) relationship.

- Why wasn't Paul's message (who lived and died before any of the gospels were written) about the Spirit but about Jesus, if he was a Spirit-person? And why is that message so definitely christological and divinizing of Jesus at such an early moment? And why didn't the early communities come to experience Paul increasingly as God?
