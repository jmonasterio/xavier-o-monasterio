SARTRE AND MacINTYRE ON "TRUE STORIES"

"I AM PRESENTING.... HUMAN ACTIONS ,... AS ENACTED NARRATIVES. NARRATIVE IS NOT THE WORK OF POETS, DRAMATISTS AND NOVELISTS REFLECTING UPON EVENTS WHICH HAD NO NARRATIVE ORDER BEFORE ONE WAS IMPOSED BY THE SINGER OR WRITER; NARRATIVE FORM IS NEITHER DISGUISE NOR DECORATION ... I ARGUED THAT IN SUCCESSFULLY IDENTIFYING AND UNDERSTANDING WHAT SOMEONE ELSE IS DOING WE ALWAYS MOVE TOWARDS PLACING A PARTICULAR EPISODE IN THE CONTEXT OF A SET OF NARRATIVE HISTORIES, HISTORIES BOTH OF THE INDIVIDUALS CONCERNED AND OF THE SETTINGS IN WHICH THEY ACT AND SUFFER... WE RENDER THE ACTIONS OF OTHERS INTELLIGIBLE IN THIS WAY BECAUSE ACTION ITSELF HAS A BASICALLY HISTORICAL CHARACTER . IT IS BECAUSE WE ALL LIVE OUT NARRATIVES IN OUR LIVES AND BECAUSE WE UNDERSTAND OUR OWN LIVES IN TERMS OF THE NARRATIVES THAT WE LIVE OUT THAT THE FORM OF NARRATIVE IS APPROPRIATE FOR UNDERSTANDING THE ACTIONS OF OTHERS. STORIES ARE LIVED BEFORE THEY ARE TOLD--EXCEPT IN THE CASE OF FICTION ... THIS OF COURSE HAS BEEN DENIED... LOUIS O. MINK.. HAS ASSERTED : 'STORIES ARE NOT LIVED BUT TOLD. LIFE HAS NO BEGINNINGS, MIDDLES, OR ENDS; THERE ARE MEETINGS, BUT THE START OF AN AFFAIR BELONGS TO THE STORY WE TELL OURSELVES LATER, AND THERE ARE PARTINGS, BUT FINAL PARTINGS ONLY IN THE STORY. THERE ARE HOPES, PLANS, BATTLES AND IDEAS, BUT ONLY IN RETROSPECTIVE STORIES ARE HOPES UNFILFILLED, PLANS MISCARRIED, BATTLES DECISIVE, AND IDEAS SEMINAL. ONLY IN THE STORY IS IT AMERICA WHICH COLUMBUS DISCOVERS AND ONLY IN THE STORY IS THE KINGDOM LOST FOR WANT OF A NAIL'. What are we to say to this? Certainly we must agree that it is only retrospectively that hopes can be characterized as unfulfilled or battles as decisive... BUT WE SO CHARACTERIZE THEM IN LIFE AS MUCH AS IN ART. AND TO SOMEONE WHO SAYS THAT IN LIFE THERE ARE NO ENDINGS, OR THAT FINAL PARTINGS TAKE PLACE ONLY IN STORIES, ONE IS TEMPTED TO REPLY, 'BUT HAVE YOU NEVER HEARD OF DEATH?' HOMER DID NOT HAVE TO TELL THE TALE OF HECTOR BEFORE ANDROMACHE COULD LAMENT UNFULFILLED HOPE AND FINAL PARTING. THERE ARE COUNTLESS HECTORS AND COUNTLESS ANDROMACHES WHOSE LIVE EMBODIED THE FORM OF THEIR HOMERIC NAMESAKES, BUT WHO NEVER CAME TO THE ATTENTION  OF ANY POET. WHAT IS TRUE IS THAT IN TAKING AN EVENT AS A BEGINNING OR AN ENDING WE BESTOW  A SIGNIFICANCE UPON EACH WHICH MAY BE DEBATABLE ... THERE IS A CRUCIAL SENSE IN WHICH THE PRINCIPATE OF AUGUSTUS, OR THE TAKING THE OATH IN THE TENNIS COURT, OR THE DECISION TO CONSTRUCT AN ATOMIC BOMB AT LOS ALAMOS CONSTITUTE BEGINNINGS..." ( AFTER VIRTUE , P. 197-198).

"An action is a moment in a possible or actual history or in a number of such histories. The notion of a history is as fundamental a notion as the notion of an action. Each requires the other. But I cannot say this without noticing that it is precisely this that Sartre denies --and indeed his whole theory of the self, which captures so well the spirit of modernity, requires that he should.  In La Nausée , Sartre makes Antoine Roquentin argue not just what Mink argues, that narrative is very different from life, but that to present human life in the form of a narrative is always to falsify it. There are not and there cannot be any true stories. Human life is composed of discrete actions which lead nowhere, which have no order; the story-teller imposes on human events retrospectively an order which they did not have while they were lived . Clearly if Sartre/Roquentin is right--I speak of Sartre/Roqentin to distinguish him from such other well-known characters as Sartre/Heidegger and Sartre/Marx--my central contention must be mistaken. There is nonetheless an important point of agreement between my thesis and that of Sartre/Roquentin. We agree in identifying the intelligibility of an action with its place in a narrative sequence. Only Sartre/Roquentin takes it that human actions are as such unintelligible occurrences: it is to a realisation  of the mataphysical implications of this that Roquentin is brought in the course of the writing of an historical biography . This project no longer makes sense. Either he will write what is true or he will write an intelligible history, but the one possibility excludes the other. Is Sartre/Roquentin right?" (Alasdair MacIntyre, After Virtue , University of Notre Dame Press, 1981, p. 199).

"What the narrative concept of selfhood requires is thus twofold. On the one hand, I am what I may justifiably be taken by others to be in the course of living out a story that runs from my birth to my death; I am the subject of a history that is my own and no one else's, that has its own peculiar meaning... To be the subject of a narrative that runs from one's birth to one's death is to be accountable for the actions and experiences which compose a narratable life... The other aspect of narrative selfhood is correlative: I am not only accountable, I am one who can always ask others for an account, who can put others to question. I am part of their story, as they are part of mine . ( Ibid ., pp. 202-203)

"SUCH INDIVIDUALISM IS EXPRESSED BY THOSE MODERN AMERICANS WHO DENY ANY RESPONSIBILITY FOR THE EFFECTS OF SLAVERY... BEING AN AMERICAN IS NOT ITSELF TAKEN TO BE PART OF THE MORAL IDENTITY OT THE INDIVIDUAL.. AND THE SELF SO DETACHED IS OF COURSE A SELF VERY MUCH AT HOME IN EITHER SARTRE'S OR HOFFMAN'S PERSPECTIVE, A SELF THAT CAN HAVE NO HISTORY. THE CONTRAST WITH THE NARRATIVE VIEW OF THE SELF IS CLEAR. FOR THE STORY OF MY LIFE IS ALWAYS EMBEDDED IN THE STORY OF THOSE COMMUNITIES FROM WHICH I DERIVE MY IDENTITY. I WAS BORN WITH A PAST; AND TO TRY TO CUT MYSELF OFF FROM THAT PAST, IN THE INDIVIDUALISTIC MODE, IS TO DEFORM MY PRESENT RELATIONSHIPS. THE POSSESSION OF AN HISTORICAL IDENTITY AND THE POSSESSION OF A SOCIAL IDENTITY COINCIDE. NOTICE THAT REBELLION AGAINST MY IDENTITY IS ALWAYS ONE POSSIBLE MODE OF EXPRESSING IT." ( Ibid ., p 205).

As Mac observes, all choices are conditioned and constrained, but there are better and worse conditions. So, pace Sartre, nobody is the total or even the sole author of his or her story.

What then about truth?

The notion of truth is contemplative--not for decision but for objective assent--and reflective--primarily about something said, in the sense of affirmed or denied ; about something already there, either contemporaneously contemplated by somebody other than the exposed agent, or contemplatively reflected upon afterwards by the exposed agent himself or by somebody else. Truth (or falsity) is predicated of a supposedly specular or referential mental act or of a statement of the agent, once the mental act or the statement are completed. Philosophers, who as seekers of certainty or warranted truth take themselves for the specialists of truth and falsity, consider the mental act or the statement and attempt to determine whether they are true, that is, whether or not they 'correspond' to reality [though of course the verification of such correspondence, as Kant remarked in his Copernican revolution, is impossible and, in his view, wrongheaded. Remember that Aris grants that the perceptual acts of people and even of animals can be "materially true," if they correspond to or grasp what is the case ].

The view of knowledge that makes it the deed of a an invulnerable mind rather than an exposed agent who makes decisions is at odds with our linguistic experience. Language in its primary mode is the language spoken and heard by desirous exposed agents who transcend themselves and the world toward the future by way of decision and action. So what they say in the primary linguistic mode of exposure, even in the case of statements, is not to be assented to but to be responded to by way of decision and action. [In this primary linguistic mode we don't narrate when we perceive linguistically and when we state to others; we speak aks agents and act. When I tell you "Your mother is on the phone," I am not aiming at your speculative understanding or assent; I am co-acting with you linguistically. And when you take the phone to talk to your mother, you are co-acting with me. This cooperative action, when reflectively reconstructed as past, will take the shape of a dramatic narrative.

When all urgent cares are taken care of we have the leisure to shift linguistic modes. And sometimes we talk in conversation, or even write, about our past or the past of other people, some of them alive, some already dead. In the latter case no future decision is involved since no future interaction is possible. Whatever our purpose may be in doing that, we reconstruct imaginarily the story of a dead person by telling it in a way that is consistent with the traces left in animate and inanimate, in living and dead documents. Of such told (orally or written) story it may be asked--and both Sartre and MacIntyre might ask--whether it aims at truth and whether or not it is actually true.

Sartre-Roquentin answers that it cannot posibly be true, that is, cannot possibly correspond to reality, for (1) a human life has no narrative structure at all, or   (2) no intelligibility of its own, (3) no intrinsic justification, since it am0unts to the same whether you are a leader of people or a lonely drunkard, indeed it is constituted by isolated, surd, disconnected, unintelligible actions. This is one possibility.  

There is no truth

Because there is no correspondence between what is believed or stated and what is the case, or at least we cannot know when such correspondence obtains. All there is is different interpretations. And since knowledge consists in correspondence and certain knowledge of correspondence, we are lost as knowers if there is no truth. And then, anything goes. This "undecidability" of interpretations, this refusal of determinability, is liberating to the deconstructivists, while it is terrifying to most others.

The above hinges on a particular conception of knowledge and truth as correspondence, consecrated by the Greek philosophical undertaking. However, if such conception is wrongheaded, if such correspondence never existed or was even possible, then nothing is lost, except a silly illusion. And then the sense of deprivation--as well as of liberation--are unwarranted.

But, if not knowledge, what is going to guide us in our actions? After all, is not this lack of guidance, this "everything goes," that terrifies us with the disappearance of knowledge?

ON ACTION AND THE AGENT        (July 31, 98)

On Aristotle and Plato

For Plato , only the universal and necessary, therefore, the eternal, immutable and purely intelligible or immaterial could be object of knowledge. Knowable was, therefore, exclusively what was object of what Aris later would call episteme or scientific knowledge. The rest--that is, the contingent, the temporal, the material--could only be object of opinion and, therefore, not genuinely knowable and actually known. Why not object of knowledge? Because what was grasped by the knower as supposedly known could be actually false, since what the knower grasped could conceivable not correspond (or be) what was actually te case, what was real. Hence the idea of truth as correspondence or intentional or epistemic identity between the known by the knower and the real was presupposed by, or a constitutive of, Plato's notion of knowledge.

Leaving aside the complex Platonic notion of the Idea of the Good and its connection with orthopraxis, as well as Plato's siginificant evolution concerning the Ideas, it is clear that at least in his early stage Plato identified orthopraxis with orthodoxia or correct knowledge or knowledge of the eternally real ideas. Indeed, he identified moral failure or failure in action with ignorance, and he made those who had intuited the eternal Ideas, that is, the lovers of wisdom into the kings or governors of the city because being knowers they had to be good and act rightly.

Aristotle's case is far more complex. For one, he did away with the heaven of the Ideas and replaced them within nature as the intelligible natures of the visible, material realities. In this regard, though with significant modifications, he perpetuated in part Plato's view of what is knowable. However, commonsensical and pragmatic as he was on the basis of 'what we people say and do'-- ta phainomena --Aris relaxed Plato's strictures concerning the knowable to the extent of accepting with certain conditions as objects of knowledge what Plato had discarded as objects of mere opinion, thereby introducing the distinction between episteme or scientific knowledge and factual or unscientific knowledge. Moreover, Aris's relentless curiosity about nature and his penchant for systematic observation led him to a deep analysis of human and animal action and a comparison between the two, particularly in De Motu Animalium and in the first lesson of the Metaphysics . In De Motu Animalium he introduced the notion of desire as the distinctive constitutive of practical knowledge.

Concerning orthopraxis, having abandoned Plato's notion of human orthopraxis as the automatic result of knowledge conceived  as the intuitive contemplation of the eternal Ideas, Aris undertook the task of building from scratch a new understanding of orthopraxis in his two Ethics and of the well ordered city in his Politics .   Plato had conceived the good by analogy to the true. As the eternal and necessary intelligible forms were the proportionate object ot the intellect with its disinterested desire to know the really real by contrast to its images, so the Idea of the good was the proportionate object of the intellect's desire to pursue through action the good for the human being. Knowing what is good necesarily involved being attracted by it and pursuing it.

It took Aris time to detach himself from Plato, and the fact that till the end he identified orthopraxis with some sort of knowledge, though clearly less than at the beginning of his evolution, shows that he never achieved his complete independence . Orthopraxis became, for Aris, the deed of the prudent member of the human community. But was prudence a form of knowledge? Certainly it was not scientific knowledge. It was not evken, properly speaking, knowledge, since what was grasped by the prudent didn't terminate in assent but in akction. Was it therefore what Plato had called "opinion," which Aristotle had classified as knowledge, though without universality and necessity? Not quite, because, although without the certainty of the necessary, non-scientific knowledge was "exact," akribós , definitely true or false, not approximately true or approximately false, for otherwise it couldn't have possibly qualified as knowledge. Knowledge is not a matter of degree, whereas in matters of action the bread can be perfectly or poorly baked. [The point is to achiee the desired goal and to have the appropriate goal]. In other words, factual or non-scientific knowledge is a classification that applies to the speculative field, whereas non-akribós applies to the practical field. Yet, Aris compares or contrasts the non-akribós of the practical to the exactness of the speculative-scientific, a contrast which presupposes at least an analogy, if not a specific commonality, between speculative and practical knowledge. And to make the matter more equivocal, he calls both by the same word "knowledge," which seems to suggest that they are actually species of the same genus. In adition, he attributes to virtuous action or to the virtuous agent a cognitive component (he speaks of "intellectual virtues") which belongs to the practical intellect and seems to involve truth-picture, as well as a component of good habit which concerns desire rather than understadning and seems to be per se a-rational, as desire is in Plato. All this constitutes a curious, heterogeneous mixture. It is due, in my opinion, to the fact that Aris began as a genuine disciple of Plato in his epistemic views and then evolved on his own, without ever completing his independence. Till the end he kept something of Plato's conception of knowledge, something of Plato's dualism between the soul and the body, something of Plato's intellectualism, something of Plato's distrust for the temporal, the perceptual and the material, and something of Plato's Socratic conflation of the speculative with the practical, of the cognitive with the active.       

If Aris was the first footnoter of Plato, the history of Western philosophy has continued that tradition for the most part and without much success, pushing even farther the Socratic primacy of knowledge as corresponding picture to the extreme of making the knowledge of the good impossible, with the logical Kantian result of reducing the ethical to a blind imperative, whereas the scientific belongs with the answer to the question "What can I know?" All this shows the extent to which we have remained subservient to Plato in our philosophical thinking, which in the post-Enlightenment age tends to be identified with rationality. But is it not possible to break definitively with Plato and open a genuinely new paradigm, profiting at least in part from Aris's failed experience and his legacy concerning the field of the practical? This is my question.

A new paradigm

Compared to perception and to action, epistemic thinking is reflective. It comes temporally after perception and action and it presupposes them as its necessary condition . Furthermore, the reflective thinker assumes that the involved agent's perception and action are as they are understood by an uninvolved, reflective thinker . By contrast, perception and action do not presuppose thinking and, by the same token, are not themselves reflective. They are pre-reflective, non-thetical, not known .

Philosophy is a particular kind of thinking enterprise whose purpose is to justify or legitimize perception (or intellection) as knowledge, and guarantee correct or appropriate action. This presupposes a need for justification. Such need was made evident at the time of the birth of philosophy in Greece by the fact that people disagreed about the truth-validity of certain or all perceptions and the appropriateness of certain or all (controlable) human actions. They were, however, in implicit agreement that truth and falsity are possible attributes of perceptions and consequent correctness and incorrectness of actions. Now this indeed is the way things look after the fact, retrospectively or reflectively, when contemplated by a non-engaged subject. But the same is not the case at the time of agency.

Perception is a purposive moment of agency. It is structured about, or focused on, a particular object, which is itself correlated to a goal of the agent. This is to say that perception  is future-oriented and informed by desire, because the perceiver is a vulnerable, exposed agent rather than an invulnerable, contemplative, objective mind.
