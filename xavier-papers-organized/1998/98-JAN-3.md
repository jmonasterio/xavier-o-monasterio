ON TRUTH AND DECISION

To escape from the understanding of knowledge and truth based on written, objectifying language, on already objectified 'realities.' For, then, what is said in the sense of stated (independently of who says it, to whom, and in which circumstances) is taken, if agreed with, for what is the case, for real. Then the real is reduced to mere spectacle for a detached, objective, unsituated knower. This entails the separation of knowledge from action as well as the privileging and primacy of the contemplative or speculative standpoint. Assent rather than action when in presence, or either dramatic or instrumental understanding when in absence, become the fundamental acts. (Cf. \wp\98\98-jan-1).

A point of contrast

Philosophy is reflective rather than direct or immediate, for it is critical. It presupposes something on whose validity or  invalidity it has to pass judgment. (Here the de-cidig, the krisis, is purely intellectual, and it is done by a reason that is above the fray, undeterred and undeterrable). That 'something' has to involve an explicit or an at least implicit claim to validity, for what is not susceptible of validity or invalidity is not possible object of philosophy.

The something on whose validity philosophy passes judgment is initially a perception of a sensible object. Whatever is perceived is perceived-as. In this sense, supposedly, it has an inchoate claim to validity. If the perceiving subject takes a critical distance from the perceived in order to pass judgment on the claim, the inchoate predication involved in perceiving-as becomes the object of judgment, that is, object of philosophy. When a definitive, that is, a categorical or unconditional judgment is passed it takes the form of the assent or allegiance of the subject to the perceived-as, and it can be made public through a statement.

Sometimes the judgment is passed on somebody else's stated but as yet unjudged, uncritical perception. But sometimes, too, the judgment is to be passed on somebody else's already judged and categorically stated perception with which one happens to disagree. Then philosophy as disputation arises. But how is the dispute to be settled when one perception-as differs from another? At this point philosophy as epistemology is likely to arise, that is, philosophy as the set of rules for correct perception-as, when the dispute concerning two different perceptions-as cannot ultimately be settled by another perception, by taking a look.

What interests me here are the presuppositions involved. Perception is always perception of a particular and supposedly real  sensible or material object. Real, in the case of perception, means extramental, actually subsisting independently of its being perceived or known (judged). But a characteristic of the real is its phenomenality, its showing itself as. And since the epistemic philosophical dispute is about the real as it shows itself, the real is automatically identified at least partially with its pohenomenality, with its capacity to show itself and be 'seen.' In short, the real is the knowable, the seeable, what appears as it is to a perceiver.

The point is that the subject has been reduced to the epistemic subject, and the epistemic subject to a contemplator, for the only relationship possible between the epistemic subject and the real that is known is the contemplative grasping of the known by the knower. Then the fundamental question becomes the unanswerable question of the possibility for the knower of moving from the contemplated phenomenal to the real, to the noumenal.

To this question the best bad answer is Kant's: the noumenal is unattainable, but there is at least a realm of what shows itself, what can become object of categorically-structured intuition, and that is what the real as knowable--but not as noumenal--amounts to. However, there has to be , in addition, the realm of the real noit as phenomenal but as noumenal, not as knowable but as believable, for otherwise human, free, responsible action would be impossible.

All the above is brought only as a point of contrast to a more promising alternative in which exposure plays the central role and in which the structure of the universe in which the subject is engaged as a part-icipant is dramatic and narrative.  

The tyranny of statements

In his commentary on Peri hermeneias Aquinas, innocently following Aristotle, consecrates the view that truth is the exclusive characteristic of one class of speech acts, namely, statements, which in their turn express one class of intellectual acts, namely, judgments. The consequence is that statements are the only speech acts for which there is a valid place in the scientific disciplines, which are reflective. *

* And even in the realm of immediate experience or perception (where truth has a place too), of which divine revelation and faith are a portion. Indeed, faith, like perception, is inchoate knowledge, though not ordinary knowledge since it rests on God's authority rather than on evidence.

That statements are the only speech acts susceptible of truth and falsity appears obvious to Aquinas. No other speech act has a content or says anything that is meant to be assented to and that can actually be assented to or dissented with. No other speech act affirms or denies anything either hypothetically as predication or categorically as affirmation or negation. No other speech act involves a predication and conveys, at least implicitly, a claim to truth about its object (independently of the subject's interests).

That divine revelation has to consist in doctrines stated by God, that the act of faith has to be an act of intellectual assertion (a judgment), and that the allegiance of faith has to consist in intellectual assent to what is affirmed or denied by God in revelation are necessary corollaries of the view that only judgments-statements are true, to all of which corollaries Aquinas subscribes. And doesn't he have in his favor a long Christian tradition that speaks of the Christian faith as true, indeed, as the only true faith or religion?

The silly intellectual problems in which such conception involves Aquinas and his successors deserve attention. Think, in this connection, of the faith of intellectuals being more reduced in scope than that of the vetula, for "scitum non potest esse creditum." Particular attention is called for by the problem of the extrinsecism of the function of the will, that is, of love, in this view of the Christian faith, and, more generally, the problems that arise from the intellectualization of faith and revelation and from the rationalistic divorce in theology between doctrine and action.

For Aquinas, knowledge consists in the adequation of the mind to reality, and truth is reached through the reflective rational grasp of such adequation involved in judging and expressed in the categorical, unconditional statement. Such view may be obvious to someone who is reflecting with a primary interest in truth. Is what is stated by others or believed by myself true? The question, however, is whether reflective statements by people interested in knowledge and truth, as Aquinas is, are identical with the stating speech acts of agents who are not reflecting, much less reflecting with an interest in truth, but who are involved in action and, at least sometimes, deliberating.

Speech acts

The theory of speech acts as acts , as producing effects by which they are defined, may be of help here. Linguistic effects are divided into illocutionary and perlocutionary. This very division, however, already presupposes that language is primarily to be understood, that it can produce its illocutionary effect of being understood independently of its perlocutionary effects, and that it acts or produces its (non illocutionary) effects on that basis or on that condition. This division is itself grounded in a reflective-objectifying understanding of language, rather than in the active-passive or interactive use of language by speaker and addressee--the very reflective understanding of language which the notion of speech-acts and linguistic interaction is supposed to supersede. This, in its turn, results in the separation and privileging of stating speech acts as the only ones that say something to be merely understood, that have a content, that are susceptible of truth and falsity, that have a mind-world fit, and in which the illocutionary force or effect is separable from its perlocutionary force or effect, assuming that statements do indeeed have a perlocutionary force or effect. According to this view, what characterizes language by contrast to other causes that produce effects is that language is to be understood (because it says something? )--illocutionary effect of language), and that, in addition , it in most cases produces and is in all cases meant to produce perlocutionary (that is, not merely illocutionary) effects parasitically on its being understood.

Leaving statements aside for the moment, is it true that the other speech-acts produce illocutionary effects apart from, or in addition to, perlocutionary ones? True, if any native speaker of English hears or reads "Come here," he or she understands what is said. "What is said," however, suggests something to be understood. In fact, however, the speaker is not saying anything to be understood, he is requesting something from someone. The illusion that something to be understood is said arises from taking language read or language overheard as the prototypical case by people who already are competent users of language, when in fact reading and overhearing as a mere contemplator are parasitic uses of language. How indeed would a child ever come to understand "Come here," if nobody ever moved toward the speaker as a result, and if the child were not positively reinforced for moving toward the speaker when asked to come? Or "Bring me the milk" if no action ever obtained as a result? When the supposed something to be merely understood is taken by itself in isolation from the conduct meant and expected by the speaker, the illusion of an illocutionary and primary effect different from the perlocutionary and secondary effect arises. Actually, however, in uttering "Come here" or "Bring me the milk" the speaker doesn't say that anything, that is, doesn't say anything to be understood; he or she gives an order to be obeyed.

OLnly in retrospect does the command appear as a sentence, as something something unbderstood and said to be understood.

What now about statements? Let us begin by realizing that perceiving is a moment of acting. In other words, we perceive applicatively, we perceive respondenda, in terms of originally innate desires and fears that structure our perception. We don't perceive predicatively and we don't perceive affirmatively or negatively. If, therefore, our stating "silent" language expresses our perception, it is not initially predicative or affirmative and negative but applicative.

Secondly, this is also the case with our stating language when we communicate with others in the mode of interaction with the world. "Your mother is on the phone" is not a picture communicated for purposes of obtaining mere understanding or even assent, but a respondendum. The same  with "Fire!" And so on. In all these cases the intended and expected is a conduct informed by the communicated object for response; in other words, the object is communicated as respondendum, not as intelligendum or as assentiendum. (This is confirmed by Searle's famous example "Ich komme von dem Land wo die Zitronen blümen." ). Hence, we are here in the order of the perlocutionary. *

* Of course the perlocutionary is linguistic, locutionary. "Your mother is on the phone" and "Fire!" are not signs of imminent presence, as the master's name is for the dog. Uttered in presence, they denote a precise object for response . However, the connotation as different from the denotation or practical reference to the denoted will arise later. And separated from the denotation or practical reference will give origin to the illocutionary and to the view of language as a primarily and essentially illocutionary system.

Yet my perceptual applicative statement may fail to denote, that is, to refer applicatively the addressee to the denoted. This is the case of disbelief or disagreement. Now only statements are susceptible of disbelief or disagreement. And this is what led Aristotle and Aquinas to declare that truth is an exclusive property of statements. When they declared that, however, they were not actully involved in linguistic interchange; they were overhearing or reading statements, objectifying language as a system, reflecting on language as spectators, and reflecting on it with the exclusive contemplative disinterested interest in the truth of statements which they had already conceived as objective adequation of the stated to reality.

Returning now to disbelief, disbelief is not the same as failure to be referred to what is denoted. Disbelief is a mental act in relation to something that appears in perception or that is objectively stated by somebody. This already presupposes the isolation of the illocutionary as well as its primacy over the perlocutionary. Failure to denote, however, is failure to refer practically to respondendum. The reasons for the failure may vary. Either the addresse has a different practical perception, or the denoted falls beyond the pale of his or her past experience, or the addresse has repeatedly been wrongly referred by the speaker in the past, etc. The reference here is not theoretical or objective, to object of assent, but practical and interested. I tell you "The hammer is in the other room" because I realize that you are looking for the hammer. Or "Your mother is on the phone" because she is object of response for you.

I speak to you as needful exposed subject to needful exposed subject engaged in a temporal world in which the structure of our life is essentially dramatic. Hence our so natural understanding of, and interest in, stories composed of episodes, within which statements mean applicatively and refer practically. According to Aquinas, however, we speak to each other primarily through objective statements to be merely understood and, at best, believed and assented to, because that is what he as a scientific theologian interested in knowledge and truth does. With this view of language is conected a view of the world as populated by permanent substances named by substantive names and possessing mutable accidental qualities, in which space and time play an enigmatic role. Indeed, being neither substances nor accidents, they end up as forms of the sensibility, with Kant. It is this sort of reality that statements supposedly denote.

When Susanne Langer speaks of connotation and denotation by contrast to signs in the case of animals she has a point. However, like Aris in the Metaphysics , she jumps from there to the conclusion that language, and in particular the stating language, is objective-cognitive rather than practical-applicative. True, language creates a distance between the denoted and the response--a distance inaccessible to animals. But the distance is of the order of deliberation and action, not of mere contemplation; it remains practical, it is not theoretical. And this is the case even when one's exposure is neutralized. One then learns the world and others either through personal observation or through the statements of other people as a potential exposed agent. Learning how things behave is not a disinterested exercise for a potentially exposed agent who shares the world with other potentially exposed agents. It is the basis for future instrumental, applicative understanding.

This understanding, however, is not true in the sense of corresponding adequation to reality, for it is not a picture but an applicative understanding whose 'usefulness' will be actualized in the application, which itself will be informed not only by the grasped characteristics of the 'object' but also by the goals of the agent which themselves will contribute to determine the object's coefficient of adversity or favorableness. This is the case with the natural sciences. As for the humanities, one learns from others' narratives and expressive writings the range of human real and imaginary experience.

Martha Nussbaum has rightly argued against the Platonic universalization and absolutization of the good. In practical matters, she concludes, there is no truth. If so, however, there is no truth at all, for 'knowledge' is ultimately applicative and practical. What we 'know' is objects for response in particular situations.

But there is also the reflective level. My interaction with the world and with others reveals myself to me as exposed to other subjects who in their turn are exposed to me, and as sharing the world with other subjects. On this level, within limits, I choose myself as constituting others as subjects or objects, and as being partly consituted by them as subject or object. In other words, on this reflective level, I am revealed to myself as partly responsible respondens to respondenda who have aclaim on me, and as respondendus who has a claim on other responsible respondentes. Even in this order, however, there is no objective truth. My grasp of others and of myself is applicative, not objective, and it is to be realized, not to be contemplated.

All the above calls our attention to the primacy and ultimacy of the perceptual world of the agent exposed to other agents and to the blind action of nature and to the action of groups. It is by reference to that perceptual world of universe that al the rest of our experience acquires its relative value. But there is such a rest of our experience. The perceptual world is primary and ultimate, but not the only one. There is the dimension of aesthetic experience, and there are imaginary worlds.

What about God?

How to talk of God? After all, this question was at the origin of my entire research.

First of all, objective language, which takes its origin in a wrongheaded epistemology, simply won't do. Not only is God, as Hauerwas says, a proper name, but it is the name for a unique character in certain stories of a particular kind. Hence the idea of proving objectively God's existence, independently of distances of exposure, is out. Believing in God is finding oneself exposed to God and in interaction with him. God's existence is of the order of existential distances of exposure, of the order of "there is somebody in the dark," within whose field of action I am. This is the order of exposure to which Sartre refers, though not under that name, in his 'proof' for the Other's existence. And he is right: the Other as such is not accessible to plain observation. If about objects idealism is possible, about subjects idealism is doubly possible, and about God idealism is triply possible.       

What Sartre doesn't realize is that his proof fails as a proof. The Other's existence is not a matter of demonstration. But neither is the existence of anything a matter of demonstration. Theoretical idealism is always possible, and not only about the Other's existence. Idealism, however, is made possible and plausible by a wrong epistemology that misunderstands the practical character of knowledge because it is based on statements as pictures, and ignores the centrality and prinmacy of decision and action.
