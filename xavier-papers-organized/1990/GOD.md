                             IS THERE A GOD?
                                  "We haven't yet decided the
                                  question of God's existence
                                  and you want to eat!"
                                  (Belinsky to Turgenev).
      The question amounts to asking: Do you believe in God? For one  cannot
answer that there is a God without believing  in  God,  and  conversely.  My
answer, therefore, since I believe in  God,  is  that  God  exists,  as  the
answer of those who do not believe in God will necessarily be that God  does
not exist.
      A further question question is: Since you  believe  in  God,  can  you
prove that God exists? To reassure those of you who believe in  God  and  to
enrage those of you who don't, I could easily tell you that the four to  six
minutes allotted to me are hardly  sufficient  time  to  deal  with  such  a
complex  and  important  question  as  God's  existence   and   to   expound
perusasively the proofs traditionally  offered  for  it.  This  would  imply
that, given sufficient time, I could persuade of God's existence  all  those
of  you  who  are  sufficiently  intelligent.  But  I  refuse  to  use  that
subterfuge, for it would be dishonest on my part. Affixed to my office  door
I have a cartoon that portrays a clergyman finishing  his  demonstration  of
God's existence with the following words: "And this is the way in  which  we
could prove God's existence!  Any  questions?."  Needless  to  say  all  the
members of the audience portayed in the cartoon  have  their  hands  raised.
That is exactly what I think of the proofs. In other  words,  my  answer  to
the question is that God's existence cannot be proven.
      I seem to have put myself, as a believer, and at least  those  of  you
who share with me the faith in the God of Christianity, in a horrible  bind.
For I  seem  to  have  implicitly  avowed  that  believing  in  the  God  of
Christianity is irrational. In fact,  however,  the  open  avowal  that  the
existence of God cannot be proven is meant to clear the way for a  sensible,
honest dialogue on  the  important  issue  of  God  which  brought  us  here
tonight, for this avowal is meant to display on  the  table  a  hidden  card
often played by those who do not believe in God. How so?
      Recently I was asked by one of my students who  may  be  here  tonight
why most philosophers do not believe in God. I think  that  the  answer  is:
because, as philosophers, they have been schooled into  believing  that  one
can assent rationally only to that which can be proven. Now,  I  agree  with
them that God's existence cannot be proven, but I deeply disagree with  them
on the presupposition that one can rationally assent only to that which  can
be proven.
Why? Well, take  one  of  the  great  models  of  those  philosophers,  Rene
Descartes. I cannot but presume that it is one of  their  models,  for  they
take you, the students, again and again,
from the course of intro to philosophy on,  through  Descartes'  Meditations
and The Discourse on Method. What is Descartes' method? It  comes  to  this:
to doubt whatever cannot be proven. In other words, they are  schooling  you
into the same presupposition they themselves were schooled. And  this  under
the  name  of  rationality!  Now,  ask  those  philosophers  how  successful
Descartes was in his  endeavor.  The  answer  I  get  from  practically  all
contemporary philosophers is that Descartes failed completely, that  he  was
unable even to prove his own existence, let alone God's existence which  was
supposed to support his proof for the existence of  the  world  and  of  the
people surrounding him. In short, if those philosophers who think that  only
that which can be proven can be assented to rationally lived  by  their  own
presupposition, they would and should be sceptics all the way, not  only  in
religious matters. What I personally resent is that many of them,  to  avoid
the bind of scepticism which would leave them without  a  job  (which  after
all they need to eat, as  everyone  else  does),  apply  their  standard  of
rationality very selectively to religious issues. For themselves  to  assent
to God's existence and to consider those who believe in  God  anything  less
than irrational, they demand proofs. For all the rest of their  convictions,
of which they have plenty, as we all do, they have no trouble giving up  the
standard of rationality invented  by  the  Greeks  and  perpetuated  by  the
Scholastics, and are content to go by what we all go in life,  to  wit:  our
best lights.
      But is it true that most philosophers induce you, the  students,  into
thinking that either  God's  existence  can  be  proved,  or  it  cannot  be
rationally assented to? Well, just take
at random any book of intro to philosophy, take whatever  textbook  you  are
using or used in  your  introductory  course,  and  go  to  the  section  of
philosophy of religion. Invariably you will find Saint Anselm's  ontological
proof, Aquinas' five proofs, perhaps Descartes' ontological  proof,  on  the
one hand, and Hume's attack on the proofs on the  other.  And  if  the  book
pretends to be up to date, you will find, in addition,  an  article  by  one
more  or  less  contemporary  scientist  offering  a  version  of   Aquinas'
teleological proof, and an  article  by  another  scientist  (most  probably
Monod) arguing that the universe  is  the  result  of  blind  evolution,  or
furnishing a reshuffled version  of Hume's argument against  finality  based
on the evil that goes rampant in the world. In other words, under  the  name
of philosophy and of rationality, the very way in which the issue  of  God's
existence is raised sets the trap, for  it  presupposes  that  either  God's
existence can be proved, or it is irrational to assent to it.

I said that I resent this double standard. Indeed, I do. The reason is  that
most philosophers are teachers, given the sad fact that there are  not  many
other things philosophers can do, myself  included.  And,  as  I  happen  to
consider the issues related to God among the most important  that  we  human
beings raise, I resent the position of those students  who  believe  in  God
being cheaply attacked and shattered by  submitting  it  to  a  standard  of
rationality by which the teachers themselves do not  guide  their  lives  in
any other matter, and the position of  those  who  do  not  believe  in  God
going cheaply unchallenged and  reinforced  for  the  same  reason.  In  the
measure that this happens, and I think  that  it  happens  very  frequently,
you, the students, are poorly served,  and  we  all  are  poorly  placed  to
entertain a sensible dialogue concerning God.
                                  - II -
The question of God's existence is an existential question
      I have so far argued that there is no such thing as a "proof"  in  the
sense of a necessary and infallible demonstration of anything--the  kind  of
demonstration that  Aristotle  dreamed  of  in  his  Metaphysics.  Although,
fortunately, most contemporary philosophers admit that, it seldom  shows  in
the way in which they introduce students  to  philosophy.  Take  practically
any book of intro. Whether the approach is by great figures in  the  history
of philosophy or by  problems,  it  comes  to  the  same:  it  goes  through
problems that were raised by philosophers  who  had  as  their  implicit  or
explicit ideal of rationality to assent only  to  what  can  be  established
with absolute certainty on the basis of evidence--from Plato  to  Hegel--and
to criticize, that is, to show that all the  proposed  solutions  fail.  The
students, however, are clever enough not to take too seriously the  question
whether the world by which they  are  surrounded  exists  or  whether  other
people think and feel as they do. But they seldom  are sophisticated  enough
