APPENDIX TO PART III: HUMAN AND DIVINE

373-377: A Platonic streak in Aris'  ethics:  The  superiority  of       the
contemplative life. A number of passages outside the     ethical works  that
seem to prescribe for humans a  quasi-    divine,  intellect-centered  life.
But these passages are  not    incompatible  with  phronesis  as  explained.
Happiness may have     many elements,  and  the  contemplative  one  be  the
highest.
      But EN X, 6-8 is truly incompatible, for  there  Aris  holds      that
eudaimonia is identical with the activity of the best    part of  the  human
being, viz., the theoretical  intellect.       Contemplation  is  explicitly
held to be the only activity      that is worthy of love or choice  for  its
own sake 1177b31-      4). Moreover, at a certain point Aris  himself  takes
the   Platonic external point of view rather  than  the     anthropological,
and considers inferior the good life of      humans  to  the  good  life  of
gods. Once this point of view is  taken, Plato reigns.  Nussbaum's  Verdict:
No absolute      reason to dispute      autheniticity.  But  these  passages
do not      fit into the     argument of EN. The passages  discussed  above,
from a wide      variety of contexts, show that  Aris  was  in    some  ways
deeply      attracted to the Platonic position,    though he rejects  it  in
the bulk of his mature ethical and      political  writing.     Perhaps,  as
someone who had personally   tasted the scholarly   or  contemplative  life,
there were  moments when he felt that it eclipsed all other pursuits.

INTERLUDE 2: LUCK AND THE TRAGIC EMOTIONS

378-394:     Aris  has  a  high  regard  for  tragedy.  Let  us  study   the
relationship between tragic action and  the  tragic  character,   and    the
nature and value of the tragic emotions.
379: In Poetics (1450a15-20), [NARRATIVE] he claims  that  a  work      that
simply displays characters of a certain sort  without      showing  them  in
action would be deficient in the values      proper to trgedy.  He  dekfends
this claim by indicating that     action has  an  intimate  connection  with
human eudaimonia             that merely being a certain sort of person,  by
itself, does     not. Something about  human  eudaimonia  is  shown  in  the
plots       of the great tragedies. What is that something?
         Plato's    Republic    recommends    speeches    describing     and
praising the goodness of  good  people.  Tragedy,  by  contrast,    includes
character along with the representation  of              action: we see  the
characters by seeing them choosing and       doing. Aris' point is that  the
value of tragic action is a       practial value; having  a  good  character
is not sufficient            for the fullness of good living.
382:  The great tragic plots explore the gap between our goodness   and  our
good living and eudaimonia, between what  we  are           (our  character,
intentions, aspirations, values) and how           humanly  well  we  manage
to live given the adverse          circumstances.  They  show  us  reversals
happening to good-     charactered but not divine  or  invulnerable  people,
exploring    the  many  ways  in  which  being   of   certain   good   human
character falls short of sufficiency for eudaimonia.
383: Connection between the gap and Aris' views  of  the  role  of       two
tragic emotions: pity and  fear.  "ARIS,  LIKE  PLATO,        BELIEVES  THAT
EMOTIONS ARE INDIVIDUATED NOT SIMPLY  BY  THE   WAY  THEY  FEEL,  BUT,  MORE
IMPORTANTLY, BY THE KIND OF  JUDGMENTS  OR  BELIEFS  THAT  ARE  INTERNAL  TO
EACH. A          TYPICAL ARISTOTELIAN EMOTION IS DEFINED AS A  COMPOSITE  OF
      FEELING OF EITHER PELASURE OR PAIN AND A PARTICULAR TYPE  OF    BELIEF
ABOUT THE WORLD. THE FEELING AND THE BELIEF ARE NOT       JUST  INCIDENTALLY
LINKED: THE BKELIEF IS THE  GROUND  OF  THE      FEELING.  EMOTIONS  MAY  BE
ASSESSED EITHER TRUE OR FALSE,    RATIONAL  OR  IRRATIONAL,  DEPENDING  UPON
THE NATURE OF THEIR    GROUNDING BELIEFS.  Well,  the  belief  structure  of
pity and    fear stands or falls with views about  the  importance  of  luck
in human life that would be accepted  by  Aris  and  by  most       ordinary
people, but rejected by Plato.  Pity is  an  emotion        directed  toward
another  person's  pain;  it  requires  the   belief  that  the  person   is
suffering, sometimes through bad  luck. Pity differs from moral  censure  or
blame: it requires     the belief  that  the  person  did  not  deserve  the
suffering,  and is closely connected  with  the  belief  that  you  yourself
are vulnerable in similar ways. ((BY CONTRAST, FOR PLATO OR    SOCRATES  BAD
THINGS ARE TRIVIAL BECAUSE THEY ARE HAPPENING      JUST TO  HIS  BODY).  But
if we believe with Aris that  human       goodness  is  not  sufficient  for
eudaimonia, then pity will be     an important and valuable human response.
385: Fear: painful emotion connected with the expectation  of   future  harm
of pain without our being able  to  prevent  it.       (Connected  with  our
sense of passivity before the events in       the  world.  What  happens  to
another whom I pity may happen to        me.  By  contrast,  Socrates  fears
nothing, for the only thing  that  is  of  serious  importance  for  him  is
secure. For Plato,     fear is to be excluded as unworthy; no sense  in  him
of    vulnerability and passivity.

Perhaps Aris ranks history below poetry      because the figures of  history

388-391: Katharsis: The work katharos  means  limpid,  without    obstacles.
For Plato it is clear intellectual vision through  which one learns.  For  a
middle-period Platonist it would  be      `shocking  to  read  of  cognitive
clarification produced by         the influence of pity  and  fear,  because
the Platonic soul      gets to clarity only when  no  emotions  disturb  it,
and   because  these emotions are especially irrational. By   contrast,  for
Aris, these emotions in us are themselves    pieces of recognition upon  the
worldly condition of our     aspirations to  goodness.  Clarification  takes
place through    emotional responses.

      Plato was a poet who controls the poetic elements  and    subordinates
