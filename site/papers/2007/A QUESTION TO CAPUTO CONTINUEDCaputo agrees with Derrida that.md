            A QUESTION TO CAPUTO CONTINUED

      Caputo agrees with Derrida that language or textuality is
inescapable. We have no other contact with reality than through language.
And language does not deliver in presence that to which it refers. It only
refers to it, and refers to it with differance, that is almost refers.

      In its truth-claiming forms language refers in a unique way, by
saying what it means: “The cup is on the table” says what it means or
refers to, but doesn’t deliver it in presence. Thus the statement is an
interpretation. And since delivering in presence is beyond the scope of
referring language, no particular interpretation can justifiably be true to
the exclusion of all other interpretations.

      In other words, language can only bring us to the door of the
promised land of reality, but leaves us outside, for it can only refer us
but not bring us to The August Presence. What Derrida gives us is another
version of the failure of Kant’s “intuition” to deliver us the thing in
itself. And the reason is simple. The presence to which Derrida and most
philosophers have in mind is phenomenal presence, presence of object to
mind.

      I analyzed that form of presence in my paper “On McIntyre,
Rationality and Dramatic Space,” by contrast to the double-sided, efective
presence which is ours in the world. Our presence is effective presence –
presence of exposure to the action of what we perceive, which by the same
token is exposed to our action. This is, on the subject’s part, dramatic
presence whose subject is the exposed agent by contrast to the Phenomenal
presence whose subject is an un-inhabited, contemplated contemplator, that
is, no subject.

      Thus my question to Caputo is definitely answered: his Derridean
linguistic deconstructionism does fail to open a space for the faith of
faith. And what is the alternative? The alternative is to inhabit our
dramatic presence, as we willy-nilly do in our everyday life. But what is
the appropriate kind of discourse for this in-habitation? Narrative
discourse, but without hope of persuasive closure with those who are not
members of our culture, since only with the members of the same culture is
the same history shared.
