1 - Two sources of knowledge and two complementary doctrines or ‘truths’

In the first article of the Summa Th (cf. note 10 in Citmine) Aquinas introduces this view.

There are two sources of knowledge–revelation and reason–and two kinds of knowledge or doctrines or truths–the revealed ones and the ones reached by reason left to its own devices. The former has to do with the process of acquisition of knowledge, the latter with the product of the process.

Aquinas maintains the unity of truth . Thereby he is not referring to the sources but to the product. Truth is one means that the truths revealed and the truths of reason complement each other harmoniously, that there is no possible conflict between them, and that together thes complementary truths form one order--the order of knowledge, of what demands our assent either on the basis of God’s authority or on the basis of its own evidence. I think that the metaphor that underpins this view of the unity of truth is that of a complete, harmonious picture, a portion of which is contributed by revelation and the other portion by reason.

Philosophy and theology . So far we have dealt with revelation and reason as sources of knowledge, of truth. How do philosophy and theology relate to revelation and reason? Philosophy seems identical with reason as one of the sources of truth different from revelation, and the truths or knowledge reached through the use of reason exclusively is philosophical knowledge. Theology, however, is not identical with revelation, and is not one of the sources of knowledge identified by Aquinas in his first article. Neither is theology identical with the product of revelation, with the truths revealed, with the knowledge gained through the assent of faith.

What then is theology and how does it compare to philosophy? The philosophical truths complement harmoniously the revealed truths, according to Aquinas. But what does theology do? What is its place? What does it produce?

Theology is an academic discipline. And so is philosophy. Both are therefore applications of reason, but in a very different way. Reason, as we know, is human intelligence left to its own devices. But in theology reason is not left to its own devices, since its object is what has been revealed and what is revealed is assented to in faith by the theologian. Theology, therefore, is a reflective discipline, a discipline that reflects on what is already known or assented to in faith.

Faith, however, is not reflective. The revealed is not object for faith in the way in which the revealed is object for theology. For theology the revealed is object of study
