2000-November-1

PIAGET AND PRACTICAL KNOWLEDGE

...Jacqueline tries again and again to bring a rooster through the bars... Everything occurs as though, to the child, the bars constituted pure images without depth or solidity (pictures and not substances) and as though these images could be traversed through and through without difficulty. But why is this so? Precisely because a sensorimotor elaboration has not yet conferred upon them the qualities of resistance and substantiality which they lack Here again it is difficult to speak with the Gestalt psychologists of a sudden reorganization of the perceptual field independently of structuring assimilatory activity: It is the action that fashions the field of perception and not the reverse (Piaget, The Origin of Intelligence in Children , The Norton Library, 1963, p. 314). (See also Piaget, The psychology of Intelligence , Littlefield ,Adams and Co, N.J. 1968, for a complete overview of his entire theory of the development of intelligence).

At the level of groping, representation does not precede action and does not even directly result from it. On the contrary, everything occurs as though the object seen were conceived as being identical to what it appears to be in immediate perception. So it is that... Jacqueline, in order to put a long chain into a small box. confines herself to putting one end of this inside, without rolling up the whole chain or foreseeing the object’s flexibility and fall. Despite the first fruitless efforts she recommences indefinitely and sensorimotor groping alone corrects her vision of things ( Ibid., p. 326).

There exists a sensorimotor or practical intelligence whose functioning extends that of the mechanisms of a lower level: circular reactions, reflexes and, still more profoundly, the morphogenetic activity of the organism itself. That is, it seems to us, the main conclusion of this study ( Ibid., p. 357).

“ Implication is a process indispensable to our needs in adjusting. Without it we would not know how to profit from experience.’ Hence implication is a primitive phenomenon which does not result from repetition, as does association, but on the contrary, from the outset introduces a necessary link between the terms which are implied. Thus implication is rooted in organic life: ‘The organism seems to us, as early as its most reflex manifestations, to be a machine for implication.’ It is also the source of the conditioned reflexes and the circular reactions. Moreover, from its inception it directs even nonsystematic groping. ‘ To imply is to expect, and that is to aim toward what one expects ( Ibid. pp. 2399-400.And see pp. 404-407).

Intelligence, viewed as a whole, takes the form of a structuring which impresses certain patterns on the interaction between the subject or subjects and near or distant surrounding objects (Jean Piaget, Psychology of Intelligence , Littlefield, Adams & Company, Totowa, New Jersey, 1966, p.167)

What interests me in Piaget

Two things, fundamentally. (1) The foundational importance of sensorimotor intelligence, which is self-referential or self-centered. Image follows interaction with the environment, and interaction is guided by need. (2) Although not at all explicit in Piaget (who, I think, ultimately tends to be intellectualistic, objectivistic, scientistic), the importance of my-body-for-myself as the foundation of all knowledge, that is, as the foundation of all teleological interaction with the environment initiated by the agent to achieve his or her goals. This objectivism or scientism gives origin to the notion of absolute space and time which admit of no situation, of no self-referentiality, which are therefore  ahistorical and exclude deliberation and human action.
