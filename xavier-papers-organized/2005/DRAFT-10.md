FAITH AND THEOLOGY

Objective and hermeneutic disciplines

Geometry, physics, engineering, biology are 'the same' in a significant sense, whether practiced by Chinese or Americans, by Catholics or by atheists, in a way in which certain other disciplines--history, biblical hermeneutics, philosophy, ethics...--are not. The latter are, so to say, culturally-loaded; their practitioner's convictions and implicit cultural presuppositions unavoidably shape the results. In connection with this, the former are a mere matter of understanding in which there is no room  for emotion, whereas in the latter there is. (I seem to remember that somewhere in the NE or in the Rhetoric Aris alludes to the fact that in matters of deliberation--where the people who deliberate, or those they care for, will be affected by the outcome of the action--people get excited and even mad at each other in a way in which they do not when they disagree in purely intellectual matters. There may, therefore, be a special connection between matters of deliberation and decision and hermeneutic disciplines).

Often the not purely objective disciplines are called "hermeneutic disciplines." These are vaguely characterized by the involvement--affective involvement?--of the practitioner as both subject who attempts to understand and object to be understood. Perhaps the difference between hermeneutic and non-hermeneutic disciplines may be expressed in the following way. Non-hermeneutic disciplines can only be practiced from the objective contemplator's point of view, whereas hermeneutic disciplines can, at least conceivably, be practiced from two different points of view: that of the objective observer and that of the involved participant. The involved participant is a needful, desirous subject, exposed however as an object to the observation as well as to the perception and the action of others (and even to his or her own reflective thought). As so exposed, the participant can be observed objectively, that is, not from his/her own point of view, not even from the point of view of any involved agent, but from a purely theoretical--descriptive and explanatory--point of view without a point of view(?) that applies appropriately to objects rather than to subjects.

An example may help. A human action may be understood as a movement or as an intentional act. A purely objective observer incapable of empathy would necessarily attempt to understand it as a movement and to explain it by reference to the environment, as behaviorists actually do. An empathetic observer , by contrast, would draw on his or her experience as a human agent-patient and understand it as an intentional human act, as the teleological act of an agent, of a subject who is center of perspective and instrumentality. There is, in  addition, the practical understanding of the interacting agent-patient, of the exposed participant , an understanding that informs the participant's response. (As Sartre suggests, exposed agents understand practically the Other and are practically understood by the Other as subjects, as centers of instrumentality, as transcendences however transcended).

I just spoke of the empathetic observer. Now, empathy is neither antipathy nor sympathy; in this sense, it suggests neutrality and is often understood as neutral. For instance, it is sometimes said that history of religion requires empathy (since religion is a mode of being in the world, a human-activity, a conduct rather than a behavior), but that the historian's empathy should be neutral. Yet, the historian of religion is either religious or a-religious and, consequently, his or her empathy will unavoidably be sympathetic or antipathetic. In the latter case, the historian of religion understands as an outsider, not as an insider. By contrast, the insider inhabits the same universe of discourse-action as the agent.

To say that both the outsider and the insider "understand" may be correct, but it obscures the difference in their understanding. The outsider has to enter sympathetically into the shoes of the subject understood and his or her objects for response--shoes which are different from his or her own--, whereas the insider wears the same shoes as the subject to be understood and, thus, understands him or her connaturally. What we are speaking about, though, is observer's understanding, theoretical or contemplative understanding . What about participant's understanding, practical understanding ?

Concretely, the point at stake may boil down to the following. Practical understanding is the understanding that actually informs one's practice, regardless of whether one's practice corresponds or not to one's stated beliefs. At any given moment, one's practice is informed by one understanding which coincides with what one is and is reflected in one's response to the object. Now, theoretical understanding abstracts object from response to it by the researcher (though not necessarily by the studied subjects in the hermeneutic disciplines). In other words, the researcher as such researches from nowhere, from  the point of view of Cereus, as far as his or her own standpoint is concerned. Therefore his or her own responses are not at stake and will not be modified by the way in which the object is object for response (for others). In practical understanding, by contrast, the agent's own responses are at stake, for the agent is exposed to the object either actually or potentially . In the latter case, knowledge is practical, in-formative, knowledge of gerundive object, of object for response, based on past practice of involved agent and for the purpose of in-forming future practice.

It seems that the difference between theology as an academic discipline, which is theoretical, and Christian reflection by Christian agents, which is practical, is to be analyzed along these lines. It seems, too, that the conflicts and confusions between theology as an academic discipline and practical and pastoral, intra-communitary, theology are to be disarmed, insofar as they can be disarmed, on the basis of such analysis.      

Faith and theology

Let us now deal directly with faith and theology. Faith is not an academic discipline in any significant sense, for the purpose of faith is not to acquire knowledge. Faith--being Christian--is a particular, distinctive mode of being in the world and related to the world. (Insofar as this mode of being in the world, whose correlate is a particular world inhabited, is shared by a number of people, it results in a distinctive society with its distinctive institutions and practices). Both the world and the mode of being in the world are 'intentional', that is, human-acts rather than movements. People implicitly or non-thetically understand their own conduct and spontaneously explain the conduct of others and their own by reference to the objects for response that constitute the world they inhabit. As one very naturally answers the question "Why did you run?" by "Because a dog came barking at me," so one answers "Why did you kneel?" "Because I was at church" (that is, in God's sacramental presence). NOTICE THAT THESE ARE EXPLANATIONS GIVEN BY EXPOSED AGENTS TO OTHER INTERESTED EXPOSED AGENTS WHO INHABIT 'THE SAME' WORLD; NOT EXPLANATIONS GIVEN BY OBSERVERS, FOR THE OBSERVERS CONDUCT IS NOT ITSELF AT STAKE AND, THEREFORE, IS NOT TO BE EXPLAINED IN ANY WAY.

If faith is not an academic discipline, what about theology? Here some distinctions are in order.

There is a level on which the distinction between faith and theology is not tight. As just suggested, people do not only act; they also reflect on what they do. One thing is to genuflect intentionally before the Eucharist, another to know reflectively that one genuflects as an acknowledgement of and a response to Christ's sacramental presence. In other words, the latric-love of Christ present in the Eucharist informs the action of genuflecting, which is the level of the direct practice of faith. But people are reflective. Not only do they act intentionally, that is, with a practical awareness of what they do, but sometimes need (for didactic and other purposes) to explain to themselves and to others their own actions, to render fully explicit their reasons, when the intelligibility itself of their actions becomes thematic. For instance, when a young child asks a parent "Why do you kneel at church?." Without an explanation of the reason the maintenance and the transmission of a human practice would be impossible.

Since people are reflective, since they do not only act but also ask questions about their actions and their respective objects, this type of theology as intra-communitary reflection on the practice of faith is itself part of the faith, of the Christian being in the world . Indeed, no lasting practice of faith is possible without it, nor can human beings who are reflective do without any reflection. This reflection, however, is intra-communitary, that is, among people who practice the practice, who respond  or are interested in responding in the characteristically Christian way to the characteristically Christian objects for response for the characteristically Christian reasons-affections .

In addition (and this may be distinctive of the Christian faith because of its very special historical dimension), the Christian community, the Church, had historical beginnings, a foundational historical moment which is itself constitutive of its faith-identity. This was the experience of Jesus by those who came into contact with Him, who followed Him, and who ultimately believed in Him after the resurrection . (Many others came into contact with Jesus who did not believe in Him and did not write Gospels). This experience was historico-dramatic and is told in the Gospels . This story or these stories present Jesus as object looking for response as well as the responses He got and how he responded further to those responses--a sequence of episodes which reveal a concrete person, a character. The experience-told in the Gospels is not the experience-itself ; it is the experience told and its selectivity shows that it is already informed by a reflective knowledge of Jesus that the people involved in the stories did not have at the time. NOTICE, HOWEVER, THAT THIS 'REFLECTIVE' KNOWLEDGE IS KNOWLEDGE OF EXPOSED AGENTS AND FOR PRACTICAL PURPOSES OF ENHANCING THE PRACTICE. These stories, therefore, are already theological stories, stories informed by the reflective realization of who Jesus was. The canonization of these stories, which obviously are the core of the Christian scriptural canon, marks them as the foundational theological narrative of the foundational experience of Jesus .

So far, then, we have two theological moments which are part and parcel of the practice of the Christian faith. Both of these moments are intra-communitary, that is, carried out or lived by people of faith and for the sake of people of faith . It is important, however, to remark that this 'theology' is totally subordinated to the practice of the faith: its purpose is to enrich, to enhance, to preserve, to teach the practice . Its purpose is not to know for knowledge's sake. But this is not to say enough. For this theology--or theological knowledge, if you want--is only theology or theological knowledge insofar as it actually or potentially functions as such, that is, insofar as it actually or potentially informs action . In other words, it is not object to be assented to (that is, to be known by itself and contemplatively), but to be transcended applicatively in informed action , much as money is not object to be theoretically recognized as money, but to be practically recognized in using it as money.  

A third reflective moment arises when it is not a matter of understanding and enhancing and transmitting the practice but of contesting either a particular practice or, more radically, the object for response. Example: "Why do you kneel at church?" "Because of the sacramental presence." "But there is no sacramental presence."

Contesting (which differs from deliberating about future Xtin action) presupposes a rift in community between the contestant and those contested. In this sense, it is not intra-communitary , except if the contested practice or object for response is peripheral rather than substantive, so that the community remains quoad substantiam beyond the particular practice or the particular object for response. THIS IS THE INTERESTING CASE FOR THEOLOGY AS A NEUTRAL DISCIPLINE, BECAUSE IT INVOLVES THE TRANSFORMATION OF OBJECT FOR RESPONSE INTO THEORETICAL OBJECT 'OBJECTIFIED' FOR EXPLANATION OR DEMONSTRATION..

Indeed, the agent is either actually or potentially exposed to the object, for otherwise the object would not be object for response. Furthermore, the agent who responds perceives gerundively, applicatively, evaluatively. Now, when two agents disagree , one way of overcoming their disagreement is to present to the other the object for response as each of them perceives it, which is what the narratives of the New Testament do for the substance of Christianity. In this case the object-Jesus is object for response and, therefore, not purely objective but evaluative, as the characters of all realistic narratives are, even the imaginary ones. This way of overcoming disagreement, in matters of faith, is supported by the respective witnessing of the agents who live their faith, and sometimes succeeds in being instrumental for the conversion of one or the other of the contestants. But there is another--RADICALLY unsuccessful--attempt at overcoming disagreements, and THIS IS THE INTERESTING ONE: FOR IT INVOLVES THE TRANSFORMATION  OF OBJECT FOR RESPONSE INTO OBJECT FOR CONTEMPLATION. In this case, the contestants objectify the object, treat it is object of contemplation to be explained. AND THIS CORRESPONDS TO THE PRACTICE OF THEOLOGY AS AN ACADEMIC DISCIPLINE, WHICH ITSELF IS CONNECTED WITH THE GREEK CONCEPTION OF PHILOSOPHY AS A CONTEMPLATIVE AND NEUTRAL WAY OF

ACCESS TO THE TRUTH. THIS IS WHY THE SCHOLASTICS MAKE THEOLOGY AND PHILOSOPHY--THE TRUTHS OF REVELATION AND THE TRUTHS OF REASON--COMPLEMENTARY. THIS DOES NOT MAKE SENSE, BECAUSE PHILOSOPHY IS A SCIENCE, AND ACADEMIC DISCIPLINE, A SYSTEM, NOT A WAY OF BEING IN THE WORLD, WHEREAS THE CHRISTIAN FAITH IS A WAY OF BEING IN THE WORLD (cf. Van Steenberghen ).

The objectified object for response is not anymore object for response, for it is neither desired nor averted. It is simply considered by itself and in its pure intelligibility, that is, in an explanatory, abstract, universal intelligibility that has nothing to do with desire or aversion, with the projects of the researcher. This is what Galileo rightly did with the fall of bodies on inclined planes. Now, when it is a matter of things and of their intelligibility (as is the case with fall), which do not and cannot function as subjects, as centers of perspective and instrumentality, nothing is ignored by objectification. But when it is a matter of people, who can and do to a large extent function as subjects, who have their own purposes and projects, who therefore have 'dignity' and are intrinsically gerundive, the subjective dimension of their reality is ignored by objectification, which hence amounts to a falsification. Now, in disputed matters of faith, or at least in matters of the Christian faith, we are always dealing with subjects, with subjects as objects for response, be they God, Christ, or other people. When, say, the sacramental presence is contested, what is contested is Jesus' eucharistic exposure to us and our consequent response to Him. Denying His presence is denying that his divine dignity is involved in our eucharistic practices. But how can objectification by the contesting parties lead to agreement? What is observed objectively is the same for both contestants, but it systematically excludes the other as subject, for the other as subject can only be experienced in exposure to it (Sartre). If this is true in matters of ordinary agential experience, how much more it is in matters of faith-experience. Hence objectification in theology is not a way of reaching the truth, of reaching knowledge .

But what about religion as a phenomenon, about theology as religious studies ?

Of course the religiosity of people can be studied, and the 'beliefs' and even the 'practices' of different religious people can be studied. Perhaps interesting similarities and dissimilarities may be discovered in this way. If we identify theology with this study, theology as religious studies is

possible and, perhaps, enlightening in certain regards. However, two questions arise: (1) Is this theology objective? (2) Are the 'religions' so understood the religions lived by the people who practice them?

(1) As remarked before, neutral empathy is not possible.

I may 'understand' somebody who practices abortion for the sake of keeping a beautiful body; I may even sympathize with (pity?) her ignorance because I appreciate it as not culpable. However, I can not admire her or even consider her action indifferent. Thus, I am not impartial at all, I understand-evaluate-respond from the standpoint which I am. The reason for this is that understanding-knowledge is practical--that is, arises from the practice which it informs and is meant to enhance that practice. (Even my understanding of the Other who practices abortion is practical--I understand her as object for response, given my general way of being situated in the world. The Other who practices abortion also understand-knows me the way I understand her, that is, not impartially. (N.B. Sometimes, unlike the captkain of the Brittish boat, there is time to deliberate before acting, to explore more carefully the object for response or the response to object).

(2) If the above is correct concerning understanding-knowing, then theology as religious studies is not impartial either, except for the fact that the researcher's practice is itself not at stake and is systematically kept out of the equation, though obviously it informs the judgment passed on the practitioners of religion and of different religions, if judgment is passed at all. And if it is impartial it is not understanding of the religion lived by the religious people studied, except descriptively, but it cannot pass judgment of validity, which amounts to saying that the researcher would not be capable of determining his or her own course of action in the world. Would the researcher practice a religion or not, and if so which one?

Catholic theology: A neutral discipline?

My interest in the difference mentioned at the beginning between objective and hermeneutic disciplines and between theology as an academic discipline and faith arises from the fact that I believe that the present controversy on theology provoked by "Ex corde Ecclesiae" is closely related to this difference. Indeed, it seems to me that today many academicians see theology as a scientific discipline, and by this they understand an 'impartial,' uncommitted, neutral, objective discipline of the type of geometry, physics and engineering, or at best of the type of impartial history or philosophical history of religion, whereas the Encyclical sees theology both as an impartial and as a committed discipline at the same time and as a theoretical and a practical discipline at the same time. As for myself, I think that neither position makes sense. Not the Encyclical's, because no discipline can be impartial and partial, theoretical and practical, at the same time. Not the academicians', because theology is, at best, a hermeneutic, not a purely objective or intellectual discipline, and theology as traditionally practiced is for the most part an intra-communitary, not a merely extra-communitary reflection which, as such, involves the practical convictions of the researcher and, consequently, is not neutral nor is heard as a neutral discipline by the non-professional members of the Church.

It is an odd fact that certain Catholic theologians speak as if theology were a neutral, purely objective discipline, and yet do not dare to deny flatly that the Catholic bishops have some (vague, undefined) right or even obligation to intervene in  departments of theology of Catholic universities. For instance, Notre Dame's Theology Dept: "This is not to deny the right of the bishops to have a concern about the quality and depth of Catholic education in those institutions that bear the name of Catholic" (p.2). Again, O'Meara (Notre Dame, second part, p. 1): "It is not outrageous that the theology department of a Catholic university may be expected by the bishops and by certain church bodies concerned with teaching to give a reasonable account of teaching Christianity and the Roman Catholic understanding of Christianity ." It seems that anybody who believes that theology is a neutral discipline whose results are, or at least should be, independent from the convictions of theologians should deny the bishops' right, for accepting the bishops right to give a determination in matters of theological truth amounts to accepting that Catholic bishops as Catholic bishops have an expertise or an authority that mere theologians do not have. Thus, these Catholic theologians, no less than the Apostolic Constitution itself, are affirming at the same time the autonomy and the non-autonomy of theology (at least within certain limits, namely, when the positions of theologians diverge from the Catholic positions in substantive matters of faith or morals). So, they are no less confused than the Encyclical, which makes such statements as the following: "...a Catholic university is distinguished by its free search for the whole truth about nature, man and God" (p. 9), and "It is in this context of the impartial search for truth that the relationship between faith and reason is brought to light and meaning" P. 9). "... by its Catholic character a university is made more capable of conducting an impartial search for truth , a search that is neither subordinated to nor conditioned by particular interests of any kind" (p. 10).  The same Encyclical, though, defends the bishops' right to intervene when the results of the supposedly neutral or impartial search for truth are not the ones they know through faith.

Confronted with this mess, certain Catholic theologians propose a radical separation of theology from faith. Theology is a scientific discipline that aims at the truth through strictly scientific means . Faith reaches the truth through revelation. Catholic Bishops know the truth through revelation and are committed to truth as revealed. Theologians aim at the truth scientifically and are professionally committed to search for the truth in that way. But, apart from other problems, what happens in case of disagreement, according to these theologians? One silly possibility is the theory of two truths. ( But why is it silly? Because theology is a practical discipline that cannot neutralize the researcher's practical convictions ). Another more palatable alternative is the view that maintains the asymptotic, necessary convergence of reason and faith, in the long or infinite run, in matters common  to both . In the meantime, however, to which of the two truths is the Catholic theologian to give his or her practical allegiance? And to which will he or she suggest that the students taught by him/her give their allegiance? Is it even possible to maintain this distinction given the practical character of theology, its

in-formational character, and the fact that faith as doctrine is abstracted from practice and refers to object for response and to response by exposed agent? It is interesting to remark that only those who are Catholics may undergo these conflicts, for only they believe something to be the truth as Catholics and discover something other to be (probably?) the truth as theologians or as taught by theologians. By contrast, those who have no faith will at most change their minds when they discover a truth that contradicts what they previously believed to be the truth. The interest of the remark is that it indicates that the Catholic as such, not as an academician, is not neutral but committed. But committed how: through mere assent?

One does not reach faith through a neutral search for the truth such as it is supposedly practiced in theological schools, anymore that one can reach the knowledge of the divinity of Christ through a historically neutral research of the type of that criticized by Kierkegaard, assuming that there is such a thing . (The silly idea of the possibility of such theoretical, neutral way to certain matters of faith or "praeambula fidei" arises from the marriage with the Platonic notion of contemplative, neutral reason. THIS IS THE MARRIAGE THAT TOOK PLACE IN THE twelfth-thirteenth CENTURIES).  The truth reached through theological study consists in a predicative statement to which assent is given by virtue of and proportionate to the evidence presented in its favor. The fact that it is predicative , though, makes it radically different from faith, which is not an act of predication, and the fact that the object of academic theology is object of predication makes it radically different from the object of faith, which is not object of predication.

Authenticity and faith

"For a person who does not cheat, what he or she believes to be true must determine his or her action."

"Not those who say 'Lord, Lord!,' but those who do my Father's will."

There is a practical dimension to knowledge, or at least to certain kinds of knowledge. If I believe-know that life is absurd, I live one way; if I believe-know that Christ rose from the dead, I live another way.

If we are to assimilate the Christian faith to knowledge, it is clear that it has to be assimilated to knowledge that involves a practical dimension, to the kind of knowledge where authenticity--the coherence between knowing and acting, between convictions and actions--has a place.

The case of theology, though, is less clear than the case of the Christian faith.

[ Theology is one of the fields in which this coherence seems to be in order, at least as far as Catholics are concerned. (This coherence is involved in the idea of a Catholic University, where the confessional or convictional dimension has to be reflected in a distinctive practice or at least in a distinctive style). This is why even those Catholics who oppose the bishops' interference with academic freedom in theology do not dare to deny flatly that they have something to say about what goes on in theology departments of Catholic universities. The acknowledgement of the authority of bishops in theological matters, though, is incompatible with the view that theology is a purely objective, neutral discipline. Were theology a purely objective discipline, there would be as little room for disagreement in theology as there is in geometry. Furthermore, were theology a discipline that looks for knowledge for its own sake rather than for the sake of orthopraxis, there would be no room for authenticity. ]  

N.B. Ex Corde Ecclesiae insists that epistemological sophistication--the very epistemological sophistication that its own text obviously lacks--is necessary in certain areas:

"An area that particularly interests a Catholic university is the dialogue between Christian thought and the modern sciences. This task requires persons particularly well versed in the individual disciplines and who are at the same time adequately prepared theologically, and who are capable of confronting epistemological questions at the level of the relationship between faith and reason. Such dialogue concerns the natural sciences as much as the human sciences which posit new and complex philosophical and ethical problems" (# 46).
