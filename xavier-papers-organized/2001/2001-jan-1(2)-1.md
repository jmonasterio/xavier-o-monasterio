         THE PLATONIC-ARISTOTELIAN EPISTEMIC PARADIGM

“ The notion of knowledge as the assemblage of accurate representations is optional and may be replaced by the pragmatist conception that eliminates the traditional dualism between representing the world and coping with it” (R. Rorty, Philosophy and the Mirror of Nature, Princeton U. Press, Princeton, New Jersey 1979), p. 11.

“I argue that the attempt (which has defined traditional philosophy) to explicate ‘rationality’ and ‘objectivity’ in terms of conditions of accurate representations is a self-deceptive effort to eternalize the normal discourse of the day, and that, since the Greeks, philosophy’s self-image has been dominated by this attempt” ( Ibid. ).

“Il (Aristote) reste attache aux doctrines de Platon en bien des points, sans s’en rendre compte lui-meme” (Suzanne Mansion, Le jugement d’existence chez Aristote ,  Desclee de Brower, Paris 1946, p. 61).

The Western philosophical tradition is characterized by being reflective. That is, it represents the thinking, not of the exposed agent actually involved in interaction with the surrounding world, but the thinking of a curious observer withdrawn from interaction, interested in understanding knowledge. Therefore this tradition  reduces the agent and, in general, the ‘object’ –what it conceives as the real, what is the case– to object of observation to be understood from the observing philosopher’s point of view. By the same token it converts the exposed, vulnerable, interacting agent into a neutral, objective observer and subordinates action to vision or knowledge, without realizing with Hume that no factual state of affairs can motivate this contemplated-contemplator to act (Monasterio).

“... in some cases, this discovery of alternatives has been taken for an argument in favor of a kind of philosophical relativism, or at least a view which might be labelled a species of non-realism, according to which reason cannot arbitrate between the alternatives.. Professor Rorty seems to defend a view of this kind... Now, I think there is something deeply mistaken about this non-realist view. It misses the whole point of what we have learned through our challenge to the epistemolgical model. It is not just that the notion of truth, of faithfulness to reality, does not need to be construed on a correspondence model. More fundamentally, it is that the very nature of the discourse whereby we escape the epistemological prison, for instance, is misunderstood if we make this non realist move...  - The point is that the debate is not between two propositions which are rivals in the ordinary way that empirical hypotheses are –say, the big bang versus the steady state cosmologies... - To treat them as rival hypotheses which one cannot arbitrate between by reason is really to fall back into the epistemological perspective... which tends to make us see all knowledge claims as touching a realm of entities beyond our representations; which in turn forces the famous moves: to scepticism, or some distinction between the transcendental and the empirical, or some reduction of the issue of truth to what works... All these moves only made sense within the epistemological paradigm... - But an even more important point arises out of this analysis concerning the limits of philosophical reason . We are not condemned to agnosticism between these two construals of mind-in-the-world; indeed, we cannot be undecided once we understand what is at stake...... But seeing these limits of philosophical reason points to another way of identifying the error in philosophical non-realism... Pure philosophical non-realism would make sense only for a subject who was thoroughly disengaged, who was equidistant from all cultures. A picture of the subject as disengaged, is, of course, another notion generated by the epistemological tradition ” (Charles Taylor, “Philosophy and its History,” in Philosophy in History: Essays on the Historiography of Philosophy, Edited by Richard Rorty, J.B/. Schneewidn,, Quentin Skinner, pp. 28-30).

The Post-moderns have given up on presence and therefore on knowledge and truth. In their view, there is no possible correspondence between what the subject knows and what is the case. Thus leads them to either relativism or total skepticism. Threatened by the perspective of such an outcome, a number of people, especially Christians who have a particularly important stake in the issues of skepticism and of truth (the Vatican’s official line with its constant criticism of modern relativism constitutes a perspicuous example of the attitude I am talking about), oppose the post-moderns by defending the human mind’s capacity to represent reality objectively and grasp the correctness of the representation. A hopeless defense, in my opinion. Yet, within the assumptions shared by both parties concerning knowledge as representational and the relationship of the subject to the world, it has indeed to be a matter of either-or between two alternatives: either epistemic relativism, or objectivism.    

How come, however, that such declared enemies share the same assumptions? And which, precisely, are the assumptions they share? And why, precisely do they share these

assumptions rather than any others? And why these assumptions are made  so spontaneously, to the point of appearing natural and inescapable? And are they, in fact natural and inescapable? And, if they are not, what changes take place as a result of escaping them? These is the cluster of questions I’ll be dealing with here.

My basic hunch is that the origin of these assumptions is to be found in the epistemic views of the Greek philosophers of the golden era, namely, Socrates, Plato and Aristotle, who consecrated the epistemic paradigm which has prevailed till our days. Far from being adequate, however, the paradigm has always proven unsatisfactory. Hence the series of modifications it has undergone since Plato introduced it, from Aristotle through Kant and beyond. What characterizes post-modernity is the full realization of the insufficiency of the paradigm, but without the definite abandonment of it for lack of a viable alternative. Hence the more or less avowed relativism or even full-fledged skepticism of the post-moderns, as well as the stubborn dogmatism of those who refuse to give up on the paradigm in spite of its obvious inadequacy for lack of an acceptable alternative.

Knowledge as correspondence

It is not my intention to explore in depth, much less in their totality, Plato’s and Aristotle’s epistemic views. For my purposes it will suffice to detect and render fully explicit certain fundamental, features that characterize them.

.

At least implicitly, Plato understood knowledge as some kind of VISUAL correspondence or conformity with reality. Granted, Plato is very far from Descartes’ definite subject-object or mind-world split. Consequently, the Cartesian project of an epistemic reconstruction of reality from scratch would have been inconceivable to Plato, as would have been Descartes’ correlative withdrawal from the realm of appearances to the epistemic subject as a starting point. Without the explicit separation of the subject from the object, Plato’s view of the correspondence with reality involved in knowledge had to remain vague, compared to Descartes’ own. In the latter, as explained by Rorty in much detail in Philosophy and the Mirror of Nature , the correspondence took the very definite shape of “knowledge as accurate representation” in “the mind.” ^(F1)  The correspondence of which Plato thinks would probably have to be characterized as a sort of mystical identification with what is. Yet, for all the significant differences between them, there is an undeniable kinship between Plato and Descartes in that knowledge consists for both of them in some kind of correspondence. whit what is.

That Plato understands knowledge as correspondence with what is is evident in his most distinctive doctrine, namely, the doctrine of the Ideas. What, indeed, led Plato to move in such an outlandish direction and postulate the existence of eternal Ideas if not the need to furnish to universal concepts a ‘reality’ that corresponded to them, for without a correspondent reality they would have been object-less or referent-less and therefore false? Moreover (and this will become clearer in a moment, when we deal with Plato’s views on the  knowledge of particulars), the fact that the Platonic Ideas were eternal and immutable insured that the knowledge the philosopher had of them was necessarily true, that is, that the correspondence with what is was absolutely certain and immutable. Indeed, Plato thought, without such necessity the correspondence of knowledge with what is could conceivably fail to obtain, and then what is taken for knowledge would, in the best case, amount to a mere belief, an “opinion”or “ doxa .” In short, without the actual existence of eternal Ideas no knowledge would be valid, for it would lack either or both of the two requirements that Plato deemed essential for knowledge to be genuine knowledge, namely, correspondence with what is , and  necessary correspondence.

So much for the knowledge of intelligible and universal realities. Very different, in Plato’s view, is the case of perceptual knowledge, for  the objects perceived are sensible rather than intelligible, particular rather than universal, and contingent rather than necessarily existing. In fact,  Plato found it impossible to justify the validity of perceptual knowledge.. Why? Precisely because knowledge requires necessary correspondence with what is, and in the case of perception the correspondence with the objects perceived cannot possible be guaranteed, since particular objects are by their very nature temporal and mutable. Hence, not only would the knowledge of sensible objects cease to correspond to them as they changed, but, in addition, even assuming that  a particular perception happened to correspond at least momentarily with the object perceived, this correspondence wouldn’t be necessary but merely contingent, given the mutable nature of the particular object. Hence the knower wouldn’t know with absolute certainty whether or not his or her perception was true, in which case knowledge would be just a “ doxa ,”an uncertain opinion or belief  rather than genuine knowledge.  

 

It is clear, then, from his consistent twofold demand of a correspondent object and of necessary correspondence with it, that Plato understood knowledge as correspondence. What now about Aristotle?

Aristotle’s views cannot be fully understood but by contrast to those of  Plato, for they resulted from of a constant, implicit dialogue with his master and it was by reference to those views that he progressively elaborated his own stances. As is often the case with disciples of great talent, Aristotle began his career by identifying himself with his master’s views and then he progressively came to differ from them in significant respects. This is so true in Aristotle’s case that the chronological order of his works is for the most part. established by detecting the progression of his differences with Plato. Concerning those differences, the specific point that interests me for the moment is whether, in his effort to improve on his master, Aristotle came to disagree with him concerning the notion of knowledge as visual correspondence.

Now, to begin with the case of intellectual knowledge, it is true that Aristotle gave up on the transcendent existence of the Platonic eternal Ideas. But, as is well known, he replaced that feature of the Platonic system with the intelligible universal essences of the  particular substances actually existing within our world. In their turn,  these immanent essences performed the same function in the Aristotelian system that the transcendent Ideas had in Plato’s. Indeed, their actual existence guaranteed the objectivity of our universal judgments, and their immutable or a-temporal nature insured the truth of those judgments by making  the correspondence to them necessary . This is the type knowledge that Aristotle eventually called “ episteme, ” characterized by its universality and necessity,. by contrast, as we shall see in some detail, to the non-scientific knowledge of the perceptual order and to the practical knowledge of the sphere of action. We may therefore conclude that, for all the avowedly significant ways in which Aristotle modified the Platonic doctrine of the Ideas, he nonetheless retained the notion of knowledge as correspondence, in the intellectual order.

As for the order of the sensible, in  which the objects perceived are particular rather than universal, Aristotle’s views are certainly more ambiguous than Plato’s. As we just saw,  precisely the absence of necessary correspondence with what is, in the case of sensible particulars, constrained Plato to reject the validity of perceptual knowledge. On this point, however, Aristotle eventually came to disagree with his master. Indeed, of the two requirements that Plato had deemed essential for knowledge to be genuine rather than merely apparent, Aristotle only kept the requirement of correspondence with what is, and dropped the requirement that the correspondence be necessary ^(F2). This resulted in the Aristotelian distinction, for which there was no room in Plato, between “scientific knowledge,” on the one hand, whose truth was necessary, and non-scientific knowledge, whose truth could not be guaranteed by strict demonstration, because it involved no intelligible necessity of correspondence,  but which was nonetheless factually valid because it actually corresponded to what is. In short, in the order of the sensible, there is a definite and important disagreement of Aristotle with his master. Indeed, Plato classified all perceptual knowledge as doxa, mere opinion or belief, whereas Aristotle maintained that the judgments that referred to the particular objects of perception could in fact be true, though never necessarily so.

As for the basis on which Aristotle felt authorized to discard the Platonic requirement of necessary correspondence with what is, the best one can say is that Aristotle was too pragmatic, or too commonsensical, not to rely on the common agreement of humankind and take perceptual evidence at its face-value within prudent limits. Unlike Plato, Aristotle was convinced that the certainty that was possible in different domains of knowledge depended on the nature of the objects found in the domain, and that to ask for more certainty than was possible within a given domain was irrational. ^(F3)

In conclusion, though Aristotle softened the Platonic requirement of necessary correspondence with what is, and in this regard he definitely differed from Plato, he nonetheless remained in full agreement with his master that correspondence with what is is the absolutely essential characteristic of knowledge. We might therefore say that for all their significant differences with Descartes and Descartes’ successors, both Plato and Aristotle can and should be considered as Descartes’ predecessors, insofar as the Cartesian mirroring of nature is just a variety of the correspondence with what is in which knowledge is supposed to consist. ^(F4) .

Theoretical and practical knowledge

It is to Aristotle that we owe the definite distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge, which is absent in Plato’s works. Not that Plato was uninterested in the practical aspect of knowledge. On the contrary, initiated into philosophy by Socrates, whose fundamental aim was attaining the knowledge that would allow us to live the way we are supposed to live as human beings, Plato himself pursued knowledge in the hope that it would lead him to the very practical result desired by Socrates. Yet, as we shall presently see, both Socrates and Plato conflated the two varieties of knowledge as if they were one and the same, and ultimately attributed to practical knowledge the characteristics that Aristotle will reserve for theoretical or speculative knowledge.  

In order to understand precisely the very significant differences between Plato and Aristotle in this area and, consequently, in their respective conceptions of philosophy, let us  begin with Socrates. The latter became the declared enemy of the sophists, whom he saw as subverters of the polis, because they educated the youth of Athens without themselves possessing genuine knowledge. Indeed, what they taught was rhetoric – the art of persuading people to do what the speaker wanted them to do, instead of teaching them to do what was right and good. The teaching of the sophists was of that kind either because they didn’t realize their own ignorance. or because they were convinced that what was right and good was simply a matter of subjective preferences. Thus they themselves did nothing wrong in training the youth to be successful by persuading others to act as they themselves wanted them to. Whether the rhetoricians did that maliciously or innocently, in either case, Socrates thought, they were doing great damage to the polis as educators. So he decided to devote his life to expose their ignorance and to look for the “ sophia ” or genuine science of the good and right that the sophists were incapable of teaching.

The Platonic dialogues are a dramatization of the way in which Socrates carried out the task he had set for himself. Their study supports abundantly the two points that are important for my present purposes. First, that the knowledge or “ sophia ” pursued by Socrates and then by Plato was practical rather than theoretical, in that it aimed at the result of living in the right way rather than at mere theoretical grasp of the truth. Second, that such practical knowledge had to be necessarily or demonstratively true, in order to impose itself to the sophists themselves and to their disciples as something more and better than what they  taught or were taught. Indeed, in the Platonic dialogues, we find Socrates repeatedly rejecting the validity of any so-called knowledge that lacked necessity. The Euthyphro, to which we will return in a moment, is a good instance of this, for there we see Socrates stubbornly rejecting Euthyphro’s claim that he knows what piety is. The reason for the rejection is that Euthyphro cannot furnish an essential rather than a merely pragmatic definition of piety, that is, a definition that renders the knowledge of what piety is necessarily true and therefore results infallibly in the right living in matters relating to the divine.

Such was, roughly, the status quaestionis  inherited by Aristotle from Plato as to the kind of knowledge that the lovers of wisdom were supposed to pursue, by contrast to the  rhetoric of the sophists. ^(F5) At any rate, such is the status quaestionis reflected in Aristotle’s early works, where there is as yet no distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge and where his own views are indistinguishable from Plato’s. ^(F6) What differences from this point of departure do    Aristotle’s late works reveal?

As remarked above,  Aristotle came to soften in a highly significant way the radical Platonic distinction between opinion and knowledge, without totally abolishing it. For Plato, opinion and genuine knowledge were mutually exclusive, since the former lacked necessary truth. But why did Plato clung so adamantly to this aspect of Socrates’ views? Because Socrates’s  entire  philosophical career had been predicated on the ignorance of the rhetoricians or sophists who pretended to know but didn’t, because their pretended knowledge was not necessarily true . For his part, Socrates knew that he didn’t know, since he realized the difference between opinion and knowledge. Thus, for Socrates to give up on necessarily true knowledge would have amounted to resigning himself to the ignorance of his sworn adversaries. In short, reaching necessarily true knowledge or “sophia ” was the only possible alternative to ignorance. Neither could Plato resign himself to possessing mere opinions without betraying his master. In fact, the doctrine of the Ideas was Plato’s contribution in order to render possible the realization  of the Socratic ideal.

But, then,  how could Aristotle soften the opposition between opinion and knowledge without giving up on the philosophical undertaking? Did he ultimately side with the sophists? We will be better placed to grasp Aristotle’s own answer to these questions if we first understand why he couldn’t preserve the distinction between opinion and knowledge in the radical Platonic-Socratic version.

From the objections raised by Aristotle against Plato’s theory of the Ideas in the Metaphysics we know that very early he found that theory unconvincing. But he found Plato’s views unsatisfactory not only on account of the outlandishness of the theory of the ideas, but even more so on account of the internal contradiction Aristotle detected in the Socratic-Platonic ideal of knowledge. Indeed, the sort of knowledge Socrates had missed in the sophists was practical knowledge, since it should result in living virtuously. On the other hand, as already explained, both Socrates and Plato thought that knowledge had to be necessarily true in order to be knowledge. Thus, the sophia they pursued in their philosophical quest was a knowledge that was at the same time both practical and necessarily true. Herein lay the internal contradiction Aristotle detected in the kind of knowledge they had proposed as the philosophical ideal. For, as  he came to realize,  practical knowledge couldn’t possibly be necessarily true, because it dealt with contingent particulars, not with immutable universals. In other words, the strictly scientific or necessarily true practical knowledge that Socrates had settled to achieve as the alternative to the pseudo-knowledge of the rhetoricians was an impossible dream.

Thus, if the name of “Sophia” were to be reserved for the kind of  knowledge whose search was inaugurated by Socrates and continued by Plato as “philosophers,”that is, as “lovers of   Sophia,” it may be said that Aristotle put an end to the philosophical tradition, which he saw as a no exit road. Insofar, however, as Aristotle sorted things out in a different way than Socrates and Plato, he may be seen as the thinker who, working on the shoulders of his two gigantic predecessors, opened a new future to the philosophical tradition. Indeed, after much groping, Aristotle came to a definite distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge. That distinction allowed him to determine that the necessarily true knowledge pursued by his predecessors was possible, but only in the theoretical domain,  not in the practical domain of human action where they had pursued it. Indeed, practical knowledge deals with contingent particulars, and in this domain there is no room for the necessary truths searched for by Socrates and Plato .

By contrast to Aristotle, then, Socrates and Plato had conflated the theoretical and the practical in one sophia that was necessarily true and at the same time practical . This was reflected in their view that the person who knows is ipso facto good. That is why, according to  Plato, the philosophers, the sophoi, those whose knowledge was necessarily true and practical,  were called to be kings and govern the polis. The Aristotelian sophia , by contrast --if this name can be used to refer to a vastly different conception for the ideal of knowledge-- ended up being twofold. It embraced the scientific or necessarily true knowledge possible in the theoretical domain, on the one hand, and, on the other hand, the contingently true knowledge that was the best possibility in  the domains of perception and of action.

On the connection between knowledge and action

There is, however, a further characteristic of the kind of knowledge Socrates and Plato pursued that we have not yet considered, concerning which it will be important to determine whether Aristotle followed his predecessors or departed from them. How did Socrates and Plato see the connection between knowledge and action, given that the knowledge they pursued was an impossible mixture of theoretical and practical? In other words, how could a knowledge that was theoretical, for it was necessarily true, result in action? There is no better place to grasp the problem herein involved than the Euthyphro .

Socrates meets the pious Euthyphro and tests him as to his knowledge on piety. What is piety?, asks Socrates. Doing what is pleasant to the gods, answers Euthyphro. Obviously, according to Euthyphro, knowing what is pleasant to the gods amounts to knowing what piety is. But that is not knowledge, according to Socrates, or at any rate not the kind of knowledge he is looking for. Yes, Socrates wants correct action, pious action. But the kind of pious action he wants requires as a previous condition a theoretical knowledge of what is pleasant to the gods. And the theoretical knowledge Socrates sees as a precondition for pious action has to be essential knowledge, that is, has to grasp the essence of piety, and thus be explanatory of the reason why certain actions are necessarily pleasant to the gods, so that in any and every case the actions guided by that knowledge be necessarily pious or pleasant to the gods . Thus ,  the kind of action Socrates wanted presupposed theoretical-essential knowledge as its necessary condition. In this sense, he subordinated practical knowledge to theoretical  knowledge, for he made correct action dependent on theoretical knowledge of what correct action is and why it is necessarily so. Concretely, then, the Socratic sophia (WHICH ACCORDING TO DEVEREUX HE CALLED PHRONESIS; CHECK WITH GAUTHIER) was really a speculative knowledge whose contemplation would automatically result in correct action. In this way, for Socrates, knowledge and virtue were identical. The theoretical knowledge of what is necessarily pleasant to the gods would be identical with doing what is pleasant to the gods. ^(F7)  By the same token, leading  people to acquire that sort of theoretical knowledge would make them automatically virtuous. Such was the teaching that the sophists were incapable of imparting.

I say for some unexplained reason, because, like Euthyphros, I do not see why one cannot be pious in the sense of acting piously without knowing theoretically what piety is, much less, without possessing the essential definition of piety. Yet, as shown in the Euthyphros , that undoubtedly is what Socrates and Plato thought. This is the characteristic of the Socratic-Platonic knowledge that I said we had not considered, to wit: that their knowledge, which Aristotle would identify as practical since it consists in virtuous action, presupposes as a necessary condition theoretical  knowledge –and even theoretical essential knowledge– of what virtue is. Which amounts to subordinating practical knowledge to theoretical knowledge by making theoretical knowledge a necessary (and apparently even sufficient) condition for right action.   

Now, by his definite distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge, Aristotle is supposed to have avoided the confusion betwen them of which Socrates and Plato were victims.

On the one hand, the knowledge they wanted was practical, since it was supposed to terminate in the knower’s virtuous action. On the other hand, as they confused theoretical and practical knowledge, they wanted a practical knowledge that was necessarily true, which is a property of theoretical knowledge. Thus,. in one way or another, they wanted a theoretical knowledge that resulted in action

Let us begin by summarizing what we have covered so far regarding the relationship of Aristotle’s epistemic views to those of Plato. For all the modifications that Plato’s views underwent in Aristotle’s hands, it seems fair to conclude that Aristotle remained close to Plato in certain important ways. He didn’t give up on the Platonic notion of  knowledge as correspondence, but dropped the Platonic requirement of necessary correspondence as a necessary condition. This resulted in the Aristotelian division of knowledge into necessarily true or scientific and contingently true. For his part,  Plato excluded contingent correspondence from the domain of knowledge and relegated it among the doxai. .Hence Aristotle included perception in the domain of knowledge, whereas Plato excluded it (2) He didn’t totally discard the possibility of necessary correspondence; he only excluded it from the practical domain of action. By the same token,  Aristotle excluded the possibility of scientific knowledge in the practical domain, whereas Plato, lacking a definite distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge,  had deemed necessary correspondence possible in the practical domain.

Only concerning perceptual knowledge is Aristotle in total disagreement with, for he dropsthere a radical disagreement  He didn’t give up on the possibility of knowledge as correspondence, though he eliminated necessary correspondence, in the theoretical domain of the sensible, whereas Plato had excluded thid domain from the field of knowledge.     

It is evident that Aristotle’s epistemic views took shape progressively, as he little by little modified Plato’s own, which he had initially embraced. This applies to the case of practical knowledge too. As Devereux puts it, speaking of Aristotle,

“... his break with Plato was a gradual process. The notion of practical knowledge did not spring up all at once, fully formed; there was a time when he thought practical knowledge was much like theoretical, the only important contrast being the different aims of each. ^(F8)”   

In other words, there was a stage in Aristotle’s evolution in which he was as unclear as his predecessors as to the nature of practical knowledge and thus identified it with  theoretical

knowledge. This is the period to which Devereux alludes, in which the only difference between theoretical and practical knowledge was a difference in aims. In the case of theoretical knowledge, knowledge is pursued for its own sake, whereas in the case of practical knowledge it is pursued for its utility. This is illustrated by the example of two people who study the medical science. One of them studies for the sake of knowing, whereas the other studies with the end of curing people.

According to this conception, then, theoretical and practical knowledge differ only by the aim in view of which the knowledge is acquired. But this amount to saying that the theopretical and practical knowledge differ only extrinsically. Indeed, the knowledge of medical science acquired by the two people of Aristotle’s example is exactly the same ; the only difference is that one of the two people will put the knowledge to practical use, whereas the other is content with enjoying the knowledge for its own sake. Thus, there was a period in Aristotle’s evolution in which he saw practical knowledge identical to theoretical knowledge, that is, as merely corresponding to what is, and only extrinsically connected with action. Now, it does not take much analysis to realize that this view is just a variation of the kind of knowledge dreamed of by Socrates and Plato, who pursued theoretical knowledge as the necessary condition for correct action. We may therefore conclude that, at least at this stage of his evolution, Aristotle had not yet achieved his independence from Plato for, like him and Socrates, confused practical knowledge with theoretical knowledge. In Devereux’s words, “there was a time when he thought practical knowledge was much like theoretical.”

These words suggest, fo course, that there was a later stage in Aristotle’s evolution in which he thought practical knowledge as different from theoretical. How, precisely, did Aristotle conceive practical knowledge in this final stage?

Now, Aristotle also speaks of practical knowledge, and he understands knowledge as correspondence. The question arises, though, as to the possibility of correspondence in practical knowledge which is knowledge not of an object but of what is to be done and which doesn’t terminate in contemplation but in action, through deliberation and decision. Deliberation is about what is to be done and it leads to decision. But deciding how to act is not an image of what is to be done (indeed, how can there be a correspondence with a future that doesn’t exist yet) but a practical disposition to act in a certain way under the appropriate circumstances, and such a disposition that it will result in action when the appropriate circumstances obtain. In other words, the metaphor of contemplation, and consequently of visual image that corresponds to the original, breaks down in the area of practical knowledge. Actually, were it not for the fact that we are, like Aristotle, sold to the idea of knowledge as correspondence, we would have no reason to look for any correspondence in the practical field, for there is no possible correspondence here. And where there is no correspondence there is no knowledge, for the essential characteristic of knowledge is correspondence. In other words, in the order of agency there is no room for the notions of knowledge and of truth. This we may be able to realize if, as Aristotle did in De motu animalium, we forget the Platonic notion of knowledge as correspondence in particular its application to the practical field of agency, and begin with the analysis of action, as Aristotle did in De Motu Animalium.

Aristotle claims that animals know in the realm of the sensible, though they do not understand. But is he thinking in terms of correspondence when he claims that animals know? Does Aristotle mean that animals have images that correspond to what is the case, as supposedly we humans do? In the case of people it is easy to speak of knowledge as correspondence, for they verbalize what they know or claim t o know, as well as their conviction that what they claim to know corresponds ‘is true,’ that is,  to what is. But animals do not verbalize their experience. On what basis, then, does Aristotle contend that animals know, and what exactly does he mean by that?

 First of all, the only knowledge Aristotle attributes to animals is practical, not only in that animals cannot pursue knowledge for its own sake but also in that it is knowledge that actually terminates in action. ^(F9) In fact, Aristotle knows that animals know only on the basis of the way they act. Now, Aristotle doesn’t affirm that all animals know. He is certain only in the case of those animals who are capable of locomotion. Why? Because the latter obviously act not merely repetitively but adaptively. That is, they do not act purely mechanically, which would only allow for repetition of inherited automatic action; they discover new conducts adapted to their goals. Desire guides them in this discovery .

The laboratory experiments with rats in a maze or with rats that learn to push a lever to get food is a good example. On the basis of past successful experience they learn the adapted conduct and go directly to the place where there is food or push the appropriate lever unhesitatingly. Mammals superior to rats, of which apes are the best example, even adapt the memorized conduct to new situations. Because of these types of action, Aristotle would say that these animals know. But what does he mean? That they possess something that corresponds to what is the case?

Needless to say, in this view the concrete circumstances in which action takes place, the “particulars,” are systematically ignored as irrelevant.

No wonder that Aristotle, whose starting point was the just characterized Socratic view (completed by the Platonic addition of the doctrine of the eternal Ideas, had a difficult time extricating himself from this confusion and determining in his own way the kind of sophia that the lovers of wisdom actually could and should pursue. In his first stage, reviewed above, he concluded that practical knowledge couldn’t be scientific, as Plato had dreamed. However, still moving along Plato’s lines, he thought that theoretical, though not scientific, knowledge was a requirement for acting correctly . His last stage, though –the one I just began to outline– starts with the case of animals, not of people, where Aristotle finds  no room for theoretical knowledge. Moreover, since action is the basis on which it can be determined whether or not animals know, in this case the focus is on action rather than on any kind of mental or linguistic activity. Two important observations in this connection. First, tough successful action precedes and generates knowledge, once knowledge is acquired it generates successful action in the future. Hence the illusion that knowledge is a condition for correct or successful action and that in order to act successfully one should first possess knowledge. Second, let us call “knowledge” whatever is acquired by the animal as a result of successful action, and which leads that animal to successful action in the future. But what, precisely, is the character of this knowledge? Is it anything resemblant to what is the case, so that we can speak of knowledge as correspondence to what is?  As Aristotle suggests, there is a sort of generalization on the animal’s part. Something like “Under these circumstances, that is what is to be done to achieve satisfaction,” which seems to be a true statement that corresponds to what is the case. However, as animals are non-linguistic, it is highly improbable that the knowledge we are talking about be of this kind, for they don’t make statements. Moreover, knowledge of this kind wouldn’t result in action. Indeed, I can possess the knowledge expressed in the statement, and yet it won’t result in action on my part even if the circumstances referred to obtain. Because I am not the rat that desires satisfaction. So, if there were a generalizing statement made by the rat, it would be a personal statement by the rat and self-referential to the rat , which would go something like this: “Under these circumstances, that is, if I find myself in this maze ( which I will remember and recognize because of my practical –that is, unformulated or ‘tacit ’-- experience of it , which is the practical or tacit memory to which Aristotle refers in the context of the knowledge animals possess),. this is what is to be done by me to achieve satisfaction.” Even then, however, this knowledge would be purely theoretical, for it wouldn’t result in action by the rat. For action by the rat to obtain, the rat has to be actually desirous of food . And desire, be it desire of food or of anything else, can be referred to by a statement, but is not itself an object of knowledge for the agent; it is a mode of being of the practical knower, that is, of the agent, without which the knower’s knowledge cannot be practical for it cannot result in action . So practical knowledge results in action because it is structured toward a telos by actual desire or aversion on the knower’s part. This is what Martha Nussbaum, in her analysis of practical knowledge in Aristotle, calls “desirous knowledge” (by implicit contrast to the traditional conception of knowledge as objective, as cold or neutral representation of what is , to which what is corresponds). And what is new in this Aristotelian conception of practical knowledge by contrast to his Platonic conception of knowledge as mere correspondence is precisely this element of desire, of hexis or movement toward . ^(F10)

In her study of the practical syllogism in Aristotle, Nussbaum has some trouble explaining its peculiar logic. (See my \wp\97\97-oct2, or Monas\Bible-5, p. 13). ^(F11) I think that the reason is that it is not a matter of logic, that is, of a combination of things objectively known. Logic is a matter of objective judgments-statements that aim at eliciting assent. To speak of a logic that involves desire and of a syllogism that terminates in, or aims at, action is an absurdity, for the simple reason that the agent’s statements (insofar as they are formulated) are not objective, predicative statements of what is the case, but personal, self-referential of what the desirous agent encounters. Therefore the ‘knowledge’ is ‘tacit” and ‘applicative.”  

I think that Polanyi’s “tacit knowledge” is more promising in connection with what Aristotle calls practical knowledge . Although Polanyi, like Aristotle, still speaks of knowledge, which suggests correspondence and objectivity, and tends to vitiate the import of the analysis, the fact that it is ‘tacit’ implies that it is not verbalized in judgements-statements and not reducible to them, for what the subject ‘knows’ is not known by the subject, for it is not stated to himself or herself by the subject. In this sense, the subject doesn’t know, for the subject doesn’t know objectively. The ‘knowledge’ is internalized or appropriated by the subject and takes the form of an ability or disposition or inclination to act in a certain unformulated way , not the form of a specified rule, under certain unspecified or unformulated  circumstances, in response to or in relation to certain unspecified objects in order to achieve certain unspecified results, much as is the case with the skills of playing the piano or driving a car or making bread or guiding oneself by using a map. If one wants formulations, which are useful for teaching, the formulations are mere rules of thumb , but the agent has to know when and how to apply them, how concretely to guide his action by them. And this cannot be taught, for it cannot be specified or formulated. Only the agent, in the concrete circumstances, can decide how to act appropriately. It is a matter of deliberation and action, when there is room and need for deliberation, or of acquired habit.  I think that this is what Aristotle sensed obscurely when, in the NE, he spoke of the imprecision of practical knowledge by contrast to the precision of scientific theoretical knowledge, and when he declared that the standard of prudent action couldn’t be otherwise specified than by saying that the standard it is the prudent action of the phronimos , of the practically wise, of the one who possesses practical wisdom.

Not without reason did Aristotle distinguish the practical knowledge involved in human action from techne , the knowledge involved in making an object or achieving a concrete result in the environment when this can be reduced to the mechanical application of rules. But perhaps he would have gained if he had underscored the kinship of the knowledge involved in human action with the knowledge involved in making or achieving an external result (even cooking a dish or making bread), when the maker has to discover how and when to apply.

My first comment concerns the Platonic conception of knowledge as correspondence with what is. As explained above, Aristotle relaxed the requirement of necessary correspondence with what is in order to be able to include in the field of knowledge the sensible particulars of perception. However –and this is the important point– he didn’t modify the requirement of correspondence with what is. In other words, he continued to believe, as Plato had, that knowledge amounted to correspondence with what is.

Now, Plato had been able to save the notion of knowledge as correspondence only because the eternity of the Ideas insured that the correspondence of universal concepts with their objects was necessary. And precisely because the correspondence couldn’t be necessary in the case of the sensible and particular objects of perception did Plato exclude such objects from the field of knowledge and relegated them to the sphere of opinion. For his part, Aristotle maintained the Platonic requirement of correspondence, but dropped the Platonic requirement of necessary correspondence. By the same token, however, he opened the door to skepticism concerning perceptual knowledge, for he couldn’t explain when the correspondence with what is obtained and when it did not, given that the correspondence itself was merely contingent and, therefore, may or may not obtain in any particular case. In short, Aristotle had two options: either abandon completely Plato’s epistemic views  by dropping the requirement of correspondence with what is, or keep the requirement of necessary correspondence as Plato had done, and then follow the Platonic logic and  renounce the possibility of genuine perceptual knowledge. Unfortunately, however, Aristotle wanted to eat his cake and have it too, thereby leaving as his epistemic legacy the impossible problem of combining two incompatible features: correspondence with what is, and contingent correspondence. The epistemic legacy of correspondence, as we shall see, would reach its critical moment with Kant.  

There is, however, another thorny feature to Aristotle’s epistemic views. Not only did Aristotle continue the Platonic tradition of conceiving knowledge as correspondence, but he didn’t give up entirely on the Socratic-Platonic ideal of knowledge as necessarily true. By bringing the Platonic Ideas from their transcendent heaven down to our world, Aristotle preserved an area within which the Platonic ideal of necessary correspondence remained, for the essences of actually existing particulars were intelligible and necessary like the Platonic Ideas. The knowledge corresponding to that area, that is, necessarily true knowledge, Aristotle called scientific knowledge or episteme . However, when in the Nicomachean Ethics Aristotle explored the field of action, that is, of practical knowledge, he realized that within it there was no possibility of scientific knowledge because, like perceptual knowledge, practical knowledge dealt with the particular and contingent. In other words, in Aristotle’s view, Socrates and Plato hadn’t been entirely wrong in conceiving the ideal of necessary correspondence. Their mistake, for lack of distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge, was to believe that practical knowledge could be necessarily true, when in fact such attribute belonged to speculative knowledge exclusively.

As already explained, the disktinction between theoretical and practical knowledge was another modification of the Platonic epistemology introduced by Aristotle. Why hadn’t Socrates and Plato felt the need for that distinction? Because in their eagerness to distance themselves from the rhetoricians, they had accused them of ignorance for failing to teach what was necessarily true in matters of conduct and action that affected the polis. Now, Aristotle came progressively to the realization that the necessarily true knowledge pursued by Plato and Aristotle was only possible in the theoretical field of the essences of material substances, for such essences were intelligible an necessary, as Plato had imagined his Ideas to be. In the practical field, however, that is, in the field of conduct and action, Aristotle found necessarily true or scientific knowledge to be impossible, for practical knowledge deals with the particular and contingent. Thus, Aristotle concluded in the Nicomachean Ethics , practical knowledge (like perceptual knowledge, for not entirely dissimilar reasons) could at best be contingently true.

The Aristotelian distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge as well as his conviction that the possibility of reaching necessarily true or scientific knowledge was not the same in the theoretical and in the practical spheres would be part of the Aristotelian intellectual legacy. Though not everybody, of course, would agree with him  

The important point, for my present purposes, is not so much that Aristotle modified radically the Socratic-Platonic legacy by discarding the possibility of scientific knowledge in matters of conduct and action, but the exact opposite: that he nonetheless continued the Socratic-Platonic line of thought by admitting that in matters of conduct and action  knowledge –however contingent and, therefore, theoretically uncertain-- was possible.

To summarize now what regards the relationship between Aristotle to Plato in their respective epistemic convictions, there is no doubt that Aristotle’s views ended up being significantly different from Plato’s. Actually, I have been pointing out some of the differences between them, the importance of which cannot be minimized. Having granted that, however, I want to conclude this first portion of my exploration by underscoring that the Aristotelian epistemic legacy is essentially Platonic, both in that it left unmodified and reinforced the Platonic conception of knowledge as correspondence, and in that it left unmodified and reinforced the Platonic conviction that in matters of conduct and action knowledge –though not necessarily true-- was still possible. Because in this two very important regards Aristotle’s epistemic views remained Platonic, I think we are authorized to see Aristotle’s works as the first in the long series of footnotes to Plato in which the Western philosophical tradition has rightly been said to consist.

Now, this idea may be so commonsensical as to appear indisputable, and this is why it has survived till our day in spite of  the numerous attacks of which it has been the object across the centuries. But the very fact that it has been so constantly the object of attacks may be significant. Why indeed would it have been so constantly attacked if it had proved to be satisfactory?

We cannot fully understand the problems involved in the notion of knowledge as correspondence without relating it to difficulties involved in the areas of application of the three  distinctions by means of which Aristotle modified Plato’s views, to wit: the distinctions between scientific and non-scientific knowledge, between necessarily true and contingently true knowledge, and between theoretical and practical knowledge.

Why, in the first place, did Aristotle distinguish between scientific and non-scientific knowledge? Because Plato had assimilated the latter to opinion or belief, which amounted to excluding the entire domain of particular objects from the sphere of knowledge. Now, concretely, this amounted to excluding from the sphere of knowledge both perceptual knowledge and practical knowledge, for both had to do with particulars. However, by dropping the requirement of necessary truth Aristotle may, on the one hand, have rescued the areas of perception and ethics or ethical action as fields of knowledge, but by the same token, on the other hand, he was opening the entire field of knowledge to skepticism. Why, indeed, had Plato so adamantly stuck by his distinction between knowledge and opinion? Not because he didn’t appreciate the difference between purely arbitrary or totally irrational belief and probable though not absolutely certain rational opinion,  but because, at least in his early stages, Plato had entertained the Socratic hope of absolutely certain or necessarily true knowledge in the practical domain of human action.   .

   

The notion of knowledge as correspondence reached its critical point –perhaps we should rather say, its breaking point-- with Kant. Such notion, Kant argued, inevitably led to skepticism, for it is absolutely impossible to know whether or not the supposed correspondence with what is, with “the thing in itself,” obtains. As a solution to this aporia, Kant gave up on the intractable

disastrous implications the requirement of necessary truth which had led Plato to postulate the existence of the eternal Ideas had on the validity of perceptual knowledge. Indeed, in Plato’s eyes, the merely contingent existence of the particular objects of perception andle; therefore, their truth is, at best, contingent rather than necessary. From this it follows that perceptual knowledge, for all its sensible evidence, is not genuine knowledge but mere opinion. Now, even without considering the offensiveness of this view to common sense, it practically leads to absolute skepticism, since all knowledge, even the necessarily true knowledge of universal natures, has its origin in perceptual knowledge. Indeed, the intelligible natures of whose necessarily true knowledge Aristotle defends are the natures of the particular, sensible objects in which they subsist. Thus, if the sensible objects themselves cannot be genuinely known, neither can their intelligible natures.

Strictly speaking, other than his own commonsensical pragmatism, Aristotle has no argument to justify his abrogation of the requirement of necessary correspondence with what is in order to accept the validity of perceptual knowledge. Yet accept it he did. By the same token, though, he softened the Platonic distinction between opinion and knowledge, since in the case of perception, which Plato had placed in the region of opinion, Aristotle made an exception and deemed it genuine knowledge while agreeing with Plato that perception could yield no more than contingent truth. This led Aristotle to distinguish two types of knowledge: the necessarily true knowledge of intelligible universal natures, which he called “episteme” or “scientific knowledge” (the only type that Socrates and Plato could conceivably have deemed genuine knowledge rather than opinion), on the one hand, and, on the other hand, the not necessarily true, but nonetheless factually or contingently true, knowledge of the sensible, contingent  particulars accessed through perception.. To the latter, non-scientific knowledge, Aristotle came to give the name of (“ phronesis”) “sophia” by contrast to “episteme.”
