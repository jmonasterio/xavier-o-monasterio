- INTERESTING (?) FACTS

- On p. 39 it is mentioned that the University of California at Berkeley and Yale University have sizable collections of Mormon history. (But the author’s own bibliography doesn’t refer to any book or document of those collections!)

All the following facts are from chapter 3:

- p. 53: Morris had his firsts revelation in 1857. In 1860 he began to have a good number of followers in the area of Ogden, some 40 miles North of Salt Lake City. The main reasons for

Morris’s opposition to the Mormons were their practice of polygamy and, even more important, that their leader had no personal revelations whereas he (Morris) had a lot of them.

- p. 56: 1861: Excommunication of the Morrisites, among them Bishop Cook. On April 6 of the same year, foundation of the new church, one of whose doctrines was the imminent arrival of Christ. The Morrisites moved to the mouth of Weber Canyon to wait for Christ. They were so sure that Christ’s coming was imminent that they didn’t plant crops that spring.

- p. 57: Between April 16, 1861 and June 15, 1862, when Morris was killed, he had 250 revelations. - The failure of Christ to arrive at the predicted date or dates provoked disillusionment and discontent among the Morrisites.

- pp. 57-58: William Jones was among the discontents. He left in 1862, but was caught and made prisoner with two others. His friends appealed to the civil authorities, but Morris refused to honor the habeas corpus.

- p. 59: The attempt to serve a second habeas corpus precipitated the war. Burton arrived with a posse of about 250 men to arrest Morris and liberate the prisoners in Kington Fort.

- p. 60: As Morris was announcing that Christ was going to protect the Morrisites, a canon ball destroyed the roof and killed two women.

- p. 61: On June 15, surrender. Burton shot and killed Morris–how exactly is not clear.

- p. 62: At least 6 Morrisites and 2 members of the posse died in the three-days skirmishes.

- pp. 63-64: Governor Stephen Harding pardoned the 140 Morisites Burton had taken prisoners. The Morrisites emigrated and created communities at Carson Valley (Nevada), Soda Springs (Idaho), and Deer Lodge Valley (Montana). In time, the new church of the Morrisites disappeared.
