Van Steenberghen, The Origins of Latin Aristotelianism

- "We have spoken earlier of the way in which THE FIRST TWELVE CENTURIES OF THE XTIAN ERA WERE DOMINATED BY THE XTIAN VISION OF THE UNIVERSE, and how this state of affairs retarded the progress of profane science. In the thirtheenth century, Aris came on the scene and completely altered the situation; for the first time, a compact system of scientific and philosophic disciplines made its way into the xtian world; theology suddenly found itself faced with Aristotelianism, the master-piece of Greek thought, enriched with elements of Arab, Jewish and Greek neo-Platonism; xtian wisdom was suddenly challenged by pagan wisdom--profane wisdom, no longer represented by the modest and inoffensive cortege of the liberal arts, but by the powerful scientific synthesis of peripatetic philosophy.

"CONFLICT BETWEEN ARISTOTELIANISM AND XTIAN THEOLOGY WAS INEVITABLE, SINCE THE TWO VIEWS OF THE UNIVERSE DIFFERED PROFOUNDLY ON SEVERAL POINTS. At Paris, two rival university groups were to come to blows over it. We have already witnessed the first skirmish, at the beginning of the thirteenth century. Theology came off the best that time, and its victory momentarily slowed down the advance of Aristotelianism; but the latter continued to gain ground in spite of all prohibitions, and March 19th, 1255, saw the final victory gained. The theologians themselves could not escape its influence, which went on growing from William of Auxerre to St. Bonaventure. And it was just at this point that Albert  came onto the scene.

" Albert the Great 's principal merit does not lie in the fact that he commented on Aristotle; that had been done long before him, at Paris, Oxford, and elsewhere; St Thomas did it after him, and his literal commentaries are far superior to his master's paraphrases. Nor is his principal merit in the fact that he used Aris's philosophy in theological speculation; William of Auvergne and Alexander of Hales had done that quite as much before him. His chief merit rests on this fact: for the first time since the origin of Xtianity, Albert the G definitely established and clearly defined the rightful position of learning in Xtianity. In doing so, he laid down the principles which could lead to the solution of the formidable problem raised by the introduction of Aristotelianism into the Xtian world, and, further, made a decisive contribution to the full development of xtian thought.

"... Faced with the new situation created by the full-scale invasion of Greek and Arab learning, Albert realized that the hour had come for Xtianity to complete its intellectual emancipation and to take an active part in the scientific movement. Aris must be welcome and his teaching assimilated according to the special genius of Latin and Xtian thought; the errors, deviations and lacunae of pagan learning would be more effectively overcome by a constructive effort of reflection and criticism than by prohibition or mutilation of the texts. Following out his fundamental intuition, Albert conceived the idea of 'remaking [in his "Physics"?] Aristotle for the use of the Latin world"; and for this purpose he undertook the composition of an encyvlopedic work destined to enrich Xtian learning with all the scientific discoveries which the Greek and Arabs had accumulated throughout their history...

...Albert's whole manner of action proclaimed that the autonomous development of profane learning was necessary and beneficial, and that theology itself would benefit from it. To this tacit avowal, he adds the most explicit statements concerning the specific nature and methods of theology, philosophy and the particular sciences, based on the data of experience and observation. Albert, then, firmly accepts the idea of a type of research which in its principles, methods and internal structure was exclusively rational; he recognizes the lawfulness of a natural knowledge distinct from supernatural wisdom...

"Albert's attitude to the assimilation of Aris is a golden mean between the conservative theologians who are soon to declare open war on Aris, and the members of the Arts Faculty who follow him blindly even in his most serious errors. With the former, he affirms that faith is superior to reason, that theology is superior to philosophy, that Aris and the pagan philosophers were sometimes sadly at fault [NOTE 1 ad p. 179: 'Dicet autem fortasse aliquis (concerning the eternity of the world) nos Aristotelem non intellexisse et ideo non consentire verbis eius... Et ad illum dicimus quod, qui credit Aristotelem fuisse deum, ille debet credere quod nunquam erravit. Si autem credit ipsum esse hominem, tunc procul dubio errare potuit sicut et nos.' (Phys., l. VIII, tr. I, c. 14). In his Summa Theologica Albert expressly condemns Aris's errors; cf. M Grabmann, Der Hl. Albert der Grosse, p. 13}, that the authority of St. Augusine must be respected in theology [NOTE 2 AD P. 179: 'Unde sciendum quod Augustino in his quae sunt de fide et moribus plus quam philosophis credendum est, si dissentiunt' (III Sent., dist. 13, C, a. 2). But with the latter he recognizes tha Aris is the most noble personification of philosophical knowledge, that philosophy must be worked out according to its own methods, and that it must be given the benefit of scientific autonomy in the correct sense.

" We said... that Albert the Great laid down the principles which could lead to the solution of the problem raised by the entry of Aris. As a matter of fact, HIS WORK CAME TEN YEARS TOO LATE TO PREVENT THE DEVELOPMENT OF ARISTOTELIANISM IN PARIS AND TO STOP RATIONALIST ARISTOTELIANISM FROM COMING TO BIRTH. In fact, his influence contributed to the development of this movement. But the abuse of his ideas obviously does not justify their condemnation; correctly understood, they would have provided a solution to minds which were laid astray by too great an attraction for philosophy, enabling them to keep their balance...

"... Albert's philosophical works were probably accepted in Paris from about 1255; and... they certainly had a profound influence on the Arts Faculty during the period immediately before the appearance of Siger of Brabant" (Fernand Van Steenberghen, Aristotle in the West: The origins of Latin Aristotelianism , tr. by Leonard Johnston, E. Nauwelaerts, Publisher, Louvain 1955, pp.176-181). COPY WHAT FOLLOWS ON PAGES 181-197 ON "THOMAS AQUINAS IN PARIS (1252-1259)".

- "He ( Aquinas ) FIRST MADE CONTACT WITH ARIS WHEN HE WAS A STUDENT OF LIBERAL ARTS IN NAPLES, from 1239 to 1244. He was a youngster,  barely feefteen years of age when he received lessons from master Martin (of Dacia?), profesor of grammar and logic, and then from master Peter of Ireland, professor in the natural sciences. Later, from 1245 to 1252, he studied for seven years under the direction of Albert the Great and this gave him the chance of broadening and deepening the philosophical knowledge he had acquired in Naples. At the same time, he began his studies in the various branches oftheology, using Holy Scripture, the works of the Fathers, the Sentences of Peter Lombard, and no doubt other more recent theological works. During this comparatively long period of training, Th. Aquinas made many reflections, meditations and comparisons, and WHEN WE COME TO READ HIS FIRST WRITINGS, WE FEEL WE ARE IN THE PRESENCE OF A MAN WHO HAS ALREADY CHOSEN THE PATH HE IS TO THREAD--A MAN WHO HAS ALREADY MADE THE FUNDAMENTAL DECISIONS.

"Th. Aquinas made his master's ideas on the autonomy and hierarchy of the sciences his own. IN PHILOSOPHY, HE WAS THE FIRST AMONG THE THIRTEENTH CENTURY THINKERS TO BREAK WITH THE HESITANT AND WAVERING ATTITUDE OF HIS CONTEMPORARIES, THE FIRST, THAT IS, WHO FIRMLY ADOPTED THE ESSENTIAL INTUITIONS OF ARISTOTELIANISM, AFTER PURGING THEM OF LATER CORRUPTIONS. Among these fundamental intuitions must be counted ESPECIALLY THE THEORY OF KNOWLEDGE, THE DOCTRINE OF ACT AND POTENCY, AND HYLOMORPHISM. In the field of knowledge, St Thomas definitely opted for Aris's intellectualistic empiricism, rejecting any special illumination at the level of natural consciousness and any direct intuition of spiritual realities. His theory of knowledge took as its basis the doctrine of abstraction, to which was added Aristotelian logic. In metaphysics, he adopted the important doctrine of act and potency with Aris had made the key-stone o his moderate dynamism, but, as we shall see, St Thomas brought out the profound metaphysical signification of this doctrine. In natural philo, he re-established the hylomorphic theory in its authentic form, eliminating all stoic and neo-Platonic excrescences which were threatening to stiffle it; such were, for exampe, rationes seminales, spiritual matter, and the plurality of substantial forms.

"We should, however, be flying in the face of historical truth if we were to present St. Thomas's philosophy as Aristotelianism pure and simple, or even if we were to explain it as a preference for Aris over Plato. A quick glance over the Thomistic system is sufficient for us to see that St Thomas, following in this the example of his Arabs and Latin predecessors, borrowed neo-Platonic elements to widen Aristotelianism; his system, like theirs, is a "neo-Platonizing" Aristotelianism. But his originality and superiority lay in the rigor of his criticism and in the perfect coherence and depth of his philosophical synthesis, in which Platonic, Aristotelian, neo-Platonic and Christian elements were all given a single, solid foundation. This new philosophy, therefore--THE FIRST REALLY ORIGINAL PHILOSOPHY PRODUCED BY THE THIRTHEENTH CENTURY AND EVEN BY CHRISTIAN CIVILIZATION IN GENERAL--cannot be given any satisfactory label based on its historical sources. Only one term is apt enough to express the profoundly personal philosophical attitude of St Thomas--HE CREATED THOMISM" (Ibid., pp. 183-4).

But, in my opinion, they are wrong in identifying this "Franciscan spirituality" or this "Franciscan ideal" with the so-called "Franciscan philosophy" of St. Bonaventure. THEY ARE TAKING THE TERM "philosophy" IN MUCH TOO WIDE A SENSE, AS A SYNONIM FOR 'WELTANSCHAUNG' OR 'OUTLOOK ON LIFE'. THAT IS THE AUGUSTINIAN SENSE, but it does not correspond to the use of the word by St. Bonaventure himself, nor to the usage which is commonly accepted today. In the strict sense of the word, we can speak of two antagonistic philosophies only after 1270; it was only then that the conservative theologians, among whom were St. Bonaventure's disciples, set themselves to forginG a philosophical system of Augustinian inspiration, in order to keep Thomism in check. "THIS DARING ATTITUDE, HOWEVER, FAR FROM APPEARING TO THE EYE OF THE HISTORIAN AS AN UNEXPECTED BREAKAWAY FROM A WELL-ESTABLSIHED TRADITION, IS LOOKED ON RATHER AS THE LOGICAL AND WELL-NIGH INEVITABLE TERM OR A LONG EVOLUTION OF TENDENCIES THAT HAD BEGUN TO MANIFEST THEMSELVES IN THE FACULTY OF ARTS FROM THE BEGINNING OF THE CENRURY. The arrival of the pagan philosophical literature led to the discovery of philosophy by the Xtian thinkers, and specially by the the [masters?] of the Faculty of Arts. THE POSSIBILITY OF A PURELY RATIONAL RESEARCH AND OF AN AUTONOMOUS PHILOSOPHICAL LEARNING GAVE THE CAREER OF THESE MASTERS AN ENTIRELY NEW SIGNIFICANCE AND TEN-FOLD INCREASED INTEREST. On the other hand, the exercise of philosophical research based on the tudy of pagan philosophers could not but throw into relief the many differences of spirit and doctrine that placed the Xtian outlook on things in opposition the the NATURALISM AND RATIONALISM OF THE PAGANS.

- "FROM 1267, THE THEOLOGIANS TOOK UP ARMS AGAINST SIGER'S PARTY.     Henri Bouillard, Logique de la foi

- "PEUT ON PROUVER LE FAIT DE LA REVELATION PAR DES ARGUMENTS HISTORIQUES? S'il est vrai que Dieu se revele a travers certaines figures appartenant a l'histoire, il est incontestable que la connaissance historique de ces figures joue un role dans la certitude de notre foi. Mais il est non moins clair qu'elle ne suffit pas a la fonder. Ce qui est ici en cause, ce n'est pas la question de savoir si une demonstration historique procure l'evidence, ou simplement la probabilite, ou plutot une conviction prudente. Ce qu'il faut comprendre, c'est que la realite divine comme telle ne releve pas du simple jugement de l'historien. - Dieu est Dieu. Il est le Tout-Autre. Il habite une lumiere inaccessible.
