TILLEY AND LINDBECK ON RELIGIOUS TRUTH

     (References to last chapter of Tilley;s Inventing Catholic Tradition )

Tilley continues to speak of “realism” and “anti-realism” and “ontological truth” and “truth is one (p. 148),” without realizing that there is room for such expressions only within the context of a specular, correspondence conception of knowledge. However,  his own notion of tradition as a practice, like Aristotle’s idea of practical knowledge, refers to the agent, not to a knower who is a pure contemplator. In this regard, Lindbeck, who rejects the correspondence theory of knowledge and, consequently, the possibility of religious propositional truth, is more consistent and accurate than Tilley.

Tilley finds no Christian theologian firmly committed to an anti-realist position and considers this “very significant”(p.159, note 12). What he fails to realize is that the people he characterizes as anti-realists are rather anti-correspondence epistemologists, unlike the  “Philosophers from Plato to the present [who] have construed a foundationalist-realist theory of truth” (p. 158), a type of theory Tilley himself rightly rejects in the same page. Someone who accepts the correspondence theory of truth is either a realist or an anti-realist. Actually, however,, Tilley himself and the thinkers he characterizes as anti-realists can be neither a realists nor an anti-realists, for their epistemology constitutes a context within which knowledge as correspondence, falsity as lack of correspondence, and truth as known correspondence have no conceivable place.

To explain this point, we need to refer ourselves to Tilley’s idea of truth as assessment, for assessment is incompatible with the kind of mirroring correspondence presupposed by propositional truth. Let us follow Tilley’s reasoning..

“Following the lead of American philosopher Wilfrid Sellars,“ Tilley’s words, “I argue that one can (and should) separate constructivism from anti-realism” (p.160). According to Sellars, “constructivists had not considered the possibility that the key realist concept of accuracy of reference , the ‘MIRRORING’ RELATIONSHIP OF REALISTIC PHILOSOPHY THAT RORTY REJECTED, might not be a criterion for judging the truth of a claim but an effect of our getting our claims right. Accuracy of reference may indeed be what makes the content of our claims true, but that fact (if it is a fact) is not something that we can use to judge whether claims are true or not. This form of realism is not defeated by constructivist arguments, for it is not a form of foundationalistst realism; it can be called a consequential realism” (p. 160).

Tilley explains his position through an example. “I may be able unerringly to pick out sugar maples from other trees in a grove. What makes my claim, ‘This is a sugar maple,’ RELIABLE IS THE SKILL I HAVE DEVELOPED AT IDENTIFYING SUGAR MAPLES. It is a skill in practical forestry that I have learned and apply in the practice of sugaring... A practical account of HOW I acquire this ability to USE A CONCEPT [namely sugar maple] makes sense of this ability I have developed. A constructivist account of the concept will make sense of the concept” (pp. 160-161).

What Tilley says here amounts to a concrete example of what Aristotle has in mind when he speaks of practical  knowledge. Notice, first of all, that it is a matter of a skill acquired through practice, not through contemplative thinking. Yet, because like the medical skill of which Aris speaks, it is a skill that consists in the ability to identify, which is a cognitive operation, Aristotle’s saw practical knowledge as a sort of knowledge , However, this knowledge, if knowledge it can be called, doesn’t consist in a concept that mirrors what is the case –in other words, no mirroring correspondence between what is in the mind and what is out there– but in a sort of practical rather than visual map that empowers the agent who knows how to use the map to obtain the desired goal of, say, to tap all the sugar maples in a certain grove. - How does one recognize a face? The knowledge, as Polanyi rightly says, is tacit. Once one has acquired this skill, one may to an extent b e able to specify certain distinctive traits that might help others to identify that face, as one is able to draw a map to a place once one knows how to reach that place without using a map.. But it is the person who already has the skill of recognizing that face without knowing its specific traits who can point out distinctive traits. The drawn map and the list of specific traits are comparable to the religious “doctrines” of which Lindbeck speaks, which he calls “second order” and to which he refuses referential force, by contrast to the “first order”
