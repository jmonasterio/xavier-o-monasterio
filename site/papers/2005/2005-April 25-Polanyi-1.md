Tacit Knowledge            

In The Tacit Dimension Michael Polanyi initially introduced the notion of “tacit knowledge,” by which he became known, to refer to those cases in which a speaker “knows more than he can verbalize.” He exemplified this situation with the experiment conducted by psychologists Lazarus a0nd McCleary in 1949.

“These authors presented a person with a large number of nonsense syllables, and after showing certain of the syllables, they administered an electric shock. Presently the person showed symptoms of anticipating the shock at the sight of ‘shock syllables’; yet, on questioning, he could not identify them. He had come to know when to expect a shock, but he could not tell what made him expect it. He had acquired a knowledge similar to that which we have when we know a person by signs we cannot tell.” ^(F2)

On reflection, the very notion of tacit knowledge (from now on TK), as characterized by Polanyi, sounds nonsensical. Indeed, it seems to amount to an unconscious knowledge, for the subject who supposedly knows is not aware of what he knows, since he cannot say what he knows. Now, an unconscious knowledge is an oxymoron. Why? Because the very notion of knowledge, or at least the ordinary notion of knowledge that common sense seems to impose on us, implies on the subject’s part the awareness of what he knows. He knows what he knows thanks to the awareness he has of what he knows, so he would not know what he knows without that awareness.

As an example of this ordinary notion of knowledge, let us consider the knowledge of which Searle spoke in the preceding chapter, since he used the word ‘knowledge’ in its ordinary meaning. According to Searle, in linguistic communication the hearer who understands what the speaker says thereby acquires the knowledge  – that is, becomes aware – of what the speaker intends to communicate to him. And it would be inconceivable that the hearer knew what the speaker intends to communicate if the hearer had no awareness at all of what he knew. In other words, what a subject knows becomes object known to him by the very fact of his becoming aware of it. Hence no knowledge without awareness of what is known, because knowledge implies and demands the awareness of the object known. Thus, when Polanyi defines KT as a knowledge in which the subject cannot say what he supposedly knows, we have to conclude that KT is a knowledge in which the subject is not aware of what he knows, which is a contradiction in terms.  

For his part, Polanyi would reply to this objection by referring us to the undeniable facts of the experiment he described: the subject of the experiment cannot say which are the shock-producing syllables because he does not know them, that is, is not aware of them, has no consciousness of them. Yet the subject’s behavior in reaction to those syllables shows that he knows them in a certain way, for he braces himself for a shock –and this ‘certain way of knowing’ which does not involve the awareness of the object known is what Polanyi calls TK.

As the facts of the experiment seem undeniable, we cannot ignore them. Let us then try to see things more clearly. For this purpose, it might be helpful to compare Polanyi’s TK to what Sartre called “non-thetic or non-positional awareness” in an analysis that led him to his famous notion of “Bad faith.” ^(F3) According to Sartre, the “censor” of which Freud speaks and to which Freud attributes the task of preventing the psychoanalysand from acknowledging his own shameful impulses by rendering them unconscious , is nothing else than the psychoanalysand himself who prevents himself from acknowledging them precisely because he is somehow conscious or aware of those. How, indeed, Sartre reasons, could the Freudian censor censure only the shameful impulses and not others, if they were totally unconscious?

What happens actually, explains Sartre, is that the psychoanalysand, who is conscious of his shameful impulses, does not want to assume responsibility for them. Now, what renders possible this hypocrisy on the psychoanalysand’s part, who pretends to himself not to know at all the impulses he himself censures with such discrimination, is that he is conscious or aware of them only under the mode of not being explicitly aware of them, because he has not of them the kind of thetic or positional consciousness of object we normally have of what  we know. The psychoanalysand, in Sartre’s view, has only an implicit or not verbalized awareness of his shameful impulses, and his awareness is only implicit precisely because it does not reach the level of verbal formulation. In short, the psychoanalysand knows his shameful impulses because he is conscious of them, yet he can pretend not to know them because he is only non-positionally aware of them, that is, cannot verbalize what he is aware of. By the way, this indicates the relationship between knowledge – which consists in the awareness of what is known – and linguistic verbalization – which consists precisely in expressing in words that of which the subject is aware, what is known to him.

In order to gain as precise an idea as possible of Polanyi’s tacit knowledge, let us now inquire whether Polanyi’s TK is equivalent to  Sartre’s non-positional consciousness. Thanks to his notion of non-positional consciousness Sartre can show, against Freud, that the psychoanalysand is conscious of his shameful impulses, that is, knows them, and is therefore responsible for his refusal to assume responsibility for them – a responsibility of which he is discharged by Freud under the pretext that they are unconscious. Sartre’s’s reply to Freud is that in order  to be conscious of something and, hence, to know it, it is not required to verbalize explicitly that of which one is only non-positionally conscious. Consequently,  the psychoanalysand does not formulate to himself that of which he is implicitly aware simply because he does not want to. Indeed, that is why Sartre considers him responsible for his shameful impulses. But this very trait of Sartre’s non-positional consciousness, which he rightly equates with our ordinary notion of knowledge because it amounts to the consciousness of what is known (though not the explicit consciousness brought about by verbalization), cannot be equated with Polanyi’s TK. Polanyi does not mean that the subject of the experiment with the shock-producing syllables is tacit because it is not even internally verbalized by the subject. The subject who has TK of the shock-producing syllables does not know at all , either explicitly or even implicitly, which are those syllables. Different in this from the Sartrean psychoanalysand, the subject of the experiment does not identify verbally the shock-producing syllables, not because he does not want to render explicit his knowledge of his shameful impulses in order not to have to assume responsibility for them, but because the subject of the experiment literally has no knowledge of them.    

Let us be clear. Sartre does not reject the ordinary notion of knowledge as consciousness of object. He does not claim that there is knowledge that does not involve awareness of object. He simply claims that the consciousness of object in which knowledge is ordinarily deemed to consist can take two forms: the form of  positional consciousness of object, thanks to verbalization, as well as the less common form of merely implicit or non-positional consciousness of object. In other words, he maintains that verbalization is not a necessary condition for knowledge, because the consciousness in which knowledge is ordinarily deemed to consist is possible without verbalization. Verbalization only renders explicit the implicit consciousness of object that the subject possesses before and without verbalization, and implicit or not verbalized consciousness is sufficient to constitute knowledge in the ordinary sense.

Thus, Sartre’s subject is initially non-positionally aware of his shameful impulses, so that, if he so decides, he can express in words the characteristics of the object of which he is non-positionally aware. The subject who possesses what Polanyi calls TK, by contrast, cannot say which are the shock-producing syllables because he does not know them at all. In other words, if TK is knowledge, it is not knowledge in the ordinary sense of the word, for it does not consist in either implicit or explicit awareness of object.

For my present purposes it is irrelevant whether or not one accepts Sartre’s view of implicit consciousness as pre-verbal consciousness of object. His analysis was only helpful to show that the commonly accepted notion of  knowledge, in whatever variety,  implies consciousness of object; TK, by contrast, does not involve consciousness of object. How, then, can Polanyi claim that what he calls “tacit knowledge” is knowledge at all? If it is, it has to be knowledge in a different sense. And can a different notion of knowledge make sense at all? How can knowledge be anything else than the knower’s awareness of the object known? A contrast between Searle’s and Polanyi’s views on the way language functions will be helpful in this connection.

Polanyi’s alternative conception of knowledge

Searle matter-of factly assumes and takes for granted the validity of the common conception of  knowledge as awareness of the object known, as well as the common conception of the relationship between the awareness in which knowledge consists and language. Indeed, the awareness of object possessed by the knowing subject is what is expressed in words when verbalization occurs. Saying, in Searle’s view, is nothing else than expressing in words that of which the subject is aware, and it is by understanding those words that the hearer becomes aware or comes to know or is informed of that of which the speaker is aware . ^(F4)

According to Searle, the primary and essential function of language is to inform of, or make known to the hearer, what the speaker intends to communicate to him. However, in saying something the speaker may have two different kinds of intentions: either simply to make his communicative intention  known to the hearer, or, in addition, to have him do something.  For instance, I can tell the doctor “I have a headache,” to make known to him my state, and I can tell him “Give me something for my headache.” In both cases I make known to him what I want to communicate to him. In the first case, my intention is to produce in the hearer the illocutionary effect; in the second case, to obtain a perlocutionary effect.  

According to Polanyi, however, TK is not awareness of the characteristics of the object known. That is the reason that TK is tacit: since TK does not involve awareness of the object known, the knowledge in which TK consists cannot be expressed in words that make it known. What occurs in the subject of the experiment at the sight of the shock-producing syllables is not an awareness of the object’s characteristics, for he does not know the syllables, but a blind fear that causes him to brace himself for the shock as if protecting himself from it. Now, this reaction of bracing himself  is not awareness of object, not even a silent or non verbalized one; it is a non-linguistic conduct or behavior caused by the affection of fear – a conduct whose reason is totally obscure to the subject, since he doesn’t know which syllables produce shocks.  

Thus, the subject of the experiment makes no linguistic enunciation either to himself or to  others. Rather, he automatically acts in a certain way, and this behavior is observed by those who conduct the experiment. Actually, this behavior is precisely the basis on which Polanyi grounds his claim that the subject ‘knows’ in one way or another, and without that behavioral manifestation at the sight of the shock-producing syllables and not at the sight of the other syllables Polanyi would have no basis for his claim of tacit knowledge on the subject’s part.

Now, this close connection with action of what Polanyi calls TK, in the absence of verbalization or enunciation of what is known, –which is why the knowledge is deemed to be tacit–  is an important clue. Indeed, it is a first indication that, in Polanyi’s view,  knowledge may consist in an action of a certain kind independent of verbalization rather than in consciousness of the object’s characteristics expressed in verbalization, as it has been traditionally assumed. In which case Polanyi’s notion of tacit knowledge, far from being as oxymoronic as it initially appeared, may even open a new epistemic avenue. Let us, therefore, review in detail Searle’s and Polanyi’s views, with special attention to the place action occupies in each of them.

The place of action in Searle’s and Polanyi’s respective conceptions of language

Searle distinguishes two kinds of linguistic effects: the illocutionary effect, which consists in knowledge of what the speaker intends to communicate, and the perlocutionary effects which for the most part consist in actions. However, in distinguishing the illocutionary from the perlocutionary effects, not only does Searle imply that knowledge is different and independent from action, but actually subordinates action to knowledge. The reason for this is that knowledge of what the speaker means appears to Searle as the necessary precondition for the success of all the perlocutionary intentions of a speaker, whereas the success of the illocutionary intentions of a speaker, that is, the hearer’s knowledge of what the speaker means, does not depend on the success of any of the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker. To put it concretely, a speaker cannot say anything without intending to convey to the hearer the knowledge of what he means, but he can say something without obtaining or even intending to obtain any other effect than the hearer’s knowledge of what he means. So Searle sees knowledge as independent from action . Correlatively, the hearer cannot fulfill the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker without knowing those intentions, that  is, without the illocutionary effect obtaining; but the hearer can know the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker, thereby insuring the success of the communicative act as such, without fulfilling the speaker’s perlocutionary intentions. For instance, if I do not know what you mean when you ask me to lend you  my pen, I cannot lend you my pen, but I can know that you ask me for my pen and not lend it to you. Moreover, you can produce in me an illocutionary effect without intending any perlocutionary effect. Thus, at least in Searle’s interpretation of saluting,  when you salute me, all you intend to produce in me is the knowledge that you mean to salute me, and the communicative exchange succeeds if only I gain that knowledge. So, in Searle’s view, knowledge is independent from action, but action is dependent on knowledge. Now, because the perlocutionary effects depend on the realization of the  illocutionary effect, but not the other way around, and because the speaker may say something without intending any perlocutionary effect but cannot say anything without intending to produce the illocutionary effect, which consists in the hearer’s awareness of  what the speaker means, Searle sees language primarily as a system of signs to communicate knowledge, and only secondarily and accidentally as a system of signs to obtain action.

Polanyi, for his part, based on the analysis of the way in which language functions, construes the relationship between knowledge and action in a totally different way. .For Searle, as just explained, action may or may not accompany knowledge. For Polanyi, knowledge without action is inconceivable because  action is a constitutive element or ‘moment’ of knowledge . Indeed, the knowledge involved in the communicative exchange is, in his view, essentially practical in that it is meant to terminate, and actually manifests itself in, a certain kind of action, not, as the case is for Searle, in a theoretical awareness of a fact whose natural manifestation is the linguistic enunciation of the object’s characteristics. For example, knowing an order received does not consist, according to Polanyi, in becoming aware of the order as a theoretical fact, but in the practical disposition to obey the order received, and in actually obeying it at the appropriate time, unless prevented by extrinsic causes. Similarly, according to Polanyi, knowing a promise received does not consist in becoming theoretically aware of that fact, as one becomes aware of a color when one sees it in the course of an experiment in a laboratory, but in actually disposing oneself for the fulfillment of the received promise at the appropriate time. If you promise to repay me the $100.00 you owe me, when I come to pick them up at your office at 3.00 P.M., the appropriate understanding of your promise does not consist in registering that fact and then doing nothing, as Searle contends,  but in expecting the fulfillment of your promise and in the action of actually coming to your office at 3.00 P.M. to collect my money.

Now, the idea that knowledge consists in a certain kind of  action rather than in the consciousness of an object’s characteristics and its linguistic enunciation is neither outlandish nor even  new, however much it may differ from the ordinarily accepted notion of knowledge as awareness of its object characteristics – an awareness that is reflected in the enunciative expression of the object’s characteristics. Spontaneously, that is, when free from epistemological prejudices, most of us believe that animals or the members of at least the higher species of mammals, say, dogs, possess the ability to know. At the same time, most of us believe that dogs are non-linguistic agents, that is, that they do not posses the linguistic ability we possess, for they do not express in words what they know. But this shows that we spontaneously assume that knowing is not identical with the consciousness of an object’s characteristics or with the consequent linguistic capacity to enunciate what one knows or is aware of.

On what basis, then, do we attribute to dogs the ability to know? On the basis of their observed actions . Certain of their actions are, we say,”intentional.” They  reveal an agent’s purpose , in that the dog’s agency is not uniform but adapted by the dog to different circumstances for the sake of a desired goal that satisfies a natural need of the dog.  By contrast, a marble’s fall down an inclined plane is a uniform action, not an adaptive or ‘intentional’ action, because it is caused by the environmental circumstances; moreover, the action is not intended or desired by the marble as a means for the satisfaction of a need or a desire and, therefore, is not modified by the marble according to the circumstances. For that very reason, we have no temptation of attributing knowledge to marbles: their action is not intentional, not for the sake of a goal intended-desired  by the marble, and the uniformity of the marble’s action shows no ability on the marble’s part to adapt its action to the circumstances in view of the realization of a goal. In a dogs’ actions, by contrast, we grasp the intention of a goal and, with their increased experience, an increasing ability on their part to adapt their actions to the circumstances and thereby make their actions more efficient for the realization of their natural goals. Hence we conclude that dogs know.

Of course, this analysis may be refined, but as it is it suffices for my purpose. For my purpose is simply to show that knowledge is not necessarily to be conceived, as Searle among so many others  conceives it, as an awareness of object that mirrors or reproduces the object’s characteristics in a way that is totally unrelated to action. Rather, our analysis of what – without any epistemological preconceptions – we spontaneously recognize as knowledge shows that what we identify as knowledge, at least  in the case of animals, involves intentional actions for the sake of desired goals, and that verbalization of what is known is not a necessary component of knowledge, since dogs do not verbalize and yet we grant t hat they know. In short, knowledge is not identified, at least in the case of animals, with a purely factual awareness of the characteristics of its object, unrelated to action, as implied by the ordinary or commonly accepted notion of knowledge,  but with an agent’s ability to achieve a desired goal through intentional action,  that is, through action adapted by the agent to the realization of the desired goal.

The fundamental difference between Polanyi’s and Searle’s respective conceptions of knowledge is that the former thinks that knowledge functions as an instrument for action, whereas Searle sees it as a purely theoretical representation of what is known, without any intrinsic relationship to action. In order, however, to understand the instrumental function Polanyi attributes to knowledge we have to study his views in more detail and to compare them to Searle’s.

Having distinguished the common or traditional conception of knowledge, shared by Searle, from Polanyi’s own, I should now make clear that the two conceptions are not only different but are mutually exclusive. In other words, the two cannot be correct . Why not? If for no other reason, because of the essential role desire plays in tacit knowledge, a role which is positively excluded in the traditional conception of knowledge, of which Searle’s views are only a particular version .  Indeed, not only does desire play no role at all in the traditional conception of knowledge, but it is positively excluded, for it is deemed to militate against the ‘objectivity’ of the cognition. The reason is that the consciousness of  object, in which knowledge supposedly consists, as well as its linguistic expression in enunciative speech, have to represent factually or ‘objectively’ the object, that is, without addition or subtraction by the subject. Not for nothing, in his close commentary on Aristotle’s treatise on linguistic interpretation, did Aquinas contrast what Aristotle sees as the genuinely enunciative speech of the scientist, which is dictated exclusively by the object and results in the knowledge of what is the case, with the non-enunciative speech of the rhetorician that appeals to the desires of the hearer and, therefore, distorts the object to be known.

Aquinas’s passage is such a succinct formulation of the distorting function of desire in matters of cognition that it deserves to be quoted in full:

“Then Aristotle says, Let us therefore consider enunciative speech, etc. Here he points out that only enunciative speech is to be treated; the other four species [to wit, deprecative, imperative, interrogative, and vocative] must be omitted as far as the present intention is concerned, because their investigation belongs rather to the sciences of rhetoric or poetics. Enunciative speech belongs to the present consideration and for the following reason: this book is ordered directly to demonstrative science, in which the mind of man is led by an act of reasoning to assent to truth from those things that are proper to the thing; to this end the demonstrator uses only enunciative speech, which signifies things according as truth about them is in the mind. The rhetorician and the poet, on the other hand, induce assent to what they intend not only through what is proper to the thing but also through the dispositions of the hearer. Hence, rhetoricians and poets for the most part strive to move their auditors by arousing certain passions in them, as the Philosopher says in t he Rhetorica. ^(F5)

Notice the contrast between the mind being led to knowledge by the object –exclusively “from those things that are proper to the thing”–  which Aristotle thinks is the case with genuinely enunciative speech, on the one hand, and, on the other hand, being led by the desires of the knower – “through the dispositions of the hearer.” As long as knowledge is conceived as a factual replication of the object by the consciousness of it the subject has and/or by the linguistic enunciation by the subject himself  of what is known to him, then desire can only be seen as a distorting element in the cognitive act. When, however, as is the case with TK, the point of cognition is not to represent or mentally reproduce the object but to function as an instrument for the agent’s appropriate conduct to achieve a desired end, then the desire of the end to be realized through action becomes an essential constitutive of cognition. ^(F6)

Knowledge as representation of object and knowledge as instrument for action

Let us return to the example Polanyi employs in The Tacit Dimension to introduce the notion of tacit knowledge. The subject in the experiment with the syllables knows which syllables produce shocks, but that knowledge does not consist in, nor does it manifest itself in, internal or external  words that express his awareness of those syllables, for the subject cannot say even to himself which are those syllables;  rather the knowledge manifests itself  in the subject’s conduct of bracing himself for a shock when those syllables appear on the screen. Thus, in the sense of being aware of the shock-producing syllables and of being able to say which are the shock-producing syllables, the subject of the experiment does not know . Yet he knows the syllables in the sense of acting in response to those syllables by bracing himself for the shock to come. Action , therefore, rather than linguistic enunciation, is the way in which knowledge manifests itself in this case.

As an initial illustration of the instrumental role Polanyi attributes to TK, by contrast to the vision or awareness of the object’s characteristics that mirror what is the case in reality, as Searle construes it, let us consider the way in which, according to Polanyi, eyeglasses function. When eyeglasses actually function as such, that is when they are worn by someone, not when they lie on a table and are looked at, the person who wears the eyeglasses does not see the eyeglasses, that is, has no awareness of them as object of knowledge, as is the case when they lie on a table and are looked at. In other words, when the eyeglasses are worn, they do not function as object known but as instrument. Now, we have to resist the temptation to complete the preceding sentence by saying that when worn on the nose the eyeglasses function as instrument of knowledge , that is, as instrument to acquire knowledge of the characteristics of the objects seen through the eyeglasses. For that is not what Polanyi means. He means that, when they are worn by someone, the eyeglasses function as an instrument for action , that is, as an instrument that guides the agent’s action in the realization of a desired goal. Such is the instrumental role played by TK, according to Polanyi, in every case; the case of the person wearing eyeglasses is only a particular illustration of the general rule.

Grasping the shift from the consciousness of object possessed by  the knowing subject of classical epistemology, a consciousness whose natural manifestation is the linguistic enunciation of what is known, to the agent’s actions, is fundamental to understand Polanyi’s conception of knowledge. Knowledge, for Polanyi, is the deed of an agent in the pursuit of a goal , not a quality of a purely contemplative, invulnerable consciousness. But this displacement of the contemplative subject of the classical epistemological tradition by the agent is insufficiently underscored, and perhaps even insufficiently grasped, by Polanyi himself. It will therefore be my task to bring this all-important point to the fore.

To gain a deeper understanding of the difference between knowledge conceived as explicit awareness of its object’s characteristics, and/or as verbal enunciation  of the characteristics of the object so known, on the one hand, as Searle conceives it, and, on the other hand,  of knowledge conceived as instrument for action, as Polanyi does, let us reflect on the way SIGNS function when we experience them as signs. Though neither Searle nor Polanyi speak specifically of sings, this reflection on the way signs function is important for two reasons. (1) Because, without using this terminology,  Polanyi thinks, as it will become apparent, that  the objects experienced in ordinary perception function precisely as signs. (2) Because reflection on the way signs actually function is required to correct a fundamental mistake in which Searle incurs. Indeed,  when actually experienced as signs, SIGNS FUNCTION AS.  INSTRUMENTS FOR action, not as signs OF something to be known, as mistakenly assumed by Searle.

How, then, do signs actually work? Let us focus on what goes on in our daily experience with traffic lights. We think of the red and green lights as signs. And we are not wrong, for they are signs. Where we go wrong –not when we perceive the traffic lights, but when we reflect on our perception of them–  is in assuming that signs are primarily signs OF something to be known by the subject who perceives the signs, something of which the subject who perceives the signs is to become aware, and which therefore will be susceptible of being made explicit when verbally enunciated by the subject. In fact, however, that is not how signs work. All signs without exception – whether natural or conventional, linguistic signs included– function as instruments FOR action, not as signs OF something that can be made known through linguistic enunciation. ^(F7) For example,  red in the case of a traffic light, is a sign FOR performing the action of stopping at the sight of the red light. Similarly, a green light is a sign FOR performing the action of moving ahead at the sight of the light. Similarly, an arrow pointing in a certain direction on a road is a sign not OF an object of knowledge that can be enunciated; it is a sign FOR performing the action of moving in the direction pointed at. It follows from this that the correct comprehension of signs is manifested not in linguistic enunciations of something signified but in the performance of a certain action, for  what is signified is in every case an action to be performed.  

All the preceding examples are examples of artificial signs that become signs by convention . Now Polanyi thinks that the objects we experience in ordinary perception, naturally and not by convention function as signs, that is, as signs For performing a certain action. If, for instance, when I am about to cross a street, I look on my left and see a car moving in my direction at a certain speed, that is a sign For stopping . It is to be remarked, though, that for a perceived object to actually function as a sign and lead to the performance of the action signified, a desire on the subject’s part is required, for whose fulfillment the action signified is relevant as a MEANS OR INSTRUMENT. For instance, the presence of water functions as a sign For drinking it only for an agent who is thirsty. And the agent’s action of drinking is the instrument through which the satisfaction of the agent’s desire is achieved. Similarly, if I am hungry, the sight of a piece of ham within my reach functions as a sign for eating it. Consequently, under ordinary circumstances, I read that sign as signs are to be read, that is, not representationally but instrumentally– as an instrument that guides me in the satisfaction of my desire of eating.  Thus, I move toward the place where I perceive the ham, and take the ham, and put it in my mouth. Of course, if I did not perceive the ham, I wouldn’t move toward the place where the ham is. But w hat I perceive Actually, my perception of the ham guides me in the performance of the signified action. It is precisely in this sense that signs are instruments for the performance of an action. When I see a car moving in my direction at a certain distance and speed, my stopping is an intentional purposeful action, a means to achieve my goal of reaching alive the other side of the road. I perceive the car not objectively, that is, independently of my dispositions as an exposed agent; I perceive the car as to be avoided, and the action signified is the instrument for the achievmeent of my goal. . ^(F8) That is what Aristotle had in mind when, as Nussbaum remarks, he observed that only when desire is present does action follow perception. Only when the role of desire and its connection with action are taken into account does the reason why certain objects of our ordinary perception function as signs FOR performing certain actions becomes intelligible.

It is clear, then, that perceptual objects, if and when they are relevant for the satisfaction of a desire of the perceiving subject, function as signs –and signs  mean performatively, not enunciatively . In other words, the signified is not of the order of awareness OF, an awareness whose content or object is or can be expressed linguistically in an enunciation of the characteristics of the sign or of the signified. For neither the sign nor the signified is not an object to be aware of, as an enunciation suggests, but an action to be performed.

An important consequence of this is that the perception of an object, when it  

f unctions as a sign, is totally different from the observation of an object ,  with which we tend to confuse it. Through perceptual observation one becomes aware of the characteristics of the object observed. However, precisely because a sign is a sign, not a thing to be itself  known, or a sign of an object to be known thanks to the sign, it refers practically, not theoretically. In other words, it is meant to terminate in action, not in awareness of object or in an enunciation of that awareness . The  observation of an object is inspired by curiosity concerning the observable characteristics of a thing. But the perception of an object as as a sign does not consist in observing the sign. This is what Polanyi was underscoring in his example of the shock-producing syllables. Because signs are neither things to be known themselves nor signs OF something to be known, the subject for whom the syllables function as a sign does not thereby gain the awareness of the individual characteristics of the syllables. Consequently, the subject cannot say which are those characteristics.

So little are we aware of the objets’ characteristics when we perceive them as signs that, as Polanyi observes, if I am fluent in both English and Spanish, sometimes I cannot remember whether something was said to me in English or in Spanish, for I am not aware of the signs themselves. Which is the very reason why the knowledge involved in the case of signs is tacit , by contrast to enunciative , for it is not meant to terminate in awareness of an object’s characteristics nor, consequently, in words that enunciate those characteristics, but in the action signaled. That is what Helen Keller discovered when she discovered language. The dictionary defines or exemplifies the conventional meaning of words as words, that is, of the conventional signs words are. Out of any context, the sound ‘w-a-t-e-r’, or the written word in the dictionary, may evoke in me, who am familiar with the English language, certain images and predicates related to the perceptual object we English speakers call water. When perceptual objects function as signs they do not function as words or as linguistic signs or as speech acts because they are not meant to refer. They function a signs FOR, but they do not mean to function as signs For, as words do, when actually used. They function as signs- For the performance of a certain action by virtue of the natural needs of animals or of people to whose satisfaction they happen to be relevant. It is from that relationship of the things denoted to the needs or desires of people or of other living creatures that names become naturally meaningful for them through association and, thus, begin to function as natural signs FOR when appropriately used to mean as such in particular contexts. Through association with the object denoted , which happens to be relevant to the satisfaction of needs, names become proxy for their object and therefore signs For action.

In the case of animals, that is all. This is how the name of its master becomes a potential sign-For, for a dog. But for us people, names also connote the concept of the object denoted, and the concept is independent of the subject’s desires and of the object’s index of immediate  instrumentality and of distances of immediate exposure to any subject.

Here we have to be careful not to confuse the connotation of a word as a word, which is identical with its conventional meaning in the language, with its connotation when actually used connotationally. In the later case, the words that are names of perceptual objects are proxy for the concept of their respective objects as subject of predicates. In this sense, they still refer to their objects, but only indirectly via the concept, and they refer only in this connotational  sense, not denotationally. However, though not denotationally and only indirectly, names still do refer to their objects. Were it not for this indirect reference, the return to observational experience of the object either to control   the validity of the  predicates attached to the concept of the object or to actually use the object as an instrument to achieve a goal would make no sense . This may help us to grasp the radical difference between Searle’s and Polanyi’s respective conceptions of language. Both of them think that a language functions as a system of signs. But of signs OF object of knowledge, or of signs FOR action? Their respective answers to this all-important question differ.

Let us consider Searle’s answer first. Searle thinks that language is primarily a system of signs OF. This is reflected in his entire view of language . Indeed, that is  the reason why he attributes to the illocutionary effect, which consists in knowledge OF object, absolute primacy over the perlocutionary effects, which consist in action. As Searle explains, the uniqueness of language, and its superiority over all other signs, is not that language means BECAUSE IT IS A SYSTEM OF SIGNS, for that language has in common with all signs, but that it says what it means. That is, language is not only a sign OF, but it enunciates what it is a sign OF.

In this connection, remember Searle’s analysis of the difference between language and games . A certain move in the game of chess means checkmate in the sense of automatically eliminating from the game the checkmated person and of terminating the game with the triumph of the player who checkmated him. However,  unlike the linguistic enunciation, the move in the game does not say or enunciate or express in words what it means . S aying, then, in Searle’s view, is making known through words that express or enunciate or describe what is to be known. The primary function of language is to inform or make known. Consequently, not only does Searle privilege the enunciative linguistic form because it is the only purely informative linguistic form, but he subordinates the speech acts that have perlocutionary force to the illocutionary ones –in that in order to have my hearer do what I want him to do I have first to make known to him what I want him to do. In so doing Searle he implicitly assimilates the speech acts that have  perlocutionary force to the illocutionary ones, for he hears them as signs OF something to be known-understood. Indeed, according to Searle, making  known the perlocutionary effects one wants to obtain is a necessary condition for obtaining those effects.

As a striking example of Searle’s subordinating of all the other speech acts to the enunciative ones let me mention his denial that the American soldier performed a linguistic action even if he obtained the perlocutionary effect he intended  by uttering the sounds “ Ich komme von dem Land wo die Zitronen blühen. ,” The reason Searle does not consider linguistic the Italians’ action is that the soldier did not say or enunciate “I am an American soldier,” which is what he intended to communicate. ^(F15) In this case, therefore, the action intended by the American speaker obtained in a non-linguistic way, not as a result fo what was said by the speaker.

But the most clear indication of Searle’s privileging of the enunciative linguistic form shows in  what he calls “the principle of expressibility,” to wit: that anything that can be meant can – and, ideally, should– be expressed  in words that actually say or enunciate what one wants to make known, that is, have replicated in the mind of the hearer. ^(F16)

This demand of expressing in words what one wants to make known makes sense only on the assumption that language is primarily a system of signs OF something to be known, rather than a system of signs For performing certain actions. If, in a game of chess, the situation of the pieces indicates or means chess mate, the game is over for any competent player.

It is important, however, to remark that Searle treats the enunciative linguistic form, to which he subordinates all other speech acts, as if it itself were a speech act. But it is not. Speech acts function as such only when meant a such by a particular speaker to a particular hearer or hearers in particular circumstances. But enunciating in general or saying in general, which is what Searle takes for the primary function of language, does not function as a speech act. Of course nothing can be said without saying something in particular. But what Searle calls “saying” is nothing else than the customary meaning of words by themselves –the very customary meaning he castigates Grice for confusing with what is meant in actual speech acts. ^(F17) Now, as Searle himself observes, words mean only when used in concrete speech acts. In short, Searle’s entire conception of the primary function of language as a system of signs that say or enunciate what they mean is grounded in the confusion of the enunciative linguistic form with the speech act of saying something to somebody in particular circumstances!

For Polanyi, by contrast, all speech acts, enunciative speech acts included, are meant to function, not as  signs OF the characteristics of an object to be known, but as signs FOR performing an action, that is, as instruments that guide the agent in the performance of the action meant. Actually, according to Polanyi, the only difference between enunciative speech acts and the other speech acts is not that the enunciative speech acts are signs OF something to be known rather than signs FOR an action to be performed, but that the enunciative speech acts are tacit in that they  do not specify the action meant but leave its determination to the linguistic agent according to his desires or needs as well as the circumstances in which the action is to be performed, much as it happens in the case of perception.*

The reason for this difficulty is that a map is not a picture of a territory, any more than a name is a picture of the object named. A map is a symbolic sign of a territory, but not of a territory to be contemplated or observed but of a territory to be walked through. In other words, not only is a map to be deciphered or ‘read,’ as all symbols are, but to be deciphered in relation to the action of moving through the territory to which the map refers, in order to reach a particular place within that territory. In other words, a map is a symbolic instrument to be used by an agent as an instrument to achieve his goal. That is why, if somebody claimed to understand a map but proved himself incapable of using it in practice, he wouldn’t know what he is saying. Indeed, since the map is an instrument for action, understanding it amounts to being able to manifest that understanding in one’s action. The mistake of the person claiming to understand a map but being incapable of actually finding it to find his way would be comparable to the one Searle makes when he claims that a person who is given an order understands it if only he grasps objectively the meaning of the words, without realizing that an order is to be obeyed, not to be understood objectively. In short, the genuine understanding of a map, as in the cae of any other instument, is to be manifested in the conduct that the map is meant to render possible, that is, in the conduct of reaching the desired place within the territory to which the map refers, much as the genuine understanding of a verbal joke is to be manifested in the affection of being actually amused by it.

Polanyi’s view of the reading of maps of the kind we are referring to goes as follows. The agent who reads the map must “inhabit” the map as he inhabits his body when he acts to realize a project, for instance, when he is nailing a nail to hang a picture. In other words, he must make the figure on the map function not as an object to be looked at and observed in itself, but as “that from which” he must act in order to arrive at the desired place. In short,  TK is always practical, for it involves the use of an instrument by an agent to achieve an intended goal, not a representation of object.

A more striking example of a symbolic instrument mentioned by Polanyi is the case of scientific theories. Used, as we are, to think of knowledge as a visual or intellectual representation of the mater theorized about, we will be surprised by the idea that scientific theories, like maps for action, function instrumentally rather than representationally. In other words, we tend to consider the statement in which, for instance, a physical law is expressed as a symbolic representation of the intelligible ‘nature’ of the particular phenomenon to which the law refers. For his part, Polanyi argues that the statements of scientific laws are meant to be used by the scientific agent as instruments to guide his conduct in the solution of concrete problems related to the portion of the world to which the law refers, much as a map is meant to functions as an instrument for the person who uses it to find his way within the mapped territory. . In the following text,  Polanyi explains how theories function in the actual practice of science, that is, when the theoretical instrument is actually used rather than talked about:

“ To rely on a theory for understanding nature is to interiorize it. For we are attending from the theory to things seen in its light, and we are aware  of the theory, while thus using it, in terms of the spectacle that it serves to explain. This is why mathematical theory can be learned only by practicing its application: its true knowledge lies in our ability to use it.” ^(F18)

This text, quoted  in isolation from its context, may be too dense and too cryptic to make us understand how, in Polanyi’s view, scientific theories function. What is clear is that Polanyi’s view contradicts the prevailing conception of science as a an exact, or at least an increasingly approximative, representational  view of reality. Thomas Kuhn, who on this fundamental point  repeatedly indicates his agreement with Polanyi, expressed it forcefully:

“A scientific theory is usually felt to be better than its predecessors not only in the sense that it is a better instrument for discovering and solving  puzzles but also because it is somehow a better representation of what nature is really like. One often hears that successive theories grow ever closer to, or approximate more and more closely to, the truth. Apparently generalizations like that refer not to the puzzle solutions and concrete predictions derived from a theory but rather to its ontology, to the match, that is, between the entities with which the theory populates nature and what is ‘really there.’ Perhaps there is some other way of salvaging the notion of ‘truth’ for application to whole theories, but this one will not do.... the notion of a match between the ontology of a theory and its ‘real’ counter part in nature now seems to me illusive in principle” ^(F19)

What Kuhn is pointing out here is that the statements in which scientific theories are expressed are not meant to function as representations of reality but as symbolic instruments to be used by agents to solve  problems that the surrounding world raises. In this regard, though, scientific statements constitute no exception. Indeed, if Polanyi is right, no statement –whether scientific or not– is  meant to function as a representation of its referent; rather, all statement are meant to function as  instruments for action, not as representations.

Searle and Polanyi on language

We are now well equipped to determine which of the two authors whose views on language we have studied is right. According to Searle, by replicating in his own mind what the speaker intends to communicate, thanks to his comprehension of what is said, the hearer gains the knowledge of what the speaker means or wants to communicate. Thus, according to Searle,  at least in the case of linguistic communication, knowledge consists in the correspondence between what is meant and said by the speaker and what is understood by the hearer. This, needless to say, coincides with the ordinary notion  of knowledge, which the Scholastics, following Aristotle, defined as adaequatio mentis ad rem : the conformation of the knower’s mind to the object. The only difference is that here Searle extends the same conception from the order of extramental reality to which it originally was applied to the order of linguistic communication. Now it is precisely regarding  this conception of knowledge that Polanyi parts ways with Searle.  

For Polanyi knowing is not a matter of becoming aware of the object to be known by acquiring a sort of reproduction of it that corresponds to the object, and which is true in the measure in which it corresponds to the object. Rather, for Polanyi, knowledge is tacit because it does not aim at reproducing –nor does it reproduce– the object in the mind by enunciating its characteristics, in which case the knowledge of the object would be explicit rather than tacit. Knowledge consists, rather,  in the practical  disposition to ACT in the way appropriate to achieve the knower’s goal, and in actualizing that disposition at the appropriate time. Thus, in the case of the shock-producing syllables, the subject has no awareness of the individual characteristics of the syllables. For that very reason the subject cannot say or express in words what are those characteristics of which he has no awareness. What the subject acquires, thanks to his past painful experience of being shocked, is not a faithful  image of the syllables. Rather, given his natural needs, the subject has a natural disposition to avoid pain and, hence, to perceive what produces pain not as object of ‘knowledge,’ in its particular characteristics, but as to be avoided through action. This  practical disposition to avoid the shock manifests itself in his bracing himself for the shock when the shock-producing syllables appear again on the screen, in order to protect himself as much as possible from the shock.

Whose conception of the relationship of knowledge to action is right: Searle’s or Polanayi’s?

Regarding those acts in which a perlocutionary effect is intended by the speaker , I have already shown how unpersuasive I find Searle’s view that in every act of communication the speaker intends to produce the knowledge of his communicative intentions –that is, the illocutionary effect–  and that this knowledge suffices to insure the success of the communicative act. For this means, concretely, that when I give you an order, what I intend primarily is that you understand and thereby be informed or acquire factual knowledge that I give you an order, not that you obey my order. Searle is so convinced that in the case of an order what the speaker intends is that his order be understood rather than obeyed that he considers the communicative exchange successful in the case in which the order is not obeyed, as long as the order is understood by the hearer.

Why do I think that Searle is wrong on this point? Because this aspect of Searle’s characterization of linguistic exchanges is  at odds with the practice of the competent linguistic agents. It is true that I cannot disobey an order without understanding it and thereby knowing it. But what is in contention is what constitutes the appropriate knowledge or understanding of an order and, in general, of all those linguistic acts that possess perlocutionary force, such as threats, warnings, promises, demands, etc. According to Searle, the primary intention of the speaker in all of the them is to inform the hearer of what he means, and only secondarily and accidentally to achieve the perlocutionary effect typical of the particular perlocutionary act, such as obeying it in the case of an order, expecting the fulfillment of the promise in the case of promising, etc.

But the linguistic facts contradict Searle’s opinion. If  Searle were right, there would be no difference in actual use between the grammatical imperative, used in the case of orders, and the grammatical indicative, properly used only when one wants to inform of a fact. Furthermore, the imperative mood  would be totally superfluous,  for imperatives should be understood as  indicatives. Indeed,  if Searle were right, giving an order (in the imperative) would be exactly equivalent to saying or enunciating (in the indicative) that one gives an order. But such is not the case. As I have already argued, when I give an order, I do not say – in the sense of enunciating– something to be merely understood, to wit: that I give an order; rather, I say the order I give in the sense of giving an order to be obeyed . When I say something in the sense of enunciating it, I refer my hearer to what I enunciate as something to be understood, that is, I intend to produce in my interlocutor the knowledge in which, according to Searle, the illocutionary effect consists. Therefore, if in giving an order I were enunciating that I give an order and the order I give, I should be satisfied if the addressee responded  “I understand your order.” But that is not the case. When I give an order –and this is why I use the imperative, not the indicative–  I expect and intend my order to be obeyed, which is the appropriate ‘understanding’ of an order by a competent linguistic agent.

Moreover, Searle’s opinion is also at odds with the practice of linguistic agents not only when what he calls ”perlocutionary effects,” but also in the case of statements, in which only an illocutionary effect seems to be intended.. For, as I have already argued, contrary to what Searle thinks, in a real-life situations, by contrast to the artificial classroom situation in which people are learning a second language, linguistic agents do not consider their linguistic action successful if a purely theoretical  understanding of what is said obtains; success, for linguistic agents, is identical with linguistic action, that is, with action that results by virtu of what is said. [Cf. GEOMETRY AS APPLICATIVE}.

Why does Searle identify saying with enunciating?  Because his linguistic characterizations and, hence, his entire conception of language,  is based not on his own experience as a linguistic agent who is actually participating in communicative interactions, but in his reflective experience as a curious philosopher who overhears the words said by those who participate in communicative interactions, or as a curious philosopher who reflects after the fact on the words said by himself and others when he and they participated in communicative interactions. I underline “the words said,” to indicate that Searle’s characterization of language, in spite of his disclaimers, focuses on the words said, independently of the conduct and the intentions or dispositions of those who say or hear them as actual participants in linguistic exchanges. This amounts to identifying linguistic meaning with saying in the sense of enunciating something, and, consequently, it amounts to considering the conduct of the linguistic agents as accidental to linguistic meaning, rather than integral to, or constitutive of, linguistic meaning. Clearly, however, this is not the kind of interiorization through agency through which children learn to play baseball, without being able to enunciate the rules they supposedly follow.

Thus Searle excludes from strictly linguistic meaning anything that in a linguistic exchange is not  words in which something is said or enunciated. Such is the case with  the actions and affections intended by the speaker in the communicative exchange, which consequentlyare seen as pure;y acidental and are excluded by Searle from being constitutive elements of linguistic meaning . That, indeed, was the reason why, in his example of the American soldier captured by the Italians , Searle explicitly denied that the action by which the American tricked the Italians into believing that he was a German soldier by uttering the sounds “ Ich komme von dem Land wo die Zitronen  blühen ” was a linguistic action, since the words expressed did not enunciate the action intended.

The exclusion from linguistic meaning of whatever is not explicitly said in words is reflected in the separation Searle postulates between the illocutionary and the perlocutionary effects, as well as in the secondary and merely accidental role he attributes to the latter. Searle himself , in explaining his principle of expressibility that  “whatever can be meant can be said,” implicitly claims that whatever is not said in words cannot be meant, for he implicitly  excludes from meaning the perlocutionary effects. ^(F20)

In short, because he identifies linguistic meaning with what is expressed in words that supposedly say or enunciate something to be understood or known, Searle automatically excludes from linguistic meaning those no less linguistic effects that do not consist in words that supposedly enunciate something to be understood, namely, the perlocutionary effects. No wonder, then, that  the way in which language functions in the artificial context of a course of a language for foreigners, in which only understanding  what is said counts as linguistic, to the systematic exclusion of any of the affections or actions intended by the speaker , ends up appearing as the model linguistic situation for Searle. Indeed it corresponds to Searle’s defective philosophy of language.

Now, to understand the differences between Searle and Polanyi, we have to realize that Searle’s linguistic philosophy amounts to an application to the field of language of the Platonic-Aristotelian epistemology inherited by the Western philosophical tradition. In that tradition,  knowledge consists, on the subject’s part,  in the representational conformity of his mind to reality, and, on the object’s part, in the ontological correspondence or conformity to what is in the subject’s mind. By contrast,  Polanyi’s conception of tacit knowledge constitutes an alternative to that tradition, for tacit knowledge is to be used instrumentally by the agent to achieve a desired goal, not a representation of what is the case.

As we shall now see, Polanyi does not contend that the Searlean perlocutionary effects as Searle conceives them, that is, as separated from the illocutionary effect, are a constitutive element of linguistic meaning, for the Serlean conception of the illocutionary element as different from the perlocutionary presupposes the legitimacy of his conceptual distinction between them. Polanyi would contend, rather, that this Serlean distinction reflects the separation between the knowing subject and the agent that is inherent to the epistemology inherited from Aristotle and Plato, which begins by conceiving knowledge as different from and separable form action. The Searlean distinction between illocutionary and perlocutionary effects is possible only on the wrong assumption, common to Aristotle and Plato, that knowledge and action stand to each other in the relationship presupposed by their own epistemology.

Quant a Polanyi, il dirait que la conduite des agents qui participent dans les echanges lingusitiques font partie  constituante, autant que les mots, de la signfication linguistique, de laquelle par consequent les effets perlocutionairess sont inseparables.

Le type de connaissance qui, d’apres Searle, resulte chez l’interlocuteur par la comprehension de ce qui lui est dit par le locuteur est une information purement factuelle sur les intentions communicatives du locuteur. Pour ma part, j’ai deja essayé de montrer combien peu convainquante est cette theorie dans les cas oú le locuteur entend obtenir des effets perlocutionnaires, c’est-a-dire, d’autres effets que la simple connaissance de ses intentions communicatives. Par example, lorsque le locuteur veut produire dans l’interlocuteur l’affection d’etre amusé dans le cas d’une plaisainterie, ou lorsqu’il veut obtenir du locuteur l’action de lui obeir dans le cas d’un commandemant.

De meme dans le cas de la lecture de la page musicale par le pianiste: le joueur de piano n’ observe pas les notes ecrites sur la page pour les connaitre objectivement dans leurs caracteristiques particulieres; il les emploie instrumentalement comme ce “a partir de quoil” il doit produire la musique signifiee par la page musicale. De meme dans le cas d’une plaisanterie linguistique: l’interlocuteur auquel la plaisainterie est adressee par le locuteur l’entend non pas comme objet a connaitre objectivement,  mais, ainsi que le locuteur en a l’intention en racontant la plaisanterie, comme instrument pour s’amuser. De meme, finalement, dans le cas d’un ordre reçu: l’interlocuteur l’entend, c’est-a-dire, le comprend, comme instrument pour guider son action obeissante, ce qui est en fait l’intention du locuteur. En effet, le locuteur qui donne un ordre n’entend pas faire connaitre objectivement l’ordre qu’il donne; il ne DIT pas un ordre a etre enregistre par l’interlocuteur, il commande, c.a.d., il,  donne un ordre a etre obei. Par consequent, c’est en obeissant que l’interlocuteur manifeste de facon adequate qu’il comprend de facon adequate l’intention de l’interlocuteur et, ce faisant, il rend reussie l’intention de ce dernier.

Voila l’alternative fournie par la CT polanyienne dans le terrain du langage, a la theorie de Searle, pour ce qui a trait a la comprehension linguistique des effets perlocutionnaires. Mais l’atlernativ e fournie par Polanyi va bien plus loin que le cas particulier des effets perlocutionnaires. Elle constitue une toute autre conception du langage que celle presupposée par Searle, puisque la distinction meme entre effets illocutionnaires et effets perlocutionnaires perd sa raison d’etre dans la perspective de Polanyi, pour la simple raison que dans cette derniere il n’y a pas de distinction entre sujet connaissant et agent. C’est l’agent lui-meme qui connait, mais qui connait tacitement, non objectivement, PARCE QUE SA CONNAISSANECE EST GUIDEE PAR LE DESIR DU BUT. En d’autres mots, pas de desir D’UN BUT PARTICULIER qui ne consiste pas dans une connaissance.

Dans la conception de Searle, par contre, la comprehension qui resulte dans la connaissance de ce que l’interlocuteur veut dire est le fait d’un sujet connaissant qui n’est pas lui-meme agent expose a l’action du monde environnant. De là la separation etablie par Searle entre, d’une part,  la connaissance, qui est le seul effet illocutionnaire et qui est independant de l’action et, d’autre part,  l’action, qui fait partie des effets perlocutionnaires est est subordonnée a la connaissance . Or, etant donne que, d’apres Searle, la realisation des effets perlocutionnaires n’est pas essentielle pour la reussite de l’action linguistique en tant que telle, il s’en suit que dans la conception de Searle il n’y a qu’une  place acidentelle pour l’agent et la veritable agence. Donc pas de  place pour la veritable agence, dont la fonction dans la communication lingusticque  n’est pas acidentelle mais essentielle.

Ce que je viens de dire peut sembler surprenant, puisque l’ecole linguistique des “s peech acts ” ou de l’action linguistique, a laquelle appartient Searle, est censée se distinguer precisement parce qu’elle voit dans le fonctionnement du langage une forme sui generis d’action, par contraste avec la connaissance. Cependant, quoi qu’il soit des autres membres de cette ecole, en separant le composant illocutionnaire des composants perlocutionnaires, en faisant de l’efffet illocutionnaire qui consiste dans la connaissance le seul element essentiel pour la reussite de l’action linguistique en tant que telle, et en faisant l’action dependre de la connaissance qui constitute l’effet illocutionnaire comme condition sine qua non pour que l’action ait lieu, Searle reduit effectivement l’action linguistique a la connaisance objective, a la connaissance comme pure vision d’objet . C’est cette vision d’objet purement theorique qui constitute l’effet illocutionnaire, laquelle suffit pour la reussite de l’action linguistique et sans laquelle les actions qui constituent les effets perlocutionnaires (par example, l’action d’obeir)  ne peuvent avoir lieu, tandis que la vision d’objet ne depend nullement des actions. Par contre, dans la conception de Polanyi, la vision d’objet ou connaissance objective disparait tout a fait du champ du fonctionnement du langage. Elle est substituteee par la CT, qui n’est pas connaisance objective mais instrumentale et donc en rapport intrinseque avec l’action. C’est pour cette raison que j’ai insiste plusieurs fois sur le fait que ce qui caracterise la conception de Polanayi, c’est le deplacement du sujet connaissant de la tradition epistemologique clasique par l’agent.

Que la conception Polanyienne eLimine tout a fait la connaissance objective du champ du fonctionnement du langage je crois l’avoir deja etabli pour le cas des effets perlocutionnaires. J’en viens maintenant au cas de l’effet illocutionnaire . Dans la communication linguistique, ce cas est representé concretement par les assertions, qui expriment les jugements intellectuels ou perceptuels du locuteur. Pour ma part, ce qui m’interesse exclusivement  ici c’est les assertions sur des faits particuliers du monde environnant du locuteur et de l’interlocuteur, c’est a dire du monde qu’ils percoivent et dans lesquels eux-memes vivent et agissent, ainsi que tout le monde.

Aristote s’occupe des assertions an tant que faits linguistiques dans son traité sur l’interpretation, Ρερι  Ηρμενειας,.MAIS LA IL NE FAIT QU’APPLIQUER AU CHAMPS DE  LA COMMUNICATION LINGUISTIQUE CE QU’IL AVAIT DEJA DIT SUR LES ASSERTIONS EN TANT QUE JUGEMENTS, C’EST A DIRE, EN TANT QUE FAITS EPISTEMOLOGIQUES SUSCEPTIBLES DE VALIDITE GNOSEOLOGIQUE DE PAR LEUR REFERENCE A LA REALITE .D’apres lui, les assertions, aussi bien affirmatives que negatives, expriment LINGUISTIQUEMENT des jugements en tant qu’actes gnoseologiques du locuteur. Cependant, A LA DIFFERENCE DE CE QUE JE FAIS ICI, Aristote ne se limite pas a etudier les assertions sur des faits perceptuels particuliers, c’est-a-dire, sur des etats particuliers du monde environnnat, parce que son projet epistemologique personnel de justifier  la verite des jugements “scientifiques” l’obligeat a s’occuper de preference des jugements intellectuels qui trouvent leur expressions dans des assertions universelles plutot que dans des jugments perceptuels sur des faits particuliers. En tout cas, et c’est ce qui compte pour nous, ce qui est commun aux jugements de deux types et aux assertions linguistiques aussi bien afirmatives que negatives qui les expriment, c’est, d’apres Aristote,  que eux et elles referent a ce qui est le cas en realite. Par la meme occasion, que leur referent soit ou bien un fait perceptuel particulier, ou bien  la “nature’ universelle d’une ou plusieurs realites particulieres (ce qui est le cas dans les jugements “scientifiques), les assertions sont, ainsi qu”Aristote le souligne fortement, les seules formes linguistiques qui affirment leur propre verite et, par consequent, les seules formes linguistiques qui soient susceptibles d’etre vraies ou faussess. En effet, comme le remarque Aristote,  une requete faite, un ordre donné, un souhait exprimé, etc., ne peuvent etre ni vrais ni faux. D’où la place privilegiée que les assertions occupent aussi bien dans l’oeuvre d’Aristote qu’en general dans toutes les oeuvres du genre philosophique inspiré par Aristote. Ce n’est pas par hasard que  la Critique de la raison pure de Kant est entierement devouée a l’etude des jugements et leur verité.    

D’apres Aristote, la verite des assertions aussi bien affirmatives que negatives et des jugements exprimés par elles depend de leur conformite a ce qui est le cas en realite. En d’autres termes, a propos des jugements et des assertions linguistiques, Aristote soutient la theorie de la connaissance objective. En fait il est le philosophe qui, le premier, dans sa theorie du jugdment, donna forme a l’epoistemologie qui  allait devenir pour ainsi dire canonique dans la tradition philosophique occidentale. ^(F21)

Revenons a Searle. Dans mon apercu de ses vues au chapitre precedent, je n’eus pas a m’occuper de la facon dont il traite le rapport de verite ou faussete des assertions linguistiques, car il n’adresse pas ce sujet sous la rubrique de la communication entre locuteur et interlocuteur mais sous celle des assertions comme l’ acte linguistique particulier qui refere au monde et qui comporte l’afirmation de sa propre verite. Cependant, quand il en vient aux assertions comme acte linguistique particulier, il les concoit a la maniere d’Aristote comme expressions  d’une connasissance objective possedée par le locuteur, comme expressions d’une vision d’objet qui est jugée etre vraie par le locuteur et qui l’est en fait, ou qui ne l’est pas, dans la mesure ou elle est conforme, ou ne l’est pas, a ce qui est le cas en realite. Bref, sous ce rapport, Searle n’est pas un disciple de Kant mais d’Aristote, pour lequel la realite extramentale ne faisait pas de doute. Par consequent, Aristote concevait la verite des jugements et de leurs expressions linguistiques par rapport a la realite extramentale, et par un rapport de subordination a elle, puisque c’est la connaissance qui doit se conformer a la  realité. et non pas la realité a la connaissance. La seule difference, d’ailleurs secondaire, entre Aristote et Searle, est que ce dernier separe la question de la conformité de la connaissance que l’interlocuteur acquiert des intentions communicatives du locuteur avec ces intentions, de la question de la conformité  des assertions perceptuelles a la realite, tandis qu’Aristote, qui n’a  pas de theorie explicite de la communication linguistique, ne distingue pas les deux questions. Pour Searle, par contre, une chose est la communication liguistique entre locuteur et interlocuteur; une toute autre chose est le cas particulier de la verite out faussete, disons “ontologique,” des seuls actes linguistiques qui se proclament porteurs de verite de par leur reference a la realite, a savoir, les assertions de ce qui est le cas ou ne l’est pas en realite. La communication en tant que telle comprend tous les actes linguistiques, aussi bien ceux dans lesquels l’intention du locuteur est exclusivement de communiquer une connaissance, que ceux par le moyen desquels, en outre d’une connaissance de ce qu’il veut dire le locuteur veut obtenir un effet perlocutionnaire. Dans le premier cas, les assertions tombent sous la rubrique de la communication, comme un acte linguistique parmi tous les autres. Dans le second cas les assertions sont etudiées comme le seul acte linguistique susceptible d’etre vrai ou faux. Or puisqu’Aristote ne traite pas de la communication en tant que telle, il n’a aucune raison de distinguer les deux questions ni de les traiter separement.

Demandons-nous donc si, au moins dans leur cas, Searle a raison de les concevoir a la maniere Aristotelienne comme expressions de la vision de ce qui est le cas , possedée par le locuteur en tant que sujet connaissant, c’est-a-dire, d’interpreter les assertions comme porteuses de connaissance objective. Or, s’il s’agit de transmission de connaissance objective, ainsi que Searle l’entend,c’est-a-dire, s’il s’agit de communiquer a mon ami ma vision objective de ce qui est le cas, vision qu’il acquerra par la comprehension de ce que je dis etre le cas, alors une reponse parfaitement adequate de la part de mon ami serait de me dire: “J’ai compris que ma soeur est au telephone” et de rester la ou il est sans rien faire d’autre. Pour ma part, en tant qu’agent linguistique competent, je m’attend a une toute autre reponse et je serais interloqué si la reponse de mon ami etait celle que je viens de decrire. Je m’attends, en effet  (et je suis persuade que, contrairement a Searle, n’importe quel agent linguistique competent en langue francaise me donnerait la raison), je m’attends a ce que mon ami prenne le telephone et parle avec sa soeur. Pour le dire autrement, je m’attends a ce que mon ami fasse fonctionner mon assertion, non comme porteuse de la connaissance objective de ce qui est le cas, mais comme instrument pour son action, comme CT “a partir de laquelle’ il doit agir et prendre le telephone. De meme si je dis a mon ami qui cherche le marteau: “Le marteau est dans la salle a manger,” je m’attends a ce qu’il aille dans la salle a manger prendre le marteau, non pas a ce qu’il acquiere une vision purement theorique, c.a.d. sans rapport intrinseque avec l’action, du marteau dans la salle a manger, ainsi que Searle le pense (ou devrait le penser s’il croit vraiement a sa theorie linguistique). De meme, pour terminer, dans le cas de n’importe quelle assertion linguistique d’un etat concret particulier du monde environnant.

En fait, j’avais deja plus haut fait la critique de la position de Searle au sujet de la connaissance objective dans la communication linguistique, en disant que son modele du fonctionnement du langage est la maniere bâtarde dont le langage fonctionne dans le cadre artificel d’un cours de langue pour des etrangers. La seule difference est que dans le chapitre precedent je me referait au chapitre de son essaai où Searle parle de tous les actes linguistiques sous la rubrique de la communication, et ici  je me refere au chapitre oul il etudie le cas particulier des assertions dont le referent est ce qui est le cas en realite EXTRAMENTALE, TOTALEMENT INDEPENDANTE DES DISPOSITIONS OU DESIRS DU SUJET . En tout cas, pour mon propos, cette difference n’est pas significative, puisque Searle concoit les deux cas comme examples de connaissance objective ou connaissance d’objet, tandis que je soutiens avec Polanyi que ce que nous appelons ordinairement “connaissance” fonctionne comme instrument pour l’agent , non comme representation d’objet pour le sujet connaissant de la tradition epistemologique classique.

Quant a la fertilite de la substitution du sujet connaissant par l’agent dans la conception polanyienne de la CT, nous aurons l’occasion de l’apprecier au prochain chapitre dans son application dans le terrain de la science. Effectivement, ainsi que la conception polanyienne fournit une alternative a la facon ordinaire de comprendre le fonctionnement du langage, representée par la theorie de Searle, ainsi elle fournit egalement une alternative a la facon ordinaire de concevoir le fonctionnement de la science, representée par la conception classique de la science comme vision correcte ou du moins de plus en plus approximative de la  nature de la realité.
