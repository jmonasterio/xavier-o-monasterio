Tacit Knowledge            

In The Tacit Dimension Michael Polanyi initially introduced the notion of “tacit knowledge,” by which he became known, to refer to those cases in which a speaker “knows more than he can verbalize.” He exemplified this situation with the experiment conducted by psychologists Lazarus and McCleary in 1949.

“These authors presented a person with a large number of nonsense syllables, and after showing certain of the syllables, they administered an electric shock. Presently the person showed symptoms of anticipating the shock at the sight of ‘shock syllables’; yet, on questioning, he could not identify them. He had come to know when to expect a shock, but he could not tell what made him expect it. He had acquired a knowledge similar to that which we have when we know a person by signs we cannot tell.” ^(F2)

On reflection, the very notion of tacit knowledge (from now on TK), as characterized by Polanyi, sounds nonsensical. Indeed, it seems to amount to an unconscious knowledge, for the subject who supposedly knows is not aware of what he knows, since he cannot say what he knows. Now, an unconscious knowledge is an oxymoron. Why? Because the very notion of knowledge, or at least the ordinary notion of knowledge that common sense seems to impose on us, implies on the subject’s part the awareness of what he knows. He knows what he knows thanks to the awareness he has of what he knows, so he would not know what he knows without that awareness.

As an example of this ordinary notion of knowledge, let us consider the knowledge of which Searle spoke in the preceding chapter, since he used the word ‘knowledge’ in its ordinary meaning. According to Searle, in linguistic communication the hearer who understands what the speaker says thereby acquires the knowledge  – that is, becomes aware – of what the speaker intends to communicate to him. And it would be inconceivable that the hearer knew what the speaker intends to communicate if the hearer had no awareness at all of what he knew. In other words, what a subject knows becomes object known to him by the very fact of his becoming aware of it. Hence no knowledge without awareness of what is known, because knowledge implies and demands the awareness of the object known. Thus, when Polanyi defines KT as a knowledge in which the subject cannot say what he supposedly knows, we have to conclude that KT is a knowledge in which the subject is not aware of what he knows, which is a contradiction in terms.  

For his part, Polanyi would reply to this objection by referring us to the undeniable facts of the experiment he described: the subject of the experiment cannot say which are the shock-producing syllables because he does not know them, that is, is not aware of them, has no consciousness of them. Yet the subject’s behavior in reaction to those syllables shows that he knows them in a certain way, for he braces himself for a shock –and this ‘certain way of knowing’ which does not involve the awareness of the object known and cannot therefore be enunciated is what Polanyi calls TK.

As these facts seem  undeniable, we cannot ignore them. Let us then try to see things more clearly. For this purpose, it might be helpful to compare Polanyi’s TK to what Sartre called “non-thetic” or “non-positional awareness” in an analysis that led him to his famous notion of “Bad faith.” ^(F3) According to Sartre, the “censor” of which Freud speaks and to which Freud attributes the task of preventing the psychoanalysand from acknowledging his own shameful impulses by rendering them unconscious , is nothing else than the psychoanalysand himself who prevents himself from acknowledging those impulses precisely because he is somehow conscious or aware of those. How, indeed, Sartre reasons, could the Freudian censor censure only the shameful impulses and not others, if they were totally unconscious?

What happens actually, explains Sartre, is that the psychoanalysand, who is conscious of his shameful impulses, does not want to assume responsibility for them. Now, what renders possible this hypocrisy on the psychoanalysand’s part, who pretends to himself not to know at all the impulses he himself censures with such discrimination, is that he is conscious or aware of them only under the mode of not being explicitly aware of them, because he has not of them the kind of thetic or positional consciousness of object we normally have of what  we know. The psychoanalysand, in Sartre’s view, has only an implicit or not verbalized awareness of his shameful impulses, and his awareness is only implicit precisely because it does not reach the level of verbal formulation. In short, the psychoanalysand knows his shameful impulses because he is conscious of them, yet he can pretend not to know them because he is only non-positionally aware of them, that is, does not verbalize what he is aware of. By the way, this indicates the relationship between knowledge – which consists in the awareness of what is known – and linguistic verbalization – which consists precisely in expressing in words that of which the subject is aware, what is known to him.

In order to gain as precise an idea as possible of Polanyi’s TK, let us now inquire whether Polanyi’s TK is equivalent to  Sartre’s non-positional consciousness. Thanks to his notion of non-positional consciousness Sartre can show, against Freud, that the psychoanalysand is conscious of his shameful impulses, that is, knows them, and is therefore responsible for his refusal to assume responsibility for them – a responsibility of which he is discharged by Freud under the pretext that they are unconscious. Sartre’s reply to Freud is that in order  to be conscious of something and, hence, to know it, it is not required to verbalize explicitly that of which one is only non-positionally conscious. Consequently,  the psychoanalysand does not formulate to himself that of which he is implicitly aware simply because he does not want to. Indeed, that is why Sartre considers him responsible for his shameful impulses. But this very trait of Sartre’s non-positional consciousness, which he rightly equates with our ordinary notion of knowledge because it amounts to the consciousness of what is known (though not the explicit consciousness brought about by verbalization), cannot be equated with Polanyi’s TK. Polanyi does not mean that the subject of the experiment with the shock-producing syllables is tacit because it is not even internally verbalized by the subject. The subject who has TK of the shock-producing syllables does not know at all , either explicitly or even implicitly, which are those syllables. Different in this from the Sartrean psychoanalysand, the subject of the experiment does not identify verbally the shock-producing syllables, not because he does not want to render explicit his knowledge of his shameful impulses in order not to have to assume responsibility for them, but because the subject of the experiment literally has no knowledge of them.    

Let us be clear. Sartre does not reject the ordinary notion of knowledge as consciousness of object, for he does not claim that there is knowledge that does not involve awareness of object. He simply claims that the consciousness of object in which knowledge is ordinarily deemed to   consist can take two forms: the form of  positional consciousness of object, thanks to verbalization, as well as the less common form of merely implicit or non-positional consciousness of object. In other words, verbalization is not a necessary condition for knowledge, because the consciousness in which knowledge is ordinarily deemed to consist is possible without verbalization. Verbalization only renders explicit the implicit consciousness of object that the subject possesses before and without verbalization, and implicit or not verbalized consciousness is sufficient to constitute knowledge in the ordinary sense.

Thus, Sartre’s subject is initially non-positionally aware of his shameful impulses, so that, if he so decides, he can express in words the characteristics of the object of which he is non-positionally aware. The subject who possesses TK, by contrast, cannot say which are the shock-producing syllables because he does not know them at all. In other words, if TK is knowledge, it is not knowledge in the ordinary sense of the word, for it does not consist in either implicit or explicit awareness of object.

For my present purposes it is irrelevant whether or not one accepts Sartre’s view of implicit consciousness as pre-verbal consciousness of object. His analysis was only helpful to show that the commonly accepted notion of  knowledge, in whatever variety,  implies consciousness of object; TK, by contrast, does not involve consciousness of object.

How, then, can Polanyi claim that what he calls “tacit knowledge” is knowledge at all? If it is, it has to be knowledge in a different sense. And can a different notion of knowledge make sense at all? How can knowledge be anything else than the knower’s awareness of the object known? A contrast between Searle’s and Polanyi’s views on the way language functions will be helpful in this connection.

Polanyi’s alternative conception of knowledge

Searle matter-of-factly assumes and takes for granted the validity of the common conception of  knowledge as awareness of the object known, as well as the common conception of the relationship between the awareness in which knowledge consists and its linguistic expression.. Indeed, the awareness of object possessed by the knowing subject is what is expressed in words when verbalization occurs. Saying, in Searle’s view, is nothing else than expressing in words that of which the subject is aware, and it is by understanding those words that the hearer becomes aware or comes to know or is informed of that of which the speaker is aware. ^(F4)

According to Searle, the primary and essential function of language is to inform of or make known to the hearer what the speaker intends to communicate to him by saying it. However, the speaker may have two different kinds of intentions: either to make something known to the hearer, or to have him do something.  I can tell the doctor “I have a headache,” to make known to him my state, and I can tell him “Give me something for my headache.” In both cases I make known to him what I want to communicate to him. In the first case, however, my intention is to produce in the hearer the illocutionary effect; in the second case, to produce in the hearer a perlocutionary effect.

Polanyi disagrees with Searle concerning the nature of knowledge. According to Polanyi,  TK is not awareness of the characteristics of the object known. That is the reason that TK is tacit: since TK does not involve awareness of the object known, the knowledge in which TK consists cannot be expressed in words that make known what the subject is aware of. What occurs in the subject of the experiment at the sight of the shock-producing syllables is not an awareness of the object’s characteristics, for he does not know the syllables, but a blind fear that causes him to brace himself for the shock as if protecting himself from it. Now, this reaction of bracing himself  is not awareness of object, not even a silent or non verbalized one; it is a non-linguistic conduct or behavior caused by the affection of fear – a conduct whose reason is totally obscure to the subject, since he doesn’t know which syllables produce shocks.  

Thus, the subject of the experiment makes no linguistic enunciation either to himself or to  others. Rather, he automatically acts in a certain way, and this behavior is observed by those who conduct the experiment. Actually, this observed behavior is precisely the basis on which Polanyi grounds his claim that the subject ‘knows’ in one way or another, and without that behavioral manifestation at the sight of the shock-producing syllables and not at the sight of the other syllables Polanyi would have no basis for his claim of tacit knowledge on the subject’s part.

Now, this close connection of TK with action   in the absence of verbalization or enunciation of what is known is an important clue. Indeed, it is a first indication that, in Polanyi’s view,  knowledge may consist in action of a certain kind  rather than in consciousness of the object’s characteristics expressed or at least expressible in verbalization, as it has been traditionally assumed. In which case Polanyi’s notion of tacit knowledge, far from being as oxymoronic as it initially appeared, may even open a new epistemic avenue. Let us, therefore, review in detail Searle’s and Polanyi’s views, with special attention to the place action occupies in each of them.

The place of action in Searle’s and Polanyi’s respective conceptions of language

Searle distinguishes two kinds of linguistic effects: the illocutionary effect, which consists in knowledge of what the speaker intends to communicate, and the perlocutionary effects which for the most part consist in actions of the hearer intended by the speaker. However, in distinguishing the illocutionary from the perlocutionary effects, Searle ipso facto subordinates action to knowledge. The reason for this is that knowledge of what the speaker means appears to Searle as the necessary precondition for the success of all the perlocutionary intentions of a speaker, whereas the success of the illocutionary intentions of a speaker, that is, the hearer’s knowledge of what the speaker means, does not depend on the success of any of the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker. To put it concretely, a speaker cannot say anything without intending to convey to the hearer the knowledge of what he means, but he can say something without obtaining or even intending to obtain any other effect than the hearer’s knowledge of what he means. So Searle sees knowledge as independent from action. Correlatively, the hearer cannot fulfill the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker without knowing those intentions, that  is, without the illocutionary effect obtaining; but the hearer can know the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker, thereby insuring the success of the communicative act as such, without fulfilling the speaker’s perlocutionary intentions. For instance, if I do not know what you mean when you ask me to lend you  my pen, I cannot lend you my pen, but I can know that you ask me for my pen and not lend it to you. Moreover, you can produce in me an illocutionary effect without intending any perlocutionary effect. Thus, in Searle’s interpretation of saluting,  when you salute me, all you intend to produce in me is the knowledge that you mean to salute me, and the communicative exchange succeeds if only I gain that knowledge. So, in Searle’s view, knowledge is independent from action, but action is dependent on knowledge. Now, because the perlocutionary effects depend on the realization of the  illocutionary effect, but not the other way around, and because the speaker may say something without intending any perlocutionary effect but cannot say anything without intending to produce the illocutionary effect, which consists in the hearer’s awareness of  what the speaker means, Searle sees language primarily as a system of signs to communicate knowledge, and only secondarily and accidentally as a system of signs to effect action.

Polanyi, for his part, based on the analysis of the way in which language functions, construes the relationship between knowledge and action in a totally different way..For Searle, as just explained, action may or may not accompany knowledge. For Polanyi, knowledge without action is inconceivable because  action is a constitutive element or ‘moment’ of knowledge . Indeed, the knowledge involved in the communicative exchange is, in his view, essentially practical in that it is meant to terminate, and actually manifests itself in, a certain kind of action, not, as the case is for Searle, in a theoretical awareness of a fact whose natural manifestation is the linguistic enunciation of the object’s characteristics. For example, knowing an order received does not consist, according to Polanyi, in becoming aware of the order as a theoretical fact, but in the practical disposition to obey the order received, and in actually obeying it at the appropriate time, unless prevented by extrinsic causes. Similarly, according to Polanyi, knowing a promise received does not consist in becoming theoretically aware of that fact, as one becomes aware of a color when one sees it in the course of an experiment in a laboratory, but in actually disposing oneself for the fulfillment of the received promise at the appropriate time. If you promise to repay me the $100.00 you owe me, when I come to pick them up at your office at 3.00 P.M., the appropriate understanding of your promise does not consist in registering that fact and then doing nothing, as Searle contends,  but in expecting the fulfillment of your promise and in the action of actually coming to your office at 3.00 P.M. to collect my money.

Now, the idea that knowledge consists in a certain kind of  action rather than in the   consciousness of an object’s characteristics and its linguistic enunciation is neither outlandish nor even  new, however much it may differ from the ordinarily accepted notion of knowledge as awareness of its object characteristics – an awareness that is reflected in the enunciative expression of the object’s characteristics. Spontaneously, that is, when free from epistemological prejudices, most of us believe that the members of at least the higher species of mammals, say, dogs, possess the ability to know. At the same time, most of us believe that dogs are non-linguistic agents, that is, that they do not posses the linguistic ability we possess, for they do not express in words what they know. But this shows that we spontaneously assume that knowing is not identical with the consciousness of an object’s characteristics or with the consequent linguistic capacity to enunciate what one knows or is aware of.

On what basis, then, do we attribute to dogs the ability to know? On the basis of their observed actions . Certain of their actions are, we say,”intentional.” They  reveal an agent’s purpose , in that the dog’s agency is not uniform but adapted by the dog to different circumstances for the sake of satisfying a natural need of the dog.  By contrast, a marble’s fall down an inclined plane is a uniform action, not an adaptive or ‘intentional’ action, because it is caused by the environmental circumstances; moreover, the action is not intended by the marble for the sake of the satisfaction of a need and, therefore, it not modified by the marble according to the circumstances. For that very reason, we have no temptation of attributing knowledge to marbles: their action is not intentional, not for the sake of a goal intended-desired  by the marble, and the uniformity of the marble’s action shows no ability on the marble’s part to adapt its action to the circumstances in view of the realization of a goal. In a dogs’ actions, by contrast, we grasp the intention of a goal and, with their increased experience, an increasing ability on their part to adapt their actions to the circumstances and thereby make their actions more efficient for the realization of their natural goals. Hence we conclude that dogs know.

Of course, this analysis may be refined, but as it is it suffices for my purpose. For my purpose is simply to show that knowledge is not necessarily to be conceived, as Searle among so many others  conceives it, as an awareness of object that mirrors or reproduces the object’s characteristics in a way that is totally unrelated to action. Rather, our analysis of what – without any epistemological preconceptions – we spontaneously recognize as knowledge shows that what we identify as knowledge, at least  in the case of animals, involves intentional actions for the sake of desired goals, and that verbalization of what is known is not a necessary component of knowledge. In short, knowledge is not identified, at least in this case, with a purely factual, purely theoretical or non-practical awareness of the characteristics of its object, as implied by the ordinary or commonly accepted notion of knowledge,  but with an agent’s ability to realize a desired goal through intentional action,  that is, through action adapted by the agent to the realization of the desired goal.

Knowledge and desire

Having distinguished the common or traditional conception of knowledge, shared by Searle, from Polanyi’s own, I should now make clear that the two conceptions are not only different but are mutually exclusive. In other words, the two cannot be correct. Why not? If for no other reason, because of the essential role desire plays in tacit knowledge, a role which is positively excluded in the traditional conception of knowledge, of which Searle’s views are only a particular version .  Indeed, not only does desire play no role at all in the traditional conception of knowledge, but it is positively excluded, for it is deemed to militate against the ‘objectivity’ of the cognition. The reason is that the consciousness of  object, in which knowledge supposedly consists, as well as its linguistic expression in enunciative speech, have to represent factually or ‘objectively’ the object, that is, without addition or subtraction by the subject. Not for nothing, in his close commentary on Aristotle’s treatise on linguistic interpretation, did Aquinas contrast what Aristotle sees as the genuinely enunciative speech of the scientist, which is dictated exclusively by the object and results in the knowledge of what is the case, with the non-enunciative speech of the rhetorician that appeals to the desires of the hearer and, therefore, distorts the object to be known and fails to attain the truth.

Aquinas’s passage is such a succinct formulation of the distorting function of desire in matters of cognition that it deserves to be quoted in full:

“Then Aristotle says, Let us therefore consider enunciative speech, etc. Here he points out that only enunciative speech is to be treated; the other four species [to wit, deprecative, imperative, interrogative, and vocative] must be omitted as far as the present intention is concerned, because their investigation belongs rather to the sciences of rhetoric or poetics. Enunciative speech belongs to the present consideration and for the following reason: this book is ordered directly to demonstrative science, in which the mind of man is led by an act of reasoning to assent to truth from those things that are proper to the thing; to this end the demonstrator uses only enunciative speech, which signifies things according as truth about them is in the mind. The rhetorician and the poet, on the other hand, induce assent to what they intend not only through what is proper to the thing but also through the dispositions of the hearer. Hence, rhetoricians and poets for the most part strive to move their auditors by arousing certain passions in them, as the Philosopher says in the Rhetorica. ^(F5)

Notice the contrast between the mind being led to knowledge by the object exclusively

– “from those things that are proper to the thing”–  which Aristotle thinks is the case with genuinely enunciative speech, on the one hand, and, on the other hand, being led by the desires of the knower – “through the dispositions of the hearer.” Only in the former case does knowledge of the thing result, not when desire intervenes. Indeed, as long nowledge is conceived as a factual replication of the object by the consciousness of it the subject has and/or by the linguistic enunciation of what is known to the subject , then desire can only be seen as a distorting element in cognition. When, however, as is the case with TK, the point of cognition is not to represent or mentally reproduce the object but to function as an instrument for the agent’s appropriate conduct to achieve a desired end, then the desire of the end to be realized through action becomes an essential constitutive of cognition. ^(F6)

Knowledge as representation of object and knowledge as instrument for action

One important difference between Polanyi’s and Searle’s respective conceptions of knowledge is that the former thinks that knowledge functions as an instrument for action, whereas Searle sees it as a purely theoretical representation of what is known, without any intrinsic relationship to action. In order, however, to understand the instrumental function Polanyi attributes to knowledge we have to study his views in more detail.

Let us return to the example Polanyi employs in The Tacit Dimension to introduce the notion of tacit knowledge. The subject in the experiment with the syllables knows which syllables produce shocks, but that knowledge does not consist in, nor does it manifest itself in, internal or external  words that express his awareness of those syllables, for the subject cannot say even to himself which are those syllables;  rather the knowledge manifests itself  in the subject’s conduct of bracing himself for a shock when those syllables appear on the screen. Thus, in the sense of being aware of the shock-producing syllables and of being able to say which are the shock-producing syllables, the subject of the experiment does not know . Yet he knows the syllables in the sense of acting in response to those syllables by bracing himself for the shock to come. Action , therefore, rather than linguistic enunciation, is the way in which knowledge manifests itself in this case.

To gain a deeper understanding of the difference between knowledge conceived as awareness of its object’s characteristics, and/or as verbal enunciation  of the characteristics of the object so known, on the one hand, as Searle conceives it, and, on the other hand,  of knowledge conceived as instrument for action, as Polanyi does,  let us reflect on the way signs function when we perceive them as signs. Reflection on the way signs actually function is important for two reasons. (1) Because Polanyi thinks that the objects perceived in ordinary perception function precisely as signs, and (2) because reflection on the way signs actually function is necessary to correct a fundamental mistake avoided by Polanyi. Indeed,  when correctly grasped, that is, when actually perceived as signs, SIGNS FUNCTION AS INSTRUMENTS FOR action, not as signs OF something to be known.

How, then,  do signs actually work? Let us focus on what goes on in our daily experience with traffic lights. We think of the red and green lights as  signs. And we are not wrong, for they are signs. Where we go wrong –not when we perceive the traffic lights, but when we reflect on our perception of them–  is in assuming that signs are primarily signs OF something to be known by the subject who perceives the signs, something of which the subject who perceives the signs is to become aware, and which therefore will be susceptible of being propositionally enunciated by the subject. In fact, however, that is not how signs work, when perceived as signs. ^(F7) For example,  red in the case of a traffic light, is a sign FOR performing the action of stopping at the sight of the red light. Similarly, a green light is a sign FOR performing the action of moving ahead at the sight of the light. Similarly, an arrow pointing in a certain direction on a road is a sign not OF an object of knowledge that can be enunciated; it is a sign FOR performing the action of moving in the direction pointed at. It follows from this that the correct comprehension of signs is manifested not in linguistic enunciations of something signified but in the performance of a certain action, for  what is signified is in every case an action to be performed.  

The preceding examples are examples of artificial signs, that is, of signs that become signs by convention. But artificial signs are, so to say, en extension of natural signs. In other words, we humans create artificial signs because we have the experience of natural signs in that the objects of our ordinary perception sometimes function a signs-For performing  certain actions. If, for instance, when I am about to cross a road, I look on my left and see a car moving in my direction at a certain speed, that is a sign For stopping . The signification here is not a matter of convention; it is natural, for it is related to the satisfaction of our natural desire to survive.

Why do the objects of our ordinary perception function as signs for the performance of an action only sometime and not always? Because for a perceived object to actually function as a sign and signal the performance of a certain action a desire on the subject’s part is required for whose satisfaction the action is relevant as a means or instrument . For instance, the presence of water functions as a sign For drinking it only for an agent who is actually thirsty. And the agent’s action is the instrument through which the satisfaction of the agent’s desire is achieved. Similarly, if I am hungry, the sight of a piece of ham within my reach functions as a sign for eating . Consequently, under ordinary circumstances, I read the sign as perceptual signs are to be read, that is, instrumentally– as the instrument that guides my action in the satisfaction of my desire of eating.  Thus, I move toward the place where I perceive the ham, and take the ham, and put it in my mouth. Similarly, when I see a car moving in my direction at a certain distance and speed, my stopping is an intentional action to achieve my goal of reaching alive the other side of the road. Thus I perceive the car not ‘objectively,’ that is, not independently of my dispositions as an exposed agent; I perceive the car as to be avoided, and my action is instrumental for the realization of  my goal. of surviving. . ^(F8) That is what, without speaking of the objects of our ordinary perception as signs, Aristotle had in mind when, as Nussbaum remarks,  he observed in his treatise on the action of animated creatures that only when desire is present does action follow perception. ^(F9)

And this is what Hume missed when, confusing perception with objective observation, he concluded that one cannot move from “is” to “ought”. NOTE.  

It is at this  point, when the role of desire in connection with action is taken into account, that the reason why certain objects of our ordinary perception , at certain times, function as signs FOR performing certain actions becomes intelligible. Indeed, it is the subject’s desire that reveals the instrumental value of those perceived objects that, because of their physical constitution,  can either themselves satisfy the desire, or function as means for the desire’s satisfaction. If that is the case, though, then signs  mean performatively, not enunciatively. In other words, the signified is not of the order of awareness OF. Therefore cognition is not to terminate in the enunciation of the subject ‘s awareness – for there is no awareness of object or objective awareness – but in the performance of the action signified.

An important consequence of this  is that the perception of an object when it functions as a sign is totally different from the observation of an object, with which it might be confused, as Hume did. Through perceptual observation one becomes aware of the observable characteristics of the object observed. However, precisely because a sign is a sign-FOR, not an object to be known, it points or refers not to itself and its own characteristics, but, so to say, beyond itself. In short, the perception of an object   as a sign does not consist in observing the sign, for signs point beyond themselves. This is what Polanyi was underscoring in his example of the shock-producing syllables. Because signs are neither things to be known themselves –that is, to become aware of– nor signs-OF something else to be known, the subject for whom the object perceived functions as a sign does not thereby gain the awareness of the individual characteristics of the object-sign; therefore, the subject cannot say which are those characteristics. Because, on the other hand, conjoined with his natural desire to avoid the shock, the subject perceives the shock-producing syllables as signs-FOR, in the absence of a better alternative he responds to the signs with the defensive conduct of bracing himself for the shock.

So little are we aware of the objets’ characteristics when we perceive them as signs that, as pointed out by Polanyi, if I am fluent in both English and Spanish, sometimes I cannot remember whether something was said to me in English or in Spanish, for I am not aware of the signs themselves as objects. Which is the very reason why the knowledge involved in the case of signs is tacit , by contrast to enunciative , for it is not meant to terminate in awareness of an object’s characteristics nor, consequently, in words that enunciate those characteristics, but in the action signaled. Or again, as Polanyi remarks, if  when using a musical partition to play a piece in the piano ^(F10) I try to observe the particular characteristics of the notes written on the partition, my playing gets disturbed and ultimately becomes impossible. The reason is that the written notes are signs FOR playing certain notes, not objects to be known or signs OF an object to be known, as perception is traditionally conceived. In other words, the correct perception or grasp of signs as signs is manifested in the production of the action they signal, for signs are signs FOR performing an action, not signs OF something to be known or enunciated, as we tend to assume.*

Now Polanyi does not speak, as I have been doing, of signs or of  instrumental signs, even less does he speak of signs-For and signs-Of.  Instead he uses  such terms as “inhabiting” or “indwelling,” But what he calls inhabiting or indwelling amounts to what I have claimed that we  we do when we perceive signs as signs: we do not observe the sign as such; we glide through it, so to say, toward its signification. Instead of paying attention to the sign itself, we just “inhabit” or “dwell in” it, as “that from which” we move toward the signified.

“The identification of tacit knowing with indwelling involves a shift of  emphasis in our conception of tacit knowing.............. We had envisaged tacit knowing in the first place as a way to know more than we can tell. We identified the two terms of tacit knowing, the proximal and the distal, and recognized the way we attend from the first to the second, thus achieving an integration of  particulars to a coherent entity to which we are attending. Since we were not attending to the particulars in themselves, we could not identify them; but if we now regard the integration of particulars as an interiorization, it takes on a more positive character. It now becomes a means of making certain things function as the proximal terms of tacit     knowing, so that instead of observing them in themselves , we may be aware of them in their bearing on the comprehensive entity which they constitute............. It brings home to us that it is not by looking at things, but by dwelling in them, that we understand their joint meaning... Tacit Dim., pp. l7-l8. ^(F11)

As for the instrumental character of perceptual signs, that  is, their connection with action rather than with  mere awareness, Polanyi has recourse to the word “interiorization.”   

No doubt, Polanyi’s notion of tacit knowledge would have gained much in precision if he had specified that “inhabiting”, “indwelling,” are terms used by him in reference to action, not to objects of awareness. However, the reference to action is implicit in his use of the word “interiorization” to indicate the shift  that takes place in the perceptual experience  from the awareness of something in the subject’s environment to something used by the agent as an instrument. When something is used as an instrument it is, so to say, assimilated to the agent’s body as an extension of it. Hence the word “interiorization.” To return to the example of eyeglasses, when a pair of glasses rests on a table and I look at them, they do not function as eyeglasses but as object known, as object of awareness. I see the eyeglasses over there, in my environment. When I wear the eyeglasses, however, I do not see the eyeglasses, I use them as instruments to find my way toward the place I want to reach. The eyeglasses are now “interiorized” or assimilated to my body, for they now function as an extension of my eyes, which themselves functions as instruments for action, for I do not see my own eyes anymore than I see the eyeglasses. I see  my own eyes only  when I look at them in the mirror, when they function as object known, but not when they  function as the instruments I use to act efficiently in the realization of my projects. Polanyi calls interiorization this shift of an object  from object known in the agent’s environment to its assimilation to the agent’s body as instruments for its action. ^(F12)

Polanyi sees the instrumental rather than objective awareness we have of our own body when engaged in action as the ultimate model of tacit knowledge, for when we act we use our own body as an instrument:

“But our awareness of our body for attending to things outside it suggests a wider generalization of the feeling we have of our body. Whenever we use certain things for attending from them to other things,  in the way in which we always use our own body, these things change their appearance. They appear to us now in terms of the entities to which we are attending from them, just as we feel our body in terms of the things outside to which we are attending from our body. In this sense we can say that when we make a thing function as the proximal term of tacit knowing, w incorporate it in our body or extend our body to include it –so that we come to dwell in it.” ^(F13)

The generalization of the instrumental rather than objective awareness each of us has of his or her own body at the time of action as the basic model of the assimilation to our own body of the objects perceived as instruments for action, which is involved in TK, allows Polanyi to grasp the mechanism of agency. He then finds that mechanism at work in every action. In the first place, in the acquired bodily skills, such as playing the piano or typing, or even those more complex skills such as driving a car.  These skills are fundamentally similar to the way a blind person ‘drives’ his own body with the  help of a stick. They are acquired as a result of along practice of interiorization through practice; thanks to repetition, the agent transforms the stick, the typewriter, the piano, the car into extensions of his own body by using them as he uses his own body. At the end of that long practice of interiorization the use of those objects becomes as natural to the agent as the use of his own body in, say, the action of walking. When a child learns to walk, he is guided by the sensations he experiences on the plants of his feet every time he makes a step, but the child does not focus his attention on them, he does not observe his  sensations, they are only “that from which ” he makes the subsequent steps.

Language, concepts, and the return to experience in the practice of science and in action

For Searle, the primary and essential function of language is to produce an illocutionary effect, to inform, to makes something known by enunciating it, not to produce perlocutionary effects. Moreover, because of the primacy he attributes to illocutionary speech, Searle ipso facto subordinates to the knowledge of what is enunciated all the performative speech acts as to their necessary condition. As a result of this separation of the illocutionary and the perlocutionary effects of language, the intrinsic connection between knowledge and action is lost on Searle.

However, Searle is far from being the only philosopher to miss the connection between language and action.  As I have mentioned it, in this regard he is a member of a long philosophical tradition that originates with Plato and Aristotle. But how could the fundamentally performative function of  language have escaped so many intelligent people, and how could their own representational view of language appear so persuasive to them and to so many others? The reason, I think, is – at least in part –  the unique way in which language means. Tough its uniqueness does not reside, as claimed by Searle, in the fact that language, unlike all other signs, says or enunciates what it means as something to be understood rather than acted upon , Searle’s mistake is nonetheless understandable because linguistic signs (1) do not always function as signs FOR, and (2) even when they are meant to function as signs- FOR their connection with action is not always temporally immediate, as is the case with natural, non-linguistic signs.

Let me explain. We already know that the objects of ordinary perception at least sometimes function as signs-For, but that is the case (1) only when a subject’s desire reveals their  instrumentality for the satisfaction of the desire by indicating or signaling the action required,, and (2) only when the subject is actually capable of performing the required (signaled) action to satisfy the desire. These are the two conditions for an object of ordinary perception to function as a sign-FOR, and any of the two conditions, or the two of them, may fail to obtain, in which case the perceived object will not function as a sign-For. Another reason that philosophers miss the connection of a sign with the performance of an action is that the reflective analysis is usually for purposes of proving or demonstrating a truth – an attempt that is already tainted with contemplative objectivism.

But what does language, and the lack thereof in animals, have to do with this? To answer this question we have to take the water from above and say something about the action of animals and people.

 Certain reflexes are not perceptual but sensual –“sensual” refers to sensation by contrast to perception of objet. The sensation of being burnt causes withdrawal. Lacking that sensation, lepers who are smoking don’t throw away the cigarette they have in their hands, and get burnt. Here we are in the primitive realm of pure reflex, of inherited, pre-programmed, non-adaptive action. In this case, the response is automatic because it is not conceived by the subject as instrumental for the satisfaction of the desire to avoid pain , but caused by the sensation of pain. That is why the response is automatic and always the same.  Notice that here it is a matter of sensation, which is objectless, not of perception.

Regarding perceptual reflexes:

In the case of the lower non-locomotive animals, the sensed presence of a naturally desired object –the “satisfactor”–  within the reach of the animal  automatically lashes the action that satisfies the need. The connection of sensation with action is inherited, biologically programmed.

In the case of the lower locomotive animals , the perceptual presence of a desired object within the reach of the animal automatically lashes the action. Notice: within the reach of the animal. So it is not simply a matter of the presence of the objet, but of a practical relationship of the subject’s practical powers to the object. Thus, even at this primitive level, it is a matter of a situational relationship of the subject to the object –and not only in terms of desire or need but of situational opportunity and of ability. This is what is lost sight of  in the contemplative objectivism  that pays attention exclusively to the “content” of the consciousness or of the statement. This contemplative objectivism has its roots in the conception of reality as composed of real or actually existing objects (be they intelligible or sensible) that correspond to our awareness of them.   For instance, the toad’s tongue shoots toward the prey, as soon as the prey moves. Since the object desired is immediately present, it directs causally , by instinct,  the action toward itself. But, as the situation counts for the success of the action, the experienced toad, being locomotive,  either instinctively looks for the best position to catch its prey (for instance, being closer to its prey in order to aim better)– this is the case of inherited physiological reflexes– or thanks to experience progressively improves its general position by visiting the places where its preys are, say,  more abundant. The latter action is already adaptive, not purely reflex; however, it is not invented by the animal but dictated by the biological program of its species by means of association.

In the case of the higher mammals , there is an increased variety of actions thanks to a larger repertoire of natural desires, to a more retentive and associative  memory, and to a larger variety of physical abilities. (Some of the desires are relational, that is, involve other members of the species and their desires and abilities, for which the action of the animal has to be instrumental, but always according to an inherited program). The animal’s action is less and less immediately dependent on the perceptual presence of the object, because thanks to experience and association it can come to ‘desire’ those actions that are means to satisfy its needs and that are less and less immediately related to the presence of the satisfactor.

In the case of people , all of the above obtains, but multiplied and elevated to a higher level by the linguistic ability of people, which allows them to ‘map’ their actions in the absence of the satisfactor because they grasp the satisfactor’s characteristic way of acting, and even to grasp the characteristic way of acting of objects that are not immediate satisfactors but means for satisfaction.

So, what precisely has the human  linguistic ability to do with perceived objects functioning or not functioning as signs-FOR, and what has it to do with the difference between human beings ^(F14)and even the higher mammals? It has to do with the less and less immediate presence   of the satisfactor.

Let us limit ourselves, at least initially, to linguistic expressions that refer to particular perceptual objects in the actual presence of those objects. In that connection, Martha Nussbaum remarks a difference between animals and people in their perceptual experience. Animals always act immediately when they perceive an object capable of satisfying their most pressing desire at the time, whereas people may delay their action because the object may not signal the action required, and also because they can create  the circumstances that are appropriate for the realization of their project. ^(F15) For example, a hungry dog will automatically attempt to eat the piece of meat it perceives within its reach, unless the dog has at the time a more pressing desire. For the dog the perceived piece of meat automatically  functions as a sign FOR immediately performing the action of eating. By contrast, people do not always act in the presence of a desired object. In other words, their action is not immediately and passively tied to their perception. I believe that the reason for this difference is that we people are genuinely linguistic creatures; consequently for us perceived objects –even objects related to our desires and needs–  are not directly and immediately connected to the motor system, as they are in the case of animals, but are mediated by language. Perceived objects, in other words, in the case of people, are signs-For the performance of an action, not directly but only through the intermediary of a conception of the object –that is, of the object’s action , and this mediation of the subject’s action  by conception is characteristic of the way in which properly  linguistic signs function. In other words,  thanks to language, the immediate connection with action of an object of natural desire with the perceptual presence of the object ceases to obtain, because language mediates the encounter with objects of desire with a conception of those objects. This space between object and action thanks to conception is what the acquisition of language ultimately revealed to Helen Keller and made possible for her. Having been referred to water through the name w-a-t-e-r, she realized that all the objects she experienced had a name by which she could refer to them in the different ways to be indicated in a moment, regardless of the perceptual presence or absence of the object.

This important point demands illustration. For a dog, which is not a linguistic creature, the perception of the sound of a certain ring at the door, or of a certain smell, or of the sound of the name of its master are equivalent signs FOR responding to the imminent and desired presence of its master; so the dog infallibly runs to the door, wagging its tail, to meet its master. This is similar to the salivating of Pavlov’s dog in response to the sound of the bell – an acquired rather than inherited reflex, connected with the natural need to eat. For me, who am not an animal, a certain  sound of the doorbell  may well be sometimes the sign of my wife’s arrival, in which case I’ll go and open the door. In that case the sound of  my wife’s name functions for me as  the sound of the doorbell functions for a dog or for a young child. But not exactly, for in many circumstances the mention of her name functions as a sign, not For an action in response to her immediate presence, but as a sign for “thinking of her, ” * for holding her in thought rather than in presence or referring to her imminent perceptual presence. Language opens a distance between the subject and the object, a distance that allows the subject to treat the object as object of thought, be the object present or not.

“Thinking of “ her is synonymous with “conceiving” her. But there are different ways of thinking of something: * THINKING OF HER  - In her perceptual presence, that is, in situation of action and interaction, or in her absence - Concretely (imaginarily) or abstractly (conceptually, explanatorily) - Instrumentally, in relation to one’s desires and action ( ex dispositione agentis ) , or non-instrumentally ( ex his quae sunt propria rei ). The object perceived is sometimes sign-For immediate action, when there is no time to think. But sometimes there is time to think deliberatively about the way one will act. And, when there is time to think, the object perceived does not determine how to think of it, whether in terms of action or ex his quae sunt propria rei. For animals by contrast, because they a re not linguistic, because their action is not mediated by a conception of the object nasmed,  perceived objects are always signs- For action and for immediate action.

These are at least some of  the most important possibilities that Helen Keller discovered progressively when she discovered language. The sensation ‘w-a-t-e-r’ telegraphed to her by her teacher was, she discovered, a name of the object, a way of being referred and referring others to the object named in its absence and as objet of thought , and not  necessarily, as the perceptual object  itself is, a sign FOR responding to the object-water in relation to her dispositions, that is, her desires, as when she felt her hat being put on her head as a sign FOR going out. T herefore she did not brace herself for the presence of water. Rather, she realized that at least in certain cases, the name was a sign of water as something  to be conceived or thought of and talked about. In short, the name, in this case, was not a proxy for the perceptual thing in terms of her desires and action –that is, deliberatively–  but for conceiving the thing. Thus one of the ways in which she could have responded to the name was  by asking, “What about water?” If so, she had conceived water not in immediate relation to her dispositions or interests –what the actually perceived water could do to her or for her–  but what water, as itself  an agent capable of producing effects, could  do in general – the variety of effects it invariably or systematically  produced (and, therefore, was capable of producing) on the objects exposed to its action, independently of their dispositions and projects. This is the way a student of chemistry thinks of water in relation to other natural chemical substances.

In this example, for Keller, the name “water” functioned as a sign for conceiving the perceptual object. That this conceived object, which  could be enriched by other predicates resulting from future observation of its activity, was a concept rather than the particular existent thing named, shows in the abstraction from distances of exposure as revealed by the indexicality of perception. Not only the instrumentality of water (its relevance for possible projects of desirous subjects in the presence of water had disappeared from Keller’s consideration of water), but its very indexicality had disappeared. No distances of any kind unfolded from water (as conceived by Keller) to anything else, because water was – provisionally, until actually perceived with distances of exposure–  a mere concept-water, not a thing actually situated in a particular place within the world.

Now, was not this water-concept, this abstract subject of enunciative predications that constitute the concept-water, a signified by the word learned by Keller? Yes, signified as connoted or forming part of the concept of the object, but not as denoted or referring to the presence or imminent presence of the concrete thing named.

NAMES are particularly important words in that they name or refer primarily, though not exclusively, to concrete things sensible things. However, names function as names only when they are actually used, and typically they are used not isolated from other words but as parts of a sentence uttered by a concrete speaker in concrete circumstances, and all these factors contribute to determine the actual meaning of the sentence as a speech act and of the name in the actual speech act.

The range of cases I want to consider here because of the role of perceptual presence in relation to action are the cases in which a name is used orally by a speaker to say something to somebody present, that is, to somebody who by the fact of being present to the speaker shares with the speaker the same perceptual situation. ^(F16) Suppose I say to somebody in that situation, “The hammer is in the next room.”  If I am a competent user of English, I say that only to somebody who is looking for the hammer, not just to anybody at random. And because the addressee desires the hammer, I expect him to respond to my announcement by moving to the next room to get hold of the hammer. In other words, my use of the name “hammer” in that particular sentence and situation will function as a sign-For that will guide my interlocutor to get hold of the absent hammer, much as the hammer itself,  if it were actually present and perceived by him, would function as a sign-For that would guide him in his action of getting hold of the hammer he desires for whatever purpose.

Let us analyze this example a little more. In the absence of the hammer and in the appropriate sentence, in this sentence and in these circumstances the name “hammer” functions as a proxy for the concrete absent thing whose name it is. The name is a proxy for the concrete thing named, for, like the thing named, when it is present, the sentence that contains the name guides the subject to the satisfaction of his desire and makes possible its satisfaction.. Thus, not only does a  name used in the appropriate sentences and circumstances function as proxy for the object named in the absence of t he object, which when present  itself functions as a sign-For that guides a desirous subject to the satisfaction of his desire, but also the name functions as a sign-For that guides the subject to reach the presence of the object. All this, however, because of their linguistic inability, is beyond the ken of even the higher mammals. Indeed, much as I would like to, I cannot linguistically guide my dog to satisfy its desire by telling it that the bone it desires is in the garden. ^(F17) And notice that the example I have used to illustrate the function of linguistic expressions in which names appear was an extremely simple one. No wonder then that even the higher mammals have no access to the higher  possibilities opened by, say, scientific language.

Compare to the analysis of this simple case Aristotle’s famous analysis of sentences in which names appear, from which his notion of “the categories” originated. Names are, for him, the truly important linguistic elements in sentences. But their importance has nothing to do with a subject’s action or the instrumentality of the objects named to satisfy a desire of the subject . Aristotle considers them important  because they refer to “ substances ,” such as water or wood, and substances are the most important realities to be known because they are the only self-subsistent realities or “beings.”  All the other realities or aspects of realities referred to by other words than names are not self-subsistent; they are, rather, subordinated to substances, in that they are “ accidents ” or properties of substances.  Notice, by the way, that in Aristotle’s taxonomy action is only an accident of a substance and no more important than any of the other accidents. Now, these properties may be either essentially –  that is, necessarily with conceptual necessity– attached to the particular substance of which t hey are accidents, or only accidentally, that is incidentally. In neither case , however, can the properties subsist by themselves separated from the substance of which they are properties.

Aristotle’s views on the categories are too well known to need to be repeated here in detail. And, anyway, that is not required for my purpose, which is simply to point out why Aristotle’s analysis of the way names mean was bound to be radically different from my own..

On just one point  would Aristotle agree with me concerning the advantages that we humans, as linguistic creatures, have over animals. Indeed, in his analysis, no less than I in mine, he is taking advantage of the fact that language opens the necessary distance between the subject and the objects named – the distance that makes possible to conceive or think of them, regardless of their actual perceptual presence or absence. Lacking the linguistic ability, no animal would have been able to create anything even remotely similar to Aristotle’s analysis.

Yet it is obvious that Aristotle’s analysis is radically different from mine. Why? Above all, because , compared to mine, Aristotle’s analysis is totally abstract, inspired by his contemplative objectivism, by his interest in the object of contemplative or speculative knowledge and its validity. It is a reflection on a possible model-sentence which is uttered by no body, in no concrete perceptual situation in which a name of an actually perceived object is used by the speaker to refer himself,  or to refer another subject  who shares the same perceptual situation, to a concrete object. And this model sentence is supposed to represent what is the case, at least symbolically. One can hardly expect that such abstract analysis will yield the way in which names function when they are actually used by desirous agents to name concrete sensible objects in concrete circumstances. Had Aristotle followed my concrete path in his analysis, I claim that he would have reached very similar results to mine. ^(F18)

Let me elaborate on this important point. Words signify only when actually used by someone to mean something. But that is not how words function in the dictionary, for in the dictionary they are stored but not actually used. The dictionary defines and exemplifies the conventional meaning of words as words, that is, the meaning of the conventional signs that words are. Out of any context, the sound ‘w-a-t-e-r’, or the written word in the dictionary, may evoke in me, who am familiar with the English language, certain images and predicates related to the perceptual object we English speakers call water, because I am used to associate the name with the perceived object. However, the word in the dictionary  is not a linguistic sign of the predicates, for it is not actually used and meant to signify any of them in particular ( connotation ), nor to refer a person to the imminent presence of the perceptual object denoted by the name.  When I think of the word ‘water’ (as I am doing now) I am not using it or uttering it as a word to refer to anything in particular and, therefore, it does not mean or signify or refer to anything. The same with the word as it is in the dictionary.

It is from the relationship of the things denoted to the needs or desires of people or of other living creatures that names become meaningful for them through association with the presence of the named . In other words, t hrough association of the name’s sound with the presence of the object named, which happens to be relevant to the satisfaction of needs, names become proxy for, or denote, the imminent presence of the desired object named and therefore signs For action to satisfy the desire, if such action is actually signaled.

In the case of animals, that is all. Names simply denote the imminent presence of the desired object named. That is what the denotation of names consists in. This is how the name of its master becomes a potential sign-For, for a dog. But, as already explained,   for us people, names both MAY denote the presence of the object named and MAY connote a concept of the object named, and the concept may or may not be independent of the subject’s desires and of the object’s presence, depending on the meaning of the sentence in which the name appears.

Here we have to be careful not to confuse the connotation of a word as a word, which is identical with its conventional meaning in the language, with its particular connotation when actually used connotationally. In the latter case, the words that are names of perceptual objects are proxy for the concept of their respective objects as subject of predicates. In this sense, they still refer to their objects, but only indirectly via the concept, and they refer only in this particular connotational  sense, not denotationally. However, though not denotationally and only indirectly, names still do refer to their perceptual objects. This is manifested by the fact that the only legitimate or valid way to enrich the concept of a perceptual object with new predicates is actual observation of its action or actual experience of its effects through interaction with it. Were it not for this indirect reference to the perceptual objet, the return to observational experience of the object either to control the validity of the  predicates attached to the concept of the object or to actually use the object as an instrument to achieve a goal would make no sense. Since words do not mean or refer but when actually used to refer, the conventional meaning of words as defined in the dictionary is in itself linguistically meaningless, for the meaning of names amounts to their referring to the real object in the case of denotation  or to the concept of the object in the case of connotation. Now,  names or substantives may evoke the objects of which they are names but, as such, do not refer to them either denotationally or connotationally.

The uniquely human linguistic capacity consists in the ability to use NAMES, which are conventional signs, both connotationally and denotationally by including them in complete sentences. Animals, by contrast, perceive what they perceive (that is, see or hear) as signs-For, but only as signs-For, that is, directly related to their present natural desires or “dispositions” or interests.

Now, the importance of our ability to use NAMES connotationally is that, among other things , it   makes possible for us the practice what we call “science,” with all its huge benefits. Plato speaks of the silent language through which the thinker communicates with himself  when he thinks . He is referring to the connotational use of language, which allows him and us to refer indirectly to real things through their concepts, and thus to enrich our notions of the objects in our surrounding world independently of our immediate desires to whose satisfaction they may be useful. Animals cannot do that. This is the sense in which “knowledge” is disinterested –though only provisionally–  as strongly underscored by Plato and Aristotle. They failed to realize, however, that when the objects thought about are perceptual, that is, when they are concrete objects CAPABLE OF BEING NAMED, there is, through their concepts, an indirect reference to them as they are for us in perceptual observation and in interactive experience with them. Not only can their concepts be enriched only through perceptual observation or interactive experience with them, and communicated to others linguistically, but the ultimate return to experience is applicative –not “ ex his quae sount propria rei ,” but “ ex dispositione agentis, ” for it is return to the world in which we are engaged as exposed agents. In other words, the return is, at least sometimes, of the order of interested action to satisfy our needs and desires, though in other cases it is provisionally theoretical – to enrich the concept, to form an explanatory theory, to test a theory, to verify a law. Think of physics as theoretical grasp of the action of physical objects, based in perceptual observation, and of engineering as interested application either through solving problems on paper (planning action) or in reality. Think of the practical origin of geometry and of the instrumental value of the linguistic theorems’ demonstrations.

Searle and Polanyi on language as a system of signs: Signs-OF or signs-FOR?

The above may help us to grasp the radical difference between Searle’s and Polanyi’s respective conceptions of language. Both of them think that a language functions as a system of signs. But of signs OF object of knowledge, or of signs FOR action? Their respective answers to this all-important question differ.

Let us consider Searle’s answer first. Searle thinks that language is primarily a system of signs OF. This is reflected in his entire view of language. Indeed, that is  the reason why he attributes to the illocutionary effect, which consists in knowledge OF object, absolute primacy over, and independence from, the perlocutionary effects, which consist in action. As Searle explains, the uniqueness of language, and its superiority over all other signs, is not that language means BECAUSE IT IS A SYSTEM OF SIGNS, for language has that in common with all signs, but that it says what it means. That is, language is not only a sign OF, but it enunciates what it is a sign OF.

In this connection, remember Searle’s analysis of the difference between language and games.: a certain move in the game of chess means checkmate in the sense of automatically excluding from the game the checkmated person and of terminating the game with the triumph of the player who checkmated him. However,  unlike the linguistic enunciation, the move in the game does not say or enunciate or express in words what it means. Saying, then, in Searle’s view, is making known through words that express or enunciate what is to be known. The primary function of language is to inform or make known. Consequently, not only does Searle privilege the enunciative linguistic form because it suposedly is the only purely informative linguistic form, but he subordinates the speech acts that have perlocutionary force to the illocutionary ones –in that in order to have my hearer do what I want him to do I have first to inform him of what I want him to do. In so doing Searle he implicitly assimilates the speech acts that have  perlocutionary force to illocutionary ones, for he hears them as enunciative of something to be known. Indeed, according to Searle, making  known the perlocutionary effects one wants to obtain is a necessary condition for obtaining those effects.

As a striking example of Searle’s subordinating of all the other speech acts to the enunciative ones let me mention his denial that the American soldier performed a linguistic action even if he obtained the perlocutionary effect he intended  by uttering the sounds “ Ich komme von dem Land wo die Zitronen blühen. ,” The reason Searle does not consider linguistic the Italians’ action is that the soldier did not say or enunciate “I am an American soldier,” which is what he intended to communicate. ^(F19) In this case, therefore, the action intended by the American speaker obtained in a non-linguistic way, not as a result fo what was said by the speaker.

But the most clear indication of Searle’s privileging of the enunciative linguistic form shows in  what he calls “the principle of expressibility,” to wit: that anything that can be meant can – and, ideally, should– be expressed  in words that actually say or enunciate what one wants to make known, that is, have replicated in the mind of the hearer. ^(F20)This demand of expressing in words what one wants to make known makes sense only on the assumption that language is primarily a system of signs OF something to be known, rather than a system of signs For performing certain actions.

It is important, however, to remark that Searle treats the enunciative linguistic form, to which he subordinates all other speech acts, as if it itself were a speech act. But it is not. Speech acts function as such only when meant a such by a particular speaker to a particular hearer or hearers in particular circumstances. But enunciating in general or saying in general, which is what Searle takes for the primary function of language, does not function as a speech act. Of course nothing can be said without saying something in particular. But what Searle calls “saying” is nothing else than the customary meaning of words by themselves –the very customary meaning he castigates Grice for confusing with what is meant in actual speech acts. ^(F21) Now, as Searle himself observes, words mean only when used in concrete speech acts. In short, Searle’s entire conception of the primary function of language as a system of signs that say or enunciate what they mean is grounded in the confusion of the enunciative linguistic form with the speech act of saying something to somebody in particular circumstances!

For Polanyi, by contrast, all speech acts, enunciative speech acts included, are meant to function, not as  signs OF the characteristics of an object to be known, but as signs FOR performing an action, that is, as instruments that guide the desirous  agent in the performance of the action that satisfies his desire. And this applies as much to statements as to all other speech acts. Actually, the only difference between statements and the other speech acts is not that, as Searle assumes, statements are signs OF something to be known rather than signs FOR an action to be performed, but that statements do not specify the action meant by the speaker but leave its determination to the hearer as a linguistic agent according to his desires or needs as well as the circumstances in which the action is to be performed, much as it happens in the case of human perception.*

*As to why this difference, the reason is that linguistic agents, as such, are capable, because they are linguistic agents, of applying circumstantially (not automatically, as animals do), since the actual meaning of genuinely linguistic signs depends at least in part on the  circumstances. Their actions are linguistic, and words do not cause actions but are signs for actions conceived and meant by the subject who says or hears the words as signs depending on his present desires and on the circumstances.

The typewriter, the piano, the car are examples of material instruments invented by human agents to multiply the efficiency of their agency and extend its reach in order to satisfy first their elementary needs and then the desires that emerge as the transformation of the surrounding world allows the emergence of such desires. However, in addition to the invention of material instruments is the invention of symbolic (that is, significant)  instruments , among which the most important are spoken and written language, because they make possible the invention of all other symbolic instruments . such as mathematical symbols, scientific theories, etc.

After the case of acquired bodily skills as examples of TK, Polanyi refers us to the use of   

maps by an agent who, with their help, attempts to find his way in an unfamiliar territory. Before analyzing that case, let us remark that the reading and actual use of maps are far more complex operations than they seem to be for those who have already mastered them. Actually, a child, even

a child who has already acquired the skill of reading written language, is not thereby automatically capable of using a map to find his way in unfamiliar territory, much less of drawing a map to be used by others. The reason for this difficulty is that a map is not a picture of a territory, any more than a name is a picture of the object named. A map is a symbolic sign of a territory, but not of a territory to be contemplated or observed but of a territory to be walked through. In other words, not only is a map to be deciphered or ‘read,’ as all symbols are, but to be deciphered in relation to the action of moving through the territory to which the map refers, in order to reach a particular place within that territory. In other words, a map is a symbolic instrument to be used by an agent as an instrument to achieve his goal. That is why, if somebody claimed to understand a map but proved himself incapable of using it in practice, he wouldn’t know what he is saying. Indeed, since the map is an instrument for action, understanding it amounts to being able to manifest that understanding in one’s action. The mistake of the person claiming to understand a map but being incapable of actually finding it to find his way would be comparable to the one Searle makes when he claims that a person who is given an order understands it if only he grasps objectively the meaning of the words, without realizing that an order is to be obeyed, not to be understood objectively. In short, the genuine understanding of a map, as in the cae of any other instument, is to be manifested in the conduct that the map is meant to render possible, that is, in the conduct of reaching the desired place within the territory to which the map refers, much as the genuine understanding of a verbal joke is to be manifested in the affection of being actually amused by it.

Polanyi’s view of the reading of maps of the kind we are referring to goes as follows. The agent who reads the map must “inhabit” the map as he inhabits his body when he acts to realize a project, for instance, when he is nailing a nail to hang a picture. In other words, he must make the figure on the map function not as an object to be looked at and observed in itself, but as “that from which” he must act in order to arrive at the desired place. In short,  TK is always practical, for it involves the use of an instrument by an agent to achieve an intended goal, not a representation of object.

A more striking example of a symbolic instrument mentioned by Polanyi is the case of scientific theories. Used, as we are, to think of knowledge as a visual or intellectual representation of the mater theorized about, we will be surprised by the idea that scientific theories, like maps for action, function instrumentally rather than representationally. In other words, we tend to

consider the statement in which, for instance, a physical law is expressed as a symbolic representation of the intelligible ‘nature’ of the particular phenomenon to which the law refers. For his part, Polanyi argues that the statements of scientific laws are meant to be used by the scientific agent as instruments to guide his conduct in the solution of concrete problems related to the portion of the world to which the law refers, much as a map is meant to functions as an instrument for the person who uses it to find his way within the mapped territory. . In the following text,  Polanyi explains how theories function in the actual practice of science, that is, when the theoretical instrument is actually used rather than talked about:

“To rely on a theory for understanding nature is to interiorize it. For we are attending from the theory to things seen in its light, and we are aware  of the theory, while thus using it, in terms of the spectacle that it serves to explain. This is why mathematical theory can be learned only by practicing its application: its true knowledge lies in our ability to use it.” ^(F22)

This text, quoted  in isolation from its context, may be too dense and too cryptic to make us understand how, in Polanyi’s view, scientific theories function. What is clear is that Polanyi’s view c7ontradicts the prevailing conception of science as a an exact, or at least an increasingly approximative, representational  view of reality. Thomas Kuhn, who on this fundamental point  repeatedly indicates his agreement with Polanyi, expressed it forcefully:

“A scientific theory is usually felt to be better than its predecessors not only in the sense that it is a better instrument for discovering and solving  puzzles but also because it is somehow a better representation of what nature is really like. One often hears that successive theories grow ever closer to, or approximate more and more closely to, the truth. Apparently generalizations like that refer not to the puzzle solutions and concrete predictions derived from a theory but rather to its ontology, to the match, that is, between the entities with which the theory populates nature and what is ‘really there.’ Perhaps there is some other way of salvaging the notion of ‘truth’ for application to whole theories, but this one will not do.... the notion of a match between the ontology of a theory and its ‘real’ counter part in nature now seems to me illusive in principle” ^(F23)

What Kuhn is pointing out here is that the statements in which scientific theories are expressed are not meant to function as representations of reality but as symbolic instruments to be used by agents to solve  problems that the surrounding world raises. In this regard, though, scientific statements constitute no exception. Indeed, if Polanyi is right, no statement –whether scientific or not– is  meant to function as a representation of its referent; rather, all statement are meant to function as  instruments for action, not as representations.

Searle and Polanyi on language

We are now well equipped to determine which of the two authors whose views on language we have studied is right. According to Searle, by replicating in his own mind what the speaker intends to communicate, thanks to his comprehension of what is said, the hearer gains the knowledge of what the speaker means or wants to communicate. Thus, according to Searle,  at least in the case of linguistic communication, knowledge consists in the correspondence between what is meant and said by the speaker and what is understood by the hearer. This, needless to say, coincides with the ordinary notion  of knowledge, which the Scholastics, following Aristotle, defined as adaequatio mentis ad rem : the conformation of the knower’s mind to the object. The only difference is that here Searle extends the same conception from the order of extramental reality to which it originally was applied to the order of linguistic communication. Now it is precisely regarding  this conception of knowledge that Polanyi parts ways with Searle.  

For Polanyi knowing is not a matter of becoming aware of the object to be known by acquiring a sort of reproduction of it that corresponds to the object, and which is true in the measure in which it corresponds to the object. Rather, for Polanyi, knowledge is tacit because it does not aim at reproducing –nor does it reproduce– the object in the mind by enunciating its characteristics, in which case the knowledge of the object would be explicit rather than tacit. Knowledge consists, rather,  in the practical  disposition to ACT in the way appropriate to achieve the knower’s goal, and in actually executing that disposition at the appropriate time. Thus, in the case of the shock-producing syllables, the subject has no awareness of the individual characteristics of the syllables; for that very reason the subject cannot say or express in words which are those characteristics of which he has no visual awareness. What the subject acquires, thanks to his past painful experience of being shocked, is not a faithful  image of the syllables. Rather, given his natural needs, the subject has a natural disposition to avoid pain and, hence, to perceive what produces pain not as object of ‘knowledge,’ in its particular characteristics, but as to be avoided through action. This  practical disposition to avoid the shock manifests itself in his bracing himself for the shock when the shock-producing syllables appear again on the screen, in order to protect himself as much as possible from the shock.

Whose conception of the relationship of knowledge to action is right: Searle’s or Polanayi’s?

Regarding those acts in which a perlocutionary effect is intended by the speaker, I have already shown how unpersuasive I find Searle’s view that in every act of communication the speaker intends to produce the knowledge of his communicative intentions –that is, the illocutionary effect–  and that this knowledge suffices to insure the success of the communicative act. For this means, concretely, that when I give you an order, what I intend primarily is that you understand and thereby be informed or acquire factual knowledge that I give you an order, not that you obey my order. Searle is so convinced that in the case of an order what the speaker intends is that his order be understood rather than obeyed that he considers the communicative exchange successful in the case in which the order is not obeyed, as long as the order is understood by the hearer.

Why do I think that Searle is wrong on this point? Because this aspect of Searle’s characterization of linguistic exchanges is  at odds with the practice of the competent linguistic agents. It is true that I cannot disobey an order without understanding it and thereby knowing it. But what is in contention is what constitutes the appropriate knowledge or understanding of an order and, in general, of all those linguistic acts that possess perlocutionary force, such as threats, warnings, promises, demands, etc. According to Searle, the primary intention of the speaker in all of the them is to inform the hearer of what he means, and only secondarily and accidentally to achieve the perlocutionary effect typical of the particular perlocutionary act, such as obeying it in the case of an order, expecting the fulfillment of the promise in the case of promising, etc.

But the linguistic facts contradict Searle’s opinion. If  Searle were right, there would be no difference in actual use between the grammatical imperative, used in the case of orders, and the grammatical indicative, properly used only when one wants to inform of an explanatory fact of a perceptual phenomenon, or of an object of desire in its absence. Furthermore, the imperative mood  would be totally superfluous,  for imperatives should be understood as  indicatives. Indeed,  if Searle were right, giving an order (in the imperative) would be exactly equivalent to saying or enunciating (in the indicative) that one gives an order. But such is not the case. As I have already argued, when I give an order, I do not say – in the sense of enunciating– something to be merely understood, to wit: that I give an order; rather, I say the order I give in the sense of giving an order to be obeyed . When I say something in the sense of enunciating it, I refer my hearer to what I enunciate as something to be understood, that is, I intend to produce in my interlocutor the knowledge in which, according to Searle, the illocutionary effect consists. Therefore, if in giving an order I were enunciating that I give an order and the order I give, I should be satisfied if the addressee responded  “I understand your order.” But that is not the case. When I give an order –and this is why I use the imperative, not the indicative–  I expect and intend my order to be obeyed, which is the appropriate ‘understanding’ of an order by a competent linguistic agent.

Moreover, Searle’s opinion is also at odds with the practice of linguistic agents not only when what he calls ”perlocutionary effects,” but also in the case of statements, in which only an illocutionary effect seems to be intended.. For, as I have already argued, contrary to what Searle thinks, in a real-life situations, by contrast to the artificial classroom situation in which people are learning a second language, linguistic agents do not consider their linguistic action successful if a purely theoretical  understanding of what is said obtains; success, for linguistic agents, is identical with linguistic action, that is, with action that results by virtu of what is said. Why does Searle identify saying with enunciating?  Because his linguistic characterizations and, hence, his entire conception of language,  is based not on his own experience as a linguistic agent who is actually participating in communicative interactions, but in his reflective experience as as a curious philosopher who overhears the words said by those who participate in communicative interactions, or as a curious philosopher who reflects after the fact on the words said by himself and others when he and they participated in communicative interactions. I underline “the words said,” to indicate that Searle’s characterization of language, in spite of his disclaimers, focuses on the words said, independently of the conduct and the intentions or dispositions of those who say or hear them as actual participants in linguistic exchanges. This amounts to identifying linguistic meaning with saying in the sense of enunciating something, and, consequently, it amounts to considering the conduct of the linguistic agents as accidental to linguistic meaning, rather than integral to, or constitutive of, linguistic meaning. Clearly, however, this is not the kind of interiorization through agency through which children learn to play baseball, without being able to enunciate the rules they supposedly follow.

Thus Searle excludes from linguistic meaning anything that in a linguistic exchange is not  words in which something is said or enunciated. Such is the case with  the actions and affections intended by the speaker in the communicative exchange, which consequently are excluded by Searle from being constitutive elements of linguistic meaning. That, indeed, was the reason why, in his example of the American soldier captured by the Italians, Searle explicitly denied that the action by which the American tricked the Italians into believing that he was a German soldier by uttering the sounds “ Ich komme von dem Land wo die Zitronen  blühen ” was a linguistic action, since the words expressed did not enunciate the action intended.

The exclusion from linguistic meaning of whatever is not explicitly said in words is reflected in the separation Searle postulates between the illocutionary and the perlocutionary effects, as well as in the secondary and merely accidental role he attributes to the latter. Searle himself , in explaining his principle of expressibility that  “whatever can be meant can be said,” implicitly claims that whatever is not said in words cannot be meant, for he implicitly  excludes from meaning the perlocutionary effects. ^(F24)

In short, because he identifies linguistic meaning with what is expressed in words that supposedly say or enunciate something to be understood or known, Searle automatically excludes from linguistic meaning those no less linguistic effects that do not consist in words that supposedly enunciate something to be understood, namely, the perlocutionary effects. No wonder, then, that  the way in which language functions in the artificial context of a course of a language for foreigners, in which only understanding  what is said counts as linguistic, to the systematic exclusion of any of the affections or actions intended by the speaker, ends up appearing as the model linguistic situation for Searle. Indeed it corresponds to Searle’s defective philosophy of language.

Now, to understand the differences between Searle and Polanyi, we have to realize that Searle’s linguistic philosophy amounts to an application to the field of language of the Platonic-Aristotelian epistemology inherited by the Western philosophical tradition. In that tradition,  knowledge consists, on the subject’s part,  in the representational conformity of his mind to reality, and, on the object’s part, in the ontological correspondence or conformity to what is in the subject’s mind. By contrast,  Polanyi’s conception of tacit knowledge constitutes an alternative to that tradition, for tacit knowledge is to be used instrumentally by the agent to achieve a desired goal, not a representation of what is the case.

As we shall now see, Polanyi does not contend that the Searlean perlocutionary effects as Searle conceives them, that is, as separated from the illocutionary effect, are a constitutive element of linguistic meaning, for the Serlean conception of the illocutionary element as different from the perlocutionary presupposes the legitimacy of his conceptual distinction between them. Polanyi contends, rather, that this Serlean distinction reflects the separation between the knowing subject and the agent that is inherent to the epistemology inherited from Aristotle and Plato, which begins by conceiving knowledge as different from and separable form action. The Searlean distinction between illocutionary and perlocutionary effects is possible only on the wrong assumption , common to Aristotle and Plato, that knowledge and action stand to each other in the relationship presupposed by their own epistemology.

De là la separation etablie par Searle entre, d’une part,  la connaissance, qui est le seul effet illocutionnaire et qui est independant de l’action et, d’autre part,  l’action, qui fait partie des effets perlocutionnaires est est subordonnée a la connaissance. Or, etant donne que, d’apres Searle, la realisation des effets perlocutionnaires n’est pas essentielle pour la reussite de l’action linguistique en tant que telle, il s’en suit que dans la conception de Searle il n’y a pas de place pour l’agent et la veritable agence.

Ce que je viens de dire peut sembler surprenant, puisque l’ecole linguistique des “s peech acts ” ou de l’action linguistique, a laquelle appartient Searle, est censée se distinguer precisement parce qu’elle voit dans le fonctionnement du langage une forme sui generis d’action, par contraste avec la connaissance. Cependant, quoi qu’il soit des autres membres de cette ecole, en separant le composant illocutionnaire des composants perlocutionnaires, en faisant de l’efffet illocutionnaire qui consiste dans la connaissance le seul element essentiel pour la reussite de l’action linguistique en tant que telle, et en faisant l’action dependre de la connaissance qui constitute l’effet illocutionnaire comme condition sine qua non pour que l’action ait lieu, Searle reduit effectivement l’action linguistique a la connaisance objective, a la connaissance comme pure vision d’objet. C’est cette vision d’objet purement theorique qui constitute l’effet illocutionnaire, laquelle suffit pour la reussite de l’action linguistique et sans laquelle les actions qui constituent les effets perlocutionnaires (par example, l’action d’obeir)  ne peuvent avoir lieu, tandis que la vision d’objet ne depend nullement des actions. Par contre, dans la conception de Polanyi, la vision d’objet ou connaissance objective disparait tout a fait du champ du fonctionnement du langage. Elle est substituteee par la CT, qui n’est pas connaisance objective mais instrumentale et donc en rapport intrinseque avec l’action. C’est pour cette raison que j’ai insiste plusieurs fois sur le fait que ce qui caracterise la conception de Polanayi, c’est le deplacement du sujet connaissant de la tradition epistemologique clasique par l’agent.

J’en viens maintenant au cas de l’effet illocutionnaire. Dans la communication linguistique, ce cas est representé concretement par les assertions, qui expriment les jugements intellectuels ou perceptuels du locuteur. Dans mon apercu de ses vues au chapitre precedent, je n’eus pas a m’occuper de la facon dont il traite le rapport de verite ou faussete des assertions linguistiques, car il n’adresse pas ce sujet sous la rubrique de la communication entre locuteur et interlocuteur mais sous celle des assertions comme l’ acte linguistique particulier qui refere au monde et qui comporte l’afirmation de sa propre verite. La seule difference, d’ailleurs secondaire, entre Aristote et Searle, est que ce dernier separe la question de la conformité de la connaissance que l’interlocuteur acquiert des intentions communicatives du locuteur avec ces intentions, de la question de la conformité  des assertions perceptuelles a la realite, tandis qu’Aristote, qui n’a  pas de theorie explicite de la communication linguistique, ne distingue pas les deux questions. Pour Searle, par contre, une chose est la communication liguistique entre locuteur et interlocuteur; une toute autre chose est le cas particulier de la verite out faussete, disons “ontologique,” des seuls actes linguistiques qui se proclament porteurs de verite de par leur reference a la realite, a savoir, les assertions de ce qui est le cas ou ne l’est pas en realite. La communication en tant que telle comprend tous les actes linguistiques, aussi bien ceux dans lesquels l’intention du locuteur est exclusivement de communiquer une connaissance, que ceux par le moyen desquels, en outre d’une connaissance de ce qu’il veut dire le locuteur veut obtenir un effet perlocutionnaire. Dans le premier cas, les assertions tombent sous la rubrique de la communication, comme un acte linguistique parmi tous les autres. Dans le second cas les assertions sont etudiées comme le seul acte linguistique susceptible d’etre vrai ou faux. Or puisqu’Aristote ne traite pas de la communication en tant que telle, il n’a aucune raison de distinguer les deux questions ni de les traiter separement.

Il est grand temps de porter notre attention sur les actes linguistiques a intention exclusivement illocutionnaire, dont je ne me suis pas encore occupé. Demandons-nous donc si, au moins dans leur cas, Searle a raison de les concevoir a la maniere Aristotelienne comme expressions de la vision de ce qui est le cas, possedée par le locuteur en tant que sujet connaissant, c’est-a-dire, d’interpreter les assertions comme porteuses de connaissance objective. Quant a Polanyi, il pense qu’il s’agit , non pas de connaissance objective ou vision d’objet, mais de CT, c.a.d., d’action adaptee pour realiser un but desire. Autrement dit, tous les speech acts, les assertions inclues, ne doivent pas etre comprises comme des representations, même des representations symboliques, de ce a quoi elles referent; elles doivent etre employées comme instruments pour l’action, comme ce a partir duquel le sujet doit agir. Pour departager entre ces deux conceptions si differentes, considerons quelques examples.

Imaginez que letelephone sonne. Je reponds et, me tournant vers mon ami qui se trouve dans la meme piece, je lui dis:  “Ta soeur est au telephone.” Voila une assertion toute simple. Or, s’il s’agit de transmission de connaissance objective, ainsi que Searle l’entend,c’est-a-dire, s’il s’agit de communiquer a mon ami ma vision objective de ce qui est le cas, vision qu’il acquerra par la comprehension de ce que je dis etre le cas, alors une reponse parfaitement adequate de la part de mon ami serait de me dire: “J’ai compris que ma soeur est au telephone” et de rester la ou il est sans rien faire d’autre. Pour ma part, en tant qu’agent linguistique competent, je m’attend a une toute autre reponse et je serais interloqué si la reponse de mon ami etait celle que je viens de decrire. Je m’attends, en effet  (et je suis persuade que, contrairement a Searle, n’importe quel agent linguistique competent en langue francaise me donnerait la raison), je m’attends a ce que mon ami prenne le telephone et parle avec sa soeur. Pour le dire autrement, je m’attends a ce que mon ami fasse fonctionner mon assertion, non comme porteuse de la connaissance objective de ce qui est le cas, mais comme instrument pour son action, comme CT “a partir de laquelle’ il doit agir et prendre le telephone. De meme si je dis a mon ami qui cherche le marteau: “Le marteau est dans la salle a manger,” je m’attends a ce qu’il aille dans la salle a manger prendre le marteau, non pas a ce qu’il acquiere une vision purement theorique, c.a.d. sans rapport intrinseque avec l’action, du marteau dans la salle a manger, ainsi que Searle le pense (ou devrait le penser s’il croit vraiement a sa theorie linguistique). De meme, pour terminer, dans le cas de n’importe quelle assertion linguistique d’un etat concret particulier du monde environnant.

En fait, j’avais deja plus haut fait la critique de la position de Searle au sujet de la connaissance objective dans la communication linguistique, en disant que son modele du fonctionnement du langage est la maniere bâtarde dont le langage fonctionne dans le cadre artificel d’un cours de langue pour des etrangers. La seule difference est que dans le chapitre precedent je me referait au chapitre de son essaai où Searle parle de tous les actes linguistiques sous la rubrique de la communication, et ici  je me refere au chapitre oul il etudie le cas particulier des assertions dont le referent est ce qui est le cas en realite EXTRAMENTALE, TOTALEMENT INDEPENDANTE DES DISPOSITIONS OU DESIRS DU SUJET. En tout cas, pour mon propos, cette difference n’est pas significative, puisque Searle concoit les deux cas comme examples de connaissance objective ou connaissance d’objet, tandis que je soutiens avec Polanyi que ce que nous appelons ordinairement “connaissance” fonctionne comme instrument pour l’agent , non comme representation d’objet pour le sujet connaissant de la tradition epistemologique classique.

Quant a la fertilite de la substitution du sujet connaissant par l’agent dans la conception polanyienne de la CT, nous aurons l’occasion de l’apprecier au prochain chapitre dans son application dans le terrain de la science. Effectivement, ainsi que la conception polanyienne fournit une alternative a la facon ordinaire de comprendre le fonctionnement du langage, representée par la theorie de Searle, ainsi elle fournit egalement une alternative a la facon ordinaire de concevoir le fonctionnement de la science, representée par la conception classique de la science comme vision correcte ou du moins de plus en plus approximative de la  nature de la realité.
