THE QUESTION OF TRUTH

,

(1) “... the second operation of intellect–by the very nature of its reflective character, by the very fact that it raises the question of truth, which is conformity between mind and thing ,– introduces the duality of idea and thing and makes the former the medium in and through which one apprehends the latter . Thus, our knowledge of God’s existence is just our knowledge of the truth of the judgment, Deus est ” (Lonergan, Verbum , p. 8).

(2) “ Western philosophy characteristically takes ontology–the study of essences, of what is–as first philosophy; upon this ostensibly sure foundation it then constructs an ethics . Levinas’s originality and importance lies in the attempt he makes to reverse this hypothesis : ethics is first philosophy ” (David Jopling, “Levinas on Desire, Dialogue and the Other,” American Catholic Philosophical Quarterly ,, vol. LXV, Autumn 1991, issue 4, p. 406).

(3) “In the Introduction it was mentioned that many of the Western approaches to ethics implicitly concern themselves first with epistemological and ontological questions (e.g., about the nature of reality and the nature or essence of human being) before turning to questions of ethics. The assumption is that once it is known what human nature is, and why it is as it is, it will become clearer what goodness is (and, derivatively, what we ought to do ).... Concerned with matters of fact, we put intelligibility before goodness and responsiveness . - But if Levinas’s hypothesis is correct–that ethics is ‘first philosophy,’ that truth presupposes justice...–then it might be the case that at the most fundamental level of inter-personal relations there just are no such facts of the matter. (5) Aristotle thought his predecessors in general, and Plato in particular, made a serious mistake in failing to mark the boundaries separating the different sciences and branches of philosophical inquiry. All of them failed to grasp the fundamental distinction between practical and theoretical knowledge . Ethics and politics, the prime examples of practical knowledge, differ from such theoretical sciences as metaphysics and physics not only in their aims but in their methods and subject matter as well. Indeed, Aristotle thinks the differences are such that we cannot regard practical and theoretical knowledge as two species of a single genus, for there is no common definition of knowledge which applies to both . - If we look at Plato’s middle dialogues, especially the Republi c, from Aristotle’s vantage point, it will appear that ethics and metaphysics have been brought together in an unnatural union , the science of ‘dialectic.’ The form of the good serves a double function: it is the first principle of ethics, and also the foundation of metaphysics insofar as it is the basis of our understanding of reality as such. [HENCE, GOODNESS IS A MATTER OF INTELLIGENCE, FOR PLATO, OF KNOWING THE GOOD]. Platonic dialectic is a confusing blend of the practical and theoretical. Questions about value and how we ought to act are not regarded as different in kind from questions about the ultimate nature of reality. The same method of treatment is apparently appropriate for both types of question.  - ‘Wisdom’ or phronesis is the name Plato gives to this highest form of knowledge which is at once practical and theoretical. My aim in this paper is to see how Aristotle separates out of this Platonic mixture his own special concept of practical wisdom or phronesis . He eventually came to believe that action is both the goal and the subject matter of phronesis ; [HERE IS THE CONFUSION: EITHER GOAL OR SUBJECT MATTER, BUT NOT BOTH1]. and since action is concerned with particulars, phronesis must be about particulars rather than universals. We must try to clarify what Aristotle means by these claims. But I also want to say something about how he arrives at his final position; as we shall see, his break with Plato was a gradual process. The notion of practical knowledge did not spring up all at once, fully formed; there was a time when he thought practical knowledge was much like theoretical, the only important contrast being the different aims of each ” (Daniel T. Devereux, “Particular and Universal in Aristotle’s Conception of Practical Knowledge,” Review of Metaphysics 39 (March 1986), pp. 483-484).

(6) Aristotle mentions in the sequel to passage 3 that perception is a source of knowledge of particulars, e.g., we know through perception that fire is hot. (10) Aristotle had previously (Book III 3) analyzed the activity of deliberating arguing that (i) we deliberate about things that can be realized in action (praxis); (ii) we deliberate not about good ends but about means to a previously determined end; (iii) that choice ( proairesis ) is both the endpoint of deliberating as well as the beginning point of action, and (iv) that ‘object of choice is something we desire as a result of deliberating, [so that] we may define choice as deliberate desire ’ (1113a11). In other words, even if a person already desires or wills some good as end., she must deliberate in order to discover for herself that certain means will lead to that end, and thereby persuade herself that those means are likewise desirable;. Once having done so, the enacting of those means is motivated by a desire born of deliberation . - All this is carried into Book VI, where now Aris uses these results to examine the kind of reasoning that pertains to human action. If a choice is to be really good, and choice is ‘deliberate desire, ’ he argues, then ‘the reasoning must be true , and the desire correct,’ (1139a25) that is, the good state in matters of human action is not some form of knowledge alone, but ‘truth in harmony with correct desire ’ [HERE, AGAIN, IS THE MISTAKE]. The notion of knowledge as conformity or similarity or correspondence or adaequatio to what is the case, to reality, is  inspired by the case of vision. Indeed, vision is clearly dualistic in that it takes place at a distance between the seeer and the seen, and at the same time presupposes conformity between what the seeer sees and the reality seen,  in order to be true. (Aris attempts to erase the duality by conceiving of knowledge as intentional identity between the knower and the known, but the duality remains, in that the knower ‘s knowledge remains ontologically different from the reality known and is only intentionally identical with it). The seeer, however, sees the object, but doesn’t see himself. Therefore he doesn’t see himself as distant from the object. For the subject who sees, the distance arises only–and not as distance seen but as distance obstacle--in the practical dimension, when the seeer attempts to grab or otherwise deal practically with what he sees; not in the dimension of contemplation . (As Merleau-Ponty said, depth is the most existential of the dimensions). Knowledge, though, belongs in the order of contemplation, since it is inspired by the case of vision, of contemplative vision, of observation, not of action. In short, the notion of knowledge is based not on the seeer’s experience, but on the experience of a seeer or observer who observes a seeer seen in the act of seing an object and to whom the observer wrongly attributes the distance he perceives but which is not perceived by the seeer .

The notion of truth as known with certainty rather than merely assumed conformity is also based on the experience of a seeer seen in the act of seeing an object. Indeed truth and falsity are correlates. If knowledge were always true, that is, if the correspondence of  knowledge with the object known obtained always, then the  notion of knowledge would not have needed to be supplemented with the notion of truth, of known rather than merely assumed correspondence. Now, falsity or failure of correspondence presupposes a comparison between the knowledge and the object seen or known . This, however, cannot be the deed of the seeer as such, since he is only aware of object seen, not of correspondence or lack thereof. Falsity is therefore the discovery of an observer who–on the basis of the seeer’s statement (or of his action)-- realizes that what the seen seeer sees is not genuine knowledge, is not true, because it is not the same object he himself sees.

But how does the observer know what the observed subject sees or knows?

Let us begin with  the case of animals, which do not state what they know . Yet,  according to Aristotle (the same Aristotle who will tell us that truth and falsity are exclusive properties of judgments and statements), animals know materially, in that what they see (or expect?) is adequate or conform to what is the case, but they don’t know formally, in that they don’t know that they know. [THIS MAY MEAN TWO DIFFERENT THINGS: (1) ANIMALS KNOW BUT DON’T REALIZE THAT THEY KNOW. (2) EVEN WHEN THEY KNOW, ANIMALS DON’T KNOW WITH CERTAINTY, SINCE THEY DON’T GRASP SUFFICIENT REASON]. Now, for Aristotle the epistemologist to affirm that an animal knows he has to hypothesize that what the animal sees is what he himself sees, for animals don’t say what they see. But Aris ‘knows’ that animals see, not by observing them seeing (for they don’t state what they see) but by observing them coping practically with the surrounding world--avoiding obstacles and moving intentionally–and, in the case of the superior animals, ever more skillfully, on the basis of past experience--toward their objects of desire or away from their objects of aversion. Indeed, Aris affirms that animals possess only  practical knowledge, because he sees them act, that is, interact with objects in a way appropriate to their desires,  and act on the basis of their previous experience, but doesn’t see them make statements, which express speculative knowledge through predication. Animals do not make predications–judgments--because neither are they interested in knowledge for its own sake nor do they grasp theoretically what is the case, but only applicatively, in terms of practical expectations and of dispositions to act in determinate ways.

As for people, Aris knows that they know not only practically, as animals do, but also theoretically, unlike animals, because they judge and make predicative statements .  For my part, I am convinced that the very idea of theoretical knowledge and of truth as known correspondence arises from the fact that we make statements which are misREAD as theoretical predications rather than being HEARD as performatives . Indeed, the statements we make as engaged agents are not predicative; they are communicative statements to others about relevant features of the surrounding world to be responded to either immediately or at the appropriate time of actual exposure. These statements, therefore, are oral statements by engaged agent to engaged agents in a commonly shared situation. They are practical and implicitly evaluative and meant to motivate, not theoretically predicative. However, when there is disagreement between the speaker and the addressee, the statement itself becomes the object–and object of consideration as to its truth, not object for response.   (This is what Socrates did. He asked a theoretical question that was to be answered by a predicative ontological statement, such as “Piety is...” Then he explored that statement as to its truth. And that is what philosophers have done ever since).

Thus, for example, if you are looking for the hammer and I tell you “the hammer is in the next room,” you’ll go to the next room to look for the hammer. But if you don’t believe me, you won’t. That is practical understanding of language. My statement referred you to a feature of the world as object for response, and you responded practically to it by not going to the other room, since you didn’t believe, since you failed to be referred. Not believing amounts to not being practically referred to the world as I referred you to it. It may also happen, however, that apart from not responding to the object in the way indicated by my statement, you turn your attention to my statement, away from the hammer for which you were practically looking, and tell me “What you said is false; it doesn’t correspond to what is the case.” At this point my statement has ceased to sound as practical, which is the way it was meant and the way in which oral communicative statements from agent to agent are meant and ordinarily heard, and it has been converted into a merely predicative, theoretical statement to be considered and explored as to its truth, as to its validity as knowledge, as to its correspondence to what is the case. It was meant to refer you practically to object for response, but now it has become “a picture” of a state of the world. At this point we are going to discuss what “evidence” ( from “videre”) do I have for my statement, that is for the correspondence of my picture to the state of the world.

A further and very significant step beyond conceiving knowledge as correspondence or conformity was taken by the “lovers of wisdom” in their fight against the cynical rhetoricians, the Sophists. Having adopted for knowledge the paradigm of predicative judgments as pictures to be contemplated, for Socrates discussed the validity of his adversaries statements, the lovers of wisdom added the requirement of their necessary correspondence to what is the case,  in order to escape the hazards of “opinion,” of merely assumed correspondence.  Knowledge, in other words, is not mere correspondence, but knowledge of correspondence, which can be guaranteed only if the correspondence is necessary.      

This shift toward necessity in knowledge and truth seems to have been effected by Socrates in his search for essential, unchangeable definitions, which in its turn led Plato to the substantialization of the essences in the eternal ideas as the genuine realities which justified the validity of universal statements of essence, that is, of essential predications. [NOTICE THAT ONLY ON THE HYPOTHESIS THAT KNOWLEDGE CONSISTS IN CORRESPONDENCE DOES THE REQUIREMENT OF THE ACTUAL EXISTENCE OF THE ETERNAL IDEAS ARISE, FOR OTHERWISE THE KNOWLEDGE WOULD BE OBJECTLESS AND, CONSEQUENTLY, NOT KNOWLEDGE].Those who contemplated those realities, namely, the philosophers, were the genuine knowers who possessed the eternal truth. This Socratic-Platonic move, however, encountered at least two significant problems. (1) How  the mundane, worldly, earthly realities--which are particular and temporal and changeable and contingent--were to be coordinated with the eternal ones–which are universal and eternal and unchangeable and necessary--both in terms of their existence and in terms of their knowability and truth. (2) How practical knowledge as to what ought to be done was to be coordinated with theoretical (from “theoria” ) or speculative (from “speculum” ) knowledge.

(1) Plato’s solution, in terms of existence, was that the worldly realities were feeble images of the eternal ones by participation in them. By the same token, in terms of knowledge, the worldly realities were, so to say, less true. But here Plato had to cope with an impossible conundrum that plagued him all his life through, to wit: that because of the temporal, changeable, contingent, particular character of the sensible realities, there was no possible genuine knowledge of them. Indeed, by their very nature, sensible objects, according to Plato, were only objects of belief ( doxa) , not only because the truth of perceptual statements couldn’t be demonstrated with necessity, but because the sensible realities themselves were b y their very particularity, temporality and contingency unknowable since unintelligible. In short, perceptual knowledge was for Plato not merely probable, unverifiable knowledge, but certainly wrong knowledge, since sensible realities were not objects of knowledge but of mere doxa . (NOTICE THAT DOXA MEANS TWO DIFFERENT THINGS (A) MERE BELIEF, THAT IS, FALSE BELIEF, AS OPPOSED TO KNOWLEDGE. (B) OPINION, THAT IS, PROBABLE KNOWLEDGE, AS OPPOSED TO GENUINE, CERTAIN, GUARANTEED KNOWLEDGE).

.

(2) The second problem was equally intractable within the Platonic constraints. Socrates, whose interest was in practical knowledge, in ‘the good life,’ failed to distinguish practical from theoretical knowledge and reduced practical knowledge to speculative knowledge. The good person was the person who knew, and the bad person was the one who didn’t know, who was mistaken . Indeed,  Plato  placed the Idea of the Good among the eternal Ideas whose contemplation resulted in knowledge, for otherwise the knowledge of the good would be impossible. This amounted to identifying practical knowledge with speculative knowledge. However, action takes place in the sensible world, and in response to changeable and contingent objects. Now, if the problem of correspondence in the case of speculative knowledge of particular, sensible objects was already intractable for Plato, because the sensible is unknowable, only object of opinion, the problem is doubly intractable in the case of practical knowledge. For here we are in the realm of desire and aversion, not of mere intellect, and objects of desire and aversion are irremediably relative; furthermore, here we are in the order of action, not of specular vision ^(F1), where correspondence through similitude has no relevance.

 

So far we have dealt with the notion of knowledge and truth as correspondence. However, in order to have a complete idea of Aris’s notion of knowledge,  we need to consider another or his tenets, to wit: that truth is a property of judgments, and of their linguistic expression in statements, exclusively . In other words, only judgments and statements can be true or false, according to Aristotle . [HERE WE ARE IN THE ORDER OF PREDICATION: WHAT IS, AND WHAT IS TO BE DONE. PLATO SEEMS TO HAVE REMAINED IN THE ORDER OF CONTEMPALTION RATHER THAN PREDICATION]. Here the contrast is not with perceptual acts, which can be materially true if only they correspond or conform to the reality perceived (even the knowledge of animals can be materially true ^(F2)), but  with simplex apprehensio which can be neither true nor false, even if and when it corresponds or conforms to what is the case.. Why does Aris deny that simplex apprehensio can be true or false?  As indicated by its name, apprehension grasps what is the case, much as the perception of animals does grasp what is the case when it is correct, when it is materially true . Yet Aris doesn’t say that simplex apprehensio is true, not even materially ^(F3). Why not? Because, by contrast to judgment, in simply apprehending something, the knower is not yet committed to the conformity of the apprehension  to reality. This apprehension, insofar as the grasp of what is the case is concerned, is purely potential, as far as the knower is concerned. Only when the knower commits himelf or herself to the apprehension being conform to what is the case does the knower genuinely know, because this commitment obtains only when the subject knows that his or her apprehension is true. This is what happens when the subject makes a judgment. . Whether affirmative or negative, the judgment, like the statement by which it is expressed, is categorically predicative, whereas the simplex apprehensio is not. Only those acts which involve or express the commitment of the knowing subject to the identity between what is known and what is the case can be genuinely  true or false, and those acts are judgments and their verbal expression, namely, statements.

This seems to imply that knowledge and truth are somewhat different things for Aristotle. Knowledge consists in conformity or adequatio or intentional “similitude” (see Lonergan, Verbum, p. 168). Truth consists in rationally justified commitment to the conformity thanks to the grasp of sufficient reason for affirming or denying that the case is such. When the mind grasps sufficient reason it denies or affirms, and when it grasps necessarily sufficient reason it necessarily affirms or denies. Conformity without grasp of sufficient reason and, therefore, without theoretical commitment to the case being such, which arises out of the grasp of sufficient reason, is at best material knowledge. Conformity with grasp of sufficient reason is formal knowledge, knowledge that one knows.. Conformity with grasp of essentially or causally necessary or apodictic reason is scientific knowledge or episteme, knowledge that one’s knowledge is necessarily true.

In what precedes we have not completely explored what Aris calls practical knowledge . Unlike Plato, Aris distinguishes speculative from practical knowledge. Insofar, however, as Aris calls practical knowledge “knowledge,” the implication seems to be that it, too, consists in conformity or similitude. Since it is practical knowledge, however, it is, unlike speculative knowledge, connected with action. One way of construing this connection, suggested by Aristotle himself, is that speculative knowledge is guided by the sheer, disinterested desire to know, whereas practical knowledge is guided by other desires. Therefore, they differ only because of their different goals. (This is one of the stages in Aris’s self-distancing from Plato concerning practical knowledge, as analyzed by Deveruex). In this interpretation, the object known in the case of speculative and practical knowledge may be the same, except that speculative knowledge is satisfied with the knowledge itself, whereas practical knowledge will move beyond mere knowledge to action. [AS HUME OBSERVED, HOWEVER, THE PASSAGE FROM THE PICTURE TO THE ACTION IS UNINTELLIGIBLE]. Thus, in Aris’s s own illustration, one can study medicine either for the sake of acquiring medical knowledge, or for the sake of curing. [ Another aspect of practical knowledge, indicated by the case of medical knowledge, is that it is at least in part based on practical repeated experience of effects, which in animals produces practical expectation concerning the future, whereas in people it leads to the speculative grasp of the general rule. In this case, however, the theoretician and the practician do not know quite the same (perhaps they only know partly the same), in that the theoretician lacks experience without which he cannot apply practically the general rule to the particular case] A second way of construing the connection between knowledge and action in the case of practical knowledge, suggested too by Aris himself in relation to the practical syllogism, though not clearly spelled out, and perhaps not even fully realized by him , is that affective, that is, interested, desire (by contrast to  the purely objective, disinterested  desire to know) is intrinsic to practical knowledge so as to make it totally different from speculative knowledge, not a different species from the same genus . In this interpretation, speculative knowledge aims at and consists in conformity to what is the case as well as in assent or commitment of the mind to such being the case. Practical knowledge, by contrast, does not aim at, and does not involve, knowledge of the truth; it aims at bringing about through action the desired state of affairs.

The difference between these two ways of construing the connection between knowledge and action is the following. In the former interpretation , speculative knowledge is a necessary though not sufficient condition for practical knowledge.  The desire to see my friend Patroclos guides my search for his whereabouts, and I discover that he is in Athens. Somebody else who is not particularly desirous of seeing Patroclos but merely and disinterestedly curious about Patroclos may discover that he is in Athens. Our knowledge is the same, though in one case the goal is to simply to know for the sake of knowing, and in the other case the goal is to see my friend . Now, since I want to see Patroclos, my knowledge that he is in Athens will lead to an additional step not included in the (speculative) knowledge itself, namely, action in addition to the knowledge--that is, actually going to go to Athens if at all possible--, whereas the other guy’s knowledge will end  in mere assent. To put it differently, both the theoretician and the practician know the same–where Patroclos is--, but their goals differ. And since the goal of the theoretician is knowing for the sake of knowing and the goal of the practician is acting appropriately or successfully in view of achieving his goal, the latter has to go through the additional step of acting, guided by knowledge. (This sinterpretation is open to Hume’s objection concerning the passage from is to ought).   In the latter interpretation speculative and practical knowledge have nothing in common, and the former is not a necessary condition for the latter. They are simply two different things. Speculative knowledge is guided or informed by the objective or disinterested desire to know, which has nothing to do with changing the existing state of affairs. Furthermore, it aims at and consists in conformity and terminates in assentful grasp of conformity with what is already  the case.  Practical knowledge, by contrast, is inspired or guided by interested desire. Furthermore, it aims at bringing about a not yet existent state of affairs desired as a means to achieve the subject’s goal, and it consists in the actual achievement of the desired state of affairs. Deliberation, decision, and action are the constitutive moments of practical knowledge in this second interpretation, whereas disinterested thinking and affirming or denying are the constitutive moments of speculative knowledge.

I think that Plato–perhaps misled by Socrates, with his obsession with essential definitions (of piety, for instance, in the Euthyphro) , favored the first interpretation to the point not only of positively excluding the second interpretation but of assimilating practical knowledge to speculative knowledge totally. Aris is more ambiguous than Plato. He wavers between the two interpretations, without ever clearly formulating the second, but approaching it, particularly when he speaks of the practical syllogism which is irreducible to the speculative one. Nonetheless, he never fully escaped Plato’s speculativism and intellectualism. In this regard, I think that Devereux is wrong. Aris never realized that speculative and practical knowledge were generically different, and in fact sees practical knowledge as a mixture of knowledge of the true and or correct desire, as we shall see below.

Be that as it may, one wonders why the temptation to reduce practical knowledge to speculative knowledge, or at least to subordinate practical to speculative knowledge, has been, historically, so strong, which I see as a scandalous case of putting the cart before the horse.

It is my opinion ,

Concerning Plato and Aris,

(a) That, in his dependence on Plato, Aris never realized that what he called practical knowledge didn’t involve speculative (that is, specular, pictorial) similitude or conformity and, consequently, that to call practical knowledge ‘knowledge’ involved a bad equivocation.

(b)  That, since he initially was a ful-fledged Platonist and, pace Devereux , he never really came to see practical knowledge as  radically, generically and even more than generically, different from speculative knowledge. Aris always subordinated practical to theoretical knowledge, at least to the extent , if not of prerequiring theoretical knowledge in order to reach practical knowledge (so that if someone doesn’t know something theoretically cannot know it practically), at least of considering practical knowledge an inferior kind of knowledge because of its lack of exactness due to the particularity, the contingency, and the temporality of its object.

As for myself,

(c) I think that all ‘knowledge’ (if the name can be kept at all without suggesting similitude or conformity) is ultimately practical or applicative, in that it is interested rather than disinterested, and is meant to terminate in action capable of bringing about the deisred state of affairs.

(d) That the illusion of speculative knowledge arises from the objectification of the judged-stated that takes place when the judged-stated becomes object of attention, instead of referring practically to the world, and is then explored as to its truth, that is, as to its similitude to reality. [NB. HOWEVER, IT CAN BE EXPLORED AS TO ITS INTELLIGIBILITY–FORMAL OR CAUSAL CAUSE OF THE UNIFORMITY, AS WE SHALL SEE BELOW.]

(e) That practical knowledge has the primacy over speculative knowledge, at least in the sense  that there would be no basis for the reflective moment without the familiarity with the world that results from experience .

(f) That, at least probably, there really is no theoretical knowledge as such. Reflection is reflection by agents and for the sake of action, so that all understanding is ultimately applicative. This is why, in matters of geometry as well as of physics, we test people’s understanding not by extracting from them predicative statements but by giving them problems to solve. In support of my opinion

- The conformity of the mind to what is the case presupposes the existence of the reality to be known , for, pace Kant , it is conformity of the mind to reality, not the other way around that was involved in the idea of speculative knowledge with the duality between knower and known, the similitude between knowledge and reality, and the primacy of reality over knowledge which underlie the Greek conception before the difference between speculative and practical knowledge arises). However, in the case of practical knowledge, that is, of deliberation-decision-action, there can be no conformity in the sense of intentional similitude, because the reality aimed at is future and to be brought about through action. Therefore, if knowledge consists in intentional conformity, practical knowledge is not knowledge but something totally different from knowledge .

- The original model for the Greek conception of knowledge was speculative, that is, specular, knowledge–intentional conformity or similitude. Plato didn’t distinguish between speculative and practical knowledge and reduced practical to speculative knowledge.  Therefore he conceived knowledge as conformity. It was Aristotle who, progressively, came to distinguish practical from theoretical knowledge. But I don’t think that Aris ever came to the full realization attributed to him by Devereux, according to whom “Aristotle thinks the differences are such that we cannot regard practical and theoretical knowledge as two species of a single genus, for there is no common definition of knowledge” (cf. quotations supra). As a result, in spite of his progress over Plato, Aris bequeathed us the idea that speculative knowledge is a precondition of practical knowledge, that we need previous speculative knowledge of the human being in order to act, and that I cannot practically go to fetch the hammer in the next room without previous theoretical , predicative knowledge that the hammer is in the next room, much as Socrates imagined that he needed to know what piety predicatively and essentially is in order to be pious. Perhaps Lonergan would , in the same Platonic and partly Aristotelian vein, say that I cannot pray God without previous knowledge of the truth of the theoretical  judgment that ‘God is’ (see quotations above), whereas I would rather say that I cannot know that God exists (a knowledge which is itself practical, applicative) without praying and serving God.

- Aris distinguishes existential from essential (?) predication–the ‘is’ of existence from the ‘is’ of truth, the ‘is’ of intellectual assent from the ‘is’ of exists as judged. It seems that existence has generated confusion in the realm of knowledge because existence is not a predicate. I suspect that the confusion arises from importing to the order of contemplation what belongs in the order of praxis and exposure. In this way it is possibly connected with the general issue of the relationship between practical and theoretical knowledge and therefore needs to be explored in connection with it.

Appendix: Reflections on the basis of Devereux’s article

Aris began, as a good Platonist, with speculative knowledge as the model for knowledge. Therefore, for Aris, knowledge consists in correspondence or similitude. Then, by contrast, he discovered practical knowledge, where the correspondence is (he wavers between the two) either ‘inexact’ (approximative, imprecise) or ‘uncertain’ (not necessary, merely probable ore, at best, merely factual). After introducing practical knowledge, Aris never eliminated speculative knowledge or the model of knowledge [THIS TWO ARE NOT THE SAME] implied by it, and in fact he extended it to practical knowledge. Hence, two kinds of sciences, and the theoretical ones–among which geometry and physics, and of course metaphysics)–are superior (either because of their precision or because of their certainty, or because of both). The theoretical sciences have no application, or at least are gained without applicative interest and, perhaps, even without repetitive experience of effects by which the agent is affected and in which he or she has a practical interest. What is left is experience-observation (like “bodies fall”), not experience of effects, much less of effects by which an agent or a species of agents  is affected–effects connected with a certain object with which the agent has to cope and wants, if  possible, to control instrumentally for his or her own purposes.

What Aristoteles should have eliminated is (a) the idea of correspondence-similitude which is possible only concerning speculative-specular knowledge-truth, and (b) the idea of universal-necessary objects (natures and essences) connected with specular knowledge that consists in correspondence and, thus, demands a universal object for a necessary concept or predication. (INDEED, ONLY ON THE HYPOTHESIS OF KNOWLEDGE AS CORRESPONDENCE IS THE IDEA OF UNIVERSAL AND NECESSARY OBJECTS INTELLIGIBLE).

He should therefore have begun, as agents do, with practical-interested knowledge, and should have remained within the sphere of the exposed human agent .  But this he couldn’t do because he had at the beginning absorbed from Plato the idea of specular knowledge as the model of knowledge. He demoted it from the contemplation of the eternal Ideas to the level of intellectual predication in his view of knowledge judgments-statements corresponding to, or grasping, essential natures. This allowed for scientific knowledge, but at the same time left the issue of correspondence in the case of perception (which Plato had relegated to the sphere of opinion-belief) as a loose end. (This is the point at which Kant, without giving up the idea of specular knowledge, reversed the correspondence through his Copernican revolution, but also leaving perceptual, empirical judgments as a loose end). Usually this loose end in Aris’s gnoseology is hidden through the attribution to Aris of a common-sense pragmatism on a point on which every commonsensical person would agree.

Let us, unlike Aris, begin with practical knowledge:

Superior, locomotive animals (mostly mammals) learn regularities in phenomena through repetitive experience of exposure to those regularities, but they learn only concerning those actions-effects that affect them and those of their species. Furthermore, they learn only applicatively, not objectively or predicatively, through practical expectations and dispositions to respond. Being non-linguistic, animals are like Hellen Keller before language. Through repetitive experience they learn practically what to expect. Their expectations are inscribed in their bodies, which  become habitual instruments, somewhat like our bodies in driving a car.

Hellen knew that when her hat was put on she was going to go out and braced herself for that.

People, however, are linguistic, and so they become like Hellen Keller after language.They can, not only respond but, among many other linguistic practices in which they begin participating as hearers rather than as speakers, to state-applicatively objects for response. They can also state the uniformity that obtains in certain objects for response, that is, the general rule of the action of the object-for-response and, consequently, the rule for the future response. In addition,  people can share the above with others through language, and others can share the above with them.

Before statements, however, come names and the grasping of names, as was the case with Hellen Keller. Indeed, unlike dogs, which do not name and which respond to names as signs of imminent presence by bracing themselves for what is named, people can name denotatively, that is, refer themselves and others to objects for deliberation and response, and name connotatively as concepts to be enriched by future personal or shared experience and fixed by predication. In both cases, though, the animal immediate response is neutralized . In the former case, deliberation becomes possible as to how to respond, given one’s goal and situation. Here the reference is immediately practical, from agent to agent in situation of immediate exposure . The latter case, however, is more complicated and deserves attention.

For a dog, its master’s name is a sign of its master’s imminent presence and nothing else. The reason is that dogs, being non-linguistic, are always in the mood of exposure and according to their passively received natural desires . But if I pronounce your mother’s name, you don’t necessarily take it as a sign of her imminent presence, and therefore ask “What about her?” Here is the ‘concept’ (Aquinas would say the inner word oir the conceptus intellectus) connoted by the name , to be enriched through experience and fixed through predication, rather than the object denoted by the name . This is, at least in part,  what Hellen Keller discovered when she understood the word-name ‘water.’ It sometimes denoted, and referred her, to real water in her immediate surroundings, but other times it just connoted the concept water, so that she could ask, “What about water?” Now, thanks to the name-concept,  she could talk and think about water either in the presence or in the absence of water and without necessarily considering it in relation to her needs or desires, that is, applicatively.  In fact, as she would progressively discover, thanks to the connotative value of names she could think and talk about whatever, regardless of whether what she thought of or imagined actually existed or not .

I just said that Hellen Keller could think and talk .  The two activities are different.  One refers to what Plato characterizes as the silent dialogue or conversation with oneself, and the other involves others with whom one shares as a speaker. The two activities, however, are linguistic, in that both are possible thanks to our linguistic capacity. Animals can neither think nor talk.

Of course talking with others and even with oneself involves not only names but predicative sentences. But the sentences themselves can be meaningful without being denotative, that is, without referring the addressee to actually existing objects either as objects for deliberation and response or as objects for consideration. As remarked by Lonergan, “Logical positivists to the contrary, false propositions are not meaningless; they mean something; what they mean is an inner word, and only because that inner word is false, does the proposition lack objective reference” ( Verbum , pp. 2-3. And see below, noite 4). [In support, he quotes Aquinas In I Periherm., lect. 2: “... haec vox, ‘homo est asinus’ est vere vox et vere signum; sed quia est signum falsi, ideo dicitur falsa.”]. Moreover, there is the case, not considered at this point by Lonergan, of imaginary narratives in which, by contrast to historical narratives, the issue of truth doesn’t arise. Such narratives are meaningful without being denotative. The same with poetry.

Searle’s view of language seems to be based on this connotative characteristic of language, isolated from its denotative characteristic, which is seen by him as linguistically secondary or not essential, as well as parasitic on the connotative. Language is always understandable and to be understood; sometimes, in addition, it means or refers to an effect to be achieved linguistically [(?) BUT THIS EFFECT IS NOT ACHIEVED BY VIRTUE OF THE MEANING!] or an action to be performed.  (However, because of his ‘realistic’ interest in statements, that is, because of his concern with truth, Searle doesn’t deal with imaginary worlds or any other usage of language which is not what he calls ‘literal’). The illocutionary force indicator in a speech act (I warn you, I ask you...) expresses and shows how it is to be taken,  that is, “what illocutionary act the speker is performing in the utterance of th4e sentence” (Searle, Sp[eech Acts, p. 30). In addition, certain speech acts have a perlocutionary force that has to do with “the consejkquences or effects such acts have on the actions, thoughts, or beliefs, etc., of hearers. F. e., by arguing I may persuade... someone, by warning him I may scare or alarm, by making a request I may get him to do something, by informing him I may convince him (enlighten, edify, inspire him, get him to rea,lize)” (Ibid., p. 30). But the perlocutionary effects are linguistically accidental, in the sense that there is no genuine speech act that is not to be understood as to its illocutionary force, whereas a genuine speech act may have no perlocutionary force, and anyway the achievement of the perlocutionary effect intended by the speaker is linguistically accidental since it doesn’t pertain to the linguistic meaning.

Similarly Aquinas, because of his similar concern with truth (as correspondence) and consequently because of his central concern with statements (as objects of assent). ^(F4) In his commentary on Peri Hermeneias Aquinas speaks of enunciativa oratio qua significatur ipse mentis conceptus, conceptus intellectus in quo est verum et falsum. [ And he speaks of scientia demonstrativa in qua animus hominis per rationem inducitur ad consentiendum vero ex his quae sunt propria rei (by contrast to rhetor et poeta [qui] inducunt ad assentiendum ei quod intendunt, non solum per ea quae sunt propria rei, sed etiam per dispositionem audientis]. . This conception of enunciativa oratio  presupposes the passage from participatory engagement in situation of exposure, (in which the meaning or reference is directly to object for response and applicative sor practical) to contemplation-observation-explanation-exploration as to truth corespondence--from Abraham’s exposure to Yaweh’s commands or practical statements to the Serlean contemplative understanding of commands, and from there to the question of truth as to whether the understood command by Yaweh actually took place. Correspondingly or correlatively, it involves a shift from denotation to connotation as the genuine meaning of speech-acts. Existential depth, distances of exposure, have disappeared. Objectivity, pure desire to understand and to know, disinterested curiosity reign.  - There is, in addition, concerning enunciativae orationes, the case of explanation. For instance, where there is uniformity in the observed the explanatory  reason (what Aris calls the ‘ formal cause’) is sought, as we will see in a moment.

In the mood and in situation of exposure the grasp of the object is, through deliberation and decision, directly applicative, structured in a complex of end and means. Such is the case in the example of the British captain. The aim is to bring about the desired state of affairs that is possible under the circumstances, (or to make it possible). Let us contrast this with the case just mentioned in connection with the Peri Hermeneias , where the mood is not of exposure and consequently of interested desire, but of contemplation and curiosity. In the latter case  the aim is not a state of affairs to be brought about, but to understand an observed (not imagined or dreamed) phenomenon by explaining it, either formally or causally. There is no deliberation here, and no goal to be achieved trough action. (An action may take place as an application by an exposed subject at a later time, an action that is related to a goal of the applying agent for which the applicative understanding of the stated yields the grasp of the means. Of course, the application couldn’t have been found as the means for a goal without the previous (theoretical?) discovery, but the understanding of the cause is pursued for its own sake and is reached independently of the action and without any intention of action. Thus, Galileo looked for and discovered the (formal) law of falling bodies on inclined planes independently of any application. The same with geometry, at least as far as application goes: one may want to understand, and actually come to understand, the formal constant properties of geometric figures independently of any application thereof .

Now, I claimed above that there is no such a thing as speculative knowledge, and that Aris should have eliminated it. Is this not in direct contradiction? Am I not  discovering that there is, after all, such a thing as speculative knowledge? Let us see.

The essential characteristic of speculative knowledge is correspondence or similitude to reality. But there doesn’t seem to be any specular correspondence between Galileo’s mathematical law and the actual fall of bodies . There is, rather, an act of understanding of the constant features of an observed phenomenon, capable of generating hypothetical predictions for the future on the basis of the intelligible and understood regularity observed in the past.  Each fall is a particular case whose intelligible regularity is expressed  by the mathematical formula. “The fit to reality,” to use Searle’s expression, is not specular but practical, in that it is  generative of an intellectual practice of  problem-solving, that is, of dealing with observed or hypothetical concrete cases of falling bodies. This intellectual practice in its turn, when the agent is  in situation of exposure, will lead the agent to an actual expectation and response using the expected as a means to achieve his or her goal . Indeed,  how did Galileo know that his law was ‘true’? Not by the “aha!” of mere intellectual assent, but by problem-solving, that is, by finding that what he had understood allowed him not only to ‘explain,’ that is, to grasp the intelligibility of  the observable data in all the cases he had observed in the past, but also to predict correctly the observable data in cases he had never previously observed.–the observable data with which agents would be confronted as objects for deliberation and response.  And in fact problem-solving, not the “aha!” of evidence,  is the way in which any teacher of physics tests whether the students have really understood, whatever they may claim. And out of the classroom and the laboratory, in real engineering life, the test will be the actual transformation of the environment to tailor it to the needs of people.

Thus the ‘truth’ in this case  is not a matter of pictorial correspondence. In fact, it is meant to be grasped denotatively and applicatively so as to inform action, although it was not necessarily discovered with that interest in mind. Actually,  the connection with ‘the real’ world was never quite severed. Indeed, the observation and measurements were not imaginary but performed on actual perceptual data, so that the intelligibility is of phenomena of the real world, and the actual response of the agent will be to actual objects for response.  

Uniformities experienced and observed in the world by which we are surrounded are important for us ajs interested agents because they lead to uniformity in expectations, which in their turn tend to result in successful action. This attention to uniformities we have in common with animals. However, being linguistic, unlike animals, we are capable of attending to the uniformities as such and of grasping the intelligibility of the given uniformities, so that we can use expect them and respond to them them instrumentally for the realization of our purposes. Now, Aris and Aquinas take the understood law of uniformities as a specular grasp of the ontological nature  of a class of observable phenomena or actually existent realities. And, since they consider reality as something to be known contemplatively, they would rest satisfied with the contemplation of this ‘truth’ that supposedly corresponds to reality as, in Lonergan’s view, the knowledge of the truth of the judgment ‘Deus est’ corresponds to reality. Such ‘truth,’ however, is not generative of any intellectual or practical practice and, therefore, is not to be ‘verified’ in practice. In other words, this ‘truth’ is not to be understood applicatively but specularly. Here is where Galileo left behind the Aristotelian understanding of knowledge and truth. Discovering  reasons for uniformities may not be pursued for practical purposes but it is practical in that it is to be understood applicatively and may lead to more successful practice than mere animal expectation, as well as to instrumental control of our surroundings. At a later stage than Galileo’s own, within his own field of physics, whole systems of explanation (which fix more general expectations and render possible a more extended instrumental control) are developed. Such is the case at least in the natural sciences, where genuine uniformities obtain. This is what, to his disappointment, Aris missed in the domain of human action and particularly in ethical matters, where the applicable rule cannot be known-stated in advance..

The notions of (theoretical or contemplative) knowledge as similitude and of truth as known or guaranteed similitude have not appeared yet. Actually, they appear only when agents disagree, cease to be referred practically to the practical field by their statements, and wrongly take the statements themselves–be they explanatory or merely descriptive of regularity–for objects of theoretical consideration. (The more written language prevails, which was the case in Greece with Euclidean geometry and perhaps with written codes of laws, the more statements tend to be taken as objects of theoretical consideration, since they are not spoken and heard by agents in the mood of exposure). In that situation, the stated, which is actually meant  to refer practically, appears as knowledge-similitude; then what is in dispute is its truth-correspondence, because the stated has ceased to refer denotatatively and to be heard applicatively. Hence the practical-referential function of statements has been lost sight of, and statements have become objects of discussion as to their truth, that is,  as pictures of reality, which they are not and were never meant to be. Predication ceases to be potential practical reference and is interpreted as theoretical,  ontological predication, that is, as representation of reality, as implied by Aquinas in Peri Hermeneias .

Once this occurs, discussion as the search for knowledge not as believed correspondence but as certain truth is likely to come to occupy the center of the stage. Then the move towards theoretical evidence for assent and, consequently, towards demonstrative knowledge begins, because perceptual ostension cannot suffice to guarantee truth–correspondence. In that context, necessity and universality–and therefore the intellect against the senses, and cold objective reason against desire and interest-- become paramount, to the detriment of the perceptual-theoretical and of the perceptual-practical. This is what happened in the battle between the cynical rhetoricians and the lovers of wisdom. And the lovers of wisdom, for all their good intentions, are the ones who enthroned the notions of speculative knowledge and truth, and devoted all their energies to the justification of this view, within which they attempted to integrate practical ‘knowledge.’

In Aquinas’s view, there are two kinds of inner words meant by the outer words: either inner words that “significant [that is, mean] ipsum conceptum intellectus in quo est verum et falsum”–and these are judgments-statements, “enunciativa oratio”–, or inner words that [significant, that is, mean] quemdam ordinem ad hoc consequentem–and these are the speech-acts that, unlike statements, demand some kind of action. The other practical intentions expressed in speech-acts that are not statements presuppose the truth of the related satements and are parasitic on them: quemdam ordinem ad hoc consequentem .

Aquinas has the specular notion of knowledge-truth-reality. Hence he cannot conceive of direct linguistic reference to objects for deliberation and response whose outcome is not assent to what is stated but action.
