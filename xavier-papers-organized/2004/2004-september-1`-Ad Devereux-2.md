“Perception is influenced by motor activity from the outset, just as the latter is by the former. This is what we, for our part, have asserted when speaking of sensori-motor schemata in order to describe the simultaneously perceptual and motor assimilation which characterises the behavior of the infant.” (Piaget, Psychology of Intelligence, (Littlefield, Adams, and Company, Totowa, N.J., 1966), p. 87.

“... as though intelligence were an extension of those mechanisms which in their automatic form appear as habit. In connection with habit, we once again find the genetic schemata of association, of trial an error, and of assimilatory structuring.” ( Ibid. , p. 88).

“The formation of habit always involves a fundamental means-end relation; an action is never a succession of mechanically associated movement, but is directed towards a satisfaction, such as contact with food or releas3, e.g. Limena, when placed upside down return more an more rapidly to their normal position” (p. 89).

“The more we analyse the formation of habit as opposed to the automatic exercising of it, the more we realise the complexity of t he activities that come into play at the outset” (p. 89).

“But if the existence of conditioned behaviour is a fact..., its interpretation does not imply the reflexological associationism with which it is too often identified. When a response is associated with a perception there is more in t his connection than a passive association (i.e. becoming stamped in as a result of repetition alone) meanings also enter into it, since association occurs only in the presence of need and its satisfaction... a conditioned reflex is stabilised only as long as  it is confirmed or reinforced; a signal associated with food odes not five rise to an enduring reaction if real food is not periodically presented together with the signal. Association thus comes to be part of a complex piece of behaviour, which starts from a need an finishes with the satsifaction (actual, anticipated, or make-believe, etc). This amounts to saying that it is not a case of association in the classical sense of the term, but rather a of the constitution of a complex schema bound with a meaning” (p. 81).

“Thus ... the conditioned  response is not the simple substitution of responses originally due to a simple reflex, but new behavior which arrives at stability only by a structuring of the whole environment” (92).

“Adaptation to reality means an assimilation of the subject’s schemata... In its finished state, a mental experiment is the reproduction in thought, not of reality, but of actions or operations which affect it ” (p. 93).

“Claparede thus goes so far as to interpret the conditioned reflex in terms of implication. Pavlov’s dog salivates at the sound of ta bell after having heard it at the same time as he saw food, because then the bell ‘implies’ food. This gradual reversal of the trial and error theory [from blind activity] is worth careful examination” (p. 96).

“... does a black cat, seen by an infant, involve immediately from its first perception the relation ‘cat implies black’?... This is not a necessary kind; it is the beginning of a possible schema which, however, will only engender relations felt to be necessary as long as they form a genuine schema through transposition or generalisation (i.e. an application to new elements), in short, by introducing an assimilation. The assimilation, then, is the source of what Claparede calls implication. .. The subject will not arrive at the relation ‘A implies x’ on first perceiving an A with quality x, but will be led to the relation ‘A implies x’ inasmuch as he assimilates A2 to the schema (A), this schema being created precisely by the assimilation A2 = a. The dog that salivates at the sign of food will not salivate in this way at the sound of a bell unless he assimilates it, as a sign or a part of the total act, to the schema of this action. Claparede has good reason to say that repetition does not engender implication but that it appears only in the course of repetition, since implication is the internal product of the assimilation that ensures the repetition of the external act. Now this necessary intervention of assimilation further support the reservation that Claparede was himself induced to make with regard to the general role of trial and error. Firstly, it is obvious that when trial and error occurs, it cannot be explained in mechanical terms. Mechanically, that is to say with t he hypothesis of simple traces, error should be reproduced as often as successful trials. If such is not the case, if t he law of  effect ‘ holds, it means that the subject anticipates his failures and successes. In other words, each trial operates on the next, not as a channel opening to new responses, but as a schema enabling meanings to be attributed to subsequent trials. So trial and error in no way excludes assimilation. But this is not all. The very first trials are difficult to reduce to simple chance. In maze experiments, Adams finds respsonses directed from the outset. Dennis and Dashiel lay stress on the continuation of the sets initially adopted. Tolman and Krechevsky even speak of ‘hypotheses’ in describing the behavior of rats... As for Tolman, he brings out the role of generalisation in the formation of habits themselves. Thus, when an animal is placed on a new maze different form the one known to it, it perceives general analogies and applies to the new case the behavior that met with success in the previous  case (particular routes). So there is always complex structuring, but, for Tolman, the structures concerned are nos simple configurations in the sense of Kohler’s theory; they are sign-gestalts ,, i.e. schemata provided with meanings... Thus from elementary learning to intelligence, there seems to be involved an assimilatory activity which is necessary to the sturctuingf of the most passive forms of habits (conditioned responses and associative transfers) as it is to t he unfolding of the visible manifestations of activity (directed trial and error... Just as perceptual activity is not identical with intelligence, but links up with it a soon as it is freed from centering on the immediate and present object, so the assimilatory activity   that engenders habits is not th  same as intelligence but lead to the later as soon as irreversible and isolated sensori-motor systems are differentiated and coordinated in mobile articulations. Besides this, ther affinity between these two kinds of activity is obvious, since perceptions and habitual responses are constantly unite in complex schemata, and since the1111 ‘transfer’ or generalisation characteristic of habit is the exact equivalent, on the motor side, of ‘transposition’ in the domain of spatial figures, both involving the same generalized assimilation” (pp. 97-99).

I want to explore Devereux’s and Aristotle’s mistake in considering as “practical

sciences or arts,” medicine, ethics, politics, poetics, and rhetoric when both of them are referring to the written treatises or textbooks on those subject, not to the actual practices of the agents. In fact, however,  the study of the corresponding treatises does not result in action, which is what makes different the theoretical from the practical sciences. Aristotle confuses the statements he makes about ethical matters in his Ethics , when he reflects, with the agent’s ethical practice. My purpose is to investigate the origin of this confusion.

That Aristotle considered his Ethics a practical science in the sense of resulting in action is certain, for he himself declared that explicitly when he said that the aim of his treatise was not to obtain the assent of his readers to his knowledge of what virtue is, but their virtuous action. ^(F1)  For his part, Devereux himself aptly distinguishes between the practitioner of medicine, who learned his trade through practice, from the medical student who learned it from textbooks that correspond to Aristotle’s treatise on ethics. The latter’s knowledge his purely theoretical, for he has had no practice of medicine, whereas the former’s knowledge is gained through experience in the sense of  practice. But what does this mean, concretely?

Let us consider the case of the person who has learned to react to the syllables that come accompanied by an electric shock, but is incapable of telling which are those syllables. The knowledge he has acquired allows him to avoid those syllables, and consequently the shock. This is practical knowledge, in that it results in the action appropriate to achieve the desired goal, and this not by accident or chance. Compare this to the knowledge possessed by those who conduct the experiment. They can identify verbally the shocking syllables. Suppose, however, that these people, like totally inexperienced children,  have never experienced an electric shock and have no idea that electric shocks are painful. Will their verbal knowledge result in the action of avoiding the shocking syllables, or in any action at all? Of course not. Their knowledge is purely theoretical, that is, without any direct connection with action, with practical conduct. Moreover, one can wonder whether their verbal statements constitute any knowledge whatever, since they themselves do not understand what they mean when the say that the syllables produce shocks. In other words, their supposed knowledge of the shocking syllables does not refer them to anything, since they themselves have no idea of what they are referring or being referred to by the words. If they know syllables, they do not know them as shock-producing, which is what their language portends. They themselves do not understand what they say.

Let me backtrack. In de De Motu Animalium Aristotle raises the question why sometimes knowledge produces act ion –that is, is practical– and why sometimes not. His answer, according to Nussbaum, is that sometimes the knowledge is informed by desire, and sometimes not. But this is too vague. Desire of what? And  why only sometimes rather than always? And how does this relate to the difference between the tacit or not verbalized knowledge of the people exposed to the syllables and the verbalized knowledge of the designers of the experiment?

As a first approximation, what is the role played by language –that is, by verbalization in statements– in knowledge? It seems that in practical knowledge its role is not essential, since animals do not possess language and yet they are capable of practical knowledge. Indeed, animals would react to the syllables in a similar way as those who recognize the shocking syllables without being able to identify them linguistically. It seems, then, that practical knowledge is tacit, non-linguistic. Theoretical knowledge, by contrast, is essentially linguistic .

But is this answer sufficient? Are not all the knowing activities essentially linguistic? Is not Aristotle right in saying that only judgments and statements are susceptible of truth and falsity, for only they refer to something as being or not being the case? Let us be precise. For something to refer to what is the case, what is the case has to be explicitly formulated. Now, a desire is intentional but not cognitive-objective, for it refers the one who is desirous (but not others, unless it is expressed verbally to them –and not being desirous themselves, they are not referred the way the desirous agent is) to what is desired, but not as being or not being the case, but as desirable. As observed by Nussbaum, a desire can function as desire without being expressed linguistically, but a judgment cannot unless it is expressed verbally either internally or overtly. Animals have desires, but they neither do nor can nor need to express them linguistically in order for their desires to function as desires, for desires do not function as referring to what is the case but as informing the conduct of the desirous agent for him to achieve the desired goal.

To see this point more clearly, let us consider the case of animals or of babies, both of which lack language. A newborn baby wants milk. That is, it is born with an active disposition to drink milk. Psychologically, this disposition takes the form of the baby’s feeling of hunger. But the baby doesn’t know that it desires milk, of which it has no previous experience, and doesn’t need to know that enunciatively in order to desire milk. Thus desiring milk and knowing enunciatively that one desires milk are two different things. Ideally the baby’s hunger should result in the action of procuring milk. But that is impossible for the baby. It will eventually get milk with somebody else’s help. Now, as the baby grows in experience of the surrounding world and of itself because it has had its hunger satisfied by sucking the mother’s breast, it learns to actively procure the breast (in order to satisfy its hunger). But this learned or acquired activity is a purely bodily disposition genetically programmed, a disposition that the baby’s body acquires without, so to say, in any way passing through the knowing mind or objective consciousness of the baby. In other words, instinctively guided by its past experience of hunger, of procuring the breast, and of being satisfied, the baby will look for the breast whenever hungry, without in any way knowing-that looking for the breast and sucking it are means to achieve the goal of satisfying its hunger. At least at this early stage, then, practical or instrumental knowledge is tacit and totally different and independent from linguistic or enunciative knowledge. ^(F2)

If so, however, why call these two different things by the same name, ‘knowledge,’ which originally means correspondence between what is enunciatively judged by the knower and what is the case – none of which applies to practical knowledge? This, I think, is at the origin of the confusion that has plagued Western philosophy, compounded by the primacy attributed to theoretical knowledge and the subordination of practical to theoretical knowledge, by the Greek idea of scientific or necessarily true knowledge –all of which should have been destroyed by Kant’s critique, if he himself had been logical instead of sparing scientific knowledge under the pretext of its supposed intuitive character–, and by Aristotle’s invention of formal cause-nature-substance and accidents.

In his Ethics, which supposedly is a practical discipline (composed of statements)  bound to terminate in action, Aristotle confuses the way in which desire functions in a desirous agent who is pursuing a goal with the objective statements that refer predicatively or objectively to the desire or the conduct of the desirous agent. But the objective statement of or about a desire is not a desire and doesn’t function as a desire anymore than, as Sartre observes, the eye seen by the philosopher is, and can be identified with, the agent’s seeing eye, or the body spoken about by the physician – the body “entity”–  can be identified with the agent’s body, the body I am, “my body for me.” A desire actually functions as a desire when, under the appropriate circumstances, it guides or informs the conduct of the desirous agent to obtain the desired goal. That is why Monsieur de Rollebon ^(F3) instilled fear of hell in the dying person by speaking of hell in a way that appealed to the sensitivity and the imagination of the situated agent, thereby  provoking fear, rather than by making objective statements about hell.

Carthago delenda est romanis, sed non carthaginenisbus .  For a given state of affairs to reveal its coefficient of adversity or favorableness, a project is required on the part of the agent.

In short, there are ways of referring others through statements otherwise than referring to what is objectively the case. All those who are exposed to the surrounding world as affectible and  vulnerable because they have needs and desires that can be frustrated or satisfied by the world to which they are exposed, can be referred by others, and refer themselves, to the world in a different way than the cognitive way a conceived by Aristotle. And this is the case of all agents.

But let me return to the question: what is the role of language in practical knowledge?

I said that language is not essential, since animals do not possess language and yet are capable of practical knowledge. But we need to be precise. Animals do not possess enunciative language –  what Aristotle calls “enunciative speech” – (by contrast to communicative language). They cannot enunciate to themselves or to others what is the case, objectively. That is, they are incapable of making what Aristotle calls ‘judgments’ and communicative ‘statements’ to others. Certainly they are incapable of making judgments in the strict Aristotelian sense of the word, that is, as reflective acts that result from finding sufficient reason for believing and affirming or denying something. Actually, they don’t even know the something that is to be believed or disbelieved, since they are incapable of enunciating it to themselves. Nonetheless Aristotle grants them the capacity to perceive, and even admits that their acts of perception may be materially true, that is, actually correspond to what is the case. Thus, according to Aristotle, without being able to verbalize it, animals possess theoretical knowledge. I think this is nonsensical and shows how confused Aristotle is by language – not the language he uses but the language he observes with curiosity and reflects upon as a philosopher. It is true that animals perceive, but not they perceive objectively; they perceive ‘signally,’ instrumentally and tacitly. What they perceive is, for them, a signal for action, depending on their inherited and acquired reflexes. They grasp what they perceive in the way Susanne Langer says that animals understand names – with practical denotation but with no connotation – conceptual or otherwise.

To give a concrete example, fear  is the way in which the agent was referred to hell by Rollebon. The reference was practical, related to action. Hell was not meant by Rollebon predicatively or objectively, as object of mere assent, that is, as object to be known theoretically, as object without any particular existential relation to the subject, but as timendum, as to be responded to with fear which disposes the agent to act so as to avoid it.

Aquinas distinguishes two ways of being referred or related to the object: ex his queae sunt propria rei and ex dispositione agentis. In the former case, the object is considered in itself and by itself, abstracted from all concrete existential relation to the subject. This objectifying abstraction is made possible by the connotational character of language. In the latter case, by contrast , the existential relations of the affectible subject to the object, which are tacit, are taken into account by the exposed, desirous agent. Science limits itself to the former relationship of the object to the curious subject. But not, as Aristotle does, by concentrating on the concept of what the thing is and moving from there to a nature as substance, but by concentrating on how the thing acts and on the effects it produces, which refers indirectly to the way the agent’s dispositions enter into play, and which renders possible the return to the world of agency. Actually, this concentration on action by contrast to entity is the shift that inaugurates modern science with Galileo and separates it from Aristotelian science. Aristotelian science looks for the nature or substance of the object as the intelligible entity that supposedly corresponds to what is the case. Modern science looks for the regular activity of the object in the world in which the agent finds himself as vulnerable.  

The previous paragraph is not precise enough. What is what language makes possible?

Names, in the case of people, can function connotationally or denotationally. Connotationally , names or ‘substantives’ lead to ‘conceive’ an object, what they name. Denotationally or signally , when completed by a gesture or another contextual indicator, they announce the immediate or imminent presence of an object to a vulnerable, needful agent exposed to it. Because words connote, in the case of names, we ‘conceive’ “objects,” i. e., what they connote. But we –we people, by contrast to animals– conceive them by themselves, as concepts, without any direct relation to the subject. The objects-concepts evoked by names may be concrete or abstract, singular or universal, real or imaginary or purely conceptual. In ordinary perception, by contrast, we are potentially exposed to what we perceive, and we are tacitly indicated to ourselves as exposed to them. I.e., we normally perceive ex dispositione agentis,, whereas the conception evoked by a name is neutral as to the relation of what is perceived to the subject and opens the way to think of the object ex his quae sunt propria rei, one is in the mode of curiosity.

Names normally appear in sentences. The only sentences that are important to us for the present are statements. By the very character of the objects connoted by the names contained or  involved in the statement (singular and concrete-perceptible or general or universal, concrete or abstract, etc.), as well as by the circumstances in which the statments take place, statements ^(F4) indicate the possible relations of the addressee to the object. In the case of reference to perceptible objects in ORAL STATEMENTS, which are always in immediate or imminent presence of the speaker and the addressee to the object(s) named, the names of the object(s) are always singular and concrete. The  exposure of the experiencing-judging subject, who is an agent, is tacit, indexical, for all consciousness or experience of object, be it immediately acquired or linguistically received, involves a tacit consciousness (of) the subject in relation to it (and, in the case of communication, a tacit understanding or awareness by the speaker of the tacit relation of the addressee to the object named). This tacit consciousness depends on the agent’s inheritance and in his previous experience. And the actual relationship of the experiencing agent to the object named, which presupposes a concrete goal, is hodological, that is, instrumental, involving relations of power of the subject to the object and vice versa: “I can,” or “I cannot.”
