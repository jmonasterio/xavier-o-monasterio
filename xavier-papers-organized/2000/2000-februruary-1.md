2000-february-1

On James Fallom, the Trinity, and all other things – “freedom, equality, intersubjectivity, reciprocalness, love, knowledge, etc.”

A number of comments in Professor Fallom’s paper  refer to the history of ancient philosophy (Socrates, Plato, Aristotle) in relation to Aquinas’s views. While these comments may be of interest to you who are teaching a course on Aquinas, I don’t think that would be a good focus for the colloquium in your department.

I haven’t read the paper you sent to Fallom. I gather, however, that your paper was on the Holy Trinity, and that your intention was to see what your philosophy teacher, a Catholic with a Catholic sensitivity, could contribute on topics like freedom, equality, etc. that had been prominent in your paper and that had been treated by modern thinkers.  

Now you ask me, on the basis of my reading of Professor Fallom’s reflections to suggest

what he might talk about in the colloquium. Obviously my suggestion is tainted by my interests,

my preferences, and my personal experience in the field of philosophy.

I think that Fallom is right in claiming that up to Kant (and Kant included) the very notion of intersubjectivity is absent. Now, without that absolutely central notion, all the other topics, knowledge included, take a totally different meaning.  Not for nothing there is a rift between the

biblical conception of “heart” and the Greek conception of knowing. I would suggest that Fallom begin by stating his claim about the absence of the notion of intersubjectivity (with some exploration, if possible of the reasons for this long absence as well as its significance for the topics

you gave him), and then that he develop the views that he begins presenting on p. 16 of his paper.

Such as “...when the child says “father”... he sees [and is therefore related to} the man differently than the mother... or the neighbor... The question that has to be asked is “How does the judge (the Supreme court) see the child? or the fetus?” I think it is important for the father and the mother to say of the fetus “That’s my child.” “ [Couldn’t these considerations be extended to what we say of God and what God says of us?] Also, on the same page: “St Thomas began the Summa with five proofs for the existence of God and it can be asked whether the first mover, the unmoved mover, is god or God? Is the speaker (‘This is what we call God’) a pagan or a saint?”   [I think that a question like this couldn’t have occurred to Saint Thomas, for the very same reason

that the notion of intersubjectivity was absent from his philosophical-theological horizon].

Well, Dennis, I tried. I hope that my suggestions are of some help.

Xavier
