            Aristotle: Speculative and Practical Reason

            Aristotle: Scientific reason and practical reason
                 "I have so far said  little  about  how  this  account  of
           Aristotle's philosophical/scientific method, constructed largely
           from the Metaphysics and the specific scientific  treatises,  is
           to be put together with the account of scientific  understanding
           developed in the Posterior  Analytics.  Two  pressing  questions
           might be raised at this point. The first concerns the  Analytics
           ideal of a finished science as a hierarchical deductive  system:
           how does this norm cohere with Aristotle's aims  and  procedures
           in the appearance-saving passages on which I have drawn? This is
           clearly a huge question, which  can  barely  be  broached  here.
           Butwe can provisionally say that  the  appearance-saving  method
           could be fully compatible with the Analytics demand that, in the
           natural sciences (as opposed to ethics), the  expert  should  in
           the end be able to validate his claim to understanding by giving
           systematic demonstrations of the type described.  The  two  aims
           would  be  compatible  if  the  deductive  ideal  were  seen  as
           something  that  arises,  itself,  from   the   appearances,   a
           commitment which we believe ourselves to undertake  when  we  do
           science. And this,  in  fact,  is  how  Aristotle  presents  his
           account of episteme there:  as  an  articulation  of  what  'we'
           believe scientific understanding shoud be and do. He begins from
           an  account  of  the  conditions  under  which  'we'  dthink  we
           understand something, and goes  on  to  whow  what  this  shared
           conception requires of the scientist. ... Aristotle is concerned
           to show how his norm arises out of the appearances and  embodies
           their requirements. In ethics, on the other hand, he takes pains
           to argue that our beliefs  about  practice  do  not  yield   the
           demandfor a deductive system. He isevidently not  interested  in
           assimilating the appearances to a theoretical  ideal  where  the
           appearances themselves do not reveal a  commitment  to  such  an
           ideal.
                 "But a more troublesome question arises when  we  consider
           that the first principles of science in the Analytics have  been
           thought  by  centuries  of  commentators,   via   the   medieval
           tradition, to be a priori truths  grasped  by  special  acts  of
           intellectual intuition, apart from all  experience.  Surely,  we
           might obgject, the finished structure of an Aristotelian science
           rests on these, and not, ultimately, on the appearances. Or,  if
           the scientific works do rest on appearances, they depart, in  so
           placing themselves, from the ideal set forth in the Analytics.
                 "The objector and I can agree on a number of points  about
           the principles mentioned in the analytics: that they are  to  be
           true, indemonstrable, necessary, primary, both  prior  and  more
           knowable than the conclusion; that they transmit their truth  to
           the conclusion; even (as it will  turn  out)  that  they  are  a
           priori according to some understanding of the a priori. But this
           leaves, it is plain, much scope for disagreeement:  for  a  deep
           and basic human appearance can be all of  those  thinggs,  as  I
           shall show; and to say this about a principle commits us neither
           to special acts of rationaql intuition, nor to the  notion  that
           the principles are true outside of all conceptual  schemes,  all
           language.  The  objector,k  it  emerges,  derives  these   extra
           elements of this famous interpretation from an  exiguous  amount
           of evidence, especially fromsome alleged evidence  in  Posterior
           Analytics (II, 19) . [Fortunately Kosman, Lesher,  and  Burnyeat
           in their work on nous (intellect) and  episteme  (understanding)
           in the Analytics...]. We move from  the  confused  mass  of  the
           appearances to a perspicuous ordering, from the grasp that  goes
           with use to the ability to give accounts. There is no reason  to
           posit  two  philosophical  methods  here,   one   dealing   with
           appearances, one resting on the a priori;  dialectic  and  first
           philosophy have,as Aristotle insists in Metaphysics IV,  2  (cf.
           below) exactly the same subject matter. The  appearances,  then,
           can go all the way down" (Nussbaum, 250-1).
      As far as the scholastics are concerned, I doubt that the evidence  is
so scant. The  Aristotelian  notion  of  judgment  and  the  coalescence  of
judgments (cf Lonergan) seems ample evidence. The taxonomies of science  and
the ultimate good, that Nussbaum dismisses or defuses (P.  374),  count  for
something. What can barely be doubted, at any rate, is that the  scholastics
read Aristotle in that way. Part of my job is to  demonstrate  this  by  the
objections of the non-Aristotelians and anti-Aristotelians and by  the  ways
out found by Bonaventure and Aquinas.
      Another thing that seems to me indubitable is that Aquinas placed  the
act of faith, at least of 'the formal object of faith",  in  the  sphere  of
the speculative, not of the practical intellect.  And  I  suspect  that  his
objectors were arguing from the  'Catholic  appearances'  of  the  agent.  I
think the latter were right. But this has created endless  problems  in  the
tension between the way we live our faith and the way we think our faith.
      Finally, I may have an interesting  beginning  in  the  passage  where
Aquinas talks about theology being a theoretical and  a  practical  science.
For this rejoins Nussbaum's confusion between
the non-scientific science she and Aristotle want and what the  agent  does.
I will deal  with  this.  But,  first,  some  important  points  related  to
Nussbaum.
Nussbaum and my thesis
      Perhaps what  disconcerts  me  most  in  Nussbaum  is  her  systematic
ignorance of episteme and its relationship  to  phronesis,  as  well  as  of
epistemic questions  of  truth.  She  collapses  theoretical  and  practical
reason under the saving of appearances. Truth is what at  any  time  appears
to be the best ordering, but with an element of  correctibility  that  comes
with the unexpected, with the not previously seen (reality?).  This  implies
that the truth-validity of speculative and practical  judgments  is  of  the
same  kind.  This  is  hard  to  accept.  Aristotle  did  not   think   that
propositions  of  geometry,  which  are  universal   and   necessary,    and
propositions which are "true only for  the  most  part,"  possess  the  same
truth-validity. He might, if Nussbam is right,  which  is  disputable,  have
thought that both in physics and in ethical reflection  the  method  is  the
same: reaching the  best  appearances.  But  in  physics  "appearances"  are
merely observed and abstracted (phantasma),  which  are  the  same  for  all
understanders and do not involve the  vulnerability  of  the  observed.  Inn
physics, therefore, there is theorizing, strictly explanatory,  "scientific"
understanding; in ethical reflection [explanation of ethical phenomena;  not
rules for action--two people who agree with Aris on  ethical  phenomena  may
disagree with him as  to  the  best  life]  there  is  no  inductive  theory
mediated by explanatory understanding. In other words,  the  intelligibility
of the phenomenon is not of the same kind.
      The question of truth is  vital.  It  seems  that  what  I  have  been
calling "the thing in itself" is, in Nussbaum's  interpretation,  the  thing
for any human who understands, "the thing for reason". This  is  the  object
of  speculative  reason--episteme  as  theoretical  understanding   and   as
theoretically understood, that  is,  as  "scientific  knowledge."  Does  the
"realism of forms" of which Nussbaum speaks correspond to this? It does  not
quite seem so. She seems to assimilate it not to the  unrevisable  forms  of
geometry, of the uninvolved subject, but  to  the  revisable  forms  of  the
ethics, of the imprecise or for the most part universals.  My  question  is:
what is the kind of truth involved in the latter? The  thing  for  involved,
threatened, vulnerable humans? This is the the truth  after  which  Nussbaum
seems to be  when  she  reflects  in  her  "mixed  style."  She  wants  what
Aristotle wanted: understanding. But is the truth of action in  hitting  the
target, or in a seeing a la Nussbaum? All theory in the world  cannot  reach
perfect foreseeance  because  the  future  is  contingent,  because  we  are
involved in history in the making, not involved in a theory or  in  a  world
that corresponds to a theory. Does Aristotle  have  a  view  of  truth  that
corresponds to phronesis, as he has a realism of forms that  corresponds  to
episteme, to propositional truth that expresses categorical judgments?
      Whether or not Aristotle does, let us assume--as I believe it was  the
case with the scholastics--that someone  buys  the  Aristotle  of  Posterior
Analytics. Truth  is  a  matter  of  judgments  exclusively.  Truth  is  the
correspondence between the mind and its  object.  The  appetitive  does  not
grasp the real, it tends toward the  real  as  good--as  good  perceived  as
good,  or  as  good  understood  as  good.  Perception  and  intellect   are
cognitional, epizumia and boulesis  are  appetitive,  non-cognitional.  They
cannot appetize but on the basis of cognition.  There  are,  therefore,  two
kinds of knowledge and of judgments: theoretical (what is  true  for  reason
and corresponds to what is the case for any knower, whose paradigmatic  case
is God, Prima Veritas), and practical (what is the case for the human  being
as appetizer, not necessarily what appeals to the appetizer  as  perceiver).
Practical judgments  are  species-relative  (what  faith  gives  us  is  the
ultimate goal as real and some of  the  general  means  as  commanded),  but
their truth is of the same kind--correspondence between judged and reality--
as that of speculative judgments. Even  if  particular  practical  judgments
are context-dependent and,  therefore,  cannot  be  the  direct  product  of
theoria, there is, as Nussbaum says even of Aristotle, one [TRUE,  AT  LEAST
ASSYMPTOTICALLY] plan of best life for human beings (p. 305).
      This plan is experimental and reformable, for Nussbaum. But  the  plan
as a telos of the understanding is  not.  There  is  such  a  thing  as  the
species-good for all human beings,  in  relation  to  which  the  particular
judgments of agents are true  or  false  and  their  actions,  consequently,
right or wrong. Philosophical deliberation is about this plan as a goal.
      A concrete example might help. The goal of a war is victory  over  the
enemy. The possibilities of the planning general are partly ontological  and
partly context dependent. A soldier is by definition someone  who  wants  to
win the war, but how many soldier the general has, how many the  enemy  has,
and in what terrain and with which weapons is  context  for  the  plan.  The
first is ontological and  the  second  context-dependent.  The  plan  is  to
soften the Irakis and then to kill them in  a  ground-attack.  The  strategy
for each sortie of planes determines the type of  planes  and  the  type  of
weapons to be used. Assume the ideal case that the army moves like the  body
of the projecting general: it does exactly what the general  wants  them  to
do. Yet, the general cannot make decisions  for  each  soldier,  for  these,
too, are context dependent. The most he can give them is the  strategy,  the
particular goal, for each sortie, for each action. We have in  this  case  a
single and simple goal shared by all.  Let  us  call  it  ontological.  (The
goals to be pursued by human beings: what every body,  abstractly  ought  to
want). The plan itself  is  context-dependent:  to  use  planes  presupposes
planes and an enemy that cannot  prevent  all  of  them  from  causing  some
damage. Does the plan help? It is better than nothing. But  another  general
might have come with another, better plan, for it is  context-dependent  and
thesalient features may be otherwise grasped. Thus it is  by  decision  that
the "wisest" general is chosen and his plan  adopted,  when  there  is  some
implicit agreement, shared goals by which the  ability  of  the  general  to
pursue and achieve them has been demonstrated and assessed. But  what  if  I
do not share the goals of General Aristotle even in reflective  equilibirum?
What if for me he is not the wisest planner, for he  never  demonstrated  to
be the wisest agent, much less the wisest agent within the story to which  I
belong?
      Let us grant that Aristotle did not put episteme and  phronesis  under
the same genus,  Knowledge.  If  knowledge  is  identified  with  scientific
knowledge, then phronesis  is  not  knowledge.  And  if  I  take  scientific
knowledge  for  knowledge  tout  court,  I  will  dismiss  phronesis.   Yet,
Aristotle compares, disparagingly, phronesis to episteme, or  at  least  the
imprecise universals to the precise ones. He has a comparative  taxonomy  or
taxonomies.  So  the  very  hypothesis  is  suspect.  Or  perhaps  what  the
hypothesis suggests is that phronesis is cognitional in a totally  different
way, to which I am sympathetic. But this amounts to  saying  that  truth  in
phronetic matters is of a totally different kind. I believe  the  latter  to
be the  case.  Truth,  here,  is  more  like  success,  realization  of  the
desirable,  not  contemplative  satisfaction  of  the  intellect.  But   the
desirable is conceivable only within a shared  story  (not  theory)  in  the
making,  with  a  contingent  future.  That  is  what  Nussbaum   means   by
vulnerability.
      Now, anybody who believes in episteme, as the  scholastics  did,  will
prefer epistemic  knowledge  to  phronetic  knowledge.  And  will  find  the
phronetic as not knowledge, by  the  definition  of  knowledge  involved  in
episteme. If the good is not to be ignored, he will conflate the  good  with
the true, as Plato did. And he will despise those who care about  individual
matters. Or, as  Aristotle  did,  will  in  practical  matters  renounce  to
episteme, with regret. And, if as Nussbaum says, sometimes it  is  difficult
to know whether it is a matter of episteme or of phronesis,  he  will  leave
open the project of reducing everything to a single episteme,  as  scientism
(and Rorty) do. Or, he will, as the scholastics did, think that there is  an
episteme in practical matters at a certain level  of  abstraction  ("natural
law") by reference to  which  concrete  decisions  and  actions  are  to  be
assessed.
      There is, however, another possibility: there is,  strictly  speaking,
no episteme. Episteme is the illusion  created  by  an  abstraction  from  a
story seen, not from  a  story  lived.  Since  objects  do  not  live  their
actions, there is no difference or distance between lived and seen in  their
case.  This,  plus   the   consequent   regularity   of   their   abstracted
interactions, creates the illusion of episteme. In no case is  knowledge  of
the singular correspondence between the  understood  and  the  actual.  This
possibility,  so  poorly  formulated  here,  is  the  one  that  I  consider
acceptable.
