Essay. 7                                Chantilly, May 26, 93
                 Applying Frei: Another Attempt

                       "Theologia est philosophia Christianorum;
                       philosophia est theologia paganorum."

      According to Frei, in the eighteenth century a whole way of reading-
understanding the Bible was lost, which he intends to rescue because he
considers it fundamental. Why it is fundamental he never quite explains.
Nor, in my opinion, can he adequately explain, since his strategy involves
a systematic separation of the religious from the narrative reading of the
Bible. This   implies that there is such a thing as a narrative reading of
the Bible that is neither religious nor non-religious, with which view I
disagree.
      True, without reading the Bible narratively one cannot read it
religiously, but one cannot read it narratively without reading it either
religiously or non-religiously. And if one does not read it religiously,
one cannot read it realistically but only as fiction. In other words, one
cannot read the Bible in the way that makes it possible for oneself to
situate onself within the story, as Frei acknowledges that people used to
situate themselves and regrets that they don't and cannot anymore as long
as the eclipse of the narrative reading persists. On this he is right, for
reading the Bible narratively is being projected into an inhabitable world,
which is one of the senses of the word 'realistic.' However, reading the
Bible religiously is actually
inhabiting the biblical world, whereas reading the Bible non-religiously is
participating in that world only imaginarily--not as a vulnerable, exposed
agent within that world--, and therefore not really inhabiting it, for that
world is only imaginarily inhabitable, not really habitable. This is why,
as Frei himself suspects, Barth would be unhappy with his bracketting of
the realism of Barth's narrative reading of the Bible. At least in this
sense, then Frei, unlike Barth, is a child of the Enlightenment.
      On one point, though, Frei is right. There is a radical difference
between a narrative reading of the Bible and the Enlightenment's reading of
the Bible, which is not narrative but scientialis reading. But this is not
to say enough. For it is constitutive of the Enlightenment's reading of the
Bible that its own reading is what genuinely realistic reading is. In other
words, only what qualifies as real under the filtering effected by the
Enlightenment's typical reading of the Bible is what is genuinely real in
the Bible, while all the rest is fictitious. Thus, what the Enlightenment
brought about was not simply a different way of reading the Bible, but a
prescriptive way of reading the Bible meant to grasp what is real in the
Bible, something that no other reading can possibly achieve. In fact, then,
contrary to what Frei seems to suggest, the Enlightenment does neither
suppress nor oppose the narrative reading of the Bible; it simply relegates
narrative understanding-reading to the category of the fictitious, of the
non-real. But this is to say that what the Enlightenment definitely
suppresses is the religious, the Christian and Jewish reading of the Bible,
for the latter reading is realistic, that is, takes for real what according
to the Enlightenment is not and cannot be real. Because Frei does not seem
to have noticed this, he has left the fundamental problem untouched. Frei
rendered legitimate and desirable, say, Kermode's narrative reading of the
Bible as a reading that captures what the Enlightenment's reading cannot
capture, namely, an imaginarily inhabitable world, a fictitious world. He
did not make possible, much less legitimate, a Christian reading of the
Bible, such as Barth's reading. On the contrary, he hid the difference
between the religious and the non-religious reading of the Bible by
assuming that a narrative reading is possible which is neither religious
nor non-religious.
      My project consists in achieving what Frei did not achieve. Not in
the sense of rendering the Christian reading of the Bible prescriptive for
everybody, but prescriptive and legitimate for Christians, possible and
nominally or theoretically legitimate for others. For at the present time,
by our very Christian practice we Christians inhabit the biblical world,
while qua intellectuals corrupted by the Enlightenment we forbid ourselves
as illegitimate the religious reading of the Bible.
      Realizing this project will demand much time. For the moment I will
limit myself to rendering intelligible the resistance of the Augustinians
to Aristotelianism--that is, to the transformation of theology into a
science of the Aristotelian type-- by means of a reinterpretation of their
objections.
      The pre-Aristotelian reading of the Bible, what we might call the
Augustinian reading and I called above the religious reading, was of the
kind characterized by Frei as common before the eclipse. The Aristotelian
reading of the Bible made possible and inevitable the Enlightenment's
reading and was of the kind characterized by Frei as common (and normative)
after the eclipse. What happened between the twelfth and the thirteenth
century was the transformation of the former into the latter, though the
consequences of delegitimization of the religious reading were not drawn by
its main perpetrators, not even clearly foreseen (though vaguely sensed and
feared by some), much less willed. My task consists, therefore, in the
first place, in showing the correctness of this hypothesis by bringing to
the fore the correspondence between the Augustinian and the Aristotelian
moment and the characteristics attributed by Frei to the pre-Enlightenment
and the Enlighthenment moment respectively. If such correspondence obtains,
all I will have shown in this first step is that Frei's analysis of events
has to be antedated and that its original version is to be found in the
encounter of the West with the Aristotelian corpus. But this will lay the
needed foundation for the intelligibility of the further steps.
      I will take a stab at this in "Essay. 8." and in what follows here.

What is the difference between the modus scientialis and the modus
narrativus?
      Ricoeur suggests that the fundamental difference between the modus
narrativus (within which what Frei calls "realistic narratives" finds its
place) and the modus scientialis is that the former "refers" the reader to
an inhabitable world. Whether the world, and therefore the reference, is
merely imaginary or real, has to do both with the events narrated and with
the intersection between what Ricoeur calls "the world of the reader" and
"the world of the text."
      For the text to be susceptible of a narrative understanding by a
"competent reader," that is, for the text to "refer" to an inhabitable
world, it has to be narrative. This means, I think, that it has to be
"dramatic," to portray a vulnerable agent-patient exposed to his/her
surroundings with a contingent future.
Having read the text or otherwise knowing the events narrated, the future
of the agent may not be contingent for the reader, but it 'was' contingent
for the agent. (Hence the typical 'past of narratives').
      Whether or not the events narrated will or can be taken by a
competent reader as real rather than as imaginary depends on how the world
to which the text refers and the events narrated "intersect with the world
of the reader."
      What is "the world of the reader" remains to be spelled out in more
detail.
      The distinctive effect of narrative texts as such, or at least of the
narrative texts that interest me here, is "emotive participation" by the
reader. Charlemagne could aptly say "They wouldn't have crucified Him if I
had been there with my Francs," as someone could aptly say "They would not
have executed Socrates if I had been there." One can even be moved--in the
sense of "emotive participation"--by a realistic but not historical text,
as some people are moved by a movie. By contrast, a scientialis text--be it
composed of a single sentence or a combination thereof--neither produces
nor is by its nature meant to produce "emotive participation" as its
effect. Why not? And what is the distinctive effect of scientiales texts?
      A scientialis text is a text that by its nature and by the (at least
supposed) intention of its author exclusively produces as its distinctive
effect "information" or "knowledge," that is,
mere intellectual assent to what the text asserts.
      If a text is narrative in that it narrates and tends to produce
emotive participation, a scientialis text is scientialis in that it asserts
and tends to produce intellectual assent. A narrator may announce his/her
narrative intention by saying "I am going to tell you a story." If the
author of a scientialis text is to announce his/her intention concerning
the forthcoming text to be uttered, he/she will say, "I say or state or
assert that..."
      Typical of a scientialis text by contrast to other assertive but not
scientiales texts is either the supposition that what is asserted is
supported by evidence (ideally by evidence necessitating assent), which can
be produced to satisfy the reader if needed, or the actual furnishing of
what makes the asserted evident in this way.
      Typical of the scientialis genre is "evidentialism," that is, the
conviction that only what is supported by evidence--ideally by assent-
necessitating evidence--is true and, in the case of assertions of existence-
-real. This implies that what is not so supported is not true and, in the
case of assertions of existence, not real.
      "Evidence," in the scientialis genre, is a correlate of assent, of
mere assent. Only that can be true or real which can be assented to, and
only that can be assented to which is evident, that is, seen by the eye or
understood on the basis of what is seen and is accessible to all the eyed
and intelligent, universally accessible. From this it follows that what
cannot be seen or understood in this way cannot be true or real. In other
words, only what can be seen or understood in this way is objective (that
is, true or real)--genuinely signified by statements, genuinely stated--and
all the rest is illusory, imaginary, subjective.
      The case of the Greek scientialis genre is still complicated by the
intellectualism of philosophers, for whom truth implied eternal, atemporal
necessity. Hence all that is transitory belongs in the realm of "opinion,"
not of episteme, and is therefore, even if perhaps true, unimportant,
worthy only of the attention of those who live in the cave. (Yet the
lovers' of wisdom original concern was practical, not theoretical wisdom!).
      Application to the medievals' quandary. The scientialis genre
presupposes a justificatory theory of knwledge, of language, and of reality
that renders imperative the scientialis practice and results in sicnetiales
texts. A scientialis text, as such, can only be the speech act of someone
who has 'bought' that theory, who is a competent scientialis speaker. The
medieval theologians found themselves confronted with the Aristotelian
corpus of scientiales texts which contained both the justificatory theory
and concrete accomplishments formulated in scientiales texts that relfected
(or were supposed to reflect)  the application of the theory.  For their
part, the medieval theologians themselves possessed no other central text
than the Bible (accompanied by the Patristic writings) and no other
justificatory 'theory' than that the Bible was true and infallibly so.
Having 'bought' the Aristotelian theory for lack of one of their own, and
being certain of the truth of the Bible, they were constrained to submit
the truth of their text (with the necessary accommodations to safeguard the
non-evidentialist or authoritative character of their text) to the
strictures of the Aristotelian theory of science.
      Actually, however, neither did the text of the Bible possess the
characteristics of a scientialis text, nor was their Christian reading of
the Bible scientialis at all.
      That the text of the Bible was not scientialis is obvious. However,
the Bible was true. Hence, for those theologians who had 'bought' the
Aristotelian theory, this characteristic of the Bible had to be
superficial, a mere matter of a different mode of expression, for the Bible
had to be a scientialis text, given that they possessed no alternative
theory capable of accommodating the truth of the Bible. Yet, even the most
Aristotelian theologians, at least before Aquinas, balked before the
difficulty, for the biblical text was not exclusively composed of
statements and was not argumentative but, rather, composed of an enormous
variety of speech acts and literary genres. Now, not only did they fail at
reducing that variety of speech acts to statements, let alone scientiales
statements, and thatvariety ofliterary genres to the argumentative genre,
but they more or less obscurely sensed that even if that were possible it
would be undesirable, for it could not be achieved without significant loss
in the text's meaning.
      This is connected with the second point, namely, that their Christian
reading of the Bible was not scientialis. What was it, then, and why?
      If they were relatively alert to the non-scientialis character of the
biblical text, which has to do with the obvious variety of speech acts and
literary genres in the text itself, they seem to have had not even a hint
of a theory of reading that would have allowed them to articulate their own
resistance to their resistance to Aristotelianism. We have, therefore, to
supply to this deficiency in order to render their resistance intelligible
and, hopefully, justified.

      Both the narrator and the author of a scientialist text "say
something."

an imaginarily inhabitable world, as being an actually inhabited world.
From this it flows, concerning the sense of a text, of what a text means, a
further distinction cncerning the (2) Literal. For this distinction to
appear we have to attend to the privileged status accorded to statements by
