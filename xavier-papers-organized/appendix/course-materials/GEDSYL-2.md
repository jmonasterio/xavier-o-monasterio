PHL 103-01: INTRO TO PHILOSOPHY

INSTRUCTOR: XAVIER O. MONASTERIO

Course description

The substantive part of the course will consist in the examination of four significantly different philosophies: Skinner's, Sartre's, Marx's, and Maslow's. We will use these philosophies as a sounding board to begin the process of finding out how to live our own lives. Appreciating the merits and demerits of these philosophies will furnish each of us, through personal reflection, the opportunity to start developing our own philosophy, which is the whole point of philosophizing. To Be Human , by Xavier Monasterio, is the basic textbook in which these four  philosophies are expounded. Socrates' "Apology," reproduced in The Trial and Death of Socrates by Plato, will provide us with a concrete model of a person who philosophizes. Two additional readings-- A Brave New World , by Aldous Huxley, and Man's Search for Meaning , by Viktor Frankl, will contribute to our realization that different philosophies do in fact result in different ways of living and that not all ways of living are equally acceptable and worthwhile.

Relationship to the themes of the Integrated Humanities Base

Skinner's philosophy pushes to an extreme the undeniable influence of one's culture to the point of suppressing totally the individual's freedom and responsibility. Sartre's philosophy, on the other hand, pushes to an extreme the individual's ability to escape all cultural influence through reflection to the point of making the individual a totally autonomous, self-legislating creature with no other responsibility than to himself or herself.  Both of these philosophies are a-historical, as if the level of material and cultural development of the society to which a person belongs either had nothing to do with what the individual is and is capable of becoming (Sartre), or as if it determined absolutely what the individual is and is capable of becoming (Skinner). Marx introduces the all-important factor of historical development in terms of the social conditions that make possible or impossible the emergence of the person as a free and responsible individual as well as of a genuine human community of interdependent persons. Marx thinks that the present material conditions that characterize capitalism render impossible the existence of real persons and of genuine human communities, but he predicts their emergence, under the changed conditions of communism, as the natural telos of our still alienated humanness. For his part, Maslow contends that the existence of free, responsible individuals and of genuine human communities are possible now, however imperfectly, in the measure in which each of us strives to grow up to his or her full human stature by satisfying the basic human needs and overcoming the pseudo-needs created by our defective cultures.

This course, therefore, whose explicit core is a reflection on what to be human means and demands, deals constantly and from different angles with such themes of the Integrated Humanities Base as autonomy and responsibility, person, culture and other cultures, as well as individual and community.
