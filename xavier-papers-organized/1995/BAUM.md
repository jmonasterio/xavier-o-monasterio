II -  Religion as a product of alienation: The young Marx.

- P. 2: EVALUATIVE sociologyy. (objective?).
- P. 4: Each Sociology has an implicit theodicy
- P. 24: "The  discrepancies  in  social  institutions  inflict  burdens  on
people, diminsh their humanity, distort their  self-understanding  as  human
beings...-a distortion of awareness according to which  the  present  social
order becomes the measure  of reality" (IDEOLOGY IN THE PEJORATIVE SENSE).
- p. 28: Marx based his critique of society on  a  humanistic  understanding
of man's social existence and on the conviction that human  fulfillment  and
happiness were in fact the destiny of humankind."
- p.33: RELIGION AS A  PRODUCT  AND  SIGN  OF  ALIENATION:  Unable  to  find
happiness in this world, people create a future happy world.
- P. 35:ACCORDING TO MARX, IDEOLOGY CAN BE OVERCOME:  "MARX  HELD  THAT  THE
OPPRESSED CLASS ALONE WAS FREE OF IDEOLOGY, AND THAT TRUE CONSCIOUSNESS  WAS
AVAILABLE ONLY TO THE WORKERS WHO HAD BECOME AWARE OF  THEIR  EXPLOITAITION.
(For Marx,  "ideology"  was  always  a  pejorative  term,  a  distortion  of
consciousness. "Ideology is the deformation of the truth  for  the  sake  of
social   interest"   "through   mental   processes   that   remain   largely
unconscious." "The corrupting trends tend to attach people  uncritically  to
their tradition, protect them from coming  to  self-knowledge,  defend  [and
legitimate] the authority of the dominant classess, create a false sense  of
superiority over others, and produce dreams of victory over  outsiders."  p.
75. "For ideological reasons, the  Church did  not  integrate  the  biblical
critique into its preaching" but rather projected it into the pharisees  and
the Jewish religion excluding herself from the criticism, p. 76).

IV - The ambiguity of religion: A biblical account

- P. 62: The Bible, NT included,  paints  a  highly  ambivalent  picture  of
religion. "Denunciation of hypocrisy, legalism,  collective  blindness"  (p.
77), self-exclusion by the Church of  the  criticsm  of  religion  projected
onto Judaism and Pharisees, self-idolatry for lack of  self-criticism.  Paul
and John, for polemical reasons, contributed  to  this  negative  projection
(with  implicit  self-absolution)  onto  the  Jews.  (LACK  OF   PARADOXICAL
LANGUAGE AND SELF-UNDERSTANDING ON THE CHURCH'S PART).

V - The ambiguity of relgion: A social science account.

- P. 85:  The  social  critics  of  modern  times:  Durkheim's  and  Weber's
distinction  between  religion  and  magic,  Fromm's   distinction   between
authoritarian  and  humanistic  religion,  and   the   distinction   between
ideological  and  utopian  religion  derived  from  Manheim's  Sociology  of
knowledge.
- P. 97: "Fromm understands religion as any style  of  thought  and  action,
shared by a group, which gives the individual a frame of orientation and  an
object of devotion".
- P. 99: Asking to what extent the inherited symbols  initiate  people  into
dependencies, guilt and blindness, and to what extent these same  symbols...
deliver people from dependencies, guilt  and  blindenes.  The  Gospel  is  a
healing message, which demands that we submit the teaching and  practice  of
the the Church to an ongoing therapeutic critique.
- P. 101: Karl Mannheim: Marx did not apply an ideological critique  to  the
ideas of revolutionary  movements;  the  most  oppressed  classes  were  not
vulnerable to ideology, or at least could acquire true consciousness."

VIII - Creative Religion: Max Weber's perspective.

- P. 162: "It has happened that Christians  who  opened  themselves  to  the
negative critiques in the hope of purifying their  own  religious  tradition
found to their dismay that these critiques affected all  akspects  of  their
religion, left nothing intact, and ultimately dissolved their faith  in  the
Christian message. Is there kgood sociological evicnec, I ask, for  creative
religion?  Have  sociologists  presented  strong  evidence   for   creative,
innovative religion?
-  P.  184-185:  EARLY  AND  LATE   ENLIGHTENMENTS:   The   industrial   and
decomocratic  revolutions  at  the  end  of  the  18th  century  created  an
atomistic consciousness that corresponded to  the  rational  and  scientific
"Early  Enlightenment."  The  reaction   was   the   "Late   Enlightenment,"
represented by   the great critics of rational society, such  as  Durkhkeim,
Weber, and Freud, who tried to overcome positivism, diccovered the  symbolic
dimension of human life, and  favored  a  holiistic  rather  than  atomistic
understanding of society. While  the  Early  Enlightnement  was  hostile  to
religion, the Late E, while often appearing unreligious, was basically  open
to religion."

IX- Critical theology.

- 193: "By theology I here refer to the reflection of  believing  Christians
on  their  religion."  Theology  is  the  critical  task  of  the  believing
community. It presupposes faith, is an ekxpression offaith, and thus  enters
into the very  shape  that  faith  takes  in  people's  lives.  [PRACTICALLY
IDENTIFIES THEOLOGY WITH CRITICAL THEOLOGY OR REFLECTION ON ONE'S  RELIGION.
THEOLOGY IS COMMITED TO FAITH BEFORE BEING CRITICAL  OF  THE  WAY  FAITH  IS
ACTUALLY LIVED].
- P. 194: "The sustained dialogue with the  critical  thought  of  the  Late
Enlightenment I wish to call 'critical theology.'" It is the application  of
the  various  theories  of  alienation  to  the  self-understanding  of  the
Christian Church.
- p. 195:  Critical  theology  enables  the  church  to  assume  theological
responsibility for its social reality [the consequences of its  praxis].  In
this preliminary sense  critical  theology  is  reflection  on  praxis.  The
traditional formulation of christology has had unrecognized and  uninteneded
effect:  inferiorization  of  the  Jews,  church-centered  understanding  of
history, legitimation of white colonialism. It is  the  measurement  of  the
structural consequences of dogma by the revealed  standard  of  the  gospel,
not a submission of the gospel to other standards.
We shall discuss one application: the privatization of th gospel,  i.e.  the
excessively individualistic interpretation of the Christian  message,  which
has legitimated and promoted the atomization of  the  social  order  and  an
economic system of each man for himself.
- P. 207: "In the Catholic Church the privatization of  sin  eventually  led
to the denial that the church as church could be sinful. Since all  sin  was
private it was unnecessary to engage in critical reflection on the  church's
corporate life."
- P. 214-16: LIBERATION THEOLOGY IN LATIN AMERICA. (See also p. 289).

XII - Heaven as revealed utopia.

- P. 267: Is it possible to understand Christ's preaching of the Kingdom  as
utopian rahter than as ideological religion?
- P. 268: "Pope John XII condemned this new trend: 'The soul separated  from
the body does not enjoy the vision whhich is its total reward and  will  not
enjoy it prior to the resurrection'."
Afterwards, the emphasis on individual salvation  became  almost  exclusive,
except in the text of the liturgy.
-  P.  270:  Sociologists  like  Schutz  and  Peter  Berger,  influenced  by
Heidegger, place one's own mortality at the center--the horror of chaos  and
the fear of death become the generating force that make people  look  for  a
safe society. "WHAT WE HAVE HERE IS A PSYCHOLOGY RATHER THAN A SOCIOLOGY  OF
RELIGION."
- p. 274: "The Xtian message of  the  coming  kingdom  promises  deliverance
from evil on a universal scale including  all  peoples  of  the  earth,  and
thanks to this message we want to think of our personal  lives  as  situated
in this holy and universal drama."
- P. 274-283: ERNST BLOCH.
       Distinctions  between  ideology  and  utopia,  and  between  concrete
(realizable) and abstract (unrealizable and therefore paralizing) utopias.
- P. 280: Bloch is  a  passionate  atheist.  He  opposes  the  divine  as  a
dangerous factor alienating man from her material foundation--hunger.
- P. 282: Not every dream (or imagination) is utopian. The utopian  includes
a qualitativey new element.
- P. 287: "Christians entertain a messianic view of history. They yearn  and
hope for the triumph of God over  evil  in  personal  and  social  life.  To
express the historical messianism of the gospel it may be necessary  to  use
PARADOXICAL LANGUAGE. Xtians believe that  the  orientation  of  history  is
unalterable, yet the certainty they derive  from  this  is  often  only  the
trust that after the great catastrophes  produced  by  human  sin  we  shall
again begin to move forward."
- P. 288: "THE MESSAGE OF GOD'S  COMING  REIGN  OFFERS  A  CRITIQUE  OF  ALL
SECULAR UTOPIAS. For by negating the religious dimension  a  purely  secular
utopia produces... a repression of the Holy Spirit and thus  easily  creates
a   substitute   religion,   with   unintended   and   uncontrolled   social
consequences.
- P. 291: "XTIANS WHO HAVE TRUSTED IN A TRANSFORMIST GOSPEL  AND  UNDERSTOOD
THE MESSAGE OF THE KINGDOM HAVE OFTEN  EXPERIENCED  A  WANING  OF  RELIGIOUS
INTEREST... THEY NO LONGER FELT AT HOME IN THE LANGUAGE OF THE  CHURCH,  AND
THEY HAD DIFFICUTIES IN DISTINGUISHING THEIR OWN  COMMITMENT  FROM  THAT  OF
THEIR SECULAR FRIENDS. One reason may well have been the  hostility  of  the

it may, the loss of interiority and religious  ekxperience  has  destructive
consequences for a Xtian community. A CHURCH THAT LOSES  ITS  ROOTEDNESS  IN
THE SAVING ACTS OF GOD, IN THEIR LITURGICAL CELEBRATION AND IN THE  PERSONAL
SPIRITUALITY OF ITS MEMEBERS LOSES ACESS TO THE SPIRIT THAT KEEPS  IT  ALIVE
AND SENDS IT ON ITS EARTLY MISSION. - [Thus, NOT ONLY  PARADOXICAL  LANGUAGE
TO EXPRESS OUR FREEDOM AS WELL AS OUR DEPENDENCE, THE  TENSION  BETWEEN  THE
PRESENT AND THE  FUTURE,  BUT  ALSO  DIALECTICAL  LANGUAGE  TO  COMBINE  THE
