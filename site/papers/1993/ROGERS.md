March 1, 1993

Dear Gene:

I finished a first reading of your dissertation (except for the missing pages) and a more serious re reading of Part I. Since I assume that you are curious about the impression that your work produces on interested readers (just because that is my case as an author), I will share my first impression with you. Take this as a token of gratitude for having sent me your work. Before that, two other matters.

I did all I could to have Phillip Cary hired by my Department because, as I honestly wrote to him, I found him head and shoulders above his competitors for the position. Also, out of self-interest, because I would have loved to have such a partner in future dialogue by me. Unfortunately, I failed. My colleagues have been shaped by the silly idea that in order to think philosophically one's faith has to be neutralized, and Phillip therefore did not correspond to their standards. As a result they closed themselves to any other considerations.

Concerning Latin quotations in your dissertation. There are a number of small errors in them, probably typographical errors. Take care to have them corrected when your book is published. Since my knowledge of Latin is good, I would be willing to double -check that part of your manuscript when the time comes, if it helps. In any case, one expression I think you ought to correct, because you use it several times starting on p. 57, is "praecognit um finis." "Finis" is masculine, not neuter. So you should write "praecognit us finis," I think. Your error probably comes from the fact that in the quotation (p. 42, note 53) "praecognit um " appears, but there it appears in the

accusative, not in the nominative, related to "fin em ," not to "finis."

Now, very briefly, to your work. Will you be offended if I say that I find it fascinating, challenging, splendidly argued, yet unconvincing? Unconvincing means simply that I was not fully persuaded; at least I have not been yet. Perhaps it is just a case of the tremendous difficulty of unasking asked questions. If it is just that, give me time and I will come to agree with you, because your argument of reading the Summa in a way that harmonizes with what Thomas says in Ad Romanos is powerful.

Let my try and see if I can briefly explain why, or in what sense, I find your work unpersuasive. Pace Corbin (whose work I have read), the evolution he sees, and you too, between the Contra Gentiles (at least the beginning) and the Summa amounts to a radical shift of paradigms, a totally new and different perspective on the relation between sacra doctrina and philosophy, between 'faith' and 'reason.' I find it very difficult to think that Thomas could have not left any explicit acknowledgement of such epistemological rupture, and even more so that he would have been totally unaware of it. Now, many of what you would call misreadings of Aquinas' depend on the lack of recognition of such rupture, for if such rupture does not occur then those are not misreadings. Secondly, I am uncomfortable with your "the more Aristotelian, the more scriptural" or Christian. I understand what you mean as well as your arguments. However, I have a lot of trouble with your formulation, for (to put it in Aristotelian terms) nothing remains of the Aristotelian original form: aqua has been transformed into vinum. And would it not be highly equivocal to say "The more vinum it is, the more aqua it is?". If this is the case, no wonder that other people who have taken more seriously Thomas' purported Aristotelianism have systematically misread him. Thirdly, I have not yet been cured of my old, strong suspicion that the Aristotelian idiom of "forms" which, even according to you, Thomas wholeheartedly adopts can be transferred without vitiating equivocation to historical, singular events as those narrated in Scripture. Can Thomas or anybody get away with substituting revelabilia of the just mentioned kind for intelligibilia and still consider himself or herself Aristotelian in any significant sense? Or, the other alternative which is worse, can one remain Christian while being Aristotelian in a significant sense?

I have no time for more now. Perhaps in the future, if you are interested and I have time. For the moment, best wishes and the peace that the world cannot give.

Xavier O. Monsterio
