Reworked on March 12, 2005.

ON LINDBECK AND TILLEY

Are George Lindbeck and Terrence Tilley in full agreement concerning the doctrines of faith?

According to Lindbeck, as we shall see in detail, doctrines are second-order sentences rather than first-order propositions. Consequently they function – primarily, if not exclusively – as grammatical rules that regulate what can be said and done by Christians, not as propositions that refer directly to realities.

Tilley’s formulation is less clear:

“However, doctrines are the written and/or remembered content of the acts of a community or [of] the authorities within that community. To do their job properly, such acts must also have a specific referential force” ^(F1)

The emphasis in the preceding quotation is mine. It is meant to underline that

in addition to functioning as grammatical rules doctrines also function as propositions that refer to realities. And such reference is “objective,” that is, corresponds to what is objectively the case, independently of the believer’s dispositions. But what exactly is “the content of an act”? Remembered or written acts may be said to have a content because, as remembered or written, they have an enunciative structure and may be said to have a content. However, as remembered.   or written, they are not acts, they are enunciations. Lindbeck’s formulation is far more clear. So I’ll deal with it.  

LINDBECK

Cognitivism and Cultural-linguistic perspective

- Cognitivism or primarily cognitive propositional theory of religion (64)

- Sees religion as a primarily cognitive system more geometrico : purely intellectual assent to logically connected true propositions that express what is actually the case by corresponding (pictorially) with it. The correspondence is exclusively mental, of a mind with reality; in other words, it is independent of the subjective dispositions of those who utter the propositions (65-66). [Exactly as in his Peri hermeneias Aristotle defines the reference of enunciative speech (the only speech susceptible of truth and falsity and dictated exclusively by the object, by contrast to all the others kind of speech – deprecative, imperative, and vocative– which are not dictated exclusively by the object but also by the dispositions of the agent)] .

- Cultural-linguistic perspective (64)

- A religious system is more like a natural language than a formally organized geometry.

- The right use of this language cannot be detached from a particular way of behaving. Here the correspondence is not mental and pictorial but, so to say, behavioral: the conduct is appropriate because it corresponds to God’s will. The propositions have a role in constituting a form of life, a way of being in the world, which itself corresponds to the Most Important. This perspective does justice to Paul and Luther: they believed that Jesus’ lordship is real in the sense of independent from the faith or unfaith of those who say or hear the proposition, but they meant that the only way to ‘assert’ this truth is to do something, to commit onself to a certain way of life. Religious utterances acquire propositional force only through performatory use (66). [So religious utterance can also have propositional reference!].

- In xtianity the system is constituted not by definitions, etc., but by a set of stories used in specifiable ways to interpret and live in the world. [Why stories rather than  propositional definitions? Because it is a matter of events in which the agent is involved or participates, not of Kantian objects of knowledge . Xt’s presence in the Eucharist, for example, is not a truth to be understood and assented to, but an event to be involved in through action and adoration.]

First and Second-order Propositions

- Sentences may be used propositionally or non-propositionally

- Only first order propositions are susceptible of first-order or ontological truth (64)

- Only sentences that are used propositionally to refer to particulars under the right conditions of reference (particular time and place in relation to speaker and addressees) are used propositionally [ that is, with intention of ontological truth? ] (67) [The speaker and the addressees are exposed, vulnerable agents. That is why their first order utterances do not refer to Kantian objects of knowledge but to dramatic events to be participated in by the exposed agent. That, too, is why the dispositions of the believer count and are constitutive of the event. In speaking of propositional use of sentences Lindbeck shows that he has not fully escaped the Aristotelian conception of language-knowledge. Yet he ‘gets’ well, from Wittgenstein, the practical, regulative character of language.]  

- Religious sentences acquire enough referential specificity to have first-order or ontological truth only in determinate settings. This RARELY IF EVER happens on the pages of theological treatises or in the course of doctrinal discussions. (67-68). [Again, no doubt that Lindbeck attributes ontological reference to first order religious propositions, though only rarely if ever to second order doctrines. But this rarely if ever is very confusing. Why, and when?]

- The theological and doctrinal uses of, e.g., ‘Xt is Lord” are important but not propositional, i.e., it is a second order sentence. For xtian theological purposes that sentence becomes a first order proposition capable (so non-idealists would say) of making ontological truth claims only as it is used in the activities of adoration, proclamation, obedience, promise-hearing and keeping, which shape individuals and communities into conformity to the mind of Xt. Consequently those unskilled in the language of faith (in the Xtian religious use of language) cannot affirm or deny that Jesus is Lord (67-68) [ because the only way of asserting that Jesus is Lord is conducting oneself in relation to him as Lord ].

- Believers have the implicit conviction that when they rightly use such a sentence as “Xt is the Lord” they are using a first order proposition. (68-9) Most of the theological tradition grants that (68-9). Nothing in the cultural-linguistic approach demands the acceptance or rejection of this epistemological realism and of the correspondence theory of truth implicit in this epistemological conviction, but cognitivists and cultural-linguists understand that differently. For the cognitivists, technical theology and doctrine are propositional (68-9). For the culturalists, propositional truth and falsity characterize ordinary religious language when used in the activities of prayer, preaching, and exhortation (when the language actually molds lives). It is only on this level that humans linguistically exhibit THEIR truth or falsity, THEIR correspondence to the Ultimate mystery (68-9). Technical theology and doctrine are second-order discourse about the first intentional uses of religious language . Here one RARELY IF EVER succeeds in making affirmations with ontological import; one rather engages in explaining, defending, analyzing and regulating the liturgical, kerygmatic, and ethical modes of speech and ACTION . As a grammar neither affirms or denies anything about the world in which language is used, but only about language, so theory and doctrine, TO THE EXTENT THAT THEY ARE SECOND ORDER ACTIVITIES, assert nothing about God and his relation to creatures, but only speak about such assertions. Such assertions cannot be made except when speaking religiously, i.e., when seeking to align oneself and others performatively with the Most Important in the universe by worshiping, promising, obeying, exhorting and preaching (68-69)

- The Nicaean and Chalcedonian doctrines express second-order guidelines for xtian discourse rather than first-order affirmations about the inner being of God or of Xt. (93-94). If so, the terminology and concepts of ‘one substance and three persons’ and ‘two natures’ may be absent, but if the same rules that guided the formation of the original paradigms are operative in the construction of new formulations --and if the doctrines expressed in different terms have equivalent consequences (93-94)– then they express one and the same doctrine ((95-96).

- Lonergan shows that the fact that these doctrines can be understood as second order propositions or rules is an ancient insight. These doctrines are the product of systematic reflection on the confused multiplicity of symbols, titles and predicates applied to God and Xt. Athanasius saw the Nicaean doctrines as rules to speak in a certain way. He and the early Fathers did not deny first order interpretations, but according  to Lonergan these were at first only ‘incipient.’ Only later, in medieval scholasticism, was the full metaphysical import asserted . Lonergan sees this as a desirable development, but this is debatable (93-94).

- The Nicaean creed has acquired liturgical and expressive functions that are more important than its doctrinal use for large parts of Xtendom. Thus better not to change them. But they could be changed, for the authority belongs to the rules instantiated by the doctrines. If these rules are such regulative principles of monotheism, historical specificity, and xtological  maximalism, it is possible to claim that Nicaea and Chalcedon represent historically conditioned formulations of doctrines that are unconditionally and permanently necessary to  mainstream xtian identity . (95-96)

- In short, rule theory allows (though it does not require) giving these creeds the status that the major xtian tradition have attributed to them, but with the understanding that they are permanently authoritative paradigms, not formulas to be slavishly repeated (95-96).

- Practical doctrines cannot be but rules . For the case of the Creeds, Lonergan and Rahner give a first-order ontological or metaphysical interpretation, but Lonergan adds a regulative account of the trinitarian and xtological doctrines . In the latter case, the contemporary propositionalism represented by Lonergan is intermediate between the classical variety and a consistently regulative theory of doctrine. For most medieval and post-medieval theologians, a doctrine is a rule only because it is first a proposition. F.e., it is because Father and Son are ontologically consubstantial that xtian discourse  must conform to the Athanasian rule that whatever is said of the Father must be said of the Son, except that the Son is not the Father. Lonergan’s historical work reverses this relation. The regulative function appeared first; only later, with Scholasticism , was the metaphysical import fully grasped and affirmed. He therefore agrees with rule theory that the doctrinal authority, not only of practical norms but of credal affirmations, does not NECESSARILY depend on first-order ontological reference (104-105).

- In some theoretical disputes, the practical consequences of disagreement are minor. Except for high velocities and extremely large or small masses, Newtonian end Einsteinian physics produce the same results (105-106).

The major difference between our two theories is that from the regulative perspective ontological or propositional interpretations are superfluous. Rule theory does not prohibit speculations on the POSSIBLE CORRESPONDENCE of the trinitarian pattern of xtian language to the metaphysical structure of the Godhead; it simply says that these are not doctrinally necessary and cannot be binding. These are like discussions of whether there is a substance-attribute structure of finite entities corresponding to the subject-predicate structure o sentences, as Aris holds. Similarly, Aristotelian, Newtonian and Einsteinian theories of space and time are evaluated scientifically independently of the metaphysical question of which is closer to the way things are . If relativity is ontologically true, then both Aris and Einstein are superior to Newton with his space and times as absolutes. Scientifically, i.e. for predictive purposes , Newton’s is superior to Aris’s and inferior to Einstein’s. Rahner’s view of economic and immanent realities may correspond better than Lonergan’s stress on immanent psychological analogies concerning the Trinity, but the issue is unanswerable this side of the Eschaton and may be unimportant for theological evaluation (105-106).

- If doctrines are just rules for the construction of trinitarian theories , both may be correct; but if they are propositional, only one can be correct , which would suggest that the other is unwittingly heretical, though not yet declared so by the church (106-107).

- To see the practical difference between the two perspectives consider the contrast between interpreting a (propositional) truth and obeying a rule . If the immortality of the soul is a first-order proposition , then those who stand in the tradition within which it is a doctrine and find its mind-body dualism unacceptable have to discover what truth is expressed, however improbable that truth may be from the dualistic viewpoint of the original formulation. But if it is a rule, attention is focused on the concrete life and language of the community. Because the doctrine is to be followed rather than interpreted, the theologian’s task is to specify the circumstances, temporary or enduring, in which it applies (106-107). Wittgenstein: “There is a way of grasping a rule which is not an interpretation, but which is exhibited in what we call ‘obeying the rule and going against it’ in actual cases ” (111,note 26).

- Pagan converts to the catholic mainstream did not ... first understand the faith and then decide to become xtians; rather, the process was reversed: they first decided and then they understood [LIKE THE INVERTING LENSES, WHERE APPROPRIATE ACTION BRINGS ABOUT APPROPRIATE VISION, that is vision that favors or facilitates appropriate action]. More precisely, they were first attracted by the xtian community and form of life. They submitted themselves to prolonged catechetical instruction in which they practiced new forms of behavior and learned the stories of Israel and their fulfillment in Xt. Only after they had acquired proficiency in their alien xtian language and form of life were they deemed able intelligently and responsibly to profess the faith to be baptized (132-133). [ Which, I observe, seems to be what Pascal had in mind when he said that if one wanted to believe, one should first fall on his knees. This amounts to inverting the commonly accepted subordination of action to vision. But not quite, for the vision that results from action is not the objective vision of ontological truth of the epistemological tradition of correspondence; rather, it is motivating vision, sign FOR rather than sign OF. In the case of faith, though, there is no vision; nor is it absolutely required, as long as there is appropriate action. In short, in the case of faith, the action is like the appropriate action before vision in the case of the inverted lenses –the action that brings about the motivating vision. Another comparison: it is like the action without vision that allows the sufficiently trained or habituated blind person to find his or her way with the aid of a stick, which as action is comparable to the efficient action of t hose who are not blind, but which lacks the motivating vision possessed by them ].

  

My reflection

Let us first consider Lindbeck’s views on doctrine in detail.

Because of his ecumenical concerns, Lindbeck says that the “only job that doctrines do in their role as church teachings is regulative” ^(F2) Clearly this is the stance that fits better with his ecumenical concerns: “... And similarly, ontological interpretations of the Trinity do not, or should not, be made communally normative for the way Christians live and think”. ^(F3) Yet there are indications that he thinks that regulation is not “ the only job that doctrines do... as church teachings.”

For instance, Lindbeck grants that the doctrinal teaching “Jesus is Lord” may have propositional value:

“Paul and Luther, at any rate, quite clearly believed that Christ’s

Lordship is objectively real no matter what the faith or unfaith of those who hear or say the words. What they were concerned  to assert is that the only way to assert this truth is to do something about it, i.e., to commit oneself  to a way of life; and this concern, it would seem, is wholly congruent with the suggestion that it is only through the performatory use of religious utterances that they acquire propositional force”. ^(F4)

It seems to me that here Lindbeck incoherently regresses to the very epistemological paradigm of correspondence from which his performative notion of assertions as rules to be followed had liberated him. If, as he asserts, what  Paul and Luther were concerned to communicate to their hearers is that he only way to assert the truth that Christi’s lordship is to do something about it, then only through the performatory use of the asserted lordship can the sentence uttered or heard or believed be meaningful . In other words, here Lindbeck falls into the same trap I criticized Tilley for falling into. One cannot have it both ways: either first order  

sentences mean performatively, or they mean objectively, through mental correspondence, though Aquinian-Aristotelian adaequatio mentis ad rem . And this observation does not apply only to religious language. Thus Lonergan is equally wrong when he “adds” an ontological interpretation to the performative one. The two views belong in different and incompatible universes of discourse.

The reason why we so easily fall into this trap is that we are so used to the correspondence epistemology of knowledge and truth that, if we do not affirm correspondence with what is the case in matters of our faith, then, by virtue of  our very (and very Aristotelian) definition of truth, we seem to be denying the truth of our assertions of faith. But that, of course, cannot be the case if the only meaning of our assertions is performative, that is, to be manifested in the appropriate conduct, not in a further assertion that supposedly corresponds to what is the case.

In short, I maintain against Tilley, Lindbeck, ^(F5) Lonergan, and Rahner that the Christian faith can only be genuinely expressed in appropriate conduct, which is what first order sentences are meant to terminate in.
