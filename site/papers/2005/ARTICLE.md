ARTICLE

I am not as much concerned with what Aristotle  historically thought as with what Aquinas saw as occurring in Aristotle's works, and more precisely with that part of what he saw of which he approved.

"La revolte du logos contre le muthos" (Curtius). Quoted by de Lubac, Exegese Medievale, Seconde Partie, II, p. 127).

Spoken and written language . Contemporaneousness and cospaciality of speaker and hearer in spoken language, not found in written texts. Since speaker and hearer share the same perceptual space, the same immediate field of action, and enough of the same culture as to communicate in the particular language in which they communicate, there is comparatively little room for misunderstanding of what they say to each other, and in any case such misunderstanding can easily be detected and corrected. The case is very different with written texts which may have been authored in a different place, at a different time, in a different culture, and in a different language from that of the reader. Such, for us, is the case of the biblical texts.

Language incarnates communicative intentions between or among agents exposed to each other and to the surrounding world, not between or among invulnerable contemplators of other invulnerable contemplators of each other and of a mere spectacle surroundig them . If pure contemplators could communicate with each other, their communication would be about what appears to them. Thus, their language would be exclusively composed of descriptive and explanatory statements and of questions to be answered by further statements of the same kind. In short, it would be the strictly factual language to which so many philosophers have aspired and perhaps continue to aspire. By contrast, our language, that is, the language of vulnerable, exposed agents, is not composed just of statements and of questions to be answered by further statements, much less of affectively neutral and purely theoretical or objective statements, but of an immense variety of practical or performative speech acts that express an immense variety of ways of intending the world and the other, and of intending to communicate to the other the world intended and our ways of intending it.
