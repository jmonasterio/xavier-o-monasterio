Aquinas's View of Holy Scripture

Some of Aquinas's ideas on Scripture are explicit, some are

explicitly state by him, some are implicit in the way he uses

Scripture. Both are important for my purpose because they confirm my thesis that he completely ignores its narrative character and forces the Scripture into the mould of Aristotleian science. The following observations come from the Summa. I will add at the end a summary of his views on Scripture, on the Symbols, on theology, and on faith.

Part I, Question 1 : The nature and domain of sacred doctrine

- Art. l: Scripture is inspired by God. Not part of the philosophical disciplines discovered by reason. Necessary that there should be a doctrine revealed by God besides the philosophical disciplines investigated by reason, because certain truths exceed human reason, and because what can be known of God by reason would be known only by a few. What is revealed by God must be accepted through faith. And sacred doctrine consist in such things. Things treated by philosophical disciplinjes may also be treated by theology insofar as known by the light of revelation. Hence the theology included in sacred doctrine differs in genus from natural theology which is part of philosophy.

- Art 2: Sacred doctrine is a science because it proceeds from principles made known by the highest science: the science of God and the blessed. Individual facts, such as the deeds of Abraham, Isaac and Jacob are not treated in sacred doctrine as its principal concern, but rather as examples to be followed in our lives (as in the moral sciences), and also to establish the authority of those through whom the divine revelaion , on which this sacred scripture or doctrine is based , has come down to us.

- Art. 3: Because Scripture considers some things under the formality of being divinely revealed, all thing which have been divinely revealed have in common the formality of this science. In this way sacred doctrine bears the stamp of the divine science, which is one and simple, yet extends to everything .

- Art. 4: Sacred doctrine is a practical science, for a practical science is that which ends in action according to the Philosopher. Sacred doctrine includes both speculative and

practical truths, as God by one and the same science knows both himself and his works. But it is more speculative than practical, because it is more concerned with divine things than with human acts and because inasmuch as it treats of these acts it treats them inasmuch as man is ordained by them to the perfect knowledge of God, in which consists eternal beatitude .  

-Art. 5: Other sciences derive their certitude from the natural light of human reason, which can err; this one from the light of divine science, which cannot err. This science treats chiefly of those things that transcend human reason , which are the most sublime; the others consider only what is within reason's grasp. A practical science is all the nobler as its end; the end of this science is the noblest--eternal beatitude, which is the ultimate end of all sciences. The fact that some doubt the articles of faith is due to the weakness of reason, not to incertitude of their truth. This science does not need other human disciplines, for their principles are revealed, but it draws upon the philosophical disciplines to make its teaching clearer due to the defect of our intellect which is more easily led by what is known through natural reason.

- Art. 6: Sacred science is a wisdom, for it essentially deals with God viewed as the highest cause, for it treats of Him not only as He can be known through creatures just as philosophers know him, but also as far as He is known to Himself alone and revealed to others . The business of this science is not to prove the principles of other sciences, but only to judge them. Whatever in other sciences is contrary to the truth of this science must be condemend as false . A man may judge by knowledge, just as a man learned in moral science may be able to judge rightly about virtuous acts, though he had no virtue. This second manner of judging belongs to this doctrine, inasmuch as it is acquired by study, though its principles come from revelation .

- Art. 7: God is the subject-matter of this science, where all things are treated under the aspect of God, either because they are God himself, or because they refer to God as their beginning and end. This is also clear from the principles of this science, that is, the articles of faith, for faith is about God . In this science we make use of God's effects, either of nature or of grace, in place of a definition, in regard to whatever is treated concerning God; even as in some philosophical sciences we demonstrate something about a cause from its effect by taking the effect in the place of a definition of its cause.

- Art 8: Sciences do not argue in proof of their principles, but from their principles to demonstrate other truths. Same with this science: the Apostle argues from the resurrection of Christ in proof of the general resurrection . Since Sacred Scripture has no science above itself (like metaphysics), it disputes argumentatively with one who denies its principles only if the opponent admits some at least of the truths obtained through revelation. If our opponent believes nothing of divine revelation, there is no longer any way of proving the aricles of faith by argument, but only of answering his objections against faith. Since faith rests upon infallible truth, and since the contrary to a truth can never be demonstrated, the proofs against faith are no demonstration but arguments that can be answered .

Arguments from reason cannot prove what belongs to faith. It is proper to this science to argue from authority, for its principles are revealed; hence we must believe the authority of those to whom the revelation was made . Sacred doctrine does not use reason to prove faith (for that would do away with the merit of faith), but uses it to make clear other things set forth in this doctrine, for grace does not destroy but perfects nature . It may also use the authority of philoso-phers in things we can know from natural reason, but only as extrinsic and probable arguments. It properly uses the authority of the canonical Scriptures as necessary demonstration , and the authority of the Doctors of the Church may be used but only as probable.

- Art. 9: Should holy Scripture use metaphors , given that the aid of various similitudes and figures is proper to poetic, the least of all the sciences , and that similitudes hide the truth? The Scripture uses similitudes, which is a way of using metaphors, because all human knowledge originates from sense, and because it is meant also for simple people who are otherwise unable to understand. Poetry uses metaphors to produce representation, fro we are naturally pleased with that. But sacred doctrine uses metaphors as necessary (we cannot be enlightened by the divine rays except they be hidden within the covering of many sacred veils) and useful. The ray of divine revelation is not extinguished by the sensible imagery wherein it is veiled, and its truth remains because it does not allow the minds of the recipients of revelation to rest in the likenesses, but raises them to the knowledge of intelligilbe truths . Hence those things taught metaphorically in one part of the Scripture, in other parts are taught more openly . And the hiding of truth in figures is useful for the exercise of thoughtful minds and defends against the ridicule of unbelievers. It is fitting that divine things should be expounded under the figure of less noble bodies. For then it is clear that these things are not literal descripitions of divine truths, and because this is more fitting to the knowledge of God we have in this life (What He is is not is more clear than what He is, and similitudes show that God is above whatever we may say or think of Him), and because thereby divine truths are better hidden from the unworthy.

- Art. l0: Senses of Scripture : historical, etiological, analogical, allegorical, and parabolical. The author of the holy Scripture is God , who has the power to siginify not only by words, but by making the things signified to have also signification. The first signification whereby words signify things belongs to the historical or literal sense . The literal sense is that which the author intended. The signification whereby things signified by words have also signification is called the spiritual sesne, which is based on and presupposes the literal. The spiritual sense is threefold: as far as the old Law signifies things of the New Law, allegorical sense; as far as the things which signify Christ are also signs of what we ought to do, moral sense; as far as they signify what relates to eternal glory, anlogical sense. No confusion arises, for all the senses are founded on the literal, from which alone can any argument be drawn . History, etiology, analogy are grouped under the literal sesnse, for it is called history whenever anything is simply related; etiology when its cause is assigned, f. e.  when it is explained why Moses allowed the putting away of wives; analogy when the truth of one scriptural text is shown not to contradict the truth of another. Allegory alone stands for the three spiritual senses. The parabolical sense is contained in the literal, for by words things are signified properly and figuratively. Nor is the figure itself, but that which is figured, the literal sense. When Scripture speaks of God's arm, the literal sense in not that God has such a member, but only what is signified by this member, namely, operative power . Hence it is plain that nothing false can ever underlie the literal sense of Holy Scripture.

My reflections

- Aquinas assimilates the historical sense to the litteral sense. Not only in explicit words but in his whole conception. Revelation consists in God's knowledge imparted to us, and knowledge is primarily (intellectual vision of an intelligible).

He sees Scripture as a disorderly collection of statements by God, which in order to uioeld their full intelligibility need systematization, and in order to yield their implicit message need discursive complementation. Historical figures and events contained in the Scripture are infallibly true and revealed, but they work only as examples and to confer divine credibility to the recipients of revelation, that is, the recipients of God's knowledge. (Aquinas's treatise on faith defines faith as intellectual assent, that is, to a judgment). His way of dealing with metaphor and its uses shows no inkling of the necessity of narrative. His use of Scripture is argumentative, Aristotelian: a statement that functions as a principle, as a premise, or an argument of authority that settles a point. And he uses indisfferently what has the form of a statement or what has the form of a command but understood as a statement ("Be ye doers of the word, and not hearers only", art. 3).

- Aquinas is not an Averroist. He is far from being a rationalist. He makes the part of faith beyond reason. He does not believe in philosophy without theology. He is far from being a fideist, he makes the part of reason ("The existence of God and other like truths aboyut God, which can be known by natural reason, are not articles of fatith, but are preambles to the articles; for faith presupposes natural knowledge, even as grace presupposes nature and perfects the perfectible. Nevertheless, there is nothing to prevent a man who cannot grasp a proof, from accepting, as a matter of faith, something which in itself is capable of being scientifically known and demonstrated. I, a. 2, a. 2, obj. 1). He does not want theology without philosophy, although he admits faith without philosophy. But whether Aquinas

famous synthesis of faith and reason is valid is quite another matter.

- Aquinas is quite a believer. This explains his openess. He has absolute confidence in the truth of his faith. And, since grace perfects nature, in the compatibility of his faith with reason. And, not being a rationalist, he has his faith to fall back upon when pressed by pseudo-proofs of reason.

- There is, in his treatise of theology, an uncomfortable confusion between Scripture, Sacra Doctrina, and theology. He seems to believe that a statement imported from Scripture functions under the Aristotelian regime as it functions within its proper context of Scripture.

Aquinas on God's existence

I do not summarize every article as I did above. I only take important points.

Part I, Question II : The Existence of God

- Art. 1: A proposition is self-evident because the predicate is included in the essence of the subject: e.g. 'Man is an animal', for animal is contained in the essence of man. If, therefore, the essence of the predicate and subject be known to all, the proposition will be self-evident to all; as is clear with the first principles of demonstration, the terms of which are certain common notions of which nmo one is ignorant, such as being and non-being, whole and part, etc. To those to whom the essence of the predicate and subject is unknown, who do not know the meaning of the predicate and subject, the proposition will not be self-evident. Thus some notions of the mi8nd are self-evident only to the learned, as that incorporeal substances are not in space. The proposition 'God exists' is of itself self-evident, because God is his own existence. But because we do not know God's essence, the proposition is not self-evident to us, but needs to be demonstrated by things that are more known to us, though lesws known in their nature--namely, His effects . Even granting that everyone understands that by the name 'God' is signified something than which nothing greater can be thought, it does not follow that what the name signifies exists actually, but only that it exists mentally. Nor can it be argued that it actually exists, unless it be admitted that there actually exists something than which nothing greater can be thought, for this is precisely what is not admitted  by those who hold that God does not exist.

- Art. 2: Object. 1: The existence of God cannot be demonstrated. For it is an article of faith that God exists. But what is of faith cannot be demonstrated, because a demonstration produces scientific knowledge whereas faith is of the unseen , as is clear from the Apostle (Heb. xi, l). Reply: The existence of God and other like truths about God, which can be known by natural reason , are not articles of faith, but are preambles to the articles; for faith presupposes natural knowledge, even as grace presupposes nature and perfection the perfectible. Nevertheless, there is nothing to prevent a man, who cannot grasp a proof, from accepting, as a matter of faith, something which in itself is capable of being scientifically known and demonstrated .- Demonstration can be made in two ways: One is through the cause, and is called propter quid, and this is to argue from what is prior absolutely. The other is through the effect, and is called a demonstration quia; this is to argue  from what is prior relatively only to us. When an effect is better known than its cause, from the effect we proceed to the knowledge of its cause. And from every effect its proper cause can be demonstrated, so long as the effects are better known to us. Hence the existence of God, insofar as it is not self-evident to us, can be demonstrated from those of its effects which are known to us..- When the existence of a cause is demonstrated from an effect, this effect takes the place of a definition of the cause in proving the causes existence . In order to prove the existence of anything, it is necessary to accept as a middle term the meaning of a name, and not its essence, for the question of its essence follows on the question of its existence . Consequently, in demonstrating the existence of God, we mak take for the middle term the meaning of the name God.- From effects not proportioned to the cause no perfect knowledge of the cause can be obtained. Yet from every effect the existence of the cqause can be clearly demonstrated, and so we can demonstrate the existence of God from his effect; though from them we cannot know God perfectly as He is in his essence.

- Art. 3: Whether God exists. I answer that the existence of God can be proved in five ways . Obj. l: If God existed, there would be no evil. But there is evil in the world. Reply: As Augustine says, 'Since God is the highest good, he would not allow any evil to exist in his works, unles his omnipotence and goodness were such as to bring good out of evil.   

N.B. Q12, art. 12: Our natural knowledge begins from sense. Hence it can go as far as it can be led by sensible things. But our intellect cannot be led so far as to see the essence of God, because sensible creatures are effects of the power of God which do not equal his power. But because they are his effects and depend on their cause, we can be led from them so far as to know of God whether He exists, and to know of Him what must necessarily belong to Him, as the first cause of all things. Hence, we know his relationship with creatures, that is, that He is the cause of all things; also that creatures differ from him; and that his effects are removed from him because he superexceeds all of them.- Since the knowledge of God's essence is by grace , it belongs only to the good; but the knowledge of him by natural reason can belong to both good and bad. Many who are not good know many truths, that is, by natural reason.- Rweply to obj. 1: Although by the revelation of grace in this life we do not know of God what He is , and thus are united to him as to one unknown, still we know Him more fully according as many and more excellent of his effects are demonstrated to us, and according as we attribute to him some things known by divine revelation, to which natural reason cannot reach, as, for instance, that God is three and One.- Reply to obj. 2: Fr om the images either naturally received from sense, or divinely formed in the imagination, we have so much the more excellent intellecual knowledge, the stronger the intelligible light is in man; and thus through the revelation given by the images a fuller knowledge is received by the infusi0on of the divine light.- Reply object. 3: Faith is a kind of knowledge, inasmuch as the intellect is determined by faith to some knowable object. But this determination to one object does not proceed from the vision of the believer, but from the vision of Him who is believed. Thus, as far as faith falls short of vision, it falls short of the nature which knowledge has when it is science; for science determines the intellect to one object by the vision and understanding of its first principles

Aquinas on Faith

II, IIae, q. l-7 :

- Art. l: The object of faith is the First Truth: The formal aspect of faith is the First Truth, for faith does not assent to anything except because it is revealed by God. Considering materially the things to which faith assents, they include not only God, but also many other things contained in the Holy Scriptures; but they come under the assent of faith only as  bearing some relation to God (from certain effects of the divine operation man is helped in his journey towards the enjoyment of God. Consequently, from this point of view also the object of faith is in a way the First Truth, even as the object of the medical art is health, for it considers nothing save in relation to health.

- Art 2: Whether the object of faith is a proposition?

Obj. 2: The exposition of faith is contained in the symbol. The symbol does not contain propositions but thing, for it does not state that God is almighty, but I believe in God almighty .- Obj. 3: Further, faith is succeeeded by vision, according to Cor.. xiii, 22: We see now through a glass... But the object of faith is something simple, for it is the divine essence.- "On the contrary": Faith is a mean between science and opinion. The mean is in the same genus as the extremes. Since science and opinion are about propositions, so is faith. - "I answer that": The thing known is in the knower according to the mode of the knower. The proper mode of the human intellect is to know the truth by composition and division (cf. Chenu, 'Contribution a l'histoire du tratite de la foi. Commentaire historique de la IIa IIae, q. 1, a. 2, in Melanges Thomistes (Bibliotheque Thomiste, III: Le Saulchoir, Kain, 1923, p. 132). Hence the things that are simple are known by the intellect with a certain complexity. Accordingly, the object of faith may be considered in two ways: The thing itself about which we have faithis simple; on the part of the believer the object is complex, such as a proposition. Hence in the past both opinions have been held with a certain amount of truth. The symbol mentions the things about which faith is, insofar as the act of the believer is terminated in them, as is evident from the manner of speaking about them . The act of the believer does not terminate in a proposition but in a thing, for we do not form propositions except in order to have knowledge agout things through their means; and this is true of faith as well as of science. The object of heavenly vision will be the First Truth seen in itself, according to John iii, 2. Hence that vision will not be by way of a propostion, but by way of simple understanding.

- Art. 3: Whether anything false can come under faith, as in believing that a bread not rightly consecrated is the body of Christ.

- Art 4: Whether the object of faith can be something seen

Faith signifies the assent of the intellect to that which is believed . The intellect assents to a thing in two ways. First, by being moved to assent by its very obeject, which is known either by itself (as in first principles) or through something else already known (which are held by the habit of science) Secondly, the intellect assents to something, not through being sufficfiently moved to assent by its proper object, but through an act of choice, whereby it turns voluntarily to one side rather than to another. If this be accompanied by doubt and fear of the opposite side, it is opinion; if there be certainty and no fear, it is faith. Those things are said to be seen which, of themselves, move the intellefct or the senses to knowledge of them. Thus neither faith nor opinion can be of things seen.

- Art. 5: Whether those things that are of faith can be the object of knowledge . Obj. 3: Things which are demonstrated are an object of science, since a demonstration is a syllogism that produces science. Now, certain matters of faith have been demonstrated by philosophers, such as the existence and unity of God.- "I answer that": All science is derived from self-evident and therefore seen principles ; so all objects of science must needs be, in a fashion, seen. However, it is impossible that one and the same thing should be believed and seen by the same person . It may happen, however, that a thing that is an object of vision or science for one, is believed by another because he does not know it by demonstration. Nevertheless, that which is proposed to be believed equally by all is equally unknown by all as an object of science. Such are the things which are of faith absolutely. Consequently faith and science are not about the same things. Things which can by proved by demonstration are reckoned among what is of faith, not because they are believed absolutey by all, but because theyu are a necessaryu presupposition to matters of faith; so that those who do not know them by demonstration must possess them at least by faith

- Art. 6: Whether the things of faith should be divided into articles.- Some things proposed to our belief are in themselves of faith, while others are of faith, not in themselves, but only in relation to others . Since the chief object of faith consists in those things which we hope to see in heaven , those things are in themselves of faith which order us directly to eternal life. Such are the Trinity, the Incarnation, and the like. On the other hand, certain things in Holy Scripture are proposed to our belief, not chiefly on their own account, but for the manifestation of those mentioned above; ex. g., that Abraham had two sons, thjat a dead man rose again at the touch of Eliseus' bones, and the like, which are related in Holy Scripture for the purpose of manifestin the divine majesty or the Incarnation of Christ; and such things should not form distinct articles. - The formal aspect of the object of faith, on the part of the thing believed, is the First Truth--and from this point there is no distinction of articles; but from our point of view, the formal aspect of a matter of faith is that it is something unseen; and from this point of view there are distinct articles.

- Art. 7: Whether the articles of faith have increased in the course of time.- Just as the faith of Christ has reached us through the Apostles, so too, in the Old Testament, the knowledge of faith was delivered by the early patriarchs to those who came later, according to Deut. xxxii, 7: 'Ask thy father, and he will declare to the. Now the Apostles were fully instructed about the mysteries, for they received them more fully and earluier than others. Ther3efore, knowledge of matters of faith has not increased.- "I answer that":

SUMMARY OF AQUINAS'S VIEWS ON SACRED SCRIPTURE

- Scripture contains primarily God's knowledge (whose vision is our ultimate end and will beatify us) of Himself in Himself (eternally), and of Himself in relation to us (in time). This knowledge amounts, in a nutshell, to the Trinity and Redemption (Incarnation, passion, death, and resurrection of Christ).

- Scripture contains secondarily many other things: that a dead man rose again at the touch of Eliseus' bones, "which are related in Holy Scripture for the purpose of manifesting the divine majesty or the Incarnation of Christ (p. 16); that Abraham had two sons; the deeds of Abraham, Isaac and Jacob, which are treated not as the principal concern of the Scripture but rather as examples to be followed iun our lives (as in the moral scinces), and also to establish the authority of those through whom the divine revelation has come to us--"individual facts", p. 2).

- Scripture is a book of doctrine, a teaching--a sort of divine textbook whose indirect author, as inspirer, is God Himself.

- Scripture contains God's revelation of his knowledge, whose immediate recipients are the Fathers of the faith "who were instructors in the faith, p. 17) in the Old Testament, and the Apostles in the New."

- Although the Scripture is a sort of textbook, "the truth of faith is contained diffusely (disorderly?), under various modes of expression, and sometimes obscurely, so that in order to gather the truth of faith from it one needs long study and practice", p. l9. Among the different modes of expression counts, in the first place, the use of metaphors (p. 5)--the similitudes and figures characteristic of poetics "which is the least of the sciences', though "those things which are taught metaphorically in one part are taught more openly in other parts." Although metaphors are pleasing, here they are not used primarily for pleasure but for teaching, and are necessary and useful and fitting. Among the modes of expression, or perhaps the way in which things are meant, count the two senses of the Scripture: literal and spiritual, with its subdivisions, pp. 5-7. (On this  see my "Aquinas on Senses of the Scripture").

- The purpose of the Scripture is to teach us the truths that we need to know in order to reach our ultimate goal.
