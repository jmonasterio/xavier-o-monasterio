CHAPTER 3: EASTERN PHILOSOPHY, RELIGION, AND EDUCATION

(1) Overview of the Chapter

Eastern philosophy consists of four major areas of thought: Indian, Chinese, Japanese, and Middle Eastern.  The chapter studies each of them in this order, after a short contrast with Western philosophy that begins with the Greeks.  The main point of contrast is the systematic attempt within Western thought to separate philosophical and religious studies, an attempt that often proves to be impossible.  Who, for instance, can effectively separate Aquinas's philosophical views from his theology?  What is certain, however, is that Eastern thought definitely relies more on intuition, faith and mysticism than Western thought.

Under Indian thought, whose written sources are the Vedas, the Upanishads, and the Epics, Hinduism, Buddhism, and Jainism are studied.  Chinese thought is represented by Confucianism and Taoism; Japanese thought by Zen Buddhism; Middle Eastern thought by the Islamic religion.  Finally, Judaism and Christianity, though generally viewed as part and parcel of the Western cultural tradition, are introduced in this chapter because of the possible influence of Eastern thought on them.

The last two sections of the chapter deal with the specific characteristics of Eastern thought in relation to education and with a critique of Eastern philosophy in education.  I will deal with them, in an indirect way, in the following critical appraisal.

(2) Professional Critique of the Perspective

When one considers the enormous differences among, say, Hinduism, Buddhism, and Confucianism, to name only three varieties of Eastern thought, one  has a certain trouble in seeing them as varieties of one perspective.  However, in relation to education one can detect in all of them an emphasis that differs significantly from what characterizes our Western philosophy of education.

Our education in the West is primarily geared to gaining the skills necessary for the exercise of a money-earning profession. Eastern thought places the emphasis on the development of the wise person who will live a genuinely human life in which contemplation will occupy an important place.I think that, without renouncing completely our Western utilitarian inclination, which has contributed so much to our technological development and to the creation of a better life in material terms, we could gain much by balancing it with some of the inner peace and serenity promoted by Eastern thought.  Perhaps some of the techniques of yoga or of Zen Buddhism, which in no way interfere with the religious convictions of our students, could be fruitfully taught in our schools in a systematic way.

Another aspect of Eastern philosophy that involves a valuable educational lesson for us relates to suffering.  In the Western world we have become obsessed with happiness to the point of forgetting that suffering is an integral part of life, and that we should be trained to face suffering in a productive way.  In this particular regard, we might have much to learn from the teachings of the Buddha on the disciplining of desire, without falling into the lurking excess of considering pleasure and worldly success as dangers to be avoided.

For all its undeniable virtues, of which I have only mentioned two, there are in Eastern thought two traits that I find disturbing.  One is a too exclusive insistence on interiority and self-control, which tends to relegate the transformation of the surrounding world for our benefit to a very poor second place.  The other trait--and here I am thinking of Hinduism in particular--is its system of castes, within which the subservience of woman to man tends to appear as a minor detail.  One cannot read without shuddering "the ideal life plan" for a Hindu woman elaborated by Manu:

"A female is first subject to her father or brothers, then to her husband,. and after her husband's death to her sons. She must never be independent and must constantly worship her husband as a god, even if he is 'devoid of good qualities.' She must always be cheerful and clever in the management of household affairs Women may never perform a sacrifice, vow, or fast apart from their husbands" (pp. 85-86).

(3) Summary Statement

If one can speak of the large variety of Eastern schools of thought as "a philosophy," one may say in general that it contrasts with Western thought by its insistence on interiority and on the development of the wise person. While these are eminently desirable values in the field of education, they should come to complete, rather than to replace, our Western system of values.

LORETTA KING

CHAPTER 4: PRAGMATISM AND EDUCATION

(1) Overview of the Chapter

In a general way, pragmatism, as indicated by its very name, is a philosophy that places the emphasis on what is practical (by opposition to what is merely theoretical) and has appreciable consequences.  As a philosophical current, "pragmatism seeks to examine traditional ways of thinking and doing... and to reconstruct our approach to life more in line with human needs."

After this general definition, the chapter, in the first place, broaches the roots of the pragmatist world view, represented by a number of European thinkers, and then the full-fledged philosophy of pragmatism elaborated by American philosophers.

Among the Europeans we find Francis Bacon with the primacy of experimental induction over purely logical deduction, John Locke with the centrality of sensory experience as the origin of knowledge, Jean-Jacques Rousseau with his defense of the natural against the artificial-social, and Auguste Comte and Charles Darwin as actual applicators of the scientific method to the study of natural, social, and economic phenomena.  Darwin happened to be particularly influential with his notion of the interplay between organism and environment, and his notion of reality as an open-ended process.  Among the Americans, Peirce clarified the function of ideas as practical, testable tools to master the object, and William James elaborated the notion of truth as dependent on practical consequences.  It was, however, John Dewey (1859-1952) who brought the pragmatist philosophy to full development.

Dewey himself did not believe in immutable absolutes; this applies in the first place to his own philosophy which is itself open-ended.  As a consequence, it cannot be summarized as a closed system; only its main features can be highlighted: (1) Reality is in constant evolution; (2) the primary datum is experience; (3) experience is not a purely internal psychological entity, but is itself part of the process of nature; (4) nature, in its turn, is not only constituted by minerals, plants and animals, but also by people, societies, and human institutions; (5) experience is an adaptive, intelligent, problem-solving instrument, and it is therefore to be evaluated in terms of its practical results; (6) there are no enduring solutions; problems are emergent and have to be faced and solved as they emerge.

The very open-endedness of his philosophy led Dewey to concentrate on the education of the problem-solver rather than on solutions.  He saw the individual and organized society as interdependent and, in the ideal case, as mutually supportive. According to him, no genuine individuality was possible without humane, democratic, and educative conditions.  He advocated a school that would nurture both the individual and the social capabilities of children.  He defined individuality as the interplay of personal choice and freedom with objective conditions, and he thought that the school should provide the opportunity for intelligent choice within controlled conditions in a participatory democratic framework.  While Dewey had no sympathy for organized religion and supernatural beliefs because of their actual historical consequences, he had an almost religious respect for the human being in the realm of nature.  Concerning ethics, Dewey thought that moral rules should be considered in light of particular situations and in terms of their actual consequences. As for the moral development of people, he deemed that moral traits are to be acquired both by individual participation in the social context as well as by learning about morality through living and reflective inquiry.  Finally, Dewey saw the development of an artistic sensibility and of a problem-solving ability as two complementary varieties of the same creative impulse, and wanted both to be attended to and nurtured in the schools.

Dealing with the "resurgent pragmatism" represented by Kohlkberg's (pp. 137-138), Bernestein's (pp. 139-141), and Rorty's views (pp. 142-145), which are also reviewed in this chapter, would exceed the parameters of this paper.  

I will consider the section on "Pragmatism as a Philosophy of Education" in the following  critical appraisal.

(2) Professional critique of the perspective

A first important point is that "Pragmatists do not believe that 'training' is the same as education" (p. 146).  One can condition children to behave the way adults want them to behave, as Skinner claims. For Dewey, though, educating is essentially different from conditioning.  Humans can understand and act accordingly.  The aim of education is to promote conduct based upon understanding.  While I personally think that conduct is initially a matter of habituation, and that no good conduct is possible without good habituation, I deeply agree with Dewey that the role of the school is not to promote good conduct on the basis of blind conditioning but, rather, to promote adaptive conduct based on flexible understanding.

A second positive point relates to Dewey's conviction that individuals should be educated as social beings, capable of participating in, and directing their own, social affairs.  Indeed, education should play a vital part in helping people to become the kind of moral persons who are not only interested in promoting their own growth but also in promoting the growth of others. This lesson is particularly important in a world in which, much against Dewey's wishes and teaching, we seem to have lost sight of the interdependence and mutually supportive roles of the individual and the group.   

    Finally, I want to mention my measured sympathy for Dewey's insistence on the use of the method of real life situations to encourage the problem-solving ability of the students in a practical setting.  If this is taken with moderation and applied intelligently, it is an excellent method.  On the other hand, it is also part and parcel of the real life situation that educators have to educate large groups of people who are on different levels of mental and motivational development, so that a concrete problem that challenges and enthuses a particular student does not work in the same way for others.  The experimental method is very good in theory, but it takes a good educator to know how and when it can be fruitfully applied.  In addition, one has to take into account that oftentimes the experimental method takes a disproportionate amount of time to attain a solution that is already available, and that a very important portion of education in a developed society consists in receiving what previous generations have accumulated, in order to begin at a more advantageous point.  In short, without disparaging the experimental method, one should combine it, at least in my opinion, with a solid, time-proven curriculum.

(3) Summary Statement

As a reaction against the oppression of blind tradition, of unwarranted authority, of insecurity in the face of the unavoidable fluidity of the world, the promotion by pragmatism of experientialism, experimentalism, and intelligent adaptability--and their application to the field of education--are most welcome.

However, whether these rather general principles amount to a coherent philosophy, and whether their application has had the consistently "good", "testable consequences" promised by William James and Dewey is far from clear to me.

LORETTA KING
