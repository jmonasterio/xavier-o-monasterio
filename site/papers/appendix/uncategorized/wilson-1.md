METAPHYSICAL QUESTIONS IN SARTRE’S PHENOMENOLOGICAL ONTOLOGY:

A COMMENTARY ON JEFFREY WILSON’S PAPER

Xavier O. Monasterio

University of Dayton

Jeffrey Wilson has written a dense paper on the questions designated by Sartre as metaphysical in Being and Nothingness. Jeffrey’s own treatment of those questions shows an impressive mastery of Sartre’s philosophical project. Hence my first and sincerely complimentary comment on Jeffrey’s paper is: “I wish I possessed half that mastery!” Needless to say, my other comments will have to be slightly longer...

Jeffrey’s purpose is “to clarify what metaphysics is for Sartre and how it is to be distinguished from ontology; why some metaphysics is spurious, some legitimate; and why the legitimate philosophical questions are important for Sartre given his ontology” (p. 1).

For obvious reasons, Jeffrey’s paper focuses primarily on the two questions that Sartre himself sees as legitimate metaphysical questions, both of which are raised in the first section of the Conclusion of BN.  However, in order to clarify how metaphysics differs from ontology, and why some metaphysical questions are spurious, Jeffrey also deals with the questions that Sartre deems illegitimate, three which are scattered through the corpus of BN and two others are included in the Conclusion. Let me begin by reviewing this part of Jeffrey’s paper.

First question: “How was there an in-itself before the birth of the for-itself?”

Second question: “How was the for-itself born from this in-itself rather than from another?”  Third question: “Why are there Others apart from myself?” .

“Sartre mentions two more spurious metaphysical questions in the Conclusion”:

“Why is being in-itself other?” and “Why is it that there is being?”

As for Jeffrey’s exploration of the grounds on which these questions are spurious, the results are as follows. Some of them are spurious because they are not truly metaphysical, since in fact they are actually answered within the Sartrean ontology. Some others  are spurious because they cannot be coherently raised on the presupposition of the validity of Sartre’s ontology. Finally, some are spurious because the only answer they can receive, on the presupposition of the validity of Sartre’s ontology, is that things are as they are for no reason at all, just because they are the way they are, by sheer contingency

This leaves to be considered only the two questions raised in the Conclusion to

BN that Sartre himself deems to be genuinely metaphysical.. Before coming to them, however, it will be useful to remind ourselves of the lessons concerning the difference between metaphysics and ontology drawn by Jeffrey in the course of his exploration. The fundamental difference is that ontology, according to Sartre and as exemplified in BN as “an essay in phenomenological ontology,” is descriptive, and therefore attains certainty, whereas metaphysics “is a matter of explanations, especially explanations of origins and causes of beings, and therefore its explanatory hypotheses are merely probable” (p.4).    

 

I come, at last, to Jeffrey’s treatment of the two questions Sartre considers genuinely metaphysical. The first of them, that is, the sixth in my series, is “Why does the for-itself arise in terms of Being?” (p.  1). The longest, and by far the richest portion of Jeffrey’s paper, is devoted to a superb analysis of “what Sartre calls ‘the ontological problem of being’” (p. 1) with which the metaphysical question of the origin of the for-itself in terms of Being is closely connected. Jeffrey’s conclusion, with which I cannot but concur, is that–contrary to Sartre’s own view--this question is not genuinely metaphysical either, since Sartre has already given to it within his ontology the full and fully satisfactory answer specified by Jeffrey. If Jeffrey is right on this point, however, then out of the seven questions that Sartre raises as possibly metaphysical six are definitely spurious, and only the second one raised in the Conclusion remains as a possible candidate for genuiness.

The seventh and last metaphysical question raised by Sartre “has to do with the unity of being or the character of being in general (p. 6); in other words, with the possibility of formulating a general theory of being that would complete his ontology” (p. 6). Is this a genuine metaphysical question?  In agreement with Sartre this time, Jeffrey thinks that it is. “Even though Sartre rejects the classical notion of being in general in the sense of to holon , Sartre maintains that we have an awareness of being as totality in the form of a preontological comprehension of ens causa sui , the God who is absent, nonexistent, and the concept of whose existence is self-contradictory” (op. 7).  “Here, as in the problem of the existence of others, ontology ends in an ‘as if,’ the ontologist can propose that being has as its meaning the impossible God who escapes it, but to affirm that being does in fact have that meaning would be a metaphysical assertion” (p. 6). But why is this metaphysical question, Jeffrey asks very pertinently, more genuine than that of the existence of others, which also involves a totalization? Because in the case of the existence of others a witness disengaged from the totality would be required, which is nonsensical. Here, by contrast, “the for-itself can be exhaustively conscious of the detotalized totality through its power to look both ways, [since] human consciousness is both consciousness of being and self-consciousness” (p. 6). As to why  Sartre thinks that metaphysics should at least attempt to answer this particular question, the reason is, in Jeffrey’s view, that, though Sartre is certain that human consciousness is free in the sense that it determines its own manner of being and makes its own choices, another aspect of freedom is power not over other consciousnesses but over things in the world. Thus, as Jeffrey eloquently concludes his paper to show the importance for Sartre of this genuinely metaphysical question, “If metaphysics could demonstrate this transcendent efficacy, it would powerfully allay the residual fear–or, from the point of view of bad faith, it would remove the consoling possibility–that things have power over consciousness” (p.8).

With this point Jeffrey completes his inquiry.  Which is to say that the rather odd result of his exploration of the metaphysical questions suggested by Sartre is that, according to Sartre himself, only two out of seven turn out to be genuine questions. And the case is even odder if we side with Jeffrey rather than with Sartre, in that only one out of seven–the question concerning the unity of being in its connection with the efficacy of human action--is genuine.

For my part, I must confess that I am rather surprised by the oddity of these results and want to raise a question that Jeffrey fails to consider. If, as Jeffrey concludes, Sartre is wrong concerning the legitimate character of one of the only  two metaphysical questions he sees as genuine, couldn’t Sartre and Jeffrey be also wrong concerning the legitimacy of the other? And shouldn’t this possibility prompt the suspicion that all the supposed metaphysical questions raised by Sartre are spurious because metaphysics itself is spurious, art least within the framework of Sartre’s ontology? This is an issue concerning which I would like to hear Jeffrey’s opinion.

The first fact in support of my view that all metaphysical questions are spurious within the framework of Sartre’s ontology is that the distinction Sartre establishes between metaphysics and ontology is untenable. Indeed, according to Sartre, as we were reminded by Jeffrey, the difference between ontology and metaphysics is that ontology is purely descriptive, whereas metaphysics is explanatory. However, as forcefully argued by Jeffrey, the fact is that Sartre’s ontology is not merely descriptive but heavily explanatory. Hence Jeffrey rightly wonders “how much of Sartre’s system would remain if he were to operate on the level of pure ontology, obeying his own edicts against metaphysical speculation” (p.6).  In other words, Sartre’s first criterion to distinguish metaphysical from ontological questions fails as a criterion and is actually contradicted by Sartre’s own ontological practice, which is not merely descriptive. In this regard, therefore, Sartre’s ontology id indistinguishable from metaphysics.

There is, however, a second difference proposed by Sartre between metaphysics and ontology, to wit: that ontology is certain, whereas metaphysics is only probable. Does this leave room for metaphysics aside from ontology? I don’t think so. Indeed, the reason why Sartre attributes certainty to ontology and denies it to metaphysics is that ontology is purely descriptive, whereas metaphysics is explanatory and therefore hypothetical. However, Jeffrey has persuasively shown that Sartre’s ontology is not purely descriptive. By the same token, however, ontology cannot attain the certainty that Sartre attributes to it and is indistinguishable from metaphysics.

Should we therefore conclude that phenomenological ontology doesn’t differ at all from what Sartre calls metaphysics and is as suspect as metaphysics? No. And the reason is that Sartre is wrong as to the difference between metaphysics and ontology. Indeed, the real difference is that, as pointed out by Jeffrey himself, phenomenological ontology can at least appeal to our experience–as Sartre constantly does in BN–although without thereby attaining the absolute certainty or infallibility Sartre claims for it, whereas metaphysics would necessarily transcend experience and, therefore, be spurious as a source of answers. What, for instance, could be the basis on which metaphysics would answer the question of the efficacy of human action aside from the fact that sometimes we experience that the effects intended by us attend our actions? And, if so, is not the only question that both Sartre and Jeffrey deem legitimate, spurious as a metkaphysical question, because no meaningful answer that transcends experience can be given to it?

In short, my contention is that Sartre failed to see the incoherence of any metaphysical project with his ontology, and that Jeffrey, although right in his criticism of Sartre, didn’t go far enough in his analysis, since he granted him the legitimacy of at least one metaphysical question.
