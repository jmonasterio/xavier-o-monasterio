                                        PREFACE

      The prevailing philosophical paradigm, which comes  from  the  Greeks,
is at odds with our pre-reflective experience because   it  is  modelled  on
our reflective experience. By reflective experience I  mean  the  experience
we have, not when we live, but when we think about what we  live;  not  when
we participate, but when we observe. The result is that the paradigm  itself
prevents  us  from  understanding  ourselves  and   our   world.   This   is
particularly true of our religious experience. In philosophy of religion  it
all is matter of "evidence" for God's existence. The same  is  true,  though
to a lesser  extent,  in  ethical  matters.  This  book  was  born  from  my
impatience with the philosophical paradigm.  And,  because  of  my  personal
interest in religious matters, I decided to concentrate  on them. In a  way,
then, my project is similar to Kant's: to make room for faith, for there  is
none within a paradigm that forces us to look for  'evidence,'  and  for  an
'evidence' of a purely intellectual kind--unmotivating evidence.
      Several hunches have guided me in this undertaking.
(1) Sartre's criticism of the point of view of exteriority.
(2) Hans Frei'sattempt to retrieve the originality of the narrative genre.
(3) An application of the above to the birth of Scholasticism.
(4) The dramaticism of narration as distinguished from description.
(5) Plantinga's separation between the affective  and  the  intellectual  in
the case of the problem of evil.
(6) Hume's distinction between facts and values.
(7) Sartre's treatment of the Other's existence as well as his criticism  of
leveling the Other's existence with  the  existence  of  things.  (Descartes
puts on the same level the existence of a table, the existence  of  the  guy
he sees from his window, and the existence of his own body).
(8) Shearson's "notion of encounter."
(9) Existential understanding as the participant's understanding  (cf.  John
Macquarrie, An Existentialist Theology).
(10) Problems of concern (existential problems) and  problems  of  curiosity
(puzzles).
(11) Ricoeur on Job.
(12) The traditional distinction between the God  of  philosophers  and  the
God of faith, between natural revelation and supernatural  revelation.  Here
enters the question of exposure and of mere observation, and of exposure  to
the Other and to things.
(l3) Martin Buber's "I and Thou."
      Perhaps the main problem of my work so far is that I have remained  on
the  methodological  level.  I  need,  at  the  same  time,  deal  with  the
particular content that interests me.
      I am at the point where I have to decide the concrete approach I  will
use concerning the Scholastics. To this I will address myself now.
      1) Frei: Determine very exactly what is his point or points.
De Lubac  had  noticed  the  same  shift  concerning  the  Scholastics  and,
specifically, Saint Thomas (p. 57 of The Sources of  Revelation).  De  Lubac
may even be right in saying that today "The general  atmosphere  is  hostile
to symbolism. Only the 'conservatory' o the liturgy offers it some  kind  of
haven and affords it a precarious continued existence" (p. 62). But,  thanks
to Frei, this is no longer true of narrative as  such  (cf.,  for  instance,
Scriptural  ASuthority  and  Narrative  Interpretation,  edfited  by  Garret
Green).  Even  in  philosophy,  there  is  Ricoeur  in  particular  and  the
hermeneutic  current   initiated   by   Gadamer,   though   there   is   the
deconstructionist current that applies to  all  literary  genres.  There  is
also the recognition  of  other  speech  acts  than  the  stating  one.  And
MacIntyre's at least initial concern with the narrative structure  of  life.
And the Vico current and the philosophical interest in rhetoric and  in  the
Aristotle of the Ethics and Politics rather than of the Categories  and  the
Metaphysics. Most of these are positive elements to be taken  into  account,
though they remain elements, for they hardly can be said  to  have  resulted
in any kind of coherent synthesis with a definite and  intelligible  matrix.
My ambition, of course, is to reach that point. But my  particular  emphasis
is that, at least in professional philosophy of religion, there has been  no
impact of such currents. It remains dominated by Cartesians like  Plantinga.
Given this more narrow emphasis, how does the Scholastics' case fit in?
      Frei made clear the originality of narrative (cf. my future  paper  on
Frei). I believe that the loss of narrative started  with  the  Scholastics.
Why, how. and with what disastrous and long-term
effects is something I have to show, following Frei's model  to  the  useful
extent. But, like him, I do not intend this as a mere  piece  of  historical
scholarship, adding a link to Frei's chain as a contribution to the  history
of ideas; I intend it, as Frei does, as a regret for a loss--and I  have  to
make clear what was actually lost,  which  is  much  more  than  de  Lubac's
spiritual sense, though connected with it--and, hopefully, as  a  suggestion
for a viable alternative. Thus I have to start with  my  paper  on  Frei  to
formalize the model and, then, to apply it to the Scholastics with the  help
of Chenu.
