2002-feb-1-Rel. Truth February 12, 2002

Lindbeck on Religious Truth

   ( The Nature of Doctrine, Ch. 3, Sect. !V)

- p. 63: That Xt is Lord is a propositional truth, i.e., the particular individual of which the stories are told is, was, and will be definitively and unsurpassably the Lord.

- p. 64: Ontological truth of statements : truth of correspondence to reality, which according to epistemological realists is attributable to first order propositions.

Intrasystematic truth of statements or utterances : Is truth of coherence with the total relevant context. In the context of religion viewed in cultural-linguistic terms , coherence with other utterances and also with the correlative forms of life. For a xtian “Xt is Lord” or “God is three in one” are true only as parts of a total pattern of speaking, thinking, feeling and acting. They are false when their use is inconsistent with what the pattern as a whole affirms of God’s being and will. The Crusader’s battle cry “Xtus est Dominus”, f. e., is false when used to authorize cleaving the skull of the infidel: It contradicts the xtian understanding of Lordship as embodying suffering servanthood. If the form of life and understanding of the world shaped by an authentic use of the xtian stories does in fact correspond to God’s being and will, then the proper use of “Xtus est Dominus” is not only intrasystematically but also ontologically true.

The ontological truth of religious utterances, in cultural-linguistic terms , that is, their correspondence to reality, is only a function of their role in constituting a form of life, a way of being in the world that corresponds to the being and will of God. In this way, as performatives, by realizing the conformity, statements may be conceived as having the propositional force of an ontological truth claim, even though they do not fit the model of a proposition as adaequatio mentis ad rem.  (P. 66): However, they fit Paul’s and Luther’s model. Luther: I cannot genuinely affirm that Xt is ‘the’ Lord unless I thereby make him ‘my’ Lord. This view is difficult to accommodate with the cognitivists’ view that the meaning, truth and falsity of propositions are independent of the subjective dispositions of those who utter them , as well as with the experiential-expressive interpretation which reduces the religious significance of such statements as ‘Jesus is the Lord’ to their role of symbolizing inner experiences or existential orientations. Of course Paul and Luther believed that Xt’s lordship is objectively real no matter what the faith or unfaith of those who hear or say the words. What they meant is that the only way to assert this truth is to do something about it, to commit oneself to a way of life, to conform oneself to Jesus’s lordship .

In the linguistic view the correspondence is as limited as in the modest cognitivism or propositionalism of Aquinas, .who maintains that in statements about God the human mode of signifying ( modus significandi ) does not correspond to anything in the divine being, the signified ( significatum ) does. [ST I,13,3; CG I, 30}. F. e., when we say that God is good, we do not affirm that any of our concepts of goodness ( modus significandi ) apply to him, but rather that there is a concept of goodness unavailable to us, viz., God’s understanding of his own goodness, which does apply. What we assert, in other words, is that ‘God is good” is meaningful and true’ but without knowing the meaning of ‘God is good.’ Theologians use analogies to exclude erroneous interpretations, but they are only able to specify how these predications cannot correspond, not how they correspond to reality. Yet despite this informational vacuity the significatum can be affirmed: it is possible to claim that the judgment “God is good” refers or corresponds to an objective reality even when one cannot specify the modus significandi by offering a falsifiable description of God’s goodness. - (p. 67): But what is the function of truth claims about God if their cognitive content is so minimal? They can appeal to the performative function of religious utterances earlier mentioned. They might say that the claim that God is good in himself is of the utmost importance because it authorizes responding as if he were good in the ways indicated by the stories of creation, providence and redemption which shape believers’ thoughts and actions. Or, to put the same in another way, seriously to commit oneself to thinking and acting as if god were good in relation to us ( quoad nos ) in the ways indicated by the stories involves asserting that he really is good in himself ( in se ), even though as the canonical texts testify, the meaning of this latter claim is utterly beyond human comprehension. Similarly, the significatum of the claim that Jesus truly and objectively was raised from the dead provides a warrant for behaving in the ways recommended by the resurrection stories even when one grants the impossibility of specifying the mode in which the stories signify. There is no reason not to apply Aquinas’s notion of analogical knowledge to resurrection (and creation, consummation, etc.) as well as to the being of God. In short Aquinas’s ‘agnosticisim” is not incompatible with the kind of performative-propositional theological theory of truth that fits a cultural linguistic approach.

Coherence is as necessary in geometry as in the religious domain. A theorem that wold demonstrate that parallels would meet would be false. The difference is that in the xtian case the system is constituted not in purely intellectual terms by axioms, definitions and corollaries, but by a set of stories used in specifiable ways to interpret and live in the world. The mistake of a primarily cognitive-propositional theory of religion is to overlook this difference, to be unable to do justice to the fact that a religious system is more like a natural language than a formally organized set of explicit statements, and that the right use of this language, unlike a mathematical one, cannot be detached from a particular way of behaving.

N.B. A sentence may be used propositionally or non-propositionally. “This car is red,” as used on this page, is not a proposition, for it specifies no particular car and no particular time; it can be neither true nor false. The same with religious sentences : only in determinate settings do they acquire enough referential specificity to have fist-order ontological truth or falsity, and this rarely happens on the pages of theological treatises or in the course of doctrinal discussions.  F.e., the sentence “Xt is the Lord” becomes a first order proposition capable (as non-idealists would say) of making ontological truth-claims only as it is used in the activities of adoration, proclamation, obedience, promise-hearing and promise-keeping, which shape individuals and communities into conformity to the mind of Xt. - If so, those unskilled in the language of faith cannot affirm or deny that Jesus is Lord, as one cannot affirm or deny that this car is red unless one knows the circumstances and reference in which the sentence was uttered. One must be, so to say, inside the relevant context. In the case of religion, one must have some skill in how  to use the language and practice its way of life before the propositional meaning of its affirmations becomes determinate enough to be rejected. Thus non-believers (atheists or members of other religions) cannot accept or reject xtian salvation. This explains why apostasy is regarded as more serious than refusal of faith. In the absence of actual practice, the non-believer cannot understand the propositional claims of xtianity.

Conclusion concerning epistemological realism and the correspondence theory of truth supposedly implicit in the conviction of believers.

My comments

FOR THE COGNITIVISTS, a language is primarily a cognitional system, that is, a system  that  renders possible to mirror in the mind what is the case in order to know it and, on this basis, to act appropriately . The basic linguistic unit is the statement which expresses a judgment of what is the case, for only statements can mirror or fail to mirror what is the case. Each statement is true or false in itself , independently of the circumstances in which it is made and of the subject’s dispositions toward the object known. Characteristics of this model of  language are: - Primacy of knowledge over action, - independence of knowledge from action, and - independence of knowledge from feelings or sentiment (pure objectivity, visual conformity to the object, dictated by the object).

The above applies to religious knowledge, too , so that the subject’s dispositions toward God and the subject’s actions are irrelevant to the meaning and truth of statements. The only difference from non-religious language is that, because of God’s nature and the nature of the human mind, we cannot know God directly but only through analogy with created reality. As a result, what we predicate of God mirrors what He is quoad significatum but not quoad modum significandi .  When we say “God is good” we don’t know in what way He is good, but only that He is good in a way that totally escapes us. So really we are equivocating, if we take our knowledge literally; but our analogical knowledge helps us to exclude false predications.

FOR THE CULTURAL-LINGUISTS, a language is a particular way of structuring the totality within which the subject himself or herself is situated, not therefore as a contemplating transcendent, invulnerable mind, but as an exposed, vulnerable agent. The best way to appreciate the difference between the two alternatives is to compare the way in which first-order statements function in each of them, beginning with religious language.

In the cognitivist alternative, as remarked by Lindbeck, doctrines are first-order statements.

That is, they mean propositionally, they say  what is the case and, consequently, are either true or false by corresponding or failing to correspond to what is the case. Not so according to the linguists. For the latter, truth and falsity characterize  religious  language only when it is used religiously or as first order language, that is, when it is used to mold lives through prayer, praise, preaching and exhortation, for only then do the linguistic agents exhibit their truth or falsity, their correspondence or coherence with the system shaped by the biblical stories.

Think of Luther and Paul: I can only truthfully say “Xt is the Lord” if I thereby make him my Lord by constituting myself as his unconditional servant. Here the truth is not theoretical but practical. This was Jesus’s model, too: It is not those who say “Lord, Lord” but those who do my Father’s will.   

In this linguistic model, truth is not a matter of theoretical or contemplative correspondence between mind and reality – seeing what is the case– but of practical correspondence –disposition to act in the way appropriate to the object under the appropriate circumstances. Here the truth is not to be seen but to be performed or realized through speech and action by saying the appropriate thing and doing the appropriate action under the appropriate circumstances. In the cognitivist model the subject is a knower, a knowing mind; correlatively, the object is a pure known, a purely objectively known. By contrast, in the linguistic model the subject is an exposed agent; correlatively, the object is object-to-be-responded-to according to its identity. If the object-to-be-responded-to is Lord, it cannot be grasped as Lord but by being served by the agent. Serving Him as Lord is the only way to know Him as Lord, which is what Luther meant. It is also what Jesus meant when he said that a merely theoretical recognition of God’s lordship in words, without actual service in deeds, is no recognition at all.

Of course, if knowledge is inseparable from action because the object is not a to-be-known -objectively but a to-be-responded-to, then the affective attitudes toward the object are intrinsic constitutives of the linguistic model.

Before coming to the question of doctrines, let us explore a little further the issue of truth in the two models. Aquinas says that we cannot know God directly in Himself or in his essence but only by analogy to his creatures Lindbeck comments that, in spite of its informational vacuity, this mild agnosticism  allows, in the linguistic model, to respond to God as if he were good in the way indicated by the biblical stories. The implication here is double: (a) Something is missed concerning God by the informational vacuity of religious language regarding God’s essence. (b) This doesn’t happen in the case of statements in non-religious language. I want to dispute these implications, for I think that the case of statements in non-religious language is the same as the case of statements about God in religious language, and that nothing is lost by not knowing God’s essence (that is a notion that can be meaningful only within the context of knowledge as theoretical correspondence),

as nothing is lost in non-religious language by knowing practically rather than theoretically.

Take the case of the statement. We are together in the same room, and I tell you, “Your mother is on the phone.” If you hear me and have no reason to believe that I am lying or joking, you take the phone to talk to your mother. My speech-act was thereby fully successful, and you acted as a competent linguistic agent, for that was what I intended to achieve.

What now about doctrines? In the cognitivist model they are supposed to function propositionally, as first order assertions that mirror the object. In the linguistic model the case is more complex, since sentences don’t mean by themselves; it is the utterers of sentences who mean, and they utter in particular circumstances on which what they mean depends. Thus the meaning of “This car is yours” changes if I am the addressee or not, and it cannot be uttered as if I were the addressee but under circumstances under which the message can reach me, which depends on the circumstances of the utterer in respect of me. In the linguistic model, first order propositions are always particular in that they are addressed by a particular addresser to a particular addressee or group of addressees, in particular circumstances which contribute to determine what is or was meant, and they always refer to a particular object to-be -responded to.   The question, therefore, concerning whether doctrines can be first-order propositions is whether doctrines can fulfill these conditions, so as t o mean as first order propositions.

In the cognitivist model, there is no problem, since propositions supposedly refer to an object-to-be-known, not to-be-responded-to. The doctrine, “Christ is the Lord,” in the cognitivist model, refers to an object to be known theoretically. And anything asserted can be known theoretically in any circumstance, as long as it is intelligible and what is asserted reaches a hearer. If it is assented to by a hearer, as an assertion is meant to be, the knowledge that the utterer possessed and wanted to convey is gained by the hearer. The hearer, though, has nothing else to do than understand and assent to the doctrine, for that suffices to render successful the doctrinal speech-act. If, however, as is the case in the linguistic model, in order to function as a first-order proposition,  a doctrine refers to an object-to-be-responded-to --a Gegenstand rather than an Objekt–, then the addressee has to align himself dispositionally or practically in order to render the speech act successful. Then the question is whether doctrines, which because of their lack of circumstantial specificity seem to convey general teachings, can and do actually convey a Gegenstand in certain cases.

For the cognitivist, it is chiefly technical theology and doctrine which are propositional; for the linguist, propositional truth and falsity characterize ordinary religious language when it is used to mold lives through prayer, praise, preaching, and exhortation. Only on this level do humans linguistically exhibit their truth or falsity, their correspondence or lack thereof to the Ultimate Mystery, while theology and official doctrine are second-order discourse about first-intentional uses of religious language. On the level of doctrine, one seldom if ever succeeds in making affirmations with ontological import, bur rather one explains defends, analyzes, and regulates the liturgical, kerygmatic, and ethical modes of speech and action within which such affirmations from time to time occur. Just as grammar as second-order language affirms nothing either true or false regarding the world in which language is used, but only about language, so theology and doctrine, as second-order activities, assert nothing either true or false about God and his relation to creatures, but only speak of such assertions. These assertions, in their turn, cannot be made except when speaking religiously, i.e. when seeking to align oneself and others performatively with what one takes to be the most important in the universe by worshiping, promising, obeying, exhorting, preaching.
