                       ORAL EXAMS : SOPHIST

[Division of genera into species to create classifications and  definitions;
the problem of being and non-being raised by Parmenides, connected with  the
one and the many, the same and the other; language. All  this  in  order  to
define the sophist by contrast to the philosopher].

- Subject of the dialogue: The Eleatic Stranger  brought  by  Theodorus  and
Theaetetus, a true philosopher (but not of the disputatious kind),  disciple
of Parmenides and Zeno, is asked by  Socrates  whether  in  Italy  "Sophist,
statesm  and,  philosopher"  are  synonyms  and  if   not   how   they   are
distinguished and defined.  "By us, says the Stranger, they are regarded  as
three. But to define precisely the nature of each is by no  menas  a  slight
or easy task." (The Stranger  chooses  Theaetetus  as  his  partner  in  the
dialogue).

- First on the nature of the Sophist
      At the beginning we only have a name in common.  We  should  reach  an
understanding of the thing itself in terms of a defniition.  Before  tacking
the difficult definition of Sophiust some minor excercises are in  order.
      - An "angler": You and I have  come  to  an  understanding  about  the
definition of the thing itself. One half of all art  was  acquisitive,  half
of the acquisitive was conquest by force, half of this was hunting, half  of
hunting was junting animals, half of this was hunting  water  animals,  half
of this was fishing, half of fishing was  striking,  half  of  striking  eas
fisihing with a barb, and one half of tghis (being the  kind  thakt  strikes
with a hook and draws the fish from below upwards--is the are we  have  been
seeking. Now, following this pattern, let us find out hwkat a Sophist is.
      - In the rest of the dialogue he defines the sophist, of  whom  he  is
very critical, by contrast to the philosopher. His
FINAL CONCLUSION IS A CHARACTERIZATION OF THE SOPHIST AS:
"He, then, who traces the pedigree of his art as follows--who, belonging  to
the  conscious  or  dissembling  section  of  the  art  of   causing   self-
contradiction, is an imitator of  appearance,  and  is  spearated  from  the
class of phantastic which is a branch  of  image-making  into  that  further
division of creation, the juggling of  words,  a  creation  human,  and  not
divine--anyone who affirms the real Sophist to be of this blood and  lineage
will say the very truth."
