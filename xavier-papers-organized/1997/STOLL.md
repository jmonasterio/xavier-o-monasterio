December 16, 1997

Mary Lyn Stoll

Department of PHilosoophy

1360 Liberal Arts and Education Building

Purdue Univeristy

West Lafayette, IN 47907-1360

A long time ago I asked you for your paper on Kierkegaard and postmodernity. When you sent it to me you solicited questions and comments. I'm sorry it took me so long to get back to you.

I am not an expert in Kierkegaard. I have read, and even reread, a number of his works (much more the Postscript than the others), but very little of the secondary literature. So you shouldn't take my comments too seriously.

I found your paper very illuminating. You did a very good job in reminding your readers that truth, subjectivity, and indirect communication go together, and that taking any of them in isolation from the others unavoidably leads to misunderstanding Kierkegaard. I particularly appreciated your sharp treatment of the radical difference between Kierkegaard and the postmodern deconstructionists, which was the main thrust of your paper. Kierkegaard's indirect communication was at the service of truth in matters of passionate interest to the subject; to confuse this with the continual dissemination of doubt of the postmodern deconstructionists is wrongheaded.

I must add, however, to be complete, that I found your treatment of indirect communication less compelling than the rest of your paper. I have always found Kierkegaard himself confused to an extent, and certainly very confusing, on the issue of indirect communication, particularly in its relationship to pseudonymous authorship. Thus it may well be that I was expecting too much clarification on that subject, which is not by itself the central issue of your paper. Anyway, I think that your concrete example, "I have an abiding passion for teaching," cannot do the job you assign it. It seems to me that indirect communication has to do with truths (like the truth of Christianity) which the receiver of the communication has to appropriate by way of decision. I do not see how the receiver of your communication that you have an abiding

passion for teaching can be involved in that way. To be blunt, your example itself makes me doubt that your understanding of indirect communication is correct. HOWEVER, as I remarked above, I am not at all an expert in Kierkegaard, and any clarification on your part would enlighten me.

If ever you write something more on indirect communication, please let me know. Of all the aspects of Kierkegaard's thought it is the obscurest for me.

I thank you heartily for having shared your paper with me, and I wish you a merry Christmas.

Sincerely,

Xavier O. Monasterio
