2002-Jan-1-Indexical January 21,2001

All texts are indexical

“Thinking of religion as a cultural product thus directs attention to the process of its mediation; in other words, to how the content of religion is produced, transmitted, and received. Since the transmission of religion or any other symbolic form involves the packaging of meanings, the mediation process is a thoroughly interpretive activity... Interpretive activity, as we know from scholars of the mass media, is an active and creative process, one that is socially, historically, and locally contextualized... In this sense, as Dorothy Smith (1990b; 197) observes), ’ALL TEXTS ARE INDEXICAL’ SINCE ‘THEIR MEANING IS NOT FULLY CONTAINED IN THEM BUT COMPLETED IN THE SETTING OF THEIR READING.’  In making sense of the meanings packaged by a producer, the interpreter creates new meanings. Accordingly, the ‘reception’ or interpretation of meaning is itself part of the meaning process... In practice, therefore, a sharp dividing line cannot be drawn between the production and reception of symbolic content, and this includes religious doctrine” (Michelle Dillon, Catholic Identity: Balancing Reason, Faith, and Power, Cambridge U Press, 1999, p. 26).

To determine what interests me about indexicality and the indexicality of all texts, let us begin with the concrete case of the indexicality of perception, which is totally ignored in the common understanding of perception by its ‘content’ or ‘object.’

Perception is structured around the subject who perceives as its center. Indexicality is this tacit pointing of any particular perception to the perceiver. Husserl called this the perspectival structure of perception. Sartre related perception to action and completed Husserl by showing that the subject toward which perception is oriented is not a contemplator but an exposed agent, an agent within the perceived field . From seeing a picture one can determine where the camera was and, therefore, where the eye of the photographer was. Actually the picture places us imaginarily or virtually where the photographer was by making us see what the photographer saw, for the spectacle that was oriented toward the photographer is now oriented, thanks to the picture seen, toward the contemplator of the picture. One difference, however, between the photographer and myself who see his picture is that the photographer was the center of perspective of the picture as an agent, whereas I am not the center toward which his picture is oriented as an agent but just as a contemplator. He could do something to the objects he saw, but I cannot, for I am not perceiving what he photographed, I am perceiving a a photograph of those objects.

Now, the important point for me concerning the indexicality of perception (which can be extended to all intentional acts, that is, to all conscious moments) is that, as Sartre puts it, all consciousness of something is consciousness (of) self. In other words, all awareness of anything involves on the subject’s part a non-tethical awareness of the subject as the center toward which the object is oriented. In the case of perception, though, this means that whenever I perceive, by the very fact that what I perceive points at me as center, I am non-focally aware of myself as subject in relation to what I perceive. Concretely, I do not only perceive the table, but I perceive the table near (me-subject) or far away from (me-subject). And since perception points at me as exposed agent, this near or far from me comes qualified with an index of positive or negative instrumentality depending on my project. In other words, an “I can” or “I can’t” is the way in which what I perceive points at me.

One of the defects of Sartre’s analysis is that it deals with the agent’s consciousness rather than with the agent. Supposedly, Sartre replaces the agent in the world, but he replaces the agent as a consciousness, not really as an agent, for a consciousness can neither act nor be acted upon.

Having started with consciousness, like Descartes-Husserl, his body-mind dualism, and therefore his subjectivist idealism, constricted him at least until “Materialism and Revolution.” And even afterwards he couldn’t quite get rid of his individualism and quite overcome the dualism between his subjectivism and his materialism. It seems to me that he would have had a better chance of putting things together in the right way if he had started his analysis from the standpoint of language and culture, in the stile of what for other purposes Simone de Beauvoir conducted his analysis of being woman in Le deuxième sexe.

De Beauvoir shows that being woman in the Western world is not a biological destiny, and therefore, inescapable, but a Western cultural destiny. Woman has been socially or culturally  predestined to be object --other than man who is subject. But how does this predestination actually take place? Through language. Our native language colonizes us.

Language is not an abstract, preexisting system of signs that one learns to use as an instrument to say what one wants to say and to understand what others say. Language is always a particular language. It is always learned and spoken within a particular community of speakers-hearers, and there is a close relationship between verbality and conduct. To put it otherwise, there is no way one can genuinely come to understand a language, that is, be a genuine participant in a given linguistic community, without being an agent in it, that is, without acting within it and being acted upon. This acting and being acted upon is linguistic, in-formed by language. It consists, concretely, in playing roles. Or, in Wittgenstein’s idiolect, in playing linguistic games.

A very important characteristic of language is that, like Sartre’s intentionality of consciousness, language is indexical, in that the speaker is always indicated to himself or herself and to others by the roles he or she plays, precisely because a role is necessarily linguistic, not merely a behavior; it occurs as a common game played with others rather than a solitary game, within a linguistic community without which it would be impossible. I couldn’t possibly learn the social game of obeying without an other giving me orders that I didn’t issue and may or may not like, and that I follow in acting accordingly because I feel first constrained and then obligated to follow. If Gyges had been born with his famous ring on, he wouldn’t have become a member of a linguistic community and therefore wouldn’t have become a genuinely linguistic agent, for he wouldn’t have been able to play the passive linguistic roles, which constitute as essential a half of  being a linguistic agent as the active ones. For instance, he wouldn’t have been able to be held responsible effectively for his conduct by being punished for his bad actions, and therefore he wouldn’t have been able to learn to hold himself responsible for his actions. Actually, he wouldn’t even have learned that certain actions are bad and certain actions are good, for this is learned in the first place by being object of the linguistic response of others to our actions, before being subject who responds positively or negatively to other people’s actions because we have interiorized the standards of the community.  

Consider first, abstractly, the roles that language renders possible. As a member of a linguistic community I can act as an addresser or as an addressee. Theoretically at least, I can give and receive orders. A dog cannot do that, not only because dogs cannot articulate verbally an order, but because they cannot learn to participate in a linguistic community. In ‘obeying’ an order of his master, a dog is not obeying as a competent linguistic agent obeys. In the dog’s case it is pure conditioning, as it is in a young child. In an adult’s case, obeying an order is a linguistic response. What does this mean?

Consider Helen Keller’s accession to her linguistic community. According to Susanne Langer, Helen accessed to language by learning the concept ‘water’ as a concept, not as a sign of water. For a dog, its master’s name is a sign of the imminent presence of his master. A human can hear his mother’s name and ask “What about her?” A human can conceptualize (connote), and then refer (denote)  to his or her mother in different ways through the concept. Langer has a primitive idea of language. She thinks primarily of a system of symbols and totally ignores linguistic roles determined by the games one can play with others. Helen accessed to language not by learning that words signify concepts (this, at best, is the case only of names), but by learning to communicate with others, to play linguistic games with others in which he played definite roles.

If Helen had made statements to others, such as “The hammer is on the table,” and others hadn’t played the game by acting according to what Helen had stated, Helen wouldn’t have learned to state nor to act according to what others stated. If nothing had happened when Helen asked for water, Helen wouldn’t have learned the begging game. And so on. In other words, the illocutionary effect of speech-acts is only an abstraction from the perlocutionary one, and speech-acts are themselves abstractions from the roles one plays.

In the linguistic games one plays definite roles. Not only the purely linguistic roles of

speech acts, but the real linguistic games in which others participate in the world of social action as the necessary feedback for the linguistic agent to go on. As a speech-agent, I am perfectly capable of giving orders. As a social agent, though, I cannot give an order to the President of the United States. As a poor man, I cannot play the game of buying a house.  As Sartre suggests,  I truly am –to myself and to others– the roles I play. Of course, the roles I play are roles I can play. For Sartre, however, I can change my roles at will. So there really are no roles I cannot play if I choose to. In other words, my identity depends on me. I am totally free to choose myself. In order to create for myself a new identity, all I need to do is to nihilate through reflection the roles I have played in the past and to project the roles I want to play in the future. Sartre doesn’t get the difference between projecting an dreaming. A project that cannot be realized is not a project but a dream. And since the roles I can play are roles that I play in games with others, if others do not play with me certain games I cannot play certain roles, however much I would want to.

I remember my little friend Joe’s answer to my childish question, “Why do I have always to choose the games we are going to play, and you never do?” “Because you are the master’s son.” As a young child, Joe had already interiorized ‘his station,’ that is, who he was, given the games and the roles in them he was socially predestined to play, as de Beauvoir says Western woman is predestined to play certain roles. This is what I meant by indexicality. At any given time, the “I” indicated by the field of action is the I who can play certain roles and who cannot play certain other roles. Theoretically, language as an instrument of communication is equally available to all those who know how to speak the language, that is, to the theoretical speech-acts agents. Practically, however, not everybody can play the same linguistic roles. And if I am even a minimally skilled linguistic agent as Joe was in his very young age, with Xavier I know the games I can play and the games I cannot play and the roles I can and I cannot play in the limited number of  games available to me. The games and roles available or possible to me constitute my repertoire as an agent. This repertoire of games and roles defines my identity, my persona, and is inscribed in my ‘expert’ body as agent-patient. I AM INDICATED –HENCE INDEXICALITY–by the successive situations in which I find myself as an agent. As my expert physical body tells me whether I can or cannot jump over a hurdle, so my expert linguistic body tells me whether I can or cannot address this person, whether I can or cannot give her an order.

Indexicality, therefore, is as personal as individuality. Each person has his or her own indexicality which is developed through interiorization of past experience as agent-patient. This indexicality is relative to the agent’s social and physical environment by which one is indicated to oneself. Thus, at the same time that it involves lived, bodily comprehension of onself as agent, it also involves a correlative bodily comprehension  of the agents –both persons and things– with which one interacts in the linguistic games, as well as of their specific coefficient of favorableness and adversity. And this interiorization is not only gained through first person’s experience as agent but also through observation of the roles palyed by other people in the linguistic games . Of course, this bodily comprehension or habitual disposition to respond to the world is fluid rather than static, for it is constantly changing and depends on a multitude of factors. Moreover, the agent himself or herself is not totally at the mercy of the cultural grammar as to the roles he or she can play, as the above may have suggested. But this is a theme for another day.  

Compare this notion of language to that of Susanne Langer. What Helen Keller discovered  the day the linguistic miracle happened was that he could play linguistic games because she had played the linguistic game of naming things by having her teacher name water for her. That this game involved conceptualization I don’t dispute. But what Helen really learned was not that a name connoted a concept and that denotation presupposed connotation, for of this she had no idea at the time, but that she could converse with others. And then, little by little she learned with which others she could and  could not converse, and about what, and in which circumstances. Conversation is a linguistic game, and one develops this skill through the practice of participating in conversations occupying different roles. Or compare this notion of language to that of speech-acts theory, which focuses on abstract speech acts, not on actual linguistic games and roles in concrete geographical and social environments.

How does all this relate to Kuhn’s view of science? He sees science as a particular cultural game into which the students of sciences are socialized. As they develop as scientists, they come to occupy different roles within the scientific community that plays the game. What he fails to see is that the game, which as all social, games is regulated by its members by the very fact of sticking to their respective roles and protecting their place in the pecking order against their inferiors while themselves aspiring to a higher place, is not –to speak with Maslow-- a merely expressive game of self-actualization but a purposive one. Its purpose is to gain control over our natural environment to tailor it to our needs. Kuhn fails to see this because he is still a prisoner of the representational view of knowledge; though he realizes that this view is wrong, he sees no viable alternative to it. The alternative is the practical ‘knowledge’ of agents who participate in socio-linguistic games.

What is more important, how does this relate to Christianity? Science is the game we have developed to multiply our chances of being in control of the natural environment. However, science is a reflective, second order game, based on abstract, interested observation of nature ex iis quae sunt propria rei . Religion, by contrast, is a first order game based on the experience of exposed, vulnerable agents concerned about their dependence on powers beyond their control, which they personalize, mythologize in sacred histories, and placate in ritual communicative actions. The young members of the religious culture are socialized into it as our children are socialized into believing in Santa. Religion ‘takes’ easily and is fairly universal because it satisfies our profound needs for security, for ultimate sense, for ultimate dignity as people who ‘count,’ and for ultimate happiness. In this sense, we tend to be the gods’s creators rather than the other way around. “Religion” is a human invention, not a divine initiative.

Although Christianity satisfies the needs that religions satisfy –for otherwise it would have no possible ‘take’ on us--, Christianity is not a religion. Indeed, it doesn’t personalize natural phenomena, its central stories are not mythical but historical, and it begins at God’s initiative. But precisely because Christianity satisfies the same ‘religious’ needs that religions satisfy, and that the ‘progress’ of modernity has  killed religion and unmasked the gods by showing that they are the creatures of our wishful thinking, for all its differences with religions, seen from the outside Christianity has to remain irremediably suspect.

Now, linguistic cultures characterized by their games structure reality for their participants, because the games are linguistic games. However, we usually don’t think of languages as true or false. And rightly so, for only a few linguistic games are susceptible of truth and falsity. Actually, among the games mentioned, only religion and science seem to be of this kind. How does this come about?

Aristotle among many others, in his Peri hermeneias , remarked that of all the speech-acts only statements which express judgments are susceptible of truth and falsity.
