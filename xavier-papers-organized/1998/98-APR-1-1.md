Statements in written language

In spoken language one can distinguish speech acts by the effects--perlocutionary effects, for there are no merely illocutionary ones--that the speaker achieves or at least intends, either intrinsically by virtue of the sheer abstract meaning, or most often circumstantially. In spoken language, however, there is no reason to privilege statements, for they too are characterized by their perlocutionary effects. By contrast, statements (or, rather, propositions) have been privileged in the philosophical and in certain theological or even religious circles (cf. Aristotle and St Thomas in Peri hermeneias ). Statements are privileged because, on the one hand, supposedly they are truth-claiming linguistic pieces, and the only truth-claiming ones, and because, on the other hand, orthopraxis is deemed to depend on (grounded) orthodoxia which is expressed in statements. Didn't Plato make philosophers into kings of the well-ordered city because of their intuitive contemplation of the eternal Forms, which guaranteed their orhopraxis?

Now the philosophical conception is possible and even persuasive once written language has been separated from spoken language, and once statements have been singled out because of their supposed truth-claiming characteristic, and once they have been reduced to propositions to be grounded. Before that occurs, however, that is, before the separation of written from spoken language and the consecration of the latter as the linguistic model, all this is inconceivable and totally unpersuasive, as we shall see now.

Searle: Written language as the linguistic model

We all are orally initiated into language, and this through practice rather than being formally taught, as is later the case with the initiation to reading. Indeed, reading comes later, when one is already a competent user of spoken language, and it is parasitic on the possession of oral language in that its acquisition presupposes thge possession of spoken language. This is an important point missed completely by the Greek seekers of wisdom.

Under the influence of the Greek view based on written, objectified language, we tend to think that the essential function of language is to communicate a meaning to be understood , and that being a competent user of language is being habitually capable of sending and receiving such meaningful, that is, intelligible messages. Certainly other effects often accompany the use of language--speech-acts theorists call them perlocutionary effects--but these effects are not essential, for they do not always occur or even are always intended, and they can be achieved through means other than language.

In his view of speech-acts, Searle devotes special attention

to statements. According to him, the statement "The hammer is on the table" being a linguistic act, "says," that is, expresses linguistically the speaker's conviction that the hammer is on the table, and is meant to produce in the hearer the understanding (Not necessarily the berlief) that the hammer is on the table. Understanding of what is said is the primary and essential effect of all speech-acts, statements included. What the words say is what the speech-act means to communicate , and this is what it actually communicates when what is said is understood. Searle calls this essential linguistic effect, which consists in understanding of what is said, the "illocutionary effect."

All speech-acts, then, by virtue of the meaning of the words said convey a message to be understood, and they succeed as speech-acts if that message is understood. In addition, however, other effects may be intended by the speaker and achieved through language. These are, therefore, genuinely linguistic effects, too, locutionary effects. Insofar, however, as these effects are not mere understanding but something other, they are not essential for the success of linguistic communication which is achieved when what is said is understood. To distinguish these additional effects from the essential, illocutionary effect, while marking at the same time their genuine locutionary or linguistic character, Searle calls them perlocutionary effects. Thus, for example, when I give an order to John, I intend for him to obey my order. However, my speech-act is successful as a speech-act if John understands the order I give him, even if he doesnt' obey it. In other words, his obeying it is not essential for the success of the speech-act as a speech-act. It is a perlocutionary effect, an effect in addition to the essential or illocutionary effect which in all cases consists in mere understanding.  

Now, the perlocutionary force of a locution is clear, or can at any rate be made clear, by the words themselves. For instance, if I say "I beg you," it is clear that I intend you to freely and without obligation give me what I beg for (where, needless to say, giving is a perlocutionary effect, for it is an action, not a mere understanding). Again, if I say "I command you," it is clear by virtue of the words said that I intend you to obey my order in recognition of the authority I have on you (where, again, your obeying is a perlocutionary effect for it is an action, not a mere understanding). In this connection, however, statements are exceptional in that they have no perlocutionary force and, therefore, neither produce  nor are meant to produce a perlocutionary effect. Hence for a statement to be fully successful ikt suffices that it be merely understood. Such, by contrast, is not the case with a command, which cannot be fully successful if it is merely understood but not obeyed,   

Searle considers the hypothesis that the distinctive perlocutionary specific effect of statements is to be believed by the addressee or addressees, for the intention of the speaker is that what is stated be accepted as true. In other words, in addition to their being understood, the typical and distinctive effect of statements, at least of categorical or unconditional statements, is their being believed by the addresse, for they involve the implicit claim that what is stated is conform to reality. Searle, however, denies this. For instance, if in a discussion I state to you who are an atheist "God exists," I necessarily have the intention, by virtue of the meaning of my words, that you understand what I said, namely, that God exists, but I may well have no intention, under the circumstances, of your believing what I state.

Let me summarize Searle's view on statements. A statement is a speech act that, by virtue of the meaning of the words by which it is composed, intends exclusively to produce understanding and nothing else. In short, unlike all or most other speech acts, statements have no "perlocutionary force" by virtue of their meaning. This implies that, according to Searle, statements, though susceptible of truth and falsity, are notby virtue of their meaning truth-claiming acts. This characteristic, however--I repeat--should not be construed as a denial, on Searle's part, of the fact that statements are susceptible of truth and falsity. Actually, statements are the only speech-acts that can be true or false, for they are the only linguistic forms whose relationship to reality is of the kind "mind-to-reality-fit" rather than "reality-to-mind-fit." Indeed, in the case of all other speech acts...

Such is Searle's view of language couched in terms of speech-acts. But is that view persuasive? Is it not based on the reduction of spoken to written language?

On the primacy of spoken language

Our introduction to language is not oral by accident. It is not simply that in all languages orality precedes written language (and in some cases the stage of written anguage is never reached), as if the reverse could have equally happened. The fact is that spoken and written language do not function in the same way, and that the way in which written language functions radically prevents it from being the version of language through which people can be initiated into language. The reason for this is that the understanding typically involved in reading is purely private to the reader, in that it is not connected with observable behavior related to the meaning linguistically comunicated. Thus the person to be initiated cannot relate anything to the written signs.

When one reads a text one is not, typically, in the presence of a speaker or speakers, one is not addressed individually, one is not expected to act-in-response when addressed, and one does not observe others who are addressed and respond both orally and behaviorally. In this case, the primary appropriate response to a written text is (as Searle wrongly claims for all language, spoken included) mere understanding of the meaning expressed through the wrods. Furthermore, in reading, if any other responses or effects are connected with the meaning expressed--such as agreeing or disagreeing, liking or disliking, being moved or not, etc.--these have no typical behavioral manifestations. Consequently, these effects or responses are unobservable, so that by looking at the reader I couldn't possible know from his or her reactions whether or not he or she understood the text, and whether my understanding of the text is the same as his or hers and actually is the one intended by the writer. In addition, in the case of written language the effects which are not mere understanding are accidental, in that they may or may not be intended by the speaker, are not constitutive of the meaning of itself of the text, and may therefore not occur without the success of the communication expressed in the words ("Whatever can be meant can be said") being impaired.

Such being the case, one cannot learn how to read, as one learns oral language, by mere practice (which involves personal exposure to speakers and their reactions as well as observation of other hearers). The casde of learning how to read is very different. One learns how to read by being formally taught, at least by being taught the correspondence between letters and sounds or between images and sounds, so that one grasps connotation and denotation (meaning and reference) through actual experience of particular denotations and conotations.

But why, precisely, cannot one learn how to read without some formal teaching? Because in the case of understanding-meaning involved in reading there is nothing one can observe in the readers that relates to the meaning of what they read. Moreover,  one cannot be directed or expected by the readers to do anything in relation to that meaning, since there is no particular observable conduct to which that meaning is related. The case of spoken language, however, is significantly different, in that meaning, at least on the most fundamental linguistic level, which is the level of practical, survival-related interaction with the surrounding world rather than of mere conversation, is essentially connected with conduct on the addressee's part--behavioral, linguistic, or both.

The first communications with the child are practical. "Come here," addressed to the child, is expected to be met with a certain behavioral response on the child's part. "Come here," addressed to somebody else in the child's presence, is met by a a certain behavioral response on the addressee's part. If no behavior were meant to happen in response to those words (a behavior to whose presence or absence rewards and punishments are atached), or if such behavior never happened in response, the expression "Come here" would remain forever strictly meaningless for the child, and for the speaker too, who would therefore never give such an order.

This example shows an important thing, namely, that the meaning of a command is not something to be understood, in the sense given to this term by Searle, which has a purely theoretical conotation, but a certain performance on the addressee's part, a certain behavioral, observable conduct in response, which is in response because it is informed by the intention to respond. This performance-in-response-to-the-particular-command is what the "Come here" addressed to the child means, for this is what the speaker intends to obtain through the communication, this is what the child understands practically by obeying, and this is what satisfies the speaker who gives the command (without whose satisfaction certain other unsavory behavioral responses on his or her part would follow).

At other times the conduct required of the addressee is not directly related to the desirous or fearful speaker but refers to objects in the surrounding world of the exposed, vulnerable addressee. "Drink your milk." This presupposes, in part, and in part enlarges, the scope of denotation or reference to the object milk and of its connotation or concept. Progressively the child grasps practically that the objects denoted are not only denoted in reference to the addresser or the addressee but also in reference to others, and not only denoted in terms of the desires and fears of the addresser or the addressee and even of others but also objectively, that is, independently of their capacity to satisfy or dissatisfy the addressee's or anybody's desires. In this way the child begins to learn the surrounding world in relation to him or her and to others, and the ways in which he or she is expected to behave and the roles he or she is expected to play.

  Gradually the child learns that the world is no only for him or her but also for others, and that the world is not only to be measured in terms of his or her desires or fears but also in terms of those of others, and that he is part and parcel of the world for others and exposed to their linguistic and other action, as others are part and pracel of the world for him and exposed to his linguistic and other action, and that the world can be manipulated to an extent to fit his or her desires or those of others, and that this manipulation has limits imposed by the 'nature' of that which is to be manipulated. In this way the 'objective' or not directly related to desire and fear gradually emerges.

This connects with the Serlean notion of statements, which are supposed to be factual, informational, without reference to exposed subjects, and even without perspectival ties to spatially and temporally situated subjects. Such seems to be the character attributed within the Greek tradition, of which Searle's position is a variety, to the truth-claiming pieces of language. That tradition, however, arises not from the agent's experience of being addressed and addressing in the practical context, which is the originary linguistic contextual practice on which all other uses of language are parasitic, but is based on an explicit theory of knowledge built to explain truth and falsity (of judgments and statements) and, as much as possible, to guarantee on that basis the attainment of truth.

According to that theory, originally developped in Greece by the so-called lovers of wisdom or knowledge, knowledge consists in the reflectively known, guaranteed, intentional or cognitive conformity of the knower (through knowing acts) to the real. Knowing acts are those acts that are capable of intentional conformity to the real and which, through their actual conformity, produce actual knowledge--information--in the subject.

The unsolvable problem of truth-conformity

What characterizes all those "epistemic theories" whose origins are to be traced to Greece is that, in all of them, knowledge consists in some kind of actual conformity between the knower and the real, which thereby becomes actually known, and that this conformity is attained through the cognitive or intentional contact between the knower and the real. The unsolvable problem that view meets is the impossibility of guaranteeing the difference between opinion and knowledge, between believing that one knows and actually knowing, between the subjective feeling of certainty and objective or fully justified certainty of con formity to reality. The Aristotelians took for a solution the immediately self-justifying character of the "principles" [(contradiction, sufficient reason, partes et totum... (?)] by virtue of their intuitive absolute indubitability, and the subordinate indubitatibilty of the intellectual knowledge of the natures of actually existing realities either themselves directly accessible to perception or indirectly through their effects. Needless to say, this solution left open the thorny question of the knowledge of the purely factual by contrast to the necessary, which of course involves the question of the knowledge of existence, as well as the question of the unavoidable dependence of the knowledge of the intelligible and necessary on the knowledge of the sensible and contingent (Nihil est in intellectu quod prius non fuerit in sensu).

The problem of objective certainty became even more acute with the Cartesian separation between the subject and the object. How, indeed, can this subject separated from the object ever come to know for sure that the knowleldge in the mind actually conforms to the reality supposedly known ? Descartes' attempt to solve the problem by the indubitability of the clear and distinct idea obviously failed. That led Kant, in reaction to Hume's well-argued skkepticism, to his so insatinsfactory  solution. Through his "Copernican revolution" which suppressed radically the anciennt issue of objective certainty, transformed the notion of truth from conformity to actual (intentional?) identity, and reduced the field of the knowable, leaving to faith the grasp of God, of immortality, of freedom (and of other subjects), and to a blind imperative the whole field of the practical and moral.

It is within this context that the idea of statements as the only truth-claiming speech acts finds its place. Indeed, statements supposedly are the only speech acts susceptible of truth, that is, of conformity to the real, for they are the only acts that involve predication--firm, unconditional predication in the case of categorical statements. Needless to say, this again involves the old problem of truth, which Searle attempts to solve rather insufficiently in his articles on truth. *

No wonder, given the long history of failure of this view of knowledge and of truth, that the deconstructionists finally declared defeat. Texts, particularly statements, refer. But they refer with "différance," never delivering in actual presence that to which they refer. Hence all texts are interpretations, which can be substitued by other interpretations in endless succession, wihout escape from the hermeneutic circle, the circle of textaality. In short, the deconstructionists grant that statements and texts composed of both statements and grounding statements that supply evidence for the stated are the truth-claiming texts, but they deny that the truth they claim is attainable.

This long disquisition was necessary to render intelligible   my position.

Statements are not truth-claiming speech acts

Having explained in some detail Searle's views on speech-acts in general and on statements by contrast to other speech-acts in particular, it is time I introduce my own views by contrast to his. Concerning, in the first place, statements, I agree with Searle that statements are not truth-claiming speech-acts. But not for the same reason. Indeed, he thinks that statements are susceptible of truth and falsity, but he doesn't think that, as speech acts, they have the prelocutionary force of claiming truth and, therefore, of intending to effect belief. For my part, I think, on the one hand, that statements are not truth-claiming speech acts, because they are not susceptible of truth and falsity, of being or not being conform to reality. In other words, unlike Searle, I don't think that statements are unique among speech acts in that they can be conform to reality, for the very notion of intentional or epistemic conformity to reality is, in my opinion, monsensical. It arises out of taking written language as the model, and therefore of understanding inventing the notion of knowledge as contmeplation,  as theory. On the other hand, I don't think, as Searle does, that statements are unique among speech-acts in that they can succeed by being merely understood, since supposedly they convey or mean only something to be understood. On the contrary, like all other speech acts, statements aim at eliciting a behavior from the addresee or addressees. The differences, however, between Searle and myself demand detailed unpacking, for our positions are radically diferent. Indeed, to put my position in a nutshell, I think that the distinction between illocutionary and perlocutionary effects is illegitimate.

This Searlean distinction rests on the assumption that language is something primarily to be understood, and that what in linguistic communication exceeds understanding is based on understanding--a purely mental entity independent of conduct, as conduct is a purely bodily entity that sometimes accompanies linguistic understanding. Thus, one can understand a command and fail to obey it, but one cannot obey a command without first understanding it. This supposedly shows that the essential effect of all linguistic action is understanding, for it is to be found in all cases of successful linguistic communication; that this effect is distinct and separable from the perlocutionary effects; that the perlocutionary effects (though genuinely linguistic and intended or meant) are  not essential to successful linguistic communication, for not all speech-acts have perlocutionary force; and that the perlocutionary effects are parasitic on the illocutionary effect.

For my part, I think otherwise . Precisely because linguistic communication is a form of agency--of interaction, more precisely--not a mere exchange of speculative meanings, the achievement of the effect intended--Searle's perlocutionary effect, though this name implies that there are is such a thing as the illocutionary effect that differs from the perlocutionary ones--is essential for successful communication. Concretely, if I command you to pass me that book, the linguistic action succeds only if you pass me that book in obedience to my command, for that is what I intend or mean, not the mere understanding typical of written expository predicative texts . The action of passing the book to me is linguistic in that it is meant by the speaker to be performed in response to the linguistic command and is actually performed by the addresse in response to the linguistic command. (In Searle's view, there is no difference between the understanding of someone who reads or overhears an order, and the understanding of the oral addressee). If, to continue with my example, you happened to pass me the book by chance, without hearing or understanding my command and therefore not in obedience to my command, the action would not be the effect of a speech act and consequently would not constitute a linguistic action. In other words, in that case, though the action of passing the book obtains, the speech act failed. And--in my view, by contrast to Searle's--the reason is not that the adresse didn't understand, but that he did not perform the action meant to be performed by him in response. [In Searle's view, whether or not the action follows the understanding is linguistically irrelevant or accidental, as long as the understadning that an order was given obtains. But this indicates, precisely, that the action is not informed by the practical understanding of the order, and thus that the action is not really an act of obedience, anymore than the absence of the actionm is an act of disobedience, a refusal of obedience, a linguistic response to me as the speaker of the spoken, a practical denial of my authority.

Let me elaborate the point. The understanding of which Searle speaks is purely speculative. One understands a statement or a command exactly as one understands the impersonal [speakerless, addresseeless) proposition expressing a geometric theorem. That the understanding of which Searle speaks is purely speculative shows in the fact that, according to him, in the case of statements there is no perlocutionary effect meant to obtain, and Searle doesn't see any diference between the (purely speculative) understanding required for the success of the statement as a speech-act and the understanding of the command . Actually, he reduces the understanding of the command to the purely theoretical understkanding of the statement . Well, my position is exactly the opposite: I reduce the understanding of the statement to the practical understanding of the command. Such understanding ispractical in that it terminates in a conduct-in-response, that is, in a conduct informed by the practical understanding suscitated by the meaning of the speech act, which aims at such conduct.

That the understanding of which Searle speaks is purely speculative shows in the case of other speekch-acts than statements. For instance, in the case of a command, by its very perlocutionary force, an action is involved. However, according to Searle, the action is not required for the success of the speech act. The reason for this can only be that the perlocutionary effect, the action, is meant to be understood contemplatively , but is not meant to be performed .

But isn't Searle's view queer? To speak of an avowedly different order than the linguistic, Searle's position amounts to saying that when I give a command (and a command is always definite) to my computer what I intend is that the command be understood, not that it be carried out! (Of course, I don't say to my computer "I command you to... Thus,  granted, it is not a matter of linguistic communication but rather of cause effect. But what is important in tghe example is the commander's intention, whether the commqander intends to be obeyed or simply understood). Searle sees the understanding as different and independent from the action of passing me the book, when I command that, for he sees the linguistic understanding itself as purely speculative. By contrast to Searle, when I give the order I mean the commanded action to be performed, not to be theoretically understood. For me the understanding involved is practical, for I see in the action in obedience to my command the genuine andgenuinely linguistic understanding of the command. I call this understanding practical because it informs the action itself, so that the action is not a mere behavior but an act of obedience, a genuinely linguistic action.

Certainly, you, the addressee, may disobey my command, and you can disobey it only if you understand my command. But this is a parasitic case, not the prototypical lingistic case . This is, for Searle (together with the case of statements, which supposedly have no perlocutionary force, intend no perlocutionary effect), the model case, that is, the case on which he models his distinction between illocutionary and perlocutionary effects and determines the comparative essentialness of the illocutionary effect for linguistic communication. In other words, he reduces statements to communication of knowledge or  information about something, to be gained through understanding rather than through personal inquiry. In a parallel way, he reduces other speech-acts to communication of knowledge of, or inform ation about, intentions , again to be gained through understanding of the intentions rather than through execution of the intentions.

Underlying this view is the Scholastic-Aristotelian idea of (speculative) knowledge as silent language--the language of predication, of the contemplator of the world and even of his own acts --where knowledge doesn't reside in perception or in intellection but in judgment to be communicatekd to others through statements. [THIS CONFLATION OF THE COGNITIVE ACTS (PERCEPTION, JUDGMENT) DETECTED BY AN OBJECTIFYING THEORY OF KNOWLEDGE OR EPISTEMOLOGY, WITH LANGUAGE (EXPRESSED JUDGMENTKS, THAT IS, STATEMENTS), TYPICAL OF THE GREEKS, IS AT THE ROOTS OF THE WHOLE ISSUE.] What is communicated, according to Searle, is the object known through silent language by the speaker. And this even in the case of the speaker's intentions, which supposedly are themselves known and stated rather than actually intended.

On the other hand, to return to my example, if you didn't pass me the book because you didn't hear me, you wouldn't have disobeyed me. Now, how does this case compare to the one in which you hear and understand but refuse to obey me? Only the latter qualifies as disobedience. However, the action is identical in both cases. How would Searle distinguish between the two? He would say that disobedience presupposes understanding. But how would Searle distinguish mere understanding from the action of non-obedience?

---------------

There is no speech-act in the practical, interactive domain that intends to be merely understood, or can be successful as a speech act by being merely understood. In other words, all speech acts, statements included, intend something other than being understood and, therefore, cannot be succesful as speech acts by merely being understood.

[Searle has this wrongheaded view of language because he thinks that words have an already conventional meaning--this is what he argues against Grice--and that the speaker selects them because of their already fixed meaning to convey what he wants to convey to the hearer or addresse].

--------------------

The view of statements as the truth-claiming speech-acts unavoidably arises as soon as written language is taken as the model and  therefore spoken language is assumed to follow that model. Actually, however, spoken statements do not function, nor indeed are they meant by the speaker to function, as truth-claiming pieces in the sense of conveying a picture (which may or may not be acurate) of realty, of the surrounding world, rather than of the intentions of the speaker.

"YOUR MOTHER IS ON THE PHONE"-"FIRE!"-"THE HAMMER IS ON THE TABLE"

The speaker always expects, and often intends, a conduct-in-response to his or her statements (for the interactive speaker communicates to the interactive addresse an object for response to be transcended practically in a response), though the conduct (allghough forseen and perhaps intended by the speaker) may sometimes be entirely left to the determination of the addressee  (depending on circumstances). At any rate, mere understanding is never the successful (intended or even expected) achievement of statements as linguistic or locutionary acts.

Assuming this, what does follow? That language is, primarily, the language of exposure, of desirous-fearful-agents-patients involved in interaction and who have sometimes or often to decide how to act. If so, they don't speak the language of neutral description and their statements cannot possess the truth- correspondence conceivable in the case of objective statements on the basis of which the action would follow mechanically--or, rather, no action would follow, for a non-exposed agent has no motivation to act. They speak the language of already culturally-personally committed agents, but whose commitments to goals are to an extent free but not arbitrary, and which can change in time.  

Searle mixes two views of language: language as action, to which the idea of perlocutionary effects is related, and language as knowledge, to which the idea of understanding as the illocutionary effect is related. The former is connected with the Greek notion of practical knowledge, of knowledge which consists o (appropriate) action, the latter with the Greek notion of speculative knowledge which consists in a mental or intentional entity assumed to be conform to reality. The former arises from the experience of speaking and being spoken to, that is, from oral or spoken language, the latter from reflection on written, objectified language.

On the one hand, his theory of speech- acts , as indicated by its very name, corresponds to the notion of communication among linguistic agents who speak and hear as vulnerable agents involved in interaction, who intend to modify the situation through linguistic action.

On the other hand, his view of speech-acts defined by their capacity to produce the illocutionary effect of understanding, of statements as meant to produce only understanding and therefore of being successful as speech- acts if understanding is achieved, and of statements being susceptible of truth corresponds to a notion of communication among invulnerable, undesirous, objective contemplators-knowers, and of reality as something static to be contemplated, not something desirable or undesirable to be transformed through action.

Spoken language establishes the relationship between the speaker and the addressee as agents-patients in a shared world, a world shared also with others, which is prototypical of language and which we call communication .
