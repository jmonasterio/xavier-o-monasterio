            Useful points for Summer Grants in CIT

      "If we are ever  to  reestablish  communication  among  the  scattered
      realms of scholarship, ever to find common  ground  of  discourse  and
      build anew the lost  unity  of  knowledge,  we  will  do  so  only  by
      constructing a new intellectual  tradition.  Right  now  the  idea  is
      utopian, but the Catholic university  is  the  natural  home  for  the
      utopian quest. Turner finds it ironic the embrace of secular knowledge
      by Catholics at the very moment when 'secular knowledge has  collapsed
      in an undignified heap of squirming confusion.'" (Last section  of  my
      notes \wp\monas\O'Brien).

- P. 4: Philosophy or Theology as the core in the curriculum.

- pp. 4-: No synthesis of professionalism  with  concern  for  questions  of
ultimate moral or social significance.

- p. 5: Protestant schools: from  denominational  to  generic  xtianity,  to
vaguely defined religious value, to total secularization.

- p. 6: Vatican II drprived Catholics of the language with  which  they  had
habitually ordered their religious  universe  and  through  which  they  had
articulated their own self understanding.
      Shea maintains that  this  is  good:  revise  the  conviction  of  the
superiority of their own religious belief (because true?) and  come  to  see
it for what it is: belief, not knowledge.

- p. 7: Absence of a strong commitment to Catholic identity.

- p. 7: Dupré: not only doctrinal tradition , but  extending  the  religious
attitude into all areas of  existence.  This  demands  not  only  study  but
contemplation.

- p. 8: Despite its sacramental heritage, Catholicism in  practice  assigned
religious value only to  specifically  religious  activity,  which  embraces
family values and  intimate  relationships,  bot  not  to  social  'secular'
activities like politics and work.

- p. 8: Ninetheenth century:  A-historical,  anti-modernist  ideology,  with
super-institutionalism and division of work between  authority  and  fideles
and legacy of fear and emphasis on sin prevailed  from  IXXth  till  Vatican
II.

- p. 8: Post-Vatican: implications of moving from nohistorical  orhodoxy  to
historical, adaptive understanding  of  faith  and  life.  Certain  Catholic
intellectuals (Sheehan) had undermined the
foundational character of the  Resurrection.  Die-hard  Thomists  protested.
The gap between theology after the "critical turn"  and  popular  piety  had
become enormous. And campus ministry gets low marks for  communicating  that
idea that Catholicisms makes intellectual claims.

- p. 9: Secularization of scholarship. Sociology. Even academic  theologians
felt that they should have no responsibility for the Ckatholic character  of
the institution.  Theology  and  religious  studies.  Theology  in  research
universities despised as  confessional;  even  in  other  universities  many
favor an approach to religious studies modeled on the  sciences--descriptive
and
explanatory except in ethics, where moral advocacy is encouraged.

- p. 10: In matters of justice and peace, questions are not posed  in  terms
of natural law or papal teachings but on  what  Jesus  would  do.  If  these
extremists are isolated this is not their fault.  The  college  has  favored
the segregation of academic from religious matters in a way which  goes  far
beyond the authentic respect for the autonomy  of  the  secular  favored  by
Vatican II. [The 'scientific' attitude  of  colleges  marginalizes  religion
and  institutionalizes  political  and  civic   irresponsibility.   Duyality
between discipleship and citizenship. Schizophrenic existence.

- p. 11: Hard for xtians to see their public role as disciples in a  society
marked by pluralism and expressive individualism.

- p. 11: Originally the American university had three  conflictive  visions:
Research for its own sake (search for truth),  preservation  of  traditional
culture, and public service.

- p.  11:  Problems  with  courses  in  ethics.  It  is  philosophical,  not
theological  (separates  value  questions  from  questions  of  meaning  and
faith),  and  isolates  the  individual  from  institutional  settings:  the
decider is not Catholic, Jew, Protestant, not republican  or  democrat,  not
liberal or fundamentalist.

-- p. 12 on 191: Suppression of questions of value and meaning  in  graduate
schools.

- p. 12 on  182:  The  problem  of  pluralism:  to  have  people  of  widely
diverging conceptions  of  the  good  life  live  peacefully.  The  formerly
accepted  ideologies--social  contract,  inalienable  rights  of  autonomous
inidivuals, natural law dictated by  reason--not  openly  rejected  but  not
embraced pasionately anymore.

- p. 12 on 183: The ideology of modern  liberalism  relegates  religion  and
particular conceptions of the good life to  the  private  domain.  Sectarian
beliefs are tolerated as long as  they  are  considered  equally  valid  and
equally irrelevant to matters of public concern. The common life  is  to  be
conducted on essentially secular principles.

- p. 13 on 183: The American Catholic option of Murray: to  reconstruct  the
foundations of public discourse and thus of the common  life.  Continued  by
Gaudium et Spes and the pastoral letters  on  war  and  economics.  But  its
advocates are fekkwer and usure of themselves.

- p. 13 on 183-184: By excluding religious views and thus  particular  views
of the common good  liberalism  drains  the  public  realm  of  meaning  and
undermines the need for civic virtue. This liberal  ideology  worked  to  an
extent when the Englightenment's faith in reason was alive.

- p. 13 on 184: The secular neutral resolution of the problem  of  pluralism
seems increasingly inadequate. Schwehn argues that  the  virtues  of  public
life are nurtured best in communities sustained by a common narrative  about
life and history, and that such communities shouldn't be  neutralized.  Mnay
of the foundations of the scientific domination of  the  academy  come  into
question.

- p. 14 on 104-11:  AN  EPISTEMOLOGICAL  REVOLUTION:  A  new  faith  in  the
scientific method as  the  only  path  to  truth,  rendering  denominational
colleges  anachronistic,  sending   theology   to   seminaries,   separating
intellect from religious commitment, and making the latter not a  matter  of
theological coherence but of idiosyncratic psychology.
