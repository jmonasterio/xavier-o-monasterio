Aquinas on "Scitum" and "Creditum"

N.B. I take my basis on A. R. Motte, O. P., "Theodicee et theologie," Revue des Sciences Philosophiques et Theologiques , vingt-sixieme annee, 1937, pp. 5-26.

"One must... make certain of the identity of the being with whom one has the honor to discourse... Does he in fact exist? And if he does, is he then not in the process of becoming... does he ever face the future by way of action? And if he does not, will he not forgive an ethical individuality for saying that he is an ass?" (Kierkegaard, CUPostscript ).

"Fundamentally I had understood the old gentleman at once, for my studies had in many ways led me to take note of a dubious relationhip between a modern Christian speculation and Christianity, but the matter had not in any decisive manner enlisted my interest. Now it was invested with its own proper significance. The venerable old man with his faith seemed to be an individual with an absolutely justified grievance , a man whom existence had mistreated, because modern speculation, like a change in currency, had made property values in the realm of faith insecure . His sorrow over the loss of his son, not only by death, but as he understood it, still more terribly through speculative philosophy, moved me profoundly; while the contradiction in his position, that he could not even explain how the enemy had conducted the campaign, became for me a decisive challenge to trace out a definite clue . The entire matter appealed to me as a very complicated criminal case, where the crisscrossing of many trails made it difficult to find the truth. This was something for me. And I thought to myself: 'You are now tired of life's diversions, you are tired of the maindens, whom you love only in passing; you must have something fully to occupy your time. Here it is: to discover where the misunderstanding lies between speculative philosophy and Christianity.' This therefore became my resolve...

"My studies, which had already in a manner brought me to my goal, were now systematically organized; but whenever I was tempted to transform my investigations into an erudite learning, there always came before my mind the venerable figure of the old man. But chiefly I sought by reflection to trace the misunderstanding to its roots . My many failures I need not here recite, but finally it became clear to me that the misdirection of speculative philosophy, and its consequent assumed justification for reducing faith to the status of a relative moment, could not be anything accidental, but must be rooted deeply in the entire tendency of the age. It must, in short, doubtless be rooted in the fact that on account of our vastly increased knowledge, men had forgotten what it means to exist, and what INWARDNESS signifies.

"When I understood this, it also became clear to me that if I desired to communicate anything on this point, it would first of all be necessary to give my exposition and indirect form. For if inwardness is the truth, results are only rubbish whith which we should not trouble each other" (Kierkegaard, Concluding Unscientific Postscript , Princeton U. Press, 1941, pp. 215-216).

On one point Aquinas never veered: " creditum non potest esse scitum nec visum ": that which is believed through faith cannot be known deductively or intuited as evident ( Motte, p. 17). In other words, knowledge and religious belief are mutually exclusive. If one knows something one does not--and cannot possibly--believe it * , and if one believes something one does not--and cannot possibly--know it. That Aquinas never veered on this point is clear from the fact that from his first work, the Commentarium in Sententias , through his last, the Summa Theologica , he persisted in this view in spite of the constant difficulties it forced him to confront. The difficulties arose from the fact that Aquinas also maintained, and no less firmly, that human reason left to its own devices and without the help of any divine revelation could come to know demonstratively certain things, such as God's existence, which seem to be credita on the basis of divine revelation, that is, which are object of religious assent.

[* Notice that "believe," here, means to assent on the basis of authority as opposed to evidence. Obviously, in this sense, to believe and to know are mutually exclusive. And if faith is belief, then faith and knowledge are mutually exclusive. ONLY IF.]

  Let us provisorily assume that Aquinas was right as a Christian thinker , that is, as a thinker who wanted to be consistent both with the nature of his Christian faith and with the abilities of his reason, in maintaining simultaneously the two mentioned positions. If so, all those of us who are Christian thinkers have to agree with Aquinas and adhere to the two positions. However, since we are thinkers and not just Christian believers,  it is clear that we can adhere to the two positions only if they are consistent with each other, if they are not mutually exclusive. This, in its turn, depends on whether the solutions offered by Aquinas to the difficulties he encountered in maintaing simultaneously the two positions are valid and persuasive. For if they are not, we shall be logically constrained either to maintain that something accepted through faith ( creditum ) can be known ( scitum ), or that what we come to know by purely rational means concerning God has nothing to do with the God of Christian revelation ahdered to in faith. Needless to say, the latter point has to do with the validity of what in the past was known as "natural theology" and today is often called "philosophy of religion." Can Christian thinkers accept as valid any knowledge concerning God reached by purely rational means?

Why what is creditum cannot be scitum? Gilson once put it bluntly: because it is impossible to believe something and to know it at the same time. Indeed, knowledge rests on evidence, and belief on God's authority. One can only believe because, and as long as, one does not know. One cannot continue to believe when one knows, because this would amount to being prompted to assent to the same for two mutually exclusive reasons, namely, evidence and authority. In saying this, Gilson is undoubtedly, and very c onsciously, following Aquinas. Indeed, that is at least one of the reasons that led Aquinas to maintain so firmly and constantly that "creditum nont potest esse scitum nec visum." And such persuasion is inescapable if, as Aquinas did, one conceives of faith as intellectual assent and of the object of faith as object assented to on the basis of divine authority, that is, as creditum.

In certain cases, indeed, Aquinas seems to move on this basis. For instance, when he discusses whether someone who knows God's existence through one of the proofs doesn’t  thereby reduce the number of the truths he or she assents to because of God’s authority, and whether consequently his or her faith is less meritorious than the faith of those who accept God’s existence only on the basis of divine revelation. At other times, however, the reason that prevents Aquinas from accepting that something believed can be known has nothing to do with the nature of knowing and believing, but with the fact that believing is not a matter of intelligence but of grace.

[ In my view, Aquinas is inconsistent, and inconsistent in such a way that there is no way of rendering him consistent . He himself tried again and again in his effort to deal with the problem of the creditum and scitum, proposing different, incompatible--and unsuccessful--solutions to it. The reason for this hopless inconsistency is that while being totally unaware of it, Aquinas actually works with two radically different notions of faith . One of them, explicitly stated, is intellectual assent to what is revealed, on the basis of God's authority. The other, of course, is unstated, and part of my task will be to detect it. One of them, the stated one, results from Aquinas' conception of faith by analogy to (speculative) knowledge, and his consequent attempt to conform faith and revelation to the requirements of this Aristotelian epistemological framework  includes revelation as complementary to reason. The other, the latent one, arises from his experience of living as one of the faithful. However, since Aquinas was convinced that his stated conception of faith captured the essence of his experience of living as a believer, he could not possibly realize his inconsistency.

Aquinas' solutions to scitum non postest esse creditum

In a number of cases (pp. 1 and 2 of my notes on Motte), concerning God's existence as known ( scitum ) and as believed through faith ( creditum ), the solution given is that, depending on whether it originates from revelation or from reason, the statement of God's existence means either something partly different, or something totally different, or means differently what is meant. For instance, "Deum esse non est articulus fidei, sed praecedens articulum, nisi cum hoc quod est Deum esse aliquid aliud cointelligatur... utpote quod habet unitatem essentialem cum Trinitate personarum, et alia hujusmodi" (De Ver; p. 1 of my notes). Alia hujusmodi, like God's Providence, God's retributive quality, God's omnipotence, God as object of adoration and cult (p. 2 of my notes).

First possible interpretation : God's existence as philosophically known, as scitum, differs totally from God's existence as object of faith, as creditum, because the latter involves "aliquid aliud." In other words, what the philosopher assents to on the basis of evidence and what the faithful assent to on the basis of God's authority are totally different bjects. Reason: although they use the same words, philosophers and faithful mean something totally different because what they understand by the words–that is, what they are referred to and the way they are referred–are totally different. This is the tack taken by Motte. However, this interpretation conflicts with other of Aquinas's views , namely, the ability of reason to know certain truths about God; the fact that God revealed certain truths accessible to reason because they are not equally accessible to the more and to the less intelligent, and because they are not easily accessible even to  intellectuals, which implies that what intellectuals know is not totally different from what Christians believe. All this implies that Aquinas does not think that what the faithful assent to by faith and what philosophers affirm on the basis of evidence is totally different.

Second possible interpretation : what the faithful believe and philosophers know is partly the same ; the faithful assent to that and something more, because they understand the same and more than philosophers do. This interpretation is free of all the problems of the preceding one; this is to say that it coheres with the positions taken by Aquinas concerning the matters mentioned above. However , in this case, the scitum and the creditum are the same, at least in part, and this contradicts Aquinas' position that nothing can be scitum and creditum. For instance, when philosophers know the existence of God as a result of a proof they know the same God in whom the faithful believe, although the faithful know, in addition, something more, such as that God is creator. But, in that case, if knowledge excludes belief, how can a believer who, like Aquinas himself, comes to know God through one of the five proofs keep his belief in God as belief, given that he or she knows that God exists? This, precisely, is the difficulty which Aquinas has to confront again and again. And, if this second interpretation of Aquinas' solution is correct, then his solution is invalid. A concrete example: Assume that I and John know on the basis of historical proofs that Napoleon existed. In addition, John, on the basis of his father's authority, believes that Napoleon had a fobia for mice, something which I neither know nor believe. Clearly, John and I know the same concerning Napoleon's existence and assent to the same, though John has and additional creditum I do not have. Thus, the fact that a Christian has additional credita concerning God does not exclude the fact that both a philosopher and a Christian who know God's existence through demonstration know the same. But then, how can a Christian at the same time know and accept by faith that God exists, if indeed knowledge and faith are mutually exclusive?

[In fact, when Aquinas deals with this difficulty he implicitly comes to admit not that faith and knowledge about the same are not mutually exclusive since the intellectual believer believes fewer truths, but that the intellectual’s merit is not less than that of the simple believer, on  condition that the intellectual's assent be primarily prompted by God's authority, so that the believer would continue to believe even though the proofs for God's existence happen to be invalid).

Third possible interpretation : the philosopher and the believer assent in a totally different way . Indeed, this is clearly the case when that to which the philosopher assents and to which the believer assents is the same, for one assents on the basis of evidence and the other on the basis of God's authority. But the problem arises precisely in the case of the Christian who assents to something which is part of his faith on the basis of evidence. How can this Christian, at the same time, assent to the same on the basis of evidence and of God's authority? Either he knows that God exists (scitum), or he believes on God's authority that God exists (creditum), but he cannot do both at the same time. Thus, that the philosopher and the believer assent in a totally different way cannot work as a principle of solution when the Christian and the philosopher are the same person.

Let us summarize. In every case in which Aquinas confronts the difficulty concerning the impossibility of knowing and believing something at the same time, his solution is that there is "aliquid aliud" involved in knowing and in believing by faith. If we interpret "aliquid aliud" as that which is known through reason and that which is believed through faith are two different things , we put Aquinas in contradiction with a number of his most firmly held positions. This interpretation is, therefore, disqualified. Secondly, iIf we interpret "aliquid aliud" as that which is known and believed is the same in part, but the believer in addition believes something more about the same object , then this interpretation coheres with Aquinas' explicit and firmly held positions, but by the same token his principle that scitum and creditum are mutually exclusive fails. In other words, this solution is invalid, for the scitum and the creditum are the same: the God whose existence is known by the philosopher and believed by the Christian is the same, though the Christian, in addition, believes something that the philosopher does not. Finally, if we interpret "aliquid aliud" as the way in which something scitum and something creditum are accepted is totally different , then we have an interpretation that makes little sense and that anyway does not solve the principle that scitum and creditum are mutually exclusive.

To conclude: in any conceivable hypothesis Aquinas' position is inconsistent, for he maintains both that to know and to believe something is impossible, and that there is a certain ground common to faith and to reason in which what is creditum can become scitum and what is scitum can be creditum.

The latent notion of faith

At this point we may ask: What is all this fuss about? Why does not Aquinas simply grant that some truths can be scita and credita at the same time? The obvious answer is that given by Gilson and by Aquinas himself ( where? ). What distinguishes the assent from faith from the assent of reason is the motive by which they are respectively prompted, namely, evidence and divine authority, and these motives are mutually exclusive. And, indeed, if both faith and knowledge are judgments, as Aquinas deems them to be, then the difference between them can only be the motive by which the judgements are prompted.

[BUT WHAT IF THE ACT OF FAITH IS NOT AN ACT OF JUDGING? THIS IS SOMETHING WHICH IS INCONCEIVABLE FOR AQUINAS, FOR IT WOULD AMOUNT TO A DISAVOWAL OF HIS ARISTOTELIANISM. And yet...]

And yet--and this brings us to the real answer, as opposed to the obvious one given by Aquinas-Gilson ,  Aquinas maintains that the scitum and the creditum are not--and cannot--be the same, because his Christian instinct, based on his experience of the life of faith, tells him, as it told so many others, that believing in God is totally different than what he (following Aris) understands by knowing and knowing  God.

The case of the vetula

To repeat, I maintain that the deep, unstated reason for Aquinas' conviction that nothing that is creditum can be scitum and nothing that is scitum can be creditum arises from his Christian 'instinct,' that is, from his experience as a practicing Christian. I further suspect that the same instinct was at work in all those who manifested a strong reluctance to the marriage of their faith with 'Aristotelianism,' whether they ended up joining the Aristotelian wagon, as most of them did, or whether, like Bonaventure or Roger Bacon, they refused to do that at the price of being labelled--and perhaps of seeing themselves--as obscurantists and irrationalists and ‘mystics.’. What sustained them was a deep  a deep fidelity to their faith as they experienced it. Could not the same conviction that the creditum cannot be scitum be obscurely at work in Aquinas? I think that the case of the vetula shows that such indeed was the case.

" Nullus philosophorum ante adventum Christi cum toto conatu suo potuit tantum scire de Deo... quantum post adventum Christi scit vetula per fidem" ( p. 10 ??? ). In my opinion, this is a powerful indication that Aquinas' Christian instinct tells him that faith is not a matter of intelligence and truth-knowledge but of decision and action under grace. Let us consider this vetula, a simple woman who lives the Gospel but has never studied theology. Aquinas realizes that this vetula is a model of faith, and that the faith that saves--the Christian faith--cannot be equated with his own science or scientific, explanatory knowledge as a theologian, for she has none of it. In a discussion with a pagan philosopher or with the Christian theologian, Aquinas realizes, this woman will be inarticulate and easily confused, as by the way were Socrates' opponents. Thus, contrary to what Aquinas states, this woman does not know (scit) more than the philosopher to whom he compares her, for she doesn’t know anything in the Aristotelian sense of judging truthfully, much less in the sense of judging truthfully on the basis of evidence.   Aquinas may know (scire) more about  God than any philosopher before the advent of Christ, but this woman does not .

Yet Aquinas says that this vetula knows immensely much more than any philosopher before Christ. In what sense? In the same sense in which Aquinas could say that the vetula knows immensely much more than many a theologian, though Aquinas grants that the theologian ‘knows explicitly’ (that is, in my interpretation, knows propositionally and specularly) many more truths than the vetula. Indeed, Aquinas would consider the vetula who lives the Gospel a model of faith, whereas he would not consider a model of faith a theologian who assents explicitly to each and everyone of the truths of faith but who does not live the Gospel. It is, therefore, in the sense that the vetula lives the Gospel that Aquinas means that the vetula knows PRACTICALLY much more than the philosopher and even than the theologian. If so, however--and here we arrive at the central point--, faith is not, as Aquinas defines it, a matter of assent to the truths revealed, for in that case the theologian who knows more than the philosopher would be the model of faith, not the vetula. Thus, Aquinas is working with two different nd incompatible notions of faith. Different, because the stated kaith consists in assent to what is revealed on the basis of God's authority, whereas the unstated faith consists in living the Gospel. Incompatible, because byto the stated notion of faith Aquinas as a theologian knows immensely much more than the vetula, whereas by the  unstated notion the vetula who lives the Gospel knows God–not “about God”--immensely much more than Aquinas as a theologian. Now, it was this latter, unstated notion of faith that made Christian intellectuals instinctively reluctant to understand their faith the way Aquinas did in order to fit it to the demands of the Aristotelian paradigm of knowledge.

Unpacking the unstated notion of faith

We might say that the difference between the learned theologian and the vetula is that the former has theoretical knowledge whereas the latter has practical knowledge. But the expression is dangerously equivocal, for it suggests that both the theologian and the vetula know the same, except that the vetula, in addition , lives according to what she and the theologian know theoretically (though she only implicitly), whereas the theologian of my example does not. Such hypothesis, however, has already been discarded, for the vetula does not know what the theologian knows. This, indeed, is the sense in which the theologian, and even the non-Christian philosopher who knows something about God may rightly be said to know immensely much more than the vetula. Now, if the vetula does not know what the theologian knows, her practice--that is, his living by the Gospel--cannot obviously be guided by what she does not know, that is, by what the theologian knows. In fact, the theologian knows--and knows immensely much more than the vetula-- about the Christian faith (if indeed such theoretical, explanatory, specular knowledge can qualify as knowledge), but knowing about and living the faith are entirely different things. An intellectual atheist interested in knowing about the Christian faith can come to know as much about the Christian faith as an orthodox theologian. The difference between the two is that the orthodox theologian assents to what he knows, whereas the atheist does not; the atheist merely understands what the theologian understands and assents to, that is, judges the case to be such. But the vetula neither understands nor assents to that, yet she is a model of faith. So the theologian's knowledge about the Christian faith is totally different from faith .

The reason why we do not realize this radical difference is because we are used, following Aquinas, to identify faith with assent to revealed truths, which misleads us into assuming that the vetula assents implicitly to the same to which the theologian assents explicitly . But if the vetula is a model of faith and does not assent to the truths to which the theologian who knows about faith assents, but who is not a model of faith in spite of his assent, then faith cannot be a matter of assent. And it is only because Aquinas understood faith as assent to certain truths that he could confuse the philosopher's assent to the truth of God's existence with the Christian faith he professed. This, too, is why he had to struggle with the problem of the incompatibility of the scitum and the creditum in order not to reduce the Christian faith, or at least a portion thereof, to something that could be achieved by reason left to its own devices., For Aquinas knows by faith (or through his experience of the life of faith) that faith is not a matter of intellectual acuity and that faith--that is, saving faith--is a divine gift, not a human achievement .

Let us return to the vetula. She is a model of faith because she lives the Gospel. If we ask her what she knows that allows her to live the Gospel she will be very inarticulate, for, one would say, she lives by Christian instinct much as animals live by animal instinct propted by the environment. The vetula has an ability to act as a Christian ought to in response to every circumstance. When someone is in need, she will go out of her way and at high personal cost to help as much as she possibly can. When insulted, she will not respond with an insult, much less will she plan a revenge; she will, on the contrary, help the person who insulted her when that person is in need. When adversity falls on her she will praise God as much as when things go her way, etc. In other words, she possesses "Christian wisdom," a wisdom that is entirely different from--and in many cases even contrary to--worldly wisdom. (Notice, however, that he or she ‘possesses wisdom’ who acts wisely, not he or she who identifies intellectually the correct action.

Now animals are creatures of instinct. But our vetula is not an animal. Her instinct is not Christian instinct, it is Christian wisdom. Animals do not have to learn how to act as animals, for such practical knowledge is inborn in them. People, on the other hand, not being cretures of instinct, do learn how to act as people, and Christians learn how to act as Christians . Furthermore, being creatures of instinct, animals act necessarily according to instinct, whereas people choose the way they act and decide whether or not they will act according to their Christian culture.

This little woman learned her Christian wisdom. How did she learn it? What is what she learned? She  learned it in the same way in which any child, as a member of a particular culture, learns the world in which he or she lives and has to respond to, as well as his or her place within it. This little woman learned her world as a still developing sacred story in which she finds herself involved as one of the characters or agonists or participants, a story in which the protagonist is a loving, all-powerful, provident, mysterious God in whose hands she, and everybody, and everything is, whose beloved creatures are she and everybody else, and a story which is moving towards God's-willed completion which will consists in shared bliss with His creatures. How did she learn it? By listenining to the story as her own story and practicing her humble personal role in the story, by loving her loving God above everything, herself included, and by loving others as she loves herself. Initially this practice was guided by other more experienced participants in the still developing story, who guided and corrected her, and now she needs little guidance and correction because playing her appropriate role in the still developing story comes to her naturally, though not necessarily effortlessly. With God's help and for God's sake, she responds as a Christian to the developing contingencies and moves peacefully, though not necessarily effortlessly, towards the destiny of all flesh in the firm hope of her glorious resurrection in the flesh.

And, mind you, she freely enacts her own role. She could go the comfortable way many other people go, both those who profess to be part of the same developing history in which God is the protagonist but who live as if this did not define in any way their own roles in the history, and those who do not see themselves as characters in a developing history whose protagonist is God. Our vetula could go that comfortable way too. She even feels sometimes tempted to go that way. But she does not because she chooses out of love for God to go God's way. Thus the fact that this vetula was trained from her infancy to see herself as a character in the developing story of which God is the protagonist does not preclude her personal, free participation in that story. In this sense, it is not a matter of instinct, of a pre-set, ineluctable way of living, but of wisdom--of Christian wisdom. So much so that, when confronted with situations of which our vetula has no previous experience, she finds guidance in her experience as a participant in the developing story of which God is the protagonist to invent by herself the apt response to the new situation to which no one ever taught her to respond.

Now, let us assume that this sort of wisdom is to be called knowledge, and even that it is the only thing that deserves such name. If so, this vetula knows infinitely more than the philosopher, for the philosopher knows nothing; Indeed, in the assumption that the vetula’s wisdom is knowledge, then knowing the existence of God in the sense of assenting to the truth that God exists on the basis of evidence does not qualify as knowledge, since it is not what the vetula does. Moreover, this vetula knows infinitely more than the Christian theologian who knows the existence of God--even of the triune God--, for the theologian knows nothing; indeed, knowing the existence of such a God in the sense of assenting to the truth that such God exists on the basis of God's own authority does not qualify as knowledge either, since it does not exhibit the characteristics of the vetula's knowledge. The theologian has beliefs--let us say true beliefs, infallibly true beliefs if you prefer--about God. However, the theologian--at least the theologian conceived as knower in the sense of assenting--does not know, as such, what the vetula knows, for the vetula does not assent; she lives God's existence by living in His presence.

The theologian's and the philosopher's knowledge

How does the vetula's knowledge differ from the others' non-knowledge? Let us take the philosopher's non-knowledge first. Suppose that no one had ever believed in God, our philosopher included. In that case the philosopher would not even know that there is no God, nor would he ever ask questions about God's existence. Thus, only because others believe that God exists can the philosopher question God's existence. Now, assume that our Catholic theologian asserts that God exists. (As Searle says, “it is not true that God exists” presupposes the proposition that God exists). The philosopher will ask for the reason for the statement because he has become curious about whether there is a God. For the philosopher, therefore, knowing is assenting because of sufficient reason,

that is, because of evidence that makes the assent fully justified: what explains--in the sense of rendering rationally evident, or rationally justified--what is affirmed (in this case God's existence) is knowledge . Now, supposedly the Catholic theologian, as Catholic, affirms God's existence on the basis of God's authority, much as an American child affirms that Mexico exists because his father says so, whom he trusts. For the philosopher, however, authority motivates the theologian’s assent, but doesn’t motivate it rationally, for it does not render rationally evident what is affirmed . Thus the theologian, confident in the power of his or her God's-given reason, will confront the philosopher in the latter's very turf, where nothing is taken as real except if there is sufficient reason for assenting to it. Here enter the five proofs.

That the theologian-become-philosopher will persuade the philosopher of God's existence is rather doubtful, as shown by a long history. The scenario, however, is interesting in other ways. For one, the theologian has implicitly accepted the turf--that is, the constitutive rules--of the philosopher's game and is playing it seriously, that is, as being at least a --though not the only--valid way of access to reality. Secondly, our theologian has implicitly accepted, since he affirms God's existence on the basis of divine authority but grants that it can be proven, that there are two valid ways of knowing, that is, of accessing reality: divine authority and rational evidence . In other words, our theologian has implicitly defined "faith" as functionally equivalent to philosophy, to the rational rather than the authoritative way of acquiring knowledge. Thirdly, having done this, our theologian has already an enormous vested interest in the validity of the rational demonstration of God's existence. Why? Because, once he has accepted that faith is functionally equivalent to philosophy, the theologian cannot fail to realize that the  validity of his faith-knowledge hinges on the validity of the rational demonstration of God's existence, for otherwise his faith would be totally irrational and therefore not knowledge at all. Why? Because the theologian cannot fail to realize--and if the theologian fails, the philosopher will bring it to his attention--that his belief in God's authority presupposes God's existence as its praeambulum , that is, as its rational condition of possibility , without which faith is a purely blind, rationally unjustified, trust . Indeed, how can the theologian trust a God whose existence he does not know ?  Saying that he knows God's existence on God's authority will not do, for that precisely is the vicious circle from which he has to extricate himself, and the only way is to demonstrate rationally that God--a Being whose veracity and omniscience cannot be doubted, and is therefore unexceptionably reliable or trustworthy--exists. In other words, philosophical theology or theodicy has become foundational, as its condition of possibility, of the life of faith . Fourthly, our theologian-become-philosopher has already implicitly introduced a distinction of classes within the community of believers: the enlightened ones--that is, the theologians-become-philosophers-kings--and the vulgar Christians, like the vetula, who go by blind faith in God and do not even realize that their faith hinges on the validity of the five proofs of the theologians-become-philosophers. Fifthly and finally, our theologian, by reducing faith to the functional equivalent of knowledge as defined by the philosopher, has put himself in a bind as a Christian, as a believer. For, if he is to be consistent, he has to concede that the authority of God's revelation is ultimately dependent on what is acceptable to reason left to its own devices, but this is unacceptable to his faith.

This, in fact, is the bind in which Aquinas found himself, for he was in fact the theologian-at-least-potentially-become-philosopher that I have been characterizing. This is why he had to confront again and again the difficulty mentioned above--with what success we will see in a moment. This is why, out of fidelity to his faith, Aquinas stubbornly maintained--whether consistently or inconsistently we shall see--that faith is in no case identical with philosophical knowledge. And this is why, too, people like Luther and the early Barth, even at the price of being accused of fideism and seeing themselves as fideists, rejected Aquinas' conception of faith and the consequent undertaking of theodicy which seemed to them to subordinate God's judging Word to man's judgment.

The vetula's knowledge

There is another way in which, before encountering a Catholic theologian who affirms God's existence, our pagan philosopher could have been confronted with the bare possibility of God's existence. He might have encountered our vetula who lives her faith rather than a theologian who asserts God's existence. Seeing her live and hearing her mention her God the philosopher might have been alerted to the possibility of God's existence. Having become curious, he could have interrogated the vetula and found out, not without difficulty, because the vetula is not very articulate, that she believes in the existence of a God who created the universe, on whom everything in the universe depends, who cares for his creatures, who loves us and is supremely worthy of our love, who became one of us and suffered and died and rose from the dead, and who will resurrect and judge us and share His eternal, blissful life with us at the end of times. So informed, and not without amused irony, the philosopher will ask the vetula why she believes that such a God exists, for he does not find the slightest shred of evidence for His existence. Needless to say, our vetula will have no even remotely satisfactory answer, if any, of the kind expected and demanded by the philosopher. If the philosopher wants to waste his time, he will try to explain to the vetula that she only believes but doen’t know that her God exists, and that she should therefore give up her belief in God. Needless to say, the vetula will not even understand what the philosopher is talking about, and they will part ways--the philosopher to occupy himself in more promising knowing endeavors, the vetula to continue her life of faith.

Now, according to Aquinas, the vetula knows more than this philosopher who does not even know that God exists (something which the philosopher, according to Aquinas, could and should know), much less that God is triune (something that the philosopher, as such, neither is expected to nor can know). But does the vetula really know, as Aquinas says she does? Not if knowing is what the philosopher claims that knowledge is. But not, either, if what Aquinas claims that faith-knowledge is. For faith is, according to Aquinas, a knowledge motivated by the intelligent grasp of God's authority (that is his omniscience, infallibility and veracity), and this vetula certainly has no such grasp on the basis of which she believes what God says. She believes because she believes. In other words, she is a fideist,* a blind, irrational believer. In short, not only does our vetula not know more than the philosopher and, at least implicitly, the same as the theologian, but she does not know anything at all; she merely believes blindly.

And, indeed, she does not know anything at all, if knowing is what the philosopher says it is (for knowing is the opposite of blind blelieving), and if faithing is what Aquinas is it is (*for the result of faithing is partly achievable by knowing). However, our inconsistent Aquinas also maintains that knowing is "aliquid aliud" than what the philosopher says it is , for otherwise the philosopher would be able to attain at least a portion of the faith-knowledge thorugh his own efforts, not "aliquid aliud," and he also maintains that the vetula is a model of faith (as Abraham was) and thus Aquinas would logically have to say that, in this sense, the vetula knows "aliquid aliud" than a mere theologian knows.

[Strictly speaking she is not a fideist. Fideism is blind belief, belief without any evidence whatsoever. However, this presupposes that knowledge is a matter of evidence, and that at least certain revealed truths can be known on the basis of evidence . Well, all this paradigm of knowledge with its evidentialism is out. Chirsitan wisdom is not knowledge, at least not knowledge in this evidentialist sense. It is choosing God as one’s end by choosing to act accordingly. Now, the end is in the future, and it becomes an end by the Christian being disposed to pursue it and actually pursuing it. Hence no room here for the  specular conformity in which truth supposedly consists].

Let us pursue this more promising avenue of which Aquinas gives us some scattered hints but which he himself could not pursue because of his having been coopted into becoming a philosopher.

Does our vetula have any "beliefs," whether rationally or authoritatively justified? I do not think so. Belief is characterized by intellectual assent. As the geometer believes that the three angles of a triangle amount to two square angles, so does Aquinas believe that God exists on the basis of is intellectual grasp of one or the five of the proofs, and that God is triune on the basis of his intellectual grasp of God's authority supported by the proof of the existence of an infinitely veracious and omnisicient Being. But our vetula has no intellectual grasp of this kind, and therefore no belief to speak of. Yet she is a model of what Aquinas as a Christian (that is, in his unguarded moments, when he is not speaking as a theologian-become-philosopher) calls faith as the result of grace. What, then, does she have that neither the theologian nor the philosopher has? The answer is that she has faith, meaning that she is a Christian in the sense that she lives as a Christian, which is something that only human beings can do. But how can this be spelled out?

Certainly not in the way in which Aquinas spells it out. For he spells it out in terms of her possessing knowledge--be it philosophical knowledge or faith-knowledge--, mere objective judging which has already been discarded. Yet Aquinas as a Christian is right: this vetula is a model of faith (though not of reflective knowledge of faith). How so?

Christianity and Judaism, before being intellectually coopted by Greek intellectualism into self-misinterpretation, are good models, for they are whole cultures, whole self-interpreting ways of life, whole practical-historical modes of being in the world represented by those who participate in the culture . Our vetula is a member of the community of those who live the Christian way, what early Christians who had not yet recieved the label "Christians" simply called " the way." Faith is, therefore, to be understood, as a way of being in the world, and--for those who live it and by it--as the way of being in the world, the way into which one is born by God's grace or into which one is, sometimes, later coopted by God's grace through the phenomenon called conversion. What is that particular way?

First of all, it is an entire way of being in the world , that is, not only surrounded by it but involved interactively and exposed to it as part and parcel of it.

Secondly, the world is temporal and so is our being in it. Our identity and the world's identity are temporal. This means that it is in the process of becoming--that it has become what it presently is, and that what it has become and presently is is still becoming what it is not yet and what it will become. And we, the beings in the world, are in the same temporal manner, for we too are part of the world. This means that we live by way of facing what is to become of us and of the world, from the present in which we are confronted with what is to become, on the basis

of what the world and ourselves have already become.

Thirdly, the world and our being in it is not only temporal; it is historical. In other words, insofar as it has an intelligibility, it has the intelligibility of a history, not of a mere process. The intelligibility of a past process taken as it now is , that is, of a process that was but is not in process anymore, is the intelligibility of a static system; and, if the process is regular, it is the intelligibility of a system of interrelated variables, as in the case of falling bodies on inclined planes. This yields intelligible succession, change but not history. History is structured by intention and by desire and fear; it adds to change desired and feared change, change at least intended though not always sucessfully achieved, change by which the participants are affected. This makes history, unlike process and change, dramatic.

The goals pursued by individual agents who choose their own goals, that is, by historical subjects-objects, are not chosen arbitrarily, insofar as they are chosen rather than just given and mechanically received. There is a choice of future world and correlatively of future self. One makes oneself motivated. For this one needs models, "heroes," originally provided by the culture of which one is a member, with which one shares a common history within which one occupies a place and which one contributes to carry out to its hoped for conclusion. But a history in the making, a developing history, is defined by the character of its foreseen ending--both personal and collective--which is either favorable or unfavorable, for the ending gives its proper significance to the characters and events . (We say: "With hindisght I would have acted otherwise," either as to the means employed or the particular end pursued). We have no hindisight of the ending, for it is future and to be actively brought about. Concerning the desired ending, if considered possible enough to be pursued, we only have foresight in hope, for the success of our action is not totally dependent on us but contingent on circumstances beyond our control. As for a collective ending to be collectively pursued , it is possible only in the measure that the members of the collectivity share in common that ending as their end, that is, as to be pursued, as hoped for in common. And for that to happen the ending has to be object of hope, that is desirable.

And for that to happen fully, the developing history has to have a real ending, a closure, a conclusion that gives its proper ultimate significance to the characters and the events.

[The eternal recurrence, the circularity or even the spirality of time, allows at best for a temporal and relative community of interests among individuals with tragic adventures interrupted by feared death, not for a common history . This is what Christians have in common, defined by a common ending-end pursued and hoped for in common from which "the way" takes its meaning .]

What I have been groping for is the realization that the key to the vetula as a model of faith is a self-understanding defined by the developing history in which she participates as an actor. "The way" is inconceivable without this particular conception of the history in which God Creator, Redeemer, and Traveling Companion toward the ending-end is the main character . Actually, the conception of the developing history in which one participates by the mere fact of existing as a human is the key to every life. For even the denial of an ending-end makes possible the self-definition of the character. And the denial of a common history renders inevitable the definition of life as a tragic, absurd individual adventure. Now, a common history comes to us from a tradition, and so does the denial of a history and of a common history . And so does the diversion by which, through forgetfulness of death and the inventions of 'progress' and individual happiness, we escape the terror of absurdity, of the senseless of our lives and of everything. To this diversion philosophy has contributed much. For it has led us to think that there is a rational way out. A first attempt, that of theoretical reason, is to reduce to a "system." But the attempt is hopeless for it suppresses ongoing temporality with its open, contingent future; by the same token it suppresses history, in which we are dramatically involved as participants. And within this context Aquinas' explicit understanding of faith as knowledge of sorts is to be placed .

A second, more promising attempt, that of practical reason, fails too, for it rests on an un-historical (and, in this sense, 'systematic'), abstract understanding of human nature on which all reflective persons can agree, whatever their conception of the ongoing history in which they are involved as participants. In fact, however, with the same understanding of human nature, two characters involved in two different histories will be totally different. Thus, for instance, humility is a virtue for Aquinas and not for Aristotle, though they share fundamentally the same understanding of human nature and of happiness as its telos , but not the same understanding of the historical adventure in which they are involved. Furthermore, even assuming that Aristotle's understanding of human nature were correct, human nature by itself cannot motivate; what motivates is the encounter of a given traditional-individual with his/her world as a developping history and as defined by the understanding of that history.  

I and Thou: An incomplete, half-cooked appendix

( My references here are to Emil L. Fackenheim, "Martin Buber's Concept of Revelation," in The Philosophy of Martin Buber , Edited by Paul Arthur Schilpp (Cambridge U. Press 1967, pp. 273-296).

Reading Fackenheim's article I sensed that I could use his ideas as another approximation to distinguish the vetula's 'knowledge' from the theologian's and the philosopher's. Let me try.

One characteristic of Aquinas' idea of faith as a sort of knowledge is that it amounts to the 'content' of a judgment-statement, for faith is true and only contents can be true. (God Himself becomes a content--an "It". This is why Aquinas needs to prove the existence of one omniscient, veracious God as a praeambulum. Aquinas fails to realize that the presence of an "It" is uni-directional, that an "It" cannot address).

This is the move of the philosopher: to examine contents--which is what Socrates did--until a content is found that is necessarily and universally worthy of assent by virtue of its compelling evidence. Knowledge obtains when such content is found. Aristotle, if Nussbaum is right, came to realize that such knowledge was just an ideal, that even in the field of theoretical reason such knowledge could only be approximated, and that in the field of practical reason, that is, of deliberation, decision, and action, the approximation could only be less exact, so that practical knowledge could be 'knowledge' only in a highly analogical sense. Analogical, because there still was the diminished universality of rules of thumb, without which the very idea of knowledge would be absent and the analogy would disappear. For the moment, however, we do not have to concern ourselves with practical knowledge, because Aquinas squarely placed faith within the paradigm of theoretical knowledge.

Of all speech acts, only statements, which express judgments, possess a content separable from the speech act . And the speech act that corresponds to such content amounts to a claim of truth concerning the content. Since no other speech act is a formal claim of truth, no other speech act can be either true or false. And since divine revelation is true, it has to take the form of statements, and faith has to consist in the appropriation of divine statements through assent.

Such are the notions of revelation and of faith criticized and rejected by Buber. His alternative is that what we call faith is a living encounter, a complex, ongoing interactive relationship of a living person with God. This is what I attempted to describe above in the case of the vetula.

Let us return to 'contents'. If faith had no content it would be, much as Kierkegaard seems to suggest that it is, an inexpressible and totally untransmissible relationship between 'the single individual" and God. Certainly, however, that is not the case of the Christian faith, where doctrine occupies an important place, particularly in the Catholic tradition. Furthermore, in all the Christian traditions we have the Scripture. Kierkegaard, of course, grants this, but without giving it its due. Objectively, he says, the story of the Bible may be false, since the historical is not susceptible of proof. Faith is the decision to break the objective uncertainty through the leap of the faith-decision that places us in the living relationship with God of which Buber speaks. In other words, Kierkegaard is as Greek as Aquinas in his conception of knowledge, but he opposes faith to knowledge because he does not share Aquinas' confidence in the power of reason to reach certainty in existential matters. I think that both Aquinas and Kierkegaard are wrong, though in different ways and for different reasons. For Aquinas the content itself is the object of faith;

for Kierkegaard the content is an extrinsic occasion for faith.

Concerning doctrine , my position is that it is a necessary but secondary component of faith (which should not, therefore, by itself, be simply identified with faith, as Aquinas did), and that insofar as it consists of statements the statements are involved agents' statements, not explanatory, not contemplators' statements. For the moment I will deal with the latter point, though in a very roundabout way necessary to render my position intelligible.

Aristotle rightly claims that emotions are not mere feelings in that they are intentional of object. One is not afraid as one is in pain or in pleasure. Being afraid is being afraid of something-dangerous, intending something as dangerous. Here the perceiving is, as always, perceiving as, but it is an emotional perceiving withoutwhich the perception itself is impossible. The object is dangerous in the same way it is big or round. The fact that its being dangerous is relative to the perceiver does not make the perception of its being dangerous subjective as opposed to objective. Dangerousness may be perfectly objective, though necessarily relative. And the perception of dangerousness which consists in the attitude of fear may only be  true or false by analogical analogy tothe  perception of the size or the shape of the object feared . There is, therefore, a sense, in which emotions are cognitional.

Notice, however, that in this case the object is intended gerundively--as avertendum--, which implies futurity and (ideally) interaction, not as true . The latter is the case only in judgment, which is reflective. Thus, an emotional perception, as well as an 'objective' one, is not a claim to truth, for it is not a judgment. Thus, in this sense, it is neither true nor false . What, then, is an emotional perception? In the case of an emotional perception the object motivates, moves the subject, makes the subject emote toward the object, feel moved by the object gerundively, in this case as avertendum; not so in the case of unemotional perception. Motivates the subject to what? To act in response to the motivating object according to the emotion, but not in response to the emotion (for the emotion is not the object for response except in pathological cases). It is the object as perceived that motivates the action on the basis of actual (though not perceived as object) exposure of a subject to the object's action. If the emotion is appropriate to the object, not as perceived but as real, the action in response to the real object will be appropriate, for the response intends not the object as perceived but the object as real, as capable of producing effects independently of its being perceived.

In the case of humans, though, at least in a range of cases, the action is not necessarily automatic, but can be the result of deliberation and decision. What is the deliberation about and on what basis is it conducted?

I THINK that Buber partly confuses I-Thou relationns with aesthetic relations . There is no way of having an appropriate I-Thou relation with a tree, anymore than with a desk, for neither is a Thou. But the important point is the contrast between philosophical contemplator and involved participant. To this I come now.

(1) The philosophical contemplator is not in interactive situation, in situation of exposure and decision. Her concern is not with response but with what is the case, with truth (unrelated to future response because of the following trait). (2) Furthermore, her model of truth is borrowed from geometry; it is visual all the way, it involves sensory and intellectual vision from which assent arises. (This was Socrates' case who wanted an algorithm for action, much in the way in which geometrical theorems are algorithms for geometrical action because the algorithms themselves are true).

Because of these combined traits the philosophical tradition inaugurated by Socrates was significantly misleading. The first trait, by itself, could preserve at least the understanding of the practical character of 'truth', that is, the relationship of the inquiry to motivation, deliberation, decision, and action. (This understanding is the critic's understanding, not the agent's understanding, for the critic is not exposed and does not have to act, whereas the agent is an does. This seems to be, at least in part, Aris' understanding in the NE). Regarding the past , as in judicial judgment, it amounts to the imaginative  re-enactment of the agent's deliberation compared to that of an ethos-shaped ideal agent who has the benefit of hindsight. Regarding the future , as in the senatorial deliberation about a particular like the Gulf War, it amounts to a collective deliberation. This senatorial deliberation is closer to life (assuming that one has time to deliberate at leisure) for it aims at decision and action and does not have the benefit of hindisght and, therefore, exemplifies the agent's position. The distinction between "regarding the past" and "regarding the future" shows the profound difference between the two; the critic is at best an emotionally involved spectator who is not herself exposed and does not have to pay for the effects of her action, who is not subject to the pressures of the moment, and who does not have to act on her decision. The British captain underscored well this difference: "You had a year to deliberate, I had a minute."

If this distinction is lost, if the involved agent's 'knowledge' is reduced to the contemplator's knowledge, or if the contemplator's knowledge is attributed to the agent in the analysis of decision as Aris does in the practical syllogism, one is on the way to intellectualize the agent's 'knowledge." And when this mistake is compounded with the second trait, that is, with the visual model of truth, as if the agent's knowledge were of the same kind as the explanatory knowledge of the geometer, then the possible practical character of the first trait is voided and with it the motivational character of the object and the connection with response. This is what Socrates did in searching for essential definitions. Here the divorce between "fact" and "value" has been consummated. This 'objective' confrontation of a pure contemplator merely interested in the truth, with an object by itself, voided of all motivating power, is supposed to yield what deserves the name of "knowledge," with the consequent result that the emotions become blind feelings and the connection between knowledge and action is severed. No definition, essential or otherwise, can have motivating power.

Apart from the two mentioned traits, or perhaps involved in the second, there seems to be a third step taken. The agent who "faces the future by way of action" is confused with, and reduced to, object to be explained and, in this way, known.

How and why did this happen? See my paper to be completed on Socrates, Plato and Aris on the origins of philosophy.

The above is supposed to lead us to an enlightening contrast between the agent's and the contemplator's knowledge. What, then?

Let us say that the vetula possesses the agent's knowledge. What about the philosopher and the theologian? The three, supposedly, know God. But are we not simply equivocating in calling what the three possess by the same name? And, if so, which of the three possesses what deserves to be called knowledge?

The vetula is a human being, not an animal. She deliberates. We already know that the vetula is a member of a culture, of a tradition. That is, she shares with her Catholic community not only dispositions to act in certain ways, but dispositions to act in certain ways that are intelligible as responses to a world of a particular kind shaped by a particular ongoing history in which she occupies a particular place, a history which defines in vague outline the value-charge of what she has to respond to-- not a system of deductive rules for action for which the motivation is interest limited by other's interest determined in a state of reflective equilibrium through blindness as to one's position in society. In this history the protagonist is God, withwhom the vetula interacts as she interacts with other characters and non-characters in the history in which she herself is a self-defining character.

It is to be remarked that the vetula's knowledge is practical. She does not proceed in her deliberations from reflective knowledge of the history and by way of deduction, but from what she is within that ongoing story. Her "perception," to use Aris' own term, is Christian because she is a Christian . That to which she has to respond in concrete circumstances already motivates her to respond in the Christian way, in a world with an already determined--in outline--coefficient of instrumentality, where her Christian emotions already define in outline the object to be responded to, the goal to be pursued, and the means to be employed or discarded, so that her deliberation is intensely focused and her action almost automatic except in conflictive cases.

What would the philosopher, as such, think of the vetula's

knowledge? The philosopher is a foundationalist. He needs compelling evidence that constrains assent--ideally grounded on intuition of essence, if not at least on extrapolation from effect to cause as the best explanation, as Aquinas resigns himself to go in the proofs for God's existence. For the philosopher, practical knowledge is grounded in theoretical knowledge. First you have to know demonstratively that God exists, then you can be addressed by Him and respond. Since the vetula cannot demonstrate God's existence independently from her being addressed and her response, the vetula does not know.
