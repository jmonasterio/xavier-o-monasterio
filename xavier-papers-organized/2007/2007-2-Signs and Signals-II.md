Knowledge and verbalization: Searle’s enunciative model of knowledge and Polanyi’s tacit knowledge

Michael Polanyi introduced the notion of “tacit knowledge,” by which he became known, to refer to those cases in which a speaker “knows more than he can verbalize.” He exemplified this situation with the experiment conducted by psychologists Lazarus and McCleary in 1949.

“These authors presented a person with a large number of nonsense syllables, and after showing certain of the syllables, they administered an electric shock. Presently the person showed symptoms of anticipating the shock at the sight of ‘shock syllables’; yet, on questioning, he could not identify them. He had come to know when to expect a shock, but he could not tell what made him expect it. He had acquired a knowledge similar to that which we have when we know a person by signs we cannot tell.” ^(F2)

Searle would say that  the very notion of tacit knowledge (from now on TK), as characterized by Polanyi, is nonsensical. Indeed, it seems to amount to an unconscious knowledge, for the subject who supposedly knows is not aware of what he knows, since he cannot say even to himself what he knows. Now, an unconscious knowledge is an oxymoron. Why? Because the very notion of knowledge, or at least the ordinary notion of knowledge that common sense seems to impose on us, implies on the subject’s part the awareness of what he knows. He knows what he knows thanks to the awareness he has of what he knows, so that he would not know what he knows without that awareness. And that awareness is expressed in enunciative language.

As an example of this ordinary notion of knowledge, let us consider the knowledge of which Searle spoke in the preceding chapter, since he used the word ‘knowledge’ in its ordinary meaning. According to Searle, in linguistic communication the hearer who understands what the speaker says thereby acquires the knowledge  –that is, becomes aware of– what the speaker intends to communicate to him. And it would be inconceivable that the hearer knew what the speaker intends to communicate if the hearer had no awareness at all of what he knew. In other words, what a subject knows becomes object known to him by the very fact of his becoming aware of it. Hence no knowledge of object without awareness of what is known, because knowledge implies and demands the awareness of the object known. Thus, when Polanyi defines KT as a knowledge in which the subject cannot say what he supposedly knows , we have to conclude, Searle would argue, that TK is a knowledge in which the subject is not aware of what he knows, which is a contradiction in terms.  

For his part, Polanyi would reply to this objection to what he calls “tacit knowledge” by referring us to the undeniable facts of the experiment he described: the subject of the experiment cannot say which are the shock-producing syllables because he does not know them, that is, is not aware of them. Yet the subject’s behavior in reaction to those syllables shows that he knows them in a certain way which does not involve verbalization –and what is involved in this certain way of knowing is what Polanyi calls TK.

Polanyi calls it “tacit knowledge” not only because what is known is not formulated or verbalized, but because, contrary to common belief, linguistic formulation of what is known plays no essential role in knowledge.

Polanyi maintains that, contrary to what Searle assumes  the knowing process does not terminate in a representational awareness of object, which  supposedly is enunciated linguistically either internally or externally. Rather, the knowing process terminates in action without the mediation of verbalization.

In this process, through INTERIORIZATION, the so-called ‘object’ plays the role of instrument, not of representation. Interiorization is practical in the sense that it is achieved and becomes fixed  or habitual through  practice of the game not through verbal formulation of the rules. A child learns to play baseball, not by formulating the rules repeatedly ( for he doesn’t   kno w them), but by ‘ following ’ them, that is,  by imitating again and again in his own practice of the game the practice of those who play the game competently until following the rules becomes habitual. Later, through objectifying reflection, the adult may be able to formulate the rules of the game. However, neither is this formulation necessary for the correct performance by the child, nor is the formulation equivalent to the performance.

Polanyi’s alternative conception of knowledge

Searle assumes that the consciousness of object in which knowledge is ordinarily deemed to consist is an awareness of the object’s characteristics. The connection of knowledge with language is that what is known can be said, that is, can be expressed and communicated to others linguistically. Indeed, when enunciative verbalization occurs, what is enunciated is nothing else than the subject’s awareness of object. If I tell you, for instance,“The door is open,” what else am verbalizing but my perceptual consciousness of the present state of the door?

As we know, however, Searle claims that enunciative speech is only one possible mode of speaking, the appropriate mode to communicate what one knows. But communicating what one knows, that is, attempting to produce in a hearer the effect Searle calls “illocutionary,” doesn’t exhaust our linguistic possibilities, which comprehend also the production of “perlocutionary” effects.  The latter effects do not consist in knowledge to be acquired by the hearer; it conaists in action. However, the illocutionary effect has a primacy over all the others in that the acquisition of the knowledge or awareness by the hearer of what the speaker intends to communicate is a necessary condition, according to Searle, for the realization of the perlocutionary effects, if any are intended by the speaker. Both the illocutionary and the perlocutionary effects are linguistic effects in that the speaker achieves them  through saying , and saying is communicating what one intends to communicate by expressing it in words. Thus, though not all saying is enunciative, saying is the fundamental speech act on the success of which depends the success of all linguistic action.

Given the fundamental importance of saying, we should pay special attention to what Searle calls “the principle of expressibility,” which expounds the connection between that of which the speaker is aware and its linguistic expression. The principle to which Searle refers is “that whatever can be meant can be said.” ^(F3) Searle introduces this principle for his own purposes, which have to do with the possible limitations of the conventional meaning of particular languages. For my part, I consider important this principle for what it indicates as to the way in which Searle understands the fundamental linguistic act of saying. Through saying in a particular language common to the speaker and the hearer, the speaker makes understood or known what he or she intends to communicate. It is, therefore, the ideal in any language that what is said express as exactly as possible what the speaker intends to communicate so as to make it exactly understood by the speaker. And the principle indicates that the speaker can always find the way to express exactly what he or she means to communicate, though in everyday communication incompleteness and lack of precision do not necessarily prevent the success of the communication.

Now, what does the principle of expressibility tell us about Searle’s conception of the way in which language functions? It tells us that saying is an explicit formulation of what one wants to make known to the hearer. I say, in English, “The door is open” because this English expression says exactly what I want to make understood to the speaker. I say, in English, “Lend me your pen..,” because this expression conveys exactly the meaning that I want to make known to the hearer so that he or she may lend me his pen. The hearer, for his part, may or may not lend me his pen ,but if he speaks English he will understand what I mean. Linguistic “meaning” is, therefore, of the order of intelligibility, something to be understood, not something to be perceived.

Notice that the notion of knowledge as correspondence is implicit in Searle’s conception of saying..According to t he principle of expressibility ,what we say must correspond as precisely as possible to what we have in mind or mean to communicate in order to make it known. If what my hearer understands corresponds exactly to what I mean to communicate, the linguistic action is successful. ^(F4)

In my analysis of saying I have insisted on the Searlean ideal of the exact correspondence between what is said by the speaker and what is understood by the hearer because it reveals a conception of language that differs radically from Polanyi’s conception.    Indeed, Polanyi sees language as a system of SIGNS. Now Searle believes the same, but he has a different idea of the way in which signs  signify. Searle thinks that signs signify as signs Of a meaning  to be understood, whereas Polanyi thinks that signs function as signals For the performance of an action. Let us explore in depth  the difference between these two conceptions.     

 

Searle explains that the difference between linguistic signs and all other signs is that linguistic signs mean by saying or enunciating what is meant. On this point Searle sees the dysanalogy between linguistic enunciations and games. ^(F5) A certain move in the game of chess means “checkmate” but doesn’t say it , while the verbal enunciation “checkmate” says it. Again, the red traffic light  is a sign because it ht means “stop,” but doesn’t say it, whereas the verbal enunciation “stop” says what it means. In other words, linguistic signs, unlike all other signs, make explicit what they mean by saying it. Hence, as we just saw, the underlying idea of Searle’s “principle of expressibility, that whatever can be meant  can be said,” is that the speaker ideally should say exactly what he means, because only by enunciating exactly what he means can the speaker make known to the hearer exactly what he means.

This point is confirmed by Searle’s understanding of his own  farfetched but so revealing example of the American soldier faced by the Italians in World War II, Searle would deny that the American soldier who said to the Italians, “Ich kome von dem Land wo die Citronen bluhen” obtained linguistically the effect of being set free (assuming that  he had actually been set free by the Italians). The reason is that what the American said to the Italian doesn’t mean in German “I am a German soldier” (which Searle assumes is what the speaker intended the Germans to undertand); it means “I come from the land where the lemon trees flourish.”

We can take advantage of this example to begin presenting Polanyi’s very different conception of the way in which signs function. Searle thinks that signs are signs OF, that is, refer to, what they mean. That is why he deems linguistic signs superior to all other signs, since they make explicit what they mean. As he argues, through a gesture, which is a non linguistic sign, I can ‘tell’ somebody to leave the room, but only by saying it can I tell somebody to research the way in which.... The reason is that linguistic signs are the only signs that signify by saying what they signify. Hence their superiority over all other signs. Polanyi, however, denies that this is the way in which linguistic signs signify. Actually –Polanyi thinks–that linguistic signs, as all other signs , are signs, not OF a meaning to be understood, but signs FOR performing a certain action.  This shows in Searle’s own example of the American soldier in Italy during World War II. In that example the enunciation “Ich kome...” is intended to substitute for “I am a German soldier.” However, this enunciation, in its turn, was not meant by the Americans soldier, as Searle assumes, to terminate in the theoretical or purely objective awareness that the American was a German soldier; rather its was meant  to elicit from the Italians the action of setting free the American soldier.

But let us take the water from above. How, concretely, does this linguistic enunciation, be it in German or in English, “ elicit  from the Italians the action of setting free the American soldier? Not by being a sign OF what is meant by the English words uttered, for what is said is not meant to terminate in knowledge of what is said, as claimed by Searle, but by functioning as a signal for action in the same way in which the objects we perceive when we are engaged in ordinary action. Thus, if I am about to cross a road, a car coming in my direction at high speed signals to me not to cross the road but to stop. Why? Because I want to reach alive the other side of the road.

This is the opportunity to consider the essential role desire plays in the case of Signs. For something to function as a sign, that is as a sign For performing an action, it has to be conjoined with a desire or a need on the agent’s part. Water perceived signals the action of drinking it, but only to an agent who needs water and is actually thirsty.  In short, the perceived water functions not as a picture or representation of what is the case, something to become aware of, but as a map-instrument that guides the agent’s action to satisfy his need or his desire. When I am driving on a road, I perceive all the signs that give directions to drivers, but only the sign that announces the city I desire to reach functions actually as a sign for me, for it signals to me the action that will led me to my desired destination.

The difference between a map conceived as a picture of a terrain and a map actually used as an instrument to achieve a goal through action is the difference between a sign OF a meaning to be deciphered and a signal FOR performing an actiom.. To return  to the experiment with the shock-producing syllables, it is the difference between observing the syllables that appear on the screen to become aware of their observable characteristics, and using the experienced sensations produced by the syllables as instruments to guide one’s action toward the realization of the desire to escape the expected shock. Much as the blind person who carries a stick to find his way uses the sensations produced in his hand every time the stick hits an object on his path as an instrument  or map to guide himself toward the place he wants to reach. To a desirous agent, these sensations are not representations OF anything but signs FOR action .

When verbalization occurs, Polanyi objects that the subject’s awareness of .object, in the case of TK,  does not consist in a consciousness of the characteristics of the object, or in the verbal enunciation of those characteristics. What occurs in the subject of the experiment at the sight of the shock-producing syllables is not an awareness of the object’s characteristics, for he does not know the syllables. It is, rather, a blind fear of certain syllables that leads the subject to brace himself for the shock as if protecting himself from it. Now, the subject’s bracing himself  is not a verbal enunciation, not even a silent one; it is a non-linguistic conduct or behavior motivated by the affection of fear – a conduct whose reason is totally obscure to the subject, since he doesn’t know which syllables produce shocks.  

The subject of the experiment makes no linguistic enunciation. Rather, he behaves in a certain way when the shock -producing syllables appear on the screen, and this behavior is observed by those who conduct the experiment. Actually, the observed behavior of the subject is precisely the basis on which Polanyi grounds his claim that  the subject ‘knows’ in one way or another, and without that behavioral expression at the sight of the shock-producing syllables and not at the sight of the other syllables Polanyi would have no basis for his claim of tacit knowledge. Now, this connection of what Polanyi calls TK with action , in the absence of either external or internal verbalization –which is why the knowledge is said to be tacit rather than explicit–  is an important clue. Indeed , it is a first indication that knowledge may consist in action of a certain kind rather than in  consciousness of the object’s characteristics enunciated in verbalization, as it has been traditionally assumed. In which case Polanyi’s notion of tacit knowledge far from being as oxymoronic as it initially appeared, may even open a new epistemic avenue. Let us, therefore, review in detail Polanyi’s views on TK, with special attention to the role played by action instead of enunciation of the characteristics of the object of which the subject is aware.

In distinguishing the illocutionary from the perlocutionary effects, Searle’s attributes primacy to the illocutionary effect, which consists in knowledge, over the perlocutionary effects, which for the most part consist in actions. The underlying reason for this unique primacy attributed by Searle to the illocutionary  speech act over all the others is that the illocutionary effect consists in knowledge, and knowledge of what the speaker means is, in Searle’s opinion, the necessary condition for the success of all the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker, whereas the success of the illocutionary intentions of a speaker does not depend on the success of any of the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker. To put Searle’s point concretely, a speaker cannot say anything without intending to convey to the hearer the knowledge of what he means, but he can say something without producing or intending to produce any other effect than this knowledge. Correlatively, the hearer cannot fulfill the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker without knowing those intentions, that  is, without the illocutionary effect obtaining, but the hearer can know the perlocutionary intentions of the speaker, thereby insuring the success of the communicative act, without necessarily fulfilling the speaker’s perlocutionary intentions. Still more concretely: if I do not know what you mean when you ask me to lend you  my pen, I cannot lend you my pen, but I can know that you ask me for my pen, and not lend it to you. Moreover, you can produce in me an illocutionary effect without intending any perlocutionary effect. For instance, in Searle’s interpretation of saluting,  when you salute me, all you intend  to produce in me is the knowledge that you mean to salute me, and the communicative exchange succeeds if only I gain that knowledge. So knowledge is independent from action, for it may obtain in the absence of any action,  but action is dependent on knowledge.

Polanyi, for his part, construes the relationship between knowledge and action in a totally different way. He does not ignore that knowledge involves a relationship to action; on the contrary, he sees action as an intrinsic component of knowledge. Indeed,  he identifies tacit knowledge with disposition to act and with the actual appropriate action when the right opportunity arises . Given this intrinsic relationship of knowledge to action, Polanyi would necessarily deny that knowledge is independent from action, as Searle claims. In other words, for Polanyi, the knowledge involved in the oral communicative exchange is ESSENTIALLY practical, that is, bound to terminate in action, not in a purely theoretical awareness of a fact or even of a rule, as it is for Searle. For example, knowing an order received does not consist, according to Polanyi, in becoming aware of the order as an objective fact, but in the practical disposition to obey the order received, and in actually obeying it at the appropriate time. Similarly, according to Polanyi, knowing a promise received does not consist in becoming  aware of that fact, as one becomes aware of a color when one sees it in the course of an experiment in a laboratory, but in actually bracing oneself for the fulfillment of the received promise at the appropriate time. Thus, if you promise to repay me the $100.00 you owe me when I come to pick them up at your office at 3.00 P.M., the appropriate understanding of your promise does not consist in my registering that fact and then doing nothing, as Searle contends,  but in expecting the fulfillment of your promise and in the action of actually coming to your office at 3.00 P.M. to collect my money.

It will be useful to realize that the Polanyian notion of knowledge as intrinsically related to action rather than to consciousness of an object’s characteristics and/or its linguistic expression is not new at all. Spontaneously, that is, when free from epistemological prejudices, most of us believe that the members of at least the higher species of mammals, say, dogs, possess the ability to know. At the same time, most of us believe that dogs are non-linguistic agents, that is, that they do not posses the linguistic ability we possess, for they do not express in words what they know. But this shows that we are implicitly assuming that knowing is not identical with the awareness of an object’s characteristics or with the consequent linguistic capacity to enunciate what one knows or is aware of.

On what basis, then, do we attribute to dogs the ability to know, in the absence of verbal enunciation? On the basis of their observed actions. Certain of their actions are, we say,”intentional,” that is, for the sake of achieving a purpose. They  reveal an agent’s purpose , in that the dog’s agency is not mecanically uniform but adapted by the dog to different circumstances for the sake of a desired goal that satisfies a natural need of the dog. By contrast, a marble’s fall down an inclined plane is a mecahnically uniform action, not an adaptive action, because it is caused by the environmental circumstances, and thus is not intended by the marble for the sake of the satisfaction of a need and is, therefore, not modified accordingly by the marble . For that very reason, we have no temptation of attributing knowledge to marbles: their action is not intentional, not for the sake of a goal intended-desired  by the marble, and the uniformity of their action shows no ability on the marble’s part to adapt the action to the circumstances in view of the realization of a goal. In a dogs’ actions, by contrast, we grasp the intention of a goal and, with their increased experience, an increasing ability on their part to adapt their actions to the circumstances and thereby make their actions more efficient for the realization of their goals. Hence we conclude that dogs know.

But are we assuming that animals know enunciatively, though silently? In other words, are we attributing to them an internal or cerebral linguistic ability, that only lacks the vocal organs to express externally in words that of which they are conscious and which they verbalize internally? Or are we , rather, implying that they know in a non-enunciative, tacit way? The correct answer is the latter . And the difference between the two answers shows in the different relationship in which knowledge stands to action in each of the two conceptions, which implies two different notions of knowledge irreducible to each other. In one of them –the one I reject, together with Polanyi, and attribute to Searle– knowledge is conceptually or intrinsically independent from action, and therefore can obtain in the absence of any action (remember that, according to Searle, the illocutionary effect is the only essential effect, the only effect that cannot fail to obtain if the communication succeeds). Action, however, is dependent on knowledge, for representational knowledge of object is deemed by Searle to be a necessary condition for appropriate action. In the second version – the one I (together with Polanyi) consider correct–   knowledge is not representational of object but instrumental for the realization of a desired goal . It is therefore intrinsically or “conceptually” ^(F6) related to action in that it is meant to terminate in action, and will actually terminate in action unless prevented by circumstances alien to the agent.  

This analysis may be refined, but as it is it suffices for my purpose. For my immediate purpose is simply to show that knowledge is not necessarily to be conceived, as Searle among many others conceives it, as an awareness of object that mirrors or reproduces in a factual way the object’s characteristics . Rather, my analysis of what – without any epistemological preconceptions – we recognize as knowledge shows that what we identify as knowledge, at least in the case of animals, manifests itself in intentional actions for the sake of desired goals. It shows, furthermore,  that verbalization of what is known is not a necessary component of knowledge, for animals are incapable of verbalization. In short, knowledge is not identified, at least in this case, with a factual awareness of the characteristics of its object, as implied by the ordinary or commonly accepted notion of knowledge shared by Searle, but with an agent’s ability to realize a desired goal through intentional action,  that is, through action adapted by the agent to the realization of the desired goal.

The notion of knowledge as awareness of its object’s characteristics has its roots in Plato and Aristotle and was adopted as canonical by the classical epistemological tradition. Searle’s philosophy of language reflects his own allegiance to that tradition. But, as we shall now see,  precisely therein lies a radical difference between Searle and Polanyi.  For the latter parts way with the classical epistemological  tradition, since he sees knowledge as “ tacit. ” This means that knowledge does not consist in possessing or gaining an implicit or explicit awareness of its object’s characteristics, actually expressed or at least susceptible of being expressed in enunciative language. Rather than consisting in an awareness of its object’s characteristics and in a consequent verbal enunciation of the object’s characteristics,  knowledge functions as a signal FOR action.

The difference between Polanyi’s and Searle’s conceptions of knowledge is that the former thinks that knowledge functions as a sign for action, whereas Searle thinks that it functions as a (verbal) representation of its object’s characteristics . This difference may be exemplified by the case of geometrical  knowledge. Does a student of Euclidean geometry who is able to prove a theorem and who sincerely claims to understand the proof and can even formulate it on his own, but who is totally incapable of applying that understanding to the solution of pertinent concrete problems, really understand? With most teachers of geometry, I would say no, because geometrical understanding is applicative and practical or instrumental, not representational of an actually existing  Aristotelian ‘nature.’ ^(F7)

--------------

Having distinguished the common or traditional conception of knowledge, shared by Searle, from  Polanyi’s own, I should now make clear that the two conceptions are not only different but are mutually incompatible. In other words, the two cannot be correct. Why not? If for no other reason, because of the esssential role desire plays in tacit knowledge,, whereas desire is not only not included but is positively excluded in the traditional conception of knowledge as representation, , for it is deemed to militate against the ‘objectivity’ of the cognition. The reason is that the consciousness of  object, in which knowledge supposedly consists, as well as its linguistic expression, have to represent the object factually or ‘objectively’– that is, without addition or subtraction, for that would disfigure the object and would, therefore, cease being true. Not for nothing, in his close commentary on Aristotle’s treatise on linguistic interpretation, did Aquinas contrast what Aristotle sees as the genuinely enunciative speech of the scientist, which is dictated exclusively by the object and leads to knowledge of the truth, with the non-enunciative speech of the rhetorician that appeals to the desires of the hearer and, therefore, distorts the object to be known. Aquinas’s passage is such a succinct formulation of the disturbing function of desire in matters of gnition when cognition is conceived as awareness of its object chraracteristicsthat it deserves to be quoted in full:

“Then Aristotle says, Let us therefore consider enunciative speech, etc. Here he points out that only enunciative speech is to be treated; the other four species [to wit, deprecative, imperative, interrogative, and vocative] must be omitted as far as the present intention is concerned, because their investigation belongs rather to the sciences of rhetoric or poetics. Enunciative speech belongs to the present consideration and for the following reason: this book is ordered directly to demonstrative science, in which the mind of man is led by an act of reasoning to assent to truth from those things that are proper to the thing; to this end the demonstrator uses only enunciative speech, which signifies things according as truth about them is in the mind. The rhetorician and the poet, on the other hand, induce assent to what they intend not only through what is proper to the thing but also through the dispositions of the hearer. Hence, rhetoricians and poets for the most part strive to move their auditors by arousing certain passions in them, as the Philosophers says in the Rhetorica. ^(F8)

Notice the contrast between the mind being led to knowledge exclusively by the observed object (“those things that are proper to the thing”), which Aristotle sees as the mark of genuinely enunciative speech, on the one hand, and the mind being led to knowledge by the desires or affections of the knower (“through the dispositions of the hearer”). As long as knowledge is conceived as a factual replication of the object by the consciousness of it the subject has and/or by the linguistic expression by the subject himself  of what is known to him, then desire can only be seen as a disturbing element in the cognitive process. When, however, as is the case with TK, the point of cognition is not to represent the object but to function as an instrument for the agent’s  conduct in order to achieve a desired end, then the desire of the end to be realized through action becomes an essential component of cognition. ^(F9)

Keeping this important  difference in mind, let us revisit the objections I previously raised to Searle’s linguistic philosophy, which is grounded in the representational conception of knowledge . And let  us consider as well the extent to which Polanyi’s instrumental conception of knowledge (whose aptness is immediately recommended to us by the fact that it squares with our spontaneous attribution of knowledge to at least the higher species of animals, even though they do not posses any linguistic ability) coheres with our linguistic practice and with the intuitions of the competent users of language.

WHY DOES SEARLE DEMAND EXPLICIT ENUNCIATION BY THE SPEAKER OF WHAT IS MEANT?, BECAUSE HE THINKS THAT ENUNCIATIVE LANGUAGE SAYS WHAT IS MEANT; FURTHERMORE,  HE SEES ACTION AS DEPENDENT ON SAYING (SEE BELOW). POLANYI, BY CONTRAST, INSISTS THAT KNOWLEDGE IS TACIT BECAUSE ITS FUNCTION IS NOT TO SAY OR ENUNCIATE WHAT IS THE CASE BUT TO TERMINATE IN ACTION BY BEING A SIGN FOR ACTION.

It will be useful to preface my examination of Polanyi’s views on TK by remarking that, unlike Searle, Polanyi never wrote a treatise on language, nor was he primarily interested in language. Actually, his central idea is that knowledge is tacit because it functions as a sign FOR. This implies not only that knowledge does not consist in the linguistic enunciation of its object’s characteristics, but that linguistic enunciation is not even required. Indeed, Polanyi’s shift from consciousness of the object’s characteristics and its consequent linguistic enunciation to action in the absence of linguistic enunciation implies a different conception both of the way in which language functions and of its role or lack thereof in cognition.

TK is intrinsically connected with action. Herein lies the radical difference between the ordinary notion of knowledge, shared by Searle, and Polanyi’s TK: the ordinary notion of knowledge involves the consciousness of object as vision of its object, with no direct  relationship to action.

The instrumental role of TK by contrast to the vision or revelation of object in which the ordinary notion of knowledge consists may be illustrated by means of a comparison to the way in which eyeglasses function. When the eyeglasses function as such – that is, when they are worn by someone, not when they lie on a table and are looked at– the person who wears the eyeglasses does not see the eyeglasses, that is, has no awareness of them as object of knowledge, as is the case when they lie on a table and are looked at. In other words, when the eyeglasses are worn, they do not function as object known but as an instrument. As an  instrument for what? Polanyi answers: as an instrument for action , that is, as an instrument that guides the agent’s action. Such is the role played by TK, according to Polanyi, and therein lies the radical difference between our ordinary notion of knowledge and TK. However, in order to fully grasp this difference we have to follow in some detail Polanyi’s analysis of TK in which the knowing subject of the classical epistemological tradition is replaced by the agent .  

In his initial analysis of the notion of TK, in The tacit Dimension ,  referring to the experiment with the shock-producing syllables,  Polanyi’s emphasis was on the fact that the subject knows more than he can say. Let us remark in this connection, in the first place, that in affirming that the subject knows more than he can say Polanyi is by the same token taking his distances from the epistemological tradition that equates knowledge with the awareness of its object’s characteristics and with  the consequent verbal enunciation of those characteristics, for in that tradition the awareness of the object’s characteristics entails the ability to enunciate what is known . Secondly,  Polanyi shifts the emphasis from verbal enunciation of what is known. to action. Indeed, given that the subject knows more than he can express in words, his knowledge is not manifested  in the verbalization of what is known by the subject, but in the subject’s actions. This is why, as we shall see, all of Polanyi’s examples of tacit knowledge refer to an agent actually engaged in action in order  to achieve a desired goal, not to a contemplating consciousness that expresses its knowledge through enunciations of the characteristics of the objects contemplated.  

Grasping this shift from the consciousness of the epistemic subject of classical epistemology to the agent’s actions is fundamental to understand Polanyi’s conception of knowledge. Knowledge, for Polanyi, is the deed of an agent exposed to the action of the surrounding world, not the deed of a contemplating consciousness invulnerable to the surrounding world.

Unfortunately, this displacement of the epistemic subject of the classical epistemological tradition by the agent exposed to the action of the surrounding world is insufficiently underscored, and perhaps even insufficiently grasped, by Polanyi himself. It will therefore be my task to bring this all-important point to the fore.

Let us begin with the example Polanyi employs to introduce the notion of tacit knowledge. The subject in the experiment with the syllables knows tacitly which syllables produce shocks. His knowledge is tacit because it does not manifest itself  in words that express his awareness of those syllables. Indeed, the subject cannot say even to himself , much less to others,  which are those syllables; rather, his knowledge of the shock-producing syllables manifests itself in his conduct of bracing himself for a shock when those syllables appear on the screen. Thus, in the sense of being aware of the characteristics of the shock-producing syllables and of being able to say which are the shock-producing syllables, the subject of the experiment does not know, for both the awareness of object and the linguistic enunciation of the object characteristics, in which knowledge is ordinarily supposed to consist, are absent in this case. Yet, Polanyi claims, the subject of the experiment knows the syllables, not in the sense of being aware of the observable characteristics of the syllables, but in the sense of acting in response to those syllables by bracing himself for the shock to come when those syllables appear on the screen . Action, therefore, rather than linguistic enunciation, is the way in which knowledge manifests itself .

As Polanyi’s analysis of tacit knowledge progresses, his emphasis shifts from the inability to express in words the characteristics of the object to what he calls “INTERIORIZATION .” Interiorizing something, Polanyi explains, is to identify ourself with it by making it function as the immediate term of TK IN THE WAY WE USE OUR OWN BODY WHEN WE ACT , thereby transcending it toward the realization of the desired goal.

To grasp the difference between knowledge as verbal enunciation (be it internal or external) of the characteristics of an object known, on the one hand, and, on the other hand, knowledge as instrument for action, let us reflect on what goes on in our everyday experience of SIGNS  which themselves are instruments for action, as we shall now see. As a concrete example, consider our experience with traffic lights. We think of red and green lights as signs. And we are not wrong. Where we go wrong is in thinking that signs are primarily signs OF something  to be known which can be verbally enunciated, when in fact signs do not function as signs OF anything to be known, but as instruments FOR ACTION. Thus, red, in the case of a traffic light, is not necessarily a sign that a car is coming in the transversal street –something that can be enunciated and understood.. Rather, it is  a sign FOR PERFORMING the action of stopping. Similarly, green is a sign FOR PERFORMING the action of moving ahead. And the same is the case with all signs, linguistic and non-linguistic.. The ringing of t he doorbell is a sign for opening the door, not for becoming aware of the ringing. An arrow on a road  is a sign FOR performing the action of moving in the direction pointed at by the arrow, not for becoming aware of the arrow. In short,, the correct comprehension of signs is not to be manifested in linguistic enunciations of what is signified, for there is nothing signified as to be known or understood*. Rather it is to be  manifested in the performance of the action signaled, for that is what signs signal or signify.*

 * But is not for the dog its master’s characteristic ringing of the bell a sign OF his desired presence? Why then does the dog wag its tail and moves joyfully to the door, if not  because he is expecting to find its master behind the door? Indeed, as the door-bell rings in a certain way, the dog expects to find its master at the door. Consequently, the dog runs to t he door to meets it master. But expecting and funning are not at all the same as understanding a meaning..In short, the sound of the bell does nor function as sign OF something to be understood, much less verbalized, but a signal for the action of running to the door. Not a sign THAT..., but a signal FOR The conduct of going to the door manifests the proper ‘understanding,’ not the propositional verbalization of a known meaning.

But doesn’t Searle have a point one the superiority of linguistic signs as manifested by his example of demanding a complex research on..?

Indeed he does, in a sense, but not in the sense he himself thinks. For he

thinks that the superiority of linguistic signs is that they signify by referring to a linguistic meaning to be understood. This is a relic of Plato’s and Aristotle’s intellectualism, that resulted, among other things, in their relegation of perception to a secondary, at best subordinate place, and in their notion of the superiority of the human being as the rational animal possessing a spiritual immortal soul. The sense in which the linguistic ability of human beings, totally absent in animals, renders them capable of deeds of which animals are incapable has nothing to do with their possessing a soul, but with their unique ability to reverse their dependence on the environment thanks to the delayed applicative power of language.

A semiology or theory of signs

Traffic lights are examples of artificial signs, that is, of signs invented by people for the purpose of regulating the conduct of people in certain circumstances. But artificial signs are just an extension of the so-called “ natural signs,” which are not invented but inherited and characteristic of each species. A certain sound by a mother sheep  functions as a not learned but inherited sign for her ewe – and only for her ewe, not for other sheep– to gather around her. This is a natural , that is, inherited sign. Once this is repeated, the ewe expect to find their mother when they hear the sign, for experience has taught them to associate the sound with the presence. There are, however, in addition, non-inherited, acquired signs. For instance, with a certain amount of training, certain shepherds manage to direct their dogs with the sounds of whistling to behave in a way that controls the direction in which their flock is supposed to move. Now, the sound of whistling is not a natural sign for the dogs to direct the flock, for it is not inherited but acquired through special training; in this sense, not only is it an acquired rather than inherited sign, but it is an artificial sign for the trained dogs for behaving in a certain way. All artificial signs are extensions based on natural signs. That is, we people can invent new signs only for those species that have the natural ability to respond to natural signs as signs.

The mechanism through which an acquired sign becomes and remains a sign for behaving in a particular way, in the case of animals, is complex. Many theories have been proposed to explain that phenomenon. What is certain is that the range of conducts that can be elicited through signs varies widely from species to species. For instance, very few species, comparatively, can become genuinely domesticated, that is, to behave as domestic animals, even under the best trainers.

Now, to come to humans, the most salient way in which we socialize or domesticate the young members of our own species and teach them certain conducts that we consider preferable, once they have reached a certain level of developmental maturity, is through the semantic system we call language .Unfortunately we are as confused as to the way in which language itself as a system of signs functions as we are concerning the way in which other non-lingujistic signs function. This is manifested by the multiple explanatory theories of language.

In our culture,  permeated by the intellectualism of the Platonic and Aristotelian traditions, the objects that are “signs” are generally deemed to be signs OF something to become aware of by understanding the sign, and what is understood can be verbalized or enunciated linguistically. Combined with this is a representational theory of knowledge, in which the intellectual and reflective act of judgment –which finds its natural expression in enunciative language– rather than the perceptual act of judgment which finds its natural expression in action, occupies the most important place, for it alone is supposed to put us human beings in cognitive contact with the real. So, according to this theory,  the “real” is not something to be perceived,  but something to be understood and enunciated in a linguistic judgment that says what is a-temporally (i.e., abstractly) the case, for the verbal”is” is itself  a-temporal. It means objectively and "ontologically" but not temporally as a present tense. By the same token, all the speech acts that mean or intend  to elicit a conduct (such as commands, requests, etc.) are assumed to be implicitly enunciative of the conduct to be elicited, for the conduct to be elicited cannot obtain but on the condition that its enunciation be understood in the first place, as Searle claims. Language is, therefore seen, primarily and essentially, as an enunciative semantic system, and only secondarily and accidentally related to action. No wonder that in a culture in which prevail such interrelated ideas of knowledge, of enunciative language, and of reality as that which corresponds to what is in the mind and  is enunciated as to be understood, the notion of signs FOR performing an action without the mediation of a meaning previously  understood and enunciated, as well as the notion of linguistic enunciations as signals FOR acting in a certain way rather  than as signs OF something  to be understood, find such a strong cultural resistance.  

But why did I introduce the theme of  signs? Because the objects of our ordinary perceptual experience, which takes place when we  are actually engaged in action, not when we are reflecting or just contemplating the surrounding world, function precisely as signs FOR performing an action,  as signs FOR behaving in a certain way. Thus, as already explained., the car I see coming in my direction at a certain speed, when I am about to cross a road, is for me, who want to reach safely the other side, a sign FOR not crossing the road. Similarly, the water I perceive near me when I am thirsty is a sign FOR moving  toward it and drinking it.

For Searle. given his conception of language, the more explicit and precise the enunciation of what is known, the better. Not so for Polanyi, who sees knowledge as intrinsically related to action. In this connection, it is important to realize that we do not observe or know objectively  the thing that is a sign; it just refers us to what it signals. To borrow Polanyi’s expression, we just act from it toward the satisfaction of a desire. In other words, my perceiving it shows not in objective awareness of  the sign, but in my behaving in the way signaled by it. Thus, the subject of the experiment with the shock-producing syllables had no awareness of the observable qualities of the syllables that produced shocks; he responded to them automatically with the self-protective behavior they signaled of bracing himself for a shock to come.  This is typical  of perceptual experience. ^(F10)

Previously I mentioned that all our acquired and artificial signs are “extensions” of natural signs.  By that I meant that the objects that function as natural signs have to be  related, at least indirectly, to the satisfaction of the natural  needs of the species for which they function as signs . Thus, sensed humidity functions for us as a sign to start looking for water only because we have the natural need for water, without which we cannot survive. Had we not that need, sensed humidity would signal nothing to us, for it would be “irrelevant.” Again, only those animals that have a natural need, say, for the kind of comfort or security that living with people provides them can learn to live with people and be trained to perform certain tasks because they inherit those needs, of which the mentioned behaviors acquired through training are, so to say,”extensions.”

Now, the human species seems to be the most malleable or adaptable species by far, in that it can be domesticated –or domesticate itself– to the most varied “domi” or houses (domesticate comes from the Latin word “domus,” which means “house,” “home.” In other words, we can find our “home” or come to  feel “at home” practically anywhere. How come we are so adaptable? Because we are the only species capable of reversing our dependence on the environment by transforming  the environment to adapt it to our needs. Why?

I think that the answer to this so important question has to do, on the one hand, with (1) the fact that our human  needs do not function as instincts, as they do in the case of animals, that is, they do not dictate definite, unmodifiable, uncontrollable conducts. On the other hand, the answer has also to do with two other interrelated facts, to wit: that we are linguistic creatures and, as such, are capable of (2) deliberative reflection and (3) of applicative knowledge of the objective conditions on which successful action depends .

Let us begin with (1 )  instinctive conducts , which is the simplest element. An instinctive conduct is like a response in the S-R model of behavior, only more complex than a response. A reflex response is an inherited, automatic, uncontrollable  and  unmodifiable behavior prompted by a certain feature of the environment of a living creature. If there is intense light in my environment, my eye responds by contracting  the pupil and producing tears. If there is a certain intense scent in my environment, my stomach responds by vomiting. Well, an instinct or instinctive conduct is equally inherited or not learned, automatic, uncontrollable and unmodifiable as a response, but is much more complex conduct than a response, for there is no one to one correspondence between the presence of the stimulus and the response. . Examples of this are the complex pattern of behavior of bees in building hives, of birds in building their nests, etc. These behaviors vary from species to species. They are complex and highly skilled, unlike responses. Now, we humans, by contrast to animals, inherit some simple  responses to certain stimuli, but no instinctive complex conducts. We build our houses in the most different styles. Moreover, our houses may not have the extremely sophisticated ventilating system of a hive, which seems to be a marvel of engineering, according to the experts. Thus, the difference between the house built instinctively by bees and the house built by an architect, is not that the human builds better than the bee, but –as Marx comments– that the human builds in a totally different way, for the human invents and chooses what he is going to build and how, whereas the bee just executes blindly the plan of nature as dictated to it by instinct. ^(F11)

To complete my answer to the question why we humans have more varied forms of behavior than any other living species, which allows us to adapt to practically any environment and even, within limits, to plan and choose our future environment, I return to the other two factors I mentioned, to wit: that (2) we are linguistic creatures and that, (3) as such, are capable of deliberative reflection to choose our conduct whose success in achieving our goals depends on objective conditions independent from desire.

REFLECTION is often mischaracterized in the epistemological literature, by contrast to perception , as a second-order activity , in that it is about the past moment of perception-interaction and presupposes it, whereas perception confronts us with the present and presupposes nothing. For instance, if you tell me that you offer me a job, I may answer that I will reflect on your offer. I can reflect on your offer because you already have made me an offer, but I cannot possibly  reflect on something which has not taken place yet and of which I did not become previously aware at the perceptual moment.

Wherein lies the MIScharacterization of reflection? In that perception does not confront us with the present as an object known or to be known – that is, as a phenomenon, as a phenomenal object; rather what is perceived functions as a sign  FOR action and  refers us to a desired future, because we are exposed to what is perceived. . For instance, concerning your offer of a job, which takes place at the interactive moment of perception, I am not objectively aware of your offer, but only tacitly, as a sign For action. Consequently,  I will accept your offer, if I want to have a job, or reject it if I don’t want a job. By contrast, the very comparison of the perceptual moment with the past object of reflection presupposes that we perceive in the way in which we grasp the object of reflection, that is, objectively. Under the weight of the representational epistemology characteristic of the Western philosophical tradition, enunciative language is deemed to function as a descriptive or representational sign OF what is enunciated, that is, of what is perceived. My conduct in response to your verbal offer of a job presupposes, it is argued,  that I am OBJECTIVELY  aware of your offer. However, that is not the case, for I am not aware of your offer as object known, but only tacitly and practically or instrumentally, as a sign-For .    

But there is, in addition to this objectifying reflection, a different conception of reflection, which unfortunately occupies a very secondary place in our philosophical tradition, obsessed as it is with objective representational  knowledge. Let us call it deliberative reflection by contrast to the spurious objectifying reflection whose impossible purpose is to know the real through linguistic representation . The purpose of deliberative reflection is not to know objectively what is the case, but to determine how to act – the way the British captain reflected or deliberated to determine whether to abandon ship or fight the fire. Deliberative reflection, which may take place during the perceptual-interactive moment, presupposes the capacity to neutralize or put in abeyance the signaling force of a sign FOR, thereby putting in abeyance one’s own conduct in response to it, instead of responding  automatically to the signal, as animals do under the pressure of the prevailing desire. It results in the capacity to know the world and its signs in an only potentially instrumental way through its regularities in order to adapt the means to the desired-chosen ends. Both of these capacities depend on our linguistic ability as their condition, for language refers us primarily to CONCEPTS, NOT TO REAL OBJECTS TO WHICH WE ARE ACTUALLY EXPOSED .

Now, animals do not reflect in any sense. All their dealings with their surrounding world are participatory or applicative, immediately practical. Why? Because they are not linguistic creatures capable of conceptualizing not only ”objects” but events and situations . The conduct of animals is predetermined by heredity and previous training, they neither have to, nor can, genuinely plan their conduct nor the consequent modifications of the surrounding world. That is, they cannot invent new goals-means to satisfy their needs. That is why each species of animals, aside from the evolutionary modifications that happen to them, lives in approximately the same way as their ancestors lived. Not so the human species. Based on the observed regularities, we invent means to transform the world to adapt it to our needs (agriculture is a case in point),  and in the process transform ourselves to adapt ourselves to the transformed world. But what has this to do with language?

The judges of the British captain mentioned above determined that he could, and therefore should, have saved his ship instead of abandoning it to the fire. Yet they were not present at the time of the events. This shows that conduct in response to a situation can be planned –“on paper,” as it were– while the planner is not actually involved in the situation and is not being pressured to act by the urgency of the situation. This is a variety of deliberative reflection, that involves putting the world in abeyance and, by the same token, putting oneself in abeyance before acting.  However, to put the world in abeyance in a complex situation like the one just described one needs language to enunciate (based on past experience or experiment), the regular reaction to fire of the different materials of which the ship was made, under the climatic conditions that prevailed at the time. Enunciative language makes possible these complex, ideal objectifications or conceptualizations of regularities. Only a linguistic species, like ours, can do that, for enunciative language renders possible to think of, or conceptualize, objective regularities and their effects on our world, in their absence, in pure hypothesis.    

Notice that these judges were not deliberating, they were considering whether the desirable action of fighting the fire could be successful given the objective conditions, because the realization of projects through action depends on natural regularities that are objective in the sense of being  independent of desire. Now, these conditions can be very complex, as Searle indirectly suggests ^(F12), and only the use of language –particularly the language of science , which, as a problem-solving activity is ultimately applicative– makes possible their knowledge and the storage of that knowledge in writing, destined to to the satisfaction of those desires concerning which the stated information will be pertinent at one time or another.

This is the activity to which Aristotle referred by the name of episteme, in Latin scientia. He understood it as yielding necessarily true knowledge, because it was knowledge per causas , which were intelligible and, therefore, not contingent but necessary. However, unlike myself, Aristotle was an objectivist (hence his “categories” of the objects known), an intellectualist (hence his intelligible  “substances”), and a realist concerning “really existing natures.” But the very notion of signs-FOR skips the Serlean and Thomistic step that requires theoretical representational understanding as a condition for action. As indicated by Wittgenstein, following a rule or obeying an order is not an interpretation or understanding of the rule as an ‘object” of knowledge ^(F13); rather it is a result of the inherited tendency to respond to signs-FOR. The Aristotelian “causes” at work in our world, conjoined with desires, are signs-For, because they can be manipulated as instruments to satisfy our desires . If I know that cold air, under certain conditions, causes an undesirable cold, I can invent   the means to raise the temperature in my home or to cover myself to avoid the cold.. What the activity we call “science” does is not, as Aristotle thought, to grasp intelligible and necessary laws or objective natures, but, through careful observation and measurement , to grasp regularities of action in our world which, at the perceptual-interactive moment of application and under the appropriate conditions of desire, will function as signs-instruments FOR problem-solving action. (Notice that the important or remembered regularities enunciated by science are related to common, permanent human desires, such as health).

To consider now Aquinas own example in I Meta, Lect. 1, as developed by Peghaire in his article, “A Forgotten sense, The Cogitative,”

So far, Aristotle would say, we are on the level of perception-interaction, of “judgments of the senses.” We still do not know why the herb cures the illness. Now, the physiological  regularities with which physicians deal are more complex and less exactly measurable than the physical regularities with which physicists deal. The former cannot be enunciated in a mathematical statement, like the law of falling bodies on inclined planes. The mathematical (i.e. involving  measurment) law of physics, however –contrary to Aristotle’s assumption about “natures”– doesn’t explain or make intelligible why bodies fall the way they do on inclined planes, it only renders their fall exactly predictable at the time of application, thanks to the measurements involved in the law. As a consequence, the law makes possible the instrumental use of the grasped regularity to achieve our human purposes at the appropriate perceptual moment.

Searle suggests that language makes possible complex conceptual objectifications ( Spech Acts, pp. 38-39) that combine a number of observed regularities under one enunciation. These are based on remembered regularities of the world, which are conceptualized in an enunciation and, when and if conjoined with a particular desire, will become signs FOR action at the perceptual moment of interaction. In the absence of desire the registered regularities are only concepts,  potential signs FOR. Anyway those complex regularities couldn’t be remembered and even detected if they were not linguistically registered not as statements of fact to be understood,  but as regularities of our world  to serve as maps For action at the right moment when a desire can be realized. Thus, in conjunction with our human ability to plan action, instead of acting immediately and automatically according to the strongest desire, as animals do when perceptually confronted with signs, enunciative language makes possible complex delayed actions through non-representational conceptualizations of regularities.

But doesn’t this show that, after all, enunciative language is representational OF what is enunciated?   No, not in the only important sense of being objectively representational of something to be understood , but of referring to regularities to be made relevant by desire,  something that will function as a sign FOR action under the appropriate perceptual-interactive conditions . As indicated by Kuhn’s problem-solving, instrumental, applicative conception of science (by contrast to the representational “ontological” conception involved in what he calls “textbook science”),  this is the way in which scientific enunciations mean and in which their meaning is to be cashed.  If cashed as if they were a representation of what is the case in reality, it results in the metaphysical conundrum that has infected Western philosophy from Plato and Aristotle through Kant and beyond. - What philosophical reflection has traditionally attempted to do is to retrieve the Aristotelian "realities". (From it resulted Aristotelian metaphysics, a well as traditional metaphysical doctrinal theology, both of which are hopeless, misleading ventures). ^(F15) Science, by contrast, went the applicativte way. This reflection on signs may help us to grasp the fundamental difference between Searle’s and Polanyi’s respective conceptions of the way in which language functions. Searle thinks that language functions, primarily, as a system of signs OF something to be understood and formulated linguistically, for “the characteristic effect of meaning is understanding” ^(F16) Only secondarily and only sometimes, when there happens to be a perlocutionary intention, does language function as a sign For performing an action. For his part,  Polanyi thinks that language that refers to perceptual objects  functions a sign FOR the actual performance of an action, without any intervening objective understanding or enunciation of the action to be performed. According to Searle, in order to obey a command I have first to understand the command. In other words, the verbal command is a sign OF something to be understood theoretically. According to Polanyi, by contrast, the verbal command, in the presence of the appropriate desire,  is directly a sign FOR performing the action commanded. The understanding here involved is practical, in that it is meant to be manifested in the actual performance of the action commanded, not in the theoretical understanding of the action commanded.

An important observation is pertinent in this respect. Contrary to Searle’s conviction, enunciative language is no exception in functioning  as a sign OF something to be known rather than as a sign FOR performing an action, for it too functions as a sign FOR.  Indeed, in its original usage, that is, in oral face-to face communication among agents who share the same situation , even enunciative language does not function as a sign OF something to be understood, namely, what is said. Rather, as is the case with all other signs, it, too, functions as a sign FOR performing an action.

The reason why we get easily confused on this point, as Searle himself does, is that in the case of enunciative language, which is not a complete speech act –different in this from the speech acts possessing an explicit perlocutionary force– the action signaled is not specified, because (to return to the same Serlean example) the speaker rightly assumes the addressee’s desire to treat well his German allies, which will automatically convert the enunciation into SIGN FOR or instrument for action.

Notice, however, that the agent’s desire is not sufficient by itself to result in action. When, thanks to our human linguistic ability, the project is conceived in advance, in thought or “on paper”, in the absence of the concrete circumstances in which it is to be realized , the agent has to determine when is the appropriate moment to act and realize his project. In the case of perceptual co-presence of speaker and addressee who share the same situation, as is the case in Searle’s example of the American soldier with the Italians, there is no problem, for the perceptual co-presence of speaker and hearer in the same situation, conjoined with the ORAL statement, functions itself as the sign For immediate action. However, in the case of a so-called “scientific law,” as, for instance, the application of the enunciation of the WRITTEN law to the solution of a concrete problem, it is through repeated practice of the application either in the laboratory or on paper that the student learns how and when to apply the regularity enunciated by the law to solve a problem to which the law applies. (Incidentally, this is the way one learns to use the map of a region to find his way to any place within the region).

This is the opportunity to consider the essential role desire plays in the case of Signs. For something to function as a sign, that is as a sign For performing an action,

it has to be conjoined with a desire or an inherited need on the agent’s part. Water perceived signals the action of drinking it, but only to an agent who needs water and is actually thirsty. The perceived water functions not as a picture or representation of what is the case,  to become aware of, but as an instrument for action, as a map-instrument that guides the agent’s action to satisfy his need or his desire.

The difference between a map conceived as a picture of a terrain and a map actually used as an instrument for action is the difference between a sign OF and a sign FOR. To return  to the experiment with the shock-producing syllables, it is the difference between observing the syllables that appear on the screen to become aware of their observable characteristics, and using the experienced sensations produced by the syllables as instruments to guide one’s action toward the realization of the desire to escape the expected shock. Much as the blind person who carries a stick to find his way uses the sensations produced in his hand every time the stick hits an object on his path as an instrument  or map to guide himself toward the place he wants to reach. Like all other animals we come to the world ‘wired’ to act in certain ways in response to certain objects in order to satisfy our needs. The objects that satisfy  needs vary from species to species, as well as the needs and the response. The complexes object-need-response characteristic of each species are hereditary. Both chicken and ducks instinctively follow the first animal that takes care of them at birth, but ducks follow it into the water, whereas chicken stop at the border of the water. Instincts are complex, teleological  forms of behavior, inherited rather than learned . They are teleological in that they are meant to fulfill the purpose of satisfying a need of the animal, but they are not intentional, in that the animal doesn’t choose the behavior nor intends the purpose. A step lower in complexity than the just mentioned “conducts” are those inherited “movements” characteristic of a species that we call “reflexes” . Such are the sucking reflex in newborn children at contact with the nipple, the vomiting reflex in response to certain scents or tastes, the toad’s reflex of sticking its tongue in the direction of whatever comes near its mouth and is of a size that can be degluted by the toe. Reflex movements are simple responses to a stimulus, which obtain automatically whether or not they are appropriate to satisfy a need. Instinctive conducts are complex and not purely automatic, in that the animal may adapt its conduct, according to the perceptual signs-FOR,  to obtain the desired result. In contrast to reflex movements and instinctive conducts, in which the goals to be obtained are inherited, stand those complex conducts in which both goals and means are invented. Agriculture is an example.

To begin with Searle, he thinks that language is a system of signs that say or enunciate or describe what is meant or signified by the speaker in order to make his meaning known to the hearer. If, by understanding what the speaker says, the hearer becomes aware of what the speaker means, the speech act is successful, for the essential function of language, which consists in making known what the speaker intends to communicate, is fulfilled. This is what Searle calls the “illocutionary effect,” the effect that the speaker cannot fail to intend whenever he says something, for the speaker necessarily intends to make known to the hearer what he means. In addition to the illocutionary effect, sometimes the speaker may also intend  to produce a “perlocutionary effect” which does not consist in knowledge but either in an action of the hearer (for instance, the action of obeying in the case of a verbal  order) or in an affection of the hearer (for instance, the affection of feeling threatened in the case of a verbal threat, or the affection of being amused in the case of a verbal joke). However, to the difference of the illocutionary effect, which is essential to the success of the speech act as such, the perlocutionary effect is accidental, because it is not necessarily intended by the speaker in every communicative action, and because the success of the  communicative action is insured if only the hearer gains the knowledge of what the speaker intends to communicate, independently of whether the speaker intends a perlocutionary effect in addition to the illocutionary effect, and independently of whether the perlocutionary effect, if intended, actually obtains or not.

Searle thinks that the essential function of language is to make known to the hearer what the speaker means by saying or enunciating what is meant. Indeed, that is why:

Now, Searle’s view that what is said by the speaker must express or describe what the speaker intends to make known to the hearer, and that the hearer gains that knowledge by understanding what is said, rests on the assumption that enunciative language is a system of representational signs. What is said must represent as faithfully as possible what the speaker intends to communicate, and what the hearer understands must be as faithful a representation as possible of what the speaker intends to communicate. Thus what is said by the speaker is a sign OF what the speaker means because it is a verbal representation OF the speaker’s communicative intention, and the same goes for what is understood by the hearer.

Totally different from Searle’s is Polanyi’s conception of the way in which language functions, because Polanyi thinks that signs –verbal signs included—  do not mean by representing in any way what is meant. Neither a red nor a green traffic light is in any way a representation of what is signaled; each of them signals an action to be performed, not a meaning to be understood. Similarly an arrow on a road signals the kind of action to be performed by the agent who encounters the sign, but does not signal the action to be performed by representing or enunciating the action signaled. Hence the appropriate understanding of the arrow encountered on the road consists in the actual performance of the action signaled, not in a mental or verbal representation of the action signaled.  

But is not language a unique case among signs? Is not Searle right that the uniqueness of language among signs is that it means or signifies by saying what is meant by the speaker? Not so. In Polanyi’s opinion, Searle is wrong on this point. The problem with Searle’s view of language is that he thinks of “saying” as enunciating, and he thinks of what is enunciated as a piece of information to be understood or to become aware of by the hearer, rather than, as is the case, as to be responded to by  performing the action called for. Precisely because Searle is wrong on this point, he  claims that the saying involved in a request, such as “Lend me your pencil,” succeeds as a speech act if only the hearer becomes informed of the speaker’s desire, as if in making a request the speaker intended to inform the hearer of his desire rather than having the hearer comply with his desire by actually lending him his pencil.

I have already argued against this Serlean view which ignores the grammatical difference between the subjunctive or the imperative in which desires are expressed and the indicative in which pieces of information are imparted. This is the reason that, as I argued in the previous ch apter, if Searle were right on this point, language would function in the bastard way English functions in a course of English for foreigners, where the request “Lend me your pencil” is understood as a piece of information to be acquired by the hearer rather than as a request to be complied with. In that artificial situation, all the sayings of speakers function as Searle wrongly thinks that sayings normally function – as mere factual enunciations Clearly, this is not the way in which competent users of English understand requests, nor the way in which requests actually function in ordinary linguistic, face-to-face, oral exchanges, nor the way in which a person who makes a request intends his or her requests to be understood. However, rather than merely repeating the arguments already given against Searle’s views on language what we need is to explore the alternative conception of language furnished by Polanyi.

Polanyi’s alternative conception of language

To begin with,  both Searle and Polanyi agree that language is a semantic system, that is, a system of signs. They disagree, however, concerning the way in which signs function when they are actually experienced as signs, that is, when we encounter them and are actually referred by them to what they signal.

Now, the first thing to realize regarding signs is that being referred by a sign is not the same as observing and becoming aware of the characteristics of the thing that is a sign. In the case of written language this is obvious. If I have a book in a language I do not know, I see perfectly the signs on the page, but I do not experience them as signs , for  they do not refer me to what t hey mean.

Secondly, regarding the experience of signs as signs  Polanyi claims that when a sign functions as a sign, the sign is not itself observed or perceiv ved ass such,  for it signals “tacitly” or practically rather than explicitly or verbally . This is why, in the case of the experiment with shock-producing syllables, Polanyi claimed that the subject didn’t know –in the sense of observing or becoming explicitly aware of the syllables that produced shocks; consequently, he could not say which were those syllables. Actually, the subject ‘knew’ those syllables only in their referring function, only in his actually being referred by them to their shock-producing  virtue.

Thirdly, not only was the subject’s knowledge of the shock-producing syllables “tacit,” in that he was not able to say which were those syllables, but the very verbal identification of the syllables would have been  superfluous, according to Polanyi. The reason is that, contrary to Searle’s assumption, the syllables did not signify something to be understood, that is, the knowledge of the verbalizable fact that those syllables produced shocks; rather, they signaled to the subject the performance of an action– the action of protecting himself from the shock to come ..

Generalizing his misinterpretation of linguistic signs, Searle thinks that signs in general function as signs OF something to be understood and known by the hearer. For his part, Polanyi claims that all signs, linguistic signs included , are signs FOR performing an action – the action signaled, that is. In other words, signs are meant to terminate in an action on the subject’s part, not in what Searle calls understanding of something signaled.

Searle’s own farfetched example of the American soldier in Italy shows that –contrary to his own view–  this is indeed the case. For what the American soldier intended by saying “ Ich kome...,” was not that the Italians understand what the German sentence means, but that they perform the action of setting him free . In short, even in the case of linguistic enunciations, enunciations do not  mean as a sign OF something to be known by the hearer, namely what is enunciated, for the purpose of face-to-face ennciating is not making anything known (in the sense of understood), as Searle claims, but to elicit an action. In other words, according to  Polanyi, saying is a sign FOR performing an action.  Which is to say that, pace Searle, enunciations mean perlocutionarily, not illocutionarily. The only difference between enunciations and other perlocutionary acts is that enunciations do not specify the action meant (as, for instance, commands and requests do),  but leave its determination to the linguistic agent according to his desires or needs in the circumstances in which he experiences the sign as a sign. As to why this difference, the reason is that genuinely l inguistic agents, unlike animals, are not inescapably led by their needs or desires but are capable of deciding how to act.

Far from being what is signified by a red traffic light, the linguistic enunciation –if it occurs at all–  is only meaningful as a  practical direction or rule for the agent to follow in the performance of the conduct signified. This is obvious in the case of an order or of a request – that is, in the case of what Searle calls perlocutionary acts– , for an order is to be obeyed and a request is to be granted by performing the action requested, not by conceiving or enunciating or even assenting to the representational statement of the action to be obeyed or of the action requested.. And the same is the case with oral enunciations and statements. For, contrary to what Searle thinks, saying is not a sign OF what is said, and so it is not meant as a sign to conceive or imagine (as a copy of)  what is said, but a sign FOR linguistic action in response. Much as is the case with a music partition, the appropriate comprehension of which consists, and is manifested in, the actual performance of the musical piece. ^(F17)

After this long introduction, we are well prepared to understand Polanyhi’s own presentation of his views, by contrast t o Searle’s.

What, precisely, is interiorized here? The stick, in the sense that rather than being perceived itself as an object in the environmnet,  it is assimilated to the agent’s body to the point that it ceases to count  as an external object and functions, rather, as an extension of the agent’s body, much as is the case with eyeglasses . When worn by an agent, the eyeglasses are not seen or perceived themselves, but function as an extension of the eyes of the agent to guide him in his action. Similarly, the blind man’s stick. Think of an agent in total darkness. His situation is comparable to that of a blind person. Instinctively, this person, in order to find his way, extends his arms so as to touch the objects that are on his way before hitting them, thus being able to avoid them, not by seeing them, but  by using the sensations in his hands as instruments to guide his steps .towards the place he wants to reach.

“The identification of tacit knowing with indwelling involves a shift of  emphasis in our conception of tacit knowing. We had envisaged tacit knowing in the first place as a way to know more than we can tell. We identified the two terms of tacit knowing, the proximal and the distal, and recognized the way we attend from the first to the second, thus achieving an integration of  particulars to a coherent entity to which we are attending. Since we were not attending to the particulars in themselves, we could not identify them; but if we now regard the integration of particulars as an interiorization, it takes on a more positive character. It now becomes a means of making certain things function as the proximal terms of tacit knowing, so that instead of observing them in themselves, we may be aware of them in their bearing on the comprehensive entity which they constitute. It brings home to us that it is not by looking at things, but by dwelling in them, that we understand their joint meaning.. By concentrating attention on his fingers, a pianist can temporarily paralyze his movement. ^(F18)

In connection with internalization Polanyi uses such terms as “inhabiting” or “indwelling.” Now, “inhabiting” or “dwelling in” are different from “perceiving” or “knowing,” which refer to the object perceived or known.  Polanyi’s notion of tacit knowledge would have gained much in precision if, instead of speaking of the proximal term of  knowledge he had specified that “inhabiting”, “indwelling,” etc., which  appear always in relation to interiorization,  refer to instruments used by an agent, not to objects known. Thus, the stick in the hands of the blind person in which the blind person “indwells” to find his way is not what the person knows but the instrument he uses to guide his steps to the place he wants to reach. Similarly, the eyeglasses worn by a myopic person. are not what the person who wears them  knows or sees, but the instrument he uses to guide his action to achieve the purposes he desires.

But why does Polanyi use the word ‘interiorization’? Because a shift takes place, as it were, from something perceived in the subject’s environment to the agent’s body as instrument for action. As already indicated, this shift may be illustrated by the example of eyeglasses . When a pair of glasses rest on a table and I look at them, they do not function as eyeglasses but as object of knowledge, as object known. I see the eyeglasses. When I wear the eyeglasses, however, I do not see the eyeglasses, I “inhabit’ them, that is, I use them as instruments to find my way as an agent in the realization of my projects. The eyeglasses ar “interiorized” in the sense that they now function as an extension of my eyes, which I do not see but which I use as instruments for my action. My eyes only function as object known when I look at them in the mirror But those are not my eyes that see , but my eyes seen as any other observed object. When my eyes see they  function as the instruments I use to act efficiently in the realization of my projects. Polanyi calls interiorization this shift of an object  from object known in the environment of the agent to its functioning as an extension of the agent’s own body when actually used as an instrument. When worn, the eyeglasses are not known themselves; they are not seen, anymore than our eyes are seen when we use them as instruments to act in the world. ^(F19)

            When a blind person uses a stick to find his way to the place to which he wants to go, he is guided by the sensations he experiences in his hands every time that the stick hits an object. However,  these sensations are not known in themselves by the blind person; rather, they function as “ that from which ” the person finds his way towards the place he wants toreach . Thus, if the blind person is asked to what he pays attention, he won’t mention the sensations he experiences in his hand, for he has no objective awareness of them. Yet he is aware of them in the sense that he uses the sensations transmitted by the stick as instruments to find his way. He ”inhabits” them, or “dwells in” them, much as when we walk we use the sensations we experience on the plants of our feet at each step we make; we do not know them, rather we transcend them as “that from which” we make the next step. The blind person doesn’t know the sensations he experiences in the palm of his hand, he knows them only tacitly in that he uses them to make his next step.  He ‘inhabits” them as we inhabit our body when we use it as an instrument to realize our projects. Thus, when  we walk we don’t pay attention to the sensations we experience on the plants of our feet every time they touch the ground; we “dwell” in those sensations to make the next step, we pass through them as “that from which” we make the next step, much as right now I pass through the sensations I experience at the tips of my fingers every time I type on the keyboard toward the word I want to write.

Polanyi had initially explained TK as knowing more than one can say. His conceptual shift to the “interiorization” involved in TK helps him to become more precise regarding the nature of  TK. When playing the piano, the pianist is using it as an instrument to produce the desired music. In that case, the consciousness he has of the keys he touches is instrumental. If the word knowledge is to be employed in this context, it has to be described as “tacit,” for it is totally different from the consciousness of object with which the ordinary notion of knowledge (and saying-enunciating) is identified. So much so that, as just explained by Polanyi,  if, in the process of playing, that is, of having tacit knowledge of his fingers and of the keys they touch, the pianist turns his attention to his fingers and observes them, thereby turning them into objects of what we teake for knowledge, automatically his activity of playing becomes impaired. Similarly, if, while reading a text, we shift our attention from the meaning we are getting through reading to the letters that compose the text in order to know them as such, our reading becomes impaired. The reason for this is that while reading we only have a tacit awareness of the written signs, for we are not attending to them as such but using them as instruments to get their meaning. If we turn our attention to the signs, we gain what we ordinarlily take for knowledge of their characteristics, but they cease to function as instruments that convey a meaning. The two forms of consciousness are not only different from each other but incompatible with each other, which is the reason why we cannot get the meaning of a text if we try to know the letter themselves.  

To summarize, Polanyi calls “interiorization” the special, non-objective, quasi oblique consciousness we have of what we we actually use as an instrument to achieve a purpose through action. And he calls it “interiorization,” because we assimilate it to our own body, since the consciousness we have of our own body when we act to achieve a  purpose is itself instrumental and the ultimate model of tacit knowledge:

“But our awareness of our body for attending to things outside it suggests a wider generalization of the feeling we have of our body. Whenever we use certain things for attending from them to other things,  in the way in which we always use our own body, these things change their appearance. They appear to us now in terms of the entities to which we are attending from them, just as we feel our body in terms of the things outside to which we are attending from our body. In this sense we can say that when we make a thing function as the proximal term of tacit knowing, we incorporate it in our body or extend our body to include it –so that we come to dwell in it.” ^(F20)

The generalization of the awareness of our body at the time of action as the model of the interiorization involved in TK allows Polanyi to discover the mechanism of agency, which he exemplifies with  a large number of particular cases of ACTION. In the first place with the case of acquired bodily skills, such as playing the piano or typing, and in the case of those more complex skills such as driving a car.  These skills are fundamentally similar to the way a blind person ‘drives, ’so to say, his own body with the  help of a stick. These skills  are acquired as a result of along practice of interiorization; thanks to repetition, the agent transforms the stick, the typewriter, the piano, the car into extensions of his own body by using them as he uses his own body. At the end of that long practice of interiorization the use of those objects becomes as natural to the agent as the use of his own body in, say, the action of walking, which doesn’t require any other instrument than his own body. When a child learns to walk, he is guided by the sensations he experiences on the plants of his feet every time he makes a step; however, he does not focus his attention on them, he does not observe his  sensations, they are only “that from which ” he makes the subsequent steps towards the place he wants to reach..

The typewriter, the piano, the car are examples of material instruments – the technology invented and created by human agents to multiply the efficiency of their agency and extend its reach in order to satisfy first their elementary needs and then the desires that emerge as the transformation of the surrounding world allows the emergence of such desires. However, in addition to the invention of material instruments is the invention of symbolic (that is, semantic, signifying)  instruments , among which the most important are spoken and written language, because they make possible the invention of all other symbolic instruments . such as mathematical symbols, scientific theories, etc.

After the case of acquired bodily skills as examples of TK, Polanyi refers us to the use of   maps by an agent who, with their help, attempts to find his way in an unfamiliar territory. Before analyzing that case, let us remark that the reading and actual use of maps are far more complex operations than they seem to be to those who have already mastered them. Actually, a child, even a child who has already acquired the skill of reading written language, is not thereby automatically capable of using a map to find his way in unfamiliar territory, much less of drawing a map to be used by others. The reason for this difficulty is that a map is not a picture of a territory, any more than a name is a picture of the object named. A map is a symbolic SIGN of a territory, but not of a territory to be contemplated or observed but of a territory to be walked through. In other words, not only is a map to be deciphered or ‘read,’ as all symbols are, but to be deciphered in relation to the action of moving through the territory to which the map refers, in order to reach particular places within that territory. In other words, a map is a symbolic instrument to be used by an agent to achieve his goal. That is why, if somebody claimed to understand a map but proved himself incapable of using it in practice, he wouldn’t know what he is saying. Indeed, since the map is an instrument for action, understanding it amounts to being able to manifest that understanding in one’s action. The mistake of the person who claimed to understand a map while being unable to use it would be comparable to the one Searle makes when he claims that a person who is given an order understands it if only he grasps objectively the meaning of the words, without realizing that an order is to be obeyed, not to be understood objectively. In short, the genuine understanding of a map consists in the performance of the conduct that the map is meant to render possible, that is, in the conduct of reaching the desired place within the territory to which the map refers, as the genuine understanding of a verbal joke is to be manifested in the affection of being actually amused by it.

Polanyi’s view of the reading of maps of the kind we are referring to goes as follows. The agent who reads the map must “inhabit” the map as he inhabits his body when he acts to realize a project, for instance, when he is pounding a nail to hang a picture. In other words, he must make the nail function not as an object to be looked at and observed in itself, but as “that from which” he must act in order to arrive at the desired place, that is, as a SIGN FOR performing the action of pounding. In short,  TK is always practical, for it involves the use of an instrument an agent to achieve an intended goal, not a representation of object. ^(F21)

In other words, we tend to consider the statement in which, for instance, a physical law is expressed as a symbolic representation of the intelligible ‘nature’ of the particular phenomenon to which the law refers. For his part, Polanyi argues that such an interpretation is wrong. In his view, the statements of scientific laws are meant to be used by the scientific agent as instruments to guide his conduct in the solution of concrete problems related to the portion of the surrounding material world the law refers to, much as a map is meant to functions as an instrument for the person who uses it to find his way within the mapped territory.

In the following text,  Polanyi explains how theories function in the actual practice of science, that is, when the theoretical instrument is actually used rather than talked about:

“To rely on a theory for understanding nature is to interiorize it. For we are attending from the theory to things seen in its light, and we are aware  of the theory, while thus using it, in terms of the spectacle that it serves to explain. This is why mathematical theory can be learned only by practicing its application: its true knowledge lies in our ability to use it.” ^(F22)

This text, quoted  in isolation from its context, may be too dense and too cryptic to make us understand how, in Polanyi’s view, scientific theories function. What is clear, hoiwever,  is that Polanyi’s view contradicts the prevailing conception of science as an exact, or at least an increasingly approximative, representational  view of reality. Thomas Kuhn , who on this fundamental point  repeatedly indicates his agreement with Polanyi, expressed it forcefully:

“A scientific theory is usually felt to be better than its predecessors not only in the sense that it is a better instrument for discovering and solving  puzzles but also because it is somehow a better representation of what nature is really like. One often hears that successive theories grow ever closer to, or approximate more and more closely to, the truth. Apparently generalizations like that refer not to the puzzle solutions and concrete predictions derived from a theory but rather to its ontology, to the match, that is, between the entities with which the theory populates nature and what is ‘really there.’ Perhaps there is some other way of salvaging the notion of ‘truth’ for application to whole theories, but this one will not do.... the notion of a match between the ontology of a theory and its ‘real’ counter part in nature now seems to me illusive in principle” ^(F23)

What Kuhn is pointing out here is that the statements in which scientific theories are expressed are not meant to function as representations of reality but as symbolic instruments to be used by agents to solve practical problems. In this regard, though, scientific statements constitute no exception. Indeed, if Polanyi is right, no statement –whether scientific or other– is  meant to function as a representation OF its referent; rather, all statements are meant to function as instruments or signs  FOR action at the appropriate time.

Searle and Polanyi on language

We are now well equipped to make sense of the differences between Polanyi and Searle concerning language.  At the end of chapter one I expressed my dissatisfaction with central points of Searle’s philosophy of language. Let us now contrast the two views, to find out both how they differ from each other, and whether that contrast throws any light on the points concerning which Searle’s position proved unsatisfactory.

The above indicates Searle’s conception of knowledge. By replicating in his own mind what the speaker intends to communicate,  thanks to the hearer’s understanding of what the speaker says, the hearer gains the knowledge of what the speaker means or wants to communicate. Thus, according to Searle,  at least in the case of linguistic communication, knowledge consists in the correspondence between the object to be known and what the knower comes to have in mind thanks to his knowing activity. Such, needless to say, is the ordinary notion  of knowledge, which the Scholastics, following Aristotle, defined as adaequatio mentis ad rem : the conformation of the knower’s mind to the object. What Searle does is to extend to the field of linguistic communication the ordinary  notion of knowledge, that refers primarily to the conformity of the  knowing subject’s mind to the object in the perceptual world, that is, to “reality.” Now it is precisely regarding  this conception of knowledge that Polanyi parts ways with Searle. For Polanyi knowing is not a matter of becoming aware of the object to be known by acquiring a sort of reproduction of it that corresponds to the object, and which is true in the measure in which it corresponds to the object. Rather, knowledge is tacit because it is essentially practical, not theoretical. It does not aim at reproducing the object in the mind, in which case the knowledge of the object would be explicit rather than tacit. Knowledge consists, rather,  in a practical  disposition to ACT in the way appropriate to achieve the knower’s goal. Thus, in the case of the shock-producing syllables, the subject has no awareness of the individual characteristics of the syllables; for that very reason the subject cannot say or express in words which are those characteristics of which he has no visual awareness. What the subject acquires, thanks to his past painful experience of being shocked, is not an image of the syllables, but the practical disposition to avoid the shock, which manifests itself in his bracing himself for the shock when the shock-producing syllables appear again on the screen, in order to protect himself as much as possible from the shock.

Whose conception of the relationship of knowledge to action is right: Searle’s or Polanayi’s?

Regarding those acts in which a perlocutionary effect is intended by the speaker, I have already shown how unpersuasive I find Searle’s view that in every act of communication the speaker intends to produce the knowledge of his communicative intentions –that is, the illocutionary effect–  and that this knowledge suffices to insure the success of the communicative act. For this means, concretely, that when I give you an order, what I intend primarily is that you understand and thereby be informed or acquire factual knowledge that I give you an order, not that you obey my order. Searle is so convinced that in the case of an order what the speaker intends is that his order be understood rather than obeyed that he considers the communicative exchange successful in the case in which the order is not obeyed as long as the order is understood by the hearer.

Why do I think that Searle is wrong on this point? Because this aspect of Searle’s characterization of linguistic exchanges is  at odds with the practice of all competent linguistic agents. It is true that I cannot disobey an order without understanding it and thereby knowing it. But what is in contention is what constitutes the appropriate “understanding” of an order and, in general, of all those linguistic acts that possess perlocutionary force, such as threats, warnings, promises, demands, etc. According to Searle, the primary intention of the speaker in all of  them is to inform the hearer of what he means, and only secondarily and accidentally to achieve the perlocutionary effect typical of the particular perlocutionary act, such as obeying it in the case of an order, expecting the fulfillment of the promise in the case of promising, etc.

But the linguistic facts contradict Searle’s opinion. Indeed, Searle’s opinion does not do justice to the practice of linguistic agents concerning the use of the indicative mood, on the one hand, and of the subjunctive and of the imperative moods, on the other. If  Searle were right, there would be no difference in actual use between the grammatical imperative, used in the case of orders, and the grammatical indicative, properly used only when one wants to inform of a fact. Furthermore, the imperative mood  would be totally superfluous,  for imperatives should be understood as  indicatives. Indeed,  if Searle were right, giving an order (in the imperative) would be exactly equivalent to saying or enunciating (in the indicative) that one gives an order. But such is not the case. As I have already argued, when I give an order, I do not say – in the sense of enunciating– something to be merely understood, to wit: that I give an order; rather, I say the order I give in the sense of giving an order to be obeyed. When I say something in the sense of enunciating, I refer my hearer to what I enunciate as something to be understood, that is, I intend to produce in my interlocutor the knowledge in which, according to Searle, the illocutionary effect consists. Therefore, if in giving an order I were enunciating that I give an order and the order I give, I should be satisfied if the addressee responded  “I understand your order.” But that is not the case. When I give an order –and this is why I use the imperative, not the indicative–  I expect and intend my order to be obeyed, which is the appropriate ‘understanding’ of an order by a competent linguistic agent.

Moreover, Searle’s opinion is also at odds with the practice of linguistic agents even in the case of statements. For, as I have already argued, contrary to what Searle thinks, in a real-life situations, by contrast to the artificial classroom situation in which people are learning a second language, linguistic agents do not consider their linguistic action successful if a purely theoretical  understanding of what is said obtains; success, for linguistic agents, is identical with linguistic action. Cf. GEOMETRY AS APPLICATIVE.           

Why does Searle identify saying with enunciating?  Because his linguistic characterizations and, hence, his entire conception of language,  is based not on his own experience as a linguistic agent who actually participates in communicative interactions, but as a curious philosopher who overhears the words said by those who participate in communicative interactions, or who REFLECTS after the fact on the words said by himself and others when he participated in communicative interactions. I underline “the words said,” to indicate that Searle’s characterization of language, in spite of his disclaimers, focuses on the words said, independently of the conduct and the intentions of those who say or hear them as actual participants in linguistic exchanges. This amounts to identifying linguistic meaning with saying in the sense of enunciating something, and, consequently, it amounts to considering the conduct of the linguistic agents as accidental to linguistic meaning, rather than integral to, or AT at least partially constitutive of, linguistic meaning. Clearly, however, this is not the kind of interiorization through agency through which children learn to play baseball, without being able to enunciate the rules they supposedly follow.

Thus Searle excludes from linguistic meaning anything that in a linguistic exchange is not  words in which something is said or enunciated as to be known. Such is the case with  the actions and affections intended by the speaker in the communicative exchange, which consequently are excluded by Searle from being constitutive elements of linguistic meaning. That, indeed, was the reason why, in his example of the American soldier captured by the Italians, Searle explicitly denied that the action by which the American tricked the Italians into believing that he was a German soldier by uttering the sounds “ Ich komme von dem Land wo die Zitronen  blühen ” was a linguistic action, since the words expressed did not enunciate the action intended.

The exclusion from linguistic meaning of whatever is not explicitly said in words is reflected in the separation Searle postulates between the illocutionary and the perlocutionary effects, as well as in the secondary and merely accidental role he attributes to the latter. Searle himself , in explaining his principle of expressibility that  “whatever can be meant can be said,” implicitly acknowledges that whatever is not said in words cannot be meant, for he excludes from meaning the perlocutionary effects. ^(F24)

In short, because he identifies linguistic meaning with what is expressed in words that supposedly say or enunciate something to be understood or known, Searle automatically excludes from linguistic meaning those no less linguistic effects that do not consist in words that express something to be understood, namely, the perlocutionary effects. No wonder, then, that  the way in which language functions in the artificial context of a course of a language for foreigners, in which only understanding  what is said counts as linguistic, to the systematic exclusion of any of the affections or actions intended by the speaker, ends up appearing as the model linguistic situation for Searle. Indeed it corresponds to Searle’s defective philosophy of language.

Now, to understand the differences between Searle and Polanyi, we have to realize that Searle’s linguistic philosophy amounts to an application to the field of language of the Platonic-Aristotelian epistemology inherited by the Western philosophical tradition. In that tradition,  knowledge consists, on the subject’s part,  in the representational conformity of his mind to reality, and, on the object’s part, in the ontological correspondence or conformity to what is in the subject’s mind. By contrast,  Polanyi’s conception of tacit knowledge constitutes an alternative to that tradition, for tacit knowledge is to be used instrumentally by the agent to achieve a desired goal, not a representation of what is the case.

As we shall now see, Polanyi does not contend that the Searlean perlocutionary effects as Searle conceives them, that is, as separated from the illocutionary effect, are a constitutive element of linguistic meaning, for the Serlean conception of the illocutionary element as different from the perlocutionary presupposes the legitimacy of his conceptual distinction between them. Polanyi contends, rather, that this Serlean distinction reflects the separation between the knowing subject and the agent that is inherent to the epistemology inherited from Aristotle and Plato that begins by reflecting on knowledge as different from and separable form action. The Searlean distinction between illocutionary and perlocutionary effects is possible only on the wrong assumption , common to Aristotle and Plato, that knowledge and action stand to each other in the relationship presupposed in the epistemology inherited from Plato and Aristotle by the Western philosophical tradition.
