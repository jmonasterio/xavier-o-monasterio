\wp\chantill\oct92-1 October 25, 1992

MacIntyre and Aquinas' Synthesis

In Three Rival versions of Moral Enquiry MacIntyre makes two fundamental claims. ^(E1) One, he defines, and affirms the viability of, Aquinas' synthesis of the Augustinian and the Aristotelian traditions. Two, he affirms the demonstrable superiority of the tradition of moral enquiry to which Aquinas gave final shape over the encyclopaedic and the genealogical traditions.

While I find many of MacIntyre's analyses profound and enlightening and, being a Catholic myself, I resonate sympathetically to his opposition to both the encyclopaedic and the genealogical traditions, I am uncomfortable with the substantive claims he makes and to which I just alluded. My purpose here is to clarify the reasons for my discomfort. In order, however, not to disperse myself in an endless task, I will zero in on my more radical disagreements with MacIntyre's position.

System or history?

What was "theology" in the Augustinian tradition, before the irruption of the Aristotelian corpus brought about a definite distinction betwen theology and philosophy as academic disciplines? It was the increasing explicitation of the self-understanding of the Catholic faithful by the faithful for the practical purpose of living ever more intensely as faithful. To this lived, rather than explicitly stated, conception of theology corresponed a particular way of reading the sacred Scripture, aptly characterized by MacIntyre as follows:

"In medieval Augustinian culture the relationship between the key texts of that culture and their reader was twofold. The reader was assigned the task of interpreting the text, but also had to discover, in and through his or her reading of those texts, that they in turn interpret the reader. What the reader, as thus interpreted by the texts, has to to learn about him or herself is that it is only the self as transformed through and by the reading of the texts which will be capable or reading the texts aright. So the reader, like any learner within a craft tradition, encounters apparent paradox at the outset: ... only by learning what the texts have to teach can he or she come to read those texts aright, but also... only by reading them aright can he or she learn what the texts have to teach.

"The person in this predicament requires two things: a teacher and an obedient trust that what the teacher, interpreting the text, declares to be good reasons for transforming oneself into a different kind of person... will turn out to be genuinely good reasons in the light afforded by that understanding of the texts which becomes available only to the transformed self. The intending reader has to have inculcated into him or herself certain attitudes and dispositions, certain virtues, before he or she can know why these are to be accounted virtues. So a prerational reordering of the self has to occur before the reader can have an adequate standard by which to judge what is a good reason and what is not. And this reordering requires obedient trust... in the [authority] of the whole tradition of interpretive commentary into which that teacher had had earlier him or herself to be initiated through his or her reordering and conversion.

"The key texts were of course those of sacred Scripture... The reader in his or her own life enacts and reenacts that of which he or she reads in Scripture... So the reading of texts is part of the history of which the same texts speak. The reader thus discovers him or herself inside the Scriptures." ^(E2)

What we have here is a community of Christians who, as the preceding generations of Christians, recognize the Scripture as sacred , who by the same token are addressed by the Scripture as involved in the history narrated by the Scripture, and who, for the practical purpose of living as Christians, interpret the Scripture from their own present and in the particular world in which they continue the history narrated by the Scripture. This is what theology was for them, fundamentally.

Now, according to MacIntyre, Aquinas managed to combine  the Augustinian tradition of moral enquiry with the Aristotelian tradition of moral enquiry in a harmonious and superior synthesis

which overcame the deficiencies of both while conserving the strengths of both. Now, though there are many different ways in which the tradition shaped by Aquinas is characterized by MacIntyre, for my present purposes one characterization is particularly relevant :

"For anyone whose point of view is defined in terms of some synthesis of the Aristotelian and Augustinian traditions the concept of personal identity must have three central dimensions:... part of being one and the same person throughout this bodily life is having one and the same body. Secondly... part of being one and the same person throughout this bodily life is being continuously liable to account for my actions, attitudes, and beliefs to others within my communities.

"Thirdly, because my life is to be understood as a teleologically ordered unity, a whole the nature of which and the good of which I have to discover, my life has the continuity and unity of a quest, a quest whose object is to discover that truth about my life as a whole which is an indispensable prt of the good of that life. So on this view my life has the unity of a story with a beginning, a middld, and an end, beginning with birth and endiding, so far as concerns the final judgment to be passed on it--in respect of the achievement of my good--with death." ^(E3)

Nwo, this "complex and metaphysical account of the identity and continuity of human beings" is common to all those "whose point of view is defined in terms of some synthesis of the Aristotelian and Augustinian traditions." MacIntyre takes care of clarifying that "although this is a Thomistic account.., it is not an account invented primarily by philosophical theorists," for "this is how the identity and continuity of human lives are or were understood in a great many, perhaps in all traditional societies... This conception of personal identity and continuity was then embodied in practice long before it was articulated as a theory." ^(E4) In addition, MacIntyre remarks that

"it is important to notice that this complex metaphysical conception is not proposed as a solution to what philosophers have thought of as 'the problem of personal identity,' a problem to which rival answers have been given from Locke and Butler to Flew and Parfit. That problem is only generated, and perhaps it can only be generated, in the aftermath of some tradition, when the shared beliefs which formerly underpinned the complex metaphysical conception of personal identity and continuity, such beliefs as that in life-long accountability and in the teleological ordering of each life, are no longer widely held, but there still remains as part of the common stock of shared concepts some residual conception of a personal identity which is more and other than that of either bodily continuity or psychological continuity, such as memory. The concept of same person , detached as it has been from the context of belief in which it was at home, then poses a problem with rival solutions... From the traditional point of view this dissolution of a whole into its parts is precisely what should be expected in a society in which the background beliefs which made it possible to identify and understand that whole are no longer shared." ^(E5)

What is the interest of all these quotations? To put it squarely, I believe that my discomfort with MacIntyre's position stems, at least in part, from the fact that he confuses "traditions" with "traditions of enquiry," with damnable results, and I think that these quotations will help me to start rendering my point intelligible.

A first remark. In the very same paragraph, MacIntyre speaks of "this complex and metaphysical account of the indentity and continuity of human beings," and tells us that "this is how the identity and continuity of human lives are or were understood in a great many, perhaps in all traditional societies," and that "this conception of personal identity and continuity was... embodied in practice long before it was articulated as a theory."

What are we supposed to make of this? If it is a complex and metaphysical account, it is rather unlikely that this is how the identity and continuity of human lives are understood perhaps in all traditional societies. Yet I think that MacIntyre is right in saying that in all [traditional] societies the identity and continuity of human lives is so understood. But, then, it is not a complex and metaphysical account. What is a complex metaphysical account is what philosophers look for when they attempt to solve "the problem of personal identity," that is, to explain the conditions that make possible the identity, continuity and accountability of people and to prove that such conditions do or do not actually obtain. But the people who hold themselves and are held responsible by others are not trying to do what philosophers do, nor do they cease holding themselves and being held responsible by others because philosophers fail to solve the problem. For, as MacIntyre himself says, this conception of personal identity is embodied in the very practice of people who hold themselves and are held responsible for others, and their practice is not a theory, much less a complex metaphysical theory. However, the fact that MacIntyre speaks of this practice a complex metaphysical conception leads me to think that he confuses what people do with what philosophers do.

What is what people do? They hold themselves and are held by others accountable for their actions. This means two things. First, that their actions, insofar as they are intentional, possess a practical intelligibility so that, if necessary, the agents can make them intelligible to those who do not understand them. Second, that their actions, insofar as they are affect others, have to be justified as to the way in which they affect others, so that the agents themselves ought to consider them justified when they act, and should be capable of justifying them before others, if necessary, or bear the consequences.

Let us consider the first aspect--the practical intelligibility of actions. "Why did you go to the store? Because I needed food." One acts to realize a purpose. Accounting for an action, in this sense, amounts to rendering intelligible to others either the purpose embodied in the action, or the appropriateness of the action for the realization of its purpose. Being a human agent is the same as being an agent whose actions embody purposes to be realized and which therefore have to be appropriate to the realization of those purposes. "Why did you go to the store? Because I needed to sleep." Since there is no evident appropriateness to the action of going to the store for the purpose of sleeping, this account is unsatisfactory. And if the agent is incapable of satisfying him or herself and others as to the appropriateness of going to the store for the purpose of sleeping, he or she fails as a human agent.

Notice, however, that actions are singular and that they take place in singular circumstances. Consequently, the account for each action is singular too. And only those who share with the agent a familiarity with the circumstances--material and cultural--in which actions take place can understand the agent's account. Memebers of a culture without foodstores cannot understand going to a foodstore to buy food. We do not understand the Hengewood ruins for we do not know the purpose of the builders and how appropriate their actions were for the realization of that purpose. Notice, too, that if one does not share with the agents, at least imaginarily, the practical world which the agents inhabit no account of their actions can be satisfactory, for both their purposes and the appropriateness for their realization of the actions that embody those purposes are linked to the character of that practical world. Thus, all accounts of actions meant to render actions intelligible implicitly refer to the world inhabited by the agents as their field of action. And this is why, in their accounts, agents unavoidably refer to the world they inhabit, in terms of which their concrete purposes and actions are intelligible.

Now, let us contrast with this the accounts or explanations of philosophers about, say, personal identity. Whatever they might mean by personal identity, which is what they want to account for, it certainly is not an action. Should we say, then, that they are speaking about the agent as author or source of all of his or her actions, past, present and future? If so, it would seem that they are talking about the same agent who is called to account for his or her actions in every society, that is, the agents to whom I referred in my previous examples. But in fact it is not so. For the explanations or accounts alluded to above are of a totally different structure from those of philosophers. Indeed, the latter do not refer to the world inhabited by the agent which renders intelligible the agent's purposes and the appropriateness of the actions that embody his or her purposes. What philosophers want to render intelligible, that is, account for, is not the agent's actions--which is what people demand when they demand and give accounts for actions--but something very different, namely, why people hold themselves responsible for their past, present and future actions.

That people do that is both unquestionable and unquestioned, for otherwise philosophers could not question why people do that.

Now, philosophers question this because they find it questionable, that is, because they have reasons not to consider people responsible for their actions. Which reasons? Well, some philosophers do not see how a bodily behavior can be connected to a spiritual mind and, therefore, attributable to it. Others do not see that there is a mind behind the behavior which can be held responsible for the behavior. Others do not see how a mind seated in a body whose permanence across time is doubtful can be permanent itself and held responsible for its past actions. Others do not see how a person can be held responsible for a past action, since what is past does not exist any longer, etc., etc. Now, obviously, insofar as the philosopher as philosopher identifies himself, say, with a mind seated in an impermanent body and sees the permanence of the body as the condition for attributing himself responsibility for his past actions, he cannot assent to the statement made by others that he is responsible for his past actions and cannot himself judge that he is responsible for them. Yet, this very philosopher will hold the rapist of his daughter responsible for having raped his daughter and will himself be held responsible for raping somebody's daughter. What, then, is going on?

I think that what is going on is that because the philosopher uses the same word "agent" that people use when they speak of the human being, he thinks that he is speaking of what people do when they demand and give accounts for actions, when in fact the philosopher is speaking of something totally different. The agent of which people speak and whom they hold responsible cannot be understood or held responsible but as a participant in a field of action in which he is irreversibly affected by what happens around him or her and he affects irreversibly through his actions those who are around him and share with him or her that field of action. About this structure of common participation in a field of action the agent has no choice, for such is the nature of things. Being born is being born into a world of that structure. We are affecting and affected participants in a shared field of action. Because we are not self-subsistent and self-sufficient, because we have needs and desires that can only be satisfied by the surrounding world, we have goals. Because we are capable, within limits, of choosing the goals we pursue and the means by which we pursue them, our actions are intelligible to ourselves and can be made intelligible to others in the measure that we share with them the same cultural world. Because our actions affect those with whom we share our field of action, in the measure in which our actions depend on us and their effects are foreseeable, we hold ourselves responsible for the way they affect others and hold others responsible for the way in which

theirs affect us and others.  

What the philosopher does, though, is to abstract himself or herself from his or her participation in such a world, to abstract what he or she calls the "agent" from his or her participation in such a world, and then to try to render intelligible to himself or herself how such an "agent" can be held and hold himself or herself responsible for his or her actions. Now, obviously, no such "agent" can intelligibly be held responsible.
