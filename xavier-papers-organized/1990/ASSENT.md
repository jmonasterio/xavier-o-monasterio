                                    "The   poet   nothing   affirmeth,   and
                     therefore      never      lieth."      (Sir      Philip
      Sidney) (My entry II, 55).

                                    "Rather,   they   [the   historian   and
                     the      poet]      differ      in      this,      that
while the  former  speaks  of                                incidents  that
have  come  to  be,  the                                latter   speaks   of
incidents that                                might  come  to  be.  On  this
account                                 making is both more philosophic  and
                                    more   worthy    than    history,    for
                      making      speaks      more      of       universals,
              while         history         speaks          more          of
particulars."  (Aristotle.  See  my                               entry  II,
62).

      But (to continue the chapter on "Consent") there is another  important
point to be considered. The biblical author of Genesis  wrote  a  narrative,
not a  series  of  statements  about  God  or  about  Abraham,  whereas  the
medievals interpreted or understood or read the narrative  as  a  series  of
statements. I turn now to this second monumental mistake of the  medievals',
the mistake so forcefully denounced, but insufficiently tracked back to  its
medieval origins, by Hans W. Frei.
       The author of a novel does not lie when she writes  "John  was  still
in bed when he heard someone knocking at the door." The reason she does  not
lie is not  that  the  whole  thing  is  imaginary  rather  than  real  and,
therefore, that it never happened; it is, rather, that the  novelist  cannot
lie, for she is not  affirming  or  denying  anything,  she  is  not  making
statements at all. The author  is  narrating.  Of  course,  a  narrator  can
narrate something imaginary or something real or that she  takes  for  real.
But, by the very nature of the  narrative  genre,  a  narrator  narrates,  a
narrator does not make statements.      What Julius A. Elias  wrote  in  his
Defence of Plato will be pertinent here:

           But I shall also advance a 'strong' defence. This will trace  in
           Plato, to the extent that it can  be  found,  an  acknowledgment
           that dialectic cannot attain  its  goals  by  demonstrating  the
           truth of its presmises. If that is so, then those first premises
           are  myths.  The  irony  of  Santayana's  remark  ("Lying  is  a
           privilege of the poets because they have  not  yet  reached  the
           level on which truth and error are discernible")... is  that  he
           intended no irony, but  Plato  does  when  he  calls  his  myths
           "lies." The axioms of a system of  thought  are  myths  in  this
           sense, and if the strong  defence  can  be  made  plausible  the
           autonomy and integrity of poetry will be vindicated.  (My  entry
           II, 25).
      What MacIntyre says in "The Religious Significance  of  Atheism"  will
also be enlightening:
      In the 19th century Robertson Smith  was  accused  of  heresy  by  his
      Scottish  Presbiterian  ministerial  peers  because  he  insisted   on
      investigating the Old Testament from  an  anthropological  standpoint.
      What worried his colleagues was something  that  they  themselves  did
      not, and perhaps could not, make wholly  explicit,  that  is,  Smith's
      tendency to see beliefs in a social context and to ask  not  Are  they
      true? but Why should men in that sort of society  hold  that  sort  of
      belief? We experience little discomfort when we see the beliefs of the
      Nuer or the Azande people's in this way, since nothing is at stake for
      us in the truth or error of their beliefs. But when we  focus  on  our
      won beliefs we find it  difficult  to  go  on  asking  both  questions
      simultaneously. I have tried to view the debate between  atheists  and
      theists as if I were an anthoropologist from another culture. (See  my
      entry II, 33).
      Let us start with Plato as  refferred  to  by  Elias.  In  his  "weak"
defence of Plato Elias argues that Plato used myths  (that  is,  narratives)
compatible with the truth "to render it [the truth] accessible at  least  at
the emotional level and within the  limited  understanding  of  his  various
hearers," because most people cannot  understand  the  truth  of  which  the
philosopher-kings have absolute knowledge.  Plato's  myths,  therefore,  are
useful lies. Lies, because they degrade or misrepresent the  truth;  useful,
because they nonetheless give some sense of the truth to  those  who  cannot
grasp it in all its glory. In his "strong" defence,  however,  Elias'  claim
is very different: Plato's myths are not lies at all, in any sense, for  the
axioms  of  every  system  of  thought  are  myths,  that   is,   they   are
undemonstrable but can be grasped and articulated through  narratives.  This
renders "the autonomy and integrity of poetry vindicated."
      The weak defence, if correct, assumes  that  the  appropriate  way  of
expressing a truth is a statement. But  when  the  statement  would  not  be
understood by people of inferior hability,  it  can  be  translated  into  a
narrative (a "myth"). The translation is always  poor  (in  this  sense,  "a
lie"), but it gives the  hearers  at  least  a  dim  glimpse  of  the  truth
(therefore they gain a  sort  of  degraded  'knowledge')  that  moves  their
emotions.
      The strong defence, by contrast, assumes that the appropriate  way  to
express certain truths, namely, the fundamental truths of  a  system,  which
are axiomatic and undemonstrable,  is  not  a  statement  but  a  narrative.
Therefore, Plato's myths, or those at  least  of  his  myths  which  express
fundamental truths of  his  system,  are  not  lies  in  any  way.  This  is
different from Aristotle's  position  in  his  metaphysics.  Aristotle  also
acknowledged that the first principles  are  axiomatic  and  undemonstrable;
however, he deemed that the appropriate way of expressing  those  truths  as
not a narrative but a statement. It seems likely,  however,  that  Aristotle
thought that concerning human affairs, which are  true  only  for  the  most
part, the appropriate evidence to support a  general  statement  about  them
was a narrative example  or  examples  rather  than  a  static  experiential
presentation or a mere analysis of the terms involved.
      A few observations on the above. (1) Both  Plato  and  Aristotle  were
concerned with universal truths,  primarily.  They  wanted  "episteme."  (2)
Both assumed that truth was a matter of pure understanding and assenting  to
the understood, that is, of "judging." Pure contemplator from  nowhere.  (3)
Both came to acknowledge,  although  in  different  regards,  the  need  for
narrative, but only in the context of argument (silent with self, or  verbal
with others) in view of assent. "Consent" is alien to  their  views,  though
perhaps the persuasive arguments of which he  speaks  in  the  Rhetoric  are
meant to elcit consent in absence.
      What do I get from this reflection on Plato and  Aristotle  concerning
the issue of narrative, which is my subject here?
What interested me initially was the point of the  lie  of  the  poets.  For
lying is the deed of a stater, and I  am  working  on  the  assumption  that
narrating  is  not  stating.  Furthermore,  this  point  interested  me   in
connection with the medievals, for I think they misunderstood the  truth  of
the Bible as the truth of  statements.  This  reflection  on  Aristotle  and
Plato helps me to sharpen my point. Consent  is  incarnated  in  intentional
action, not  in theoretical judgments. Consent as  well  as  the  action  in
which it is incarnated are appropriate or inappropriate, not true or  false,
for tough they may ground future judgments about the agent  or  its  object,
they are not judgments. The form that the understanding by the witness of  a
consent-action takes is an inchoate narrative, not  a  statement.  The  form
that the reflective understanding of  a  witness  takes  is  a  full-fledged
narrative, not a statement.  The  imaginary  witness  who  becomes  narrator
expresses his understanding in a narrative, not in a series of statements.
All question of truth is impertinent in the  case  of  this  narrative,  for
such a narrative expresses just imagined action  by  an  imaginary  witness.
The voice of the narrator is the voice of an imaginary witness, where  there
is no possible gap or discrepancy between the imagined and the  narrated  or
between  the  intention  of  narrating  and  the  act  of  narrating.   More
interesting for our purposes is  the  case  of  the  real  witness.  If  his
narrative expresses his understanding of sombebody  elkse's  consent-action,
the question of truth seems appropriate, for his  understanding  may  be  at
odds with the dramatic events that actually took place,  or  at  least  with
the reflective understanding  of  the  involved  agent  based  on  his  pre-
reflective understaning (which differs  with  the  inchoate,  pre-reflective
narrative of the witness), or with the  reflective  understanding  of  other
witnesses. But it is important to  realize  that  this  understanding,  like
that of the imaginary  narrator,  takes  pre-reflectively  the  form  of  an
inchoate narrative, not of a statement, and reflectively the form of a full-
fledged narrative, not of a statement. So I may have been wrong in  assuming
that because narratives are not statements they cannot  be  either  true  or
false. Was I?
      Let  us  notice,  first,  that  if  narratives  express  the  witness'
understanding, this understanding is not  primarily  of  his  understanding,
still less of his reflective agreement with his own  understanding,  but  of
the agent's  consent.  True,  we  can  hear  the  narrator's  voice  in  his
narrative, but his voice narrates the agent's consent, not his own voice  or
his own understanding or  his  own  assent  to  his  understanding.  And  it
narrates rather than describes or states because its 'object'  is  dramatic,
a  consent-action,   which   can   only   be   understood   pre-reflectively
dramatically, in an inchoate narrative, and reflectively in  a  full-fledged
narrative. What is understood can  be  questioned  as  understood,  but  the
agent's  consent  is  not  itself  a  merely  understood.  The  captain  who
evacuated his ship consented to the fire in evacuating  his  ship.  This  is
what is narratively understood and narrated by the narrator.  And  this  can
be questioned as narrated, but not as lived. What do I mean?
      The agent's questioning is not suspension of  assent  but  of  consent
and is meant to terminate in a consent-action, not in a  mere  understanding
or in a judgment. It is in this sense, it seems to me, that I was  right  in
saying that narratives are neither true nor false.

      My interest is in the contrast of these two kinds  of  truths  and  in
the relationship of undemonstrable truths to narrative. And  I  will  return
to this point. But first I have to  clear  the  ground.  For  I  started  by
suggesting that narratives are  neither  true  nor  false,  and  here  I  am
already involved in the relationship between narrative and truth. I have  to
backtrack. In what recognizable sense, then,  are  narratives  beyond  truth
and falsity?
      A narrative narrates a dramatic  event  lived  by  somobody.  But  the
person who lives the event does not narrate the event while living  it;  she
lives  it,  which  is  very  different.  She  lives  it  with  an   inchoate
understanding of the event, but she does not narrate it by lving it.  It  is
not the same to live and to write one's autobiography. The  latter,  by  its
very nature, comes later. And is of a totally different kind.  Living  takes
place within the sphere of exposure, where the agent changes the  world  and
is changed by it. When the agent narrates  after  the  event,  he  takes  in
relation to himself the witness's position, which was not his  own  when  he
acted. He stands as a "he" to himself, whereas during the  action  he  stood
as exposed to the  surrounding  world.  A  narrative  narrates  the  exposed
agent's action-passion, but is not itself in the sphere of exposure.
      Now, whatever the legitimacy  of  the  question  of  truth  concerning
narratives, my fist point is that the question of truth  is  impertinent  in
the case of actual exposure,  for  there  is  nothing  on  that  level  that
demands or  is  liable  of  assent  or  dissent.  My  point  is  similar  to
Aristotle's and Aquinas's who consider perception  to  be  beyond  or  below
truth and falsity and reserve this question for  judgment,  where  there  is
assent or dissent involved. My only disagreement with them on this point  is
that they interpret perception as contemplation,  while  I  think  that  the
agent's perception is dramatic, of the order of exposure, and  is  therefore
unintelligible without reference to action, for it is a "moment" of action.
      Does this imply that an agent or his  action  cannot  be  called  into
question? They cannot be questioned as to  their  truth,  for  there  is  no
claim of truth involved, nothing that involves or demands assent.  But  they
can be questioned as to their appropriateness. But this questioning  amounts
to suspension of consent--not of assent--by another exposed agent, or  by  a
possible agent. The captain of the British ship consented  to  the  fire  as
unextinguishable. His judges ended up condemning  his  consent  (of  course,
they had time; the captain didn't!). But this amounts to  saying  that  they
would have consented to the ship as salvageable. In  other  words,  only  on
the basis of one's consent can somebody else's consent be criticized,  since
in the sphere of exposure inaction is a form of action. And this consent  by
another possible agent can itself be criticized, but only on  the  basis  of
another consent. Which is to say that at any given point the basis  is  pre-
reflective consent, not assent.
      Assent refers to what demands assent, not  to  what  demands  consent.
The realities I  encounter  as  an  agent  demand  practical  response,  not
assent, for they do not confront me as stating  anything  about  themselves;
they confront me by demanding a response. Confronted with fire I consent  to
fleeing and, implicitly, to the threatening reality  of  the  fire  I  flee.
Descartes would have flown away from the fire as much as I do, for  all  his
suspension of assent to (the statement or judgment of) the  reality  of  the
fire. As agents we both would have  been  in  agreement,  in  the  sense  of
responding similarly and, on reflection, of judging the  action  of  fleeing
as appropriate. Nonetheless, Descartes would have argued that assent is  the
foundation of consent, whereas I just maintained that consent is  the  basic
unit and can only  be  questioned  by  someone  who  in  his  turn  consents
otherwise.
      To move further, let us turn our attention to assent. We already  know
that agreement is the co-consent of two or more agents or  potential  agents
as to the appropriateness of an action in response to something. The  judges
of the British captain
disagreed with him.  This  means  that,  as  potential  agents,  they  "saw"
(grasped, felt themselves exposed to) the fire as extinguishable and to  the
ship as salvageable. If the captain, though, had stated that "The  ship  was
not salvageable," the judges would  have  not  assented  to  the  statement.
Notice, however, that this assent is still assent about object for  response
and assent by potential agents. It still is on the basis of another  consent
which would ground, potentially, a different kind of response  to  the  fire
under the circumstances. But this is not the "assent"  of  which  Qristotle,
the Scholastics, and Descartes talk. What is different?
      Consent is intentional-practical response to  reality,  to  object  of
exposure, to object for response. It is on the basis of such a consent  that
the assent of which I was speaking in the preceding paragraph  is  possible.
One cannot assent but on  the  basis  of  a  consent.  But  the  Scholastics
suppress the axis of practical reference  in  perception.  In  other  words,
they transform the object for response  into  object  for  contemplation  in
view of assent. Not only do they filtrate from the experience of object  all
its  affective-moving  elements,  not  only  do  they  cut   the   practical
connection between the agent and the encountered object, but  they  identify
such an object for contemplation with reality  (that  is,  with  the  object
encountered by the agent), and such a purely contemplating subject with  the
knower.
In that case, of course, assent ought to precede consent, for consent  ought
to be grounded on assent in order  to  participate  in  the  rationality  of
assent.
