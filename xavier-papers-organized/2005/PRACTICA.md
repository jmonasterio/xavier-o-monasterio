PRACTICAL SYLLOGISM

"Desire never has a purely abstract object"

(Nussbaum Aris's De Motu Animalium, p. 265).

" And he is also right to make it the job of

MY COMMENTS

Many pages of Nussbaum's Essay remain to be explored. But from the chunk already explored certain points begin to emerge.

(1) One thing is to act, another to explain action. The agent acts, the inquirer explains. Explanation is essentially linguistic; action is not. As stating that somebody acts is not acting but stating, so explaining somebody's action is not acting but explaining. Furthermore, action is always particular; explanation may be of the particular (of this action) and of the general (like the explanation of action as such that Aris gives in De Motu Animalium). My question is: do the continuous shifts from one to the other involve an illegitimate confusion?

- The agent goes through the process we call action, constituted by three moments. Action is the whole process, not only the motion . But the agent does not deduce motion from desire and belief. Nor does motion toward the realization of the goal follow, for the agent, with the necessity of a conclusion. Nor does the agent need to have the notion of action as consitituted by the three moments in order to act. The agent does not move because he grasps the necessity, whereas the deductor deduces because he grasps the necessity. Thus, as far as the agent is concerned, there is no logical necessity, the motion does not follow as a conclusion.

Moreover, Aris himself admits preventing factors; thus, the motion is contingent on the absence or presence of preventing factors. In addition, Aris himself allows for akrasía. Does akrasía involve insincerity on the agent's part? Not according to Aris.

- Aris identifies the process of deliberation on the agent's part with the process of deliberation as he understands it as a contemplator who wants to explain action. Let us call Aris "A" and the agent "B". B is deliberating whether in this particular case--for deliberation is always particular--he will constitute as his goal making money given that it involves sailing to Aegina. Of course, he would not deliberate if he had no general desire for money, but as long as the general desire for money is not structured as to be realized in the present situation it is not a goal. The general desire for money polarizes B's attention toward possible means, that is, to structure the world hodologically around that general desire so that the world reveals to him its coefficient of instrumentality and adversity. This coefficient is objective for this desiring subject. It also is objective for any similarly situated agent capable of appreciating coefficients of instrumentality and adversity, given a possible project. If no means are presently found, the desire cannot become a goal; it remains a mere wish, a dream, for a goal is by definition as particular as the means. Indeed, a goal that is not particular (this, at such a place, at such a time, given who I am and my situation) cannot determine means, and means that are not particular cannot be chosen as means for they cannot be conducive to the goal. What is grasped as impossible cannot, by definition, be a goal, though if the wish remains polarizing it might become so in the future. What Sartre calls the fundamental project is the self-definition in wish, the wished self. What Aris defines in his ethics is the wishable self . Suppose B weighing coefficients against his own forces and desire reaches a decision. His decision simultaneously constitutes the goal as goal and the means as instruments, not as mere understood conditions of possibility: his goal is to find himself rich in Aegina. The two are inseparable, concretely. And goals and means are never abstract for, as Nussbaum rightly said in the epigraphe, desire--that is, the relevant desire, the goal-constitutive desire--is never abstract. Here we have the Aristotelian practical "premises" from which action will necessarily follow. But we do not have two separable, selb-standing premises. What we have is one decision to sail to Aegina to make money. One can of course distinguish the goal of making money from the means of sailing to Aegina. But the means are not actually means but for B who has chosen as his goal to become rich, and the goal is not goal but for B who has decided to sail to Aegina.

Now, to A . A is interested in an explanatory understanding of motion toward a goal. From his experience as agent he has a foundation to understand the Other and can check by talking with the Other. He sees two different things: desire and belief. Desire to make money, and belief that sailing to Aegina is necessary to make money. It is true that B's deliberation included the judgment that sailing to Aegina was necessary for him to make money. This is an objective judgment in which Aris

could agree with B. But this judgment does not constitute sailing to Aegina into means. It is true that B wishes to make money; but this general desire does not constitute making money into a goal.

What consitutes the goal into a goal and the means into a means is the decision or project to go to Aegina to make money. What is concretely desired, all in all, is to make money in Aegina, to sail to Aegina to make money. The two are one and the same, aspects of the same. Someone who would decide to go to Aegina to visit his parents would not engage in the same action as B. Nor would someone who would decide to sail to Aegina for the pleasure of sailing. The motion would be the same, but not the action, for the motion involved in action is intentional motion thoroughly qualified by the end desired as a goal, that is, the intentional motions involved in sailing to Aegina and making money there. Thus there are three inseparable moments of one action, not three things: desire, belief, and motion. Thus all Aris says amounts to this: someone who chooses a goal by choosing the means among the available possibilities and bysimultaneously choosing to move or not to move accordingly. If there is to be what is called "action" the three must obtain. But this does not imply (a) that if there is is projected goal and means there necessarily is motion toward the goal (for I can change my project or abandon it before starting to realize it), nor (b) that if there is desire of a goal as such there is mere theoretical belief (for the belief has to be practical), nor (c) that desire of goals and belief--even practical belief-- function as syllogistic premises for the agent (for neither the motion follows necessarily, nor are the 'premises' grasped as premises by the agent. - In actual reality, what we can say is that if there is intentional motion or lack thereof toward a goal there is a projected goal-means, which is a truism for it is the definition of what is called "action" and what distinguishes intentional motion from mere or unintentional motion.

What about the witness? First of all, in the witness's case there cannot be question of a practical syllogism, for the conclusion of a practical syllogism is an action, not the knowledge of an action. Furthermore, what occurs on the witnesses part is an expectation of a motion toward the realization of the goal, not a proposition, though the expectation can be verbalized as much as the agent's goal can be verbalized. Is this expectation necessary? No, for the agent's action is not necessary. The expectation is reasonable and for the most part.

What about the explanatory contemplator who is A? A has an explanatory theory of motion of self-sustaining creatures. His theory, as far as it concerns us, is that the motions of sensitive creatures as such are intentional, that is informed by desire and belief. "Informed" means resulting as an effect from the conjunction of two things: something desired as a goal, and something grasped as the necessary means to achieve the goal. Secondly, that the agent as such, as practically-minded, expects with certainty his own action from his desire of a goal and his understanding of something as the necessary means. I think that the theory is wrong at least concerning deliberate action. For neither is the motion necessary with the necessity of an effect, nor are there two different things--desired end and understood means--that produce a third thing called motion and, therefore, there is no possible analogy with the syllogism.

The literary genre of Aristotle's Ethics

Is the Ethics a theoretical work or a practical work? Aris suggests that it is practical, meant to result in action, and therefore not susceptible of the certainty of theoretical works.

Yet it is composed as a theoretical work, constituted by statements and persuasive arguments. Very puzzling. But important, too, for the purpose of understanding the confusion between the theoretical and the practical .

Aris himself: The Ethics is not theoretical knowledge in two different senses: because its object, being particular and changeable, does not lend itself to demonstrations that yield episteme, and because its purpose is not to reach mere contemplative assent but to contribute to correct action.

1095a2-a11: Qualifications of the student of ethics

(1) Political science argues from and about actions in life. Since the youth lack experience of them, they are not suitable students.

(2) A person who tends to be guided by his feelings (youth or immature) will waste his time in this study, for here the end is action, not knowledge. For an immature person, like an incontinent person, gets no benefit from his knowledge.

(3) If, however, we are guided by reason in forming our desires and in acting, then this knowledge will be of great benefit.

- Here we argue from and about actions .

In political science we argue not by means of demonstration but of persuasive arguments, in order to reach the practical knowledge of the target at which to aim. The clearer the notion of the target, the more likely we are to hit the right mark (1094a24).

What we argue about are "the actions in life about which political science argues" 1095-a4). These obviously are the actions that are good for the human being as such.

But we argue not only about good actions but from our experience of actions. This "from" is extremely important.

In the theoretical sciences the point is to reach knowledge through assent to true statements. In this case the thinker needs only to be intelligent, for the point is merely to understand. And the point is merely to understand and assent to the true because the theoretical sciences only argue about, not from. I believe that here Aris is pointing out a difference between the theoretical and the practical sciences that goes beyond the fact that in the practical sciences there is no possible episteme because there is no necessity and universality in their object, since the object of the practical sciences is variable because it has no "nature" so that it seems a matter of convention (1094b15), which renders demonstrations and necessary assent impossible in argument. (This is what we acknowledge when we say that there is no Catholic physics. YET WE TAKE AS A MATTER OF COURSE THAT THERE IS NATURAL THEOLOGY, THAT GOD'S EXISTENCE CAN BE DEMONSTRATED, OR THAT IF iT CANNOT BE DEMONSTRATED IT SHOULD BE DISCARDED, OR THAT IF IT CANNOT BE DEMONSTRATED IT FALLS UNDER THE IRRATIONAL FIELD OF FEELING!). As Aris suggests, even if the ethical arguments happen to persuade intellectually an immature person who acts by feeling, this intellectual persuasion is futile, "for an imature person, like an incontinent person, gets no benefit from his knowledge" (1095-a5), that is, will not act according to this knowledge but according to his feelings.

Aris does not explain why in physics we only argue about, whereas in ethics we argue about and from. But he suggests that feeling enters into ethics and not into physics. And his De Motu Animalium indicates that the reason is that action is horexic by nature, that is, involves desire. And it does so because here it is a matter not just of action but of good action, and the good is good only when it functions as good, that is, when it attracts in such a way that it results in action. In other words, good assented to as good but not desired as to be pursued is no good; good is what effectively moves to action, what is effectively desired, targetted as to be pursued. In the case of the inexperienced youth and the immature, what is effectively desired is not what is rational but what appeals to their feeling. ( Monsieur Rollebon led the sick man to effectively fear hell, not to understand hell. Or rather, he led him to understand fire practically by fearing it, as Aris wants us to understand the good for man).  

What is the difference between arguing about and arguing about and from? As a first approach, let us say that it has to do with whether the argued about is mere object or is subject-object. By subject I understand something that experiences, subject of experience, not mere object of somebody else's experience. For, obviously, only something that experiences can argue from experience, whereas anything that is experienced can be argued about. Furthermore, since it is a matter of arguing about and all arguing about involves understanding, here we are talking primarily of human beings, for only human beings argue about since only they understand in the sense relevant in our case. Hence the primary subject-matter of 'political science" in general and of ethics in particular is the human being as subject argued about on the basis of his/her experience as subject of experience.

There is no communist or Catholic physics because physics does not aim at understanding subjects as such. But if I am a Catholic and you are a communist and I understand you from my experience as a Catholic human being and imply that my understanding of the human being is the correct one, you will not recognize yourself in my understanding of the human being and, arguing from your own experience, will reject my argument by implicit reference to your communist understanding of the human being. If both of us happen to be physicists, we will be in basic agreement as physicists, yet we will be in basic disagreement as a communist and a Catholic in our self-understaning and in our understanding of the other. Why? Because as physicists we argue about, and in philosophy we argue about and from. By the same token, in the latter case we argue about each other, which is not the case in physics, and by implicit reference to an understanding based on our experiences which obviously yield different understandings of ourself and of each other.

Both in physics and in philosophy there is understanding involved, since we argue about in both cases. But the word "understanding" becomes equivocal, for the experiential grounds are of different kinds. In physics we abstract systematically from the subjective aspects. And by subjective aspects I do not mean only our 'internal' experiences but our experiences of the world as related to us and of ourself as related to the world. This is the experience "from" and "about" which we argue in philosophy. By contrast, that "about" which we argue in physics is, by definition, not an experiencer. Thus the "understanding" involved in physics and in philosophy are totally different understandings. For what is to be understood in physics--and, in general, in the natural sciences--is something which has no experience of the world as related to him or herself, and of himself or herself as related to the world, whereas in philosophy it is. The price of confusing the understanding that goes on in the natural sciences with the understanding that goes on in philosophy is high. [SEE NUSSBAUM BELOW].

Now let us concentrate on philosophical understanding , that is, knowledge about and from.

Aris wants this in his Ethics. He conceives of it as practical knowledge by opposition to theoretical whose purpose is to assent and rejoice in the contemplation of the truth. Here the purpose is to define 'the target.' But, according to Aris, one can accept it as the target to be aimed at in action only if one has the experience of targetting it.

( NUSSBAUM, however, seems sometimes to confuse seeing with aiming at (they are the same, except for desire) and setting the phaenomenon to be explained in physics with setting the phaenomenon to be aimed at (his controversy with Owen).  

Let us start with what I consider genuinely practical knowledge, which is the agent's knowledge WHO IS ACTUALLY involved in action. The target is the future state of the world that satisfies the agent's desire. Knowing it as a target means not only knowing it as desiderandum but actually aiming at it.

The practician of medicine 'knows' that aspirin heals headaches. but he knows in the practical sense by using aspirin for the sake of healing this patient's headache. Those are physicians who function as successful physicians, not those who know what physicians know. Those are pilots who function as successful pilots, not those who know what pilots know but have never flown a plane. Practical knowledge does not result in action, it is a constitutive moment of action called project to be realized, in which there is reciprocal definition of means by end and of end by means, which finds its realization in the changing of the stuffs of nature until the desired state of affairs is brought about.

Knowledge, then, informs action, constitutes knowledgeable action , informs it practically by targetting and hitting the mark, where actually hitting the mark is the culmination of practical knowledge. Edberg knows how to play tennis by playing it knowingly, that is, successfully, under different circumstances and against different adversaries. What defines the means as means is, on the one hand, its effectiveness, on the other, its actually being selected and used as means. Using fire to heal a headache is not part of a project since a project is to be realized, and this cannot contribute to its realization; it is, at most, a wrong candidate for a project. But also simply knowing that aspirin is the means is not part of a project since a project is to be realized. Using aspirin to heal a headache is practical knowledge, the knowledge that informs the actual realization of the disappearance of the headache which satisfies the desire.

Now practical knowledge is not only actual but also virtual. Edberg does not only have practical knowledge when he plays tennis because he can actualize his knowledge any time.

I have to return back, for I stranded. My point was to move toward the difference between the agent's knowledge and the reflective knowledge involved in Aris's Ethics. The former is direct; the latter is reflective and from and about the direct.

The former involves desire as its constitutive; the latter involves virtual desire, at best (?) . Finally, the knowledge about involved in the natural sciences is of a different kind which results in purely 'objective' statements. All this deserves attention.

Insofar as the agent may be said to know, his knowledge is direct, that is, his attention is outward directed, toward a future state of the world in relation to a subject exposed to it. Furthermore, because of his exposed situation as agent-patient, his attention is practical, directed toward possible objects for response. If there are judgments involved, the judgments themselves are practical and informed by desire. The agent acts from what he is in relation to the world, not from what he knows himself to be. If he is attracted or repelled by something, it is as being attracted or repelled that he acts, not as knowing himself being attracted or repelled. His being attracted or repelled is 'indicated' to him by the desired or feared object. Later on he will say "I was attracted." Then he knows himself as having been attracted by the object; but his knowledge is different from his being attracted, for he may well not be attracted anymore while he knows that he was attracted. The point is that the agent has no objectified knowledge of himself, and that later he will stand to his past self in a comparable relationship to that in which a witness of him acting stands to him; the witness knows him attracted by such, but the witness is not attracted. As for the agent's knowledge of the object, if it is to be called knowledge it is of a very special kind. Indeed it is knowledge of attractive object, not simply of object, where there is a mutual constitution of the agent as desiring by the atractive object and of the object as attractive by the desiring subject. As far as agency is concerned, a witness who sees the object desired by the agent but does not desire it, sees, in a certain way , a different object (though not simply a different object since the coefficient of adversity and instrumentality is independent from the agent and the witness: it takes time for the sugar to melt, one cannot build a house with water nor can one walk on air). Thus, if we are going to reserve the term "knowing about" for 'objective knowledge,' the agent does not function on the basis of knowing about the object. In other words, the intentionalities of knowing about and of desire and repulsion are of totally different kinds, not as NUSSBAUM assumes of the same kind: same object, plus desire.

This entails consequences both for the way in which

Aris conceives practical knowledge in the practical syllogism and for the way in which he conceives the knowledge involved in his Ethics. I start with the latter.

The Ethics is clearly a work of someone who reflects, not of an agent actually involved in action. Thus, if it is practical knowledge, it is practical in a different sense than the knowledge of the agent is practical. As observed by Nussbaum, in the agent's case verbal expression (statement) is irrelevant. By contrast, Aris Ethics is composed of statements and persuasive arguments. Furthermore, Aris grants that the immature can agree with his statements, though it will not profit them.

Another important point is that the Ethics statements end up being about the ideal agent, the virtuous agent as such, not about particular actions or even classes of actions. Thus, what Aris foresees in the inexperienced youth who tends to go by feeling, that is, to desire what gives him pleasure, is lack of assent to the ideal as regulative idea as to the kind of person he should attempt to be. And what he foresees in the immature is possible theoretical assent to an ideal that appears reasonable, but no practical assent that will result in actual attempting to shape himself according to the ideal.

Now, Aris defines the knowledge yielded by ethics as knowledge about and from. He says this of the "political sciences," not of all sciences, the reason being that the political sciences are about "actions in life." Clearly he would not say that the study of geometry involves argument from and about, but only about. Why not from? On this point he is ambiguous: it may be because geometry is not practical but theoretical, or because it does not argue from experience, or because of both. I think that in Aris's view it is because of both. Geometry does not argue from because the nature of its object and of the geometrical experience of it is such that it is and can only be experience about, so that there is no from on the basis of which to argue. Furthermore, geometry is not meant to lead to action, nor can it use persuasive argument precisely because it is not practical and does not involve experience from.

Why cannot the experience involved in geometry and in the natural sciences be experience from? Because, as already suggested, experience from is experience of exposed subject tied to the world by need or desire. This, I think, is what Aris means by "actions in life ," for our actual situation in life is that of exposed agents, not of invulnerable contemplators. The subject's experience involves not only what surrounds him but what surrounds him  in the mood of exposure.

(3) OBJECTIVE NECESSITY: Breathing (an action?) is necessary for life. Independent of desires and beliefs. Is this THEORETICAL NECESSITY or PRACTICAL NECESSITY? UNIVERSAL; IN ALL CIRCUMSTANCES - To get the money I desire I have to go to Aegina. SUBJECTIVE NECESSITY: I have to sail to Aegina because I desire money. Hypothetical, because dependent on a desire that may be there or not. If I don't desire, I don't have to sail to Aegina. Hypothetical, because dependent on a belief by the agent that it is necessary to sail to Aegina to satisfy his desire for money.

Mansion comments that Aris did not always distinguish between logical necessity (the grasp of sufficient reason for affirming that necessitates the judgment) and physical necessity. Some of this confusion may be at work here.

First of all, can we cleanly distinguish these necessities?

Under conditions of gravity bodies fall. This is called a physical necessity. But in fact it is only a strict past regularity, as Hume realized, which automatically creates expectations for the future. We are so made as agents. Gravity is an explanatory concept of the observed regularity. Gravity is what explains the motion or lack thereof of observed bodies which are not self-moving  or 'self-sustaining' or insofar as they are not self-moving or self-sustaining. Why do we put this explanatory concept out there? Because it is reached not through pure thinking but through observation and experimentation. But this is an explanatory concept for people who want to understand, for researchers, though it does have applications for people who want to act in control. It is our way of understanding what we observe (as opposed to what we experience). That concept of gravity works as a principle of deduction, once it is assumed, for particular cases, though of course the principle itself was not reached deductively or inductively but experimentally. It works as a principle of deduction because, given the well justified assumption, it involves logical necessity. (COMPARE GRAVITY TO THE ARISTOTELIAN SOUL AS A PRINCIPLE OF EXPLANATION. SEE NUSSBAUM ON THIS). Deduction makes the conclusion necessary for the one who grasps the gravity relationship among the bodies involved in the situation. This is logical or conceptual necessity that concerns judgments. But it is not physical necessity; judgments are not subject to gravity. However we say that, given what gravity is, bodies necessarily fall. This necessity is hypothetical: if gravity is what we understand it to be, such a body under such conditions will behave in such a way--necessarily. That is, it will be necessitated to act in such a way. Naturally, if we misunderstand gravity (as some people misunderstood certain pheanomena by relating them to aether), such a necessity does not exist. Yet the conclusion of the hypothetical judgment will continue to follow necessarily.

How does this differ from practical necessity and the necessity of a practical syllogism? If I desire this now more than anything else (eating a piece of chocolate) and grasp the necessity of spending money to buy it, I necessarily spend the money. It is the logic of action itself: action is (and is understood and defined as) the purposive activity (doing something for the sake of a goal) that necessarily follows a "desire" (of the goal) and a "belief" (about doing something as the necessary and sufficient means). If the action does not follow, another person has to conclude either to the insincerity of the desire or of the belief, unless given an explanation about either or a preventing factor.

Concerning the necessity of the action . If desire and means, the agent is necessitated to act, that is, his action necessarily follows. For the action to obtain necessarily these are sufficient conditions.

This does not seem right, at least in the case of human beings. Is one necessitated to act, in an exceptionless way, under these conditions, as one is necessitated to fall down after jumping under conditions of gravity, or as one is necessitated to assent to the conclusion of a syllogism? There are reasons to reject such claim.

Aris himself admits in humans that practical judgment can interfere with desire. Certain people in the concentration camps desired bread and grasped that they could steal it from their comrades, but abstained and even died because of it. And less exceptional cases of sexual abstention under intense desire and grasp of possibility. In all these cases, usually we do not need explanation for abstention; and if we need it and are given the appropriate one, we 'understand'. Furthermore, Aris admits that, unlike animals, people can plan their future. And we bring rapists to trial, not animals. In the cases of these people, if we are given the Aristotelian explanation of action, we reject it as illegitimate for people, though perhaps we accept it for animals. How does this affect the necessity of the practical syllogism?

Concerning the necessity of the practical syllogism :

Obviously, if what I just said is correct, the practical syllogism does not work for the case of people.

Let us start with the real practical syllogism, which is in the first person, the agent's person, and results in action. Later we will consider the explanatory syllogism in the third person, the observer's person.

Two possible rejoinders to what I said above. One concerns abstention as a form of action, the other the objection that the Aristotelian formula applies to what is desired mostly; to be a decent person, in the case of people who did not steal bread.

Concerning abstention. Certainly abstaining from bread or from sex is an action, when abstention is grasped as the means to obtain what is desired. But here abstention is not at all grasped as the means to satisfy one's hunger or sexual need, which is what is desired. Thus this is not the abstention of which Aristotle speaks in relation to action. This brings us to the second objection. According to Aris, the objector says, his formula applies to what is mostly desired, and it fits perfectly the case of abstention from food or from rape. In that case, I answer, what Aris is talking about is not "desired" but "grasped as desirable," at least in the case of humans, so that putting animals and people under the same formula, as Aris seems to do, is a mistake. Furthermore, the issue is complicated by the case of akrasia, where one does not do what one grasps as desirable. If action followed what one grasps as desirable, akrasia would be impossible. Aris's rejoinder would be that even in the case of akrasia, action follows what the akratic mostly desires and, therefore, what he grasps as mostly desirable in the circumstances. But this is does not fit the phaenomenon as set by the akratic, who confesses that he did not do what he grasped as mostly desirable, but that he desired what at least abstractly he grasped as less desirable or undesirable. He did what he desired, not what he grasped as mostly desirable. The abstainers did what they grasped as mostly desirable, not what they desired most. It seems that an equivocation is involved here. What is Aris's real claim?

Something may be desired by me without being my goal. Thus only what is desired as a goal is supposedly followed by action.

In animals there is no distinction between what is presently desired mostly and what is desired as a goal; in people there is. In people a goal is constituted as such by the decision to pursue it, that is, to act to bring it about. And the decision to pursue may come after deliberation about goals and may not coincide with what is mostly desired but with what is mostly desirable, or the inverse. So, again, what is Aris's claim?

Clearly his claim concerns action, not statement. Thus it is primarily about the agent, not the witness or the observer or the scientist. His primary claim seems to be that desire + belief results in action. His secondary claim seems to be that the connection is conceptual and therefore results in a hypothetically necessay syllogism.

Is his claim concerning the agent correct?

What exactly is the object desired? A desired state of affairs that cannot obtain without the agent's action concerning the means. - Dissatisfaction may be a condition for desire, but it is not desire. The desire of which Aris speaks in connection with action is always desire of something particular and future to be brought about by the agent's agency.

What exactly is the belief? That the desired state of affairs is possible if such an action on the agent's part, which is grasped as concretely possible and necessary and sufficient to bring the goal about is executed by the agent. I say "concretely possible," for it depends on the agent's abilities to will and execute it.

What is action? The motion on the agent's part, or the active abstention from motion as a means, deemed appropriate by the subject to bring about the desired state of affairs that will satisfy the agent.

According to Aris, if the first two obtain, the third will obtain as a matter of hypothetical necessity. That is, whenever the first two obtain, unless the agent is prevented by something that does not depend on the agent, the action deemed necessary and possible will necessarily obtain.

There appear, from Nussbaum's analysis, to be three things involved: desire (appetitive of something as vitandum or amplexendum), belief (purely cognitive of the way the world is and action works and what the agent is capable of), and action (actual motion or active abstention from it). That there are three different things involved is easy to understand: for one can desire a state of affairs without believing, and believe that a state of affairs is possible without desiring it, and neither desire nor belief by themselves are actual motion or active abstention from motion. Notice, however, that this is an analysis made by a thinker who wants to understand or explain action, who thinks about action but is not involved in action. Things may be otherwise with the agent. And I believe they are.

In the first place, concerning the desire. The desire that results in action is not any desire --not even in animals, it seems. A deer that desires to approach the water to drink may rather move away from it if it senses the presence of a tiger in the vicinity. The deer has the desire to drink, but this desire does not result in action. Aris might say either that the deer was prevented by the tiger, or that the belief of possibility was lacking on the deer's part. But my present point is that the desire of which Aris speaks in connection with action is only a desire of a certain kind, a desire of a future state of affairs already constituted as his goal by the agent. Now an intrinsic, essential component of such desire is that the desired state of affairs appear to the agent as concretely possible and necessary, for a goal is a goal only insofar as it is to be pursued (indeed, it would be oxymoronic to say "I have a goal that I do not intend to pursue"), and it cannot be desired by the agent as a goal to be pursued without the agent's belief that it is concretely possible and necessary or conducive. Thus the desire of which Aris speaks in connection with action is desire of something grasped as possible and necessary, not just desire of something. But this is to say that the desire of which Aris speaks is not separable from the belief, anymore than the belief of which Aris speaks is separable from the desire, for it is belief about something desired as a goal. Thus, we are simply equivocating if under the name "desire," as Aris means it in this context, we understand just any desire, and if under the name of "belief" we understand any belief about the objective and subjective possibility and necessity of a means. The fact that one can desire something without having made the desired into one's goal does not imply that the desire of which Aris speaks and which results in action is of that kind.

Secondly, concerning the belief. The belief of which Aris speaks is not any belief concerning the possibility and necessity of an action, but the belief informed by the agent's desire to pursue as his goal a certain state of affairs. The fact that one can have the belief that an action is possible and necessary for a certain state of affairs to obtain does not imply that it is the belief of which Aris speaks as resulting in action. I cannot set as my goal eating this piece of bread, however much I may desire it, if I cannot believe that it is subjectively possible for me to steal it, which is the only means available. I certainly can have the belief that stealing money is possible and necessary for me to travel, and I can even have the desire to travel, but that desire and that belief will not result in action unless I have absolutely made travelling into my goal under those conditions of possibility. In fact, that is why one deliberates as much about goals as about means, that is, one deliberates whether one will desire something as a goal given the means and the price its realization will imply, and one deliberates whether one is capable of and willing to use the means that appear as objectively necessary . Nussbaum's idea that it is a mere belief, that is a purely objective, intelligent calculation of the means, that is, of what makes the desired state of affairs objectively possible, is wrong, for the calculation involves the subjective abilities and willingness of the agent.

What then, in the third place, about action? The very fact that Aris sees the necessity of the action when one has a goal of the kind described, that is, a desire-belief of the kind described, shows that action itself, at the appropriate time, is a constitutive element of the desire-belief which Aris has in mind. He who has set himself as his goal to bring about a state of affairs either has decided to act to bring it about and acts at the appropriate time, or has not set himself as a goal to bring about that state of affairs. By definition, something desired as a goal under accepted particular conditions of possibility and necessity is something that results in action, as far as the subject is concerned. If it does not have this active constituent it cannot be the desire-belief of which Aris is talking. The fact that an action like, say, stealing, can obtain to satisfy one's hunger, if that is one's goal, as well as to satisfy one's desire to travel, if that is one's goal, does not make it the same action, in the sense relevant in the context of which Aris speaks. Thus action cannot either, anymore than desire-belief, be considered as a different entity as the particular desire-belief.

In short, it is constitutive of the very notion of having a goal to desire something as to be pursued, not simply to desire something. And it is constititive of the notion of desiring  something as to be pursued to have grasped it as necessary (or at least conducive) and possible. And it is constitutive of the notion of desiring something as to be pursued to be willing to act in the way grasped by the agent to be necessary and possible and to act at the appropriate time. The illusion that there are three different things rather than the one process we call action of which actual motion or active abstention from it as the means to bring about the realization of the desired state of affairs that will satisfy the desiring agent is a constitutive  element or moment arises from the fact that we are linguistic predicators, and that linguistic predication is the deed not of the agent as agent but of a merely curious contemplator of the agent who wants to understand.

Predicating involves naming, which is a way of setting down or substantifying the object to be understood, defined, explained. Except for proper names, names are common. That is, they name common features and, except when used by actually involved agents, who perceive hodologically in terms of the dramatic future expected or to be brought about , rather than by curious contemplators who just talk about , the common features they name are congealed, abstract, timeless and spaceless features of past experience or observation. "Desire", "belief", "action" are names of this kind. They are meant to name all the objects that share certain common features, to substantify them for purposes of consideration, that is, of further predication. Of course, names, which are the central and an essential element of predication , can be qualified to reduce their extension. Thus, we can speak simply of desire of something, or of desire of something as a goal. We use, however, the same word "desire" for both. Hence the temptation to think that we are speaking about or naming in the nominative (which is different from naming in the vocative ) a particular species of the same genus "desire," that is, something which shares the same essential features of "desire" and possesses some other separable features not found in all desires but only in that species of desires. ( Genus and species, as Aristotle thought, are purely logical or grammatical entities). This amounts to assuming that, because we use the same word which has only one common definition, the agent who desires a piece of cake and the agent who has made into his goal to get a piece of cake are doing the same thing. Furthermore, this amounts to assuming that because, given the substantification involved in predication, desire is understood and conceived as different from belief and from action, the agent who desires something as his goal, and who believes that something is necessary and possible for him to achieve that goal, and who moves or actively abstains from moving as a means to realize his goal, does three different things.

There seems to be something of this in Nussbaum's interpretation. In one of the epigraphes of this paper she identifies the practical with the non-practical, except for desire, and praises Aristotle for not having given two different explanations of them. According to her, perceiving is discriminating, seeing something as. This is what the agent does, according to Nussbaum, except that the agent adds desire to perception. In other words, the engaged agent does exactly the same as the contemplative thinker who wants to understand, as far as perceiving is concerned, and in addition does something else, namely desirring what he perceives. I suspect that Nussbaum is wrong. Desiring is not perceiving and something else; it is perceiving as an agent, hodologically, dramatically. What Nussbaum calls perceiving and identifies with the non-practical--that is, with mere knowledge, with the purely theoretical--is not at all what the agent does. It is what a pure contemplator does when he has neutralized his exposure to the world, thereby transforming himself into invulnerable observer. Nussbaum's illusion arises from the substantifying nature of predication, which necessarily makes of desiring a different thing from perceiving and considers both as objects for a contemplator.

Defining desiring implies 'seeing' desiring as a pure observer (rather than being engaged in desiring, or in being desired, or in being potential object of desire, or in having one's loved or coveted desireds desired by an Other), substantifying it as object of predication, and reducing it to a a genus by finding the traits common to all the cases of observed desire. The genus "desire" is reached through abstraction of common relevant traits from all specific irrelevant differences, as the species "desire" is reached through abstraction of common traits from all individual differences. The same with the genus "belief" and the genus "action".

But I am not, myself, doing the same as Nussbaum, that is, the same which I criticize in others? For what I propose is to redefine desiring something as a goal by understanding it as a temporal process. I also am substantifying and predicating, observing the process rather than engaging in the process of acting. What, then, is the improvement?

In a way I am doing the same, since I am talking about action. But in another and very relevant way, I am not doing the same. For I am substituting for the Aristotelian observer's understanding of action the practical understanding of the vulnerable agent's own acting. USING SARTRE'S APPROACH, SUGGESTED A LITTLE ABOVE, ABOUT BEING DESIRING SUBJECT AND BEING DESIRED SUBJECT MIGHT HELP TO EXPLAIN WHAT I WANT TO EXPLAIN.

Action is a transaction with the environment, for it involves a change to be effected in the environment for the agent's sake and through the agent's agency. Change involves passage from one state to another, and this is what is brought about through the agent's agency. For action to take place the agent has to find himself in an unsatisfactory situation in relation to the present state of the world, to imagine a more satisfactory state of the world and the means required for him to bring it about, to constitute it as his goal by deciding to use the appropriate necessary means, and to actually employ the necessary means at the appropriate time.

According to Aris, the structure of action replicates that of a syllogism, except that the necessity is hypothetical: If I have set myself such a goal, and if I have found the means that make possible and are necessary for the realization of my goal, the action necessarily follows as a conclusion, except if prevented by something independent from the agent's agency. The difference between this practical syllogism and the theoretical syllogism is that in the former case the conclusion is an action and in the former is a judgment that takes the form of a statement. If A is necessarily B, and if B is necessarily C, the judgment follows necessarily that A is necessarily C. In this case, the necessity is logical, that is, implied by the very concept of A, B, and C. Is the necessity logical in the case of the practical syllogism?

Logical necessity involves understanding of concepts. What Aristotle proposes to his reader is a notion of action to be understood and assented to; its result is a judgement-statement that action necessarily follows, but it is not an action. Thus the syllogism proposed by Aristotle to his reader is a theoretical syllogism about the nature of action, not the practical syllogism he has in mind, much as his ethics is about the virtuous man, but the assent to his ethics does not make one virtuous. Only Socrates and Plato who confused knowing about with doing or being, and who reduced the latter to the former,  would have thought that the agreeement with what Aris says about ethics makes one virtuous. We all know that the people who talk most about ethics and even with full intellectual assent are often not the ones who behave most ethically, Washington having slaves being a good example of this--all men are created free, but...! It is to be remarked, however, that we accuse them of inauthenticity, that is, of discordance between beliefs and actions, which seems to indicate that the logic of beliefs is the same as the logic of action, which is precisely what Aris suggests by reducing action to a practical syllogism.

All this is very puzzling. For, on the one hand, it seems that knowing and acting are two very different activities; on the other hand, we sense a relationship between the two and demand authenticity on the knower's part. What, then?

One interesting aspect of the theoretical syllogism proposed by Aris to his reader is that it does not involve desire in any way on the reader's part, whereas the practical syllogism does on the agent's part. What Aris expects from his reader is understanding of the nature of action as he proposes it and, ideally, assent on the basis of evidence. There is no desire involved in this process because there is at stake no good to be pursued. What Aris presents is not a particular action in pursuance of a particular good but the structure or intelligible form of action--an abstraction of the features common to all actions. Certainly Aris's reader has to have had the experience of acting to understand and agree or disagree with Aris statements about action, and he has to remember the kind of questions he asked somebody who seemed in the situation to act as the reader expected him to do and who failed to do so.  But neither Aris nor his reader are actually involved in action, not even in trying to understand an agent in a particular situation, but in trying to understand the general nature of action.

Now, the very fact that many readers tend to agree with Aris statements about the nature of action on the basis of their experience as agents concerning the goal, the means, and the motion seems to confirm that Aris is right about the agent. And the fact that the questions addressed to an agent who failed to act when or as expected fit with Aris's explanation is a further confirmation, for the reasons for the failure will concern the goal, or the means, or preventive factors.

On the other hand, whether Aris is right or wrong, the agent does not need to know what Aris and his reader know about the nature of action in order to act. The agent does not need to know that desire is required to act, anymore than the bee needs to know the nature of flying to fly; what he needs is to desire some concrete state of affairs and so on. The bee flies, the agent desire, Aris knows that the agent (among other things) desires, but Aris and his reader as knowers do not desire, they explain.

Someone who desires something as his goal-means, necessarily acts (or 'moves'?), says Aris, much as someone who grasps that A=B and B=C necessarily concludes that A=C. Since the latter is a matter of logical or conceptual necessity involved in grasping concepts, the suggestion is that in the case of action the necessity concerning end and means is conceptual too, as Nussbaum seems at times to think. However, as we just saw, one can act without having any concept of action, without knowing anything about action. Thus the necessity of the action following cannot be conceptual. More preisely: someone who knows can deduce that the agent will necessarily act, but he himself will not act, he will judge as a matter of conceptual necessity; by contrast, someone who has goal-means will act, but he will not deduce conceptually. It seems, therefore, that Aris is wrong in suggesting logical necessity by speaking of practical syllogism. The only syllogism involved is theoretical and is the logical procedure of the knower, not of the agent.

Why, then, the problem of authenticity, and the experiential agreement with Aris's statement about action which works as 'evidence' for his reader? As a step to answering this question, let us ask about the necessity of the action itself, not of the deductive process.

There is no deductive process without content, that is, about nothing. And the deductive process depends on the content. From A=B and C=D no deduction is possible. Furthermore the necessity involved in A=B and B=C, therefore A=C is not affirmed about the process but about the content, however necessary the proccess itself may be, for in fact what renders the process necessary is the conceptual content grasped. This would indicate that the agent's motion follows necessarily; only the agent does not know that necessity, and his knowing it is in no way required.

This issue has some similarity with God's foresseance of human action. If God's foreseeance is infallible, the agent acts necessarily according to God's foresseance. Thus the agent's action is not free; the agent necessarily chooses what God foresaw. The rejoinder is that God foresaw what the agent was freely to choose. But the interest of the comparison is somewhere else. For God foresees the particular action, whereas Aris's syllogism refers to its structure only. Or does it?

If Aris knew the ends-means chosen, he would know the particular motion that would follow, if indeed there is such necessity as he claims. Now, the agent is in that position, for he is aware of the end-means he has chosen, once he has decided. Thus the agent knows the action that follows necessarily. I have decided to be courageous, therefore I will act courageously. I have sincerely promised my fidelity forever, therefore I will be faithful to my wife forever. But, as Sartre reminds us, this is clearly false.

The reasons are many. Leave aside physical prevention. What appeared to me, the agent, desirable as a goal under the present circumstances, may cease to appear so, because I or the world has changed, and I can change my goals. What appeared to me acceptable as a means when I decided to pursue the goal may cease to apppear so. Sartre is naive in making it all depend on me once the decision is made, as if the world that makes actions possible did not change, and as if I were in absolute control of my own changes, but certainly he is more perceptive than Aris in not considering action a syllogistic matter involving conceptual necessity.

Apart from physical (or physiological) prevention beyond the agent's control, the only exception Aris considers is akrasia.

But he would argue that the intemperant ultimately acts according to the practical syollogism, for he really does not set as his goal what he abstractly knows as desirable but what he actually desires, which is what determines his motion. As for Sartre, akrasia would simply be a free change in one's decision--even under torture. And the only thing he would blame the agent for is bad faith, in case the agent denied that he freely made a new decision and refused to assume responsibility for it, since for Sartre there is no abstractly desirable and people can always disengage themselves from themselves as well as from the world and make themselves desire what they freely choose.

What about akrasia for me?  Is it an exception to the Aristotelian practical syollogism? Does it do away with the whole idea of the practical syllogism? I already said or implied that the theoretical syllogism about the nature of action is false. It does not take into account that action is a temporal, transcendent, interactive exchange between a subject exposed to a changing world and a world exposed to a changing subject. Aris's model is oversimplified. His idea of action is motion of self-sustaining (living) creatures, which covers a lot of different things because they are to be differently understood by agents and, therefore, differently responded to. It shows in our responses. We do not blame or praise people for contracting their pupil under light, nor do we expect them to act otherwise. We understand and even excuse people for failing to move according to their project under extreme physical or psychological duress, and even expect them to act so, though we admire the few who don't. This is part of the "phaenomenon," a part which Aris does not take into account. We distinguish theoretically certain basic categories of motion, for which we give different explanations, because our practical experience as agents offers grounding evidence: reflexes (without desire), perceptual reflexes (like closing one's eyes before a mobile), sensual reflexes (like withdrawing when burnt), instinctual desirous actions, invention of desirous actions, etc. Calling all of them actions because they involve motion--even living motion--is equivocating. Even Nussbaum's "horexic action," to which the practical syllogism applies, is a bad equivocation. In fact, it forces Aris into the practical syllogism, which may apply to animals and to people at a certain age or under extreme circumstances, but does not apply uniformly. A movement rightly understood in a different categorical way than another movement is categorically different and, therefore, using the same name for both is a source of endless equivocations. This is what Aris does with "horexic movement." Why does he not do quite the same with reflexes? Or, as Skinner does, with all animal motion except the basic physiological reflexes, which he calls behavior and subsumes under one and the same explanation? In that case, the behavior of the dozen healthy infants becomes foreseeable and shapeable.

This brings us to the crucial point. In fact, the famous practical syllogism is just another rule of thumb (a fact which fits perfectly with the rest of Aris's ethics), and explains our demand for authenticity and for explanations in case of failure of motion as well as our understanding and even our sympathetic understanding of explanations and excuses for failures to move. To this I will come later. But the crucial point, for my interested purposes, is why Aristotle makes this mistake of confusing theoretical and practical. I believe, for the same reason that Skinner: they give the primacy to the theoretical and reduce the practical to the theoretical, whereas in fact knowledge is subordinated to reality, theory to experience.

We always perceive-as. But we only perceive particulars. And we perceive primarily in interaction, objects for response--at the beginning passively, later actively and toward the future. As Hume realized, we are so built that from objects that look similar we expect similar future effects. This is an automatism that works well for survival, statistically. Language, through predication, allows us to enrich our experience of their action (that is, of what they do to us, human beings who live under the conditions of this tribe) with the experience of other members of our tribe. (Blacks, as agents exposed to whites, perceive whites in a different way that whites perceive whites). Thus, there is automatic generalization of future  effects (and 'stable properties), which is an economic way of dealing with multiplicity by reducing it to unity. But this generalization is from the practical standpoint of success and failure of expectations, structured by the we of the tribe, constantly reinforced by approval and disapproval, admiration and contempt by the members of the tribe. The important shift occurs when we pass from the practical to the explanatory level, when we neutralize exposure and transform the particular into object of consideration and predication of properties, when we want to understand and explain the object in itself. Not only is that object not anymore object for response in the ordinary situations shared by the members of the tribe (Carthago delenda est Romanis, sed servanda Carthaginensibus; Christmas is a festive day for people but tragic for turkeys), but it is not particular and situated, but merely representative of a class, ready for further abstraction. It gives the impression of being the same object with which we interact, for it bears the same name and can be observed or imagined as the object of the same name with which we interact. But it is not object of aversion or desire, constitutive element of neither goal nor means .

This is object for consideration, for understanding, for explanation, that is, purely intelligible object, grammatical subject of merely intelligible, explanatory predicates. But it is not the object of reciprocal exposure with which we interact, which is always particular and hodologically situated, not merely intelligible. Thanks to language, through which we enrich our expectations for interaction, the object is also grammatical subject. But not grammatical subject for explanatory purposes, not object as object of pure (by opposition to practical) intelligibility . Take, for instance, oxygen. The chemist observes and describes it in itself and in its behavior in relation to other elements, such as H2o. This is setting the phaenomenon for explanatory purposes, that is, why oxygen, unlike carbon, behaves in that characteristic way.

There seems to be another way of setting the phaenomenon more closely connected with response. It is the one involved in ordinary language, through which we, unlike animals, objectify in order to enrich our concept of object for response. It is in relation to "us," to "people like us." I mentioned in this connection blacks and whites for themselves and for each other.

What kind of abstraction goes on here?

Think of means first. In a way, means are 'objective'. To see my mother I have to travel to Mexico. To play the lottery I have to buy a ticket. But they refer to the subject's action.

And they are always in concrete circumstances for a situated agent. They refer to the agent who has the goal as their center, as "tools" do.

Nonetheless, we agents have a certain implicit idea of the world, of how things generally work. MACINTYRE'S CHAPTER ON SOCIOLOGICAL PREDICTION MIGHT BE OF INTEREST HERE. And we define in general chairs, tables, mountains, houses, rocks, trees, computers, snow, winter, etc. What kind of objectification and abstraction is involved here? It involves a sort of Aristotlian "form" expressed in the dictionnary's definition, but not for purposes of explanation. For what, then?

Definitions refer us to concepts, not to things, not to the world. The word "siren" refers to a concept, not to a thing in the world. The "is" of definitions, per se, is not predicative, it indicates what is conceived by the definer and to be conceived by the one who receives the definition. A concept is a potential hanger of predicates--real or imaginary, through observation or invention. The predicates enrich the concept and, therefore, the definition. But we are not yet naming a thing, only a concept. Only when we predicate primarily of the thing (not of the concept) do we really name something other than a concept. Now the thing is the hanger of predicates. In this case we are naming something in the indicative--something in the real or in an imaginary or possible world. But still the predication is theoretical, mere object of assent, not of response.

"The pencil is on the table." I am not enriching the concept of pencil or of table. I am talking about a concrete object in a concrete world hodologically structured, as an agent who is situated in that world and for agents who are situated in that world and whose situation (which involves their project, their intentions) I rightly or wrongly grasp. But I am not always in actual interaction; then I store information for potential encounters--general and particular information. The information is potentially practical, for an agent hodologically situated. Same with "The mountain is on my way." How does this concrete mountain compare with the mountain defined in the dictionary?

"Santa Claus: Personage who fills children's stockings with presents at Christmas" (Pocket Oxford Dictionary, p 726). "Wolf: Wild beast allied to dog preying on sheep & hunting in packs" (Ibid., p 969). "Seesaw: Backward & forward movement or up & down movement as of a saw" (Ibid., 746). "Slave: One who is another's property, a human chattel, helpless victim of or to some dominating influence..." (Ibid., p 779). "Mountain: Hill of impressive height" (Ibid. p. 513). "Thought: Thinking as a process or a faculty, a conception or conclusion or intention or metnal image formed in the process, a notion that is ormay be meditated upon or that occupies the mind..." (Ibid., 872).

"Multiple: Qunatity exactly divisible by another" (Ibid., 515).

We speak in the presence and in the absence of an object, in situation of encounter or not, for immediate or not immediate purposes of encounter. The object is ob-ject for thought and understanding, or ob-ject for thought and response, or ob-ject for response. Clearly, the dictionary's definitions are in absence. And they are of the object itself--"hill of impressive height"--that is, without reference to its instrumentality (except in the case of instruments like the hammer), "out of situation" or at least independently of any situation of encounter. In this sense, it is a matter of intellectual or imaginary contemplation. That is, the definitions do not call for action, for response. Immediately they call for conception of what the name means, for the name given in the dictionary is primarily a name of a concept. Mediately or indirectly, the dictionary's definitions call for assent, insofar as they implicitly affirm that such is the concept named by the name.

Now, dictionary names 'mean' concepts, but concepts do not mean names, they 'mean' that of which they are the concept. Mediately, therefore, through the concept, the dictionary refers to things without meaning them.

All the above definitions are meant as identifiers of something conceived, be it directly from experience or from invention. The guy who invented the blackboard first invented and then realized.

Thanks to industrialization, progress in communications, and technological advances, we live in a very different world from Aris's.

Take "abortion." Or, more simple, take "murder," for in this case most people are, at least supposedly, like us.

Physical necessity (like falling under conditions of gravity) works whether realized (known) or not by the falling subject and independently of his initiative. The subject is purely and totally passive. He has to do nothing; he is done.

Biological necessity (like breathing) is like physical necessity in living organisms.
