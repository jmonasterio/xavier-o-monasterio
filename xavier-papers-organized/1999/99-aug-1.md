CHRISTIANITY IS NOT A DOCTRINE

About being or not being afraid of the dog Phyrro could do nothing, because the perception of the fierce dog indicated him to himself as an imminent victim of the dog.  Thus Hume was wrong; there is no required passage from is to ought. Signals render the passage superfluous (Monasterio. See below, p. 11).

“For Augustine our hearts are restless until they rest in God; for Aquinas, not our hearts, but first and most our minds are restless until they rest in seeing him” (Lonergan, Verbum, p. 90).

“ Underlying that is the remarkable fact that academic culture in the Western world breathes an atmosphere of unbelief... Now the deeper historical reasons for this are hard to define, and I won’t try here (although this is perhaps one of the most important intellectual tasks of our time) . I want to talk about how it manifests itself. It’s not just that there is a certain difficulty or embarrassment in introducing views that draw on some theistic basis or in avowing religious belief, though this is part of it. It is much more that unbelief has informed more than the answers. It has also shaped the questions. A young entrant into this world –say, an undergraduate or graduate student– might have strong faith, or be looking for ways of clarifying it through courses in history, politics, philosophy, or whatever. But in face of what is actually being discussed, it is often unclear how this relates to the student’s agenda and even less clear how the things that are personally important could impinge on the discussion that is going on. There just seems to be no relevant place to make the kind of remarks that the student would like to make...  - A striking example of this pre-shaped agenda is the aspect of moral theory, which I talked about ... in my lecture here... The issue here is the quality of our moral motivation –in more old-fashioned terms, the quality of our will and the nature of the vision that sustains it.  - ... I was struck in some of the comments on Sources   by how many people couldn’t seem to grasp what question I was addressing. They took ‘moral sources’ to be another name for the highest principles. They literally couldn’t think outside the contemporary agenda. - ... But one wants to protest don’t you see that it also matters whether people can actually bring themselves to do the right thing? ... - But other streams were immensely influential. Two dominate the mainstream Enlightenment... These offer each its own way of casting the motivation to justice and benevolence as imminent, while they also bring about a revolution in relation to the earlier Christian and Stoic understandings of this motivation; the revolution consists in a rehabilitation of ordinary, untransformed human desire and self-love, previously seen as an obstacle to universal justice and  benevolence, which now is cast as either innocent or as a positive force for good. - The innocentizing strategy paints human motivation as neutral; always a mode of self-love, it can be well or badly and irrationally or rationally directed. Guided by reason, it leads to justice and mutual aid. The extreme case, in which this ideal type is virtually unalloyed is, for example, Helvetius. Here the issue of the nature of our motivation disappears altogether. Everything depends on what guides it. - There is something here, analogous to the rise out of base, sensual self-absorption into a broader, higher, purer perspective, that figured in earlier views; this is the move from narrow, irrational, brutish, unenlightened ways of seeing the world to enlightenment and science. Science by its very nature involves our taking an objective and in this sense, universal perspective on things. To see human life in the view from nowhere or, to use a term of the epoch, from the standpoint of the ‘impartial spectator’ is to think in universal and no longer parochial terms. But this rise is now coded as exclusively in the register of the understanding; the will remains constant. -Within this framework, it is clear why the quality of the will is irrelevant to ethics. What is needed to work out what we ought to do is purely disengaged reason . - And this leads me to believe that the most important thing Christian scholars should attempt is to change the agenda, open it up. This may mean bringing back issues that may not immediately, on their face, relate to Christian faith–like this question of the place of the quality of the will in moral philosophy. We have to think of the ways in which our whole debate with modernity, our friendly yet adversarial exchange, is impeded and stifled by a drastically foreshortened intellectual agenda and then move to widen this. If we can get to the point where mainstream people have to d efend the narrow focus, we will have succeeded ” (Charles Taylor, “Concluding Reflections and Comments”, in A Catholic Modenrity; Charles Taylor’s Marianist Award Lecture, with responses... , Edited by James Heft, Oxford U. Press, New York-Oxford, 1999) pp. 119-123).

“What modern philosophy understands by ‘faith’ is what properly is called an ’opinion,’ or what is loosely called in everyday speech ‘believing.’ Christianity is made into a doctrine, and the next state is to comprehend this doctrine, and that is what philosophy does. On the whole, this is quite right, in case Christianity were a doctrine; but since it is not that, it is a crazy proceeding” (Soren Kierkegaard, The Offence) .

Faith would be believing or assenting without sufficient evidence or on the basis of sheer authority IF Christianity were a doctrine. But it is not. A doctrine is a statement or collection of interrelated statements to be assented to. One can assent to a doctrine with or without sufficient evidence, or one can refuse to assent. That is all one can do concerning a doctrine. Assenting, however,  is not what people of  faith do or what faith consists in. Even the demons, as characterized by James in his epistle, who have no ‘faith,’ do better or otherwise than having a belief, since they are afraid of God. One is not afraid of a doctrine or a statement. A doctrine cannot be feared since it cannot do any harm and since the distances of the subject to a doctrine are not distances of exposure, existential distances. (Monasterio)

“There are, however, two characteristics found in St. Thomas which indicate the beginning of a change of orientation which was gradually brought about in the theology of the Schoolmen. On the one hand, the process of conceptual analysis proper to Scholasticism eventually led, by becoming more rigid, to a categorical distinction between the intellect and the will which was reflected up to a point in a certain reciprocal exteriority of faith and charity .  No doubt, since faith is an act of the intellect moved by the will, whatever it includes which is accidental to the intellect can still remain essential to it. Hans Frei on biblical narratives

“... since the world truly rendered by combining biblical narratives into one was indeed the one and only real world , it must in principle embrace the experience of any present age and reader. Not only was it possible for him, it was also his duty to fit himself into that world in which he was in any case a member ... its [biblical interpretation’s] direction was that of incorporating extrabiblical thought, experience and reality into the one real world detailed and made accessible by the biblical story--not the reverse ” (Hans W. Frei, The Eclipse of Biblical Narrative , Yale U. Press, 1974, p. 3).

N. B. But the one world referred to here is a world that has the unity of a drama in which all people are actually involved, though not all of them realize their participation in it. By contrast, the Cartesian, scientific world, the world of Spinoza and Cocceius (see next quotation), is a contemplated or observed world in which there are ‘objects” individualized by their position in space and time.

“The above situation started being reversed by Spinoza and Cocceius... a kind of detachment of the ‘real’ historical world from its biblical description. The real events of history constitute an autonomous temporal framework of their own under God’s providential design. Indispensable as means of access to the events, now simply verify them, thus affirming their autonomy and the fact that they are in principle accessible through any kind of description t hat manages to be accurate either predictively or after the event... There is now a logical distinction and a reflective distance between the stories and the ‘reality’ they depict ” (Ibid., pp.-5) [See quotation above on Cartesian coordinates, article by Mensch].

“In the days before empirical philosophy, Deism, and historical criticism, the realistic feature had naturally been identified with the literal sense which in turn was automatically identical with reference to historical truth. But once these thought-currents had had their effect, and the ‘literal sense’ of the stories came to be governed with a heavy hand by, and logically subordinated to, probable and language-neutral veracity , the reverse had to be the case: in order to recognize the realistic narrative feature as a significant element in its own right (viz. as a story’s making literal rather than allegorical or mythical or some other nonliteral sense regardless of whether the literal sense is also a reliable factual report) one would have had to distinguish sharply between literal sense and historical reference. And then one would have had to allow the literal sense to stand as the meaning, even if one believed that the story does not refer historically. But commentators... failed to understand what they had seen when they had recognized the realistic character of biblical narratives, because every time they acknowledged it they thought this was identical with affirming not only the history-likeness but also a degree of historical likelihood in the stories . Those who wanted to affirm the historical factuality used the realistic character or history-likeness as evidence in favor of this claim, while those who denied the factuality also finally denied that they had seen what they had seen because (once again) they thought  history-likeness identical with at least potentially true history . - In both the affirmative and the negative cases, the confusion of history-likeness (literal meaning) and history (ostensive reference), and the hermeneutical reduction of the former to an aspect of the latter, meant that one lacked the distinctive category for understanding what one had actually recognized: the high significance of the literal, narrative shape of the stories for their meaning ” ( Ibid. , pp. 11-12).

“But in effect, the realistic or history-like quality of biblical narratives, acknowledged by all, instead of being examined for the bearing it had on its own right on meaning and interpretation was immediately transposed into the quite different issue of whether or not the realistic narrative was historical. - This simple transposition and logical confusion between two categories or contexts of meaning and interpretation constitutes a story that has remained unresolved in the history of biblical interpretation ever since. Were we to pursue our theme into the biblical interpretation of the 20th century, I believe we would find that... the story has remained much the same ” (Ibid., pp. 15-16),

“... the philosophical or conceptual apparatus, including the theory of meaning, underlying historical criticism of the gospel narratives tends to move it from every explanation of texts not directly governed by a referential theory of meaning [either ostensive or ideal reference, or an extension of such reference] or by the cognate identification of meaning with knowledge... The situation has remained the same since the 18 th century” ( Ibid., p 135)

“ The situation in the study of the Bible was at one with the prevailing outlook in philosophy . It mirrored not only what was going on in technical philosophy itself but also in theoretical assumptions about the natural sciences, in some (though not all) moral philosophy and practical aesthetic considerations” (Ibid. , p. 139).

“... once meaning had become firmly identified with reference and therefore with knowledge of the referent... Epistemological theory dominated the way in which texts mean” (Ibid. , p 140).  (CF AQUINAS ON STATEMENTS’S REFERENCE).

“... began to view the unity of factual and saving meaning in scripture as a matter of distinctive positioning of the reader or interpreter. The bible is not simply, if at all, a book of dogma for intellectual assent. While it is a book of facts, it is obviously far more than that... Its inspiration and its topics involve writer, text, and reader in a common mode or disposition or faith” ( Ibid. , p. 180).

Its meaning is a unitary complex, consisting of the history of saving events, the history of the witnesses’ faithful response to them and finally the present faithful stance toward that complex history as a present and future reality” ( Ibid.,  p. 181).

“ The upshot of Marx’s view of the criticism of religion was that the very development of historical-critical investigation into the Gospels, whether in support or denial of belief in JC, was itself already one of the signs that christological belief or doctrine under investigation had ceased to be of historical significance... “ Application was a different matter because it is concerned with the significance, and possibly with the authority of the text in a context other than the original . Existential distances and narrative

Without specifying it explicitly, Ricoeur is working his way through the maze of the differences between statements (written and oral) and narratives (imaginary and historical, present and past). His interest on the subject may be prompted by the fact that the Bible is listened to, and read in the Church, as being primarily a live narrative*, but (under the influence of Western philosophy) is often confused with a collection of (necessarily true or ‘ scientific’) statements about the past, or at best with a dramatic but imaginary narrative.

*A live narrative is a narrative in which, because the hearer(s) inhabits the dramatic history narrated, the hearer(s) is presently exposed, and has to respond to, what is narrated. (Example: The Martians have arrived, in Orson Welles’s broadcast). This is not the case with imaginary narratives and even with historical narratives. The closest we come to the case of the biblical narrative as live is the case of the history of one’s country or one’s family, within which the hearer or reader situates himself. The difference with biblical narratives, however, is that, because of the nature of the divine protagonist of the biblical narratives, the hearer is as exposed to His action as the past participants were. By contrast, in historical narratives the past is simply past, however much the hearer may be in a relationship of continuity with the past events.  

In his attempt to create a scientific theology after the Aristotelian model, Aquinas read the Bible as a collection of scientific, objective, a-temporal, written, necessarily true judgments-statements rather than as, fundamentally, a dramatic narrative. Such,  in his view, had to be the case, given his Aristotelian conviction that only statements can be true or false, as well as his Christian conviction that God’s word is necessarily true. Aquinas’ theory of reference in the case of statements is that the assent is given to the reality stated (be it intelligible as a theorem or factual as a historical event or a perceptual object), not to the  locution or statement itself. Thus real is what is to be assented to. Needless to say, concerning particular historical facts, Aquinas inherited from the Greeks the classical difficulties as to their truth, given their temporality and contingency (See Summa Th , q. 1, a. 2). Indeed, within the Greek representational-specular conception of truth, something can be true only if it is eternal and necessary, for a temporal truth ceases to be true and a changing object ceases to be real, which would either make the statements of faith uncertain or render them false in time.

As for the disastrous success of Aquinas’ attempt to create a scientific theology (that is, to submit historical revelation to the strictures of the Greek representational notion of knowledge),  it is attested by the very early lamentation “Sicut liber historiarum solebat legi!” (by contrast to statements and stated doctrines to be assented to). Now, however, as ther ein explained, the storical theologian has been for the most part replaced by the theologian-philosopher who deals in objective statements. It is indirectly attested by the strong but clumsy resistance of the series of theologians quoted by Chenu. It is indirectly attested too, at least in my opinion, in the nineteenth century by Kierkegaard and his desperate effort to clarify the confusion that led to the loss of the faith of the grandfather described in the Concluding Unscientific Postscript, for. this shows the persistence of the confusion.*  I think that the confusion hasn’t been fully dispelled yet by the end of the twentieth century, as shown by the supposedly scientific departments of theology and philosophy in Catholic universities.

*[Wherein lies the confusion? An enlightening indication comes from Orson Welles’s braodcast of The War of the Worlds . The confusion here is between live and not live narrative. More concretely, what was meant by the narrator as a not live, imaginary  narrative, was heard as a live pre-narrative. What name are we to give to this confusion?]

Merleau-Ponty wrote that depth is the most existential of the three dimensions . Why? Because it is the dimension of the object that involves an implicit reference to the perceiving subject as exposed to the perceived, not to the perceived objects among themselves. Actually, the three dimensions involve such a reference, for all perception is perspectival. What I wanted to say is that depth is the dimension that involves an implicit reference to the subject as exposed to the object and of the object as exposed to the subject, which reveals the perceiving subject as agent-patient ‘in the midst of the world,’ to use Sartre’s expression . When this dimension is thematized it results not in a statement or a collection of TRUE (REPRESENTATIONAL) statements, but in a dramatic narrative.

Hume realized that the perception of an object involved expectations on the perceiver’s part of the hidden powers of the object, that is, of its conduct, its actions--and, consequently, the effects it would produce. Now, these effects may be effects produced by the object on other objects of perception, in which case objective observation would have its appropriate place. But–and this is a distinction that Hume did not make--sometimes the effects expected are effects on the perceiving subject, either directly or indirectly. In this case objective observation is out of place, since the effects expected are favorable or unfavorable, desired or feared, depending on the needs and desires of the involved subject. Sheer curiosity–what Aristotle called “the disinterested desire to know,” may be the fundamental and appropriate attitude on the part of an invulnerable knower  (as Aristotle implicitly assumes the human subject to be in the Metaphysics and in the Analytica Posteriora because of his visual, representational conception of knowledge) or in the particular case in which only inanimate objects in a context of non-exposure are involved. But sheer curiosity–the attitude of scientific objectivity–is, or at least should be, displaced by the attitude of  ”concern” when either the perceiving subject or other animate objects of perception worthy of concern are going to be affected by the powers of the perceived object in question.*

* This, I think, may throw light on the obscure distinction between speculative and practical reason .

To the expected action of the object in the particular case corresponds, in the attitude of concern because of the agent’s or of other patients’s exposure, a disposition to act on the perceiver’s part. This disposition to act may lead to automatic action in the case of inherited instinct, or to quasi automatic action in the case of past successful experience in coping with the object, or, given time and capacity, to elaborately projected action to prevent the effects foreseen or to bring about other desired effects.

The purpose of these considerations is to bring to the fore the differences between narration and description, between ‘factual’ and ‘dramatic,’ between oral and written, between ‘live’ and ‘not live’ statements. We humans are exposed to the surrounding world. Our perception is perspectival. We grasp the world as surrounding us, as enveloping us. Any object we perceive is either above or below us, in front or behind us, to our right or to our left, near or far away from us. [ Here enters depth as existential ]. In other words, the objects we perceive indicate us as being in the midst of the world as the vulnerable, concerned center of it, since all distances unfold from us and toward us. I am the center –though by no means the only center--of the world. “ Here ” is where I am, and from my here all “there” and “over there” is determined. The same applies to temporal distances. “ Now ” is my present, and by reference to my present all “past” and “future” is determined.

Thus I am not in the midst of the world as a voyeur but as an agent-patient, exposed to the world and having the world exposed to me. Hence my space is, as Sartre says, furrowed by the ways of my projects, and my time is consequently dramatic. Indeed, my future is underpinned by desires and fears, and my past has the inchoate structure of a history because it is marked by successes and fears across time. This is the dramatic structure of experience preserved by the narrative genre  as well as by theatrical plays . Narratives and plays, as indicated by Ricoeur, reconstruct the temporal sequence of the agonists with their open future, particularly of the person chosen by the narrator as protagonist.

By its very nature, a narrative is always about the past and in the past tense. (By contrast, dramatic ordinary oral statements, which are pre-narrative because they constitute the basis for future dramatic narratives, are in the present tense ). What is narrated is past in relation to the narrator and to the hearer. If the narrator and the hearer are not present to each other, the hearer or reader has to gather the narrator’s intention (partly on the basis of what is narrated, and partly on the basis of the particular literary genre in which the narrative appears –for instance, the Gospels are written by followers of Jesus and with an explicit confessional intention) in order to grasp the relevance for him or her of what is narrated. In other words, narratives refer to the dramatic events narrated. Who is referred? The hearer or spectator or reader. But how , concretely, is this person referred to? This depends on the way in which the person is supposed to be situated in relation to the events narrated , as well as on the way in which the person grasps his or her own situation in relation to those events and to the narrator. This, it seems to me, is what Frei means by “application” (pp. 303 and 306 above), namely “the significance” of the events narrated for the hearer.  In the particular case of the Bible, as put by Frei, “The Bible becomes a ‘witness’ to a history, rather than a narrative text. Its meaning is a unitary complex, consisting of a history of saving events, the history of the witnesses’ faithful response to them and finally the present faithful stance toward that complex history as a present and future reality” (p. 181).

Applicative perception and the case of the Skeptics and of Nietzsche *

(*All perception is interpretation, but interpretation by an involved, vulnerable participant or a community of such participants). (Cf. my 99-skepticism, on Nietzsche’s skepticism).

Before application of a written or oral text, in which case what is referred to is mediated by a speaker or writer, there is the fundamental case of application of perception (of the one who experiences the surrounding world before making objective statements (to himself --Plato’s silent speech--or to others) about the experienced: we perceive as exposed agents-patients, not as needless, invulnerable, transcendent voyeurs. This is to say that application is not an additional operation to perception (as different from scientific or objective,  abstract or conceptual,  observation for purposes of reaching the truth). PERCEPTION ITSELF IS APPLICATIVE, for what we perceive indicates us as agents-patients in practical relation to the perceived (as shown by emotions, deliberation and actions) . The ringing phone is heard as to be answered or as not to be answered by me , not merely as a ringing phone, as a purely objective phenomenon to be registered or informed of. A noise at my home in the middle of the night startles me because I hear it as the announcement of a threatening presence to which I have to respond.

This is why “ Pyrrho was frightened by a dog, thus abandoning his fundamental principle of indifference. He found that ‘it is not easy entirely to strip oneself of human weakness’” (Mosser, “Should the Skeptic Live his Skepticism?” Manuscrito, XXI, 1998, p. 71). And Hume in Dialogues concerning Natural Religion has Cleantes say: “Whether your skepticism be as absolute and sincere as you pretend, we shall learn by and by, when the company breaks up; we shall then see whether you go out at the door or the window, and whether you really doubt if your body has gravity or can be injured by its fall... And this consideration... may, I think, fairly serve to abate our ill-will to this humorous sect of skeptics” (quoted by Mosser, p. 71). What Pyrrho should have found is that in situation of exposure, which is our situation, one is actually exposed and has to respond, because not responding is already a way of responding. Thus the so-called “phenomena” and “noumena,” which belong in the specular-representational context, emerge in the reflective moment, and there is no room for them within the dramatic space of perception.

What distinguishes perception from other knowing modes is its imminency, its urgency, since, as applicative, it places me, vulnerable agent-patient, in contact of reciprocal exposure with what here and now surrounds me, what can here an now affect me and to which I in one way or another have to respond. It is for me to determine how to respond, at least in the cases in which instinct doesn’t overcome me.  This is the difference concerning application between animals and people, namely, that animals are creatures of mere instinct. In us, by contrast, application is, at least sometimes, deliberative and connected with free desire. . About being or not being afraid Pyrrho could do nothing because the perception of the fierce dog indicated him to himself as an imminent victim of the dog. Thus Hume was wrong: there is no required passage from is to ought. The original perceptual mode is not the indicative but the gerundive, which is neither merely indicative nor merely imperative, neither neutrally informative nor blindly or unjustifiably imperative. Another way of putting it is to say that perception is itself applicative. What Pyrrho could do something about, within limits, (and animals can’t), was his conduct in response to the dog: he could, at least theoretically, stay where he was and allow the dog to bite him, or run away, or himself attack the dog. But he couldn’t abstain from responding to the dog, for his very abstaining would be a deliberate choice of his own response to the dog. Pyrrho’s mistake, like Hume’s, was to confuse perception (with its essentially applicative character) with curious, objective observation of “phenomena” for the sake of reaching the truth, thereby missing and badly misinterpreting their unavoidable situation as vulnerable participants.

“... the Skeptics,” says Mates as quoted by Mosser on p. 58, “were hoping to achieve ataraxia [tranquillity or peace of mind] by  resolving the anomaly of phenomena and noumena,” that is, by coming to know what really is, not merely what appears. However, “being unable to do this, they suspended judgment . But then, by chance as it were, when they were suspending judgment the ataraxia followed, as a shadow follows the body” ( Ibid. , p. 58). [ WHY? BECAUSE THEY DIDN’T HAVE TO DECIDE, AND THEREFORE WERE NOT RESPONSIBLE?] What they failed to realize is that they were confronted with phenomena only because, misled by philosophers, they mistook perception for objective observation.

Did the Pyrrhonian skeptics realize that there is no “what is”, but only “what is to be responded to “ and, therefore, relative to the goals of each agent at a given time? Not at all. They assumed that behind the appearance there had to be a nature, an essence, what is necessarily and is necessarily so, for otherwise knowledge and truth would be impossible. This is why they were so intent in resolving the anomaly of phenomena and noumena (as much as Plato was, and for similar reasons). They even attributed the primacy to the former and deemed the latter secondary and subordinate to the former. (Or, like Socrates, reduced right conduct to truth, to right knowledge of what is the case).   Like Hume in his reflective-philosophical moment, they thought that the neutrally informative, the ‘is,” had to precede the “ought.” (Aquinas obviously shared that view , since in one of the first articles of the Summa Theologica he writes that in order to pursue something as an end one needs to know it as being the end). What the Pyrrhonian skeptics call “phenomenon” is the mere neutral appearance, in practical terms unrelated to the perceiver because of the latter’s objective, uninvolved attitude, while what they call “noumenon” is the underlying, ‘real factual reality,’ equally neutral and unrelated to the perceiver.

The very fact that the Skeptics speak the phenomenon-noumenon language indicates that they are Humean. Ataraxia , for them, is theoretical ; it consists in resolving the anomaly of phenomena and noumena. From success in this theoretical undertaking, from coming to know or be present to the “is,” they expected an automatic passage to the correct “ought,” thereby avoiding the agony of deliberating, deciding, acting, and bearing responsibility As if a reality to which they were not even potentially exposed –for such is the noumenon as much as the phenomenon– could elicit a conduct, any conduct whatever (which is what Hume realized).  In this they were in the mainstream of the whole history of philosophy: for  “Western philosophy characteristically takes ontology –the study of essences, of what is– as first philosophy; upon this ostensibly sure foundation it then constructs an ethics. Levinas’s originality and importance lies in the attempt he makes to reverse this hypothesis: ethics is first philosophy ” (David Jopling, “Levinas on Desire, Dialogue and the Other,” American Catholic Philosop[hical Quarterly, vol. LXV Autumn 91, Issue 4, p. 406).  

Having failed, as was to be expected, in their attempt to move from phenomenon to noumenon, from which passage, like Socrates, they expected certainty as to the way to act , the Pyrrohnian skeptics shifted to a curious practical ataraxia : all action was blind, since it was not grounded in infallible knowledge of reality; consequently, they didn’t have to determine responsibly  or rationally how to live, since any determination –not being grounded in necessary immutable knowledge-- would be equally blind and irrational. Hence, like Montaigne, they could find tranquillity in skipping personal decision as to how to conduct themselves by becoming fideistic in matters of cult and ethics, or by simply following cultural custom –not because it was necessarily better but because it was easier than trying unsuccessfully to live their skepticism and not to be afraid of dogs.

And what about Nietzsche? He disapproves of the Pyrrhonians because of his distaste for slave morality. Obviously he sees in the will to power as he understands it a better alternative to their passive, accommodating, resigned tranquillity.  But is it better because Nietzsche prefers it, or because it ought to be preferred, because it is really better? To this question there is no definite answer on Nietzsche’s part. And the question is important. Indeed, if it is better only because Nietzsche prefers it, then the Pyrrhonians’ tranquillity is better for them or for whomever prefers it, because they prefer it. And if it is better because it is preferable in the sense that it ought to be preferred, then Nietzsche is not  living his perspectivism. [ Not necessarily. Perhaps, as Camus suggests, it is a matter of gaining one’s dignity by acting nobly and with self-respect in an absurd world in which all is a matter of preferences ].

Back to Mosser: “To conclude, ...is Nietzsche a skeptic? As Poellner has correctly noted, there is ‘a pervasive sceptical strand of thought’ in Nietzsche’s mature work. At the same time he rejects the conception of truth that makes Cartesian methodological skepticism (as well as the earlier, all embracing Pyrrhonic skepticism) even possible . While he admires the approach of Pyrrhonists, and their courage in suspending judgment relative to all dogmatic claims , Nietzsche is harshly critical of what he sees as their moral cowardice in passively adopting the customs and conventions of society. Nietzsche’s skepticism –if it qualifies as skepticism – is rather a method grounded precisely in the rejection of those conventions, seeking to ground genuine philosophical inquiry in courage [?] , manifest in the will to power and tested by one’s embrace of eternal recurrence. Thus Nietzschean skepticism is a skepticism of the future [?] , only for those brave enough to pursue questions in the face of likely futility while maintaining their commitment to fröliche Wissensshaft . And so in the end, in answer to the question ‘Is Nietzsche a skeptic?’ we get an answer that might be formulated especially to please the Pyrrhonic skeptic–yes and no” ( Ibid. , pp. 78-79).*

* Nietzsche himself may not have had a definite answer to this question, for he was confused. But the answer can be definite. Skepticism is constituted by the rejection of the possibility of moving from appearances to the knowledge of reality. Hence, for the skeptic, all assertions of reality are dogmatic. Nietzsche, however, escaped the paradigm within which knowledge, truth, phenomenon and noumenon have a place. So he cannot be, strictly speaking, a skeptic.    

Truth as correspondence

“It is in the concern with truth, and its attainability, that we see the two strands of skepticism –Pyrrhonic and its more modern, post-Cartesian version, come together... both approaches are grounded in the assumption of a correspondence theory of truth, where a given claim is true if and only if it corresponds in the appropriate way to the world, or to the ‘facts’; each, for different reasons, and with different results, asserts that ‘truth, so construed, is unreachable. This is not to suggest that the ancient skeptics employed the correspondence theory, but that it forms an essential part of the dogmatic attitude they hoped to call into question... Frederick Schmitt points out ‘ The correspondence theory is ancient and was endorsed, explicitly or implicitly, by nearly every philosopher who had much to say about truth before the year 1800... Nietzsche’s own claims relative to ‘truth’ are notoriously difficult to reconcile, and form the basis for a charge of self-referential inconsistency, as discussed by Clarke and Poellner, among others... Yet it is clear that in addition to endorsing the doubts raised about ‘truth’ by both ancient and modern skeptics, Nietzsche seeks to go beyond those doubts and examine their ramifications... As Andre Comte-Sponvill has noted, ‘It remains that Nietzsche always, or almost always, refused to consider himself a skeptic and that his criticism of the idea of truth goes beyond the critiques of Montaigne and Hume, and for good reason: Montaigne and Hume never critique truth as such’ ” (Mosser, pp. 69-70). *

[* Nor did Kant critique truth as such. He just redefined it as a result of his Copernican revolution. As a consequence, he made knowledge possible and truth attainable within the appropriate limits. In other words, he thought he had genuinely answered Hume and transcended him.]

“....Nietzsche’s strategy precludes that which is presupposed by the Pyrrhonists: that there is some ‘fact of the matter which humans, for a wide variety of reasons, are unable to establish. In contrast, Nietzsche wants to abandon any traditional conception of truth that might ground such a presupposition...” (Mosser, p. 61).

Why do we spontaneously presuppose that there is some –however attainable or unattainable-- fact of the matter? Because we conceive of knowledge as expressed in judgments and statements, and therefore think that judgments and statements are true in the sense of depicting and corresponding to what is the case. Thus, when two speakers make two different, conflicting perceptual statements, they compare what is stated to the reality they see in order to find out which of the two statements, if any, is true. If the reality seen corresponds to what is said or stated, the statement is judged to be true; otherwise, it is deemed to be false. (I say ‘the reality seen,’ not ‘the reality perceived,’ because only visual statements can be compared, or at least ascertained as true or false by independent witnesses. If I say that the water is cold and you say that it is not, we have no more evidence for our divergent judgments than our subjective sensations). What we fail to realize is that  in this kind of reflective discourse about the truth or validity of perceptual knowledge we have shifted from the practical gerundive character of perception, connected with needs and desires, to a purely objective, contemplative mode, and we attribute to perception this contemplative character. From there, in the reflective, generalizing philosophical moment, we pass to the notion of statements as expressing purely objective judgments that correspond or fail to correspond to what is the case, and to the specular theory of knowledge, and to the view of truth as correspondence. From which it follows that orthodoxia is a necessary condition for orthopraxis, and that metaphysics is a necessary condition for ethics.

Of course there are problems with this view. Aristotle wondered why not all knowledge resulted in action, and how practical knowledge was related to speculative knowledge. Actually Hume showed the impossibility of passing from speculative knowledge to action. What Aristotle didn’t realize was that there is no such a thing as speculative knowledge, and no knowledge of essences or natures, and consequently no phenomena and noumena and no possible metaphysics. Another connected and  unresolved problem in Aristotle’s views is the problem of the validity of perceptual knowledge. For this very reason he cannot deal efficiently with the issue of practical knowledge being genuine knowledge, though he pragmatically takes it for valid. Indeed, how can a knowledge in which the correspondence is not exact (not only not necessary but merely accidental) be knowledge? In other words,  how can a knowledge of a particular and contingent attain the certainty required for knowledge? Or, more to the point, how can   a knowledge in which no genuine correspondence obtains, and therefore no truth, be knowledge at all? Indeed, the correspondence between desire and outcome thanks to action is not correspondence, at least not in the same sense in which what is stated or known is supposed to correspond to (reproduce) the real. The instrumental correspondence between a key and a lock or between a hammer and a nail to obtain a desired goal is not at all of the same kind as the specular, iconic  correspondence between the image in the mirror and the original mirrored, which is the generating metaphor of the specular theory of knowledge within which truth as correspondence has its place.

 

Attempting to bring an end to and escape the disintegrating Greek epistemic specular tradition to which I just referred, Nietzsche gave up on knowledge as correspondence and on the very notion of truth. By the same token, no phenomena in the sense of mere visual appearances and no noumena in the sense of genuine and certain realities behind or under the phenomena.*

[* But did Nietzsche preserve the practical, deliberative tie between the agent-patient ( as a historical, institutional agent-patient) and the surrounding world? Any agent, Nietzsche himself included, acts –and is situated– within a given tradition that shapes his or her choices in the sense of being a significant portion of  his or her ‘from where’. The total transvaluation of all values is an illusion, for it amounts to the negative of the positive constituted by the tradition to which one belongs and rejects. The revolt against slave morality results in master morality –and such is Nietzsche’s morality.]

Certainly Nietzsche is not a naive realist, for naive realism belongs in the paradigm of the specular theory of knowledge. Nor is he a phenomenalist floating in a universe of appearances, for the same reason. Indeed, he doesn’t try to go out through the window but through the door (see above, p. 8, on Hume’s criticism of skepticism). But Hume used this argument to show that the skeptic believes that the door and the window are actually outside there as ‘objects’ corresponding to the mind’s perception, although their existence cannot be proven or demonstrated, and thus that skeptics don’t live their skepticism.  Nietzsche, however, has given up the very notion of objectivity, of something factual out there, within which the notion of specular correspondence to what is out there he criticizes, has a place. He has left behind the paradigm within which  skepticism for lack of possible correspondence had a place. A window, for Nietzsche, is not anymore what looks like a window but what looks and  functions as a window to an agent capable of using a window, and a door what looks and functions as a door to an agent capable of using a door. Both the window and the door were conceived and made as instruments, with an assigned instrumental value within a given culture, and their reality is therefore appropriately gauged in terms of their instrumental value for agents who have a use for doors and windows, for agents whose needs and projects can include windows and doors and be satisfied or enhanced by doors and windows. This is not to say that windows don’t look like windows and doors like doors, but that their appearance is appearance for an agent situated in a certain utilitarian relationship to the window and the door. This is to say that their appearance announces their function in relation to an exposed, needful and vulnerable agent-patient , that this is the way in which the door and the window are perceived by human agents, that is, as potential instruments rather than as ‘objects,’as phenomena, or as Humean collections of sensible qualities-appearances that may or may not be supported by the substance-window and the substance-door. It is not a matter of beliefs-that but of practical expectations and successful actions on the part of specific agents..

The question of truth arises only reflectively, so to say, when a perceptual statement is disagreed with by others or when the flow of action is interrupted because the practical expectation is frustrated by the object. When a statement is disagreed with it seems that I have a belief-that about the object, which is expressed in the stated. But do I really have a belief-that, or rather a set of unstated practical expectations in relation to the object that lead me to act accordingly in relation to the object? A belief-that, as Hume would argue, is not connected with action. Furthermore, if the set of expectations in which my disposition to act is grounded is a mere belief-that unrelated to action, how come that when the flow of action is interrupted and I cease to believe I cannot act anymore? And how come that I realize that the other doesn’t  believe me when he or she doesn’t respond practically to what I stated? Again, it is not a matter of passing from “is” to “ought,” from specular knowledge to action because no specular knowledge precedes action, much less is it connected with it. What we call knowledge is practical, because it is a matter of practical expectations that, under the appropriate circumstances, motivate us to act.

Truth and falsity are predicates of cognitive acts --judgments and statements, in particular–not of objects. This is why one can ask whether Santa is real or not, whether Santa exists or not, but not whether Santa is true or false, because Santa is not a judgment or a statement. Judgments and statements refer to the real, to what is the case in reality; but the real, what is the case in reality, is itself neither true nor false; only statements and judgments are.

What is meant by truth? Truth is the correspondence between what is judged or stated and what is the case. From where or how does this notion of correspondence between what a knower judges or affirms and what is actually the case originate? Let us take things on the perceptual level. If we never erred, if we never made perceptual mistakes, the notion of knowledge as correspondence would have never arisen. It is when we make a mistake and realize it that we discover that our judgments and statements are not always true. But why do we think that truth consists in correspondence? Because we compare what we perceive now with what we had previously judged or stated and we realize that what we had judged or stated doesn’t correspond with what we now clearly see to be the case. In fact, however, the agent usually discovers his or her mistakes when he or she acts and the expected effects do not obtain, not when he or she looks at that to which he or she has to respond. And in order to act the agent doesn’t have to state what is the case, to have a belief-that.

This idea of truth implies that judgments are, in one way or another, re-presentations of what is the case, and that their truth or falsity depends on the fidelity or lack thereof of the representation. Perception, we assume, automatically produces belief in the fidelity of the representation. However, even assuming that there is representation,  mere belief is not knowledge, since, as proven by the fact that we sometimes make mistakes, not all perceptions are correct. Hence philosophical epistemology –the exploration of the validity of knowledge. From there a full-fledged  theory of knowledge that begins with a radical distinction between belief and knowledge; that conceives of knowledge as accurate representation of reality, that is, of what is actually the case; that defines truth as correspondence between what is judged or stated and what is the case; and that attempts to determine the conditions under which judgments and statements are certainly correct.

As for the validity of the conception of knowledge as correspondence, the history of philosophy is enlightening. Didn’t Kant, after centuries of futile attempts by his predecessors, find himself forced to give up on the correspondence of the mental representation to reality through his “Copernican revolution” because of the radical impossibility of the mind going out of itself to attain the thing in itself? And didn’t it entail the end of metaphysics both as knowledge of extramental reality and as knowledge of such superphysical realities as God and the soul and freedom? And didn’t Kant’s last-ditch, desperate attempt to preserve a version, on the level of practical reason,  of the correspondence theory of knowledge ultimately result in  the skepticism of post-modernism? This is the context within which Nietzsche is to be understood, with his rejection of the representative conception of knowledge and, consequently, of truth as known or justified correspondence. But can Nietzsche’s case against philosophical epistemology be completed to make full sense and, perhaps, to show the relative arbitrariness or unpersuasiveness of his own position?

       

Perception and action

As previously indicated, truth and falsity are notions that have a place only within a conception of knowledge as correspondence. Now this conception, that takes its origin from the fact of perceptual errors, takes for granted that perceiving is having in the mind an image of what is the case in reality. But is this assumption indisputable? Once we begin reflecting on epistemic issues, as philosophers do, on the occasion of perceptual mistakes or of disagreements with the stated perceptual beliefs of other people, the assumption appears indisputable because it is necessary in order to explain the cases of genuine and merely apparent knowledge. But perception itself is not reflective and may not involve correspondence at all.

To start moving in a more promising direction, let us shift our focus from the supposed connection of perception with knowledge as correspondence to the connection of perception with action. That there is such a connection is obvious, to the point that we might even be justified in considering perception as an integral moment of action, for there is a strong correlation between what we perceive and the way we act. If I am hungry and I perceive bread I eat the bread I perceive. My action of grabbing the bread and eating it doesn’t only follow temporally my perception of bread but is definitely correlated with it, for if I had perceived a book rather than bread I wouldn’t have eaten it. Now, steeped as we are in the representational view of knowledge, the temptation here is to isolate perception from action, to objectify it in reflection, and to assume that perceiving amounts to having a mental image of bread that corresponds to what is actually there , and that in order to attempt to eat the bread I need first to perceive the bread, that is, to have a mental image that corresponds to the bread that is outside there. But this is a temptation that arises from the assumption of the correspondence theory of knowledge of which we are trying to get rid. So far, however, considering not perception by itself but in its connection with action, we have found no trace of representational correspondence. Where, indeed, is the correspondence between perception and action? If correspondence there is between the two, it is a matter of practical correlation between perception and action, not of representational correspondence between the bread perceived and the action of grabbing and eating it, between which there is no resemblance at all, (anymore than there is resemblance between the looks of an object and its secret powers, as noticed by Hume), but adaptation of the action to the goal made possible by perception guided by desire. (The connection is between desire and satisfactor).

Now, this intrinsic connection of perception with action is systematically ignored in the conception of knowledge as representation, for it considers perception by itself,  not as a moment of action.* Actually, not only is it ignored but it is rendered  impossible. Indeed, as observed by Hume, there is no possible connection between the mere representation of the object in which perception supposedly consists and the action that follows the perception. In other words, if the representational theory of knowledge were correct there would be no possible connection of perception with action. Yet this connection is implicitly acknowledged in the very demand that the skeptic live his or her skepticism , for the skeptic uses doors and windows while denying that he knows they are there.

* Aristotle is the first to distinguish speculative from practical knowledge. The view of knowledge he received from Plato was the speculative one, with the necessity of the eternal Ideas to guarantee the correspondence on which the validity of knowledge depends. From that Aristotle distinguished practical knowledge. However, he already had the model of knowledge –the princeps analogatum -- as correspondence (though perhaps not iconic correspondence),  and the ideal of knowledge as necessary correspondence (or intentional identity). To that model he tried –unsuccessfully-- to assimilate the “imprecise” practical knowledge. And since, considered by itself, speculative knowledge had no explicit correlation to action, Aristotle attempted to preserve the undeniable connection between knowledge and action by making speculative knowledge a necessary condition of action–a necessary “first” whose intelligibility Hume would denounce.

However, if the connection of perception with action is not a matter of iconic similarity between the two, and nevertheless there is a definite correlation between the two, of what kind is the correlation?  Hume came close to specifying this correlation between knowledge and action when he spoke of expectations –which means simply expectations, not known, formulated, ‘thetically known’ expectations, in Sartre’s idiolect-- , though Hume ultimate skepticism shows that he never escaped the correspondence theory of knowledge. According to him –and this applies as well to animals as to people– we are so built that from objects that have a similar look we expect similar effects. In animals, this expectation is purely practical, inscribed, so to say,  in their bodies as innate or acquired disposition to respond. In us humans, the expectation can be thematized and formulated in so many words, which is useful to transmit practical knowledge. However, it is not an expectation by being stated but by actually functioning as an expectation rather than as a “belief” in the appropriate circumstances.

Before moving ahead, let me respond to an objection which, if not met, would render futile what I am about to say. The objection is: Doesn’t the very fact that we recognize that certain objects look similar show that we actually have iconic images of them in the mind? And is not the case of memory exemplary in this regard, since I clearly remember my mother’s face that corresponds to the way she looked when I perceived her? The answer is, in the first place,  that memory differs from perception. More to the point, however: memories are memories when they actually function as memories, not when they are analyzed by themselves, in isolation from action. In other words, we have to guard ourselves against the temptation to commit regarding memory the same mistake we commit regarding perception when we isolate it from action. This mistake arises from reflection, which isolates and objectifies memories as well as perceptions..

The very demand to live one’s skepticism implies that there is a connection between beliefs and action. But, to repeat, we have no beliefs and no pictures but, rather, sets of practical expectations based on past experience, for objective pictures are unconnected with action. Hence Nietzsche was right in denying his own skepticism, which would imply beliefs-pictures and the correspondence theory of knowledge and truth. As for Pyrrho ’s complaint about his weakness in the presence of the dog, it shows that knowledge is not a matter of beliefs dogmatically assumed to correspond to the object perceived. Like everybody else, the skeptic is an agent who, thanks to past experience, has definite sets of practical expectations concerning dogs and windows and doors –expectations that, through spontaneous emotions, tend to motivate his or her actions in order to achieve unpremeditated goals. (Yet, because humans are capable of deliberation, and because their very desires and passions are to a large extent shaped by their culture rather than by inherited automatisms and individual, unmediated experience, they do not respond to the surrounding world with the uniformity of animals).

Because we are linguistic creatures, we people are creatures of nurture more than of nature,. The needs and desires that it is appropriate to satisfy, and the appropriate way of satisfying them, are initially defined by the culture and interiorized by the members of the culture which surveys the conformity of its members to its customs or approved ways through positive and negative reinforcements. Initially the individual, in an analogically comparable way to the animal, negotiates conflicts between the id and the superego by sheer weight of inclination, though later the individual may reach a cvertain level of deliberative independence fro the group.

Does the fact that there is no truth-correspondence mean that any response is equivalent to any other, that is, equally appropriate? As an agent, nobody is willing to live by that principle.  It is not even possible, particularly in matters related to survival, as shown by Pyrrho ’s example. Past experience automatically counts and creates definite expectations. As Hume said, from similar objects we expect similar effects. Without experience of effects, we cannot infer the effect from the looks of what is present (though nature makes certain things instinctively repellent or attractive, particularly in the case of animals). However,  with experience it is different, for experience enriches the notion or ‘looks’ of the object insofar as the object is capable of satisfying need and desire. Action is guided by need and desire, and not all actions are equally conducive to satisfy need and desire, though, as already said, the needs and desires of people differ within limits, depending on people and circumstances. If the action is inappropriate, it will prove unsatisfactory to the agent and, as a consequence, that action won’t be repeated. The next time the action will be different and, hopefully, a more appropriate action will take place, either automatically (animals) or projected. For, unlike animals, as Marx realized, people can transform intelligently their environment through inventive work .

A culture, however, is not simply a collection of unrelated and arbitrary  customs. Furthermore, the customs are not only related to biological success, for the flourishing of people is also related to what Maslow would call ‘higher needs.’ Moreover –and on this Maslow is totally silent-- people are tellers of stories –their own and other people’s. And they listen to other people’s stories, in which they are included, and discuss with them their stories. In this way, they give some unity to their lives and make communitary sense of them through shared histories.

Stories, histories, and shared histories

As I just said, a culture is not simply a motley collection of unrelated and arbitrary customs. Its injunctions and taboos are purposeful, at least initially, though things change in time and what was initially purposeful may become purposeless or even counterproductive. Cultures, however,  are composed of different groups whose interests may differ and whose power is unequal. There is, therefore, a constant negotiation among the powers that be about customs, particularly in contemporary societies. But what concerns me now is the way in which people make ultimate sense of their lives as members of communities through singular shared histories rather than through universal, objective systems to be assented to , and how these histories themselves affect the negotiations and their outcomes, for they function a little like maps by means of which people situate themselves in relation to the world, though maps of a very special kind.

         Whatever has to be said on this subject, a preliminary question has to be addressed. Are there not true and false stories, stories that are meant as true (histories), imaginary and real stories?  And if so, are we not back to square one in terms of truth as correspondence?

We have to distinguish between correspondence of statements with what is the case and correspondence of stories with reality, assuming the idea of correspondence applies to narratives. In the former case, the correspondence is iconic (one to one, point to point) and purely objective (independent of need and desire). By the same token, the assenting subject is only intellectually related to what is the case, with no distances of exposure. Hence, in this framework, the truth has to be one and potentially universal (that is, valid for all knowers). Indeed, as indicated by the Cartesian coordinates that situate facts in space at successive times, they apply to a succession of two-dimensional pictures in time. At any given time the objects referred to occupy a different place in the total picture of the instant and cannot be inconsistent with each other, since they are spatially different. In the next instant, the entire picture is temporally and spatially different and therefore there cannot be any inconsistency, either, among the objects contained in the picture or with the objects contained in the precedent picture.

The case is very different concerning the correspondence of stories (if the notion of correspondence can still make sense here). For here the tellers of their own past story, and about whom the story is told by others, are, so to say, within the picture as participants in the action. By the same token the content of the story, the told story, is not anymore a two-dimensional picture but an ongoing temporal drama, so that the told story is always incomplete . Until the temporal flux ceases, there can be no total, closed story, but only interpretations of the story by its past and present agents. Furthermore, the participants in the story have distances of exposure, which means that the story told and the ongoing drama are not merely objective, but shaped by the needs and desires of the tellers of the stories, who are themselves involved in the ongoing drama. Finally, as not two people  can have exactly the same environment because they themselves are environment to each other, so no told story can be complete and completely told in the first person’s voice. At least the last portion will be in the third person’s voice, for the last portion covers the death of the first person. Taking all this into consideration, it is clear that to speak of correspondence in the case of narratives would be a misnomer, a mere equivocation. But we need further clarification through a concrete example.

Take the Christian Gospels . We Catholics say that it is a true story, a history. But others who are not Christians wouldn’t call it a history. Did Jesus actually exist, and are the majority of the deeds and words attributed to him in the gospel “true’? The answers vary, from those who go even as far as to deny Jesus’s existence to those who are extreme literalists. At any rate,  it is a story written by people partial to Jesus, by people who had already recognized him as the Son of God, and so it is certainly not objective.

It is the story of an interaction, in a realistic cultural and geographical setting, between Jesus and a number of people, some of them his friends and disciples, some of them his enemies.
