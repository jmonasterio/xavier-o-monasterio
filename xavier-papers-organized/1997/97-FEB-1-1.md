(James R. Mensch, "Aristotle and the Overcoming of the Subject-Object Dichotomy," in ACP Quarterly , vol. LXV, autumn 1991, Issue # 4, p. 465).

Kant's epistemological revolution

It has often been said that the main function of the sophists was to prepare the way for Plato, and this is regularly said in such a way as to suggest that they are therefore of only limited importance. But virtually every point in Plato's thought has its starting point in his reflection upon problems raised by the sophists...

( Cornford, The Sophistic Movement , pp. 173-175).

Protagoras and Gorgias, came to despair of the possibility of  reaching knowledge. The sophists' arguments, however, must have had more weight than their opponents the lovers of wisdom or truth initially assumed, since under different shapes they have surfaced again and again through the ages. After all, was it not the impossibility of comparing the mental image with the reality imaged, or the impossibility of ever coming to know a reality in itself independently of its being known, what prompted Kant to effect his Copernican Revolution and give up the idea of truth--that is, of genuine knowledge--as the adequation of the mind to such reality? Thus there is an undeniable continuity in the history of philosophy from the sophists on, in that at least until Kant philosophers were still responding to the questions raised by the Sophists in the sophists' own terms, that is, sharing the Sophists' assumptions. Indeed, the problems inherited by philosophers from  the sophists presuppose the sophists' epistemological assumptions about knowledge, truth, and reality.

What about Kant? Kant went along with the Sophists as to the impossibility of knowledge as representation, while nonetheless joining forces with the philosophers as to the possibility of knowledge and truth. In the process, however, he changed the assumption that knowledge was a re-presentation of the real, that truth consisted in the correspondence between the representation and the reality represented, and that reality was indenpendent of its being known. *

* Certainly Kant changed the notion of reality or at least of the object of knowledge as something in itself and existing independently of its being known. Consequently he changed the notion of truth as correspondence with or similarity to that kind of reality.

How Kant changed the notion of reality as something in itself independently of its being known or even knowable may be grasped by contrasting him to Aristotle. Both for Aristotle and for Kant knowledge and reality are correlates. For both knowable and real are convertible in that whatever is knowable is real and whatever is real is knowable. Yet their views are radically different. For the commonsensical Aristotle, there is a primacy of being over knowledge, an independence or selbständigkeit of being, and a dependence of knowledge on being. For Aristotle, something is knowable because it is, and it is even if it is not actually known. Being is conceivable without knowledge, but knowledge is not conceivable without being, because knowledge cannot be but knowledge of being. For Kant, by contrast, there is no logical primacy of being over knowledge: something is because it is knowable or "objective," that is, object of possible knowledge. Thus there is no selbständigkeit or conceptual independence of what is from what is knowable. Being and being knowable are conceptually identical.  

Seeing as the model of knowing

For all the sweeping epistemological changes brought about by Kant, there was, however, one epistemic assumption left intact by his Copernican Revolution, an assumption that had been common to both the Greek sophists and the philosophers and which Kant himself shared with both groups, namely, that knowing consists in a certain kind of seeing and that being-known consists in a certain kind of being seen.  

Obviously, we are dealing with a metaphor, for few if any people in the epistemic tradition have claimed that only what is visible in the literal sense is real. After all, sight is only one of the five senses, and nobody would say that blind people who do not see anything in the literal sense are thereby radically incapable of knowing, or less capable of knowing what is the case than the rest of us. Though deprived of sight, blind people sense or experience the world around us no less than the rest of us. They even use as the rest of us such expressions as "I see" or "Evidently," which shows their metaphorical rather than literal character. Moreover, even those people who consider knowledge to be a matter of intellectual judgment rather than a mere affair of the senses share the assumption of visibility of which I am speaking.

What is distinctive in the epistemic visual model? Not only that it pre-defines the real as visible, as something that appears or can appear visually in presence (however differently the visibility may be conceived: as a clear and distinct idea, as object of sensible intuition, as object of intellection, as object object of universal and necessary judgment, etc.), so that what is not 'visible' in the specified way is is deemed not real, *  but that it correlatively predifines the status of the knower as a seeer, that is, as a non-situated, non-involved, invulnerable, neutral or purely objective, desireless or passionless, pure voyeur. In this view, therefore, knowledge is distinguished and divorced from feeling and acting.

 * One of the fundamental questions we humans can and should ask, according to Kant, is "What can I know?" Needless to say, the answer to this question depends on what one assumes that knowing is, and on whether or not one believes that the epistemic subject is capable of living up to the demands of what is to be known. Thus if, with Plato, one assumes that knowledge cannot be knowledge without being absolutely true and certain, and that only eternal, immutable, purely intelligible realities can be object of knowledge so conceived, then either one is constrained to maintain that such realities exist and that the epistemic subject is capable of attaining them, as Plato seems to have assumed at a certain point in his career, or one would have to to answer the question "What can I know?" with the word "Nothing."  For his part Kant would have disagreed with this answer without, however, agreeing with Plato, for he began with assumptions as to what knowledge is and what is possible to the epistemic subject that are very different from Pato's. Which proves my point: the answer to the question "What can I know?" depends on what one assumes knowledge, reality, and the epistemic subject to be.  

Kant did not drop the epistemic visual model. Rather he reaffirmed it in what he deemed to be its only appropriate and most radical form by reducing the real or objective to what appears or can appear in "intuition," and the epistemic subject as the pure voyeur of what is capable of being intuited. In a sense, for all his Copernican revolution, Kant was not revolutionary enough, for he just introduced a modification into the visual model of knowledge which, at least in my opinion, is the radical mistake.

Sensations and feelings in Kant's Frist Critique

Kant distinguishes, at least implicitly, two sorts of affections which I will call "sensations" and "feelings." Consider the following version of a point made by Kant:

The connection between pure apperception and the categories Kant establishes through an examination of judgments. Compare the following two judgments: (1) 'Bodies have weight'; and (2) 'When I lift this body, I note a pressure on the hand that lifts it.' One difference between (1) and (2) is that (1) expresses objective knowledge about bodies, while (2) does not. In (2) no objective connection is made between the concepts 'body' and 'weight.' Such an objective connection is, however, found in (1); there we have a unity, a synthesis, of these two concepts. The concept 'body' and the concept 'weight' are, so to say, thought together in the object (i.e., in the judgment's subject). Judgment (2) is subjective, it reports only what I notice when I lift the body and implies nothing about what others notice . It says nothing at all about the concepts 'body' and 'weight' being in some way connected. Judgment (1), on the other hand, is objective. It implies that regardless of who lifts the body, that person will notice a pressure. The concepts 'body' and 'weight' are brought into an objective unity in the sense that this declared unity is independent of what is subjective, but also in the sense that the two concepts are united in an object. (Justus Harnack, Kant's Theory of Knowledge (Harcourt, Brace & World, Inc., 1967, pp. 55-56).

Following Kant, though changing his vocabulary, let us call the affection of pressure a sensation, and let us distinguish two kinds of affections, sensations and feelings. "Sensations" are those affections that are 'intentional' or informative about objects. Because the affection of pressure informs that bodies are weighty, it is a sensation. By contrast, "feelings" are affections that do not possess any informational value but are self-contained and, therefore, merely subjective, non-cognitive, purely private in the sense that they do not send the subject toward the world. Pain is a "feeling" because it is not intentional.

Because feelings have no epistemic value within his system, Kant ignores them for the most part in the Critique of Pure Reason . In fact, he positively excludes them from his theory of knowledge as having no epistemic value. In this--it is to be remarked--Kant is at one with the entire Greek epistemological tradition, that is, with the visual epistemic tradition.* But is Kant right? Might Kant not have been misled by the assumption of visibility, that is, by taking for granted that knowing is to be modelled after seeing and, correlatively, the real after the visible? Let us see.

* Insofar as practical knowledge is motivating knowledge, Aris may be deemed an exception. Insofar, however, as Aris persisted in seeing in practical knowledge a species of kowledge conceived as adaequatio intellectus ad rem, although less exact, he is not an exception to the visual tradition.

Exposure as an alternative model

Why does Kant exclude feelings from his theory of knowledge? Because they are neither intentional of object, nor themselves object; they are just a subjective affection. Indeed, no less than "objects," feelings appear, too, in that they are experienced, but they appear only as subjective affections, as private experiences, whereas sensations are--or purport to be--sensations of objects which are part of the world of the transcendental knower or, what amounts to the same, of the intersubjective world of all knowers.

In dividing affections into two classes, however, Kant ignores a third class of affections--which we usually call emotions , and which are connected with attitudes as dispositions to act in response to certain objects, and with actual actions. These affections are neither purely subjective (that is, non-intentional), as feelings are, nor yet objective in exactly the same way in which sensations are (that is, having an objective, purely knowable ('visible') correspondent or correlate. *

* Here the correlate is desirable or undesirable, which refer implicitly to a situated subject, and the correlate is not something to be contemplated but to be responded to appropriately. God, for instance, is adorandus, that is, the One to be affected kwith adoration.  

Take fear, for instance. The sensation of color is objective, in Kant's parlance, because it is not a mere affection; it is not the color of the subject but of the object. In this sense, it is  an intentional affection (though, unlike being touched, it is not experienced as passive afection). Indeed, when I am afraid of a fierce dog, I do not have a surd feeling like pain. My affection is intentional of the dog, refers to the dog as fearful, as indicated by the preposition "of." However, nothing objectively detectable (that is, purely 'visible' independently of its capacity to affect the subject) in the dog corresponds to my fear. In other words, fierceness or fearfulness is not objectively (or 'visibly' or 'perceptually') in the dog in the way in which green is in the table, for it can be experienced only by someone who can be affected by the fierce dog, someone for whom the dog is dangerous.  Yet, my fear is not a surd feeling, for it is fear of the dog, it is cognitional (in a certain sense) of the fierceness of the dog, not simply caused by the dog as the pain is caused by fire, though the cognition is of object for response, of gerundive. Pain is not pain "of" fire in the sense in which fear is fear "of" the dog. Fierceness is not in the dog as a visible quality, the way the dog's color is in the dog. Rather, it is a relational-practical quality, that is, a feature in relation to a certain kind of experiencing subject. Only a sensitive or affectible subject exposed to the dog's action, a subject that can both be harmed by the dog and be aware of the dog as dangerous, can be afraid of the dog, and only an object-dog that can both harm that kind of subject can be fierce in the sense meant here . Thus fear is neither a surd affection in the subject nor an objective quality in the object, neither quite a feeling nor quite a sensation. And for this sort of affection there is no room in the first Critique.

Now, why doesn't Kant have any room for this different kind of relational affection in the subject and of relational quality in the object? Because he belongs to, or is a member of, an epistemic tradition, and all epistemic traditions are bound to conceive knowledge according to the visibility model. Let us see why.

An epistemic tradition is characterized  by its concern for the validity of knowledge and by the agreement among its members not to accept as valid any piece of knowledge without previous justification. A tradition of that kind arises on the discovery that what was taken at a certain point for knowledge turned out not to be valid, that is, not to be genuine knowledge but merely apparent knowledge. Upon that discovery, the decision is made to examine knowledge in order to find out when, and how, and why, knowledge is genuine, and when it is only apparent.

Of course, the disputes among people as to whether a particular piece of knowledge was genuine or not had always existed, and they were usually solved or, rather, settled, by appeal to the majority, by what the majority within a given culture took for genuine or not genuine knowledge. In Greece, however, at the time of the Sophists, the dispute reached the proportions of an alarming cultural crisis, in that the dispute was not anymore about particular pieces of knowledge but about knowledge as such. Indeed, the conclusion reached more or less unanimously by those who had devoted themselves to the examination of knowledge and its validity was that no piece of knowledge whatsoever could possibly be  genuine. What was still worse, some of those who had so concluded were teachers and were willing to draw the practical consequence that lawyers in the courts of law and orators in the assemblies  should not aim at the truth (since nothing was true or false anyway) but at winning their case by persuading their audience by any means whatsoever.  

This was the cultural crisis to which Socrates confidently proposed to put an end once and for all through the constructive tradition he initiated of the lovers of the truth--the philosophoi --by contrast to those who felt entitled to aim at persuading their audiences by any means, "making the worse appear better."           It may be said that the Sophists had become epistemologists not on purpose but by accident. As teachers of rhetoric, their job was to teach their students how to be persuasive in the courts of law and in the assemblies of the people. In the process of doing their job they began to investigate the means of persuasion used in the courts and in the assemblies. Hoowever, the more they explored, the more they realized that the successful orators were not necessarily those who cared particularly for the truth of their statements and the logical validity of their arguments but, rather, directed their statements and arguments to provoke in their audiences the fears and desires that were conducive to their ends. In other words, the successful orators worked on the emotions rather than on the intelligence of their audiences. In addition, the more these teachers of rhetoric investigated, the more they realized that in matters of conduct goodness and badness were not objective qualities of the conduct but matters of arbitrary convention or preference which changed from culture to culture and, within a given culture, from time to time. Furhtermore, they discovered that even in the more elementary case of perceptual knowledge strict demonstration of the truth of a perception was impossible and therefore the validity of perceptual knowledge boiled down, again, to the ultimately arbitrary agreement of the majority.

By contrast to the sophists, however, the philosophers became epistemologists on purpose, in the determined hope of succeeding in the epistemological venture in which the sophists had failed, and as the means to bring an end to the crisis brought about by the sophists' failure. In becoming epistemologists, though, philosophers became mere observers of what surrounded them, and they automatically assumed that the relationship between the objects they observed and the knowers they observed was of the same kind as that between themselves and the objects they were exposed to and interacted with. Now, what kind of relationship was this? The relationship that obtains between that which appears or shows itself and the subject to whom it appears, that is, a purely intentional relationship rather than a motivating relationship of interaction and exposure. Hence the idea of the real as what shows visibly rather than what is to be responded to, of knowledge as adequation of a purely contemplating mind to what shows visibly, and of truth as actual correspondence between what is in the mind and what is in the real object. By the same token epistemologists systematically ignore the emotional-practical relationship between subject consciously exposed to the object's action and involved in interaction with it. Hence feelings automatically become for epistemologists surd, unintentional affections arbitrarily accompanying the perception of object.

What is missing in Kant for him to have room for fearing objects and fearful objects in his universe of discourse? To pre-assume or pre-sume a reciprocal relationship of interaction and exposure between subject and object. But this would involve a totally different conception of knowledge, the conception obscurely glimpsed by Aristotle when he spoke of practical knowledge--which is anticipatory (on the basis of past experience), motivating, applicative, and leading to (hopefully appropriate) action.  

The visual model of knowledge

Unlike touching which involves being touched, seeing is not necessarily reciprocal: one can see without being seen by what one sees. The visual model is unidirectional and bidimensional.  This is to say that, according to the visual model, the seeer, that is, the subject, is that to which what appears is visible, but it is not of the concept of seeer to be itself visible, much less exposed to the action of a pourely visible object. Not so in the case of the touching subject, who cannot touch without being touched and touchable, that is, without being as exposed to what is touched as what is touched is exposed to the subject .

When I am touched in the back by somebody's hand I have a sensation of a hand touching me, which is objective or cognitive of object. Similarly, when I see a hand that is about to touch me I have a sensation. But in the latter case I cannot identify my being affected as distinct from the object.

Now, touching something involves being touched by it. However, these are not two different experiences for the experiencing subject, but one experience pointing in two directions. You who are not me, you who see me, you contemplator, can ask me to tell you about what touched me (objective direction), or about what I feel when I am being touched, but you are asking me to talk about the two directions of what for me was one and the same experience. My experience of a hand touching me is the same as my experience of being touched dy a hand. There is no sensation of your hand touching me + a surd unintentional feeling acompaning the sensation of your hand. There is, rather, an inseparable double-sided unit: I-feel-your-hand-touching-me and I-feel-being-touched-by-your-hand. (By contrast, there is no comparable experience of myself in the case of seing a hand that is about to touch me).

What I am driving at is that there is an appearing here, an experiencing, that is not of the order of pure visibility, as when I see your hand touching sombody else than myself (a visible body), and which does not refer to (or is experienced as) a subject mere contemplator, but which is of the order of [dramatic] exposure and refers to affected-affective subject and to affecting respondendum object. * When you touch somebody I see your hand touching somebody, whereas when you touch me I feel your hand touching me or, what is the same, I feel myself being touched by your hand. In this case, it is not only the case, as remarked by Descartes, that unlike the pilot in his ship the subject feels its being touched, but also that the subject feels the hand touching him or her.  

Now, since Kant's view of knowledge is based on the model of visibility rather than of exposure and interaction, his analysis of this case would be different. When I see your hand touching my arm, I see what anyone else can see--something public, objective, relatively independent from my seing it since other people see or can see it too and confirm what I see, therefore not an affection of mine. In addition,supposedly, I have a private feeling, an affection, caused by something which happens to be your hand but which itself is not intentionhal or informative about object, that is, about a hand touching me.

What I am disputing is that my experience of pain is, as Kant seems to suggest, a sort of subjective object, an object comparable to the hand seen in that it appears in my experience, but different from the hand seen in that it is not objective object for it appears only in my experience. I claim, by contrast, that what I experience in my being touched by your hand is myself-exposed to you and what I experience in my touching your hand is yourself exposed to my hand. In other words, both the subjective and the objective poles of experience are of the order of exposure, not of the order of mere intentionality as the visibility assumption leads Kant to suppose. *

* To put it otherwise, I can be seen without realizing it, for being seen is not something that one feels or is affected by. By contrast, however, I cannot be touched (in my vigilant state) without feeling it; it affects me. Now, unlike seeing or being seen, being touched refers me to myself not as "subject" (the correlate of Kantian "object," that is, as constituting subject ), but as center of affectivity, nor as constituted subject (which is a contradiction in terms), but as subject exposed to that which touches me, as being affected in a desirable or undesirable way by that to which I am exposed-vulnerable. In this way being touched is not a mere feeling or internal, surd, affection but is connected with intentional action, with action that will incarnate my purpose to be achieved through bringing about a definite state of affairs.

 More than all the other senses, as observed by both Aristotle and Aquinas, the sense of sight conveys to us the objectivity of the object and its independence from the subject, for in seeing, as remarked above, the sensation is indistinguishable from the affection. (Isn't this the reason why the Platonizing Aristotle and his follower Aquinas consider the sense of sight the most cognitional of the senses?). Its objectivity, because unlike the senses of smelling and hearing, which only announce the object, sight supposedly gives us the object in intuitive presence. Its independence, because in the case of seeing the presence is sufficiently distant for us to realize the non-identity of the object with us.

Why should we prefer at all the Kantian analysis that breaks down the experiential unit into two pieces, one objective and one merely subjective, based on the epistemic model of visibility, to an interpretation that preserves the unbroken experiential unit in which affection is object-intending as well as self-referential to being vulnerable (whereas purely visual sensation of object has as its correlate apparition, what appears? Actually, such visual sensation does not suffice to be object-intending in the sense of yielding the independent existence of its object, much less the existence of object of exposure, of that to which I am exposed. The visible objects yielded by such sensations are like Derridean 'texts' which are only to be talked about by objective contemplators rather than lived by the agents who participate in them and co-create them, and which therefore are only to-be-interpreted voyeuristically in an indefinite number of ways which are all equally possible and undecidable).

So far I have been insisting more on the subjective pole than on the objective one. Let us consider now whether, as the nature of reality is concerned, the visibility model and the exposure model are equivalent.

Suppose that the only sense we humans were endowed with were the sense of sight, and in particular that we had no sense of touch, so that when touching or being touched we experienced or felt absolutely nothing, exactly as a table feels nothing when touched or hit, or as it happens to be our own case under heavy sedation. In that case, to refer to the example used above in the paragraph about Kant by Hartnack, if I lifted a body I would see that body rise together with my hand (as I see it rise together with your hand when you lift the object), but I wouldn't feel any pressure on my hand. Consequently, I would never make the judgment that this body weighs, much less that bodies weigh (see Critique of P.R , Müller, p. 8). In ordinary perception my afection is a sensation, for the feeling of pressure informs me that this body weighs and that bodies weigh. Now, that weight is objective-real Kant acknowledges, for it is object of transcendental (or public?) intuition. However, the I-subject who feels pressured by the body I lift (and that there is even an I, that is, a personal body who lifts the body) Kant does not acknowledge as objective-real (see Critique of P. R. , transl. Müller, pp. 103-106). Why not? Because though it 'appears' to me in the sense that it is experienced by me, that I am the affected one, this I is not and cannot be object of intuition because it does not and cannot appear publicly, that is, because it is not publicly visible or transcendental, and not even visible at all. It is a mere subjective condition of the unity of apperception, which is not what is experienced by the subject.

Now Kant has a point in that the subject does not experience himself or herself as object, but he is wrong in imagining that we experience what he calls "object" as merely objectively (that is, as neither desirable nor undesirable) and ourselves as mere contemplators of visible or observable. Each of us experiences himself or herself as being the vulnerable being which encounters the other than itself (which is why we live in the mood of being careful ) as to be responded to in the future--gerundive--, and this is reflected in the kind of language we use. Certainly the subject experiences himself or herself not as experienced but as experiencer, not as object but as subject; not, however, as mere contemplator (s Kant assumes) but as vulnerable exposed, as being the subject who encounters.

This Kant cannot acknowledge because, under the visibility assumption, there is no room for this non-objective subject as being exposed, as subject of passive and active exposure, and, correlatively, of the object not as "object" in the Kantian sense but as actively and passively exposed to the subject. For Kant only what appears , that is, what shows visibly as phaenomenon, and what appears as object, that is, what appears as intuited, can be objective or knowable (or real). But does not the object encountered "appear" as encountered, that is, as to be responded to, as not merely phenomenal? And cannot being cautious-vulnerable subject (for good and for bad, for being hurt as well as for being loved and caressed, which is only experienced by the subject of passivity, but is publicly acknowledged linguistically) be the subjective-real (I cannot say manifestation or appearance, for this refers us to visibility) mode of being? And does not the narrative, rather than the scientific genre, preserve this structure?

Narrative and dramatic presence

The speaking subject cannot be objectified without ceasing to speak-address and to be spoken to-addressed in the vocative. WE CAN ONLY SUBJECTIFY OURSELVES NARRATIVELY IN THE PAST, because the narrative replicates the (past) subject as living in its present toward a not yet closed future in temporal continuity with the past-self and the past state of the world. The speaking subject is an agent-patient exposed to other agents-patients to whom he or she speaks and listens, and exposed to other non-agents-patients. The beauty of narratives is that because of the co-presence of the agents-speakers within the narrative as a temporal dramatic unit, although not copresence to the reader (narrative past), it is the literary genre that preserves the life of linguistic agency, though in relation to the reader it can only be past, or imaginary dramtic time, or at best imaginary re-production of the past in the case of plays. Scientific language, by contrast--even the language of orally given papers--is dead, ossified, objectified language, for it states the objectified observed and an unexposed subject. It is language without individual, situated, exposed speaker speaking to non-situated, unexposed purely contemplative listeners-speakers. Such language, consequently, is composed of statements that are supposed to re-produce the world and be true in the measure of the fidelity of the re-production.

Reason for the pervasivenesss of the visibility model

But, then, why is the visibility model of knowledge, which does not square with our experience, taken so seriously as epistemic model? The reason is that it is, precisely, an epistemic model, that is, a model that irremediably leads to a re-production, that is, as an image (can an image be other than visual?) of the [real] object in the mind). Let me explain.

That knowledge as an image of the real cannot in fact be justified is shown by the failure of the entire philosophical tradition to give satisfactory answers to the objections of the sophists. But the question is: why do we fall so spontaneously into the trap of this model and find it so difficult to escape from it?

The reason is, as I just said, that we are dealing with an epistemic model , that is, with an interpretation of knowledge as an image of reality and with the consequent, unsuccessful attempts to justify knowledge so conceived. Insofar as there is 'knowledge' involved in our interaction, it is practical, applicative knowledge--self-referential, existential, whose subject is an exposed agent and whose object is not to be seen but to be responded to appropriately. Philosophers and sophists, though, are not involved agents; they are epistemologists who, being aware of illusions, errors, and disagreements, cease to act and pause to understand knowledge and find a way out of their quandary. What they do is to reflect on observed knowers and knowledge (not on agents, since they are interested in knowedge, not on action, and they have already divorced knowledge from action as something complete in itself. By the same token, since feelings have nothing to do with the object seen--only with the object of exposure--and are supposedly connected with action but not with knowledge, they are also considered irrelevant). That is, they objectify knowers and knowledge as objects-there, as facts that appear 'visually,' so to say, that is, that can be, if not literally seen, at least grasped through a concept that renders their identity 'visible' to the understanding.

Now, not only are the knowers reflected upon conceived by the philosophers as contemplators of objects of contemplation, but the reflecting philosophers themselves are mere contemplators or visual interpreters--mere observers--for they themselves are not involved in action and not exposed to the knowers they contemplate or to the objects the observed knowers and the philosophers themselves contemplate. In other words, the reflecting philosophers are not involved in interaction with the knowers or with the objects known by them, they are just voyeurs who want to understand knowledge by explaining how it occurs and to justify its validity. *

* I may now be in a better position to clarify what I mean by the visibility assumption. Visibility is identified here as a relationship observer-observed by contrast to the relationship exposed agent to that to which he or she is exposed.

(1) The alternative to the visibility assumption implies thinking from the standpoint of the agent involved in interaction, of the exposed-vulnerable agent, by contrast to the pure invulnerable contemplator, the objective-disinterested observer. Hence no divorce of knowing from acting, consequently no primacy of knowledge over action and no possible separation, as in Kant's case, between a First Critique that deals with knowledge as independent from action, with a knower that has nothing to do but to constitute the object and intuit, and a Second Critique that deals with an agent who neither is the knower of the first Critique nor finds himself or herself within the order of causality, and who, as such, is called to ignore emotions and to be guided only by the categorical imperative and a respect appropriate only to the kingdom of ends.

(2) It implies, therefore, a reevaluation of practical-knowledge, with the consequent reevaluation of emotions as cognitional.

(3) It implies giving up foundationalism and any absolute pre-justification (and even post-justification) of knowledge. The most that can be aspired to is intra-cultural persuasion ad hominem , and inter-cultural perusasion on the basis of one's fidelity to one's own tradition and ultimately on the basis of witnessing.

(4) Probably the model for thinking from the vulnerable-agent's standpoint has to come from dramatic narratives, for they preserve the Kierkegaardian facing the future by way of decision and action, as well as from the incomplete, ongoing, open to an undefined future, particular narrative in which the speaker is involved in the first person.

Since we, the people concerned with epistemology, are ourselves, like the philosophers, reflecting on knowledge rather than being engaged in action, it is hardly surprising that we find their assumptions natural, thereby falling into the trap of their paradigm. What we need is to escape from their paradigm, which is what we do when we are engaged in action.

The Greek epistemic tradition

To take a different tack. I am aiming at showing the arbitrariness, and ultimately the wrongheadedness of an epistemic tradition (the Greek scientific tradition, the Greek speculative tradition) that systematically subjectivizes feeling and excludes what can be felt but not observed from the objective, cognitional realm. This tradition automatically divorces theory and practice, thereby rendering knowledge purely contemplative and human action purely behavioral. In addition, it objectivizes reality thereby rendering it a value-neutral, all-encompassing two-dimensional picture without existential depth.

It seems that in the Aristotelian tradition of practical reason, within which attitudes (which are, or at least can be construed as, neither surd subjective feelings neither contemplatively intentional), and which rejoins the intuitions of the "good sophists" of Protagoras' kind, we have at least a hint of the alternative to the epistemic tradition of visibility, namely, the alternative of exposure. *

* Aristotle cannot be confused with Plato, and yet he never became really independent from Plato. His ambiguity arises from the fact that he has two different and incompatible views of the knowing subject--the Platonic contemplator and the vulnerable, exposed agent--and he unwittingly mixes the two. In the beginning of the Metaphysics (first three Lectiones of Aquinas' commentary), in De Motu Animalium , in the Ethics --deliberation, decision, action, ends and means, practical syllogism, enthymeme, in the Rhetoric and the Poetics ) Aristotle deals with the exposed agent as knower. However, he doesn't quite abandon the visibility assumption that comes to him from Plato, and he mixes scientific knowledge or episteme with practical knowledge or prudence. This mixture is very obvious in the mentioned sections of the Metaphysics , where the senses--and especially sight--are given pèle-mèle the disparate functions of (Platonic) contemplation and of (vulnerable exposed) action.        Following Kant's idea of reality as what corresponds to the

transcendental conditions of knowledge, no less than following the Platonic tradition with its totally negative view of the body, of sensibility, of the passions, and of whatever is not purely intelligible and contributing to mere understanding, we unavoidably will exclude from the world by which we are surrounded what is not visible but to be responded to positively or negatively and, as far as the future is concerned, desired or aspired to and, in the measure that it is legitimately possible brought about through action.  

In the Greek speculative epistemic-justificatory tradition knoiwledge is conceived in such a way that it amounts to, terminates in, and is complete by being mere seeing. Whether the knowing subject acts or not is, in this conception of knowledge, irrelevant, for the subject is not more nor less knowing by acting or not acting, nor is the object known as such more or other than being seen. That something is knowable means that it is to be 'seen," and that it is actually known means that it is 'seen' in the particular way that within the given epistemology is taken as a guarantee that what is seen is what is actually the case.

In Kant's case this divorce of knowing from acting is evident. The first Critique is about knowing and has nothing to do with acting, whereas the second which has to do with acting has nothing to do with knowing in the sense defined by the first Critique .

Connected with the assumption of knowledge as visibility is the conception of the real or what is to be known as merely visible, that is, experienciable or intelligible, capable of appearing as seen or heard or smelled or touched or tasted, and finally understood or intuited or sensed with evidence. Now, if the real is equated with the knowable, and the knowable with what is visible, it follows that the real is not something to whose action the knower is exposed and which is itself exposed to the knower's action. * In other words, the real is metaphorically equated with a picture, for a picture like the real is merely to be seen. What characterizes a photo or a picture, though, is that taken as an object it is unreal.

* Only in the modern, post-Galilean scientific tradition are action and causality  focused on as object of inquiry with the purpose of controlling nature. But it is OBSERVED action and causality. The agent's action-passion, the agent's vulnerable exposure to the natural world is systematically ignored.  Here, too, the intentionality of visibility is unidirectional: the scientist intends the real but as a mere contemplator, the scientist is in no way exposed to it nor the real to the scientist.

What I am proposing is another sort of Copernican Revolution, a paradigmatic shift in which the Greek terms of 'visibility' in which we are used to conceive all matters related to knowledge and truth become obsolete. The alternative, in my view, is a return to our insertion in the world as vulnerable agents-patients as factually foundational, which restitutes to life its dramatic seriousness. Beyond that, there are only different cultures, different linguistico-practical-institutional communities with different ideal narratives, the ultimate validity of which cannot be demonstrated; it can only be preached to the members of other cultures and, at the limit, witnessed to.

The first thing to remark is that the Sophists and later the philosophers identify knowledge with sedimented knowledge, with objectified object, that is, object sensibly or intellectually seen that, when stated , claims to be, or could possibly be, a piece of genuine knowledge, depending on whether or not it is true, that is, resemblant. This image or judgment or statement is what is supposed to represent or be [that is, function as?] a copy of the real object. And, since it is a copy, it is supposed to contain or be constituted exclusively by those traits which are susceptible of similarity, namely, the experientiable-intuible, metaphorically 'visible' traits. Hence the irresistible inclination of the Greek tradition to favor as constituting reality what can be seen , be it by the senses, or by the intellect through ideally necessary predication. Feelings, therefore, become surd, merely subjective, unintentional experiences. Unidirectionality, non-exposure, no-existential-self-reference become the rule. And this applies to Kant in spite of his Copernican revolution. He is as full-fledged a member of the Greek tradition started by the sophists as any other of his predecessors and most of his successors.        

The sophists took something stated-believed by someone to be true, for instance, "The wind is cold," and showed that it had no more title than "The wind is hot" to be taken as true. Hence either there was no genuine knowledge, for truth as correspondence was impossible, or at least whether the correspondence obtained couldn't be known. Hence true for me is what appears to me, that is, what I believe and affirm to be the case, and true for you is what appears to you.

Clearly it was the first crisis of the notion of knowledge as something seen and of the idea of truth as genuine correspondence between what is seen and assented to and what is actually the case. Since they had no definite alternative, the sophists either actually gave up or seemed by implication to have given up on knowledge and truth, from which skepticism, moral relativiswm, and ultimately cynicism and mere will to power logically had to result. Needless to say, as the champions of knowledge, truth, and morality the philosophers claimed for themselves the beau role. They did  not live up to their claims, though.

As a last resort, Kant gave up the idea of reality in itself and, consequently, the notion of truth as correspondence, though keeping the assumption of visibility. He had the stroke of genius, unlike Hume, of seeing the epistemic subject as active and the known as to be actively constituted rather than passively copied. (This was important for it suggests the agent's contribution-invention in deliberation and decision). However, he persisted in considering the constituted-known as something seen and assented to rather than to be transcended applicatively, and the subject as a contemplator, as an epistemic subject, rather than as an exposed agent patient.
