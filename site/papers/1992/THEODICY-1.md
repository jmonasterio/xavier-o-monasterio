            Theodicy and "Meta-physics"
      Philosophy of physics is not itself physics but 'meta-physics,' a
reflection on the scientific endeavor called physics. But not only are not
they the same, but they are totally different--whatever the actual
relationship between the two may turn out to be. That they are totally
different is evident. All the knowledge acquired in philosophy of physics
will not yield a single piece of the knowledge acquired by the physicist in
the practice of his/her discipline, nor is the knowledge acquired in
philosophy of physics in any way necesary to be a successful physicist.
      If the two disciplines are different, confusing the two would
necessarily lead to enormous mistakes. This much is clear.
      Philosophy of religion and religion stand in a parallel relationship
to that between physics and philosophy of physics.
Religion is not philosophy of religion, and philosophy of religion is not
itself a religion or a religious practice. If the two are confused,
enormous mistakes follow. Yet the temptation to confuse these two, and the
lack of distinction between the two, have loomed large in the Western
philosophical tradition.
      Pascal claimed forcefully that the God of philosophers is not the God
of Jesus-Christ. Obviously, such a claim can be meaningfully made only if
some people confuse the two. And there can be little doubt that the people
Pascal had in mind were philosophers. There is, therefore, some reason to
think that philosophers have confused religion with philosophy ofreligion
and have mistaken what they do when they philosophize about religion with
what people do in believing in God.
      Some clarification is needed at this point, lest we go astray.
Philosophy of religion is a modern development. The philosophers Pascal had
in mind were not engaged in the study of a concrete particular religion, or
in a comparative study of religions, or in a study of religion as such.
Thus, if they were philosophers of religion, they were that in a sense that
needs to be defined.
      What they actually practised can be better named, to use a
traditional label, "natural theology" or "theodicy." Now, this
intellectual discipline has a history about which some knowledge is
essential for my purposes.
From theoloy as a practice to theology as a system [From sacred history to
theology]
      The notion of sience (epistéme, scientia) as a methodic search for
truth and certainty with its own constitutive rules and leading to a
complete system of logically interconnected, true and certain statements
hierarchically ordered is Greek. It was first incarnated in Euclid's
geometry. It was reflectively formalized and presented as the ideal
knowledge by Aris in the Posterior Analytics. And it enters the Christian
Western world, and is adopted by it, between the 12th and the 13th
centuries with the irruption of Aristotle's works. From this cultural
revolution arise our modern notions of theology, of philosophy, and of the
particular district of philosophy alluded to as "natural theology."
      If we take theology in a broad sense close to its etymological
meaning as reflection and dialogue about God and religious matters or
matters connected with God, theology was practiced within Christianity or
the Christian culture since its inception, as it had been in the Jewish
parent culture. Indeed, from the very beginning Christians reflected upon
and talked among themselves about matters of their faith in an effort to
understand them ever more deeply because these matters were central to
their lives. To mention only one perspicuous and early example, the Fourth
Gospel manifests an already high level of such reflection and talk. In this
sense theology is nothing else than the spontaneous, constant reflection on
their Christian culture as Christian by the members of the Christian
culture.  The same may be said if we take theology as as reflection upon
and exchange about matters of the Christian faith by Christians for the
purpose of discerning the genuinely Christian beliefs and practices
constitutive of the Christian community. The so-called heretical sects that
originated in the Christian culture and were ultimately considered as
deviations by the mass of Christians witness to such interpretive
reflection upon and discussion about matters of their faith by Christians.
Furthermore, very early, and partly as a result of the reflection and talk
about matters of the Christian faith in the just mentioned senses, there
was a sort of primitive systematization by the Christian themselves of the
central truths and practices of their faith, expressed in the symbola fidei
and in the catechesis. In all these senses theology is coeval with
Christianity.
      Two important and interrelated features characterize these
theological prctices. In the first place, the interpretive reflection and
dialogue to which I have so far alluded are intra-communitary practices.
Their subject is the Christian culture as such, and the reflection and
exchanges are carried out by Christians, among Christians, and for the
benefit ofthe Christian community. Secondly, and by the same token, they
are carried from the standpoint of the shared Christian faith and by
implicit or explicit reference to the shared Christian faith, whose
ultimate authoritative rule is the word of God in the Holy Scriptures. By
contrast to these theological intra-communitary practices we can
distinguish other theological extra-communitary practices.
      The Christian communities lived, at least initially and for the most
part, within larger communities which did not share their faith and with
which exchanges were inevitable. This gave birth to a different kind of
theological practice: theology as apologetics, that is, to reflection and
exchange about religious matters for the purpose of preserving the
genuinely Christian faith against the danger of corruption by the
surrounding larger culture, and of defending it against the arguments
inspired by other, different ways of understanding the universe. This
defense of their faith and way of life by Christians was, of course, based
on their own faith and way of life. However, insofar as it was not a purely
intra-communitary matter and insofar as the exchange with the alien
cultures attempted to be persuasive, it forced the Christians into a form
of argumentation in which they could not simply appeal to the shared faith
to resolve differences, for the cultures with which the exchanges took
place did not share their faith. Lacking a definite commonly shared ground
by reference to which the differences could be arbitrated at least to some
extent, such exchanges were for the most part futile. However, they had
significant side-effects within the Christian communities. One among them
was that in the course of those exchanges Christians came in close contact
with cultures--that is, ways of living, of thinking, and of interpreting
the universe--which were radically different from their own and which were
already sedimented in a sophisticated literature very different from their
Holy Scriptures. This expanded their vision, for they found themselves
enriched with notions and practices which could be assimilated to their own
Christian culture without loss of its identity.
      The Constantinian era defined the shape that Christianity
as a fully institutionalized culture would take and keep until the
Renaissance. The Church took charge of formal schooling, which was for the
most part reserved to the affluent class and the members of the clergy.
This institutionalization of Christian schooling, which by the XIth century
will result in the creation of the medieval universities, introduced a
significant transformation in the theological practice to which I have been
referring. Theology, which initially had been carried out in a rather
informal way together with preaching and cathechesis, became the field of
specialization (to use modern language) of those destined to the priesthood
and progressively acquired the identity of a specialized scholarly
discipline integrated into a larger preparatory curriculum. As we will see
in a moment, this change would pave the way for the revolutionary events of
the XIIth-XIIIth centuries. However, we are not there yet.
      None would be more influential than Saint Augustine in determining
the path that the theological practice, that is, the Christian reflection
on Christian matters, would follow within the institutional framework of
the Constantinian epoch and thereafter. Van Steenberghen portrays his
influence as follows:

      This shift from theology as a practice to theology as a particular
field of knowledge, as an intellectual discipline among others, however
privileged its status may have been
considered, deserves particular attention.
      The theological practices of early Christianity were fundamentally
practical. They responded to the needs of the members of the Christian
culture as these needs arose and were experienced by them as members of
that culture, and their purpose itself was practical--to enhance the
Christian life of the community. If what we might consider as "knowledge"
was involved or pursued in the original theological practices, it was not
pursued for its own sake, as the goal itself of the practices, but rather
as a means to protect and enhance the Christian life. To give an example,
the Symbola were not created for such theoretical purposes as gaining or
giving information or explanation about God and matters related to the
Christian way, but for the practical purpose of favoring the orthopraxis of
the Christian way and of discouraging and discarding the malpractices. The
same may be said of the catechesis and of the predication. In short,
insofar as theology involved reflection, it was practical reflection.
      The reading and the study of the Scripture, too, had the same
practical purpose. Christians did not read and meditate the Scripture for
the purpose of satisfying their intellectual curiosity but as a practical
expression of their faith and in order to deepen and intensify their own
practice of it. Preachers did not study the Scripture out of a mere desire
to know (as one may study the literature of an alien culture to gain
knowledge about it), but in order to find in it the rule of life of the
Christian community and to move their audiences to practice the Christian
way of life. [GADAMER'S 'APPLICATION'].
      All this changed gradually with the progressive professionalization
of theology and its transformation into a specialized field of study. The
intention, no doubt, remained practical. Those who studied theology were
preparing themselves for the pastoral life. Their study of such other
disciplines
as grammar, rhetoric, poetry, logic, philosophy was meant to furnish the
appropriate tools for the study of the Scripture and of the works of the
Fathers of the Church, which itself was meant to result in the enhancement
of their future pastoral functions. There was, however, a relatively long
time-lag between the period dedicated to these studies and the actual
exercise of their pastoral functions, during which period the practical
character of their studies could easily be obscured and the subject-matters
of their study could easily appear to them as mere fields of knowledge.
This, indeed, is what happened, and increasingly so as time went on.
      When theology acquired an identity as a special discipline, it
consisted initially in the study of the Scripture--"Sacra pagina"--and the
commentary of the Scripture by the Fathers of the Church. By the XIIIth
century, though, the study of the Scripture as such had been relegated to a
secondary place and the Summae Theologicae had become the theological
textbooks. How and why this shift took place and what was its significance
is our present quarry.
      The first important step was made by Peter the Lombard. His
work, which very rapidly became the accepted textbook for the students of
theology, was not particularly original or creative. What the Lombard did
was simply to collect the Fathers' dicta and to systematize them by
subjects. Now, most of the dicta of the Fathers came in their preaching,
and their preaching was itself a commentary on the Scripture for the
practical purpose of fostering the Christian life of the community. The
Lombard, however, isolated the Fathers' dicta from their practical context.
In the new context created by the Lombard what the Fathers had said to move
their audience to Christian orthopraxis lost its immediate practical
connection with the Christian way of life and acquired a different
signification. The point is important and demands explanation.
      Suppose that one of the Fathers commenting on St Paul's "Si
resurrexistis cum Christo..." tells his audience of batized Christians that
through baptism Christians resurrect with Christ.
For the Lombard, who groups the "sententiae" of the Fathers by subject,
this passage will be grouped with other "sententiae" of other Fathers that
refer to baptism. Reading these "sententiae" the theologian who studies the
Lombard's work will reach what amounts to a definition of baptism. Then our
theologian will move to the definition of another sacrament on the basis of
other "sententiae" of the Fathers. By the end of his study, he  will know
much about the sacraments. Suppose now that one of our contemporaries who
is an atheist but happens to be interested in medieval matters studies the
the Lombard's section on the sacraments as diligently as our medieval
student of theology. This atheist will come to know as much about the
sacraments (or perhaps even more) than our medieval theologian. The
difference between them is that our medieval theologian believes, say, that
being submerged in water in the baptismal rite actually makes the baptized
participate from the moment of baptism in Christ's resurrection, whereas
the atheist does not. However, as far as their knowledge about baptism is
concerned, both possess the same theological knowledge. Indeed, our modern
atheist would have passed as successfully as our medieval student of
theology the examinations given at the time to be certified as a
theologian. Now, when the Fathers preached they were not giving their
audience a course on baptism, they were exhorting their audience to live as
baptized, that is, as people who have resurrected with Christ.
      We may now conclude.  The  Lombard's  book  constituted  a  first  but
profoundly significant step toward what amounted to a  total  transformation
of the original theological practice. Theology,  which  had  initially  been
intrinsically related to the practice of the Christian life, was clearly  in
the process of becoming, as an academic discipline, simply  knowledge  about
God and about matters related to God.
      A second step soon followed  the  first.  The  Lombard's  book,  which
rapidly became the universal textbook in the schools, had at least kept  the
immediate reference to the Holy Scripture, in that the "sententiae"  of  the
Fathers collected by him were for the most part commentaries on passages  of
the Bible based on their own Christian,  practical  reading  of  the  Bible.
Now, however, the  Bible,  which  until  then  had  been,  as  it  had  been
originally for Christian  theologians,  their  central  book  of  reference,
became  progressively  displaced  from  its  central  place  by  the  Summae
Theologicae.
      The difference between  the  Liber  Sententiarum  and  the  Summae  is
important. As I just said, the Lombard's book  referred  the  theologian  to
the passages of the Scripture on which the Fathers were commenting. All  the
Lombard did was to group the Fathers' commentaries by themes.  In  the  case
of the Summae, though, the direct object on which the  student  inquires  is
not anymore  the  biblical  text  itself,  but  the  doctrine  extracted  by
theologians from the biblical text.
      Why  did  this  shift  occur?  As  remarked  above,  in  grouping  the
commentaries of different Fathers by  themes  the  Lombard  had  unwittingly
transformed the theological practice into a search  for  merely  theoretical
knowledge, with the result that theology became a corpus of knowledge  about
God and matters related to God. This  is  why  the  modern  atheist  of  our
example would have been certified as a theologian in  the  medieval  schools
as any other competent student of theology of that time.
      To appreciate the radical transformation involved  in  this  shift  we
have only to regress to the times of  the  original  theological  practices.
Consider Saint Paul's case. He did not go to a theological school  to  learn
that through  baptism  the  Christian  becomes  a  participant  in  Christ's
resurrection. Nor did that teaching come as a  shattering  surprise  to  the
members of the Christian communities, for it only made fully  explicit  what
was implicit in the praxis  of  their  Christian  culture  centered  on  the
Christ of their faith. Indeed, Paul himmself  could  make  this  point  only
because it was implicit in his very relationship  to  Christ,  in  the  very
life he led as a Christian. Now, suppose that Jesus of Nazareth had  existed
and that the events of his life had been fundamentally  the  same  as  those
narrated in the Gospels up to his death on  the  cross,  but  that  all  his
disciples, disappointed at his ignominious death, had  dispersed.  In  other
terms, suppose that there had been no Christian  communities,  no  Christian
way of life, no Christian tradition carried out by what I have been  calling
the 'Christian culture.' In that case,  there  would  have  never  been  any
Christian theology. Why not? Because the theological practice with which  we
are concerned, that is, the reflection and  talk  about  God  and  religious
matters characteristic of Christianity, is itself a constitutive element  of
the Christian culture. Indeed,  only  among  those  who  practice  and  care
deeply for the Christian way can the need arise to discern more clearly  the
beliefs and practices characteristic of the Christian way,  to  discard  the
beliefs and practices alien
to the Christian way, and to defend the Christian way  against  the  attacks
of  those  belonging  to  alien  cultures   and   against   the   corrupting
contamination  by  alien  cultures--which  are  precisely  the   theological
practices we identified in the early times of the Church.
        Imagine the atheist of  our  example  as  a  contemporary  of  Paul.
Imagine him as curious about Christian theology as  he  was  about  medieval
theology. From Paul he could have learned that  through  baptism  Christians
participate in Christ's  resurrection,  that  is,  they  believe  that  they
participate in Christ's resurrection, they have that belief  about  baptism.
Baptism, for him, is an object of study--a subject-matter, as it had  become
for the medievals thanks to the Lombard.



      But what the Lombard was writing was a textbook  on  baptism,  not  an
exhortation  to  live  as  baptized.  A  student  of  the  Lombard's   Liber
Sententiarum was expected to gain knowledge  about  baptism--knowledge  that
would enable him to answer correctly the  questions  he  was  likely  to  be
asked by his master during an examination, exactly the same  knowledge  that
would enable our modern atheist to succeed in a  similar  examination  about
baptism.
