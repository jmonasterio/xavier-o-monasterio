The Western philosophical tradition is characterized by being reflective. That is, it represents the thinking, not of the exposed agent actually involved in interaction with the surrounding world, but the thinking of a curious observer withdrawn from interaction, interested in understanding knowledge. Therefore this tradition  reduces the agent and, in general, the ‘object’ –what it conceives as the real, what is the case– to object of observation to be understood from the observing philosopher’s point of view. By the same token it converts the exposed, vulnerable, interacting agent into a neutral, objective observer and subordinates action to vision or knowledge, without realizing with Hume that no factual state of affairs can motivate this contemplated-contemplator to act (Monasterio).

(1) Postmodernism: the result of the Greek epistemic heritage

The Post-moderns have given up on presence and therefore on knowledge and truth. In their view, there is no possible correspondence between what the subject knows and what is the case. This leads them to either relativism or total skepticism. Threatened by the perspective of such an outcome, a number of people, especially Christians who have a particularly important stake in the issues of skepticism and of truth (the Vatican’s official line with its constant criticism of modern relativism constitutes a perspicuous example of the attitude I am talking about), oppose the post-moderns by defending the human mind’s capacity to represent reality objectively and grasp the correctness of the representation. A hopeless defense, in my opinion. Yet, within the assumptions shared by both parties concerning knowledge as representational and the relationship of the subject to the world, it has indeed to be a matter of either-or between two alternatives: either epistemic relativism, or objectivism.    

How come, however, that such declared enemies share the same assumptions? And which, precisely, are the assumptions they share? And why, precisely do they share these

assumptions rather than any others? And why these assumptions are made  so spontaneously, to the point of appearing natural and inescapable? And are they, in fact natural and inescapable? And, if they are not, what changes take place as a result of escaping them? These is the cluster of questions I’ll be dealing with here.

My basic hunch is that the origin of these assumptions is to be found in the epistemic views of the Greek philosophers of the golden era, namely, Socrates, Plato and Aristotle, who consecrated the epistemic paradigm which has prevailed till our days. Far from being adequate, however, the paradigm has always proven unsatisfactory. Hence the series of modifications it has undergone since Plato introduced it, from Aristotle through Kant and beyond. What characterizes post-modernity is the full realization of the insufficiency of the paradigm, but without the definite abandonment of it for lack of a viable alternative. Hence the more or less avowed relativism or even full-fledged skepticism of the post-moderns, as well as the stubborn dogmatism of those who refuse to give up on the paradigm in spite of its obvious inadequacy for lack of an acceptable alternative.

(2) Plato and Aristotle on Knowledge as correspondence

It is not my intention to explore in depth, much less in their totality, Plato’s and Aristotle’s epistemic views. For my purposes it will suffice to detect and render fully explicit certain fundamental, features that characterize them.

.

At least implicitly, Plato understood knowledge as some kind of VISUAL correspondence or conformity with reality. Granted, Plato is very far from Descartes’ definite subject-object or mind-world split. Consequently, the Cartesian project of an epistemic reconstruction of reality from scratch would have been inconceivable to Plato, as would have been Descartes’ correlative withdrawal from the realm of appearances to the epistemic subject as a starting point. Without the explicit separation of the subject from the object, Plato’s view of the correspondence with reality involved in knowledge had to remain vague, compared to Descartes’ own. In the latter, as explained by Rorty in much detail in Philosophy and the Mirror of Nature , the correspondence took the very definite shape of “knowledge as accurate representation” in “the mind.” ^(F1)  The correspondence of which Plato thinks would probably have to be characterized as a sort of mystical identification with what is. Yet, for all the significant differences between them, there is an undeniable kinship between Plato and Descartes in that knowledge consists for both of them in some kind of correspondence. whit what is.

That Plato understands knowledge as correspondence with what is is evident in his most distinctive doctrine, namely, the doctrine of the Ideas. What, indeed, led Plato to move in such an outlandish direction and postulate the existence of eternal Ideas if not the need to furnish to universal concepts a ‘reality’ that corresponded to them, for without a correspondent reality they would have been object-less or referent-less and therefore false? Moreover (and this will become clearer in a moment, when we deal with Plato’s views on the  knowledge of particulars), the fact that the Platonic Ideas were eternal and immutable insured that the knowledge the philosopher had of them was necessarily true, that is, that the correspondence with what is was absolutely certain and immutable. Indeed, Plato thought, without such necessity the correspondence of knowledge with what is could conceivably fail to obtain, and then what is taken for knowledge would, in the best case, amount to a mere belief, an “opinion”or “ doxa .” In short, without the actual existence of eternal Ideas no knowledge would be valid, for it would lack either or both of the two requirements that Plato deemed essential for knowledge to be genuine knowledge, namely, correspondence with what is , and  necessary correspondence.

So much for the knowledge of intelligible and universal realities. Very different, in Plato’s view, is the case of perceptual knowledge, for  the objects perceived are sensible rather than intelligible, particular rather than universal, and contingent rather than necessarily existing. In fact,  Plato found it impossible to justify the validity of perceptual knowledge.. Why? Precisely because knowledge requires necessary correspondence with what is, and in the case of perception the correspondence with the objects perceived cannot possibly be guaranteed, since particular objects are by their very nature temporal and mutable. Hence, not only would the knowledge of sensible objects cease to correspond to them as they changed, but, in addition, even assuming that  a particular perception happened to correspond at least momentarily with the object perceived, this correspondence wouldn’t be necessary but merely contingent, given the mutable nature of the particular object. Hence the knower wouldn’t know with absolute certainty whether or not his or her perception was true, in which case knowledge would be just a “ doxa ,”an uncertain opinion or belief  rather than genuine knowledge.  

 

It is clear, then, from his consistent twofold demand of a correspondent object and of necessary correspondence with it, that Plato understood knowledge as correspondence. What now about Aristotle?

Aristotle’s views cannot be fully understood but by contrast to those of  Plato, for they resulted from of a constant, implicit dialogue with his master and it was by reference to those views that he progressively elaborated his own stances. As is often the case with disciples of great talent, Aristotle began his career by identifying himself with his master’s views and then he progressively came to differ from them in significant respects. This is so true in Aristotle’s case that the chronological order of his works is for the most part. established by detecting the progression of his differences with Plato. Concerning those differences, the specific point that interests me for the moment is whether, in his effort to improve on his master, Aristotle came to disagree with him concerning the notion of knowledge as visual correspondence.

Now, to begin with the case of intellectual knowledge, it is true that Aristotle gave up on the transcendent existence of the Platonic eternal Ideas. But, as is well known, he replaced that feature of the Platonic system with the intelligible universal essences of the  particular substances actually existing within our world. In their turn,  these immanent essences performed the same function in the Aristotelian system that the transcendent Ideas had in Plato’s. Indeed, their actual existence guaranteed the objectivity of our universal judgments, and their immutable or a-temporal nature insured the truth of those judgments by making  the correspondence to them necessary . We may therefore conclude that, for all the avowedly significant ways in which Aristotle modified the Platonic doctrine of the Ideas, he nonetheless retained the notion of knowledge as correspondence, in the intellectual order.

As for the order of the sensible , in  which the objects perceived are particular rather than universal, Aristotle’s views are certainly more ambiguous than Plato’s. As we just saw,  precisely the absence of necessary correspondence with what is, in the case of sensible particulars, constrained Plato to reject the validity of perceptual knowledge. On this point, however, Aristotle eventually came to disagree with his master. Indeed, of the two requirements that Plato had deemed essential for knowledge to be genuine rather than merely apparent, Aristotle only kept the requirement of correspondence with what is, and dropped the requirement that the correspondence be necessary ^(F2). This resulted in the Aristotelian distinction, for which there was no room in Plato, between “scientific knowledge,” on the one hand, whose truth was necessary, and non-scientific knowledge, whose truth could not be guaranteed by strict demonstration, because it involved no intelligible necessity of correspondence,  but which was nonetheless factually valid because it actually corresponded to what is. In short, in the order of the sensible, there is a definite and important disagreement of Aristotle with his master. Indeed, Plato classified all perceptual knowledge as doxa, mere opinion or belief, whereas Aristotle maintained that the judgments that referred to the particular objects of perception could in fact be true, though never necessarily so.

As for the basis on which Aristotle felt authorized to discard the Platonic requirement of necessary correspondence with what is, the best one can say is that Aristotle was too pragmatic, or too commonsensical, not to rely on the common agreement of humankind and take perceptual evidence at its face-value within prudent limits. Unlike Plato, Aristotle was convinced that the certainty that was possible in different domains of knowledge depended on the nature of the objects found in the domain, and that to ask for more certainty than was possible within a given domain was irrational. ^(F3)

In conclusion, though Aristotle softened the Platonic requirement of necessary correspondence with what is, and in this regard he definitely differed from Plato, he nonetheless remained in full agreement with his master that correspondence with what is is the absolutely essential characteristic of knowledge. We might therefore say that for all their significant differences with Descartes and Descartes’ successors, both Plato and Aristotle can and should be considered as Descartes’ predecessors, insofar as the Cartesian mirroring of nature is just a variety of the correspondence with what is in which knowledge is supposed to consist. ^(F4) Thus the medievals were not wrong in seeing Aristotle’s idea of truth as conformity with what is, aedaequatio mentis ad rem .

(3) Plato and Aristotle on theoretical and practical knowledge

It is to Aristotle that we owe the definite distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge, which is absent in Plato’s works. Not that Plato was uninterested in the practical aspect of knowledge. On the contrary, initiated into philosophy by Socrates, whose fundamental aim was attaining the knowledge that would allow us to live the way we are supposed to live as human beings, Plato himself pursued knowledge in the hope that it would lead him to the very practical result desired by Socrates. Yet, as we shall presently see, both Socrates and Plato conflated the two varieties of knowledge and ultimately reduced practical knowledge to theoretical knowledge.  

In order to understand precisely the very significant differences between Plato and Aristotle in this area and, consequently, in their respective conceptions of philosophy, let us  begin with Socrates. The latter became the declared enemy of the sophists, whom he saw as subverters of the polis, because they educated the youth of Athens without themselves possessing genuine knowledge. Indeed, what they taught was rhetoric – the art of persuading people to do what the speaker wanted them to do, instead of teaching them to do what was right and good. The teaching of the sophists was of that kind either because they didn’t realize their own ignorance. or because they were convinced that what was right and good was simply a matter of subjective preferences and, thus, there was nothing wrong in teaching people to persuade others to act according to one’s own preferences. Whether the rhetoricians did that maliciously or innocently, in either case, Socrates thought, they were doing great damage to the polis as educators. So he decided to devote his life to expose their ignorance and to look for the “ sophia ” or genuine science of the good and right that the sophists were incapable of teaching.

The Platonic dialogues are a dramatization of the way in which Socrates carried out the task he had set for himself. Their study supports abundantly the two points that are important for my present purposes. First, that the knowledge or “ sophia ” pursued by Socrates and then by Plato was, at least in their intention, practical rather than theoretical, in that it aimed at the result of living in the right way rather than at mere theoretical grasp of the truth. Second, that, in their view, such practical knowledge had to be necessarily or demonstratively true, in order to impose itself to the sophists themselves and to their disciples as something more and better than what they  taught or were taught. Indeed, in the Platonic dialogues, we find Socrates repeatedly rejecting the validity of any so-called knowledge that lacked necessity. The Euthyphro, to which we will return in a moment, is a good instance of this, for there we see Socrates stubbornly rejecting Euthyphro’s claim that he knows what piety is. The reason for the rejection is that Euthyphro cannot furnish an essential  ( rather than a merely pragmatic) definition of piety, that is, a definition that renders necessarily true the knowledge conveyed by the definition, and therefore results infallibly in the right living in matters relating to the divine.

In summary: the knowledge pursued by the lovers of wisdom was (1) practical rather than theoretical, and  (2) it had to be necessarily true. Such was, roughly, the status quaestionis  inherited by Aristotle from Plato as to the kind of knowledge that the lovers of wisdom were supposed to pursue, by contrast to the  rhetoric ^(F5) of the sophists. ^(F6) At any rate, such is the status quaestionis reflected in Aristotle’s early works, where there is as yet no distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge and where his own views are indistinguishable from Plato’s. ^(F7) What differences from this point of departure do Aristotle’s late works reveal?

As remarked above,  Aristotle came to soften in a highly significant way the radical Platonic distinction between opinion and knowledge, without totally abolishing it. For Plato, opinion and genuine knowledge were mutually exclusive, since the former lacked necessary truth. But why did Plato clung so adamantly to this aspect of Socrates’ views? Because Socrates’s  entire  philosophical career had been predicated on the ignorance of the rhetoricians or sophists who pretended to know but didn’t, because their pretended knowledge was not necessarily true . ^(F8) For his part, Socrates knew that he didn’t know, since he realized the difference between opinion and knowledge. Thus, for Socrates to give up on necessarily true knowledge would have amounted to resigning himself to the ignorance of his sworn adversaries. In short, reaching necessarily true knowledge or “sophia ” was the only possible alternative to ignorance. Neither could Plato resign himself to possessing mere opinions without betraying his master. In fact, the doctrine of the Ideas was Plato’s own contribution in order to render possible the realization  of the Socratic ideal by furnishing a necessarily corresponding object to universal, a-temporal judgments.

But, then,  how could Aristotle soften the opposition between opinion and knowledge without giving up on the philosophical undertaking? Did he ultimately side with the sophists? We will be better placed to grasp Aristotle’s own answer to these questions if we first understand why he couldn’t preserve the distinction between opinion and knowledge in the radical Platonic-Socratic version.

From the objections raised by Aristotle against Plato’s theory of the Ideas in the Metaphysics we know that very early he found that theory unconvincing. But he found Plato’s views unsatisfactory not only on account of the outlandishness of the theory of the ideas, but even more so on account of the internal contradiction Aristotle detected in the Socratic-Platonic ideal of knowledge. Indeed, the sort of knowledge Socrates had missed in the sophists was practical knowledge, since it should result in living virtuously. On the other hand,  both Socrates and Plato thought that knowledge had to be necessarily true in order to be knowledge. Thus, the sophia they pursued in their philosophical quest was a knowledge that was at the same time both practical and necessarily true. Herein lay the internal contradiction Aristotle detected in the kind of knowledge they had proposed as the philosophical ideal . For, as  he came to realize,  practical knowledge couldn’t possibly be necessarily true, because it dealt with contingent, corruptible particulars, not with necessary, immutable universals. In other words, the strictly scientific or necessarily true practical knowledge that Socrates had settled to achieve as the alternative to the pseudo-knowledge of the rhetoricians was an impossible dream.

Thus, if the name of “Sophia” were to be reserved for the kind of  knowledge whose search was inaugurated by Socrates and continued by Plato as “philosophers,”that is, as “lovers of   Sophia,” it may be said that Aristotle put an end to the philosophical tradition, which he saw as a no exit road. Insofar, however, as Aristotle sorted things out in a different way than Socrates and Plato, he has been seen by many as the thinker who, working on the shoulders of his two gigantic predecessors, opened a new future to the philosophical tradition. Indeed, after much groping, Aristotle came to a definite distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge. That distinction allowed him to determine that the necessarily true knowledge pursued by his predecessors was possible, but only in the theoretical domain,  not in the practical domain of human action where they had pursued it, because practical knowledge deals with contingent particulars, and in this domain there is no room for the necessary truths searched for by Socrates and Plato .

By contrast to Aristotle, then, Socrates and Plato had conflated the theoretical and the practical in one sophia that was necessarily true and at the same time practical . This was reflected in their view that the person who knows is ipso facto good. That is why, according to Plato, the philosophers, the sophoi, those whose knowledge was necessarily true and practical, were called to be kings and govern the polis. The Aristotelian sophia , by contrast --if this name can be used to refer to a vastly different conception of the philosophical ideal of knowledge-- ended up being twofold. It embraced the scientific or necessarily true knowledge possible in the theoretical domain, on the one hand, and, on the other hand, the contingently true knowledge that was the best possibility in  the domains of perception and of action .

(4) Plato and Aristotle on the connection between knowledge and action

This brings us to a further characteristic of the kind of knowledge Socrates and Plato pursued that we have not yet considered, concerning which it will be important to determine whether Aristotle followed his predecessors or departed from them. How did Socrates and Plato see the connection between knowledge and action, given that the knowledge they pursued was an impossible mixture of theoretical and practical? In other words, how could a knowledge that was theoretical, for it was necessarily true, result in action? There is no better place to grasp the problem herein involved than the Euthyphro .

Socrates meets the pious Euthyphro and tests him as to his knowledge on piety. What is piety?, asks Socrates. Doing what is pleasant to the gods, answers Euthyphro. Obviously, according to Euthyphro, doing what is pleasant to the gods amounts to knowing what piety is. But that is not knowledge, according to Socrates, or at any rate not the kind of knowledge he is looking for. Yes, Socrates wants correct action, pious action. But the kind of pious action he wants requires as a previous condition a theoretical knowledge of what is pleasant to the gods. And the theoretical knowledge Socrates sees as a precondition for pious action has to be essential knowledge, that is, has to grasp the essence of piety, and thus be explanatory of the reason why certain actions are necessarily pleasant to the gods, so that in any and every case the actions guided by that knowledge be necessarily pious or pleasant to the gods . Thus ,  the kind of action Socrates wanted presupposed theoretical-essential knowledge as its necessary condition. In this sense, he subordinated practical knowledge to theoretical  knowledge, for he made correct action dependent on theoretical knowledge of what correct action is and why it is necessarily so. Concretely, then, the Socratic sophia (WHICH ACCORDING TO DEVEREUX PLATO CALLED PHRONESIS; CHECK WITH GAUTHIER) was really a speculative knowledge whose contemplation would automatically result in correct action. In this way, for Socrates, knowledge and virtue were identical. The theoretical knowledge of what is necessarily pleasant to the gods would –for some unexplained reason-- necessarily result in doing what is pleasant to the gods. ^(F9)  By the same token, leading  people to acquire this kind of theoretical knowledge would make them automatically virtuous. Such was the teaching that the sophists were incapable of imparting, for which failure they were blamed by Socrates..

I say for some unexplained reason, because, like Euthyphros, I do not see why one cannot be pious in the sense of acting piously without knowing theoretically what piety is, much less, without possessing the essential definition of piety. Yet, as shown in the Euthyphros , that undoubtedly is what Socrates and Plato thought. This is the characteristic of the Socratic-Platonic knowledge that I said we had not considered, to wit: that, according to them, virtuous action presupposes as a necessary condition theoretical  knowledge –and even theoretical essential knowledge– of what virtue is. Which amounts to subordinating practical knowledge to theoretical knowledge by making theoretical knowledge a necessary (and apparently even sufficient) condition for right action.   

Now, by his definite distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge, Aristotle is supposed to have avoided the confusion between them of which Socrates and Plato were victims. The issue I want to explore is whether Aristotle's conception of practical knowledge really succeeds in eliminating the subordination of practical to theoretical knowledge.

Let us begin by summarizing what we have covered so far regarding the relationship of Aristotle’s epistemic views to those of Plato. For all the modifications that Plato’s views underwent in Aristotle’s hands, it seems fair to conclude that Aristotle remained close to Plato in certain important ways. (1) He didn’t give up on the Platonic notion of  knowledge as correspondence, but dropped the Platonic requirement of necessary correspondence as a necessary condition. This resulted in the Aristotelian division of knowledge into necessarily true or scientific and contingently true. For his part,  Plato excluded contingent correspondence from the domain of knowledge and relegated it among the doxai. .Hence Aristotle included perception in the domain of knowledge, whereas Plato excluded it (2) He didn’t totally discard the possibility of necessary correspondence; he only excluded it from the practical domain of action. By the same token,  Aristotle excluded the possibility of scientific knowledge in the practical domain, whereas Plato, lacking a definite distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge,  had deemed necessary correspondence possible in the practical domain.

Only concerning perceptual knowledge is Aristotle in total disagreement with Plato, for he drops there requirement of necessary correspondence with what is, which given Plato's conception of knowledge was absolutely required.  He didn’t give up on the possibility of knowledge as correspondence, though he eliminated necessary correspondence, in the theoretical domain of the sensible, whereas Plato had excluded this domain from the field of knowledge.

Aristotle’s notion of practical knowledge has been carefully studied by DEVEREUX.

I will take his article as my starting point.

(5) Devereux on practical knowledge in Aristotle

As Devereux points out speaking of Aristotle,

“... his break with Plato was a gradual process. The notion of practical knowledge did not spring up all at once, fully formed; there was a time when he thought practical knowledge was much like theoretical, the only important contrast being the different aims of each. ^(F10)”   

What defines theoretical knowledge is that it is pursued for its own sake, whereas practical knowledge is pursued for its utility. Now, though the aims for the sake of which the knowledge is pursued differ, the knowledge itself is the same in both cases. This maybe illustrated by the example of two people who study the medical science. One of them studies for the sake of knowing, whereas the other studies with the end of applying his knowledge and curing people. If such is the case, then the knowledge acquired by the two people is exactly the same ; the only difference being that one of the two people will put the knowledge to practical use, whereas the other is content with pursuing and enjoying the knowledge for its own sake. Thus, there was a period in Aristotle’s evolution in which he deemed practical knowledge to be identical to theoretical knowledge , that is, merely corresponding to what is, and only extrinsically connected with action . Now, it does not take much analysis to realize that this view is just a variation of the kind of  knowledge dreamed of by Socrates and Plato, who reduced practical knowledge to theoretical knowledge, in that they saw theoretical knowledge as the necessary and even sufficient condition for correct action. We may therefore conclude that, at least at this stage of his evolution, Aristotle had not yet achieved his full independence from Plato. In Devereux’s words, “there was a time when he thought practical knowledge was much like theoretical.”

The just quoted words suggest, of course, that there was a later stage in which Aristotle   saw practical knowledge as different from theoretical. How, precisely, did Aristotle conceive practical knowledge in this final stage? Let us follow Devereux’s detailed analysis.

“All of them [his predecessors] failed to grasp the fundamental distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge... Indeed, Aristotle thinks the differences are such that we cannot regard practical and theoretical knowledge as two species of a single genus, for there is no common definition of knowledge which applies to both” (Daniel T. Devereux, ‘Particular and Universal in Aristotle’s Concep t of Practical Knowledge,” In Review of Metaphysics , 39 (March 1986) p.483).

Evidently, Devereux is one of those who think that Aristotle succeeded in making practical knowledge independent from theoretical knowledge. Whether or not this is indeed the case, however, will be clear only when the analysis is complete. So let us continue.

“ The aim of a theoretical science is simply the knowledge to be gained. Aris recognizes that such knowledge might have important practical applications, but to pursue it for the sake of these applications would betray a misunderstanding of the value of a theoretical science . An analogous misunderstanding would be involved in the pursuit of practical knowledge for the sake of the knowledge by itself. Here the end is not knowledge, but what we can do with it: we pursue the knowledge for the sake of action ( praxis ). - A second... contrast between practical and theoretical knowledge has to do with subject matter. Theoretical sciences like physics and mathematics are concerned with necessary objects, with things “whose principles cannot be other than they are. ” Practical knowledge, on the other hand, is concerned with the variable and contingent; since such knowledge is practical, it is concerned with actions in particular circumstances, and here there is ‘much diversity and fluctuation.’” (Ibid. , p. 485).

Here we find something new, for now it is pointed out by Aristotle that theoretical and practical knowledge differ not only by their ends, as he had previously thought, but also by the nature of  their respective objects. Since practical knowledge is concerned with actions in particular circumstances, that is, with variable objects, practical knowledge cannot be necessarily true. This means that if any universals are involved in practical knowledge, they will not be strict universals dealing with essences, for essences are invariable.

“Nor is practical wisdom concerned with universals only -- it must also know particulars, for it is practical, and practice ( praxis , action) is concerned with particulars . That is why in other fields too some who do not know are more effective in action than those who know – they (the former) have experience . For if a man knew that light meats are easy to digest and wholesome, but did not know which sorts of meat are light, he will not produce health, but the man who knows that chicken is wholesome is more likely to produce health. Now practical wisdom ( phrónesis) is practical; hence one should have both [knowledge of universals and knowledge of particulars], of the latter even more than the former ” (NE 1141b14-22, quoted by Devereux, p. 485).

“That practical knowledge is not scientific knowledge ( epistéme ) is evident, for it is concerned with the ultimate, as has been said, since the thing to be done is of this nature” NE 1142a11-25. Quoted by Devereux, p 487).).

Thus, Aristotle thinks that practical knowledge does involves also knowledge of particulars, not only of universals, since actions, which are the object of practical knowledge are particular. What is intriguing, however, is why Aristotle thinks that practical knowledge involves knowledge of universals, given that the universals he is speaking of are not strict universals, which is precisely why Aristotle concludes that scientific knowledge is impossible in the field of practical knowledge. Moreover, Aristotle’s own analysis shows that the knowledge of universals is not strictly required as a component of practical knowledge, since we are told that those who do not know (the universals) are sometimes more efficient in practice than those who know the universals, not only in the field of medicine but in the whole domain of action:

“ With a view to action experience seems in no way inferior to art ( techne ), and even we see men of experience having more success than those who have theory ( logos ) but lack experience; the reason is that experience is knowledge of particulars while art is of universals ” (981a123-24. Cf. NE 1112b34-1113a2. Quoted by Devereux, p. 488).

To repeat, we are confronted with the paradox that practical knowledge involves knowledge of universals, and yet the knowledge of universals can be dispensed with. By contrast, the knowledge of particulars acquired through experience is indispensable. This sounds very much as saying that, after all, the knowledge of universals in practical knowledge is superfluous. And, if such happens to be the case, should not Aristotle have realized that no knowledge of universals is involved in practical knowledge? If such were the case, however, Aristotle’s view of practical knowledge would be wrong in a a non-trivial way. Let us find out whether further analysis confirms this point.

“Aristotle says that no art examines the particular; e.g. medicine does not examine what is healthy for Socrates or Callias, but what is so for a certain type of individual . On a first reading, this might seem to conflict with what is said in passage (3) [ “ With a view to action experience seems in no way inferior to art ( techne ), and we have seen men of experience having more success that those,  who have theory ( logos ) but lack experience; the reason is that experience is knowledge of particulars while art is of universals , and actions and productions are concerned with particulars’] but, as we shall see, Aristotle distinguishes between what is relevant to the art or science and what is relevant to practicing the art or science. Knowing about individuals is important for the practice of medicine, but not for the science itself ”, Ibid. p. 489, note 18).

A rift seems to be opening here between the art or science of medicine and medicine as a practice. Indeed, the art of medicine, as all other sciences, doesn’t concern itself with particulars, whereas in the practice of medicine the knowledge of particulars is all-important. But, if the subject -matter of a practical science is action and action is particular, as Aristotle told us, then the art of medicine which Aristotle distinguishes from the practice of medicine is not a practical science and should not be considered here at all. Again, another baffling element in Aristotle’s conception of practical knowledge which we will have to take into account in our conclusion.

What kind of universal is involved in practical knowledge, and how does it originate?

“He [the untutored empiric] investigates other individuals who are sick with symptoms A and finds the same correlation: the treatment is effective for those who are phlegmatic, but not for those who are not. He concludes that it is because of the presence of this characteristic in the one group that the treatment is effective... Aristotle would say that this man is no longer simply an untutored empiric; he has made an important step towards acquiring the art of medicine. In discovering a common feature in virtue of which a particular treatment is effective for a group of individuals, he has hit upon what Aristotle here calls a ‘universal,’ and it is knowledge of such universal that qualifies as art or science ” (Ibid. p. 490).

An important point is made here concerning the origin of the universals involved in practical knowledge, to wit: that the knowledge of the universal is itself the result of practice, not of theory or of theoretical study. Indeed, the untutored empiric, that is, the medical practitioner who has studied no medical science and relies exclusively on experience, is the one who discovers the so-called universal, namely, the characteristic that is present in all the cases in which the treatment applied has been effective. From now on, of course, the medical practitioner will look for that characteristic before applying the treatment. By the same token, the untutored empiric is no longer an untutored empiric, for he doesn’t go about curing relying on pure experience. But what is he now? Not a medical scientist, for those who rely only on theory cannot reach the kind of universals that only practice can yield. At best, they will import the knowledge of  this so-called universal into their theoretical science by being taught by the medical practitioners. And, even then, as we shall see in a moment, the knowledge they have of the universal will not profit them, for without experience they will not know how to apply it to the particular case in order to cure the patients. The reason for this is that the knowledge of how to apply the universal to the particular case arises itself out of experience, together with, and inseparably from, the knowledge of t he universal. As Devereux puts it, our untutored empiric, who  now possesses what Aristotle calls practical knowledge in the medical field, “has hit upon what Aristotle here calls a universal.”

But what, precisely, is what Aristotle calls a universal in this context? As correctly pointed out by Devereux, certainly not the strict, explanatory universal of which Aristotle speaks in the Analytica Posteriora , in which “The universal is prized because it reveals the explanation/cause ( to aition ) (cf. Anal. Post . 88a5-6; also 85b23-27). ^(F11) Quoted by Devereux, p. 490, note 22). Indeed, the common characteristic discovered by our untutored empiric in all the patients in whom treatment T worked and that is absent in those in whom it did not work is discovered through observation and by itself explains nothing. Were it a strict universal connected with the essence, then the medical art could be a strict science, which Aristotle has already denied. Thus the universal whose knowledge, according to Aristotle, is involved in the practice of medicine is rather “a particular,” –something like ‘a general fact,’ a characteristic common to a number of individuals, discovered through what Aristotle calls “experience,” not deduced through thinking. Devereux comment on this is quite on target::

“Thus what is crucial about knowledge of particulars in these passages is that it falls short of being explanatory, not that it is about individuals” (Ibid., p. 491).

To continue with Devereux analysis,

“ Of course the successful practitioner of the art of medicine will combine both kinds of knowledge, and the normal procedure (nowadays at least) is to acquire the universal knowledge before the particular ... The full fledged doctor’s ‘knowledge of particulars’ will not be the same as the untutored empiric’s. The doctor will characteristically see particulars as instances of universals [he will see them as instances of the phlegmatic constitution and treat them accordingly...] If one has thorough knowledge of universals but lacks the ability to recognize particular instances, whether in medical matters or in the sphere of practical wisdom, one will not be able to achieve one’s aims ” (Ibid., p. 492).

“ One needs experience before one can effectively exercise the knowledge , and even if one has a good store of experience to draw from, there will often be situations in which careful thought and deliberation are needed in order to see how the general knowledge should be applied in particular cases ” (Ibid., p. 493).

Nowadays, when there is an established medical tradition and there are schools of medicine, the theory is taught before the practice and, thus, the universal is learned before the experience of particular cases. Hence, when actual experience comes,  the student of medicine, unlike the untutored empiric who learned through experience, will see the observed in individual cases as instances of the theoretical universal he or she learned previously. But this was not the case with  the untutored who learned strictly through experience. First he cured a number of patients by trial and error, and only then he realized that all the patients who responded to treatment T had in common a characteristic not found in those patients who did not respond to the treatment. Thus, from the successful practice in a number of individual cases the untutored moved to the so-called universal that becomes the non-explanatory theory of the theoretical doctor. However –and this is so important that it bears repetition– a scientific doctor may know perfectly the universal, but this purely theoretical knowledge of the universal will be useless unless and until he or she learns how to apply the theory by recognizing the particular cases  to which this theory is to be applied, and the latter can be learned only through experience, as the untutored learned it in the first place.

It seems, therefore, that Aristotle’s vocabulary in the NE is imprecise, not to say equivocal. What he calls “universal” is not at all the strict, explanatory “universal” of which he speaks in the Analytica Posteriora in reference to the theoretical disciplines that are genuinely scientific. Had he in the NE ethics used a more precise language, he would have spoken not of universals but, as Devereux clarifies,  of general facts reached through experience. In that way, much confusion about “universals, “ ”particulars” and “individuals,” in the area of the practical sciences, would have been avoided. But the same imprecision is to be found in Aristotle’s talk about medicine, which sometimes he calls an art ^(F12) (techne) , and sometimes a science (episteme) .

“Now every class of men deliberates about the things that can be done by their own efforts. And in the case of the precise and self-sufficient sciences there is no deliberation, [e.g. about letters] for we have no doubt that they should be written; but the things that are brought about by our own efforts, but not always in the same way, are the things about which we deliberate, e.g. questions of medicine or of money-making ” (NE 1112a33-b 4; quoted by Devereux, p. 492).

“But he clearly believes that to practice medicine one needs more than this  universal knowledge, one also needs knowledge of particulars which comes with experience . Now THE SCIENCE OF MEDICINE is not a theoretical science since its aim is not simply knowledge; its aim is the restoration or maintenance of health , and it is in this respect practical or productive. It is not ‘self-sufficient’ since it cannot achieve its aim without the help of something else, viz.. experience and knowledge of particulars. ‘MEDICAL SKILL,’ on the other hand, is practical in both its aim and its efficacy in action, and it is completely self-sufficient insofar as it needs nothing further to achieve its aim ” (Ibid., pp. 493-4).

“ The doctor, as a methodical practitioner, will undoubtedly make use of such rules, but he will use them only as rules of thumb ” (Ibid., pp. 495-6).

“This does not mean that the person with practical wisdom or medical skill will be at a loss in particular situations, or that they will have to resort to guesswork. Their knowledge of particulars built up from experience (and their ability to deliberate well) make up for the inadequacy of their universal knowledge. [Aristotle’s conception of equity ( epikeia ) is similar.] The science of medicine and the universal component of practical wisdom may be lacking in precision and self-sufficiency, but medical skill and practical wisdom are not. The doctor’s or wise person’s judgment about how to act in a particular case is perfectly determinate and appropriate to the situation; such judgments have the precision which the universal lack ” (Ibid. p. 496).

“ Statements about how to act inevitably fall short of being precise... in two ways: either they are indeterminate or they are not without exception ” (Ibid., 493, note 39).

“ A THEORETICAL SCIENCE is aimed at knowledge for its own sake, and is concerned exclusively with universals . ARTS LIKE MEDICINE AND RHETORIC share with the theoretical sciences the restriction of subject matter to universals, but they are practical in that their aim is not knowledge but what can be done or produced with the knowledge . These arts form a part of the knowledge possessed by skillful doctors or theoreticians; in addition , they will possess what Aristotle calls ‘KNOWLEDGE OF PARTICULARS,’ a knowledge based on experience which enables them to apply their universal knowledge to particular situations . Practical knowledge of this sort differs from both theoretical sciences or arts like medicine and rhetoric in that its subject matter is not restricted to universals; it is equally concerned –or perhaps even more concerned– with particulars. It is like practical sciences in being aimed at action rather than knowledge, but it is also practical in the stronger sense of being efficacious in action. Practical wisdom, in Aristotle’s view, is not a practical science like medicine or rhetoric; it is more like medical or rhetorical SKILL: it is an instance of practical knowledge . The science of politics, of which ethics is a part, would seem to have the same relationship to practical wisdom that the science of medicine has to medical skill [Note 41: “ We should note that Aris is somewhat hesitant about describing politics as a science ; also it is not clear whether he thinks knowledge of politics is a necessary condition or practical wisdom (would he say, f.e., that individuals who have not engaged in philosophical reflection about moral and political questions could not be practically wise?) ]” (Ibid., p. 497). - The universals which form the subject matter of a theoretical science are not regarded as imprecise ; on the contrary, it is the particular or individual which is indeterminate and imprecise. If there are discrepancies between the universals and the particulars falling under them, the defect is on the side of the particulars. In the case of practical knowledge it is just the reverse: it is the universals that are indeterminate and imprecise while the judgments about particular acts in particular circumstances are precise and indeterminate. If there is a discrepancy between the particular judgment of the practically wise person and a universal rule which applies to the situation, the defect is on the side of the universal; it is the particular judgment that is authoritative.  - It is also worth noting the different sorts of CONTRIBUTION MADE BY EXPERIENCE in the theoretical and practical spheres. In a theoretical science like physics, experience has the single role of serving as the stepping stone to our grasp of universals. In practical knowledge, experience has not only this role but also the more important one of providing a grasp of the salient features of particular situation in which decisions are to be made. The knowledge of particulars provided by experience ‘completes’ practical knowledge, assuring that it achieves its end in action.’ (Ibid., 497-8).

“Already in the EE we find a distinction between practical and theoretical knowledge, and phronesis is regarded as a kind of practical knowledge. On the other hand, the only difference between the two kinds of knowledge is a difference of aim . Aristotle says nothing about the problematic subject matter of practical knowledge, nor does he identify the subject matter of ethics as particular actions” (Ibid., p. 504)

(6) Myself on Devereux

Two points: (1) Actually, the universal plays no role in practical knowledge as conceived by Aristotle here. (2 ) The practical knowledge, as conceived here by Aristotle, can best be explained as the practical internalization of which Polanyi speaks, rather than as a combination of theoretical knowledge (universal) and experiential knowledge.

(1) On the basis of the above quoted texts, my suspicion is that Aristotle –and Devereux, too, since he doesn’t correct Aristotle– continues to confuse practical with theoretical knowledge and to make of the latter a condition to the former. My suspicion arises from the role that Aristotle attributes to the universal in practical knowledge, when in fact he should have concluded from his own analysis that  practical knowledge doesn’t demand and doesn’t include knowledge of the universal. The universal is imported from the Platonic conception of knowledge as something seen that corresponds to what is the case (either necessarily, according to Plato, or at least  contingently, according to Aristotle), which is prerequired as a condition for correct action, but in fact it plays no role in practical knowledge as described here by Aristotle . That the case is indeed such is suggested by the following facts included in Aristotle’s own analysis.

- Some who don’t know the universal are more efficient than those who do (p. 485). Hence, the knowledge of the universal is not required for practical knowledge.

- No room for episteme , that is, strict universal knowledge, in practical matters (p. 488).

- For action, experience is more important than logos (p. 488).So much so (he should have realized) that logos plays no role.

- No art or science concerns itself with individuals , only with particulars as typical . Knowledge of the individual is relevant for practicing the art or science, but not for the art or science (p. 489). So the science or art itself is not practical, and the practice doesn’t require knowledge of the universal.

- The untutored or empiric reaches the ‘pseudo-universal’, the ‘practical universal,” on the basis of experience, not of theoretical deduction (p 490).

- The illusion of the need of universal knowledge comes from teaching, where the theory precedes the practice (p. 492) But in fact the practice doesn’t require theoretical learning as shown by the case of the untutored. Geometry before Euclid is a case in point. And the student who assents to the theorem, that is, who knows the universal, but doesn’t know how to apply it to particular problems, has no practical knowledge.

- Again, experience is needed to effectively exercise the knowledge (493).

- Medical science is practical in that it is learned for the sake of curing, but it is not self-sufficient because it needs experience. By contrast, medical skill is practical and self-sufficient . Why? Because it doesn’t require knowledge of  the universal. (p. 494).

- The universal is pseudo, for it is mere rule of thumb (p. 495-6). And anyway, if it doesn’t quite fit the situation, it should be dropped in favor of experience (pp. 497-8).

- The knowledge of the particular and the ability to deliberate well, that is, to choose the end when the means are available, makes up for lack of universal knowledge (496). So the latter is not required because it plays no important role.

 - There are theoretical sciences with strict universals, like math and physics. There are

practical sciences similar to the theoretical in that they are “restricted to universals,” nonetheless they are practical because their aim is practical, like medicine and rhetoric (but not self-sufficient). And there are efficacious sciences like medical skill and practical wisdom, in that they actually terminate in action. (p. 497). However, the science of medicine is not necessary to cure, the science of rhetoric is not necessary to practice rhetoric effectively, and the science of politics is not necessary to act wisely (p. 497), (anymore than the science of Christian theology is necessary to

Aquinas’s “ vetula ” to be a good Christian). Now, since the universal knowledge is in the ‘practical’ science, not in the exercise of the skill, the knowledge of the universal is not required for correct action.(Rather, it seems to me, the practical sciences are constituted by the generalities and rules of thumb detected by reflection on the way the successful practitioners act. However, since the circumstances change, there are no strict universals, only rules of thumb. The case of the laws is a good example of this).

It is clear, then, that the requirement of speculative knowledge for correct action, as a constitutive element of practical knowledge, is superfluous, a mere relic of Aristotle’s Platonism of which he didn’t manage to get rid. The relic is the knowledge of the universal involved in practical knowledge. This shows that, like his predecessors, Aristotle continued to believe that theoretical knowledge of what is the case was a condition for correct action, though he realized that the knowledge could not be scientific . Thus, pace Devereux, Aristotle never saw practical knowledge as generically different from theoretical knowledge (p. 483 ).*

[* Aristotle seems to have begun with the Socratic-Platonic ideal of knowledge as a vision that corresponded necessarily to what is the case. Thus knowledge was contemplative, a vision, and different from opinion in that it corresponded necessarily to what is the case. This he applied to ethics, too, in the Protreptikon , where he conceived ethics more geometrico (cf. Gauthier, particularly in the Prologue to the NE). Gradually he modified this view. In the case of perception, he relaxed the requirement of necessary correspondence. Thus he admitted contingent (merely factual rather than demonstrative) correspondence, but visual correspondence nonetheless. In this way he blurred the radical Platonic distinction between opinion and knowledge. In the case of practical knowledge –because of its circumstantial (and desirous or teleological character)-- he also relaxed the necessary correspondence (no possibility of episteme in practical matters. But this very remark (together with the universal’s function) seems to indicate that he still thought of practical knowledge in terms of correspondence, for he doesn’t deny correspondence in the case of practical knowledge, he just denies that the correspondence is necessary. The only addition here is that the knowledge in the efficacious sciences is imprecise because they deal with “particulars.” However, he didn’t say that perceptual knowledge, that also deals with particulars, is imprecise, but only that there is no room for episteme because of the corruptible or contingent character of sensible objects. Nor did he say  in the case of perceptual knowledge that because it deals with particulars it is prudential ( phronesis) or that it involves deliberation, decision and action. Thus the sense of “particulars” in the case of perceptual knowledge and of practical knowledge is very different. I think that the difference is in terms of exposure and non-exposure and, hence, of desire and disinterested contemplation]. Aris didn’t see the connection between perception and action; perception is practical.

(2) As for the sort of ‘knowledge’ that is efficacious but doesn’t require or involve any universal knowledge, I said above that it can be best characterized as “tacit” and can be best explained as the practical interiorization of which Polanyi speaks . This applies to perception, too.

I do not claim, however, that Aristotle actually conceived it in that way. I only claim that he should have, and that he came close to something like that when he considered the action of animals and what he called their practical knowledge. What prevented him from escaping Platonism fully was that he compared and assimilated the action of animals to the pseudo-practical knowledge he had attributed to people (the one we just considered, which involves knowledge of universals under the form of induction), instead of moving in the direction of seeing the applicative knowledge of people as a higher variety of the practical knowledge of animals. Indeed, in certain cases it involves deliberation and is prudential or phronetic in a way beyond the ken of animals, a higher capacity connected with our linguistic ability (which is not in any way contemplative of what is the case, as imagined by Searle).  

Aristotle  speaks of practical knowledge, and he understands knowledge as ‘visual’ correspondence, much as Kant’s ‘knowledge’ will require ‘intuition.’. Like Socrates and Plato, he wants to know how to act, before acting and independently of acting. In other words, he wants to have an accurate mental picture of the correct action.  MOREOVER, THE PICTURE IS NEUTRAL, ‘OBJECTIVE,’ FOR LIKE IN KANT’S CASE ANY ‘INTERESTED’ OR SELF-REGARDING DESIRE IS NOT ONLY ABSENT BUT EXCLUDED AS INTERFERING WITH KNOWLEDGE, MUCH AS IT HAPPENED WITH PLATO. AND IT HAPPENED WITH PLATO BECAUSE THE RHETORICIANS HAD APPEALED TO THE BASE APPETITES OF THOSE THEY WANTED TO PERSUADE TO ACT RATHER THAN BEING MOVED BY GENUINE KNOWLEDGE OF THE GOOD IN ITSELF. IN OTHER WORDS, MUCH AS AQUINAS, THEY DISTINGUISHED BEING MOVED (TO ASSENT) BY THE OBJECT ITSELF AS IT REVEALS ITSELF TO THE DISINTERESTED KNOWER, FROM BEING MOVED BY THE OBJECT IN RELATION TO THE INTERESTED DESIRES OF THE SUBJECT WHICH HAVE NOTHING TO DO WITH KNOWLEDGE .

The question arises, though –a question not considered by Aristotle-- as to the possibility of correspondence in practical knowledge which is knowledge not of a being (of a substance or an accident, or of a sensible object in space and time, capable of being intuited) but of what is to be done in the future (a “ faciendum” –OF THE ORDER OF INTEREST AND DESIRE-- in a participatory encounter and which doesn’t terminate in contemplation but in action, through deliberation and decision. Deliberation is about what is to be done and it leads to decision. But deciding how to act is not an image of what is to be done to satisfy a desire (indeed, how can there be a correspondence with a future desirable state of affairs that doesn’t exist yet) but a practical disposition to act in a certain way under the appropriate circumstances, and such a disposition that it will result in action when the appropriate circumstances obtain. In other words, the metaphor of contemplation, and consequently of visual image that corresponds to what is, AND WHICH EXCLUDES DESIRE AND FEAR BECAUSE THE PHILOSOPHER HAS REDUCED HIMSELF TO A PURE INVULNERABLE CONTEMPLATOR OF A WORLD OR PICTURE CONSTITUTED BY PURE ‘OBJECTS,’ breaks down in the area of practical knowledge. Actually, were it not for the fact that we are, like Aristotle, sold to the idea of knowledge as correspondence, we would have no reason to look for correspondence in the practical field, for there is no possible visual correspondence here . And where there is no correspondence there is no knowledge, in the Platonic-Aristotelian sense, for the essential characteristic of knowledge is correspondence. In other words, in the order of agency there is no room for the notions of knowledge and of truth. This we may be able to realize if we forget the Platonic notion of knowledge as correspondence and begin with the analysis of action, as Aristotle did in De Motu Animalium.

(7) Aristotle’s analysis of action in De Motu Animalium

Aristotle claims that animals know in the realm of the sensible, though they do not understand. But is he thinking in terms of correspondence when he claims that animals know? Does Aristotle mean that animals have images that correspond to what is the case, as supposedly we humans do? In the case of people it is easy to speak of knowledge as correspondence, for they verbalize what they know or claim to know, and they verbalize as well as their conviction that what they claim to know is true,’ that is, corresponds  to what is. But animals do not verbalize their experience. On what basis, then, does Aristotle contend that animals know, and what exactly does he mean by that?

 First of all, the only knowledge Aristotle attributes to animals is practical, not only in that animals cannot pursue knowledge for its own sake but also in that it is knowledge that actually terminates in action, because it not disinterested (as theoretical knowledge is), but is guided by desire or fear. ^(F13) In fact, were it not for the intrinsic connection of knowledge with action in the case of the practical knowledge of animals, Aristotle wouldn’t be able to determine that animals possess practical knowledge , for the only basis on which he can determine that is the way they act. Indeed, Aristotle doesn’t affirm that all animals know. He is certain only in the case of those animals that are capable of locomotion. Why? Because the latter obviously act not merely repetitively but adaptively. That is, they do not act purely mechanically, which would only allow for repetition of inherited automatic action in response to an external cause; they discover or invent new conducts adapted to their goals. Desire guides them in this discovery .

The laboratory experiments with rats in a maze or with rats that learn to push a lever to get food is a good example. On the basis of past successful experience they learn the appropriate conduct to satisfy their need for food. Once they have learned it, they go directly and unhesitatingly to the place where there is food or push the appropriate lever unhesitatingly. Mammals superior to rats, of which apes are the best example, even adapt the memorized conduct to new situations. Because of these types of action, Aristotle would say that these animals know. But what does he mean? That they have in their minds or in their “memory”(since Aristotle speaks of memory) a kind of image that corresponds to what is the case?

An important reversal occurs –or should have occurred at this point of Aristotle’ epistemic evolution. The Socratic-Platonic assumption that knowledge is a necessary condition for acting correctly should have been discarded here, at least for the case of animals, if not of people, because Aristotle should have realized that animals do not act correctly or successfully because they know in the sense of having a visual image of what is the case. [DOES ARIS REALIZE THIS? HE DOESN’T. AND THIS IS THE QUESTION]. Rather, they come to know because they happened to act correctly and on the basis of their  having acted correctly by chance. . The action that was attended with success is automatically associated [IN THE SENSE OF PRODUCING AN EXPECTATION, A PRACTICAL BRACING ITSELF ON THE ANIMAL’S PART] with the circumstances in which it occurred, so that when similar circumstances occur in the future under the appropriate condition of desire the successful action is repeated thanks to the animal’s desire for the satisfaction that attended the action on the previous occasion. Aristotle doesn’t actually speak of association but of memory. And, in Metaphysics, Lectio !, desire plays no prominent role, as it actually does in the sections of De Motu Animalium (Nussbaum, p. 179) and of EN (CHECK THIS) that refer to the practical syllogism. What Aristotle speaks of in the Metaphysics is a kind of generalizing inference the animal does on the basis of past experience which tanks to memory and imagination or phantasia or affective imagination will lead to successful action in the future. At this stage, Aristotle still seems to think in terms of some theoretical generalization made by the animal, though obviously not expressed linguistically by the animal. Indeed , he compares animal knowledge to human knowledge and finds animal knowledge similar –though not identical-- to the knowledge quia people acquire on the basis of experience. At this stage, it seems, Aris still thinks that the difference between speculative and practical knowledge is only a difference in aims. “Cum sensus ad duo in nobis deserviant, sc. ad cognitionem rerum et ad utilitatem vitae” (wp\monas\bible-5, p. 3). On this point, see my analysis in “On truth and practical knowledge - Nov. 3.99,” and in “The question of truth - wp\98\sept 1".

Two important observations in this connection. First, tough successful action precedes and generates ‘knowledge,’ once ‘knowledge’ is acquired it generates successful action in the future. Hence the illusion that theoretical knowledge –of the kind that is transmitted through teaching by the untutored who has reflected on his past successful actions-- is a condition for correct or successful action and that in order to act successfully one should first possess knowledge. Second, let us call “knowledge,” or “practical knowledge,” as Aristotle does,  whatever is acquired by the animal as a result of successful action, which leads that animal to successful action in the future. But what, precisely, is the character of this practical knowledge? Is it anything resemblant to what is the case, so that we can speak of knowledge as correspondence to what is?  Aristotle suggests that there is a sort of generalization on the animal’s part. Something like “Under these circumstances, that is what is to be done to achieve satisfaction,” which seems to be a true statement that corresponds to what is the case. However, as animals are non-linguistic, it is highly improbable that the knowledge we are talking about be of this kind, for they don’t make statements. What is more important, knowledge of this kind wouldn’t result in action. Indeed, I can possess the knowledge expressed in the statement, and yet it won’t result in action on my part even if the circumstances referred to obtain. [Agent’s knowledge in the first person or self-referential to the agent, because internalized as a skill, not simply formulated or reducible to formulation]. Why not? Because I am not the rat that experienced the situation and the successful action. So, if there were a generalizing statement made by the rat, it would be a personal statement by the rat and self-referential to the rat , which would go something like this: “Under these circumstances, that is, if I find myself in this maze ( which I will remember and recognize because of my practical –that is, unformulated or ‘tacit ’-- experience of it (which is the practical or tacit memory to which Aristotle refers in the context of the knowledge animals possess),. this is what is to be done by me to achieve satisfaction.” Even then, however, this knowledge would be purely theoretical, for it wouldn’t result in action by the rat. For action by the rat to obtain, the rat has to be actually desirous of food . And desire, be it desire of food or of anything else, can be referred to by a statement, but is not itself an object of knowledge for the agent; it is a mode of being of the practical knower, that is, of the agent, without which the knower’s knowledge cannot be practical for it cannot result in action . So practical knowledge results in action because it is structured toward a telos by actual desire or aversion on the knower’s part. This is what Martha Nussbaum, in her analysis of practical knowledge in Aristotle, calls “desirous knowledge” (by implicit contrast to the traditional conception of knowledge as objective, as cold or neutral representation of what is , to which what is corresponds). And what is new in this Aristotelian conception of practical knowledge by contrast to his Platonic conception of knowledge as mere correspondence is precisely this element of desire, of hexis or movement toward . ^(F14)

In her study of the practical syllogism in Aristotle, Nussbaum has some trouble explaining its peculiar logic. (See my \wp\97\97-oct2, or Monas\Bible-5, p. 13). ^(F15) I think that the reason is that it is not a matter of logic, that is, of a combination of things objectively known. Logic is a matter of objective, disinterested judgments-statements that aim at eliciting assent. To speak of a logic that involves desire and of a syllogism that terminates in, or aims at, action is an absurdity. The agent’s statements (insofar as they are formulated) are not objective, predicative statements of what is the case, but personal, self-referential of what the desirous agent encounters. Therefore the ‘knowledge’ is ‘tacit” (not formulated) and ‘applicative” (disposition to act).  

I think that Polanyi’s “tacit knowledge” is more promising in connection with what Aristotle calls practical knowledge . Although Polanyi, like Aristotle, still speaks of knowledge, which suggests correspondence and objectivity or objective correspondence, which tends to vitiate the import of the analysis, the fact that it is ‘tacit’ implies that it is not verbalized in judgements-statements and not reducible to them, for what the subject ‘knows’ is not known by the subject, since it is not stated to himself or herself by the subject. In this sense, the subject doesn’t know, for the subject doesn’t know objectively. The ‘knowledge’ is internalized or appropriated by the subject and takes the form of an ability or disposition or inclination to act in a certain unformulated way , not the form of a specified rule, under certain unspecified or unformulated  circumstances, in response to or in relation to certain unspecified objects in order to achieve certain unspecified results, much as is the case with the skills of playing the piano or driving a car or making bread or guiding oneself by using a map. If one wants formulations, which are useful for teaching, the formulations are mere rules of thumb for the person taught, but the agent has to find out when and how to apply them, how concretely to guide his action by them . And this cannot be taught, for it cannot be specified or formulated. Only the agent, in the concrete circumstances, can decide how to act appropriately. It is a matter of deliberation and action, when there is room and need for deliberation, or of acquired habit.  I think that this is what Aristotle sensed obscurely when, in the NE, he spoke of the imprecision of practical knowledge by contrast to the precision of scientific theoretical knowledge, and when he declared that the standard of prudent action couldn’t be otherwise specified than by saying that the standard it is the prudent action of the phronimos , of the practically wise, of the one who possesses practical wisdom.

Not without reason did Aristotle distinguish the practical knowledge involved in human action from techne , the knowledge involved in making an object or achieving a concrete result in the environment when this can be reduced to the mechanical application of rules. But perhaps he would have gained if he had underscored the kinship of the knowledge involved in human action with the knowledge involved in making or achieving an external result (even cooking a dish or making bread), when the maker has to discover how and when to apply.

(8) On Aristotle’s Platonism

My first comment concerns the Platonic conception of knowledge as correspondence with what is. As explained above, Aristotle relaxed the requirement of necessary correspondence with what is in order to be able to include in the field of knowledge the sensible particulars of perception. However –and this is the important point– he didn’t modify the requirement of correspondence with what is. In other words, he continued to believe, as Plato had, that knowledge amounted to correspondence with what is.

Now, Plato had been able to save the notion of knowledge as correspondence only because the eternity of the Ideas insured that the correspondence of universal concepts with their objects was necessary. And precisely because the correspondence couldn’t be necessary in the case of the sensible and particular objects of perception did Plato exclude such objects from the field of knowledge and relegated them to the sphere of opinion. For his part, Aristotle maintained the Platonic requirement of correspondence, but dropped the Platonic requirement of necessary correspondence. By the same token, however, he opened the door to skepticism concerning perceptual knowledge, for he couldn’t explain when the correspondence with what is obtained and when it did not, given that the correspondence itself was merely contingent and, therefore, may or may not obtain in any particular case. In short, Aristotle had two options: either abandon completely Plato’s epistemic views  by dropping the requirement of correspondence with what is, or keep the requirement of necessary correspondence as Plato had done, and then follow the Platonic logic and  renounce the possibility of genuine perceptual knowledge. Unfortunately, however, Aristotle wanted to eat his cake and have it too, thereby leaving as his epistemic legacy the impossible problem of combining two incompatible features: correspondence with what is, and contingent correspondence. The epistemic legacy of correspondence, as we shall see, would reach its critical moment with Kant.  

There is, however, another thorny feature to Aristotle’s epistemic views. Not only did Aristotle continue the Platonic tradition of conceiving knowledge as correspondence, but he didn’t give up entirely on the Socratic-Platonic ideal of knowledge as necessarily true. By bringing the Platonic Ideas from their transcendent heaven down to our world, Aristotle preserved an area within which the Platonic ideal of necessary correspondence remained, for the essences of actually existing particulars were intelligible and necessary like the Platonic Ideas. The knowledge corresponding to that area, that is, necessarily true knowledge, Aristotle called scientific knowledge or episteme . However, when in the Nicomachean Ethics Aristotle explored the field of action, that is, of practical knowledge, he realized that within it there was no possibility of scientific knowledge because, like perceptual knowledge, practical knowledge dealt with the particular and contingent. In other words, in Aristotle’s view, Socrates and Plato hadn’t been entirely wrong in conceiving the ideal of necessary correspondence. Their mistake, for lack of distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge, was to believe that practical knowledge could be necessarily true, when in fact such attribute belonged to speculative knowledge exclusively.

As already explained, the distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge was another modification of the Platonic epistemology introduced by Aristotle. Why hadn’t Socrates and Plato felt the need for that distinction? Because in their eagerness to distance themselves from the rhetoricians, they had accused them of ignorance for failing to teach what was necessarily true in matters of conduct and action that affected the polis. Now, Aristotle came progressively to the realization that the necessarily true knowledge pursued by Plato and Aristotle was only possible in the theoretical field of the essences of material substances, for such essences were intelligible an necessary, as Plato had imagined his Ideas to be. In the practical field, however, that is, in the field of conduct and action, Aristotle found necessarily true or scientific knowledge to be impossible, for practical knowledge deals with the particular and contingent. Thus, Aristotle concluded in the Nicomachean Ethics , practical knowledge (like perceptual knowledge, for not entirely dissimilar reasons) could at best be contingently true.

The Aristotelian distinction between theoretical and practical knowledge as well as his conviction that the possibility of reaching necessarily true or scientific knowledge was not the same in the theoretical and in the practical spheres would be part of the Aristotelian intellectual legacy. Though not everybody, of course, would agree with him  

The important point, for my present purposes, is not so much that Aristotle modified radically the Socratic-Platonic legacy by discarding the possibility of scientific knowledge in matters of conduct and action, but the exact opposite: that he nonetheless continued the Socratic-Platonic line of thought by admitting that, in matters of conduct and action,  knowledge –however contingent and, therefore, theoretically uncertain-- was possible.

To summarize now what regards the relationship between Aristotle to Plato in their respective epistemic convictions, there is no doubt that Aristotle’s views ended up being significantly different from Plato’s. Actually, I have been pointing out some of the differences between them, the importance of which cannot be minimized. Taking that for granted, I want to conclude this portion of my exploration by underscoring that the Aristotelian epistemic legacy is essentially Platonic, both in that it left unmodified and reinforced the Platonic conception of knowledge as correspondence, and in that it left unmodified and reinforced the Platonic conviction that in matters of conduct and action knowledge –though not necessarily true-- was still possible. Because in this two very important regards Aristotle’s epistemic views remained Platonic, I think we are authorized to see Aristotle’s works as the first in the long series of footnotes to Plato in which the Western philosophical tradition has rightly been said to consist.

Thus, concerning knowledge as correspondence, Aristotle continued the Platonic tradition. Now, this idea may be so commonsensical as to appear indisputable, and this is why it has survived till our day in spite of  the numerous attacks of which it has been the object across the centuries. But the very fact that it has been so constantly the object of attacks may be significant. Why indeed would it have been so constantly attacked if it had proved to be satisfactory?

We cannot fully understand the problems involved in the notion of knowledge as correspondence without relating it to difficulties involved in the areas of application of the three  distinctions by means of which Aristotle modified Plato’s views, to wit: the distinctions between scientific and non-scientific knowledge, between necessarily true and contingently true knowledge, and between theoretical and practical knowledge.

Why, in the first place, did Aristotle distinguish between scientific and non-scientific knowledge? Because Plato had assimilated the latter to opinion or belief, which amounted to excluding the entire domain of particular objects from the sphere of knowledge. Now, concretely, this amounted to excluding from the sphere of knowledge both perceptual knowledge and practical knowledge, for both had to do with particulars. However, by dropping the requirement of necessary truth Aristotle may, on the one hand, have rescued the areas of perception and ethics or ethical action as fields of knowledge, but by the same token, on the other hand, he was opening the entire field of knowledge to skepticism. Why, indeed, had Plato so adamantly stuck by his distinction between knowledge and opinion? Not because he didn’t appreciate the difference between purely arbitrary or totally irrational belief and probable though not absolutely certain rational opinion,  but because, at least in his early stages, Plato had entertained the Socratic hope of absolutely certain or necessarily true knowledge in the practical domain of human action.   .

The notion of knowledge as correspondence reached its critical point –perhaps we should rather say, its breaking point-- with Kant. Such notion, Kant argued, inevitably led to skepticism, for it is absolutely impossible to know whether or not the supposed correspondence with what is, with “the thing in itself,” obtains. As a solution to this aporia, Kant gave up on the intractable

disastrous implications the requirement of necessary truth which had led Plato to postulate the existence of the eternal Ideas had on the validity of perceptual knowledge. Indeed, in Plato’s eyes, the merely contingent existence of the particular objects of perception andle; therefore, their truth is, at best, contingent rather than necessary. From this it follows that perceptual knowledge, for all its sensible evidence, is not genuine knowledge but mere opinion. Now, even without considering the offensiveness of this view to common sense, it practically leads to absolute skepticism, since all knowledge, even the necessarily true knowledge of universal natures, has its origin in perceptual knowledge. Indeed, the intelligible natures of whose necessarily true knowledge Aristotle defends are the natures of the particular, sensible objects in which they subsist. Thus, if the sensible objects themselves cannot be genuinely known, neither can their intelligible natures.

Strictly speaking, other than his own commonsensical pragmatism, Aristotle has no argument to justify his abrogation of the requirement of necessary correspondence with what is in order to accept the validity of perceptual knowledge. Yet he did abrogate it. By the same token, though, he softened the Platonic distinction between opinion and knowledge, since in the case of perception, which Plato had placed in the region of opinion, Aristotle made an exception and deemed it genuine knowledge while agreeing with Plato that perception could yield no more than contingent truth. This led Aristotle to distinguish two types of knowledge: the necessarily true knowledge of intelligible universal natures, which he called “episteme” or “scientific knowledge” (the only type that Socrates and Plato could conceivably have deemed genuine knowledge rather than opinion), on the one hand, and, on the other hand, the not necessarily true, but nonetheless factually or contingently true, knowledge of the sensible, contingent  particulars accessed through perception.. To the latter, non-scientific knowledge, Aristotle came to give the name of (“ phronesis”) “sophia” by contrast to “episteme.”      

Kant gave up on the notion of knowledge as correspondence by means of his Copernican revolution, and retreated into critical idealism. Then, thanks to his pragmatic belief in the validity of Newtonian physics (reminiscent of Aristotle’s pragmatic belief in the validity of perceptual knowledge), he made the synthetic a priori judgments of physics into the model of knowledge. This move, however, implied the impossibility of practical knowledge, for it amounted to saying that knowledge in the field of human action, the very kind of knowledge that Socrates had considered important and worth pursuing, was impossible. Kant had to admit this, and actually did so. However, in order to save the day in such a momentous area as human action from the ethical point of view, he declared the justificatory conditions for ethical action to be necessary postulates of practical reason, that is, not objects of knowledge but objects of rational faith. Which of course, I must point out, didn’t do away with the notion of correspondence in the area of practical knowledge, but simply declared such correspondence to be a matter of rational or rationally necessary faith.
