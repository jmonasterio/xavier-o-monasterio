THE THIRTEENTH CENTURY AND THE ECLIPSE OF THE HUMANITIES

Historical background The question whether theology could qualify as "a science" arose in the first quarter of the thirteenth century. And it could not possibly have been raised before, for it arose in connection with the rediscovery of Aristoteles's works and in direct reference to the notion of science introduced by him in the Posterior Analytics .

Roughly speaking, for Aristoteles a science is a corpus of statements about a specific class of objects, expressing universal and necessary properties belonging to that class of objects by virtue of their nature, and resulting either from immediate self-evidence in the case of "first principles," or from strictly deductive evidence in all other cases. Whether such knowledge is possible at all or within which limits, whether Aristoteles' 'scientific' works correspond to his own precepts, whether he changed his mind about the possibility of such knowledge or proposed other alternatives which reflect better his own practice are questions to which much attention has been given in recent years by specialists like Owens and Nussbaum among many others . For my purposes, however, these issues are of no immediate relevance in that they lie beyond the horizon of the medieval theologians. The latter simply took for granted the validity of the Aristotelian scientific model of the Posterior Analytics , and their questions were whether the theological endeavor could fit its demands, and whether consequently the knowledge resulting from this endeavor could constitute a genuinely scientific corpus of knowledge.

The debate took place mostly in Paris and Oxford. The answer finally accepted was affirmative, and the thinker whose solution carried not only the day but the immediately subsequent centuries was Thomas Aquinas. As a consequence, the voices of those who had objected to the possibility of a scientific theology were left behind and forgotten as irrelevant. My purpose is to give them--the opposition's voices--a second hearing from a 20th century perspective .  

A 20th century perspective Given the theological nature of the medieval debate, the opposition's concerns centered on the legitimacy of submitting the biblical text, by which divine revelation was deemed to be conveyed, to the strictures of the Aristotelian scientific mold. Could 'literary' works as complex and varied as the biblical books be reduced to a series of 'scientific' statements  capable of entering as logical premises in syllogisms, without violation and ultimate loss of their meaning and, consequently, of their proper validity? Could those biblical books that by their literary form purport to be historical, that are shaped as narratives, and that deal with singular characters and unrepeatable events, meet the requirements of universality and necessity of the Aristotelian model without utter distortion? Could the emotionally charged character of significant passages of the biblical books be filtered out in order to integrate their elements under the form of mere statements into the structure of Aristotelian logic without the passages losing their original meaning together with their distinctive impact? Could the temporal and dramatic structure of such passages be translated, without undergoing essential adulteration, into  the atemporal statements required by the Aristotelian model? In a nutshell, could a work like the Bible, which inspires people in their lives and fires them to act in distinctive ways, remain substantially identical under the constraints imposed by the Aristotelian conception of science? The above formulation is my interpretive summary of the opposition's objections. It is based on a preliminary study of the original sources which I carried out in the summer of 1991 at the library of the Centre Culturel "Les Fontaines," at Chantilly, France. This library, specialized in philosophy and theology, contains 600,000 documents. I should interject that all my original sources are in Latin, and that I read this language fluently, as well as French, Italian, Spanish and (less fluently) German and Greek.

The first step of my project , which for obvious reasons I would like to conduct at the same library in a month's period, will consist in a further systematic study of my sources in order to finish gathering and calibrating the pertinent material. I am, therefore, asking for $750.00 for travel, in addition to the normal stipend.

The second step of my project , which I will conduct at home during the second month, will consist in actual writing to show that the opposition's objections gain their full intelligibility and strength when formulated in the terms of two contemporary schools of thought, one philosophical  and the other philosophico-literary: speech-acts theory, and narrative theory.

(1) Speech-acts theory . The important contributions of this school for my present purposes are the distinction among different speech acts, the realization that stating is just one among many other no less meaningful speech-acts, and the implication that different speech-acts are not reducible to each other and, in particular, that other speech-acts are not reducible to statements without loss or distortion of meaning. If this is correct, as it arguably is, then the reduction of all biblical speech-acts to statements to make the biblical text fit the Aristotelian scientific structure was wrongheaded.

(2) Narrative theory .  Whereas speech-acts theory deals with sentences, narrative theory concerns itself with whole "literary genres" in general and with the narrative genre in particular. As speech-acts are different and irreducible to each other, so are literary genres. The literary genre of scientific treatises is totally different from the narrative genre. This implies that the reduction of biblical narratives to the scientific genre required by the Aristotelian model was wrongheaded.

If I succeed in establishing that the opposition's objections to the adoption of the Aristotelian scientific model  can legitimately be couched in terms of speech-acts theory and narrative theory, and in making persuasive their force when couched in this way, the limited project I hereby submit for funding will be successfully completed.

Significance of the project Taken by itself this project might seem insignificant and even hopelessly anachronistic. What purpose, indeed, may be served by reopening a medieval controversy and by attempting to resettle it in a way different from that in which it was actually settled--and this by using tools of analysis that were not available at the time to the participants in the controversy?

As a matter of fact, however, the adoption of the Aristotelian scientific ideal, which was the actual outcome of the controversy, happened to have an enormous influence on the cultural life of the Western world. Its effects, of course, were most immediately felt in the theological realm. Indeed, the reduction of the variety of faith-expressions to statements whose truth could be submitted to and decided by the rules of scientific evidence automatically created the direct confrontation between faith and reason that the Enlightenment would definitively settle in favor of reason, with the consequent subjectivization of religious faith. But that was far from all. As a result of the adoption of the Aristotelian notion of science, philosophy was correlatively defined as, and actually became, a project of reducing the complex relations of the human subject to the surrounding world, which find their natural expression in the multiplicity of speech-acts and literary genres, to the shape of statements whose validity was to be determined in terms of the evidential rules of scientific reason. Kant's Critique of Pure Reason , with its reduction of the phaenomenal to the strictly sensorial and of the cognitive function to predication, is a culminating case in point. Finally--and this is especially important for my project--the adoption of the Aristotelian model entailed as well the loss of the cognitive value of the humanities and, hence, their  prolonged eclipse. Indeed, all 'literary' works, of which the Bible is only one example, necessarily had to share with the Bible the fate of being relegated to the domain of the purely imaginary, of the mythical, of the a-rational or the irrational. Thus, my reopening of an old controversy, and my use of contemporary tools of analysis to show that the objections to the way in which the debate was actually settled had merit, is far from insignificant, given the very significant consequences of the way in which the controversy was settled.

In fact, I take this project as a first piece of a larger project to render persuasive the view-- dimly suggested by Aristotle himself in his ethical works--that the 'logic' of action is radically different from the logic of statements, and that practical rationality is irreducible to scientific rationality. This larger project is underpinned by my conviction that only by returning its proper role and its primacy to practical rationality can we hope to devolve their genuine relevance to the humanities and thereby gain a better understanding of ourselves and of the world in which we have to take our risks.
